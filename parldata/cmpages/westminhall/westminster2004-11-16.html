In September 2000, Heads of State and Government from across the world met at the United Nations millennium summit to address the major challenges facing humanity at the dawn of the new millennium. The outcome was the millennium declaration—a powerful statement of shared fundamental values and key objectives. Of course, values and objectives are essential, but in a vacuum they are just words. Crucially, the millennium declaration also contains eight goals, with 18 associated concrete, quantifiable and time-limited targets. Nearly 190 countries have signed up to the millennium development goals, and the Department for International Development has made them the main focus of its work. Goal six of the millennium declaration is to

    "Combat HIV/AIDS, malaria and other diseases".

The associated targets and indicators commit the international community, by 2015, to have halted and begun to reverse the spread of HIV/AIDS and the incidence of malaria and tuberculosis.

It was clear to anyone who knew anything about AIDS, malaria or TB that we did not have a chance of making so much as a dent in their spread without a massive increase in international effort. The figures speak for themselves. Last year, close to 5 million people acquired HIV, bringing the number of those living with the virus to about 38 million. The AIDS epidemic claimed nearly 3 million lives last year, many as a result of TB. Each year, more than 8 million people become sick with TB, 2 million of whom do not survive. Malaria strikes 300 million to 500 million people every year and kills more than 1 million, nearly all of them in Africa, and most of them children under five years of age. These three diseases are entirely preventable and treatable, but between them they cause more than 6 million deaths worldwide every year, mostly in the developing world. Every death is a personal tragedy. However, the diseases strike not only individuals; they attack communities, regions and nations, causing economic decline, social breakdown and political instability.

The Global Fund operates in a series of rounds. Every eight months, it launches a new round, issuing an invitation to interested parties to apply for grants. Applications are then scrutinised by the fund's technical review panel and sent to the fund's board with a recommended course of action. The principal recipient of a grant does not have to be a Government agency. It could be a community-based organisation or an NGO, a faith-based organisation, an affected community, an academic institution or a private sector body.

Proposals are normally made for five years, but a two-year grant agreement is initially negotiated. Disbursements are made during those two years, usually quarterly. Towards the end of that time, the overall performance of the programme is evaluated by the Global Fund to determine whether to renew the grant.
