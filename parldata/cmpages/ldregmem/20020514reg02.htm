FOREWORD

BY THE CLERK OF THE PARLIAMENTS

The Code of Conduct, adopted by the House on 2nd July 2001, came into force on 1st April 2002. The Code (the text of which is printed below) makes more extensive provision for the registration of interests than had been previously required.

The Code requires all Members of the House in receipt of a Writ of Summons, who are not on Leave of Absence, to register all relevant interests. The test of relevance is whether the interest might reasonably be thought by the public to affect the way in which a Member discharges his or her parliamentary duties. Relevant interests include both financial and non-financial interests. Interests which are deemed always to be relevant and which must be declared are set out in paragraphs 12 (financial) and 15 (non-financial) of the Code of Conduct; interests which may be relevant, depending on their significance, are set out in paragraphs 13 and 16.

The Code of Conduct maintains the "no paid advocacy" rule. Thus members "must not vote on any bill or motion, or ask any question in the House or a committee, or promote any matter, in return for payment or any other material benefit" (paragraph 4(d) of the Code). This restriction does not extend to matters relating to Members' outside employment or directorships, where the interest does not arise from membership of the House. Members should, however, be especially cautious in deciding whether to speak or vote in relation to interests that are direct, pecuniary and shared by few others.

It is recommended that registrations relating to paragraphs 13 and 16 of the Code ("other relevant interests") be restricted to those interests which might reasonably be thought by the public to affect in general the way in which Members discharge their parliamentary duties. A Member may have additional interests, relevant in particular circumstances, which will then require declaration in the context of those circumstances (for example, a debate).

In the section covering "shareholdings not amounting to a controlling interest" the Code requires that only significant holdings should be registered. For the purposes of this section the Sub-Committee on Lords' Interests recommends that, while it is for each Member to determine what is significant in this context, ownership of 5% or more of a concern might reasonably be considered significant.

Under paragraph 13(c) (the financial interests of spouse or relative or friend) it is unlikely that an interest not held by Members of the House themselves will be appropriate for registration although it may, of course, be necessary to declare such an interest when speaking in the House. The advice of the Registrar should be sought whenever there is doubt as to whether a interest is relevant for the purposes of the register, and in all cases of difficulty the Registrar will consult the Sub-Committee. Members of the House should accept its decision as if it were that of the House.

  Members of the House must register all relevant interests within one month of acquiring them. Visits made and gifts received before 1st March 2002 need not be registered. Interests which have lapsed since 31st March when the new Code of Conduct came into force have not been entered.

  The Sub-Committee recommends that "patron" is not a post which needs to be registered; some members have nevertheless chosen to include certain patronships in their entries.

  For further guidance, Members may wish to consult paragraphs 4.60-4.66 of the Companion to the Standing Orders and Guide to the Proceedings of the House of Lords; the debates and resolutions of the House of 1st and 7th November 1995 and 2nd and 24th July 2001; and take the advice of the Registrar and other clerks.

8th May 2002

Michael Davies
