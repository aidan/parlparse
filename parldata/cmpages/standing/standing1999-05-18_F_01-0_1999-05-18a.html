As I said on Second Reading, this is a historic moment. The Bill is central to the Government's commitment of achieving comprehensive and enforceable civil rights for all disabled people. It is intended to establish a commission, which will have a duty to work towards the elimination of discrimination against disabled people, and to promote the equalisation of opportunity, to encourage good practice and to work with others in the interests of disabled people.

The Bill is part of the Government's coherent and comprehensive agenda. For example, it will implement the remaining parts of the Disability Discrimination Act 1995, and reduce the threshold for small employers to bring another 75,000 people within the scope of the Bill. I chair a disability rights task force which is considering the whole range of issues that impact on the lives of disabled people, with a view to seeing what other measures can be taken to implement civil rights for disabled people. The new deal for disabled people, which is extremely innovative, will provide important civil rights to ensure that disabled people have the opportunity to work, and action is being taken by all Departments to ensure equal opportunity for disabled people.

The Bill addresses what was recognised by many to be the most fundamental flaw in the 1995 Act the lack of an enforcement body to help disabled people secure their rights and to provide a central source of advice to employers and service providers. I am sure that many members of the Committee, particularly my hon. Friends, will be proud to be part of the process of enacting the commission.

The Bill will create a strong, independent and effective voice to achieve civil rights for disabled people. I have no doubt that hon. Members will want to discuss its provisions in considerable detail. There will be a great deal of agreement about its objectives; on Second Reading, hon. Members on both sides of the house warmly welcomed its fundamental principles. However, I recognise that hon. Members will table amendments to explore it in detail. Mrs. May: I shall speak to amendments Nos. 10 to 12, and my hon. Friend the Member for Tiverton and Honiton will speak to amendments No. 18.

Amendments Nos. 10 and 11 are probing amendments. Schedule 1 specifies the commission's constitution. The amendments refer to the definitions in paragraphs 2 and 6 of the sort of person who may be appointed a commissioner or an office-holder such as a chairman or deputy chairman. The phrase used in the definitions is

    "disabled persons or persons who have had a disability."

In relation to the appointment of commissioners, the schedule refers specifically to the number of people in the commission who should be disabled or should have had a disability. Paragraph 2(2), it is phrased in the negative: a person who is not disabled or has not had a disability may be appointed to the commission only if half its members are disabled or have had a disability.

The amendments would remove the references to having had a disability. I describe them as probing amendments because I am trying to tease out exactly what sort of person the definition is intended to cover. What sort of person has had a disability but no longer does and would therefore be covered by that categorisation?

Genuine anxiety has been expressed in the House and by many disability organisations that, as the appointment of the first chairman will be a sign of the Government's seriousness or earnest of good intent and should make the commission's intentions clear, the person appointed chairman should be disabled. I understand why the Government have said that they do not feel it appropriate to include that requirement in the Bill, but the definition in the Bill of the chairmanship and deputy chairmanship provide for the second category of person someone who is not disabled but has had a disability to be appointed.

It is important that the Government make it clear exactly whom they have in mind. The requirement could be met by appointing someone who is not disabled but who, the Government feel, falls in the category of having had a disability. I have spent some time trying to envisage circumstances in which that description might be appropriate, and have raised the matter because I am worried that it would be technically possible under the definitions in the Bill to avoid the numerical requirements by appointing people who are not disabled. I should like a clear statement from the Government, about their intention and a description of the sort of person who would be described as having had a disability.

I can envisage circumstances in which someone had an industrial injury that led to a disability, but in which a subsequent change in his circumstances means that he falls within the category of having had a disability.

Such definitions would ensure that sufficient members of the commission were disabled people and could bring their experience of disability to bear on its operation. It would be inappropriate to appoint people with no experience of disability to take such decisions. I am trying to ensure that the Government make it clear that half of the commissioners and at least one of those holding the chairmanship or deputy chairmanship will be disabled people. That is why I am concerned about the alternative description in the Bill.

Amendment No. 12 also deals with the constitution of the commission. Under schedule 1, the tenure of office of those appointed by the Secretary of State can be as little as two years, but not more than five years. I am concerned about the constant changeover in members that would result if members were appointed for as little as two years. It is important to ensure continuity of experience in the commission, especially in its early days of operation.

Amendment No. 12 specifically deals with the chairman's term of office. It is an attempt to ensure a degree of independence for the chairman that might not otherwise exist, given that his or her minimum or maximum term of office is not specified in schedule 1. I suggest that a period of between four and five years would ensure that the chairman remained during any change in Government. Therefore, the chairman would not be subject to the political interference that could occur if his or her term of office was dependent on the Government of the day.

It is important that the chairman should be independent, especially as the commission's activities could bring it into conflict with the Government. The chairman should have the comfort of knowing that he or she has a set term of office, which will be longer than the lifetime of this Parliament, given that the commission will be created next year. A set term of office would mean that the Government could not remove the chairman because he or she did or said something that brought the commission into conflict with the Government.

As I have said, it is important to get the commission right because it sends important messages about the support that we are giving disabled people in creating the Disability Rights Commission. It is extremely important to ensure that the chairman is free from political interference. The Minister may say that that is the Government's intention. If so, I suggest that they accept amendment No. 12 and reinforce that intention in the Bill by ensuring that the chairman is appointed for a set term, in which he or she can say and do whatever is necessary in the interests of the commission and disabled people.
