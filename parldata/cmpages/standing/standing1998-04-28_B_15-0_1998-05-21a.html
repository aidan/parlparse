Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): We were dealing with the type of orders that can be made with a reparation order. We understand that, under clause 63(4), if a court wishes to make a reparation order, it cannot impose the sentences specified in subsection (4)(a) and (b). The court could combine a reparation order with two of the sentences, but only if it has made an action plan order or a supervision order in the first place. A reparation order could be combined as an element of them.

If the court wants to make a reparation order, it will not be able to pass a custodial sentence. That is sensible enough. However, it cannot make one of the non-custodial orders mentioned in subsection 4(b). A reparation order cannot be combined with a community service order or and a compensation order, and our main concerns relate to the latter.

I do not want to anticipate the Government's position, but I suspect that the Minister will argue that if a court takes the view that someone should do unpaid work as reparation, it would be superfluous to order community service as well. We do not feel as strongly about that as we do about compensation orders. However, it would not do any harm to make someone old enough to serve a community service order--that is someone over 16-to serve a reparation order to pay off his debt to the community as a whole and to the individuals involved. That would add an extra element of flexibility.

Compensation causes us the most concern. Reparation orders are being introduced without flexibility. Unless we have misunderstood the Government, we believe that it is not their intention to include a financial element in a reparation order so that a defendant is required to make payments to a victim. Will the Minister confirm that?

Under subsection (4)(b), it will be impossible for a court to make a compensation order alongside a reparation order. That is strange, especially because courts commonly make compensation orders and have been encouraged to do so by the House of Commons. In the ``No More Excuses'' White Paper, the Government stated:

    ``The reparation might involve writing a letter of apology, apologising to the victim in person, cleaning graffiti or repairing criminal damage.''

That is all well and good, but what if the victim of crime wants compensation as well?

We are concerned about cases in which someone's window is broken or car is damaged. Under the form of reparation to be introduced, it will be possible for the courts to order the offender to do some gardening, to tidy up or to write a letter of apology. However, the court will not be able to order that the victim is paid compensation. Many victims--especially less well-off victims who may not be covered by insurance--will want financial compensation above all else.

We are not alone in our concerns: magistrates are also alive to this apparent defect in the reparation order proposals. The Magistrates' Association

    ``would question the restriction against making a compensation order as well as a reparation order.''

We agree with that sentiment. The proposal is perverse and may deprive victims of what they want most of all. If victims are not aware, when asked to give consent to a reparation order, that it will not involve any financial compensation, they may well feel deprived.

I do not want to anticipate too much--I shall have another bite of the cherry--but the Minister may argue that if compensation is ordered as well as reparation, it will effectively punish the offender and compensate the victim twice over. If so, I am not convinced. If my car is damaged or my windows are broken, I will want financial compensation to put matters right, but I have suffered other losses as well as financial ones.

We all know how inconvenient it is to have to stay at home and wait for the window to be repaired or to take the car to a garage. There may be additional inconvenience or suffering between the commission of the offence and having matters settled. Victims should be compensated for those additional losses too, so we do not accept the argument that we would be compensating victims or punishing offenders twice over. It is possible for the courts to combine compensation orders with other orders and we see no reason why restoration should not be combined with compensation.

Perhaps we have it all wrong, and the Minister will spring to his feet to tell us that compensation orders can be combined with reparation orders or that if they cannot, reparation orders can include some element of compensation. We shall listen with interest to the Minister's response because that is what we believe fits the bill in this case. If the Government are not prepared to combine the two, reparation orders will be very strange animals.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Alun Michael): The hon. Gentleman has approached the problem constructively. The issue rests on the choice of regime and systems that are put in place. The hon. Gentleman took note of my earlier point that there is more than one way of introducing reparation into the offender's regime; it can sometimes be achieved through other sorts of order.

Although a reparation order cannot be made in conjunction with custody, that does not rule out some element of reparation being applied. A few weeks ago when I was meeting people who worked in prisons for Mediation UK, I spoke to the mother of a victim who was obviously devastated over the death of her son. Some time after the event, she had felt able to meet the offender and discuss what had happened with him. Without taking away all the hurt, it helped her to understand what had happened and to feel that the offender had done something to compensate. It did something to put events behind her and allow a healing process to start.

In that particular case, it would obviously be inappropriate to have a reparation order associated with a custodial order. We want to avoid such links, when two inappropriate disposals are combined rather than applying the idea of reparation, which we believe should underlie much of what happens in how sentences are applied.

As I said earlier, a reparation order cannot be combined with some community sentences--a community service order, a combination order, a supervision order with requirements, an action plan order or a compensation order--because reparation of one sort or another is already available as part of those orders.

The legislation that governs community service orders, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, is set out in the Powers of Criminal Courts Act 1973. It requires a person

    ``of or over sixteen years of age''

to ``perform unpaid work'' in accordance with the provisions of the Act. It is available only for offences punishable with imprisonment.

The ``National Standards for the Supervision of Offenders in the Community'', published in 1995--the standards are non-statutory, although that has not caused any problems in their application--explains that the main purpose of the community service order is

    ``to prevent further offending by re-integrating the offender into the community''.

However, it goes further than that, saying that it does so through two means, first

    ``punishment--by means of positive and demanding unpaid work, keeping to disciplined requirements''

and, secondly,

    ``reparation to the community--by undertaking socially useful work.''

The words ``reparation to the community'' are specifically used in that code of practice. We want to encourage and develop that approach.

The combination order combines elements of the probation and community service orders. It, too, requires the offender who is more than 16 years old to perform unpaid work for a number of specified hours. Encouragement should be given to the probation service and others involved in such orders to make work undertaken as reparation to the community to be relevant to the offence whenever possible. I have been involved in the supervision of people on community service, not, I hasten to add, as a probation officer--that was not my profession--but as a communiy worker accepting people under supervision. The engagements can be tremendously beneficial and such links are attempted.

The supervision order with requirements was introduced in the Children and Young Persons Act 1969, and was amended by the Criminal Justice Act 1988 to allow the additional requirements. Although none of the requirements refers specifically to reparation, ``National Standards for the Supervision of Offenders in the Community'' suggests, as one of the methods of providing supervision, that offenders aged 10 to 17 should participate in

    ``activities designed to make reparation to the community by undertaking socially useful tasks which, if possible, make good damage done by offending.''

Such tasks are therefore supposed to relate to the offending behaviour.

In many ways, it is pleasing to find that such principles are already set into some arrangements, although they can be made much more explicit. There will be greater public confidence in sentences if they are understood to involve that element of reparation.

As is the case for the supervision order, a reparation requirement is one of the options available to the court for the action plan order. As the hon. Gentleman said, the compensation order is rather different, because recompense under the order is financial, rather than in kind, as is the case under other orders. It is nevertheless inappropriate to combine a compensation order with a reparation order, as it smacks too much of punishing the offender twice for the same offence. Given that the reparation order is likely to be used for offences at the lower end of the seriousness scale, it would be wrong to approach it in that way.

In each case, the courts will decide whether financial reparation or reparation in kind is more suitable. I draw the hon. Gentleman's attention to the example given by my hon. Friend the Member for Northampton, North (Ms Keeble), especially as I was able to speak to those involved and examine how it worked. In such cases, although there is no statutory requirement, financial reparation can be part of the offender's response, guided by the supervisor, not because that is required or because there is a separate compensation order, but because it makes sense in the circumstances.

We propose those discrete periods for three good reasons. First, we want to make the sentence simpler and easier for the young person to comprehend. Secondly, we want to allow supervisors in the youth offending team and those who deal with the young person in custody to work with the young offender to produce a constructive sentence plan that is based on a fixed predetermined period. Having given great thought to the question of whether flexibility or determinate planned activity is appropriate, we have come down on the side of the latter.

Thirdly, we want to ensure that early release provisions in the detention and training order rarely mean that an offender who is given a longer sentence serves a shorter period in custody than one who is given a shorter sentence. Amendment No. 283 would unhelpfully limit the powers of the youth courts to impose detention and training orders up to the 24-month maximum.
