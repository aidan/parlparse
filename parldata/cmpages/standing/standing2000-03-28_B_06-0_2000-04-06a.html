Amendment No. 91 refers to coed cae or ffridd land in Wales. The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): Let me start by confirming the remarks made by my hon. Friend the Member for Doncaster, North (Mr. Hughes). So far, our proceedings have gone rather slowly, but they have been thorough and there has been a proper and conscientious probing of the Government's intentions. I make no complaint about that. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy for his generous comments about the clarity of my remarks on Tuesday. I hope to provide an equally clear explanation today. I entirely accept the spirit in which the amendments were tabled, and I agree on the need for the Government to clarify the meaning of cultivated land. That is exactly what I intend to do. The purpose of the cultivated land category is crucial to our response to the amendments.

When the countryside bodies assess whether land is mountain, moor, heath or down, we expect them to interpret the terms to ensure that where such areas include agricultural land, it is land that is used for rough grazing. I made that clear on Second Reading. The involvement of landowners and farmers in the mapping process is very important. There are two processes: first, the mapping has to occur and then decisions must be made about excepting the land. It is important to realise that there are two stages. The involvement of landowners in the mapping process is important, especially for the mapping of agricultural land. They will be able to take the opportunity to ensure that more intensively managed land, which could include hay and silage crops, is excluded from the draft and provisional maps at the initial stage.

When land is shown on conclusive access maps—the second stage—it will be excepted from the right of access if it is cultivated, as set out in schedule 1(1). But let me be absolutely clear: the cultivated land exception is not intended to be the mechanism for excluding improved or semi-improved pastures and fields from a right of access. Such land will not qualify as open country and should not appear on the statutory maps in the first place.

Research by the Countryside Agency shows that it would not be reasonable or practical to expect the public to identify whether land is semi-improved or improved pasture. The hon. Member for Mid-Sussex made that point, which is generally agreed. Anyone in the Committee, including people who have expertise in agricultural management and lawyers—one of my few virtues is that I am not a lawyer—would find it difficult reliably to identify pasture that has been improved or semi-improved. It would be unreasonable to expect walkers to make such judgments. That is why both sides of the Committee have called for greater clarity, and I agree with that aim. We intend that ``cultivated'' should be interpreted as meaning disturbance of the soil within the past 12 months by ploughing, harrowing, drilling or other such agricultural operations for the purpose of growing crops.

It is reasonable to expect the public to be able to tell whether land is cultivated in the way that I have described. That is how we meet the concerns about clarity. In practice, we expect that open country will seldom be turned under the plough, so the need to make such distinctions will rarely arise. I accept that there needs to be greater clarity, and I intend to table an amendment to that effect at a later stage.

The amendments would ensure that agricultural land that has been improved in some way, or which is used more intensively than rough grazing would allow, should be excepted from the right of access. I accept the spirit in which the amendments were moved, and the questions are perfectly proper, but I reiterate that it is important that such land should not be mapped as open country. If it were, we would look to landowners, farmers and the countryside bodies to review those maps and to consider whether the maps were drawn up correctly.

Amendment No. 30 seeks to except set-aside land. Set-aside takes arable land out of production. Clearly, set-aside land will not contain the features of semi-natural downland that the countryside bodies will be looking for, and I would not expect it to be mapped. Still less is it likely that it might be mistaken for heath, moor or mountain.

Amendment No. 31 would except land used for keeping horses. As a rule, land that it is intensively grazed by horses tends to deteriorate without careful management and such land is therefore unlikely to merit classification as open country. Of course, some traditional semi-natural downland may be grazed from time to time by horses, ponies or other livestock. Indeed, managed grazing can be essential to the maintenance of downland habitats. The failure to have managed grazing is often responsible for the deterioration of a number of SSSIs and other habitats. However, that in itself is no reason to exclude the land from public access.

If exclusions or restrictions are necessary to enable effective management of the land, owners and occupiers will be able to look to the relevant authorities for directions for that purpose. I hope that that will seldom be necessary. I am pleased to see that managed grazing regimes have lately returned to some of our most sensitive wildlife sites, including a number of fine downland reserves owned by the National Trust, where public access has been able to continue without interruption.

Amendment No. 31 would also exclude from public access vast areas of moor where the running of wild ponies is a traditional, if threatened, part of the local way of life. I hasten to add that it is threatened more by the low sale value of the ponies than by access. The public have had access to some such land for a very long time, whether by right or toleration, and generally without difficulty. Indeed, those problems that do arise are more likely to be caused by conflict with motor traffic or by the thoughtless feeding of the animals by visitors in the car parks than by walkers.

Amendment No. 32 would except land used for animal pens or enclosures. I assume that the amendment intends to exclude pens and other small or temporary enclosures, but it could also except all enclosed managed grazing. However, I want to deal with the amendment in the spirit in which it was moved. We believe that there may be some merit in specifically excepting the small pens used, for example, for the gathering of stock for the purposes of any temporary agricultural or veterinary operations on the stock.

I explained on Tuesday that our intention is that schedule 1 should not attempt to be all-inclusive. It lists only that land in open country on which activities occur that are incompatible with the right of access, such as golf courses, and land that is unlikely to be consistently excluded from maps of access land because of their relative size, such as some buildings. We accept the hon. Member's point that pens used for temporarily holding livestock may be situated in open country and that, theoretically, their use is inconsistent with a right of access. In practice, I do not feel that a sane walker would want to climb through a crush with a bull inside or a sheepfold at dipping time. However, we accept that the concern is real. In our view, amendment No. 32 is not in an acceptable form, but we are willing to take it away and consider it further with a view to responding on Report. I hope that that is satisfactory.

Amendment No. 52 excepts land covered by haystacks, silage or other stored agricultural products. We have to call for common sense to be exercised by those affected by the Bill. No doubt, when the Official Report is made available, it will record my speech as saying that this is a messy Bill. [Laughter.] What I mean is that the world, the countryside, the environment, is messy and it is difficult to establish a Bill that precisely fits an imprecise world. However, that is what we are trying to achieve. From time to time, access land will be used, legitimately, in such a way that the walker will find that there is some minor—and probably transient—interruption to the right of access. Perhaps, as the amendment envisages, hay or straw will be stored on the edge of the moor or equipment will be left in a field on the downs. No sensible person will see those objects as interfering with the public right, any more than any hon. Member will see a car parked at the side of the road as interfering with his or her right to use the highway in travelling from A to B. If farmers think that they must exclude the public for any reason, they will be able to exercise their discretion to close on up to 28 days a year or to apply for further closures and restrictions from the relevant authorities.

I explained in the context of the first group of amendments that we see no need for the exception of such broad categories of land. We do not expect intensively or semi-intensively managed grassland to be identified as mountain, moor, heath or down at the mapping stage. I am not sure what the hon. Members seek to achieve in the reference to the ``normal processes of husbandry'', but I am afraid that I do find that an imprecise expression. It is also unlikely that land that has been recently cultivated would be mapped as open country. If it were, the owner might well have grounds for objection to its inclusion on the map. I would not go so far as to say that cultivable land could not also be open country. The second world war demonstrated that almost any land can be put under the plough if the need is great enough. Indeed, many traditional pastures and ancient common grazing lands were lost to arable use at that time. For the same reason, I hope that even the Opposition will concede that amendment No. 56 goes too far and may be interpreted as embracing even the most hopelessly unproductive ground.

I reiterate that our interpretation of cultivated land is as a long stop. Farmers will seldom seek radically to change the use of their land to prevent access, as the cost of cultivating poor-quality soils will be high and the owner will not be eligible for agricultural support payments under the arable area payments scheme. They will also need to continue to cultivate the soil each year to ensure that the status of cultivated land continues to apply. Such cultivation would be a costly exercise if its purpose were solely to evade the right of access.
