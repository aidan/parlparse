Mr. Timms: At this early hour, we reach part XI of the Bill. It deals with the controllers of authorised persons. They exercise control over authorised firms by virtue of holding shares or voting rights in them or being one of their parent companies. I shall refer to those persons as shareholder controllers.

It is important that the Financial Services Authority is aware of the identity of authorised firms and has approved persons who can exert control over their management, as shareholder controllers might in reality call all the shots and can have considerable influence over whether a firm is fit and proper to carry out regulated activities.

Shareholder controllers might not be authorised persons, and the authorised firm cannot necessarily control who buys its shares or votes at its annual general meeting. The FSA cannot, therefore, achieve any hold over the identity of shareholder controllers by way of its rules. It is necessary to introduce statutory requirements that are backed up by criminal sanctions, and that is the purpose of part XI.

The provisions are based on European Community directive requirements, but we have applied those requirements more widely. Part XI applies to controllers of all authorised firms, not just to classes of firms that are covered by the single market directives. However, the approach is as set out in the directives. The provisions represent an extension, which is in the interests of effective regulation and consumer protection. All classes of firms will be handled in the same way.

We have taken advantage of the opportunity that the Bill offers to rationalise the existing patchwork of legislative requirements across the different sectors. That should make life easier for firms. This part of the Bill is a good example of the practical benefits of a single regulator and of our modernising the statute book.

This is another important part of the Bill and we have listened to responses to consultation and made sensible improvements to the original draft. For example, we have limited the requirements to shareholder controllers of firms that are incorporated in or formed under the laws of the United Kingdom. We have also put the control thresholds in the Bill.

The clause sets out the basic requirement to notify the FSA of any proposal that will result in an acquisition of new or increased control. It also caters for persons who acquire control without doing anything—for example, because the company restructures its capital. Such passive acquirers must submit notification within two weeks of becoming aware of the situation.

Amendments Nos. 560 and 561 clarify that the requirement to notify applies whether or not a person intends by his action to effect a notifiable acquisition of control. They were tabled in response to suggestions by consultation respondents that the current wording could be taken to apply only to those who purposely acquire control. That was not the intention, and we have clarified the wording to make that clear. Amendments Nos. 600 and 601 make similar changes to clause 159, which deals with the requirement to notify a decrease in control.

Opposition Members have tabled several amendments to this part of the Bill, including the clause. The majority of them are drafting amendments, designed to achieve greater clarity. Most of them would fall, given that the Government amendments improve clarity. Therefore, Opposition Members may wish not to press some—or all—of the amendments to a vote. However, of course I am happy to respond to points that they may wish to make in support of their amendments. However, if I understand correctly, the amendments are drafting amendments and would not change the policy intentions of the clause.

I shall say a few words about the two Opposition amendments. Clause 156(2)(b) stipulates one of the factors that the authority must take into account when considering whether a person should be approved to have control over an authorised person. In the case of paragraph (b), the issue is the threat that control might pose to people whom we may describe for these purposes as customers. I use that word in a neutral, non-specific way to make it easier to step back and consider who exactly is relevant for these purposes.

Amendment No. 514 would make the customers who are relevant to the authority's consideration the customers only of the authorised person himself rather than a wider class of customer. Clause 156(2)(b), as currently drafted, allows the authority to take into account the implications of the control on the customers of any authorised person. In reality, however, it is not that wide-ranging. In practice, the authority's ability to object to control is constrained to ground on which it is reasonable for it to take a particular view.
