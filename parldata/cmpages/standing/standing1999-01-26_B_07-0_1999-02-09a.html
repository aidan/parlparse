Mr. Jenkin: In your absence, Mr. O'Brien, a consensus has been developing in the Committee concerning the desire for openness, and to publish things, to make available the results of best value plans. The amendments relate to that process. We also wish to ensure that local authorities publish responses to the audit of best value plans. Amendment No. 168 would provide in clause 9(2) for authorities to prepare a statement in response to a report within 30 days rather than as soon as is practicable. I fear that if we allowed the phrase ``as soon as practicable'' to remain in the Bill, we would open up the possibility of an authority giving all sorts of reasonable excuses for not producing a statement in good time. For example, if an audit report proved embarrassing for an authority, it might be tempted to avoid the issue until, say, after an election. This applies also to publication of reports, to which we shall come later.

Amendment No. 167 would provide for the publishing of statements. The Bill does not yet provide for that. If reports are to be published, so should statements, so that the public are fully aware of how their authority is responding to criticism.

Amendment No. 169 would allow for the possibility of an authority having a view different from the auditor's about the nature of the report, and for the statement to say

    ``where appropriate, giving reasons for non-compliance with any guidance or specifications laid down by the Secretary of State under section 6'',

which may have been identified in the report. That is quite an important addition because it suggests that we want a dialogue between the Government and local government about the nature and value of a service as audited in the best value plan, not simply a one-way street of intstructions from the Government.

Amendments Nos. 170, 171 and 172 have been somewhat overtaken by events in view of relevant Government amendments. I am grateful to the Under-Secretary of State for Wales who earlier circulated information about the effect of Government amendments on the clause, to which I want to speak. Our amendments do not apply to the Government amendments but I hope that the Minister will reflect on their intent and consider incorporating their spirit later in our proceedings.

Amendment No. 170 would create a 30-day limit. The updated clause 9(1)—Government amendment No. 56—states:

    ``A best value authority shall publish any report received in accordance with section 7(5)(a)'',

and our amendment would ensure that that is ``within 30 days''. Let me provide an example to explain why I raise the issue.

In December 1997 the social services inspectorate produced a damning report about the standard of child care in the London borough of Ealing. Copies went to the chief executive, the council leader, the social services committee chair and others. However, the council refrained from publishing the report for five months until after the London borough elections of May 1998. When the report was subsequently released, the council said that it had been delayed on the recommendation of the social services inspectorate which had instructed that it should not be released so close to an election.

I question whether the social services inspectorate should be able to make such a recommendation. It is also questionable whether that was indeed the intention of the inspectorate. The matter of the internal circulation of the report was left by the inspectorate to the council. There was no stipulation by the opposition that it should have been withheld: it was left to the choice of council members and officers who received the report.

The delay was crucial in keeping out of the public domain during a critical election period a report that led the responsible Minister—a Labour Minister—to describe Labour-run Ealing as ``incompetent beyond belief''. It also led to calls from the local press over successive weeks for the resignation of the Labour chair of social services. Had it been known before the local elections of May 1998 it would obviously have been a major issue. The director took early retirement as a result of the report, which confirms how serious it was.

We thus want the Bill to stipulate that any salient reports should be in the public domain within a set period so that local authorities cannot abuse any of their discretions. That also applies, in amendment No. 171, to any proposed action that a local authority might take. The new provision, clause 9(3), states that an authority should ``as soon as practicable'' prepare a statement of any action that it proposes to take as a result of a report—and we believe that that should also be ``within 30 days''. It should not be left to councils to decide how practicable it is. A severely critical report requiring remedial action by the authority should be treated as a matter of urgency. If the 30-day stipulation were not met, the authority would be in breach of the Act and, if legal proceedings followed, it would have to explain why not. Surely it should be possible to produce something in 30 days to satisfy the legal requirement and clarify what an authority proposes to do about the problem.

Finally, amendment No. 172 would introduce new elements, which tackle an aspect of the Bill that mystifies me. The Bill provides for a local authority to construct a best value plan in the late months of a financial year in preparation for the next financial year. At the beginning of the next financial year, the best value plan is put into operation, audited and criticised. Yet the only requirement on the authority is to include any report, recommendation or statement in the next best value plan. Why is not the authority obliged to change the existing plan, where practical, in the light of any criticisms?

Amendment No. 172 would provide for a local authority to,

    ``(c) revise the performance plan accordingly where practical and appropriate, and

    (d) take appropriate action to implement the recommendations of the report.''

The report should contain recommendations that can be implemented. The current tendency in local authorities is to put everything in an apparently orderly way, which extends the process over an unnecessarily long period.

If best value is to work, it should lead to speedy remedial action. Our amendments aim to achieve that, and provide for maximum openness without delaying the publication of the report or the local authority's response.

I am worried that if those points are not reflected or included in the Bill, the best value process will be extremely long. We hope that best value works and that it improves the performance of local authorities. We all support that.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. Jon Owen Jones): I welcome you to the Committee, Mr. O'Brien, as I welcome many of the constructive points that the hon. Member for North Essex (Mr. Jenkin) made.

Preparation of a statement in response to audit is the first step that a best value authority must take to right any wrongs that the auditor's report highlights. By giving the authority a fixed period in which to prepare the statement, amendment No. 168 would ensure that the process was not dragged out and that the statement was prepared while there was still time in the relevant financial year for the authority to act on it. Amendment No. 169 is unnecessary. A key function of the audit of performance plans is to consider the extent to which the plan accords with the statutory requirements, including any guidance or specifications laid down by the Secretary of State or Assembly under clause 6. Where an auditor's report identified a failure to do so, an authority would, in preparing a statement of proposed action, be expected to deal with the reasons for non-compliance. In addition, identified shortcomings in performance would be taken up in best value reviews.

The Secretary of State or Assembly would take the statement into account in deciding what action should be taken. In reaching that decision, factors that the Secretary of State or Assembly could take into consideration would include an authority's failure to address the reasons for non-compliance with any guidance or specification laid down by the Secretary of State or Assembly. Authorities will be aware of that and, in preparing a statement of proposed action, they should be conscious of the importance of ensuring that it is comprehensive. I hope that Opposition Members are reassured by that explanation and will not press amendment No. 169.

Amendment No. 171 would require the auditor's report to be incorporated in the authority's next performance plan. I am grateful to hon. Members for raising this subject. I agree that it is sensible to ensure that the auditor's report is published. As currently drafted, clause 9 does not require publication of the auditor's report, however Government amendment No. 56, which we discussed earlier, will place a requirement on best value authorities to publish any report received from the auditor relating to the authority's performance plans.

That differs slightly from the hon. Member's amendment in that the report will be published as a free-standing document. That is preferable to incorporating it in the next performance plan. It will allow the report to be published more quickly and will help to ensure that the performance plan itself remains a clear and concise document. I therefore trust that Opposition Members will be reassured and will withdraw the amendment.

Finally, amendment No. 172 raises important issues about how recommendations for improvement by the auditor are translated into practical action by the best value authority concerned. I am again grateful to hon. Members for introducing this into the debate. It is clear that one of the outcomes that we are seeking is that best value authorities should have the opportunity to rectify their shortcomings wherever possible and that they should do so swiftly, effectively and in a thoroughly transparent way. I understand that hon. Members are seeking to achieve that outcome, but believe that the Bill, as currently drafteed, will deliver the same result without introducing a new level of detail into the Bill.

The combined effect of Government amendments Nos. 56 and 57 to clause 9, which we will move in their appropriate place, will be to ensure that, wherever an auditor identifies deficiencies in a local performance plan, authorities will be required to act in two ways. They will need to prepare a statement of what they intend to do to address the recommendations raised and identify a timetable for doing so. This information will need to be incorporated in the next performance plan. Local people will therefore be well placed to assess how the authority is performing and how effectively it is tackling those problems.

Mr. Jenkin: I beg to move amendment No. 173, in page 8, line 31, at end insert—

    `(c) any representations made to him by interested parties.'

Clause 10 represents a change of gear, as it concerns best value inspections, which the Audit Commission carries out on what local authorities do. What flows from those inspections is most important, so we are getting to the meat of the Bill.

Amendment No. 173 concerns to whom the Audit Commission must have regard when carrying out inspections. Whistle-blowers play an important role, as we have learnt from various scandals in organisations such as the Mirror group. Under the Bill, the Audit Commission may carry out an inspection of its own volition, but only if it complies with clause 7(4)(e), which specifies what should be included in an audit of a best value plan or what might arise from such plan or guidance issued by the Secretary of State.

We think that an additional safety valve is needed, as—it has to be said—the Secretary of State may not be totally unimpeachable in everything. Individuals with sufficient evidence and knowledge should be able to make their own representations to the Audit Commission, and the Audit Commission should be able to make an independent judgment about whether to make a best value inspection. The powers in the 1998 Act relate to audits and we are duplicating those powers in respect of inspections. That is where the difference alluded to by the hon. Member for Taunton derives.

In the normal course of planning inspections, an inspector is likely to give several weeks' notice to the authority to ensure that the inspection is as constructive and useful as possible. However, I accept that there may be occasions—in cases of serious failure of performance, for example—when an urgent inspection is necessary. Indeed, the right hon. For urgent inspections, staff will usually need to make the necessary preparations: files may have to be identified and appropriate staff may need to be available.

There may be failures where an immediate response is required—for example, when someone is at risk or when benefit fraud is suspected. However, powers already exist to respond immediately. A social services benefit fraud inspector may take action without notice if necessary—likewise for the police when a criminal offence is suspected. As a general rule, when criminal activity is suspected, I would expect appropriate powers under relevant criminal law—rather than best value inspection powers—to be used. That is my response to the concerns raised by the hon. Member for North Essex. If his concerns are such as to involve criminal activities, other powers would need to be taken under different legislation. The auditor may not be the right person to deal with such activity.

The effect of amendment No. 174 would be to allow an inspector to give no minimum notice whatever when seeking access to premises, documents or information, or if he wants an explanation from any responsible person in an authority. He would be able to arrive unannounced on an unprepared authority. That would detract from the responsibility and accountability of the authority itself to put its own house in order. It would make it difficult for the inspectorate to build constructive relationships with authorities, and would in many cases lead to inefficiency for the inspector and unnecessary disruption for the authority.

In the light of my explanation of the Government's position, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw amendment No. 174.

Under amendment No. 95, best value authorities would be given advance notice of inspection, and we anticipate that the period of notice will usually, be more than seven days. The purpose of the clause is to give statutory weight to requests for information and assistance when the authority being inspected is not fully co-operating with the inspector.

We know from existing statutory inspectorates that most inspections take place in a spirit of co-operation. We therefore expect the clause to be used only as a last resort when that co-operation is not forthcoming.

Alternatively, where there is cause for concern, where, for example, evidence of serious fraud has come to light, there may be a need for an urgent, ad hoc inspection. In such circumstances, three days' notice is adequate for an authority to arrange access to a building, a document or person for an inspector.
