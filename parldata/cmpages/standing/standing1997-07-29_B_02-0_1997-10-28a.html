Friend the Member for Chingford and Woodford Green (Mr. Duncan Smith) said on Second Reading, it should be better known as the ``Peter Lilley memorial Bill''. The Bill will, however, make changes that cause the Opposition grave concern and we want to probe the Government further on their intentions.

As we said on the first sitting, many of the changes will be made not by the Bill but through regulations, which I suspect we will not see in draft form during the proceedings of the Committee. We are being asked to approve a pig in a poke because we will not know the small print of those regulations or the detail of how the Government will seek to use their powers.

Furthermore, as the Bill is being piloted by a new Government, who seem a little unsure of the detail of many issues involved in reforming the welfare state, it would have been appropriate for them to have conducted a consultation process, if not published a White Paper, so that the complicated and far-reaching provisions of the Bill could have been discussed further before rushing into legislation. I was interested to see that a significant number of Government amendments have already been tabled, which suggests that during the summer months the Government realised that the Bill was drafted in a hurry and they have had to table amendments to deal with points that they have missed or which have not properly been drafted. The Bill smacks of having been drafted in a hurry, which could imply that the Government are trying to rush it through Parliament, which in turns implies that it contains controversial clauses. As the Bill develops--I would not say unravels--I am sure that a number of interesting arguments will develop.

Clause 1 appears to be symbolic of many of the clauses: it is vague and imprecise and raises several important questions about accountability which need to be examined in detail. The full significance of accountability did not hit me until I heard an interesting exchange on the ``Today'' programme last week with the Secretary of State, who was trying to steal a march by soundbite. It was not a soundbite based on careful research, but one in which she sought through disingenuous means to hoodwink the British public about the new deal for lone parents. The Government had not paid full attention to the facts, and the interview and the preamble by my hon. Friend the Member for Chingford and Woodford Green showed just what risks they are prepared to take for a soundbite and some press coverage. The interview was a shambles and I was amazed by the fact that the Secretary of State was prepared to compound her pitiful performance on the ``Today'' programme by appearing on ``Newsnight'' later in the day. To err once is unfortunate; to err twice in the same day is downright unforgivable. That brings home to us the importance of holding the Government accountable for their actions. They will take any steps to produce a soundbite to persuade the British people of apparent policy successes that do not actually exist. That is why the clause, which seems so innocuous, is, in many ways, at the heart of part I of the Bill.

Clause 1 creates a single-status decision maker. The Opposition support the aim of streamlining the decision-making process and the proposal to introduce a single-status decision maker. By introducing those measures, the Bill abolishes the current distinction between the Secretary of State's decisions, which are largely administrative, and decisions that are taken at arm's length by independent adjudication officers, which are appealable to a tribunal. Several issues, which I want to explore further, are wrapped up in that arrangement.

Government agencies will take on the responsibility of adjudicating on disputes in which one of the parties will be a Government agency. In effect, therefore, the clause makes provision to extend greatly the power of agency chief executives. On one level, it could be argued that that represents a conflict of interest. How the new system deals with that argument and the new pressures that arise need to be monitored. That is the logic that underpins the amendment.

In the amendment, the Opposition ask that the transfer of functions should be subject to a report by the Secretary of State within 12 months of their transfer to detail how the new system operates. As Labour Members will appreciate and as the two Ministers will, I hope, accept, when the Government came to power on 1 May they made a great play of introducing a new wave of open government and of accountability to the people. If my memory serves me correctly, we are now the servants of the people. If we are accountable to the people, we must be truly accountable to them. I hope, therefore, that, in the new spirit of open government and accountability, Ministers will find the amendment attractive. I hope that they, too, will want to enhance the means of accountability governing the powers that they are taking in legislation and offering to the people.

Currently, the chief adjudication officer draws up guidance notes for the adjudicating officers under advisement from Department of Social Security solicitors. I believe that that function will be transferred to chief executives. The guidance notes will, however, need to be framed in exactly the same context as they are now and the chief executives will need to maintain total objectivity when they finalise the guidelines. If they are slightly distorted, that will be reflected in the outcome of some decisions.

Bringing responsibility for adjudication and guidelines in-house gives rise to the possibility of a conflict of interest. That possibility needs to be scrutinised to discover whether it might have an adverse effect on the decision-making process. As the system stands, there is a degree of independence between the two organisations involved. When the responsibilities of the adjudication service are absorbed by an agency, I would like to feel confident that none of the qualities that are associated with either organisation would be diluted. That means that the adjudication of disputes and subsequent decisions would still be taken objectively, free from the immediate political and managerial influences that may be a factor in taking a decision within an agency. In saying that, I am echoing the 1996 Green Paper ``Improving Decision Making and Appeals in Social Security'', which stated in paragraph 4.12:

    ``In devising a new system, the strength of the current system--for example, the freedom from Ministerial influence on a day-to-day basis--will need to be maintained . . . If customers are to trust the new arrangements they must be satisfied that there can be no question of personal bias or patronage.''

A concern was reflected in the response to the Green Paper of Roy Sainsbury who found:

    ``The importance of independence for officers responsible for making decisions and for public confidence was stressed repeatedly in the submissions.''

Most decisions about benefit entitlement are taken by independent adjudication officers under the direction of the chief adjudication officer. The role and function of the CAO also extends to monitoring standards, resulting in written formal annual reports. It is essential for that monitoring process to continue--hence our amendment today.

As a minimum requirement it is imperative that the Secretary of State delivers a detailed report on the success or otherwise of changes to the system as prescribed in the clause. With such a report we could gauge whether the transfer was implemented well and whether the new system was operating successfully. Clearly, Members on both sides of the Committee want the system to operate as successfully as possible in the best interests of those who are affected by the decisions. That is why there should also be an appeal to Ministers to ensure accountability.

I know that the Labour party--certainly the Government--find Parliament to be an irritant which should be brushed aside whenever possible. However, Parliament is crucial--despite the views of the Prime Minister. Parliament must have the opportunity--

This unprecedented change will, of course, be taken as a precedent for subsequent legislation in many other Departments. Since its murky origins, English law has established--perhaps pre-eminently among all legal systems--the extraordinary importance of the concept of responsibility and the relationship between the concept of responsibility and the concept of decision. If one goes back to Maitland's ``Township and Borough'', for example, one sees the early strivings of the English legal system to identify and locate responsibility in the case of a corporate body. It is one of the proud achievements of English law to have found ways of dealing with decision and responsibility in such a way as always to identify the one with the other. Where a decision is made, there the responsibility lies. The fundamental concept of our criminal law is perhaps the idea of a guilty mind, which is a fundamental concept of decision making and responsibility. Where an action is not happenstance, but is taken by decision, there criminal responsibility may lie.

Clause 2, which is apparently innocuous in many ways, begins, entirely unconsciously--I cannot imagine that its drafters ever imagined that it would have such consequences--to drive a wedge between the concepts of decision and responsibility. Of course, a computer can, in an extended sense, make a decision. That is to say, a decision falls to be made and a computer can do things that will constitute the making of that decision. A computer cannot, however, be held responsible in any ordinary or important philosophical or legal sense. It cannot be imprisoned, sued or treated as the party that is responsible for the decision.

In clause 2, which is seemingly innocuous, we have the idea of an entity that is not responsible for making a decision. That divorce of responsibility from decision will ricochet through English law in ways that are entirely unforeseen and are probably regarded as unlikely to occur by those who are responsible for the clause. Surely, before introducing a change with such significant impacts across time, it would be appropriate for a body such as the Law Commission or a royal commission to investigate the question of decision and responsibility in relation to computers.

Finally, the clause is a harbinger of many other such clauses that are much wider in their implications--and perhaps even in their drafting--and which are likely to come about because of the changing nature of computers. With networking and artificial intelligence, the question of computers and their capacity to decide and their relationship to decisions and, indeed, to human beings is becoming one of the great issues of our age. To venture unwarily into that territory with an ill drafted and ill conceived attempt to solve a minor administrative problem, thereby creating a huge precedent that will be built upon in all sorts of unforeseen ways, without due consideration by the Law Commission or a royal commission, and without any serious investigations of the issues at stake, strikes Conservative Members as ill judged.
