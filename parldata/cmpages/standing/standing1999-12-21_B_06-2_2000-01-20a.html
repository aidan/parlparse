Mr. Lock: Before the break, I was explaining the difference between information to which there is a right on individual application under clause 1 and information that is required to be published in a publication scheme under clause 17. The core of the Government's objection to the amendment is that much of the information will not be of sufficient general interest to justify inclusion in a publication scheme for every public authority. I accept that there may, and almost certainly will, be some authorities for which the sorts of information listed in the amendment are appropriate for inclusion, but that does not mean that it is appropriate to require every public authority to include every item in the scheme.

Ultimately, it is a matter of judgment. The right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross suggested that the Government were retreating from freedom of information. With respect, he is wrong for two reasons. First, it is not the case that this information is not freely available: it is a question only of under which section it will become available. Its not being in a publication scheme will not remove it from the obligation under clause 1. Secondly, we have resisted the amendment to water down the terms of clause 17(1)(a)—to make the publication scheme broadly in accordance with the requirements of the Information Commissioner—and specifically required that the commissioner ``shall approve'' the publication scheme. The judgment about what is appropriate in an individual publication scheme is a matter not for the Government but for the Information Commissioner.

Clause 17 includes provisions that will require a public authority to have regard to the public interest in determining what to include in the scheme. The commissioner will take that interest into account in deciding whether to approve the scheme. I am sure that the commissioner will expect to see many of the items that are specified in the amendment referred to in a scheme for many, but not necessarily all, public authorities. As I said, the public authority and the commissioner need flexibility to determine the right balance between including information in the publication scheme in order to promote openness, and making schemes so detailed as to place barriers in the way of their effective use by the public.

It is a question not of whether information should be available under clause 1, but of getting the right publication scheme for different public authorities under clause 17. On that basis, it would not be right to include in a mandatory publication scheme the vast quantity of information referred to in the amendment. I hope that the right hon. Gentleman will accept that it is ultimately a matter of balance between the Information Commissioner and the public authority.

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 124, in page 11, line 25, at end add—

    `(4) A public authority shall take all reasonable steps to inform an applicant of where the information can be obtained if it declines a request for information by virtue of this section.

Clause 19 is important. It is relevant to what was said earlier by the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central, as it is another example of language coming down in favour of public authorities and against individuals. All information is exempt if it is

    ``reasonably accessible to the members of the public even though it is accessible only on payment'';

and it is regarded as accessible

    ``if it is information which the public authority or any other person is obliged by or under any enactment to make available to the public, whether making it available for inspection or otherwise and whether free of charge or on payment''.

Information is exempt if it can be acquired in some other way. That does not, however, place any obligation on the public authority to take any steps to inform an applicant how he can acquire the information.

It would be no bad thing if the Bill made it clear that if public authorities are to take advantage of the absolute exemption, they should have a clear duty and obligation at least to provide the applicant with details of how and where he could obtain that information. There should also be some restriction on the cost of determining the reasonableness of steps taken to give such advice. That cost is sometimes greater than expected.

There have already been difficulties in that respect. Although technically the information is supposed to be available by other means—I realise that the phrase ``reasonably accessible to members of the public'' ought to cover the point—it frequently turns out not to be accessible and some of the entitlements for obtaining it by other means are difficult to enforce. The Parliamentary Secretary may wish to take away this important point and give it thought. I have suggested that it could be covered by the code of practice. I feel strongly that if a public authority is to take advantage of an absolute exemption, there should be an obligation to tell an applicant how he can obtain information, even though we must assume that the public authority could have provided it—which is another key point.
