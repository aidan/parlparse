Mr. Lepper: Paragraph 2 of the schedule repeals section 29 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, under which nature conservation orders can be made. English Nature and the Countryside Council for Wales may ask the Secretary of State to make the orders in respect of third party activities on an SSSI that are damaging that site or when an owner or occupier refuses to enter into a management agreement. A nature conservation order can prohibit any activities occurring on an SSSI with immediate effect, thus preventing any further damage. Anyone who ignores such an order may be prosecuted and, if convicted, may be subject to a fine and ordered to restore the damage caused.

According to English Nature's annual report, 19 nature conservation orders, protecting 1,645 hectares, were in place in 1998-99. Earlier this year, two further orders were made to stop motorcyclists damaging two heathland SSSIs. As I understand it, clause 2 ensures that it will not be possible to make new nature conservation orders, so it is unclear what will happen to areas that are currently protected by such orders. The fate of special nature conservation orders made under regulation 22 of the Conservation (Natural Habitats) Regulations 1994 is also unclear in the Bill.

An important feature of conservation orders is that they apply to an activity in general. In other words, an activity will be an offence if can be shown to breach the order rather than if it can shown to be damaging in a particular instance. Another advantage of the orders is that they can apply beyond the boundaries of an SSSI. Damage to an SSSI is often caused by activities that occur outside it. English Nature and the Countryside Council for Wales can try to reach an agreement about management of activities adjacent to SSSIs under section 15 of the Countryside Act 1968, but without nature conservation orders nothing can be done if such an agreement cannot be reached.

The amendments do not merely propose retention of nature conservation orders as a last line of defence against damaging activities. They also build on the current arrangements and expand the scope with which orders can be made to include the protection of species listed in the following schedules to the Wildlife and Countryside Act: schedule 1, which deals with birds; schedule 5, which deals with animals; and schedule 8, which deals with plants. The 1981 Act ensures that those plants and animals receive the highest level of protection. The listed species often occur outside SSSIs, but although they are protected, the habitats on which they depend are not.

For example, adjacent to the Thames basin heaths SSSI are significant areas of conifer plantations that in themselves do not currently qualify for SSSI status. However, areas of the plantations are of national—and, in some cases, international—importance for schedule 1 bird species such as the woodlark. The amendment would allow English Nature and the Countryside Council for Wales to apply for an order to prevent forestry activities that would damage the non-SSSI habitat of that bird. It would also extend the scope of the orders, giving power to include the protection of priority species and habitats listed in the UK biodiversity action plan, which was the Government's response to the convention on biological diversity.

Action plans have been developed for all the priority habitats and species listed. The ability to make an order to prevent damage or destruction of priority habitats and species when all efforts to negotiate a solution have failed would contribute significantly to the implementation of the plans. The amendments propose that section 30 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act—the compensation provisions—should be retained in order to allow aggrieved third parties and non-SSSI landowners to claim compensation where it is appropriate to do so. The procedure for making nature conservation orders laid out in schedule 11 should also be retained.

I hope that my right hon. Friend the Minister for the Environment and my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary will accept the amendments.

Mr. Meacher: We support the intention behind the amendments, which is to increase the formal protection for threatened species in the wider countryside beyond designated sites. Having indicated my support for the amendment's general purpose, the issue is whether it provides the most effective way of achieving that purpose. I shall argue that it does not, but I hope that I can carry the hon. Gentleman with me—as I remember another Government spokesman said about Sir Cyril Smith.

The species listed in schedules 1, 5 and 8 of the 1981 Act and the priority species currently identified under the biodiversity action plan are those that are most at risk of population decline and disappearance and are priorities for action under the convention on biological diversity. However, we consider that although action in the wider countryside is necessary to conserve those species, the best route is through the current biodiversity action planning process, which prescribes, case by case, the specific action needed for each species.

The Government do not consider that the Nature Conservancy Council's powers, as improved by the Bill's main provisions, are inadequate to enable it to play its part in achieving the targets established in the plans. Those powers, together with the powers of other agencies involved in delivering the action plans—such as the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food, through the expanded agri-environment programme, and the Forestry Commission, through its woodland grant scheme—and the further delivery mechanisms developed by way of local biodiversity action plans, represent a significant armoury.

Many of the difficulties that affect the conservation of priority species outside designated sites relate to the fact that they are wide ranging or thinly dispersed. The amendments would not directly address the needs of those species, as they could be applied only to clearly defined areas, which, where appropriate, should be candidates for consideration as SSSIs.

The SSSI protection measures in the Bill are specifically designed to relate to designated sites and are complemented by survey, notification and consultation procedures. The Government would be unhappy about extending the provisions proposed in the amendments to the wider countryside. That would give other land similar status to that of SSSIs, which are of national importance, without the complementary provisions that properly safeguard the rights of owners and occupiers
