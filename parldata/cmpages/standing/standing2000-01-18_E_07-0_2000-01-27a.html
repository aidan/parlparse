Mr. Mullin: As the hon. Gentleman said, we have reached the new subject of the modification of licences. If I give one of the little tours of the horizon that my fellow Under-Secretary is so keen on, it might help the debate and answer a few questions in advance.

I should remind hon. Members that NATS will, at first, be the only holder of a licence under clause 5 to provide air traffic services in the UK and in the Shanwick oceanic area. The licence to be granted initially by the Secretary of State will consist of two elements. The first is the terms on which the licence is granted—essentially, its duration, the licensed area in respect of which services are authorised to be provided, and the terms on which it can be revoked. The second is the conditions—the corresponding obligations with which the licence holder must be compliant if it is to continue to provide the services. Compliance with the conditions is enforceable by the CAA under the relevant provisions in clauses 21 to 25.

The elementary features of the licensing regime are broadly in line with the regimes established—here we go again with precedents—for other regulated industries under, for example, sections 12 to 15 of the Telecommunications Act 1984, sections 11 to 14 of the Electricity Act 1989 and sections 12 to 16 of the Railways Act 1993.

The fundamental rationale for the licence modification process is as follows. By their nature, licence conditions are detailed and prescriptive, laying down requirements for the actions or behaviour of the licence holder. For several reasons, they are intrinsically dynamic, providing for matters to be reviewed or replace—price control is put in place every five years, for example—or for them to be supplemented because of changes in circumstances or technology. There must, therefore, be a modification process, with appropriate checks and balances, by which the detailed licence conditions may be kept current. It is appropriate for the process to be primarily the responsibility of the regulator, with Ministers and the Competition Commission playing a higher-level backstop role. Each judgment calls into play the general duties of the regulator or of the Secretary of State.

The process places a framework around what is usually a negotiation between the licence holder and the regulator. There will always be discussions between them on the way in which actions or events are to be viewed under the licence, and an accommodation will usually be reached. However, agreement might sometimes not be reached. One or both of the parties might believe the matter to be sufficiently important to warrant being put beyond doubt by a licence modification.

The licensee may propose licence modifications, although the regulator must make the formal proposition and licence modification. More normally, the regulator will propose them following discussion with the licensee and others involved, such as someone who has complained about the adequacy of the licence control or the licence holder's compliance with it. In the event of disagreement between the licence holder and regulator, with the latter thinking that the public interest is being adversely affected, the matter may be referred to the Competition Commission for investigation and report. The regulator must modify the licence to deal with any adverse effects found. Licences may also be modified following public interest Judgments by the commission as part of a monopoly or merger case referred to it. Clauses 10 to 14 government the process.

Amendment No. 157 would require a further criterion to be met before the CAA could modify licence conditions with the consent of the licence holder. Licence conditions are detailed and prescriptive, laying down requirements for the actions or behaviour of the licence holder. They are intrinsically dynamic for the reasons that I have given.

The CAA may already, at any time, propose a licence modification, just as the licence holder may at any time, request a licence modification under the Bill. That means that the licence holder and the regulator can develop an appropriate method of keeping the licence relevant to the changing environment in which the licence is operated. A condition need not necessarily have ceased to be appropriate for the regulator and the licence holder to agree on a modification so as better to meet the duty obligations imposed on them.

The Secretary of State will retain a power of veto over any proposed modification with which the CAA is bound to comply, thereby safeguarding against regulatory capture, which I think means the outside chance that the regulator will go native. That has not happened in the case of any regulators appointed so far, I am glad to say. In the circumstances, the amendment is unnecessary, and I therefore invite the hon. Member for North Essex (Mr. Jenkin) to ask leave to withdraw it.
