Clause 25

Charges in respect of passengers without proper documents

Amendment proposed [22 April]: No. 211, in page 1, to leave out lines 20 and 21 and insert the words--

    "(5) for the purposes of this section--

    (a) a document is what it purports to be unless its falsity is reasonably apparent, and

    (b) in deciding whether the falsity of a document is reasonably apparent it shall be assumed that the person making the appropriate judgment is not an immigration officer."

--(Mr. Clappison.)

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are considering Government amendment No. 476.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): Carriers are encouraged by the immigration service to perform simple but careful checks of a passenger's documentation. To help them avoid charges, the immigration service provides training and awareness in forgery detection and fraudulent use of passports, when requested by carriers. In addition, carriers have access to the 24-hour helpline and the published passport and visa guide.

Most carriers accept that by working closely with the immigration service and by taking advantage of free training they can achieve the combined objectives of minimising their potential liability for charges and reducing the number of inadequately documented passengers travelling here. The training is conducted by immigration officers. It enables the carriers to understand the UK's documentary requirement for travel here, to acquire basic forgery detection skills and to identify inadequately documented passengers at source.

The training includes free published guidance on passports and visas and other general administration of our carriers liability legislation. It includes regularly updated visa information cards showing who needs a visa to come here; videos made in conjunction with the International Air Transport Association on the subject of inadequately documented passengers, a 24-hour helpline and the ability, as appropriate, to make reference to the nearest British mission. There are at present 10 locations where carriers can refer to airline liaison officers.

The immigration service does not expect carriers' check-in staff to demonstrate the same detection skills as immigration officers. The test applied is clearly set out in published guidance which explains that:

    "The Immigration Service would consider a falsity as reasonably apparent, 'if it were of a standard which a trained representative of the carrying company, examining it carefully but briefly and without the use of technological aids, could reasonably be expected to detect.' A 'trained representative' could be expected to have a level of basic knowledge of how to identify false documents but not to be expert nor to have the resources for a highly detailed examination."

It is open to carriers to make representations to the immigration service if they believe that a forgery is not reasonably apparent. The incidence of forged or fraudulently used documents giving rise to charges is relatively low. For example, in 1998 only 3,136 of the 22,874 inadequately documented passengers who arrived in the United Kingdom held forged documents. Charges were pursued in only 809 cases and 138 were subsequently waived following representations.

The immigration service will continue to work closely with carriers to raise their level of awareness and to help them avoid charges. Many carriers, particularly the larger ones, generally undertake their own in-house training. As I have mentioned, we have never expected carrier staff to have the forgery detection skills of immigration officers, so the amendment is unnecessary. I invite the Committee to reject it.

On the Government amendment, the essence of clause 25 is that carriers must ensure that the documentation produced satisfactorily establishes a person's identity and nationality. Subsection (5) deals with the falsity of documentation produced. It does not, however, deal specifically with the situation where genuine documentation is presented by an imposter. Amendment No. 476 seeks to clarify clause 25 and make clear the carrier's responsibility for ensuring that a document relates to the person presenting it. Although establishing such responsibility, the amendment also provides the defence that a charge will not be pursued unless it is reasonably apparent that the document, although genuine, related to another person.

On that basis, I hope that the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) will withdraw his amendment, which is unnecessary, and that the Committee will support the Government amendment.
