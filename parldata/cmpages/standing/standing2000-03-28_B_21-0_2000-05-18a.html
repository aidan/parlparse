By coincidence, the point of order raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Sussex (Mr. Soames) has a tangential link to amendment No. 637. Under the schedule, the new section 28I(2) of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 would provide that English Nature or the Countryside Council for Wales

    may not serve a management notice unless they are satisfied that they are unable to conclude, on terms appearing to them to be reasonable, an agreement with the owner or occupier as to the management of the land.

That phrase

    appearing to them to be reasonable

gives me and many observers cause for concern at the possibility that English Nature or the Countryside Council for Wales might be tempted to offer agreements with derisory levels of payment, on the basis that those seemed reasonable to them at the time. The most obvious occasion for that might be that one of the organisations had run out of funds. We know that various figures have been bandied about with respect to costs, but there is widespread concern about whether the bodies will have enough money to achieve all that is required of them. The scenario that I have outlined is realistic, so we have tabled amendment No. 637, which would provide for arbitration at the request of an owner or occupier who did not think that the terms offered were reasonable.

Why has the phrase been included in the provision, if not to save money? According to our research, such wording is unprecedented in legislation covering management agreements. It does not appear in section 15 of the Countryside Act 1968, which is the basis of all SSSI management agreements in the past 32 years; in the National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949; in the Environmental Protection Act 1995, which contains the enabling powers for agri-environment scheme agreements; or in the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, which contains the enabling powers for local authorities to enter into agreements with owners for conservation purposes.

The absence of such wording in any other legislation causes me to wonder why the Minister has added the phrase to the Bill. It is unprecedented to insert a condition that management agreements must be made on terms that the offering body considers reasonable. It will create a suspicion that English Nature or the Countryside Council for Wales are trying to get away with conservation on the cheap. The suspicion may be unjustified, but if we are looking for genuine consensus and genuine agreement, we do not want to cause such suspicions.

As it stands, the clause will undermine the trust and positive partnership we want to foster between the agencies and owners and occupiers of SSSIs. We propose arbitration. Section 50 of the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 provides for arbitration over management agreements at the request of the person being offered the agreement. It is essential that that provision should apply in the case of any management agreements under the Bill. Owners and occupiers can expect to be fairly rewarded for the work they carry out on behalf of the public in maintaining SSSIs. We should ensure that trust exists between the agencies and the owners and occupiers.

I refer to the point of order from my hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Sussex. If we are honest, there are unreasonable people in every walk of life and there will be unreasonable owners and occupiers, but there may also be unreasonable officials. If the question of what is reasonable is left to the judgment of an official alone, we will only be creating difficulties for ourselves. I hope that the Minister will accept our intention, which is to remove a potential thorn in the Bill and to ensure that agreements are genuine rather than imposed.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Chris Mullin): Amendment No. 637 would add a requirement for arbitration on a management agreement made under section 50 of the 1981 Act to occur before the conservation agency can serve a management notice.

As the hon. Member for Mid-Sussex said, we need to look at the process that might move towards a management notice with some sense of balance. We have tried throughout the Bill to strike such a sense of balance. Indeed, we have been accused even by some Labour Members of going too far in that respect. For example, we have been criticised for allowing a right of appeal to landowners when no such right exists for walkers. We defended that point earlier and I am not resiling from it. I am simply saying that we have gone out of our way—I offered the example by way of illustration—to exercise responsibility and to recognise that landowners are losing a property right.

Management notices will be used only in exceptional circumstances. The conservation agency will want to protect the special interest and, at the same time, ensure that the owner or occupier is treated equitably. The Bill already provides that the agency will have to be satisfied that it is unable to conclude a management agreement on reasonable terms. Section 17 of the National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949 is the precedent for that provision.

During the debate on chapter III, my right hon. Friend the Minister referred to section 17 of the 1949 Act as having appeared in the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. It is, of course, an amendment to the 1981 Act as introduced by the Bill. Under both the 1981 Act, as it will be amended, and part I of the Bill, if the owner or occupier objects to the notice, he or she has an opportunity to appeal to the Secretary of State against the terms of the notice. That is a key point. There are no immediate consequences of a failure to agree. Opposition Members may have overlooked that point. The matter is complicated and I do not blame them for missing it.

The authority must serve an order and the landowner can appeal against it to the Secretary of State. We will issue appeal regulations to govern those proceedings. With regard to terms, it will be in the interest of both parties to conclude a reasonable agreement. The conservation agencies are encouraging the use of informal dispute resolution procedures. If concerns about the financial aspects of an agreement cannot be resolved, there is a statutory right under section 53 of the 1981 Act to seek arbitration. That issue is separate from the management notice, the works involved and any appeal against the requirements of the notice.

By adding a further obligation to conclude arbitration under section 50 of the 1981 Act before a management notice may be served, there is a risk of delay allowing further deterioration of the special interest. The balance would be tipped against its protection and potential restoration. That is why we are concerned not to introduce unnecessary delay.

The amendment deals with the thorny issue of compulsory purchase. The Bill provides for powers of compulsory purchase, but they extend only to areas that form part of an SSSI. In many cases, compulsory purchase orders are not a satisfactory solution.
