People will now have a choice between a salary-related occupational scheme, of which we all know the merits, and one of the Government's state second pensions, the details of which are set out in this paper. For those on £8,000 or £9,000 a year it will be a difficult choice. They will have the opportunity to join an occupational scheme. Should they instead opt for the Government's state second pension? The right answer may depend on the person's age, on whether he expects to earn £9,000 or less for long, and on whether he expects promotion, in which case he will almost certainly want to join an occupational scheme. People on around £9,000 will have to address that issue, and they cannot be expected to pay for the advice that they will need.

Those in money purchase occupational schemes will have to make an equivalent decision whether to join the state second pension and at what level of earnings. That will again be a function of many factors, including the level of employer contributions. The calculations are complicated, and will have to take account of the person's age and prospects, what he can negotiate in the market, and so forth.

The Bill then raises the fundamental issue of whether people will be drawn out of an existing occupational scheme into one of the new stakeholder pensions. They may be inclined with the Government's blessing under the incentive structure, to take advantage of that alibi to push employees out of occupational pension schemes and into the new stakeholder scheme, which is a less attractive option for employees. Many people in the industry share the concern that the stakeholder will be either an instrument or an alibi to induce employees to leave an occupational pension scheme and join a stakeholder, or for employers to wind up an occupational pension scheme for new employees and replace it with a less good stakeholder pension.

Individuals will have to decide where they stand and how their rights can be best protected. The extremely able people in those professions are not the poorest-paid members of society nor should they be, given their expertise and the important decisions that they take.
