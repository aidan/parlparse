Amendments Nos. 52 and 53 deal with technological problems. They are important because a variety of models could be taken forward. There are historical examples of things going wrong. The United States Government had proposals for a clipper chip. They wanted to stick a chip in all equipment in order to acquire an intercept capability. By the time the equipment reached developmental stage, the industry had moved on and it would have been impossible to implement the original proposals. Time scales are an important issue. Proposals must be framed for an appropriate time scale. By the time these provisions come into force, we may be living in a different technological world. As I said earlier, if we do not get the technology right, people will flee the straightforward providers to new providers, which develop precisely because they offer a way round current problems.

The hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire (Mr. Heald) asked important questions about the quantity of connections that may be intercepted. Let me use an analogy from another packet switch network, the postal system, which switches packets between customers via postal boxes to an end point. Within that network, capability to intercept everything simultaneously is not sought. Interception means setting up a particular address point and intercepting only that. It requires manual intervention. It is a specifically targeted intercept, not a general one. Not every item of mail is opened for the interceptors to find what they are looking for. Only a particular address point is investigated. That is an appropriate model for a packet switch network.

If people realised that only a software bug on a particular address point was being set up, they would have enough confidence to continue using the main providers. An address point needs to be identified and a bug placed on the line in order to start intercepting. That is preferable to the black box solution of intercepting everything that goes through. We talked about the Echelon model when we debated external communications, but it would reduce confidence.

Amendments Nos. 52 and 53 are important. We should not introduce the provisions until we have seen which model the Government intend to follow in their orders. If it emerges that they want a general intercept capability, the industry will scream loudly and will not want to proceed with the provisions. It is in everyone's interest to ensure the requisite technical competence, which can be achieved through targeted methods based on particular addresses rather than through general interceptions, which undermine confidence in the system.

That classic statement was made by Lord Green, the then Master of the Rolls, in the case of Associated Provincial Picture Houses Ltd. v. Wednesbury Corporation in 1948.

The grounds on which the courts might review the exercise of an administrative discretion would be an abuse of power, bad faith, a mistake in construing the limits of the power, procedural irregularity, or unreasonableness in the Wednesbury sense-that is, unreasonableness verging on absurdity. There are cases which do not reach that high standard of absurdity, but which are none the less wrong. The amendment would address the circumstances in which a decision behind the serving of a notice was a bad one, or in which the technical steps were dubious or disproportionate, or in which a simpler technical solution would be possible and effective. Avenues of appeal seem to exist in most circumstances in which technical requirements are placed on an individual by the Government or an authority. The Building Act 1984, for example, contains the provision for appeal against notices relating to alterations of building work, to planning notices, to listed building consents, to alterations to licensed premises, and against notices dealing with the detailed technical aspects of sports ground safety, and work to water courses.

Acts of Parliament such as the Agriculture (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1954 and the Consumer Credit Act 1974, which dealt with the Office of Fair Trading requirements, contain a great many technical details and might involve large amounts of money and big issues for companies.
