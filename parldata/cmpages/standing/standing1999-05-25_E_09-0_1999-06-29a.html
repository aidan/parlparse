The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Paul Boateng): On a point of order, Mr. Stevenson. I should like to clarify something that I said in Committee last Thursday. My hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston (Ms Hughes) had asked whether children could be cross-examined about their previous sexual behaviour. In column 224, I explained that children under 16 are not deemed to be capable of giving consent to sexual intercourse. Their previous sexual behaviour--if any--cannot therefore be used to support a defence of consent by a defendant.

At that time, I should have referred to the Harling case and to the dictum of Mr. Justice Humphreys, which makes an exception in the case of rape. As the Committee well knows, consent, or the lack of it, is one of the key elements in the offence of rape. In every case, the prosecution must prove that the complainant--whether a child, a man or a woman--did not consent and that the crime was committed against his or her will.

Where the child is young, the issue of consent is not likely to be a very live one for the jury; it is probable that the prosecution will not need to do much more than prove the child's age to prove the lack of consent. However, the nearer a child gets to 16, the more likely it is that consent will be a live issue. As we know, children below the age of 16 engage in sexual activity and on a consensual basis. Therefore, it will continue to be possible, under the Bill, for children to be cross-examined about their sexual behaviour pursuant to a defence of consent in rape cases.

Applications to admit such evidence will, however, have to follow the procedures laid down in clause 42 and meet the criteria set in clause 40 of the Bill. We have intentionally created high hurdles, and they will have to be crossed. They will ensure that children are cross-examined about their sexual behaviour only in rape cases and when that is strictly relevant to the issues at trial.

Clause 43

Restrictions on reporting alleged offences involving persons under 18

Mr. Boateng: I beg to move amendment No 74, in page 31, line 19, leave out

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments:

Government amendment No. 75.

Amendment No. 83, in page 31, line 27, after `offence', insert

    `or (ab) do not apply unless a court having or most likely to have jurisdiction in relation to proceedings arising from the alleged offence is satisfied on an application made for that purpose by or on behalf of a person referred to in subsection (2) who is not a person by whom the offence is alleged to have been committed and having regard to the particular circumstances of the case that the interests of justice, the interests of the person who is a minor and the public interest in relation to freedom of expression are better served by the application of subsection (2) than by not applying it to the particular case.'.

Government amendments Nos. 76 to 79, 84, 85, 94 and 40.

Mr. Boateng: Amendments Nos. 74, 77 and 78 remove references to a ``minor'' in clauses 43 and 44. We think that these references are not necessary, as the clauses already contain references to a person under the age of 18.

Amendment No. 40 is consequential to the agreement in the other place to move the point at which the reporting restrictions imposed by clause 43 apply, from the point of allegation to the point of commencement of a criminal investigation.

Clause 43 imposes restrictions on publishing the identity of young people who allegedly are offenders, victims or witnesses from the start of a criminal investigation until the moment that someone is charged with an offence, if the child does not turn 18 first. The remaining amendments to clause 43 are relevant to victims and witnesses only.

Amendments Nos. 84, 85 and 94 clarify who will be able to give a parental waiver in respect of children who allegedly are involved as victims or witnesses in a criminal offence committed in England, Wales, or Northern Ireland. That becomes important when a child is not living with one or both of its natural parents.

It is not impossible that the person with appropriate authority for such a child will be a Scottish resident. The Bill already provides for the possibility that the media wanting to publish the information in question will also be Scottish.

Amendments Nos. 75, 76 and 79 ensure that the restrictions--unlike restrictions provided by clause 43 in respect of young offenders--should be implemented only if a draft order to that effect has been approved by affirmative resolution procedure in both Houses. We prefer that course of action to amendment No. 83, although I well understand what prompted the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) to table it. The Opposition would, by that amendment, change the basis of the restrictions imposed on the identification of child victims and witnesses in the circumstances described in clause 43 from mandatory arrangements--for which exceptions have nevertheless been provided--to restrictions that can be imposed only at the discretion of a court.

In preparing the Bill, we considered both a discretionary procedure that involved a parent applying for restrictions to be imposed and a procedure that involved the parent claiming the exercise of an otherwise dormant right for restrictions to apply. We concluded that there was no fair and practical way of making such systems work without other changes--not least imposing a short period of mandatory restrictions initially, to allow parents to find out and examine their options, and putting the court system, the police and parents to a great deal of trouble and expense.

That is why we chose not to go down the route of amendment No. 83, but to follow the route of tabling amendments Nos. 75, 76 and 79. I repeat that the purpose is to ensure that these restrictions--unlike those provided by clause 43 in respect of young offenders--be implemented only if a draft order to that effect has been approved by affirmative resolution procedure in both Houses. I hope that the hon. Member for Ryedale will feel, on reflection, that that meets his purposes in a manner that is less burdensome for the system and for would-be applicants.

The Committee will want to examine the question as to why we should have any restrictions on the identification of child victims and witnesses, unless they are required to protect the privacy of a court proceeding.

Media reporting of crime is an important part of the criminal justice process. The media rightly have a privileged place in the criminal justice process, above that of individual members of the public. Their presence in court, reporting on proceedings, ensures that the wider public can be confident that justice is open and that the power of the Executive is not misused.

But with that privilege must go responsibilities, and the media--like all those who take part in this process--need to acknowledge that children involved or implicated in criminal offences may need to be protected from harm. That is not just so that children can help to obtain justice for a defendant. Children themselves deserve special protections by virtue of their immaturity and special vulnerability and their involvement in a process that is designed primarily for adults.

The importance of measures that protect children from being emotionally damaged by their experience of the criminal justice process is second only to that of preserving the right of defendants to a fair trial. Once one starts considering where the best interests of a victim or witness lie, the distinction between the investigation stage and the court process becomes less important.

Mr. Boateng: This group of amendments implements a Law Commission recommendation that section 69 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 should be repealed. New clause 2 will repeal that provision; Government amendments Nos. 29 to 39 are consequential amendments, relating to that repeal.

Section 69 of PACE provides that a document produced by a computer may not be adduced as evidence unless it is shown that the computer was operating properly and was not used improperly. The provision was based on the recommendations in the 1972 Criminal Law Revision Committee report and was intended to clarify the law on the admissibility of computer evidence in response to the growing importance and use of computers. Of course, comparing computer technology in 1972 with that of today is a bit like comparing the spinning jenny with a modern high-powered loom. [Interruption.]

Although sorely tempted, I shall not respond to sedentary mutterings. However, I am stung by the reference to spin-doctoring made by the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr Llwyd).

Technology has moved on, which is why we need new clause 2. The rapid advances in computer technology have made section 69 of PACE an increasingly difficult hurdle for the prosecution and defence to overcome. before computer evidence can be used, the party seeking to adduce it must prove that the computer was reliable at the material time.

Computer developments have made it increasingly impractical to examine all the intricacies of computer operation and certify that they comply with the requirement. Indeed, most Committee members know from domestic experience that one's eight-year-old child is the only person on whom one can absolutely rely to tell one how one's computer works. The younger one's children, the better they are at that.

We have been taking steps to keep eight-year-olds out of court. [Laughter.]

Mr. Greenway: What laws would be contravened if the Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department, the hon. Member for North Warwickshire (Mr. O'Brien), employed eight-year-olds to work in the passport office.

Mr. Boateng: We try to extend the hand of friendship, and what do we get? That'll learn me.

Greater use of modern technology places a growing burden on business to supply section 69 certificates for criminal trials and provide members of staff to give evidence in court. For example, everyday till receipts showing the purchase of goods have required certificates under section 69.

That is a complete waste of time for the court, the prosecution and defence. In the case of evidence from the defendant's computer, it is plainly impossible for the prosecution to provide an operator's certificate that the computer was working correctly. Frankly, it is absurd that such evidence should be excluded as the result of the unavailability of a section 69 certificate.

Of course, those problems apply equally in relation to the defence. A defendant could be prevented from using computer evidence that might exculpate him, merely because he could not prove that the computer was reliable. That would be grossly unfair. The Law Commission therefore concluded that present law was unsatisfactory and recommended that the section be repealed without replacement--a view supported by the vast majority of respondents to the Law Commission's consultation on hearsay. In Scotland, New Zealand, the United States and Canada there is no separate scheme for computer evidence and no problems appear to have arisen.

The effect of repeal will be that common law will apply and, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, the courts will presume that computer systems are in order. If there is evidence that a computer may not have been working correctly, the party seeking to adduce the evidence will still need to prove the point. That is how the law currently deals with evidence from mechanical sources--for example, traffic lights and speedometers--and it works perfectly well. The Government believe that the repeal will redress the balance and enable the courts to take proper account of the increasing value of computer-related evidence. I therefore support the new clause.
