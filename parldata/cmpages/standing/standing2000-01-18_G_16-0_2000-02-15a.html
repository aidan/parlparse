Mr. Stunell: We return to slightly more domestic surroundings than those in which we found ourselves yesterday when the Bill was being considered in the House.

Perhaps we should start by considering what the clause is about and what the outcome of our discussion should be. From my perspective, the original provision to extend a 20-year qualification to remain on the United Kingdom voters register to those living overseas was introduced for straightforward party political advantage. Those who introduced the provision intended to enfranchise a large number of expatriate United Kingdom citizens because it was their expectation that they would vote Conservative; but that piece of gerrymandering did not work. Nothing like as many people registered as the Conservatives had hoped and, although the details have been kept secret, there is little evidence that the legislation affected the results in any UK constituency.

Mr. Dominic Grieve (Beaconsfield): If one removes the phobic element from the hon. Gentleman's remarks, it could equally easily be said that a measure was introduced and taken up by a small group of people: those, one might assume, who were genuinely keen to participate in UK politics. Since that time, those people have quietly exercised that right, and there is no suggestion that any distortion of the electoral process has occurred as a result. Therefore, it was a commendable measure. It is not for Governments or political parties to try to judge how those living overseas might vote.

Mr. Stunell: I am not sure, Mrs. Michie, how far you would allow me to explore the argument about the motivation behind the original provision. My understanding is that a typical constituency contains no more than a handful of such voters. That contrasts with what Conservative Home Secretaries said at the time about the several million people who were disenfranchised and should be included in the electoral roll. However, my purpose was not to establish blame or even motivation for the legislation, but simply to say that, at the time, the measure seemed designed to serve a particular political purpose. It clearly has not done so because, as the hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) just said, it has been of value to only a few people who are highly engaged in the UK political system. The Bill will reduce the period of qualification.

The purpose behind the amendment is to ensure that, in limiting what I regard as a scam, we do not throw away one of the beneficial side effects of the legislation. The amendment would allow employees of approved international institutions to remain on the electoral roll for the whole of their employment with that institution, and defines a process for establishing which institutions shall be counted for that purpose.

Let me draw a parallel with service voters. The amendment deals with employees of international institutions, who are very often in place because of quota systems ensuring the employment of a specified number of United Kingdom nationals. Such people often have a dedicated interest in the United Kingdom and in the political process in general. Their position is equivalent to service in the armed forces. Were such people employed in the armed forces, they would be able to stay on the register as service voters, notwithstanding the fact that they might spend longer even than 20 years overseas on various international postings.

The amendment is intended to ensure that cutting the privilege currently offered in all circumstances to UK nationals living overseas does not also prune out a group of people who are serving the UK in international institutions, who have a clear and direct connection with the UK political system and who often have a firm interest in it. The hon. Member for Battersea (Mr. Linton) has made parallel proposals in the new clause, which covers the same ground as the amendment. I shall listen carefully to his remarks on the new clause, but the two proposals march together. Collectively, they are an attempt to ensure that we do not throw out the baby with the bathwater.

There are two ways of qualifying for an overseas vote: first, by virtue of one's job—until now that has applied to people who work for the Foreign and Commonwealth Office, the civil service, the armed services or the British Council—and, secondly, by means of a time qualification of 20 years. New clause 1 will add a different way of qualifying which would include all the categories that the hon. Gentleman mentioned. If I am pushing at an open door, I hope that the Minister will let me know and I can save my breath.

I want to make the case for people working for international institutions. Like many other members of the Committee, the Minister may have received a letter from four senior European Union officials—Marion Dewar, Jacqueline Minor, Sarah Lambert and Tobias Mackie—one of whom is from a well-known political family, giving reasons why the present categories of the civil service, the British Council and armed services should be extended. The letter states:

    ``Firstly, we are public servants working to serve the public in Britain as well as in other member states. Secondly, we work for a trans-national organisation of which Britain is a member and which represents Britain's interests. Thirdly, it is Government policy to try to increase the representation of British nationals in Brussels on the basis that it is in the national interest to have as many British nationals as possible working in the EU, so these institutions can better reflect our culture and attitudes.''

The letter concludes with the following relevant point:

    ``We believe that this aim is reinforced by ensuring that those working inside EU institutions retain their connection with Britain. Disenfranchisement severs this link with British political life and risks weakening their ties.''

The demands of equality and the national interest do not always coincide in such a way. The letter calls for equality of treatment with domestic civil servants for those working for international organisations and it is in the national interest of this country that they should retain a strong sense of identity with the United Kingdom when they are working abroad. It would be unjust and counter-productive not to make concessions to their case as they would thus not only be disfranchised, but would feel disenchanted and disaffected with their mother country.

I have two further points: first, many people have accepted jobs since 1989 expecting that their voting rights would be safeguarded for 20 years. Although it is within the powers of Parliament to amend legislation as it sees fit, many people were recruited on that understanding. The only thing that might prevent the Minister from accepting my proposal is that he may wonder whether the definition of international organisations is sufficiently clear to avoid creating confusion or inviting argument. The organisations involved are those of which the United Kingdom is a member; they include all European Union institutions, NATO, the United Nations and its daughter organisations, the Council of Europe and the Western European Union.

We are a long way from the days when the only representatives of Britain abroad were diplomats or soldiers. In these days of globalisation, it is time that we recognise that there are far more people who fly the flag in other countries. Given that they represent their country abroad, it is not asking too much that they should be guaranteed a say in how it is run.

The issue is settled because the Government will not drop the clause, but it is right and proper to express my view that as 20 years was originally intended to be a loophole, the Bill should close it.

Clause 132

Supervisory powers of Commission

Mr. Walter: I beg to move amendment No. 134, in page 83, line 33, after `may', insert `by notice'.

The clause relates to the commission's supervisory powers. I was slightly perturbed that subsection (3) states:

    ``A person authorised in writing by the Commission may, for the purposes of the carrying out by the Commission of their functions, enter at any reasonable time premises occupied by a supervised organisation or individual and having entered any such premises may—

    inspect any books'',

look at the computer, or do whatever that person wants to do to inspect the documents and electronic matter held by the organisation or individual. That seems a rather draconian power, similar to the power that Customs and Excise might have in investigating VAT fraud, for example. The matters to which the clause relates are not of the same magnitude; it relates to large and small political parties, to small third parties, to permitted participants in a referendum campaign and, in terms of the supervisory role, to individuals. For the commission just to turn up on someone's doorstep, bang on the door and say, ``We're coming in, because this is a reasonable time to come and look through the books,'' is a draconian power and I am not sure that such a power should be vested in the commission.

Although it is reasonable that officers of the commission should be able to inspect the books, documents, records and computer files at a reasonable time it is reasonable, too, that the commission should give notice that it wishes to carry out an inspection. Its officers should not simply turn up at the home address of an individual at a time when it may be inconvenient or when he may have other pressing business which, a court might say, is a reasonable prior engagement. If the commission felt that the individual was concealing information, it would have redress elsewhere in the Bill if that concealment took place after the notice had been given.

Mr. Grieve: The amendment raises an interesting issue about what the powers of the commission are designed or intended to be. It does not have policemen's powers; it does not have a right of entry and it does not have to get a warrant in order to enter premises. Therefore I should expect its officers' arrival at premises usually to be on notice, which is the reason for the amendment. There may be arguments that that need not be so. Under health and safety legislation, inspectors have powers to enter, but in practice I have never known any to do so other than on notice. They usually ring up and say, ``I am coming round to pick up documents,'' or they write asking ``Could you please supply me with the following documents?'' To take another analogy, should the relevant powers be different from those of the Inland Revenue, which usually succeeds in getting information by repeated written requests—certainly not by going round with a snatch squad to lay hands on books and documents?

How, in practice, does the Minister envisage the arrangement will work? Any right to enter premises is potentially a breach of civil liberties. We need to examine the matter critically before we decide how best to give the relevant powers. The Bill makes it clear that powers akin to those of the police are not intended. How are the powers derived, and on what precedents has the Minister relied in drafting the provision?

Mr. Tipping: I want to reassure the hon. Member for Beaconsfield that the commission is intended to have a positive image and will normally visit premises by invitation or agreement. We are keen to raise standards and ensure that the benefits of the commission's work are available to everyone and that everyone accepts them. I was struck by the phrase used by the hon. Member for North Dorset about being frightened of people banging on the door at any time. As he acknowledged, clause 132 makes it clear that any visit under the relevant powers should take place ``at any reasonable time''. The knock on the door at the stroke of midnight will not happen. I do not think that the commission will be keen to be seen in a similar light to Customs and Excise.

I think that Committee members acknowledge that the commission should have powers to tackle avoidance and evasion. Potentially—I shall put it no more strongly than that—for some organisations, in extreme circumstances, to conceal and destroy documents. That is the reason for the provision, which I do not believe will be misused or used often, for the commission to arrive unannounced.

There are precedents for such a power. The Gaming Board for Great Britain visits casinos regularly, unannounced. I want to reassure the hon. Gentleman about the role, purpose and practice of the commission, but it must have the power to arrive unannounced if avoidance and evasion are genuinely thought to be happening and it is thought that documents are being concealed or destroyed.

Mr. Walter: I am a little reassured by the Minister's comments. I had not thought of the comparison with the gaming board. I am unhappy, but I am willing to accept the Minister's reassurance and I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Grieve: I beg to move amendment No. 135, in page 86, line 10, after `he', insert

    `knowingly'.

The amendment deals with another perennial topic of the Committee. The question is whether we should insert the word ``knowingly'' into the clause. This occasion may be slightly different from previous ones. For once, the word ``knowingly'' may be the correct one to insert. The clause states:

    ``A person commits an offence if he—

    (a) alters, suppresses, conceals or destroys, or

The clause provides a reasonable excuse defence for failure to supply a document, but there is no defence for a person who innocently destroys or alters a document. I accept that suppressing a document appears to require active mens rea, but destruction of a document can be done innocently. The clause needs to be re-examined, to ascertain whether the offences under clause 134(1) should remain absolute offences.

Mr. Walter: I thoroughly support my hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield. The amendment is essential. These days, with so much documentation held in electronic form, it is sometimes difficult for me to find my own files, which I have not knowingly concealed. Mr. Tipping: I am delighted that at least one other member of the Committee admits to problems with new technology. I confess that in a few minutes I shall go and sharpen my quill pen.

Mr. Walter: The Minister may not be surprised that I rise to speak on the question of public access to the register. Therefore, I want to seek more guidance and information from the Minister on the details of how the commission will make registers available.

Clause 135(2) states:

    ``The Commission shall make a copy of the register available for public inspection during ordinary office hours, either at the Commission's offices or at some convenient place appointed by them.''

I am interested to know what is envisaged by the phrase,

    ``some convenient place appointed by them.''

Subsection (3) continues:

    ``The Commission may make other arrangements for members of the public to have access to the contents of the register''.

Subsection (4) states:

    ``If requested to do so by any person, the Commission shall supply him with a copy of the register or any part of it.''

The clause goes on to state that the commission may charge such reasonable fee as it may determine.

My concern is that the commission should be seen to be making the documents to members of the public available in the easiest and most accessible way. Although there have been indications that it will have offices in Cardiff, Edinburgh and Belfast, as well as in London, I want to know whether the commission will think it necessary to make hard copies of the register available in major reference libraries, for example, or in other places such as the Library of the House of Commons. Mr. Tipping: The hon. Gentleman raises important matters, some of which will be dealt with by the commission when it is set up. It will make hard copies available and circulate them, for a fee. It is highly probably that hard copies will be available through its officers, but that is a matter for the commission. It is also probable—again, it is a matter for the commission—that the material will be available on the internet. The commission picks up the work of the registrar of political parties, based at Companies house; that material is already available on the internet. I confess to having difficulties in that respect but I understand that it is a growing technology.

The hon. Gentleman asked how we can safeguard the information available. The name of the donor, the amount and the date on which the sum was given go on the register. In the interests of confidentiality, the addresses will not be on the register. Those with unusual names who wish to make donations may be identifiable, but it is unlikely that there will be invasion of privacy provided that just date, name and amount are on the list. I hope that that satisfies the hon. Gentleman.
