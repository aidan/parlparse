Monetary Policy Committee

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 18, in page 5, line 27, at end insert--

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 24, in page 5, line 27, at end insert--

    `(2A) The appointment of all the members of the Committee shall be subject to confirmation by the Treasury Select Committee of the House of Commons.'.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): I was saying this morning that the staffing of the Monetary Policy Committee is crucial. The members of that committee will determine the monetary policy of the country. The choice of members of the committee is critical, especially if hon. Members accept, as I do, that the translation into monetary policy of the inflation target, set by democratically elected Ministers, is not simply a technical matter. It requires subjective economic viewpoints. The members chosen to represent such viewpoints on the Monetary Policy Committee will determine the monetary policy of the country.

Tim Congdon's booklet, produced by the Centre for Policy Studies, states on page 39 that

    ``the Governor of the Bank of England would become a major public figure, as important in some ways as the Chancellor of the Exchequer.''

The same applies to members of the Monetary Policy Committee; they will become--indeed there is evidence that they are already becoming--personalities, political figures in their own right. Their thoughts and views will determine the public policy of the country, and that is a critical matter.

Will the Paymaster General tell the Committee how the Chancellor will pick members of the Monetary Policy Committee? Will he try to establish a balance of economic views between broad monetarists, narrow monetarists, Keynesians, or those who believe in returning Britain to the gold standard? Will the Chancellor actively seek that balance, or will he choose members who are eminent in their particular fields, but whose opinions will be a random matter, so that economic policy will be a lottery? These days the lottery seems to be part of the Government's economic policy. I hope that the Paymaster General will address these points in his concluding remarks.

The Paymaster General has said that the Government want to have a transparent and open policy; therefore, hon. Members should know the criteria for selecting members of the Monetary Policy Committee. If we cannot scrutinise the criteria for assessing the economic views of potential members of the Committee, or if the Paymaster General will not tell us what they are, it is important that we should scrutinise the individuals.

For those reasons, I support the amendment's proposal that nominations for the committee should be scrutinised by the Treasury Select Committee. That is not just my opinion, nor that of Conservative Members, nor even that of Liberal Democrat Members: it is shared by eminent and rising stars on the Labour Benches. The hon. Member for Bolton, West (Ms Kelly), for example, said on Second Reading:

    ``It is for those reasons that I argue that although I support the Bill and support the granting of independence to the Bank, the hand of Parliament should be strengthened so that a Select Committee can properly carry out its responsibilities and hold the Bank to account.''

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): Is not the hon. Member for Bolton, West a distinguished former employee of the Bank?

Mr. Gibb: Indeed; my hon. Friend is right to point that out. In view of the hon. Lady's expertise, I am surprised that she is not a member of the Standing Committee. Given the remarks that I have quoted from Hansard, however, I can see why the Government would not want her on the Committee. She continued:

    ``In particular, I suggest, as the Treasury Select Committee has suggested in its recent report, that members of the Monetary Policy Committee have the opportunity to come before the Select Committee in confirmatory hearings after their nomination to the Monetary Policy Committee by the Chancellor.''--[Official Report, 11 November 1997; Vol. 300, c. 772.]

Mr. David Ruffley (Bury St. Edmunds): My hon. Friend has missed out another rising star--the hon. So at least three Labour Members believe in a measure. The Paymaster General should therefore take notice of the opinions of his right hon. and hon. Friends.

The Treasury Committee concluded, in paragraph 47:

    ``In our view the independence of the appointment process would be enhanced by the Treasury Committee being given power under the new Bank of England Act to confirm the nominations of the Governor, Deputy Governors and all other members of the Monetary Policy Committee . . . We therefore recommend that the Bank of England Act should make provision for the confirmation of nominations to the post of Governor, deputy Governor or nominated member of the MPC unless the Treasury Committee, within 30 calender days of the nomination being made, makes a report to the House of Commons stating its reasons for considering that the candidate does not meet the criteria of competence and personal independence and recommending that the appointment be not confirmed.''

There it is in black and white.

The Treasury Committee is to be Parliament's only way of holding the new Bank accountable. A Memorandum dated 13 September, prepared by the Treasury, stated that the Bank will be accountable to the House of Commons--through regular reports and evidence to the Treasury Committee. Is it not ironic that the Government believe that the Treasury Committee is the only tool by which the Bank will become accountable, although that Committee believes that it can make the Bank of England accountable only if it has power to review nominations to the MPC? In effect, the Government are in a catch-22 position.

Jacqui Smith (Redditch): Does the hon. Gentleman think that other Select Committees might like to hold senior officials to account in a similar way? If so, how does he propose that that should come about?

Mr. Gibb: The hon. Lady has raised an important point. The evolution of Select Committees has been a gradual process. When the next steps agencies was introduced, it was clear that Select Committees would play a part.

Making the Bank of England independent is the greatest change--the biggest next steps agency of them all. In terms of its control over people's everyday lives, the Bank will have enormous independence and a lack of accountability. This is, so far as I am aware, the biggest example in history of power being taken from the House and given to an outside body. If changes to the Select Committee process are needed, this seems the ideal place to begin. I do not think that we should have the Bill because the Bank of England should not be given independence. Given that it has been given independence, we have to provide for the gradual evolution of the Select Committee process, otherwise the Bank of England will be non-accountable in the future. I should like to hear what the Paymaster General has to say about accountability, and how he intends to solve that catch-22 problem.

What are the criteria for determining the economic views of those whom the Government will appoint to the Monetary Policy Committee?

Member for Bury St. Edmunds (Mr. Ruffley) suggested in one or two remarks that there was something wrong in Members of Parliament behaving independently and not always reflecting their party's views. But that is the purpose of a Select Committee. The hon. Gentleman's points do not constitute an argument against the Government's position. It is natural for members of a Select Committee to try to gain more power for their Committee. That is a healthy sign of our evolving parliamentary system. The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton was seeking help. He wants to ensure that the good balance that currently exists in the Monetary Policy Committee is maintained. He is anxious to exclude serendipity and random choice from our selection process. I assure him that such elements will play no part in it.

The hon. Gentleman asked me to list a set of criteria for prospective members of the Monetary Policy Committee. We cannot do better than the qualities for which current members have been chosen. However, the two most important qualifications are outstanding intelligence, for which there is no substitute, and a demonstrable economic knowledge. Those are the essential criteria, but all sorts of other matters must be considered. However, it would be inappropriate to delay our proceedings with them.

I would like to pay tribute to my hon. Friend the Member for Bolton, West whom the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton also mentioned. She is a distinguished economist, who worked for the Bank of England. She might be a candidate, not in the near future, but in the longer term, for which all our policies are geared. I also pay tribute to my distinguished hon. Friend the Member for Durham, North. There is no greater protagonist of the Select Committee system. My hon. Friend's views are well known.

It is not appropriate for the Committee to decide how we should proceed. The House is reviewing the Select Committees. As my hon. Friend the Member for Redditch (Jacqui Smith) said earlier, the House's decision will be translated to each organisation for which it might be thought appropriate to have a confirmatory arrangement made for the respective Select Committee. Until that decision is taken--and in light of the arguments made here--I urge my hon. Friends to vote against the amendment.

Mr. Davey: The amendment would increase the period of office during which members of the Monetary Policy Committee could serve. It is one of a number of measures tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Twickenham and I to attempt to beef up the Bank's independence. That must affect the way in which it is perceived as well as how it operates. It is crucial that appointees are seen to be their own agents, not agents of the Government. If they were to be seen as agents of the Government, it would seriously undermine the independence and credibility of the Bank.

There must be no suggestion of pressure being put upon appointees to vote in favour of the Government's view. The term of office should last longer than the life of a Parliament, ensuring that reappointment will not be for the Chancellor of the Exchequer to decide.

I have no wish to impugn the professionalism of current members of the Monetary Policy Committee or indeed of its future members. I am sure that they will approach their jobs with a great deal of professionalism and would be horrified at the suggestion that they would be anything other than independent in their conduct of affairs. However, I stress that the appearance of independence is crucial, ensuring that the outside world will believe in their complete independence. If the period of office is only for three years, there may be question marks about that.

Appearances are crucial. The raison d'etre of an independent bank is to affect people's inflationary expectations. There must be credibility in the Bank's capacity to take decisions in a depoliticised manner in the interests of the long-term health of the economy. Anything undermining that sense of independence will undermine the whole point of the Bill.

As mentioned in debates on the Select Committee's ratification hearings, experience elsewhere suggests that other countries have opted for longer time periods in setting up arrangements for independent banks--as much as 14 years in the United States, although I stand to be corrected on that, and eight years with the Bundesbank. Elsewhere, then, a term of office longer than the electoral cycle has been adopted.

We can examine the degree of independence by drawing parallels with other types of institutions. For example, members of the supreme court in the USA have life tenure. Although they are political appointees--appointed by the President, subject to ratification hearings in Congress--their independence has always been viewed as essential to establish the legitimacy of supreme court decisions.

One example is the former chief justice, Earl Warren. When he was appointed by President Eisenhower it was thought that as he came from Republican stock he would do the President's bidding. President Eisenhower expected his new chief justice to put forward his types of social policy but is on record as being very disappointed by the reality. Chief Justice Warren was a very liberal chief justice of the US supreme court. It was because he had independence and life tenure and was not going to be reappointed by the same politician that he was able to take a different view. That underpinned and strengthened the decisions of the court under his leadership.

Terms of office and the period of those terms are very important and it is vital that they do not stay within the period of an election but span that period. If the Government accept the amendment, it would, like all the amendments we have tabled, strengthen the policies that they are trying to pursue. It is yet another example of the Liberal Democrats trying to be very constructive in our work on this Committee.
