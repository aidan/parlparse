Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Geoffrey Clifton-Brown (Cotswold): Before we broke for lunch, my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire (Mr. Moss) had been discussing amendment No. 18. In our differences towards the amendment, we and the Government may be arguing about semantics-if the Minister says that that is the case, I should be grateful.

Subsection (3) states:

    Before making a scheme under this section, the appropriate authority shall consult such persons as appear to it to be likely to be entitled to payments under such a scheme and such organisations as appear to it to represent such persons.

In other words, the Ministry could conduct a cursory consultation exercise and say, ``We have consulted but we shall go back to what we originally thought before the consultation.'' Our job is to ensure that legislation that passes through the House is clear. Clear action should also be taken as a result.

The amendment would add after the passage that I have just quoted the phrase

    and shall have regard to the views expressed by those persons and bodies.

That would ensure that the consultation was comprehensive and took people's views into account. If the matter were tested in the courts, the Minister would have to demonstrate that he had undertaken a comprehensive consultation exercise.

The Minister may say that we are merely arguing about semantics. I do not think that I am, but I look forward to his response.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Elliot Morley): I understand the hon. Gentleman's point, and I agree that it is important to have consultations and to listen to the views of those who are consulted. However, statutory obligations apply to consultations-there is an obligation on me, as the Minister, to take those views into account. The Bill does not contain such a provision because it is not required-the relevant obligation already exists and I accept it.

Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): I am grateful to the Minister for that clarification. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Moss: I beg to move amendment No. 19, in page 3, line 41, leave out ``the lands Tribunal'' and insert

    `the arbitration of such person as may be agreed between the person and the appropriate authority or, in default of agreement, to an arbitrator nominated by the President of the Royal Institute of Chartered Surveyors.'.

This is the last of our amendments that relate to clause 5. Amendment No. 19 would replace the Bill's provision that requires compensation disputes to be adjudicated by the Lands Tribunal with a provision requiring arbitration to be carried out by an agreed arbitrator or, failing that, by someone nominated by the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors.

The Bill provides for disputes about a person's entitlement under a compensation scheme or about the amount of compensation to be referred to the Lands Tribunal. However, the Lands Tribunal is notorious for reaching decisions on disputes extremely slowly and for being extremely expensive-professional fees are involved when pursuing cases.

We believe that justice should be ensured, and that that should be done in a reasonable time. A better solution would be for disputes to be dealt with through arbitration. The amendment contains appropriate provisions to realise those aims. If the appropriate Minister and the claimant cannot agree on a suitable arbitrator, one would be appointed by the president of the RICS, which has, in our opinion, the best expertise for the valuation of land and businesses for compensation purposes.

As the Bill is drafted, there is a risk that a Minister could simply refuse arbitration and then wait for nine months before making his offer, forcing the claimant to settle or face the unattractive option of going to the Lands Tribunal. That would give the Minister unfair leverage in his negotiations with claimants. We want an assurance from the Minister that he will agree to arbitration, if the various parties cannot settle. A similar amendment was tabled in Committee for the private Member's Bill presented by the hon. Member for Liverpool, Garston (Maria Eagle). At that time, the Government said that, as a general principle, a formal judicial process was more effective than informal arbitration, although they did undertake to reconsider their position. We look forward to hearing what the Minister has to say about the slightly changed wording proposed in the amendment.

Mr. Clifton-Brown: Again, I declare an interest, as one of the two Members who are chartered surveyors. As such, I would be likely to be affected by the amendment. It is more common for a lands tribunal to consist of three chartered surveyors, so there will be lots of work for chartered surveyors either way-three sets of fees, instead of one. I might, therefore, be expected to argue in favour of the Lands Tribunal, but, as it happens, I intend to argue the opposite case.

My hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire ably pointed out the difficulty with the Lands Tribunal. The tribunal is a highly expert body that exists to decide on difficult cases, but I do not think that these cases will be all that difficult. It would not, by any means, be beyond the scope of a single chartered surveyor. He will, by virtue of the fact that he has been appointed by the president of the Royal Institution of Chartered Surveyors, be a chartered surveyor of some years' standing and will have some specialism in such cases. That would be a more rapid and cheaper solution for the Government and the fur farmer who is claiming compensation. As is common in legal agreements, the case could go to arbitration initially and, if, for any of the reasons laid down in statute, the matter cannot be determined by the arbitrator, it will go to the Lands Tribunal. That is common practice in landlord and tenant agreements.

It would be perfectly satisfactory to have an arbitrator doing the job, in most of the cases that will arise under the Bill. That would involve less cost for the Government and for the fur farmer who has to pay not only the costs of his advisers-although he will be able to recover those-but the cost of the arbitration. That is a win-win situation; there is less cost and it is a quicker process.

Mr. Morley: The amendment is unnecessary. The Bill provides a choice between arbitration and the Lands Tribunal. I agreed to consider that issue, following our discussions in Committee for the Bill presented by my hon. Friend the Member for Garston, and we incorporated it into this Bill. Individuals can choose, and there is no need to delete the reference to the Lands Tribunal, because people are not obliged to go to it; they can choose arbitration.

With all due respect to the hon. Member for Cotswold, who is, I am sure, a cost-effective, bargain chartered surveyor, I must say that arbitration is not necessarily cheaper or quicker than the Land Tribunal; arbitration can be slow and expensive. The Bill leaves parties that choice. I received the impression from the previous debate that people would prefer to go through mutually agreed arbitration rather than through the Lands Tribunal, and I accept that. Therefore, that choice exists, but the Lands Tribunal remains as a fallback.

My final point is that the Lands Tribunal can deal with points of law, which the arbitrator cannot do. Again, people have the choice.

Mr. Moss: Can the Minister clarify the arbitration referred to in subsection (4)(b)? Who would carry out that arbitration? We have suggested a nominee of the president of the RICS. Whom would the Minister recommend? Do the Government intend to hold people to the wire for nine months before pushing them, perhaps kicking and screaming, into the Lands Tribunal's arms?

Mr. Morley: To go to arbitration is a decision agreed by both parties, and I cannot foresee circumstances in which the Government would refuse to go to arbitration. It is for the individuals concerned to decide whether they want to go to arbitration on that basis. If my memory serves me right, the person who chairs the arbitration must be acceptable to both sides. In that case the RICS president could, and normally does, appoint the arbiter, as the amendment suggests.

Mr. Moss: I thank the Minister for that clarification. On that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Moss: As I said in my opening remarks on the amendments, this is perhaps the most important part of the Bill after clause 1, which dealt with the prohibition of activities whereby farmers raise animals solely and primarily for their fur. It is the biggest concern of those involved in the industry, and it is a concern that remains because they are unfamiliar with the way in which legislation is written. Moreover, although we have debated the Bill for some hours now, it is still not clear what elements of the businesses should be taken into consideration when agreement is sought on the appropriate level of compensation.

The Minister was a little touchy earlier when I talked about ducking and weaving. I did not imply that he had been ducking and weaving up till now. He has been very straight on this business. He speaks with integrity, and I respect his resolve to honour the commitments given on compensation. We are not arguing about the commitment to compensation, but about the amount of compensation and a system for paying it fairly to those involved. That is where the difference of view and interpretation, if any, has arisen.

I have already said that I accept the Minister's comments regarding the enabling nature of the clause. There is some merit in leaving the wording fairly flexible, so that during the consultation period-I welcome the Minister's assurance that it will be proper consultation and that he will have regard to the representations made to him by those in the industry-he will ensure that the compensation covers as many as possible of the aspects that we have discussed. We can only hope that during the consultation period the Government will take into account land, buildings, equipment, livestock and good will-although I agree with the Minister that defining good will is very difficult, and enters the equation only if one is prepared to value a business on the basis that it is a going concern.

That takes me back to my earlier point. The real value of a fur farming business-albeit that it will in future be illegal-is its value on the open market between a willing seller and a willing buyer. That will, indeed, take good will into account as part of the value of the business. It will also involve some consideration of income. We have not homed in on income during today's debates; we shall consider it at a later stage. The Minister has given an assurance that he will consider compensation for income in the course of consultation with the industry. That is welcome. However, we may need to test the matter further by tabling more specific amendments.

During debate on the amendments, the Minister said that he would look carefully at any discrepancy that arose out of the consultation process in terms of evaluating the values of such businesses. The figure in the explanatory notes is, according to the Minister, the result of research by his staff. That figure, even including income, is a maximum of £1.6 million, yet the full value of fur farming businesses in this country is-although we do not know the exact figure-much more than that.

Given that large discrepancy, we welcome the Minister's commitment to use independent assessors and advisers to work with the industry to calculate a more meaningful figure. I cannot remember his exact words, but he said that that would have to be taken on board at the appropriate time. I wish him luck in his negotiations with the Treasury for the extra money, or in finding it from the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food budget, which is, as we know, already stretched in several directions.

Will the Minister spell out what he meant by independent advice? My understanding is that it should involve people outside his Ministry, such as members of the RICS, valuers and auctioneers, and people associated with the fur farming business who know about the details of running such operations. I would be most upset if it transpired that so-called independent advice was not quite so independent and included several people directly or indirectly employed by his Ministry.

As I said, we welcome the part of the clause that refers to consultation, and are pleased with the Minister's assurances that he will have due regard to the representations that are made to him. However, we have not discussed the timetable for the consultation process, and it would be helpful if the Minister would comment on that. Does he envisage beginning consultation before the Bill hits the statute book, or will he go by the letter of the law and wait until the Bill receives Royal Assent?

I should have thought-this point was raised by my hon. Friends-that there is a case for contact to be made with the industry before the Bill becomes law, so that headings can be set up that focus the minds of both sides on the key issues involved in the calculation of compensation. It would reassure the industry and would help us to prepare for Report if we knew what the main headings and the compensation were likely to be. I know that that was asked for when the private Member's Bill was introduced, but nothing was done before Report.

We have accepted the Minister's advice that the Lands Tribunal will be a backstop and that there is no alternative to it, but the Minister has also concluded that it is a long and often expensive process. We hope that many compensation claims will be settled and satisfied long before the end of the period set out in subsection (4).

I want to return to a point that I made in my opening remarks on clause 1 that is relevant to this stand part debate. I referred to the opposition in Europe from all the legal fur farming operations in countries such as Denmark, Spain and France, many of which have made submissions. France, as we know, is expecting a response from the Government. When does the Minister intend to make that response. Is it in the pipeline, or has it been delayed? My worry is that European producers, who see the Bill as a direct attack on their own legal operations, will take the matter to the highest level of the European Court. Could that, in the Minister's interpretation of events, delay the implementation of the Bill? If there is any possibility that the Bill might not become law until the legal machinations in the European Court have been concluded, which would not be a matter of weeks or months, but could drag on for a year or two, where will that leave the industry?

Given that period of limbo, I cannot imagine our own fur farmers investing in their businesses. They want, in the main, to negotiate sensible compensation and get out. It is not just because they think that the Government will get the Bill through because of their large majority, which is inevitable; many of them have had enough of the terrorism that they face from animal rights agitators, who visit them frequently and use methods of terror that we all deprecate but do little to stop. The fur farmers have had enough of that and want out.

I am concerned that there may be a delay in making the Bill operative, which includes the negotiations on and finalisation of all the compensation payments. It would be helpful if the Minister could tell us that he sees no reason why all the measures in the Bill, particularly those relating to compensation, cannot be finalised within an appropriate time scale, and certainly before 1 January 2003. However, I fear that hold-ups may adversely affect fur-producing farmers, who might find that the question of compensation is put on the back burner until the process is finalised in Europe.

Our main thrust is to ensure that the Government honour their commitment to fair and appropriate compensation for producers, but given existing figures of between £400,000 and £1.6 million we doubt whether that commitment can be met. In discussing the clause earlier today, my hon. Friend the Member for North Shropshire (Mr. Paterson) said that compensation of about £400 per breeding female was recently paid to an Austrian producer. We received details on that case from the European Furbreeders Association. There was no basis for making calculations according to the headings that we have discussed today, but a figure of 4.7 million Austrian schillings was agreed in respect of 600 breeding females. At a reasonable conversion rate of, say, 21 schillings to the pound, that equals between £390 and £400 per breeding female.

That is a sensible level of compensation, and a benchmark that the Government would be ill advised to ignore. There are 11 producers and 13 fur farmers in this country whom the Government are planning to put out of business. There are about 25,000 breeding females in this country. At compensation of £400 a head, the figure of £10 million-which I have mentioned more than once-is reached. Such a figure is likely to be fair and reasonable, rather than the £1.6 million compensation that the Government think appropriate. The massive discrepancy between those two figures is staring the Government and this Committee in the face, and there is nothing in the clause to reassure fur farmers that they will receive a sum nearer to £10 million than £1.6 million. On income, there are no guarantees; only today has the Minister said that he will take that into account in the consultation process.

We have serious doubts, but I reiterate that we accept the Minister's integrity and commitment to providing compensation. As he said, the Government do not need to do this, but they are using the provision to bolster their moral argument for criminalising an activity that is legal elsewhere in Europe. They have said that the real reason for this legislation is the public morality issue. They have balanced that with the moral case for paying fair and adequate compensation, and we want to ensure that such compensation is indeed fair and adequate. Our doubts remain, and we shall revisit the issue of compensation at a later stage.

Mr. Morley: I should like to say a word of thanks to you, Mr. Sayeed, for chairing the Committee-I am delighted that it has proceeded so smoothly. Members of the Committee made many valuable points, which I tried to answer directly; I also tried to respond to amendments in the spirit in which they were tabled.

We recognise that issues remain about compensation for affected farmers. That is why, from the start, we have accepted the principle of compensation, even though there were alternatives, such as raising standards to a level that would be extremely difficult to attain. Some Conservative Members argued that we should do just that-simply bankrupt people without taking into account the impact on them. Nevertheless, I thank all members of the Committee, especially those on the Government Benches, who have given valuable and valiant support. I hope that the Bill continues to meet such a reasonable, thoughtful and constructive attitude as it goes through the rest of its parliamentary stages.

Mr. Moss: May I associate myself with the Minister's comments about your professional chairmanship of our proceedings, Mr. Sayeed? We have had a good run for our money and a good debate. I thank the Minister for his constructive response to many of the issues that we raised. He has not satisfied all our concerns, especially those relating to compensation, which I am sure we shall revisit. I thank my colleagues for their contributions, voluble and otherwise. No doubt the debate will continue.

The Chairman: Now, I thank the members of the Committee and Elizabeth Payne, our Clerk.

Question put and agreed to.

Bill to be reported, without amendment.
