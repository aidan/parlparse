10.30 am

Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): I beg to move amendment No. 80, in page 1, line 14, at end insert

    "(bb) to establish a register of groups and individuals who can act as mediators:".

The amendment refers to the Parades Commission's powers, under clause 2, to promote and facilitate mediation. The amendment is lifted almost verbatim from a recommendation in the North report chapter 12.47 on page 144 which states: "we recommend that the Parades Commission should contribute to the development of mediation through establishing a register of groups and individuals who can act as mediators, and itself work with other bodies in practical ways to support mediation."

Clause 2(1)(b) states that one of the duties of the commission is "to promote and facilitate mediation as a means of resolving disputes concerning public processions."

What is meant by mediation? The dictionary definition refers to the involvement of intermediaries, without direct connection, to act as go-betweens or peacemakers. Will the Minister tell the Committee how the wording of that subsection meets the requirements of the strict definition of mediation, and also how it meets the requirements and recommendations of the North report?

Do the Government believe that the reference to promotion subsection (1)(b) would involve the commission in approaching various groups and foisting mediation on one or more non-receptive parties in a disagreement? Does "promotion" mean that the commission will have its own team of professional mediators on permanent standby? That was a point made by the hon. Member for East Londonderry (Mr. Ross) during our sittings last week. The Unionist point of view was that, in some circumstances, the present wording of the clause could mean that certain parades and groups of organisers might have mediators foisted on them from a section of the community that they found unacceptable, even though the mediation might be necessary.

How would a team of professional mediators be selected? The dictionary definition of mediation notes that it is "without direct connection". In the context of the problems that we are considering, can the Minister tell us how to find people in Northern Ireland without direct connection to one side of the argument or the other? It is extremely difficult, as I know from my experience as a Minister. When we were appointing quangos, there were problems, which continue today, in finding people who were acceptable to a broad base in Northern Ireland. There were criticisms within the Unionist community that we often overlooked people with a Unionist connection, because the Government felt that such appointments would send the wrong signals or that those people would have too much vested interest in a particular direction.

Unionist politicians in Northern Ireland often accuse the Government of the day of seeking to place Alliance party supporters or people of no political affiliation into important positions on quangos rather than attempting a broad-based political solution. I recognise that it is an almost intractable problem and that the only solution lies in proper political and democratic accountability. Like the previous Government, this Government are keen to move towards that as quickly as possible.

Clause 2(1)(b) will give the commission powers to get involved in a hands-on and direct manner. The commission, as it is currently constituted, may not wish to become so involved and Ministers probably do not intend it to do so. However, my reading of paragraph (b) gives me the impression that it could get involved directly if it so chose. That runs counter to the recommendations of the North report.

Part B of chapter 12 of the North report refers to the commission's important education role in encouraging the two sides of the community at a local level to understand each other's perception of parades. Education in itself will not necessarily bring about the desired outcome, so the report refers to the commission's second important role, which is that of facilitating mediation. Paragraph 12.38 of the report states: "It is clear from the submissions made to us that there is undoubtedly a huge network of goodwill which can be tapped."

The consultations that Dr. North and the other members of his team undertook revealed that there was a feeling in Northern Ireland that mediation was important and that a large body of people would like to be involved.

The existence of such good will is borne out by the Northern Irelandwide survey that the report instigated. Two questions were asked. First, people were asked whether it would definitely be a good idea to involve people in mediation. In response to that, 37 per cent. of Protestants and 61 per cent. of Catholics that is 46 per cent. of the overall population said that it would definitely be a good idea to involve people. Secondly, people were asked whether such involvement would probably be a good idea, and 38 per cent. of Protestants and 32 per cent. of Catholics that is 36 per cent. overall said that it would. If we add together the figures of 46 per cent. and 36 per cent. for the overall population, we find that 82 per cent. of the people consulted in the Northern Irelandwide survey were in favour, to varying degrees, of the commission having a responsibility for mediation. Members of both community groups support mediation.

The North report asked further questions about mediation. It did not answer all of them, but I hope that the Minister will take account of them when he responds. The report asked whether groups or[Mr. Malcolm Moss] individuals should become involved in mediation and whether it should be organised by central bodies or through local efforts. It asked also what training needs there were, who should supply them and how they would be funded. Finally, it asked what other bodies had a similar interest in developing mediation capability.

It is clear that the North report did not envisage a direct, hands-on approach to mediation. Indeed, the report states at chapter 12.44

    "We doubt whether the Parades Commission itself would need to develop a professional mediation capability amongst its own staff."

However, clause 2(1)(b) allows the commission the freedom and powers to become directly involved in a way that I believe the North report did not intend. The amendment spells out clearly the need to set up a register and thus is a more direct development from the recommendations and a clearer interpretation of the intention evident in the report that mediation should not be centralised in the commission. It will be difficult and problematic enough to appoint people without centring that task on the commission, with its other roles.

The amendment would also allow the scale of the problem to be set into context more easily. We discussed during the previous sitting the number of difficult conflicts between various parties with respect to parades; the figure is probably something like 20 out of 3,000. Even if that number were doubled to 40 the scale of the problem would be fairly minor in the overall context. Vast numbers of mediators and a new structure would not be needed if mediation were really to be used in cases of genuine disagreement, when the commission proved unable, through its education role, to move the process forward to the point where both sides in a dispute accepted an outcome.

Setting up the register is important and the commission should have a role in doing that. It should be mindful of the question of scale the number of likely occasions for mediation in the parade season. It should also be mindful of the method of selecting for the register people who would be acceptable to both sides of the community by virtue of their standing there and their impartiality and non-sectarian bona fides.

In some ways this is a probing amendment. I want to tease out the Government's real intentions and their interpretation of the important provisions concerning mediation. I want to know how they envisage the provisions being implemented and how they believe the North recommendations about mediation can be carried out under the current wording of the clause.
