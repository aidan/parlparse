The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Geoffrey Hoon): May I begin by thanking you, Mr. Welsh, for joining us this afternoon and by welcoming you to the Chair? I know that Opposition Members have been waiting anxiously since 1 o'clock for my comments on their various amendments and suggestions, and I am delighted to have the opportunity of putting them out of their misery--only as far as the amendments are concerned, of course.

Mr. Ian Taylor (Esher and Walton): Not put down.

Mr. Hoon: That was the suggestion of the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway).

Amendments Nos. 17 and 63 would bring membership of business and professional associations as well as--in the case of amendment No. 63--guilds and masonic lodges, under the sensitive data unmbrella.

As Committee members will know, the Government's general approach to implementing the directive is to go no further than it requires. We want to protect individuals' personal data properly, but we do not want to do so at the cost of putting unnecessary additional burdens on data controllers. Clearly, that would involve extra costs for a wide range of businesses. It is precisely in the area of sensitive data that the directive imposes the greatest burdens. I hope that Opposition Members accept that we should be wary about adding to the categories of such data and that we should be extremely reluctant to do so.

Several Opposition Members mentioned the different approach to the directive as far as trade unionists are concerned. The Bill treats information about trade union membership as sensitive. My hon. Friend the Member for Broxtowe (Dr. Palmer) made the point that there was clear evidence that individuals had been discriminated against for their trade union membership. There is no clear evidence that members of the Ryedale rotary club have been discriminated against because of their membership of that club. That, in essence, is the distinction that I seek to draw. It is made in the directive, and the Government have faithfully carried it through into the Bill. There is no requirement in the directive to extend its terms to other categories, and the Government do not consider it necessary to extend the scope of the sensitive data provisions in the way that the hon. Members for Poole (Mr. Syms) and for Sheffield, Hallam (Mr. Allan) suggested.

Amendment No. 18 would amend the reference to health and sex life to follow more closely the wording in the directive. The Government intend to adopt a slightly different explanation in this case. There may be a temptation among Opposition Members to throw back at me the explanation that I have just set out, but there is a clear distinction between the two situations. The previous amendment would have added extra categories of information to the sensitive data regime; the wording used in the Bill in relation to health and sexual life clarifies the wording used in the directive. It is not our purpose to extend the sensitive data definition. The Government find the wording in the directive uncertain. We have sought throughout the Bill to find more effective and clearer definitions to assist those who will have to implement its terms when it is passed.

We know that the directive is intended to cover all the things mentioned in clause 2(e) and (f), but we thought it essential to spell that out. I hope that there is no dispute about the fact that data about our health and sexual lives are of a most deeply personal nature and that it is essential that we deal with them properly as the directive intends. We believe that that is what clause 2 now does.

One question was why the word ``condition'' is used in the Bill. There was a fear that, without the use of that word, the expression in clause 2(e) might not deal properly with the circumstances of a person who is disabled and permanently suffering from a specific condition that is not necessarily part of that person's general health. That may seem a fine distinction, but we judged it important that disability and health were covered.

For the benefit of the hon. Members for Esher and Walton and for Hallam, I confirm that the directive's reference to health data is intended to cover genetic data. Similarly, clause 2(e) is intended to include a specific reference to genetic data.

Finally, amendment No. 19 would remove information about criminal offences from the sensitive data category in clause 2. I hope that the Committee does not accept that that is necessary.

The hon. Member for Ryedale rightly mentioned the important anxieties expressed by City institutions and other organisations, including charities, about whether the Government will make an order under paragraph 9 to schedule 3 allowing those bodies to process sensitive data. We are aware of the problem and we shall listen sympathetically to the worries of organisations that are anxious to process data about criminal convictions.

Obviously, City institutions are keen to combat fraud, but charities such as the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals and the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds have also said that they need to be able to identify people who have committed offences against wildlife. We accept that there is a problem, and we shall handle sympathetically any approach with regard to it.

Clause 5

Application of Act

Mr. Hoon: I beg to move amendment No. 245, in page 3, line 37, at end insert--

    `(1A) A data controller falling within subsection (1)(b) must nominate for the purposes of this Act a representative established in the United Kingdom.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider Government amendment No. 246.

Mr. Hoon: The amendments make a small change to clause 5 to bring it within closer conformity and consistency with the data protection directive. Clause 5 establishes the rules for determining to which processing the Bill applies. The rules are based closely on those set out in article 4 of the directive. The basic rule, in subsection (1)(a), is that the Bill applies to processing done by a data controller who is establised in the United Kingdom. Other EEA member states should be making similar provision, as we heard in our debates this morning, in respect of their own data protection laws.

Not all controllers carrying out processing in the EEA will be established in the EEA. That possibility is addressed in clause 5(1)(b), which provides that the Bill applies to a controller who is established outside the EEA, but who uses processing equipment in the United Kingdom. An example might be an interactive computer network with equipment in the United Kingdom which is managed from the United States. That is in complete conformity with the directive. However, what the directive requires, but the Bill currently does not, is that controllers to whom clause 5(1)(b) applies must appoint a representative in the United Kingdom. The Government see the function of the representative as essentially a point of contact. He or she will be someone to whom data subjects can turn if they have problems with the processing in question. It will be easier, therefore, for data subjects to contact a person based in the United Kingdom rather than one who is, by definition, established outside the United Kingdom.

Mr. Greenway: Briefly, the amendments would improve the clause. A similar point is addressed by the next group of amendments, particularly by amendment No. 21. A problem exists in dealing with data processing that is done outside this country.

Mr. Hoon: The hon. Member for Ryedale has raised an important point, and the Government do not dismiss it lightly. We are considering the significant step of giving Parliament a role in the confirmation of appointments, and we are examining carefully how best to take the matter forward. However, such an approach would have wide implications across government, and it would be wrong to deal with the matter on the piecemeal basis that accepting the amendment would imply.

I shall begin by explaining how the Bill safeguards the position of the Data Protection Registrar and, in the future, the Data Protection Commissioner. Clause 6, which is supplemented by schedule 5, provides for the office of Data Protection Registrar to continue under the title of Data Protection Commissioner.

As a Crown appointment, the commissioner will only be removable from office by Her Majesty, and the Bill provides that that can only take place following an address from both Houses of Parliament. By these means, the commissioner will be distanced from the Government of the day and cannot be removed from office by the decision of a departmental Minister.

The Government are satisfied that the constitutional position of the commissioner, as set out in the Bill, meets the directive's requirement for the supervisory authority to be both ``public'' and ``independent''. In practice, commissioners will be appointed on the advice of the Prime Minister, who in turn will be advised by the Home Secretary. That method was used, with eminently satisfactory results, to appoint the present registrar and her predecessor under the 1984 Act. The Government are not persuaded that the amendment is either necessary or appropriate at this stage.

The first report of the Committee on Standards in Public Life, otherwise known as the Nolan Committee, recommended that ultimate responsibility for public appointments should remain with Ministers, the overriding principle being selection on merit. Parliament already has considerable powers to call office holders to account. The office of Data Protection Commissioner will be fully within the remit of the Commissioner for Public Appointments, whose own post was established in response to Nolan.

I hope that the hon. Member for Ryedale and other Conservative Members accept that those safeguards will provide a suitable standard to ensure that the Data Protection Commissioner is wholly objective and independent of Government.
