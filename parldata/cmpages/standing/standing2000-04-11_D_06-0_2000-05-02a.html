Government new clause 7-Specific authorities: general statutory duty.

New clause 1-Duty to promote equality-

    `For section 71 of the Race Relations Act 1976 there is substituted-

    ``71.-(1) Without prejudice to its obligation to comply with any other provision of this Act, a public authority specified in Schedule A1 or of a description falling within that Schedule shall in carrying out its varied functions have due regard to the need-

    (a) to eliminate unlawful racial discrimination;

    (b) to promote equality of opportunity between persons of different colour, race, nationality or ethnic or national origins; and

    (c) to promote good relations between persons of different racial groups.

    (2) Where an entry in Schedule A1 is limited to a person in a particular capacity, this section does not apply to that person in any other capacity.

    (3) Where an entry in Schedule A1 is limited to particular functions of a person, this section does not apply to that person in relation to any other functions.

    (4) The Secretary of State may by order amend Schedule A1 so as to extend the application of this section if he considers that the extension is limited to functions or particular functions of a public nature.

    (5) The Secretary of State may by order amend Schedule A1 in consequence of-

    (a) any person who falls within it, or an office or other post to which it relates, ceasing to exist or otherwise changing its nature;

    (b) any person who falls within it ceasing to exercise functions in a particular capacity or ceasing to exercise functions; or

    (c) any functions of a person falling within it appearing to the Secretary of State not to be functions of a public nature.

    (6) An order under this section may contain such incidental, supplementary or consequential provision as the Secretary of State considers appropriate (including provision amending or repealing provision made by or under an enactment).

    (7) The compliance of each public authority to which this section applies with the requirements of this section shall be subject to inspection and appraisal in accordance with regulations to be made by the Secretary of State.

    (8) The power conferred by subsection (7) on the Secretary of State is exercisable only after consultation with the Commission for Racial Equality and such other bodies or persons as the Secretary of State may consider appropriate in the particular case.

    (9) If it appears to the Commission for Racial Equality that a public authority to which this section applies has failed to take appropriate steps to comply with this section, the Commission may apply to a designated county court or sheriff court for an order requiring it to take such steps; and the court, if satisfied that the application is well-founded, may grant the order in the terms applied for or in more limited terms.''.'.

New clause 2-Positive duty-

    `-(1) For section 71 of the 1976 Act (local authorities: general statutory duty) there is substituted-

    ``71.-(1) Without prejudice to its obligations under any other section of this Act, every public authority shall carry out its functions with due regard to the need-

    (a) to avoid unlawful racial discrimination; and

    (b) to promote equality of opportunity and good relations between persons of different racial groups.

    (2) Every public authority specified in Schedule A2 shall publish a racial equality scheme showing how it proposes to fulfil the duties imposed by subsection (1).

    (3) A racial equality scheme that is published under subsection (2) shall conform to the requirements specified in Schedule A3.

    (4) A public authority to which subsection (2) applies shall collect and publish such information as is appropriate and necessary to facilitate the performance of the authority of its duty under subsection (1).

    (5) After each financial year a public authority to which subsection (2) applies shall publish a report containing-

    (i) a statement of the measures taken in compliance with its duties under this section;

    (ii) an assessment of how effective those arrangements were in promoting racial equality;

    (iii) a statement of further or alternative measure proposed for the next financial year.

    (6) The Secretary of State may by order impose such requirements on such public authorities as are not specified in Schedule A2 as he considers appropriate for the purpose of ensuring a better performance of their duties imposed by subsection (1).

    (7) Before making any such order, the Secretary of State shall consult the Commission and such other bodies, as he considers appropriate.

    (8) The Commission may issue codes of practice containing such practical guidance as the Commission think fit for the implementation by public authorities of the duties in this section.

    (9) Any code of practice issued under subsection (8) shall be taken into account by the Commission in relation to the exercise of its powers under clause (Compliance notice) below, and if provision of such a code appears to a court to be relevant to any question which it is asked to consider under that section it shall be taken into account in determining that question.

    (10) Schedule (Public authorities required to publish a racial equality scheme) (which inserts Schedule A2 into the 1976 Act) is to have effect.

    (11) Schedule (Racial equality schemes) (which inserts Schedule A3 into the 1976 Act) is to have effect.''.'.

    New clause 3-Compliance notice-

    `(1) If it appears to the Commission that a public authority has failed to take appropriate steps to comply with all or any of its duties under section (Positive duty) the Commission may in the prescribed manner serve on the authority a notice in the prescribed form (``a compliance notice'').

    (2) A compliance notice in subsection (1) above shall state-

    (a) the nature of the alleged non-compliance;

    (b) the evidence that is required to demonstrate adequate level of compliance;

    (c) the time by which such evidence should be produced to the Commission.

    (3) The Commission shall not serve a compliance notice in respect of any public authority unless they have first-

    (a) given the authority notice that they are minded to issue a non-compliance notice, the proposed terms of such notice and the grounds for the Commissions' belief that the authority has not complied with its duty under this section;

    (b) offered the authority an opportunity of making oral or written representations on this matter, and

    (c) taken account of the authority's representations.

    (4) If, following service of a compliance notice and after expiry of the time specified in the notice, the public authority has failed to provide the evidence specified in the notice the Commission may apply to a designated county court or sheriff court for an order requiring the authority to comply with the notice; and the court, if satisfied that the application is well founded, may make the order in the terms applied for or in more limited terms.

    (5) Not later than 6 weeks after compliance notice is served on a public authority the authority may appeal against any requirement of the notice to a designated county or a sheriff court.

    (6) Where the court considers a requirement in respect of which an appeal is brought under subsection (5) to be unreasonable for any reason, the court shall quash the requirement.

The amendments propose a positive statutory duty to promote racial equality and good race relations. Amendments Nos. 6 and 7 provide for the implementation by public authorities of that duty, for the preparation of a draft code of practice relating to its implementation and for consultation with the appropriate bodies before it is drafted.

It is no secret that the Commission for Racial Equality suggested the substantive proposal in new clause 1, that a new duty to promote equality should be substituted for section 71 of the Race Relations Act 1976. The central proposition is that there should be a duty on public authorities to have due regard to the need, first to eliminate unlawful racial discrimination and, secondly, to promote equality of opportunity between persons of different colour, race, nationality or ethnic or national origins.

How are public authorities to be defined? We have been once round that course already and we will need to debate whether the general definition, which the Government accepted in principle at our previous sitting, can be used or whether the individual public authorities to which the proposal applies should be listed. As my hon. Friend the Member for West Aberdeenshire and Kincardine (Sir R. Smith) and I have said, we do not have a problem with there being a checklist, so that people know that they are covered; but we are glad that the Government accept that there should be a general definition of public authority. We hope that that will apply to this clause as it does to clauses discussed earlier.

We set out in new clause 2 the positive-duty provision, that every public authority shall carry out its functions with due regard to the need to avoid unlawful racial discrimination and to promote equality of opportunity and good relations between persons of different racial groups.

New clause 3 would establish a mechanism for ensuring compliance. Government new clause 7 remains different in one important respect from our new clauses, which contain the provisions for which the CRE argued. As I understand it, the Government have no compliance provision yet, and there should be one. If it appeared to the commission that a public authority had not taken appropriate steps to comply with all or any of its duties under the section that places a positive duty on an authority, our new clause 3 would allow the commission to serve on the authority a notice in the prescribed form, which would contain statements on the non-compliance and other matters.

That measure follows the normal procedure that the CRE uses regularly for serving notices, which allows it to have teeth as well as a voice. Ensuring that public authorities do not discriminate directly or indirectly on racial grounds is the Bill's core proposal, and a compliance function would rightly ensure that authorities also comply properly with their positive duty to promote racial equality. The procedure that the CRE has used over the years seems right; there is no need to invent a new procedure. That would be complicated and confusing, and the current procedure seems to us and the CRE to work well.

New schedule 1 provides 10 general descriptions of the relevant Departments and public authorities. [ Interruption.] I shall just turn off my turned-off pager, which is none the less bleeping. New schedule 2 would insert new schedule A3 into the 1976 Act and set up racial equality schemes to assess how well people are doing. The schemes would place a duty on local authorities to assess their compliance with the duties under section 71 of the 1976 Act, to consult on matters relevant to those duties, to assess and consult on the likely impact of policies and to monitor any adverse impact of policies adopted.

I shall briefly set out the arguments for the proposal, because the Government, not least because of debate in the Lords, are seized of the appropriateness of imposing a positive duty, which they have adopted in part. The debate is therefore more on the detail than on the general proposition.

A positive statutory duty would ensure that authorities not only react to people who test their racial equality policies, but accept that promoting racial equality is a public benefit and is good service and employment practice. We seek to establish a culture change by inculcating the responsibility of being not passive, but active, particularly because public authorities are role models. The police were the central authority examined by the Lawrence inquiry, and if they can be a good role model and clearly accept their duty to promote equality, there will be a knock-on effect on society and on all those with whom they come into contact. The same applies to central and local government and all other public agencies. Under new clause 3, ``racial equality'' would be defined as

    equality of opportunity irrespective of membership or perceived membership of any racial group.

That definition would avoid any narrow categorisation and would apply to any public authority in the conduct of its business.

The amendments, including the new clauses and new schedules, draw on the experience gained from the Government of Wales Act 1998 and the Scotland Act 1998. As the Minister will know, the duty in relation to equality is much more advanced in Northern Ireland. As we have discussed previously in Committee, that is the one place in the United Kingdom that has a human rights commission that gives additional support to those who argue for equality of opportunity.

Under new clause 2, the duty would apply to every public authority not only in the exercise of its powers, but in the formulation and implementation of its policies and strategies. Every public authority would publish the scheme by which it sought to carry out its positive duty, so that people could have access to it for comment and criticism, to bring about improvements. It cannot be just tokenism: the scheme by which public authorities seek to secure compliance must be worked through and owned, as it were. People understand that that would work a little like a mission statement.

There should be a scheme that works out the impact of measures. It is no good having a scheme if it is not tried, tested and evaluated. Public services are becoming much better at doing that in a way that is not burdensome, but gives people regular review opportunities and criteria by which to review. That also allows people to learn lessons: if a policy is having an adverse impact, people can see that it is amended.

There should be regulations for secondary legislation. That must be the right way to proceed, so that we do not create clutter by leaving too much to primary legislation, while having the ability to take important measures through the parliamentary process.

It is proposed that information should be collected and published, which is necessary to facilitate the public authority in doing its job and to ensure that it is not acting unlawfully. The provision for an annual report is the normal proposal, with which we are all familiar. That could be the annual check, which is published. The report would set out the measures that have been taken, assess their impact and propose measures that might be taken in the year ahead.

In the implementation of the duty, public authorities would be subject to the same external audit and inspection proposals as are all other public authorities in the exercise of their duties. I am sure that the Government are happy about that proposition in principle. As I said, public services are becoming much more used to audit and inspection and benefit from it if the right procedure is implemented and works well. I hope that my colleagues do not find that controversial.

Under new clause 3, we have suggested a compliance proposal, which follows traditional CRE practice. Public authorities would have the ability to challenge compliance notices, although my experience is that they are served after quite a long process, which normally consists of correspondence, discussion and opportunities for people to amend practice if the CRE thinks that something may be wrong. Notices are very much the weapon not of first but last or penultimate resort. As far as I am aware, they have always been used responsibly and carefully, never precipitately. In addition, a procedure would allow the courts to get rid of notices, if the Scottish courts or the appropriate courts in England and Wales find that that is appropriate.

A number of issues could be covered by regulation: the scrutiny process, consultation, ethnic monitoring, inspection and audit, annual reporting and compliance notices. Lastly, a code of practice could be available to public authorities as a good model of how people should proceed.

The provisions will have a profound effect on our society, and it is right that we should consider the nature of the duties in detail. Proposed section 71(1) imposes the general duty on public authorities. It places such authorities, as specified in a new schedule, under a duty to make arrangements to secure that their functions are carried out with due regard to the need to eliminate unlawful discrimination and to promote equality of opportunity and good relations between persons from different racial groups.

I should tell the Liberal Democrats that, although their new clause contains a more detailed approach to racial groups, our version would include broadly the same terms. The wording of the duty largely reflects the wording of section 71 of the Race Relations Act. However, the present duty, which is only on local councils, can effectively be ignored by a council that considers it ``inappropriate''. As a result, it has had a limited and uneven impact in practice. We have tried to avoid that in the new general duty.

New section 71(2) provides that the Home Secretary shall make orders to impose specific duties on public authorities to ensure the better performance of the general duty. Much of the detail of how the general duty will operate in practice will depend on the content of those orders, and the Government want to consult on what should go into them. We have in mind, for example, duties to produce and publish equality plans and to be aware of the delivery of services and the ethnic origin of staff, perhaps by monitoring them.

I must stress that proportionality and flexibility will be important. It may be appropriate to place a particular requirement on a major Department of State or a local authority, but inappropriate to place it on a small public authority. That is why new section 71(3) allows orders made under new section 71(2) to be made in relation to some public authorities, but not others. It also allows different requirements to be placed on different public authorities or categories of public authority.

New section 71(5) makes provision to allow the schedule of public authorities to be amended.

New section 71A deals with immigration and nationality functions. Public authorities that carry out such functions will be subject to the general duty in so far as it pertains to eliminating unlawful racial discrimination and promoting good relations between persons from different racial groups. However, they will not be subject to the duty in so far as it requires the promotion of equality of opportunity between persons from different racial groups. That is because immigration and nationality policy, by its nature, denies opportunities to some nationalities that are offered to others. That is a basic requirement of immigration law, and we could not have a requirement to give people with British passports the same rights as those with passports from another country.

New section 71B deals with devolution issues. Under the Scotland Act 1998, the subject matter of the Race Relations Act is a reserved matter, with two exceptions: the encouragement, other than by prohibition or regulation, of equal opportunities and, in particular, the observance of equal opportunity requirements; and the imposition of duties on Scottish and cross-border public authorities in relation to Scottish functions. The new general duty may, therefore, impact on devolved areas of responsibility. It is proposed that the order-making powers in relation to the duty should be conferred on Scottish Ministers in line with the devolution settlement. I shall go into that in more detail if the hon. Member for West Aberdeenshire and Kincardine wants me to do so.

In relation to Wales, the new section provides that the Secretary of State shall consult the National Assembly for Wales before making regulations relating to cross-border authorities and make regulations relating to Welsh authorities only with the consent of the Welsh Assembly.

New section 71C makes provisions relating to codes of practice. The CRE already has the power to issue codes of practice that are approved by the Secretary of State in relation to employment and housing. The new section gives the CRE the power to issue codes of practice that have similarly been approved by the Secretary of State to provide guidance to public authorities on carrying out their duties to promote race equality.

The procedures for preparing, issuing and consulting on draft codes are based on the existing procedures for codes on employment and housing. So, too, are the procedures for Secretary of State approval of the codes, and for laying them before Parliament. The only difference is that the Secretary of State will be under a duty, in view of devolution, to consult Scottish Ministers and the National Assembly for Wales in deciding whether to approve or reject such codes.

Codes of practice will be able to give guidance to public authorities about how to fulfil their new duties. It is envisaged that they could provide examples of what public authorities might do to fulfil their duties, and encourage best practice. A breach of a code would not be actionable as such, but a code would be admissible in evidence where it is relevant to any issue in proceedings before a court.

New sections 71D and E make provision in relation to compliance and enforcement. Subsection D gives the CRE the power to issue a compliance notice if it is satisfied that a public authority has failed to comply with any duty imposed by regulations made by the Secretary of State under section 71(2). A compliance notice would require the public authority to comply with the duty and to inform the CRE of the steps that it had taken to do so. It could also require the public authority to give the CRE other information to verify that the duty had been complied with.

New section 71E gives the CRE the power to apply for a court order requiring a public authority to provide information required by a compliance notice if that authority has failed to do so, or if the CRE has reason to believe that it does not intend to do so. It also provides for the CRE to apply for a court order requiring a public authority to comply with a requirement of a compliance notice, where the commission considers that that public authority has not complied with it within three months of the compliance notice having been served. The court may grant the order in the terms applied for, or in more limited terms. The fallback position is that the court will be the final arbiter in terms of compliance, but the CRE can initiate action, if necessary.
