Mrs. Angela Browning (Tiverton and Honiton): I beg to move amendment No. 130, in page 8, line 12, after "form)", insert

    "having due regard for the schools responsibilities under the provision of the Data Protection Act 1984 and subsequent Acts protecting both computerised and manual data.".

I tabled the amendment because of our concern about the necessity for a member of the LEA or any other person directed by the Secretary of State to enter a school to access detailed records, either manual or computerised, and the way in which that information could be put into the public domain without the caveat of protection set out in the amendment. We know that when the Government take action, which may be appropriate, they do so with a flurry of publicity. The policy of naming, shaming and identifying schools and individuals should be protected under the Bill.

Under the current requirements, paragraph 7 of part II of schedule 1 of the Data Protection Act 1984 relates generally to each of the general principles that have the support of the European convention on human rights. It applies to personal data which "are held for historical, statistical or research purposes and not used in such a way that damage or distress is, or is likely to be, caused to any data subject".

I am glad that I was not a Member of Parliament when that provision was passed into law because it was so complicated.

The measure builds in protection for the individual. The Government may wish to make public the specific actions of the individual and the reasons why he or she had gone into a school and accessed information in line with the clause. Therefore, it is important that we receive reassurances from the Minister that the Data Protection Act and subsequent Acts would afford protection to the individual whose records may be accessed by another individual, who was authorised to do that by the Secretary of State.

The Minister for School Standards (Mr. Stephen Byers): I can understand the worry expressed by the hon. Lady, but it is not the Government's intention to use and publicise personal data that might be disclosed under the clause. The measure is about allowing an officer who has been instructed to do so to find relevant information and to use it for the purposes that are outlined in the clause. It is not a question of publicly using any information that might be disclosed.

Section 34(5)(a) of the Data Protection Act 1984 allows someone to disclose information as part of another person's discharging of his statutory obligation. That power already exists and we think that it should be used in the circumstances outlined in the clause. It is not our intention to use information, which the person who has been identified will receive, to gain publicity.

We are prepared, as a separate measure, to have a broader organisational machine we do not refer to it as naming and shaming, but as public accountability. The public has a right to know what is going on in its schools and schools should be assertive and confident. I assure the hon. Lady that we do not intend to allow to be made public any personal information that may be disclosed as a result of the powers in the clause.

Mrs. Browning: Under the circumstances, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Stephen Dorrell (Charnwood): I want to return to the theme that I spoke about on clause 7, just before we rose for lunch, namely, the effect of the Bill on the Secretary of State's powers. On several occasions I have made it clear to members of the Committee that the Bill over-centralises too much power too unconditionally in the hands of the Secretary of State. I hope that the Minister will specifically answer my questions which I ask in all seriousness about the clause. The powers accorded to the Secretary of State are extraordinarily sweeping and the circumstances in which he is empowered to use them are extraordinarily ill-defined.

I should like to take the Committee again through the powers that the clause gives the Secretary of State. New section 497A(1) states that "This section applies to a local education authority's functions (of whatever nature) which relate to the provision of education."

There is not much confining of discretion there the power relates to anything relevant to an LEA's discharge of its function for the provision of education. The teeth of the section is in subsection (2), which states: "If the Secretary of State is satisfied ... that a local education authority are failing in any respect to perform any function to which this section applies"

which, as we have seen, is every function for which it is responsible "he may exercise his powers under subsection (3) or (4)."

The circumstance in which the powers can therefore be used, within the terms of the statute, is wherever the Secretary of State is satisfied that an LEA is failing, "in any respect to perform any function to which this section applies".

There are very few human institutions that perform perfectly all the functions for which they are responsible. It seems almost inconceivable that there would at any time be any LEA in the land that did not fall within the terms of the subsection. If one goes through every function for which an LEA, however well run, is responsible I am on record as not being a huge admirer of the LEA system and even assuming that it[Mr. Stephen Dorrell] reached the highest ideal of perfection that the most starry-eyed enthusiast for the LEA on the Government Benches might hold, we will find somewhere in its function an element of it that falls short of perfection. That would be sufficient for the Secretary of State freely to use his powers under the subsection.

We have reached subsection (2) and there is so far no constraint whatever on the powers that the Secretary of State is given in the clause. What are those powers? They are set out in subsections (3) and (4). One of the questions that I hope the Minister will answer specifically is about the difference between the power accorded to the Secretary of State under subsection (3) and that accorded to him under subsection (4), because they are represented as being two separate powers. As I read them, they are both so sweeping that only one is necessary he can do anything under either of them.

Can the Minister explain the drafting? Subsection (3) states: "The Secretary of State may under this subsection direct an officer to secure that that function"

that is, the function that is failing "is performed in such a way as to achieve such objectives as are specified in the direction."

The Secretary of State can give a direction over the head of the LEA to the LEA's staff, specifying what he wants to do. The Secretary of State has the right to require the officers of an elected LEA to do his bidding, rather than that of the LEA.

Subsection (4) goes even further. It states: "The Secretary of State may under this subsection give an officer of the authority such directions as the Secretary of State thinks expedient for the purpose of securing that the function ... is performed"

and "is so performed in such a way as to achieve objectives".

The Secretary of State, in the defined circumstances that I have already described which seem to include almost all circumstances has under subsection (3) the power to give direct instructions to a member of staff of an LEA to do anything that the Secretary of State chooses, and under subsection (4) the power to direct an officer to do anything that the Secretary of State thinks expedient.

I want to know why the Secretary of State needs both those powers it is no obvious. I also want the Minister to tell us at what target this extraordinary scatter-gun power is aimed. Is the power like a cruise missile aimed at a specific target with radio guidance, or is it like the carpet bombing undertaken with the technology of the first world war?
