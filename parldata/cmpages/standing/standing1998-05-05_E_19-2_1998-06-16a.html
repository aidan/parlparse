Dawn Primarolo: Good evening, Mr. Gale. Before adjourning for dinner we were discussing amendments Nos. 274 and 280. The hon. Member for Bury St. Edmunds made two essential points--rebasing and election.

On rebasing, our intention in proposing changes to the taxation of offshore trusts was to ensure fairness. We believe that people with a close and continuing connection with the United Kingdom who have set up or benefited from an offshore trust should pay their fair share of tax. Our proposals close the gaps left by the previous Administration when they undertook their changes. It would be ironic, but not, I am sure, their intention, if the Opposition had tabled amendments that would allow other loopholes to emerge. That is precisely what the amendments would do. They would allow gains that have accrued within an offshore trust set up before 17 March 1998 but which have not yet been realised to fall out of the charge in our proposals. The amendments go further and would allow gains, which would otherwise be chargeable under existing rules, to escape tax altogether.

That may go beyond the intentions of the hon. Member for Bury St. Edmunds. I am not sure whether it was his intention, but that would be the effect of the amendments and I could not accept such a proposal. They would prevent an effective tax charge on those who set up or benefit from an offshore trust, and they would go further and open up new opportunities to escape a tax charge under provisions introduced by a Conservative Government.

Mr. Ruffley: How would the amendment make people exempt under the transitional provisions and make them liable for tax for which they would not have been liable under the old regime?

Dawn Primarolo: Amendment No. 274 would allow all the assets held by offshore trusts that are brought onshore before 5 April 2000 to be rebased on their open market values at 17 March 1998. That would cause further problems, as I explained. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman is not suggesting that he does not accept my explanation. He knows that I have not been slow to rebut amendments that the Government simply cannot accept.

On election, the amendments would provide an election for the trustees or the beneficiaries of an offshore trust set up before 19 March 1991 that is not brought onshore before 5 April 2000 to be charged in respect of gains realised by the trust instead of the settlor. Clause 130 brings trusts set up before 19 March 1991 within the scope of an existing charge on settlors where they, their immediate family or companies that they control can benefit from the trust. You, Mrs. Dunwoody, implored the hon. Member for Bury St. Edmunds not to venture into that subject until we reached clause 130, and I shall not do so. The charge was introduced by the Conservative Government in 1991 but did not apply to existing trusts unless, in broad terms, the funds were added or the trusts had migrated. We did not think that those who set up trusts before 1991 should continue to be in an advantageous position and therefore proposed that they should be brought within the charge on the settlor.

For those reasons, we reject the amendment. I call on the Committee to oppose it.

When we debated amendment No. 257 a number of questions were left unanswered. The debate on this amendment, which I hope will be short, will give the Financial Secretary an opportunity to answer my points.

The clause brings into tax UK beneficiaries of trusts based abroad that were set up by non-UK resident settlors. That is an acceptable intention, but it has already been established that the clause will also bring into the UK tax net a number of foreign trusts set up by people who never dreamt that the trusts would have a United Kingdom beneficiary. Those trusts have therefore not kept the necessary records from which to calculate capital gains tax. The clause is based on the assumption that the trustees of such overseas trusts will be able to provide information that they do not have. I gave the example of an American trust, which was set up many years ago without any tax avoidance motive and has acquired a UK beneficiary. That UK beneficiary will now become liable to capital gains tax on distributions paid to him or her. In order to compute the capital gain, that person will have to obtain from the trustees information stretching back to perhaps a century ago. It is not inconceivable that the trust will have shares in an unquoted company, the base cost of which may be lost in the mists of time. Therefore, it is simply unrealistic to expect that that information will be forthcoming. The trustees have had no previous connection with this country; they never dreamt that they would have to provide information to the United Kingdom Inland Revenue; they have paid tax in the United States for many years.

Mr. Ruffley: It would be helpful to remind the Committee of what the clause will do. Gains in respect of offshore trusts with settlors domiciled in the UK, and thus UK residents for tax purposes, will be charged to tax, provided that the settlors have an interest in the trust. Such an interest is defined as an interest of the settlor, or of the family of the settlor, or of companies that the settlor controls--in other words, an interest is defined as when the settlor is a beneficiary under the trust.

That was not the case before 1991. Offshore trusts then enjoyed that tax advantage because the Conservative Government of the time decided--for policy reasons that the Financial Secretary was not prepared to discuss--that the hugely successful and important venture capital industry should, for technical reasons, be able to take advantage of existing offshore arrangements.

There were two reasons for that. First, professional trustees resident in this country would bring the trust onshore. That was perceived to be a deterrent to onshore trusts, so trusts went offshore. Secondly, the regime in operation before limited partnerships were introduced meant that there was more incentive to go offshore.

The net result was that the offshore regime was important for the venture capitalists who did so much to generate a truly entrepreneurial spirit in what many of us consider to be the high water mark of modern British politics. This country thrived: it was the sick man of Europe when the Conservative Government were first elected. We turned the country around, not least by means of our low-tax, entrepreneurial policies, which have given the Labour Government the golden economic legacy that they seem so determined to fritter away.

I notice a scowl on your face, Mrs. Dunwoody. I merely want to explain why pre-1991 trusts enjoyed that tax treatment. Clause 130 will charge capital gains tax against current offshore trusts with a settlor who has an interest in the trust. The charge will apply to all gains, not just those realised after Budget day, as the Financial Secretary seemed to imply. The charge will also hoover up any gains made before Budget day. Capital gains going back 10 years or more may have crystallised after that day, and that is why we consider the clause to be unjustifiably penal.

Amendment No. 275 refers to the transitional arrangements. The Government say that those arrangements will benefit trusts subject to the clause. One of the reasons that they adduce is that those who will be affected by clause 130 will be able to reorganise their affairs. One of the ways in which they can do that, we are told, is to wind up the trust. Alternatively, the beneficiaries can give up any interest that they have in the trust. Dawn Primarolo: I hope that the Committee will bear with me, because it is important that I put on the record the Government's position on each amendment in this large group.

Clause 130 brings trusts that were set up before 19 March 1991 within the scope of the charge on the settlor in respect of gains realised by the trustees. The charge applies only where the settlor, the settlor's spouse, their children, their children's spouses and companies that they control can benefit from the trust. Generally, the clause will apply only to gains realised by the trustees on or after 6 April 1999, which gives a transitional period for those affected to re-organise their affairs if they so wish by, for example, winding up the trust, bringing it onshore or removing themselves and their family from benefit.

However, we recognise that some beneficiaries will not have the legal capacity to remove themselves from benefit--children under the age of 18, for example. For that reason, we have been careful to exclude from this charge trusts where the only members of the settlor's family who can benefit from the trust are people who cannot remove themselves from benefit. Clause 130 creates a class of protected settlements and prevents the charge from applying to such settlements. However, protected settlements are not limited to cases in which the immediate members of the settlor's family cannot remove themselves from benefit. It also includes trusts whose beneficiaries do not include members of the settlor's immediate family--the settlor, the settlor's spouse, children or spouses of children.

The purpose of the amendments is to extend the categories of persons who are within the definition of a protected settlement. Some of the amendments are unnecessary because they cover categories of persons who are not immediate members of the settlor's family and so already fall within the definition of a protected settlement. The others are inappropriate as they cover categories of persons who have the capacity to remove themselves from benefit, and I can see no reason for including them in the protected category. Perhaps it will assist the Committee if I refer to each amendment and state into which category the class of beneficiaries falls.

Amendment No. 97 covers children of a former spouse of a settlor. The amendment is unnecessary because such people already fall within the definition of a protected settlement. Amendment No. 98 covers unborn children of a former spouse of a settlor. The amendment is unnecessary because such people already fall within the definition of a protected settlement.

Amendment No. 99 covers future spouses of a child of a former spouse of a settlor. The amendment is unnecessary because such people fall within the definition of a protected settlement. Amendment No. 100 covers current spouses of a child of a settlor. The amendment is inappropriate. Such persons have the capacity to remove themselves from benefit and should not fall within the definition of a protected settlement.

Amendment No. 229 covers children of a settlor over the age of 18. The amendment is inappropriate. Conditional exemption has been around for 102 years, since 1896. The idea was to allow families and individuals who were prepared to do so to open up their houses or the objects contained within them to public access; in return, the owners made a contract with the Inland Revenue under which they would be exempted from inheritance tax. It was possible for the heirs of the owners of those objects to apply to pay no duty or tax on the death of the owner, but to pay tax only if the objects were sold.

In 1930, that arrangement was revised so that tax was charged on the value of the objects when sold rather than their value at the date of death. Since 1949, it has been essential for owners who have been given that conditional exemption to promise that they would offer safekeeping for those objects; and from 1975, they had to guarantee that there would be some public access.

The amendments to clause 140 go to the heart of the matter. We are not talking about objects that are not accessible to the public. The objects are already available for public inspection. They are not hidden away; any member of the Committee, any Member of the House of Commons and any member of the British public can find out about and see those objects. The principle of access is already firmly established. The objects are now accessible to the general public.

The amendments tackle the problem predicted by many organisations, including the National Trust and the major art houses, which all stand to benefit. If Sotheby's and Christie's handle the sale of such objects, they will make large commissions. They are warning us of the dangers, but they have an interest; they are absolutely confident that those items will come onto the market.

We are talking about 20,000 items which are regarded as being of such historical, national or scientific interest that they have been placed on the register of conditionally exempt objects--otherwise known as the V and A list. Anyone can consult that list at the V and A, and it is available on the internet. Some members of the Committee might have taken advantage of that information, others might have an object on the list.

All owners of such objects must show them on demand to the public. Under the exemption, the public have a right to see those objects. If one believes in the importance of our national heritage, it is crucial to ensure that the public can see them.

The Bill refers to objects of national, scientific, historic or artistic interest. Until now, the capital taxes office has interpreted that to mean objects of sufficiently high quality to be displayed in public collections--whether national, local authority or university. Undoubtedly, some secondary objects have gone onto the list--by which I mean that not everything on the V and A list ought to be there, as everyone who works in the field acknowledges.

No one is saying that there should not be a major revision of the V and A list. No one is saying that we should not reconsider the criteria by which objects go on to the list or are made accessible to the public. However, the clause and the Government's failure properly to consult on it represent a sledgehammer to crack a walnut. As a matter of principle, all the major art bodies throughout the country are saying that the Government have got it wrong and that they need to reconsider the clause.

The retrospective nature of the clause is worrying. In effect, taxpayers now have a contract between themselves and the Inland Revenue. In return for meeting that contract, they receive an exemption for a set of undertakings. I have referred to those undertakings, which are principally based on the idea that the objects must be publicly accessible, having been accepted as of eminent quality. So long as the taxpayer retains the property and keeps it in good condition, the tax is payable only on a breach of those undertakings.
