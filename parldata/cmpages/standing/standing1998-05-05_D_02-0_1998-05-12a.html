The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Geoffrey Hoon): The hon. Member for Ryedale has explained that the amendments relate to the inclusion in the Bill of the words "automatic" or "automatically", which are important in legislation about data protection. People often use the shorthand term "computerised data" but, as the hon. Member for Ryedale would accept, that is just one type of automatically processed data. The introduction of digital technology means that many more types of information processing come under the scope of the term. Nowadays, for example, CCTV could well be included in a definition of processed information. The use of the word, "automatic" and its various derivatives is not new to this area of the law. It is widely used term which is well understood, both in our law and in various international instruments. The Council of Europe convention on data protection, on which our 1984 legislation was based, talks about "data undergoing automatic processing" and defines that term by reference to operations carried out "by automated means". The data protection directive, to which the Bill gives effect, also refers to processing by "automatic means".

Thus, the concept of processing being carried out "by automatic means" or "automatically" is an essential feature of the definition of the activity on which data protection law has been focused. There is nothing new in the precise form of words that the Bill uses. It reproduces, virtually word for word, a similar definition in the Data Protection Act 1984, which reads:

    "`Data' means information recorded in a form in which it can be processed by equipment operating automatically in response to instructions given for that purpose".

So far as I am aware, that definition has given rise to no difficulty in the 14 or so years since the 1984 Act came into effect. It is a tried and tested formula, which captures precisely what the Bill is intended to cover, and the Government see no good reason for changing it.

In the light of that historical survey of the use of the word "automatic" and its derivatives, I hope that the hon. Member for Ryedale will feel able to withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Howarth: I shall say a brief word of introduction. The amendments are intended to give effect to Gaskin in respect of the records to which the Access to Personal Files Act 1987, the Access to Health Records Act 1990 and corresponding Northern Ireland legislation apply. They will bring those records within the regime that the Bill will set up. The amendments deal with rights of access to information held in unstructured, manual records to which the provisions of the Bill would not otherwise apply. Those records relate to local authority housing, social services and health.

The amendments are numerous and lengthy. I apologise for that, but they seek to make technically complex and intricate provisions. I am sorry if the Committee finds them difficult to follow. I shall try to simplify them, and their underlying purpose is relatively straightforward. The amendments will consolidate the access rights given in other legislation that I have mentioned, and thereby give effect in respect of records relevant to the ECHR judgment in the case of Gaskin. The Bill already gives effect to that judgment in relation to some records, both automated and manual. The same should be done for other records to which there is a right of access and which do not currently come within the scope of the Bill.

The Gaskin judgment relates to refusal of subject access. It says that when access is refused because of the risk of identifying third parties that have not consented, a mechanism must exist for independent review of the refusal.

The Bill deals with that matter in two ways. When access has been refused, data subjects may seek a court order requiring access to be given or they may request an assessment to be made by the Data Protection Commissioner, who has power to take necessary enforcement action. To the extent that public bodies such as local authorities and NHS trusts hold records likely to be caught by the Gaskin judgment, the Bill does the job for automated or manual records that come within its scope. Those bodies also hold personal records, however, accessible under the Acts that I have mentioned, to which the effect of the Gaskin judgment applies, but which are not covered by the Bill. To deal with that problem, and because we wish to meet the UK's ECHR obligation in full in this case, we have decided to bring those additional records into the scope of the Bill. That is the purpose of the amendments.

It may be helpful for me to explain broadly how the package of amendments works. I hope that the Committee will agree that I should concentrate on the principal amendments rather than explaining each point in detail. All the points are interrelated.

For simplicity, I will describe the arrangements by references to legislation that applies in Great Britain. However, comparable provision is already made for Northern Ireland. The additional records to which we wish the Bill's regime to apply are brought within its scope by amendment No. 149, that seeks to add to the definition of "data" in clause 1 a new limb comprising "accessible records". Those are defined in new clause 1; they include certain health records and "accessible public records" as defined in new schedule 1.

The wording of the definition of "health records" is substantively the same as that used in the Access to Health Records Act 1990. "Accessible public records" are essentially those to which the Access to Personal Files Act 1987 applies, particularly local authority housing and social services records.

Amendment No. 2, new clause 1 and new schedule 1 are thus the key provisions to bring the relevant records within the scope of the Bill in so far as they are not already included within the existing broadening of the definition of "data".

I made a concession to the hon. Member for Ryedale in saying that he has got pretty near to a definition which the Government could accept. But inevitably, with all the resources available to the Government, the definition that we suggest is preferable.

The list of sensitive personal data in clause 2 is based on article 8(1) of the directive, which refers to religious or philosophical beliefs. The Government deliberately did not include a reference to philosophical beliefs in the Bill because of the ambiguity of the expression. Its meaning is clear enough in the directive, but its use in a United Kingdom Act of Parliament might well not be.

The Government seek to avoid writing into law a definition of beliefs that applies only in a rather academic sense. We do not, for example, believe it to be appropriate for the legislation to cover the ideas of Jean Paul Sartre or Bertrand Russell. It would not be right for such ideas to be considered as sensitive data; that would be inconsistent with the purpose of the directive. The reference to philosophical beliefs is intended to catch beliefs that are similar to religious beliefs, but which cannot be described as religious. Humanism is an example, which is why the Government initially preferred the expression, "religious or other beliefs". Having given the matter further thought, however, we accept that that definition might be a little to wide. It is argued that such a definition could let in beliefs whose nature is very different from that of religious beliefs. For example, it could include a belief in the value of overseas aid, or in the need for a strong pound. I recognise that Labour Members probably have greater commitment to such beliefs than Opposition Members. We are anxious to make allowance for such concerns.

The Government amendment will deal with the matter by making it clear that the other beliefs must be similar in nature to religious beliefs. The amendment covers all that the directive requires, but without the ambiguity and uncertainty that reference to philosophical beliefs might entail.
