Mr. Michael: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for that comment. Opposition Members have made serious points in a number of debates and we have tried to respond to them.

The amendments are designed to provide greater clarity on how multiple sentences imposed on a prisoner relate to one another. They respond to calls from the courts for a clear statutory framework. They amend section 51(2) of the Criminal Justice Act 1991. It provides that sentences that are consecutive or wholly or partly concurrent are to be treated as a single term for the purposes of part II of that Act, which deals with the early release of prisoners.

New clause 25 will provide that a sentence imposed by a court after a prisoner has been released but while he is still within the overall term of the original sentence should not be aggregated with that sentence. At present, sentences are aggregated when a prisoner has been released and is then returned to custody. That can produce anomalies. An offender may have to be released almost immediately despite the imposition of a lengthy new sentence. We are removing that anomaly to ensure that the effect of any sentence is clear to the court, the offender, the victim and to the public.

For sentences that are not to be aggregated, a prisoner cannot be released if he is serving more than one term until he has completed the custodial part of the longest term that he is serving. The same principle applies in respect of consideration for release on parole, the length of licence period following release and the length of the period for which the released prisoner is liable to return to prison under section 40 of the 1991 Act.

I should like to draw the Minister's attention to a serious anomaly in the Bill through the interaction of clauses 69 and 70, relating to the time spent on remand. As members of the Committee will know, under the existing rules, the time spent on remand by an offender awaiting sentence is reflected in the length of time he spends in custody after sentence.

The distinguished reader in criminal justice and the editor of "Current Sentencing Practice", David Thomas, with whom members of the Committee are already familiar, has written to me on the subject. He is worried that, under the Government's proposals for detention and training orders, time spent by young offenders on remand will be treated differently from time spent on remand by other offenders. He thinks that that might create anomalies.

David Thomas wrote that he had

    "spotted another problem which arises from the interaction of the treatment of time spent in custody on remand and the main provision governing the determination of the term of the Order ... Clause 69(5) requires the term of the Order to be chosen from the periods specified in the clause. Clause 70(6) provides that `in determining the term of the Detention and Training Order for an offence, the court shall take account of any period for which the offender has been remanded in custody in connection with the offence, or any other offence'. This clearly means that in determining the term of the Detention and Training Order, the court must deduct from the term of the Order a period equivalent to the period which the offender has spent in custody on remand. Bearing in mind that under Clause 71(2) the normal period of detention will be one half of the term of the Order the court in order to comply with Clause 70(6) must deduct from its initial determination of the term of the Order a period equal to twice the period spent in custody on remand.

    This is a mandatory obligation. The court will have great difficulty in complying with it as a result of Clause 69(5). Suppose the court has decided that the appropriate term of an order in the case of a 17-year-old, having regard to the nature of the offence and any other mitigation that may apply, would be 18 months. The defendant has spent seven weeks in custody on remand and the court must therefore make an appropriate allowance (in principle 14 weeks) for that period. The appropriate sentence will therefore be fourteen and a half months or thereabouts; but this is not a lawful sentence, and the court must therefore reduce the sentence to 12 months, thereby giving the defendant exaggerated credit for the time spent in custody. It is possible to envisage any number of anomalies of this kind."

Professor Thomas goes on to say that

    "it seems wholly unnecessary to introduce a rule for treating remand time in connection with a Detention and Training Order which is different from the rules which apply to all other custodial sentences."
