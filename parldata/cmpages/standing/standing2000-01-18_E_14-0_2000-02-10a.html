Dr. Gavin Strang (Edinburgh, East and Musselburgh): The clause accompanies one that we debated, briefly but usefully, in an earlier sitting. It concerns the major issue in the Bill-that of security. That is undoubtedly the reason why other Governments have not privatised air traffic control. Although Conservative Members have not been drawn on the issue, I suspect that that is also why the previous Administration never got round to privatising the service, notwithstanding the huge programme of privatisation that took place under the two previous Prime Ministers.

There is a conflict-a tension-between civil aviation and military needs. The great strength of the British system has been the way in which that conflict has been handled. It is not simply a question of our being better than France, as some might say. We are probably the best in the world. Our success is based on our experience and our structures, on the fact that the military controllers work alongside civil controllers in the same premises, and on the public sector.

The relationship between a Government official and a publicly-owned company-which might be totally separate from the Civil Aviation Authority, but that is not the issue-is different from that between a Government official and a private company. There would be less incentive for co-operation between the military sector and a partially privatised air traffic control service. That would also apply in the case of a fully privatised, monopoly provider of air traffic control. The Conservatives still seem to advocate that option and the Minister has not ruled out the possibility of a flotation in the longer term.

Perhaps I should not say too much on that matter, because it would be a mistake to pre-empt the debate on clause 81. Whatever instructions may be given in relation to United Kingdom airspace under clause 65 would be completely overridden by the provisions in clause 81, as it is vital to protect the military interest. The first pressure will arise through an increase in traffic. I have heard people say that military pilots should not be training in Britain and that they should go and train somewhere else. It has been achieved because the service is in the public sector, but even in that sector it will come under more pressure because of the huge growth in traffic. Only yesterday the latest figures were published in the Financial Times. The military requirement is important. That is why, as I think everyone who has spoken so far has acknowledged, it is vital that co-operation is not jeopardised.

Co-operation will also come under more pressure in time-I accept that it will not happen overnight-because of privatisation. Let us be blunt about it. It does not matter who the strategic partner is-although we may say a little more about that on clause 81. In spite of the safeguards that are in place on security and other issues, if a private-sector animal is to run the business commercially, pressure will be put on it. The dangers of the Bill are not necessarily immediate, but in time people develop different agendas. The company will have an obligation to its shareholders and to its owners, who may be anywhere in the world. No doubt we shall discuss that on clause 81. There will be a new element-an element that says, ``We must get the highest private profit that we reasonably can out of this operation for our owners.'' I have not read his remarks, because we do not yet have the Official Report from Tuesday, but he appeared to be saying that there is unlikely to be a problem. The other-I accept that this is not necessarily the view of the Committee as a whole-is the inevitable tension between a group of people who are employed and answerable to an organisation that does not necessarily sign up completely to the British way of life, if I may use that phrase to include civil rights, parliamentary democracy and so on.

The Minister for Housing and Planning (Mr. Nick Raynsford): To help the Committee I shall give a brief outline of the current arrangements for levying charges through Eurocontrol and the agreements affecting the Shanwick oceanic control area and air traffic services provided by other Governments. I shall then explain how the Bill will affect those arrangements.

The existing arrangements involve charges being levied under section 73(1)(a) of the Civil Aviation Act 1982, which authorises the Secretary of State to make regulations requiring the payment to him, the CAA or Eurocontrol of air navigation charges provided by him, the CAA, Eurocontrol or any other person. Enforcement mechanisms, including the powers of aircraft detention and sale, are contained in section 74(4) and (5) of the 1982 Act.

Three sets of regulations apply under the provisions of the 1982 Act. The most important are the route charges regulations, which require the operator and, on notice, the owner of any aircraft to which navigation services are made available in respect of each en-route flight in the flight information regions of UK airspace, to pay Eurocontrol a charge for those services. Those charges are then passed on to NATS.

The navigation services charges regulations require three kinds of charges to be specified. First, every aircraft flying across the Shanwick oceanic control area and filing a flight plan pays a charge to the CAA. Secondly, every aircraft pays the CAA the appropriate charge for navigation services provided in connection with landing at Heathrow, Gatwick, Stansted, Edinburgh, Glasgow and Aberdeen-that is, the six BAA airports. Thirdly, every helicopter flying within the specified areas of the northern and southern North sea pays a charge to the CAA.

The joint financing regulations require every aircraft making a crossing between Europe and America to pay the CAA a charge for air navigation services that are provided by the Governments of Denmark and Iceland. Until 1 February this year, the CAA also collected such charges in respect of Canada. I am afraid that I must apologise for an unfortunate, but understandable, typographical error in the explanatory notes. Paragraph 57, which describes those arrangements, refers to the Governments of Canada, Denmark and Ireland. Unfortunately, an `r' has been substituted for a `c'; it should read Iceland, not Ireland. I hope that hon. Members will excuse us for that typographical error and understand how easily that kind of thing can happen.

Mr. Jenkin: Resign!

Mr. Raynsford: I thank the hon. Gentleman for his kind comments on my performance.

On the last point-not, I hasten to say, resignation-perhaps I might usefully explain what lies behind the collection of those charges. As I said, the collection of charges in respect of the Government of Canada stopped on 1 February. Charging on behalf of Denmark and Iceland continues. The reason for that is simple. Most of the large volume of air traffic flying through the airspace of Denmark and Iceland is in transit. For that reason, neither Iceland nor Denmark has any means of taking action if charges are not paid. In many cases, the airlines will not have landing arrangements at any Danish or Icelandic airport. The ability to seize and sell an aircraft is not available, whereas the vast majority of the operators-probably all of them-will have landing arrangements in the UK, so it is easier for us to act as the enforcement agent, which we do. We collect the fees on their behalf, and charge a small collection fee for that administrative service. That is helpful, because it reduces costs for UK airspace users.

Clauses 66 to 70 propose the minimum necessary changes to those arrangements post-PPP. The guiding considerations are: first, that the legal basis on which NATS charges and recovers for en-route Eurocontrol and Shanwick air navigation services will continue to be secure, clear and unambiguous; secondly, that the international obligations of the UK will continue to be met; thirdly, that the existing powers of detention and sale, which enable debts to be enforced and recovered, will remain robust; and fourthly, that the Government and the CAA will each retain the appropriate functions, but only those which are required to be carried out in the public sector.

The charging, recovery and enforcement mechanisms will be underpinned by a new obligation in primary legislation for users to pay the relevant charges. Given such an obligation, it is no longer necessary for charges to be promulgated by the Secretary of State. Previously, the regulations promulgated by the Secretary of State provided for the obligation to make a payment. That is now enshrined in primary legislation, which is why the Secretary of State does not need to promulgate those arrangements through regulations.

In practice, the instruments serve mainly to give national legal effect to charges set by others.

I shall simply explain why we do not believe that the amendment is necessary.

The first element of the amendment makes the Secretary of State, rather than the CAA, responsible for setting charges and for doing so by order, no doubt out of a concern that the legal basis for the setting and recovery of charges should be no less robust than at present. That is the key policy consideration, which I have already outlined, behind the proposed new arrangement. The present arrangement, based on the Civil Aviation Act 1982, operates by primary legislation giving power to the Secretary of State to make secondary legislation or regulations. Once made, a regulation is the source of the obligation to pay, in addition to being the description of the charges. It is envisaged that, for the new framework, the primary legislation, which is subsection (3), should be the legal source of the obligation to pay.

With that general obligation in place, the amounts of the charges, the persons by and to whom the charges are payable and the airspace in respect of which they are so payable will then fall to be identified. That will be done by the CAA issuing notices under clause 67(1). The notices will not, of themselves, create substantive legal obligations, but will designate the tariff to which the statutory obligation to pay attaches. The tariffs will specify the charges due and will be regularly updated and amended.

The level of charges will be set, not by the CAA, but by the relevant providers-Eurocontrol for UK en-„route charges, foreign Governments for Denmark and Iceland, and NATS for oceanic and, for the present, BAA airports and North sea helicopters, for the reasons that I explained.

I hope that that answers the second element of the hon. Gentleman's amendment, which requires the Secretary of State to promulgate these charges. The proposed charging framework recognises the practical reality that the service provider is responsible for proposing its charges within the relevant regulatory parameters, whether set under the proposed charge controls for Eurocontrol and oceanic charges, the Eurocontrol charging principles, the ICAO approvals or the requirements of overseas Governments on whose behalf they are collected. Compliance with all those requirements is the direct responsibility of NATS. Therefore, unless there is clear and present evidence of those parameters being breached, the CAA will be able to promulgate only those charges in its notices. It would clearly be acting beyond its powers if it refused to promulgate the charges in that way for any other reason, knowing that the effect of so doing would frustrate the ability not only of NATS but of all the other parties involved to recover their legitimate charges.

In summary, the amendment is unnecessary because, although we understand its underlying motives, they are fully met in the Bill. Therefore, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will agree to withdraw it.
