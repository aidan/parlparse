Clause 60

Uniform prices etc. in certain areas of Scotland

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): I beg to move amendment No. 778, in page 61, line 41, leave out ``to or''.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss Government amendment No. 779.

Dr. Howells: These are both technical amendments. The first removes the words ``to or'' so that subsection (2) more accurately refers to a comparable supply of electricity in a specified area. The second amendment inserts the words

    or any particular condition of contract

at the end of subsection (3) to ensure that it reads consistently with subsection (2), to which it relates.

Subsection (2) gives the Secretary of State the power to require the holder of a licence to charge prices or to offer conditions of contract in respect of a comparable supply of electricity in a specified area. At present, subsection (3) limits that power by stating that the Secretary of State cannot use it to order any particular or maximum charge or price. The amendment extends the limitation so that the Secretary of State, in exercising that power, cannot stipulate any particular condition or contract.

Amendment agreed to.

It was commendable that the Act attempted that difficult task and succeeded in it.

The Secretary of State for Scotland made an order in 1990, specifying the area in question to be that covered by Scottish Hydro-Electric-now called Scottish and Southern-which lies to the north of a line drawn roughly from the Clyde to the Tay: the north and west of Scotland.

Since the war, successive Governments have taken pains to ensure that electricity was made available to nearly all the remote communities in the north and west of Scotland. The issue at stake in 1990 was the need to ensure that these remote communities, after privatisation of the industry, continued to be charged uniform prices for electricity. Section 3(2)(a) secure that end, under what came to be known as the common tariff obligation, which regulates the charging of electricity prices, which are averaged across all of the specified area-including Aberdeen and Dunee-to the benefit of the remote communities in the north and west of Scotland.

Clause 61

Maximum prices for reselling electricity

Dr. Howells: I beg to move amendment No. 780, in page 63, leave out lines 1 to 3.

The Government amendment is a technical one, changing clause 61 by deleting the definition of ``authorised supplier'', which means someone authorised either by a licence or by an exemption to supply electricity. That definition was thought to be useful for the clause, but was also used elsewhere in the Bill. Therefore it should be put into a general interpretations provision of the Electricity Act. That insertion is done by amendment No. 631, which adds the definition to the part of schedule 5 that amends the Electricity Act's interpretation provisions. The definition is redundant here and should be removed.

Amendment agreed to.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Ian Bruce: Will the Minister explain the purpose of the clause and how it will change the current position?

Dr. Howells: With pleasure.

Existing provisions in the Electricity Act 1989 and the Gas Act 1986 share the overall objective of preventing excessive prices from being charged when electricity or gas is resold-for example, by landlords to tenants-but differ in their details. The clause, together with clause 82, which is next in the order of consideration, reconciles the differences so that the powers granted to the regulatory authority are the same for the two fuels, except for the exemption of gas used for propelling motor vehicles. I know that that is important to the hon. Member for South Dorset (Mr. Bruce), who is concerned about kebab vans.

The clause makes the power in electricity more flexible. Instead of having to fix prices, the regulatory authority will be able to set a formula by which maximum prices will be calculated. The authority may set an interest rate to be paid on sums charged in excess of the maximum price, which would be in addition to the value of the overpayment itself, as at present; it can require resellers to provide information on prices to purchasers; and it can direct that the maximum price must be reduced by an amount or percentage if the reseller fails to comply with that requirement.

Those changes will align the electricity regime with the relevant aspects of the current gas regime. The clause updates the existing provision to give the authority greater flexibility to protect consumers.

Clause 61, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Dr. Howells: The Government agree that the amendment is important, and take it seriously. As the hon. member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) has told us, the Electricity Act 1989 provides that regulations made by the Secretary of State are subject to parliamentary control, but that those made by the regulatory authority are not subject to such procedures. That lies at the heart of what the hon. Gentleman is saying. In addition, they are scrutinised by the Joint Committee on Statutory Instruments. The amendment would also subject the authority's regulations to negative assent. Technically, that would render the clause unnecessary because it would no longer effect a significant change to the Gas Act 1986, but it would leave unchanged the Electricity Act 1989, which does not require negative assent for the authority's regulations. The two regimes would be unaligned in that respect.

Dr. Howells: I take this amendment seriously. It is important and covers a sensitive area. I realise that the spirit of the amendmentis to ensure co-ordination and consistency between the authority and the Health and Safety Commission over the regulation of, respectively, the non-safety and safety aspects of gas quality. However, given the various provisions-existing and future-to deal with the matter, the amendment is unnecessary.

The authority and the Secretary of State will have two relevant duties. First, under clause 10 they will have a duty to consult the Health and Safety Commission on all gas safety issues, whether they affect members of the public or people employed in the gas industry. They will also have a duty to take account of any advice given by the commission, whether it was sought through that consultation or not. The second safety-related part of the general duty, which is clause 8 as amended, states that, subject to the principal objective of promoting the consumer interest, both the Secretary of State and the authority shall carry out their functions in the manner best calculated to protect the public from any dangers arising from gas conveyed through pipes.

Under clause 10, ``a gas safety issue'' is defined widely as

    anything concerning the conveyance of gas through pipes, or the use of gas conveyed through pipes, which may affect the health and safety of members of the public or persons employed in connection with the conveyance of gas through pipes or the supply of gas.

Though a gas quality issue may not appear to have safety implications, good administration and prudence-against the background of the wide definition in clause 10-would ensure that the authority consulted the Health and Safety Commission before making any regulations under new section 16. The Health and Safety Executive has a duty of consultation under health and safety legislation. Depending on the content of any regulations proposed by the Health and Safety Commission and the Health and Safety Executive, the duty would include consulting the authority on each and every occasion.

The hon. Gentleman asked about a memorandum of understanding. There is another duty in the current Act that requires the regulator to agree a memorandum of understanding with the Health and Safety Executive

    for securing co-operation and the exchange of information between them.

That has not been explicitly carried over into the new duties, but we expect the regulatory authority, the Health and Safety Commission and the Health and Safety Executive to make appropriate arrangements.

I must stress that the culture of consultation that Ofgem and the Health and Safety Commission and Executive have developed now has a life of its own. Close consultation has already taken place on black dust, and I have no doubt that that will continue. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will be reassured by that, and I invite him to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment No. 596 deals with tax relief on expenditure. I am sure that, as a former tax adviser, the hon. Gentleman knows that when a company transfers or disposes of its assets, the associated expenses will be deductible only against the profits of the company's trade if incurred wholly and exclusively for the purposes of the trade and if the expenditure is not capital in nature.

Amendment No. 597 deals with whether the rules on the use of surplus advance corporation tax might apply in circumstances in which a company may have acquired a former regional electricity company within three years of the separation requirement taking effect. Several former regional electricity companies have been acquired by new owners in recent years. Circumstances have different in each case: for example, supply and distribution businesses have been sold to one owner or several owners.
