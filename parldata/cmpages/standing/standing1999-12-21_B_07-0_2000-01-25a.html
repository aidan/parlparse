Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 126, in page 13, line 6, leave out `prejudice' and insert `harm'.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss the following amendments: No. 127, in clause 25, page 13, line 17, leave out `prejudice' and insert `harm'.

No. 133, in clause 29, page 16, line 1, leave out `prejudice' and insert `harm'.

No. 139, in clause 34, page 19, line 5, leave out `prejudice' and insert `harm'.

Mr. Greenway: This is an important issue. The key point of clauses 24, 25, 29 and 34 is that they provide content-based exemptions rather than absolute exemptions. We must determine the degree to which the release or the confirmation of information would be to the detriment of the interests that are protected. Does the word ``prejudice'' convey the idea of sufficient injury? It has to impress on individuals who are charged with the responsibility of deciding how to use the discretion whether it is right to release or confirm information, or whether that could have a detrimental effect. That is the kernel of the Bill and it has caused more unhappiness in the minds of those who would like more information to be released than almost any other matter.

The White Paper, ``Your Right to Know'', did not use the word ``prejudice''. It said that there should be a substantial harm test. At the outset, the former Chancellor of the Duchy of Lancaster, the right hon. Member for South Shields (Dr. Clark), made it abundantly clear that the Government intended to introduce such a test. Paragraph 3.7 on page 11 of the White Paper states:

    ``We believe the test to determine whether disclosure is to be refused should normally be set in specific and demanding terms. We therefore propose to move in most areas from a simple harm test to a substantial harm test, namely``—

this is the key point—

    ``will the disclosure of this information cause substantial harm?''

We debated whether the words ``substantial'' or ``significant'' should preface the words ``harm'' or ``prejudice'' in the Bill. As I said, there are arguments on both sides. We are content for the moment to work on the assumption that both words are unnecessary in this context. However, we firmly believe that replacing the substantial harm test with a mere prejudice test in the four clauses is a significant rowing back of what the Government promised in the White Paper. More importantly, it also represents a rowing back from the existing code of practice. Interestingly, the code uses both ``harm'' and ``prejudice'' to describe the injury that could or is likely to be caused in the context of deciding whether information should be released. Arguably, the use of ``harm'' rather than ``prejudice'' would mean that more information would be released because ``harm'' conveys a stronger sense of damage or hurt than ``prejudice'', which has less emphasis and impact.

I suspect that the Government will say, as we have heard them say before in Committee, that ``prejudice'' is a well known legal term and that they have opted to use that term consistently and not to include ``harm''. The Committee should pause for thought, and should not be mesmerised or hypnotised by the Under-Secretary's response into thinking that there—[Interruption.] My anticipation of his response is based on what we have heard before. I have an open mind, but unless there has been a radical shift in his opinion on the matter during the weekend intermission in proceedings—for which I would be grateful but surprised—that is what we shall hear.

We should not accept at face value the contention that the use of ``prejudice'' throughout the Bill is a good idea, or the contention that there is no meaningful or discernible difference between that term and ``harm''. There is no certainty that the courts will interpret the two words in the same way or rule that they convey the same degree of detriment.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): I should be grateful if the hon. Gentleman would explain why he believes that the terms ``prejudice'' and ``harm'' are not virtually synonymous legally.

Mr. Greenway: I am explaining why it is important to use ``harm'', and the word's significance.

Let us get down to brass tacks. The Government have used ``prejudice'' and have relied entirely on previous court rulings on the word's meaning. I am not a lawyer; the hon. Gentleman is. By his intervention, he said effectively that, whatever the House decided regarding the Bill, the decision would already have been made—the courts would have already determined the standard. However, it is for the Committee and for Parliament to set the standard. It is not certain that the courts would interpret ``prejudice'' as being the same as ``harm'' if the latter was included and if we debated what it ought to mean.

The crux of the matter is that remarkably few challenges of acts of discretion by a public authority, and by the responsible official within the authority, would go to court. What really matters is what the Bill will mean for the thousands of public authorities and the people within them who will have to exercise discretion and make judgments.

Another weakness of the Bill is that the commissioner may review an exercise of discretion, but will have no power to require an authority to release information. The effect is that an individual within a public authority, which need not necessarily be a Government Department, will look at the Bill and exercise his discretion on the basis of what he understands the word ``prejudice'' to mean, but its meaning is extremely wide and extremely weak. That individual will say to the Information Commissioner, ``I am not happy about this.'' The commissioner will review the matter and say, ``I recommend that the information is released'' but there will be no power to require it to be released. So, as the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central (Mr. Fisher) has said consistently throughout our proceedings, at each turn, if there is any doubt at all, the doubt will be used in favour of the authority and against the individual who wants the information given to him or its existence confirmed.

    ``Clifford Chance quote an Anglo-American legal dictionary to the effect that `prejudice is a legalism for harm.' ''

Mr. Greenway: We do not believe that the word ``prejudice'' is strong enough. The Minister must be aware that the constant criticism of the Bill, including from his Back Benchers, is that the use of the word ``prejudice'' as opposed to the promise of a substantial harm test is a significant retreat from what the Government promised they would do. When we consider the clauses in which the word ``prejudice'' is used—doubtless, we shall have several debates on the matter in Committee—we will find that the content-based exemptions are extremely wide. We ask the Government to think again. We want to impress on the Government the deep frustration and anxiety felt by many people who want more information to be released. Those people welcome the Government's promise to free up information and change the culture in public services—Ministers have consistently promised that that is their objective in the Bill—but they do not believe that the Bill achieves that objective because the word ``prejudice'' is not strong enough.

In exercising discretion not to release information, the harm, damage or injury that would be caused by the release of information must be more than a fig leaf of anxiety or potential mischief; it must be real rather than imagined. We believe that the word ``harm'' would enable those who will have the job of implementing the legislation to achieve that objective. We do not want to have sterile debates about whether, in the unlikely event that many discretionary decisions are challenged in a court of law, some legal luminaries, including the most noble and eminent lawyers on the Committee Corridor, will turn around and say that it makes not a scrap of difference whether the word ``harm'' or ``prejudice'' is used. Both regarding the practicality of people having to work with the Bill and the expectation in the public mind of what is to be released, we think that the prejudice test is inadequate. Our purpose in tabling the amendment is to point out that ``prejudice'' is not sufficient and that the Government need to think again. Using ``harm'' rather than ``prejudice'' would ensure that more information is released.

I draw the Under-Secretary's attention to the code of practice; I do not recall there being an argument about the subject when it was introduced. The code uses the word ``harm'' throughout; on occasions it uses the word ``prejudice''. For example, clause 24 relates to defence. The code states that national security or defence would have to be harmed by disclosure; clause 24 states that information is exempt if it is likely to prejudice our defence capability or effectiveness. With some imagination, hon. Members may conclude that the clause is a rowing back from the code of practice. For example, the Ministry of Defence has decided to close a radar station in my constituency and operate it on a remote basis, with the loss of about 200 jobs. It could be argued that, because of the extremely wide exemption, information about that decision should not be available for release because it might prejudice defence capability or effectiveness.
