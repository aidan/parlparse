Committee members will be familiar with the process that was instituted for land sales at the time of the White Paper, which put a stop to the marketing of the land holdings, which were then held by the British Railways Board. Last September, however, the sales were resumed. Procedures were put in place to allow limited consultation with those who might be interested-rail interests, local authorities and other transport operators-giving two months' notice before the formal marketing process was to get under way, with a further two months to be allowed for the bids to be worked up. The critical thinking was not that-quite rightly-some form of consultation was taking place, but that the sales process was under way and therefore everything that was there to be marketed could and should be marketed.

British Rail's programme for disposing of surplus land has been going on for many years. The previous Government ordered British Rail to sell off its property as quickly as possible. Some of the land, however, is still needed for transport. Transport interests and local authorities told us that important sites were being lost because they did not know what was available and, when they learned, they had insufficient opportunity to bid. That is why we announced, in July 1998, a suspension of sales of remaining BR property. We then asked the British Railways Board to undertake a review of its land holdings, which was completed in September last year.

The BRB assessment paid particular attention to: large sites available for inter-modal or other freight terminals; land adjacent to passenger stations that might be used for improved access, interchange facilities and car parking; land already identified by transport operators and local authorities as having a specific transport use in the foreseeable future; land adjacent to already identified pinch points in the network; and, land for which the infrastructure for the former transport use remained in situ.

The BRB took into account the physical suitability of the site for transport use and whether that use was in keeping with local planning policies objectives and plans. The BRB's consideration encompassed all types of transport and allowed for imaginative ideas, provided that they were well thought out, took account of key industry players' plans for the future and had a realistic chance of funding and implementation in the foreseeable future. I shall come back to that concept of foreseeable future.

The review identified some 1,400 sites, of which only 200 were considered to have transport potential. In identifying sites with such potential, it was decided to identify all sites that, in the board's opinion, had supply-side transport characteristics rather than try to anticipate whether there would be transport demand for a given site. In some cases, there may well be no actual demand for transport purposes.

These new sections confer a new power on the Rail Regulator to give a direction to a person in a position to do so to improve an existing facility or to provide a new facility. That power can be exercised only on an application from the authority, or a third party with the consent of the authority.

Government amendments Nos. 991 and 992 combine to insert further new sections 16C to 16I, which set out a clear procedure by which the regulator considers applications for directions and gives, notifies and varies directions. The new power for the regulator will ensure that improvements can be made to the network where the authority considers that they are in the public interest. The provisions are not designed to replace the normal commercial judgments and investments of facility owners such as Railtrack or EWS-and they certainly do not preclude voluntary arrangements between a facility owner and someone wishing to agree terms for an enhancement. Clause 198 provides a mechanism for resolving obstructions to development if there is a dispute between the facility owner and the authority as to what constitutes commercial viability or the strategic priority of a particular development.

There may well be cases in which the SRA considers that there is a strategic need for a new or improved facility, but the person best equipped to provide that facility-whether it be Railtrack, EWS, a freight facility, a station operator or whoever-has other properties. In another case, a facility owner's strategic priorities for development may not match those of the rest of the industry. It those cases, the clause will provide the balance between the interests of facility owners and the long-term strategic needs of the railway.

An improvement may be sought by a third party, by applying direct to the Rail Regulator, but it must have the consent of the SRA. That will ensure that there is a genuine interest wider than the commercial interest of one party and that it fits with the wider aims of the authority. Clause 200 introduces a power for the regulator and the SRA to impose financial penalties on operators for contraventions of their obligations. By obligations, I mean licence conditions, terms of franchise agreements and certain duties relating to closures. A penalty may be imposed in respect of a breach that is continuing or in respect of a breach that has been discontinued by the time the penalty is imposed. The power to impose penalties will help to ensure compliance with licence conditions and other obligations. The amount of the penalty has to be reasonable in all the circumstances of the case. I will refer to the regulator for ease of reference, although of course the same considerations apply to the SRA when it is acting in respect of the contravention of a franchise obligation or a consumer protection condition in a licence.

This provision fills a gap in the powers of the regulator and the franchising director and gives them a powerful tool for ensuring that companies comply with their obligations. The power is to come into operation alongside existing duty of the authority and the regulator to issue enforcement orders to companies that are in breach of their obligations, but is not to be tied to it. Thus, it will be possible to impose a financial penalty even where no enforcement order has previously been imposed.

This will be useful, for example, when a contravention has taken place, but has since been discontinued by the company. Such a contravention may have caused considerable harm. However, if it were necessary to issue an enforcement order-which is intended to force the company to rectify a continuing breach, or to take steps to avoid a future breach-before imposing a penalty, it would be impossible to act if the company had already rectified the breach of its obligations.

The classic example of that was the disruption to South West Trains services immediately after the general election in 1997. Passengers suffered enormously, but the then franchising director and regulator complained that their powers were inadequate. By the time the enforcement procedures had been activated, the company's failings had been rectified and there was no opportunity to impose a penalty, despite the misery that the company had inflicted.

It might help the Committee to understand why the new powers are needed if I provide some background on how the existing enforcement duties operate. At present, the regulator and franchising director are under a duty to make orders to secure compliance with licence conditions and specified statutory obligations. They must issue an order if it appears to them that an operator is contravening a licence condition or is likely to do so. The regulator or franchising director is not permitted to issue an enforcement order where he is satisfied that the contraventions were, or will be, trivial, where the company is given and is complying with an undertaking to take what appears to him to be all appropriate steps, where his general duties preclude it or where the most appropriate way of proceeding is under the Competition Act 1998.

Where the Rail Regulator or franchising director makes an order, he has two options. The first is to issue a provisional order, which comes into effect immediately, but lasts only for a limited time. Such an order is intended as a stopgap. It must appear requisite to the regulator, and he must have regard to the extent to which any person is likely to suffer loss or damage due to the contravention and the effect on remedies. When the provisional order expires, the regulator may confirm it. Alternatively, he may proceed directly to a final order, without the interim stage of a provisional order. When he confirms a provisional order or issues a final order, the provisions of the confirmation or final order must be requisite for the purposes of securing compliance with the licence conditions. There are no sanctions for breaching a licence condition until an enforcement order is issued and takes effect. Thereafter, the regulator can seek compliance with the order via civil proceedings, including by injunction.

The principal difficulty with the current system of enforcement orders is that the regulator is, in practice, always one step behind the company and never comes to grips with the contravention until it is too late; in effect, he is left chasing shadows. The deterrent effect in relation to possible future breaches is, therefore, weak. We do not have the optimum system for ensuring compliance with obligations. The Government have been concerned by instances in which the regulators have not had effective powers to deal with breaches of obligations by operators, and I cited the situation with South West Trains services after the general election.

The power to impose penalties for current and past breaches of obligations will remedy a defect in the current enforcement system. It will make sure that the regulator and the SRA have powers to ensure compliance and deter future breaches rather than being left one step behind. Clearly, the power will apply only to contraventions that are committed after it comes into effect.

The hon. Member for Hexham asked about the scale of the penalties. The Bill does not specify a limit on the penalty that can be imposed, but I should dampen down some of the wilder speculation about the likely scale. The clause makes it clear that the penalty must be reasonable in all the circumstances of the case. The penalty could be heavy, but only if the breach is sufficiently serious and has done enough harm to warrant it. In cases where the breach is less serious, the amount would be smaller.

To ensure transparency, the regulator will be required to publish his policies on calculating the amount of a penalty and to have regard to them when he imposes one. That might include policies on having regard to the need to secure compliance, the consequences of the breach and the deterrence of future breaches.

There are detailed procedural requirements for the imposition of a penalty, including requirements on the regulator to publicise their intentions, to give notices with prescribed information and to receive and consider comments from interested parties. There are also procedures for modifying the penalty and for notifying the company and interested parties of the final decision on the imposition of a penalty. The company may apply to the regulator and the SRA to pay a penalty in instalments.

The interests of rail users should be at the heart of regulation. That objective cannot be fulfilled unless the regulatory authorities have the necessary powers to enforce the obligations that companies take on in respect of rail users.

Clause 200 remedies a significant defect in existing enforcement powers and will provide the regulator and authority with the tools that they need to ensure that companies comply with the obligations that they have accepted. The Government amendment, which we shall reach later, and the Opposition amendments contain limitation periods beyond which a breach of a franchise or licence may not be penalised.

We agree that there should be a limitation period to ensure that operators can act within a more certain environment and not be penalised for a past action. The Opposition suggested a six-month time scale, but that might be too short for the railway industry. Railway projects have a long lead time and it might be more than six months or even more than a year before it is apparent that a breach has occurred. Even if a breach became apparent to the regulator or the SRA, they could impose a penalty only if they were satisfied that a contravention had occurred. Time for investigation must, therefore, be factored into the equation.

Where complicated action is undertaken under section 55 to secure physical compliance with a licence condition of a franchise requirement, the limitation period can be as low as three months. That allows for the time that the regulator or SRA would have spent investigating the contravention before making an order under section 55. Where the regulator or the SRA are working from a standing start, however, a two-year period is fair. It means that companies cannot be penalised for actions long in the past, but allows time for a breach to become apparent and to be investigated.

In the light of that explanation, I hope that the hon. Gentlemen will agree to withdraw the amendment.
