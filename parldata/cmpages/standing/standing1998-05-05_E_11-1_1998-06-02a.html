Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): As we discovered this morning, this is a thoroughly bad clause, but it has been made a lot worse by the inadequacy of the Financial Secretary's reply to our lengthy debate this morning. She not only failed to answer many of the questions posed by my hon. Friends and others but missed the point of our amendments. She started by alleging that Conservative Members wanted to give tax relief to the very rich. Even if she had not listened to the debate, she should at least have read the amendments. The clear purpose of amendment No. 63 is to ensure that the restriction bites only on the very well paid and very well heeled who are allegedly taking advantage of a tax loophole. The purpose of the amendment is to exempt the more modest overseas contract workers who will be the victims of the clause.

The United States has a limit in recognition of the fact that anyone above the limit is probably taking advantage of tax avoidance measures and can be caught in the net and the need not to damage ordinary overseas workers. The United States also understands something that the Government do not that they have a duty to maintain the competitiveness of their overseas exporting and service industries. Most other countries will continue with the equivalent of a foreign earnings, deduction, but we shall not. We shall put our firms and industries at a massive disadvantage just at the time when the balance of payments deficit opens up.

The hon. Lady's main defence was that people based in the United Kingdom and citizens of this country should pay United Kingdom tax even when working abroad for long periods. She gave an example of pensioners who are settled here on modest incomes and asked why they should pay tax when someone working overseas does not. If that is her position, why is she not tightening the bigger loophole of exemption from United Kingdom tax by those who live abroad for more than a year during a tax period from April to April, which achieves non-resident status for United Kingdom taxpayers and, in the same way, exempts them from United Kingdom tax? If she believes that that is wrong, why is she doing nothing about it?

The practical effect of the clause will be to encourage the rich and well-advised to switch to the second option of being abroad for an entire tax year. They can do that because they can time their absence and their work abroad accordingly. That option is much more difficult for the army of consultants, teachers, nurses, engineers and aid workers whose contracts and aid programmes do not fit conveniently into a tax year. It is grotesque to suppose that aid workers should schedule their visits to disaster and famine areas to fit in with the fiscal requirements of the Inland Revenue, but that is what the hon. Lady is inviting them to do.

The hon. Lady's argument is based on a fallacy and an anomaly. The very people whom she wishes to target are those who will most easily take advantage of the second option, which remains available. Labour Members are against people failing to pay United Kingdom tax while remaining British citizens, but are doing nothing about it.

The issue of payments earned before 17 March but payable either as a bonus or otherwise after 17 March, which would therefore be subject to UK taxation, was raised by my hon. Friend the Members for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton) and other hon. Members. The Financial Secretary had no answer to give she simply said that she wanted a fairer tax system. What is fair about someone who is already on an overseas contract or tour, and who has already earned the money that will nevertheless be payable after the date of the Chancellor's Budget statement, becoming suddenly liable for full UK taxation?

The Financial Secretary said that it would be awfully unfair if remuneration were paid by way of bonuses. We gave her the example of the Rolling Stones overseas tour, on which the technicians and stagehands are paid, as part of their normal contract, a bonus based on the profits of the tour. Even the Treasury ought to be able to work out that the profits of the tour can be ascertained only once it is over. It is nothing to do with tax avoidance; it is a simple contractual arrangement whereby remuneration is properly based on something that can be calculated and paid only after the tour is over.

Despite the fact that that remuneration is to be suddenly liable for full UK taxation, the Financial Secretary claims that the clause is designed to achieve a fairer tax system; indeed, that phrase appears in the notes on clauses. It is grotesque to suppose that the retrospective application of tax in this way is anything other than unfair.

Opposition Members then raised the issue of competitiveness. I understand that that is chiefly the responsibility of the Department of Trade and Industry, but I would have hoped that Treasury Ministers would have consulted colleagues in other Departments, at least to make a gesture towards Britain's export effort and maintaining the competitiveness of United Kingdom firms. Such firms are based here but work overseas in the teeth of international competition.

I do not think that that is true: the letter that we all received from the Charities Tax Reform Group is entirely factually accurate.
