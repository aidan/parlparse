    `.—(1) There shall be a body of Commissioners named the Investigatory Powers Commission, consisting of the Investigatory Powers Commissioners.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Jane Kennedy): It may benefit of the Committee if I explain our thinking on the amendments. As the hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire (Mr. Heald) said, much comment was made on Second Reading about what he describes as the plethora of commissioners in the Bill. We said then that we take such comments seriously, and we continue to do so. We are sympathetic to the need to rationalise an oversight on what he describes as an industry of commissioners.

In considering that, we have identified two areas of possible rationalisation. However, the issues are complex and, at this stage, I have not been able to table amendments to put our thoughts into effect. I shall address those two areas shortly.

First, however, let me say that, although we are considering some rationalisation of the number of commissioners, we are not considering going as far as the hon. Gentleman and his colleagues suggest and establishing a single unified commission. I hope that the main benefits of unification can be achieved by unifying the secretariat alone. There are benefits in retaining the identity of some commissioners separately in statute because the existing commissioners are heavyweight judicial figures. They acquire considerable expertise over time, not least as a result of regular visits to those agencies whose activities they oversee. Therefore, they are a more effective check than would be a body of commissioners whose responsibilities ranged widely across the spectrum of investigatory powers. For example, Lord Nolan, the interception of communications commissioner, focuses exclusively on the interception of communications and provides a useful check on what agencies are doing in highly technical areas and source advice when knotty legal issues arise.

Questions of accountability and security also arise. An amalgamation of their functions would risk obscuring lines of accountability as well as compromising the expertise that they currently maintain. On security, I simply note that the system proposed by the Bill is designed to ensure that the need-to-know principle is strictly observed. Details about particular individuals are shared to the minimum extent possible. Within a unified secretariat, members of staff would work for particular commissioners, so the need-to-know principle could be maintained. Amalgamating the functions of different commissioners would make that considerably more difficult.

I shall explain to Committee members two areas that we have been considering. The first is the suggestion that the commissioners should belong to a unified secretariat. Earlier, my hon. Friend the Minister of State mentioned an audit team being sent out under the auspices of the interception commissioner to examine communications data requests. That demonstrates that we are considering a unified and investigative secretariat along the lines suggested in the new schedule.

Those staff would act entirely in accordance with a plan established by the commissioners. The commissioners themselves must be satisfied that the arrangements for oversight are such that they can genuinely report to the Prime Minister at the end of every year on the operation of the powers. Having said that, I see no reason why they need pay personal visits to police stations throughout Britain to check, for example, the process for recording authorisations for directed surveillance and whether particular directed surveillance is properly authorised.

The surveillance officers established under the Police Act 1997 already have a secretariat. My hon. Friend the Minister of State and I are considering whether that secretariat, which is geographically split between London, Edinburgh and Belfast, could be augmented to provide support for the interception commissioner, the intelligence services commissioner, the security services commissioner and the covert investigations commissioner, as necessary.

Two questions are at stake. The first is the desirability of that arrangement. There are security issues, for example, about whether it would be sensible to house the intelligence services commissioner, who examines the activities of the Secret Intelligence Service and Government communication headquarters, with people supporting the covert investigations commissioners, who may support those who check the activities of the sandwich police in the Department of Health so beloved of the right hon. Member for Penrith and The Border (Mr. Maclean).

In addition to the policy aspect, there are the questions of how far the arrangement for such a secretariat would need to be specified in the Bill or whether the secretariats could be set up with administrative arrangements. There are therefore several reasons why I cannot report any progress on the question of a unified secretariat, on which, however, we continue to work hard. We certainly intend to provide reassurance and table amendments while the Bill is still in Parliament.

On the second area for rationalisation, we believe that there may be scope for amalgamating the role of the new covert investigations commissioner—the only new commissioner created by the Bill—with that of the Surveillance Commission. We are also considering the roles of the intelligence services commissioner and the security service commissioner, which are currently filled by the same person, and have been for some time. We may reflect that reality in the Bill by amalgamating the roles. Many interests are at stake, but I can report some positive thoughts in that direction, and I hope that we will be able to table an amendment on Report.

Let me spell out what that would mean. At the moment, we have five commissioners, and I recently outlined possible plans to rationalise the situation. We would amalgamate the roles of the security service commissioner, and those of the surveillance commissioner and the covert intelligence commissioner, but we would keep the interceptions commissioner separate. That does not go as far as establishing the unified commission set out in the amendments. None the less, I hope that hon. Members will feel reassured by our thinking and withdraw the amendment. Our intention is not to go quite as far as the hon. Member for North-East Hertfordshire suggests, but the proposals for a unified secretariat will, I hope, go most of the way towards introducing the consistency that he seeks.

Under those circumstances, an individual should have the right to be notified, unless that will result in criminal action or prevent the police or other agencies from dealing with such action. We understand why lawful surveillance and interceptions should not be revealed to the individual. However, if an interception is unlawful, it is logical that he should be told. Such provisions exist in other countries. Canada has a system under which individuals can be notified that electronic surveillance has taken place. Jane Kennedy: I understand the intention behind the hon. Gentleman's amendment. I am not sure whether I will be entirely able to satisfy him, but I will do my best.

The intentions behind the amendment are admirable, but, for several reasons, we will resist it. First, I should say what will happen under the provisions as they stand if a commissioner finds that there has been a contravention of the Act. This is dealt with in subsection (3). In the event that the commissioners find a contravention, they are required to make a report on it to the Prime Minister, unless the tribunal has already done so. I do not know whether hon. Members have seen the annual reports that the commissioners send to the Prime Minister and which he publishes, but practice is for them to include any instances of procedures that were not correctly applied in the previous year. Those instances are suitably anonymised, for reasons that I shall describe. However, they are made available to the public where security considerations permit. That will be a matter for the commissioners, and I expect that they will continue that practice under the new regime.

Therefore, from the point of view of reassuring the public at large and Parliament in particular on the use of the powers in question, the existing regime has a good story to tell and bears comparison with other regimes. Contraventions are already published in the reports that I have described.

The amendment is about informing the individual that he or she has been the subject of unlawful surveillance. The first and obvious point that I should make is that individuals have recourse to the tribunal. If they made an application to the tribunal and it found that the provisions of the Act had not been followed correctly, they would be informed of its findings by virtue of clause 59 and the rules made under clause 60(1).

9.15 am

In addition, the tribunal will have the power to provide a variety of remedies, as set out in clause 58. Those include an order quashing or cancelling any warrant or authorisation, and an order requiring the destruction of any records of information in relation to the person concerned. Those remedies come on top of powers to award compensation or make another order, as the tribunal thinks fit. Without question, therefore, there is an avenue that the individual can take to seek redress, and the Bill will make it simpler and more accessible.

That explanation narrows down the area dealt with by the amendment to those individuals who are unaware of the fact that they have been the subject of surveillance and who are not, therefore, in a position to make a complaint to the tribunal. Although I do not expect this to offer the hon. Gentleman much reassurance, the first point is that people can make a complaint even if they do not know about, but merely suspect surveillance. Unless the complaint is frivolous, it will be investigated. Secondly, if any products of surveillance were used against people in criminal proceedings, defendants would have ample chance to challenge the evidence through the legal process. That might well include arguing that the surveillance was not carried out in accordance with the law.

I appreciate that that consideration will offer no comfort to those whose telephone calls might have been intercepted erroneously, and there are such examples in the recent reports. The material involved in such cases will not be used in evidence and cannot, therefore, be challenged. Again, however, any individual who believes that his calls have been intercepted, whether or not he was the target of an interception warrant, will be able to apply to the tribunal under the Bill. That development is significant; it is an improvement that explicitly recognises the European Court of Human Rights judgment in the case of Lambert v. France. Where people are unaware that their calls have been intercepted, and the procedures have not been applied properly, the interception commissioners will report the case to the Prime Minister and discuss it in their published report.

Those are the reasons why the amendment is not required. There is a further reason why, even if we were convinced of the need for the amendment, we would not want to accept it: the question of subject notification, which we discussed in relation to the tipping off of offences in clause 50. The nature of investigations is such that we simply cannot be sure that it will be safe at any time, even significantly after the event, to inform people that they have been the subject of surveillance. I appreciate that the amendment makes an exception for prejudicing the prevention and detection of crime. However, I note that it lacks any such qualification for the purposes of national security and economic well-being. I accept that we contemplate telling someone that he has been the subject of surveillance only when the surveillance was unlawfully authorised. But the Bill's requirements are particular, for a good reason. It describes a fastidious regime, which, although it rightly ensures that the relevant safeguards are in place, leaves open the possibility that some practitioners may, for technical reasons, not comply absolutely in each instance. I hope that there are sufficient safeguards to prevent that. If something goes wrong, the commissioner will in any event pick up the technical difficulties. It will be undesirable to have to inform someone that he has been the subject of surveillance, even if it might have been authorised properly and in accordance with the law if the correct procedures had been followed. In other words, the facts would be such that the surveillance was most definitely proportionate but the correct procedures would have gone missing at some stage. I should not like to contemplate people being notified in those circumstances. I believe that there is adequate recourse to the tribunal, which the Bill has improved considerably. For those reasons I have to resist the amendment. I hope that I have assured the hon. Gentleman on at least some of the points that he raised.

Mr. Allan: The amendments are designed to remove some restrictions on the role of the tribunal. The Bill seems to limit the way in which the tribunal will work, in that it will be required to apply judicial review principles. Amendments Nos. 226 and 227 would remedy that. We are concerned, because we believe that European court judgments have made it clear when dealing with tribunals in other instances that applying judicial review principles alone is inadequate. In the case of Chahal v. the UK, I think that the tribunal considered the case only according to judicial review principles, whereas it was held that some of the facts of the case should have been queried as well.

With amendments Nos. 215 and 216, we ask that the tribunal be able to consider cases according to their merits. That would broaden the tribunal's role and allow it to consider some of the factual evidence about the authorisation of a warrant, in addition to dealing with judicial review principles and legal arguments. We understand the tribunal's role, and the Minister kindly pointed out previously that members of the public will be able to go to the tribunal to see whether something has gone wrong. That broadening of the tribunal's ability to consider matters would be helpful.

Jane Kennedy: I appreciate the intention behind the amendments, and I am keen to address the concerns of the hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam (Mr. Allan) that by removing Human Rights Act 1998 cases to the tribunal we are offering a weaker kind of review than is available in the ordinary courts.

Amendments Nos. 215 and 226 are about cases that come under the 1998 Act. The first would require the tribunal to apply a merits test, and the second proposes the test that would apply to complaints under the Act in the ordinary courts. I will explain why we think that the judicial review test in the Bill is right, and meets the hon. Gentleman's concerns.

First, judicial review and the tests that the courts apply on judicial review are the products of the courts themselves, have developed over 30 years, and will no doubt continue to develop. By referring to judicial review principles, we are not limiting the tribunal's jurisdiction; on the contrary, we are leaving it open.

Secondly, if the claims under the Act were to come before the ordinary courts, we would expect judicial review to be the mechanism by which that would happen. Section 7 of the Human Rights Act 1998 allows proceedings to be brought by a person who claims that a public authority has acted in a way that is incompatible with convention rights. Judicial review is the obvious way in which to bring those proceedings, We are not, therefore, providing a lower level of review in the tribunal than would be available outside it.

Thirdly, and most importantly, there is no reason why the merits of a decision cannot be examined by the tribunal. That may sound a bit unorthodox, but I will explain what I mean. One of the tests applied in a judicial review is whether a decision is lawful. Under the Act, that is no longer simply a question of black-letter law, but includes the far broader question of whether the public authority has acted compatibly with the convention. Questions such as, ``Was the conduct proportionate?'' and ``Was the conduct necessary in a democratic society?'' will come within judicial review principles. That will not put the tribunal in the shoes of the decision maker or substitute the tribunal's judgment for that of the public authority being challenged. It does mean, however, that the merits of a decision will come under much closer scrutiny than would be the case in a traditional judicial review.

Amendments Nos. 216 and 227 tackle the complaints jurisdiction of the tribunal, which is the function that the tribunal will inherit from the existing tribunals and is an important part of the safeguards provided by the Bill against the abuse of power. Amendment No. 216 offers a merits test, and amendment No. 227 ties the tribunal to the test applied by the High Court. There is, in fact, no single High Court test.

Let me briefly explain why we consider the amendments unnecessary. For the complaints function/we have again referred to judicial review principles, and much of what I have said about those principles applies here, too. The cases under discussion will not, strictly speaking, come under the Human Rights Act 1998; where a convention right is being interfered with, however, I see no reason why the tribunal should not ask itself the same questions about proportionality and necessity. Indeed, the tribunal is a public authority and must also act compatibly with the convention, so it will not be allowed to ignore the convention in carrying out its work. Hon Members can be assured, therefore, that complaints will be considered fully and rigorously.

I hope that I have reassured the hon. Gentleman and answered his concerns, which I recognise are about serious matters.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider Government amendment No. 190.

Mr. Clarke: The amendments will ensure that the regulation of surveillance and covert human intelligence sources by Customs and Excise and British Transport police in Scotland is covered in the Bill. The amendments provide that officers can apply for authorisations under part II, in relation to their activities in Scotland. In that respect, the agencies are put on the same footing as those identified in clause 73(4)—the intelligence services, Her Majesty's forces, the Ministry of Defence and the Ministry of Defence police. The amendment also slightly changes the wording of clause 73(4)(e) and (f) to make it clear that when a body is designated under an order-making power in part II, the order should specify whether the part II regime extends to the body's activities in Scotland.

Flexibility is maintained for bodies designated in future. That is necessary to ensure that the arrangements under part II can dovetail with those under a Bill shortly to be introduced in the Scottish Parliament. That Bill will regulate the activities identified in part II of this Bill and carried out by the police in Scotland. It will, separately, leave open the possibility of the designation of other bodies in Scotland that may use surveillance or covert human intelligence sources.

We have discussed at length with the Scottish Executive how the separate regimes are to dovetail. The Scottish Parliament is today discussing the relationship between the two Bills. All are agreed that the priority is to provide for seamless operations on both sides of the border and to ensure that relevant activities are regulated as soon as possible with regard to the impact of the Human Rights Act. Some of the provisions in this Bill might be dealt with in the Scottish legislation, depending on precisely how that legislation is drafted, but there is no reason here to debate the detail of what may be devolved or reserved matters, as it is with the agreement of Scottish Ministers and, I expect, the Scottish Parliament that we are enacting this Bill. The priority here, for all, is to legislate for the impact of the Human Rights Act and to leave operational agencies with the minimum regulation necessary for that purpose.

The amendment provides for flexibility, allowing bodies designated in future to be designated under the Scottish Bill, where appropriate. It is possible that further consideration of the relationship between the two Bills will lead to the need for further amendment of this Bill. I am thinking particularly of the need to provide for surveillance by public authorities that may cross the border between England and Scotland. That is a matter to which I shall return later. ln the meantime, we are amending today what we already know should be changed.

I hope that that clarifies why we tabled the amendment.
