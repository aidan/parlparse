Bank of England Bill

Clause 10

Operational responsibility

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Helen Liddell): I support clause 10, which is an integral part of the Bill. Although we have rehearsed a number of the arguments that relate to it on the Floor of the House, I want to give coherence to our discussions by putting on record that the clause gives the Bank operational responsibility for monetary policy decisions. It removes the Treasury's powers to direct the Bank with respect to monetary policy. That is a significant change in the role of the Bank of England because it gives the Bank the right to determine the rate of inflation in line with the targets set by the Chancellor of the Exchequer. I am sure that we will have an interesting debate and, as we have already discussed the matter, I will not take up more of the Committee's time. I will be happy to answer hon. Members' questions.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Butterfill. Your post was more than adequately filled by Mrs. Dunwoody at our previous sitting, but we are very pleased to see you as the official alternate Chairman.

We now start to consider part II, which might be considered to be the main part of the Bill. I want to dwell a little on clauses 10 and 11, which effect the major transfer of policy from the Treasury to the Bank.

Clause 10 paves the way for later clauses, and makes it explicit that the Treasury will no longer give the Bank directions over monetary policy. It refers back to the Bank of England Act 1946, of which I have obtained a copy. It is a refreshingly brief document for such an important measure--I think that in some respects Governments legislated better in those days. The Act is remarkable for what it does not say, as well as for what it does say. With regard to clause 10, it maintains the Treasury's power of direction

    ``except in relation to monetary policy.''

I should like to explore with the Economic Secretary what is meant by monetary policy in the clause, because that phrase appears to go rather wider than the setting of interest rates. We know from Second Reading, and from elsewhere in the Bill, that the Bank is to take over the specific power to set interest rates to try to reach the Government's inflation target. Responsibility for monetary policy remains with the Treasury, but that for the setting of interest rates goes to the Monetary Policy Committee in the Bank of England. That is a much narrower concept than that of monetary policy generally. Will the Economic Secretary explain whether, and to what extent, other aspects of monetary policy will become the exclusive preserve of the Bank of England and free from Treasury direction?

The Bill envisages that the Bank's changed role will not simply consist of the Monetary Policy Committee setting monthly interest rates. The Bank will retain, and, in some respects, develop, a number of other policy areas, all of which relate to monetary policy. It will continue to be the banker of the last resort and so will have an important role in the banking system as a whole. Coinage will remain the responsibility of the Bank and it may have a decisive impact on exchange rate policy. I understand that the Bank will also retain some foreign exchange reserves in order to influence exchange rate policy when necessary, and we all know that exchange rate policy is a feature of monetary policy, which is not just setting interest rates. In its other activities, then, the Bank will have a great influence on the quantity of money in circulation.

Management of Government debt will now be taken away from the Bank of England and given to the Treasury, perhaps even hived off to some quango responsible to it. Banking supervisory functions will also be removed from the Bank. We will outline our major concerns about that later. The residue of duties heavily influences monetary policy in a number of important respects. In those other aspects will the Bank be free of direction from the Treasury?

Only today, an article in The Times reports the Governor's opinion on the adequacy of the interest rate target. He is reported as speculating that

    ``the existing 2.5 per cent. rate may be too high for entry into a European single currency.''

He was speaking quite properly in his capacity as Governor of the Bank of England, ranging more widely than the setting of interest rates, dealing with the inflation target itself. The clause may have been designed with remarks such as that in mind, allowing the Governor the freedom to range more widely over monetary matters. It requires a clear explanation of the scope of the Treasury's interference--or lack of it--in general monetary policy.

I want to put on record the reasons for our support for both the theory and the practice of operational independence. I am a student of economics, and I enjoy the theory behind economic policies. When I was at university, I had to read for my sins some of the detailed mathematical works that provide a backbone for the policy.

One summer's afternoon, when I could have employed my time more usefully on the tennis court or on the cricket field, I remember being locked up in the library at Jesus college, Oxford. I was reading an interesting paper by Professor Kydland entitled ``Intertemporal inconsistency of monetary policy''. I was told by my tutor that that provided the academic underpinning for the modern economic school that stresses the importance of credibility in monetary policy. Economic agents in all spheres and sectors should have consistent inflation expectations over time, and that requires a more credible monetary policy.

Various suggestions have been made over the years to make monetary policy more credible. The previous Conservative Government, during their first term of office, chose money supply targets in which the private sector were supposed to believe. That was then supposed to influence inflationary expectations, and thus make the control of inflation much less costly. As we all know, those targets were a complete failure, partly because they targeted the wrong measures of money supply and partly because of the way that the private sector adjusted to the Government's running of economic policy.

The next experiment designed to create a framework of credibility for monetary policy was the entry into the exchange rate mechanism. At the time, that policy had all-party support. Indeed, for several years, it was effective in reducing inflationary expectations and played a major part in reducing the inflation caused by the former Chancellor of the Exchequer, Lord Lawson. It played an important role in reintroducing stability into the economy, although, admittedly, at the cost of a major recession. However, I believe that that recession can be blamed on Lord Lawson's original inflationary policies. Membership of the exchange rate mechanism gave credibility to the monetary policy that was applied to attacking the boom of the late 1980s and early 1990s.

The problem with an external exchange rate anchor for providing credibility is that other people can blow it apart. That happened on what may be called black or white Wednesday, depending on one's point of view. For a number of complex reasons, the foreign exchange markets decided that that external anchor was no longer credible.

The third option is an institutional change to provide the credibility that is important in providing consistent expectations of inflation. It involves the creation of an independent central bank. An independent central bank provides credibility because the incentives on central bankers are different from those on politicians. When facing an election or political crises prior to an election, politicians may be prepared to take short-term measures that take risks with the long-term future of the economy and the long-term performance of inflation.

We saw such pressures on politicians operate time and again under the previous Government. They caused the two major inflations we experienced during the past 18 years, and, in particular, they led to many ill-judged monetary policy moves. For example, interest rate cuts repeatedly took place before party conferences or during periods of great political instability in the Conservative Administration.

The empirical evidence suggests a strong correlation between central bank independence and lower inflation. Several studies reviewed by the International Monetary Fund and the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development clearly point to that conclusion. A broad sweep of history looking at the performance of the Federal Reserve bank in the United States and at the Bundesbank backs up that argument.

People who do not support the policy argue that there is no clear causation. It could be argued that because people in countries such as Germany and the United States dislike inflation more, perhaps, than our own people, they have been prepared to give the Bank greater independence. It is almost a self-perpetuating theory that countries with a loathing of inflation have low inflation and other countries, perhaps because of house prices, do not worry so much about it and have a poorer record on inflation. However, the causation argument does not stand up to analysis. It is argued that Germans hated inflation after their experience in the 1930s, but many other countries went through hyper-inflation and that has not convinced their people to accept low inflation policies.

The example of middle income countries in Latin America can be cited, but equally some developed countries in Europe that experienced dreadful inflation have not given their institutions the independence or strength to tackle it.

I am convinced that the empirical evidence backs up the case for central bank independence. The potential long-term gains for our economy, if lower inflation results from the policy, are huge.
