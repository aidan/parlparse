A simple reading of the judgment given in the Lands Tribunal on the Anston case puts the matter into a slightly different context. Judge Marder said that

    "it is to be assumed for the purposes of paragraph 2(1) of schedule 6 of the Local Government Act 1988 that the hereditament should be valued as it physically was on the material day, taking into account any disrepair then existing, so far only as such disrepair affected the rental value of the hereditament."

On any given day, a property will be in some state of disrepair, but that does not affect its market rental value. In a parliamentary answer that I received today, that point is made to explain some of the statistics that were offered on Second Reading.

In the real world, a tenant is under a contractual duty to maintain a property, and valuations are made on the basis that the property is either in a reasonable state of repair or will be so placed by the tenant under the terms of the lease. In some circumstances, a property may be allowed to fall into disrepair most usually, as in the Anston case, if the building has reached the end of its economic life and is due to be replaced. However, due to the complexities of the property market, there is often a significant delay between the building having reached that point and its being demolished or substantially reconstructed.

With the introduction of the rating of empty property in 1966, valuation officers have become obliged to make increasingly unrealistic assumptions about the hypothetical use of a building in order to justify a rating liability and thus obtain the empty rate payment. That has led to several unsatisfactory and inappropriate practices, such as soft stripping and constructive vandalism, which I am sure all hon. Members deprecate.

It is clear that Judge Marder's decision in the Anston case dealt with exceptional disrepair under the rebus rule. He accepted established case law that a property should normally be valued as being in a reasonable state of repair unless there was a particular circumstance to the contrary, as in the Anston case. The Government therefore have no ground to argue that that decision implies a daily revaluation of every non-domestic hereditament. That is clearly not the outcome of the Anston case. Amendment No. 1 goes to the heart of the Bill and what we are seeking to achieve through it. As hon. Members have said the Bill will require valuation officers to assume that a hereditament being valued for rates is in a state of reasonable repair. It will further require that in deciding the reasonable repair condition of property, a valuer will have to ignore repair that a reasonable landlord would consider uneconomic. That is not a difficult concept. Indeed, it is one with which valuers are already familiar, as the Bill merely codifies what was required in so-called gross value cases before 1990 and what valuation officers have been doing in all cases since then. Case law going back to before 1990 and the practice established as a result mean that the reasonable repair condition of hereditament depends on the age and type of the property, the locality in which it is situated and all surrounding circumstances. That follows, necessarily, from the basic principle that matters relating to the hereditament are to be taken as they stand. In the case of Saunders v. Maltby, for example, in which it was established that non-economic repairs could be disregarded in valuation, the paramount question was what a reasonable landlord could be expected to do, having regard to the value of the property. It was implicit that the age of the property and its locality were highly relevant to applying that economic test of what was reasonable in the circumstances.

For example, some hon. Members will be familiar with my Department's former main building at 2 Marsham street. Indeed, I suspect that one Opposition Member spent many a long and happy hour there.
