Miss Johnson: We have now reached another important part of the Bill covering the FSA's ability to make rules and to publish guidance. There are many amendments to discuss. The importance of part IX of the Bill to the new arrangements cannot be overstated. We are mindful that the practical, everyday success of the new regime will depend largely on getting the provisions right. When Parliament has finished its work on the Bill and it is enshrined on the statute book, part IX will be part of the life blood of the new regime.

As with many other aspects of the Bill, we are doing two things in part IX. First, we intend that the rule-making and related powers should give the FSA a powerful tool so that it may regulate with a firm and effective hand. Secondly, we have balanced those powers with a number of checks and balances so that the FSA is fully accountable in exercising its powers under part IX and that it is bound to act fairly and transparently in the exercise of those powers.

Within the checks and balances applying to the rule-making function, there are two main features. First, there is a number of detailed checks and balances specific to part IX—for example, the detailed consultation and cost-benefit analysis requirements in clause 125. We shall discuss those in greater detail as we come to the various clauses. Secondly, many of the more general accountability provisions which the Committee discussed in the context of part I and schedule 1 will apply to the FSA's rule-making functions and in particular the relative statutory objectives. Amendment No. 375 recognises that and proposes linking more directly the FSA's rule-making power to its statutory objectives. I shall comment on that in greater detail later and explain why the Opposition amendment is not necessary or appropriate.

The statutory objectives are relevant to the rule-making function. Clause 2(1) provides that when exercising its rule-making powers as a whole, the FSA should as far as possible, act in a way that is compatible with the regulatory objectives and in a way that the authority considers most appropriate for meeting them. The FSA must also have regard to various regulatory principles in clause 2(3), such as proportionality, facilitating innovation and so on when it exercises its general rule-making powers. The FSA must also abide by the relevant provisions of schedule 1. Perhaps one of the more important of those in the context of part IV is the provision in paragraph 5(2) to schedule 1, the effect of which will be that, in carrying out its general rule-making functions, the FSA must act via its governing body—essentially, its board.

Another important tool in schedule 1 is the FSA's annual report. Members of the Committee will know that its contents are to be agreed with the Treasury. It is proposed that the authority's report will contain an account of the way in which it has exercised its rule-making powers in the year in question. However, accountability, in the strict sense of checks and balances, is not the only factor that the Government have borne in mind. As members of the Committee will know by now, the Government are committed to as light-touch regulation as possible, but protection for consumers where necessary. Those principles were our watchword in preparing part IX. We are only too well aware that burdensome rules can stifle competitiveness and innovation. They can lead to high regulatory costs, and damage the United Kingdom's reputation and attractiveness as a place to do business. We fully appreciate that not only firms could suffer from over-burdensome regulations; consumers' interests also could be harmed. Higher industry costs are likely to be passed on to consumers and over-burdening regulation could stifle regulation and consumer choice. A balance has to be struck. Consumers need appropriate protection. We intend to ensure that where it is needed, they get it.

As for the FSA's rules under the Bill, broadly translated, the intention is that the authority has sufficient powers to make appropriate rules that protect consumers' interests. I will expand on what we think this means when we discuss the detail of amendment No. 375.

I will make a few general remarks about the FSA's rule-making powers under the Bill and about why we think that they are most appropriate to be vested in the authority, not, as has been suggested, in Ministers and Parliament. Essentially, it is because the nature of the rules that the FSA will make are so specialist in detail—not to mention numerous—that it would be entirely inappropriate for them to be made or exercised by, say, the Treasury, or Parliament. The rules must be made in the light of market knowledge by combining, for example, financial market practices and trends with supervisory risk modelling techniques on the other. To be able to deliver the right rules in the right circumstances, the person making them needs a detailed hands-on, everyday knowledge of the markets and the players, and a detailed understanding of their businesses, as well as a full understanding of how all those factors affect consumers' interests and rights.

The sheer volume of rules and other provisions that now exist and will need to be made under the new provisions is extremely significant. Currently there are three self-regulatory organisation rule books, the old Securities Investments Board rules and regulations, the FSA guide to banking supervisory policy, the Building Society Commission's rules and regulations, and regulations under the Insurance Companies Act 1982, not to mention the wealth of notices and more informal guidance that already exists. Although under the new regime there will be a considerable improvement on the existing position of the multitude of rules and regulations, the FSA's handbook of rules will inevitably be large. Rationalising and updating the current rules and requirements to which banks are currently subject and, more important still, keeping them updated and ensuring that they are appropriate, is a task which, in reality, the FSA is best placed to fulfil, reflecting the position under the Financial Services Act 1986 and the Banking Act 1987, under which the authority has considerable discretion in relation to making policies and rules. However, the framework of accountability is much improved under the Bill.

We are glad that Lord Burns' Committee invited the House of Lords Delegated Powers and Deregulation Committee to look at the draft Bill. Commenting in particularly on the authority's rule-making powers, they concluded that

    ``there is probably no sensible alternative to the approach set out in the draft bill. We have been supported in forming this view by having regard to the reality that it will be the Authority which will have the closest understanding of market conditions, and will consequently be best placed to deal with the detailed issues, including many technical issues, which will need to be covered in regulations.''

The Committee went on to note that, in relation to the exercise of the FSA's powers,

    ``some further protections and disciplines . . . should be imposed''.

We have broadly followed them.

The Committee recommended, in particular, that the FSA should submit an annual report to the Secretary of State outlining the exercise of its rule-making powers. As I said earlier, our intention is that the report to the Treasury will involve the relevant provisions.

The Bill makes it clear that the exercise of the FSA's legislative functions can be carried out only by the FSA's governing body. Those powers cannot be delegated to individual FSA staff.

In other words, we are making rules, issuing standing guidance, publishing statements of principle and codes relating to, for example, the conduct of approved persons and market abuse. The authority must act through its board. The Bill states that the board shall comprise a majority of non-executive directors who are appointed by the Treasury.

In addition, the FSA is carrying out a major consultation exercise. It is preparing a new handbook of rules and guidance which will come into force at M2. The Bill will be implemented at that date under the rule-making powers that are contained in the legislation.

We intend the handbook to be one way in which the FSA will be able to deliver regulatory integration. The current hotch-potch of different rule books—there are, for example, 14 different versions of the conduct of business requirements—will be replaced by a single collection of regulatory standards. The intention is to deliver a consistent standard of protection for consumers and a level playing field for financial businesses, and to differentiate appropriately between different lines of business and different types of customer. In particular, the FSA said that inter-professional business will be treated differently from retail business.

I am assured that a complete FSA handbook will be in place by M2, but that its contents will be a mixture of new and old material. The FSA wants to strike a balance between continuity and change. Its intention is that the new material at M2 will concentrate on the new structures that the Bill will introduce, including the new persons provisions and other matters involving a high-priority need for rationalisation, including the regime for inter-professional dealings.

In some areas, such as the rationalisation of prudential requirements between different types of institution, the main changes will take place after M2. All that will be done in consultation with those who are interested in the regulatory system and with the public in general.

Consultation covered the proposed rules and guidance and the handbook's design and features. There has already been consultation on some important parts of the handbook, including the new FSA principles for business, and the approved persons regime. The next few months will be extremely busy. The FSA plans to publish consultations on the conduct of business requirements, the interim prudential arrangements and the authorisation, supervision and enforcement manuals, among others. We intend to allow sufficient time around M2 for firms to deal with the transitional arrangements. It is important to give firms the appropriate time in which to adjust their systems and other literature, where necessary.

The FSA signalled its determination to make the handbook user friendly and accessible. It will be available in paper form and work is under way to build an electronic infrastructure that will make it available on CD-ROM and the internet.

Clause 110 contains the Bill's basic rule-making power—it relates to what are known as the FSA's general rules. Those rules are required to authorise persons to protect the interests of consumers. I use the term ``consumer'' and shall continue to do so in this debate for ease of purpose. However, the clause does not use that term—it defines more fully the persons for whom the protection should be available, including persons who do not necessarily have a direct contractual relationship with the authorised firm to which the rules apply. For example, the FSA can make rules for the purpose of protecting the main third party beneficiary on an insurance policy.

Clause 110(1)(a) involves making rules to protect the interests of people who have used financial services. We broadly intend that, where appropriate, the basic rule-making power should cover reviews of past business, such as pensions mis-selling. We are considering whether further changes are needed to put that measure beyond doubt. What I referred to as the interests of the consumer rule-making purpose will have a broad application. We intend the clause to be broad enough to allow the FSA to make rules to further market integrity and to protect against systemic risk, as required by our international obligations under the investment services directive. Making rules in relation to such areas would be permissible in order to protect consumers.

4.45 pm

Although clause 110 does not relate generally to firms that do not carry out regulated activities, subsection (5) provides that rules that require an authorised person to maintain financial resources can take into account other members of the regulated person's group, even if they are not carrying out regulated activities. Clearly, that is important in order to ensure proper prudential supervision. For example, a bank, which is authorised and permitted to take deposits, might have in its group a leasing company or separate lending company, which is not. The authority would be able to consider the finances of the group as a whole to obtain a sensible view of its financial stability and resources.

The clause is expressly restricted in relation to European economic area passported firms. Subsection (6) makes it clear that rules made under clause 110 cannot prevent passported firms from carrying on business in the UK, as they are entitled to do by virtue of their passports. The FSA will, however, be able to apply its own conduct of business rules to firms that passport into the UK to do business. As required by the directives, rules relating to prudential supervision—in relation to capital requirements and basic fitness and propriety, for example—will remain a matter for the home state regulator.

Government amendment No. 431 is designed to make it clear that the rules under clause 110 will apply to authorised persons carrying on regulated activities, and not the other way round—regulated activities carried on by authorised persons. Keeping the existing formulation could be too narrow; on one level, it could be construed as meaning that the rule-making power would only apply to the carrying on of regulated activities, and not to the authorised person who carries them on. I trust that the amendment will not be contentious.

Government amendment No. 432 is a drafting amendment, which is consequential to the clarification proposed in Government amendment No. 431.

On amendment No. 375, I was a little surprised that Opposition Members seemed to be suggesting an expansion of the FSA's powers. I therefore find myself in the slightly unusual position of explaining why those powers should not be broader. Clause 110 is intended to provide rule-making powers in areas where the current legislation allows rules to be made. The central rule-making purpose is consumer protection, and we do not propose that the FSA should be given a broad, unlimited or blanket power to make rules for other purposes. Clause 110 provides that the FSA should be able to make rules for the broad purposes of consumer protection. To the extent that traditional rules are needed for purposes other than protecting the interests of consumers—furthering the FSA's other objectives such as maintaining market confidence or reducing financial crime—specific provision has been made in part IX of the Bill.

Clauses 115 and 117 relate to price stabilisation securities following a new issue and money laundering respectively. The clauses are an example of the Government making specific provision in addition to the basic consumer protection rule-making power, although, inevitably, there may be some overlap. For example, the FSA may wish to make rules that further market confidence and protect consumers. Under clause 110, the essential test is whether the rule is necessary or expedient for the purposes of protecting consumers. If not, it will not be permissible under the clause.

In exercising its rule-making power, the FSA will need to act, as far as is reasonably possible, in a way that is compatible with its regulatory objectives. It must also have regard to the so-called regulatory principles in clause 2(3). That is not to say that other objectives or principles considered in isolation would merit exercising the clause 110 rule-making power. I hope that that deals satisfactorily with amendment No. 375.

Government amendment No. 433 is another technical amendment to ensure that the FSA can make rules designed to protect a wider range of users of financial services or their beneficiaries. The amendment makes it clear that the FSA can make rules to protect a beneficiary under a trust. As Committee members will be aware, trustees would normally have a contractual relationship with an authorised person. However, as beneficiaries are the people who may ultimately be affected by an authorised person's actions, we propose that the FSA's basic rule-making power should extend to making rules to protect beneficiaries or, for that matter, anyone else who may be adversely affected by the use of authorised services. I hope that Committee members will agree with that, and support the amendment.

Miss Johnson: I shall now move to the amendments to clause 111. Essentially, the clause complements the FSA's general rule-making power under clause 110 by giving it the power to apply, in appropriate circumstances, rules that authorise firms' non-regulated activities. In common with clause 110, clause 111 is aimed at protecting consumers—broadly, in this context, users of an authorised person's regulated activity services.

The clause empowers the FSA to make rules on matters on which the current legislation enables the existing regulators to make rules. For example, the regulators are currently able to make rules requiring that notifications of certain events be made in respect of a firm's non-regulated activities or in relation to other group members. We also envisage non-regulated activity rules potentially being used to cover matters such as systems and controls in non-regulated parts of an authorised person's business. Another example could be financial resources—the regulator may need to monitor the financial standing of a business as a whole.

However, we do not intend that non-regulated activity rules could be invoked as a pretext for applying FSA rules to unauthorised businesses in inappropriate circumstances. The broad intention is that the clause will be used to allow the FSA to make rules in areas in which the regulator can currently make them. We particularly do not intend clause 111 to be used to apply conduct of business rules to general insurance or deposit-taking, and the FSA shares that view.

Miss Johnson: The group of amendments and the new clauses address two points, which, I fear that I should warn members of the Committee, are technical. However, they concern important matters that must be addressed adequately.

The first point is relevant to managers of authorised unit trusts and to insurance undertakings—both companies and friendly societies. Some changes covered by the amendments reflect the restrictions currently imposed, in line with EC directive requirements to prevent such persons undertaking other types of activity. The second carries forward arrangements relating to linked long-term insurance policies under section 78 of the Insurance Companies Act 1982 and section 56 of the Friendly Societies Act 1992.

I shall describe the amendments' purpose and effect in that order. As currently set out in the Bill, clause 111(4) gives the FSA powers to specify that an authorised person who has permission to act as a manager of an authorised unit trust or to deal in contracts and insurance may not also carry on specific types of non-regulated activity.

That approach was right in so far as it went, but it involved two difficulties that had to be addressed. We proposed the removal from the clause of subsection (4), which confers on the FSA general powers to make non-regulated activity rules and to make specific provision in that respect. The first difficulty was that the scope of the rules under the clause was too narrow, as they could prohibit only non-regulated activities. However, fundamental to the arrangements underpinning supervision of the firms involved is the restriction ensuring that they may not carry on other activities of any kind. As a slight aside, it may be helpful for hon. Members to know that in this case the Government are considering only companies and friendly societies. Individual people cannot usually engage in the activities in question in their own right.

Under current arrangements, the manager of an authorised unit trust may not carry on a non-financial services business, such as a travel agency or a car sales business, alongside the unit trust activities. Also, the firm may not give financial advice on pensions and endowments to retail investors. Similar restrictions apply to insurance companies and to friendly societies.

Those restrictions are contained in section 83 of the Financial Services Act 1986, which covers managers of authorised unit trusts. The insurance company equivalent is contained in section 16 of the Insurance Companies Act 1982, which states that such a company

    ``shall not carry on any activities . . . otherwise than in connection with or for the purposes of its insurance business.''

Friendly societies are covered by an equivalent provision.

The FSA could have made rules restricting the carrying on of other types of regulated business, using the general rule-making power provided for in clause 110, but that leads us to a second difficulty. If the authority had to make rules under clause 110 to carry out regulated activities, it could do so only on the grounds set out in that clause for exercising the power to make general rules.

As I explained during our discussion on clause 110 and the previous group of amendments considered, the Government have tried carefully to restrict the grounds on which the authority may exercise its powers to make general and non-regulated activity rules. Those grounds are to be linked closely, in simple terms, to the protection of the interests of the customers of the authorised firms in question.

In order to exercise those powers to introduce restrictions on the business that may be carried on in connection with insurance business or the management of an authorised unit trust, the FSA would need to demonstrate that it complied with the grounds set out in clauses 110 and 111. As I said, however, the FSA will have no option but to make such rules because of the United Kingdom's international obligations. It would not, therefore, be appropriate to impose the same restrictions on the exercise of the particular power involved.

The Government concluded that the power to make rules about the activities that might be carried on by relevant firms should be a separate power. That issue is addressed by amendment No. 438, new clause 5 and new clause 6(1) and (2). Amendments Nos. 439 and 450 are purely consequential to the matters that I have just addressed. Subsections (3) and (4) of new clause 6 address the matter of linked long-term insurance contracts, about which the Bill has so far been silent.

As I said in my opening remarks, powers covered by section 78 of the Insurance Companies Act 1982 and section 56 of the Friendly Societies Act 1992 enable the regulator to take steps to prohibit or vary terms in long-term insurance contracts that link the value of the benefits payable under such contracts to the value of particular types of investment or commodity.

I think that I had better explain that. Usually, in a long-term insurance policy such as an endowment, the amount that the contract pays out when the policy matures will reflect the performance of the money invested in a relevant fund. The precise amount is likely to reflect, for example, the performance of the stock market and interest rates during the period of the policy—as well of course as the amount invested. Normally, investments are spread to ensure a reasonably predictable return.

However, sometimes the benefits under a policy may be defined by reference to something quite specific. Examples might include small company or technology stocks or the price of some commodity, such as oil or bananas. That presents two risks for consumers. One is that they have, as it were, to put all their eggs in one basket. The other possible risk is that the index or commodity to which their investment has been tied may be especially volatile, and may change dramatically from day to day, as well as over longer periods of time. For example, we all know how much the price of oil can change.

The current legislation, which we are seeking to carry forward in subsections (3) and (4) of the new clause allows the regulator to intervene in such contracts, by either prohibiting them altogether, or requiring an amendment to the terms of the contract to substitute the index or value to which the benefits payable are linked.

Clause 118

Modification or waiver of rules

Amendment made: No. 450, in page 54, line 13, at end insert—

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 380, in page 54, line 29, at end insert—

    `(4A) In determining whether compliance is unduly burdensome for the purposes of subsection (4)(a), the Authority shall take into account what conduct is permissible in comparable circumstances in other jurisdictions which are members of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development.'.

The clause covers the FSA's ability to waive or modify rules and the circumstances in which it may do so. The provision is welcome, but I am worried about the circumstances in which the FSA may do so. Those circumstances are laid down in subsection (4) and one of the conditions concerns whether the rules would be unduly burdensome to people in the market. I am worried that the provision may never be used because it is too subjective. Some people may say that the rules are burdensome and the FSA may say that they are not, but there is no reference point by which to make a judgment. Our amendment would provide one by comparing the burden with that applying in similar jurisdictions among other members of the Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development. That provision would be useful, particularly as the Government are anxious to keep burdensome costs at least in line with those in other jurisdictions and, if possible, below them. For that reason, as well as providing a specific point of reference, it is desirable that the Government accept the amendment.

Miss Melanie Johnson: I thank the right hon. Gentleman for the amendment, but, before dealing with it in detail, I shall explain the purpose of the clause.

Clause 118 addresses the way in which the application of certain rules may be modified or waived in respect of a particular firm or number of firms. The clause relates to all sorts of rules that we discussed earlier on clauses 110 to 117, as well as to rules concerning the appointment of auditors and actuaries under clause 297.

Some of the provisions will apply in the context of collective investment schemes, but a broadly equivalent provision regarding some rules relating to collective investment schemes also appears in part XVI, which we shall discuss later. With the importance that we attach to flexible and proportionate regulation, clause 118 is particularly important to firms in carrying on their regulated activities.

As we said in our July 1998 consultation paper on the Bill, we believe that a similar outcome to the United States' ``no action letter'' procedure can be achieved by making use of the waiver and guidance powers in the Bill. We shall discuss guidance when we come to clauses 127 and 128, but in relation to the waiver requirements, we propose that in appropriate cases an authorised person will be able to go to the FSA to seek a direction that any of the rules which would otherwise apply to him are not to apply to him, or to obtain a direction that the rules will apply, but subject to certain modifications.

The effect will be that, if the FSA agrees to issue a direction that a particular rule will not apply, the person to whom the direction has been issued will not be in breach of the relevant rule. The FSA will not then be able to take action for breach of a particular rule if someone has acted in accordance with a waiver, and individual customers' rights to take action in respect of non-compliance with that rule could be severely limited.

In practice, it is vital that the FSA is ready and willing to grant waivers or modifications on request to develop the open and flexible regulatory regime that we intend. Its recent policy paper in September this year on giving guidance and waivers discusses the waiver power; we fully expect that the FSA will make good use of it.

I turn to the detail of the clause. Subsection (4) sets out the circumstances in which the FSA may consider giving a waiver or modifying a rule's application. The circumstances are pitched at a level to ensure that the direction would not result in undue risk to people whose interest the rules are intended to protect. Amendment No. 380 relates to that test. It appears to provide that, in determining whether compliance is unduly burdensome, the authority should take into account what conduct is permissible in comparable circumstances in other OECD countries.

I am afraid that the amendment is not an entirely plausible suggestion. In deciding whether to issue a particular waiver, it is for the FSA to determine what it considers to be unduly burdensome in the context of investment protection concerns. Of course, the FSA's general approach would be conditioned by awareness of the international dimension, in line with the need to have regard under clause 2(3)(e) to the international character of financial services.

The right hon. Gentleman will recall that the FSA, following a suggestion of the Joint Committee, made it clear that its annual report under paragraph 10 of schedule 1 will include compliance cost comparisons with other jurisdictions. The test proposed by Opposition Members could lead to all sorts of difficulties in practice: should the FSA take into account the OECD countries with the highest or the lowest standard in this context—or somewhere in between? What if it follows one approach and not another? It is difficult to see how the FSA could realistically carry out that analysis every time it took decisions on waivers.

I suspect that Opposition Members may be anticipating that I shall make a statement elaborating what is meant by ``unduly burdensome''. If so, I shall not disappoint them, but they may already know the answer. Whether something is unduly burdensome is to be determined ultimately in the light of all the relevant circumstances. In considering whether compliance with a rule would be unduly burdensome, the authority would probably want to consider the cost that the firm would incur; whether the rule was framed in a way that made it difficult for the firm to comply in view of the firm's internal organisation; the characteristics of its client base; or even perhaps the methods that it adopted for marketing its services.

In addition, ``unduly burdensome'' is a phrase used in the context of a broadly equivalent provision in section 47B(2) of the Financial Services Act, so it is not an entirely new concept. In applying the test in conjunction with the second limb of the test—that the waiver or modification would not place people at risk—the FSA will need to consider other ways of achieving the aims of the rule, which are more appropriate to the particular circumstances of the firm. I hope that, in the light of my remarks, the right hon. Gentleman will seek to withdraw the amendment. If not, I shall ask my hon. Friends to oppose it.
