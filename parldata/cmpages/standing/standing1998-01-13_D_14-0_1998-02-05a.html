Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I beg to move amendment No. 248, in page 25, line 11, leave out subsection (4).

The amendment deals with a complex area of law, and we must probe the Government's intentions. I cannot believe that the matter needs to be referred to the Low Pay Commission; it concerns jurisdictions and sectors to be covered or otherwise, and how the clause will operate.

Labour Members have a certain sensitivity when the word ``offshore'' is mentioned. Before the position of the Paymaster General became a matter of public debate, I led on Treasury matters in a Statutory Instruments Committee debate on offshore activities in Labuan--learned members of the Committee will know it as former British North Borneo--with which we concluded a revised double-tax agreement. In that case, and this, offshore had nothing to do with tax havens, but with harvesting oil or gas from the continental shelf. That viable, buoyant and well-paid industry is a British success story.

Does the Minister of State anticipate a significant impact if the national minimum wage is extended offshore? Given the exchanges we had under Mr. Atkinson's chairmanship this morning, it would be helpful if the Minister addressed potential anomalies over inclusion and exclusion. For example, the armed forces will be excluded. Will oil workers be included? If not, why not? Will other offshore workers be included?

It would also be helpful, not least because of my responsibility for keeping an eye on the energy industries, if the Minister said whether adverse representations have been made. Most workers will be paid substantially more than the minimum wage. I have only one reservation about that, which follows this morning's exchanges about extended hours, particularly the comments of my hon. Friend the Member for South Cambridgeshire (Mr. Lansley). It is not clear how many hours persons on rigs are said to be on duty. By definition, they are away from home. The definition of offshore employment in the clause is intended to secure the participation of such workers in the national minimum wage.

Whom does he want to bring in and whom might he exclude? We would also be grateful if the Minister said a little more about the cat's cradle of overlapping jurisdictions and responsibilities. To preface the points that I want to make on the amendment and the clause, it may be useful if I remind members of the Committee of how important the industry is in the United Kingdom. I was recently involved in an exercise to pinpoint the importance of the oil industry throughout the United Kingdom. We discovered that there were more than 200 constituencies in which more than 500 people were directly or indirectly employed in the oil industry. That is a significant figure in any terms. As the hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell) made clear, wage rates in the oil industry are perceived as being very high. It is reasonable to ask why minimum wage legislation should apply offshore. Even in unskilled forms of work such as catering and stewarding, the standard rate is between £18,000 and £19,000.

In answer to a question asked by the hon. Member for Daventry, I inform the Committee that offshore workers normally work on a two-week working cycle, which means two weeks on, two weeks off. Some companies use a three-week cycle. Most workers who are on such a cycle will work a 12-hour day, seven days a week. That is quite a lengthy period of work. For the record, I should say that United Kingdom offshore workers work longer than any other offshore workers in Europe. They work about 2,400 annualised hours. That contrasts, for example, with Norwegian workers, who work about 1,600 hours.

The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Mr. Ian McCartney): The amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Daventry would delete subsection (4), which is an important part of the clause, but I suspect that he has tabled it as a probing amendment. I did not detect in either the tone or content of what he said a desire deliberately to undermine the clause's effectiveness as it relates to people's entitlements.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Aberdeen, Central (Mr. Doran) said, there are 30,000 workers in the offshore industry but only a few hundred work in cross-boundary situations. The cross-boundary areas are mainly between the United Kingdom on the one hand and Norway, the Netherlands and Ireland on the other. The clause enables us to extend the Bill to offshore workers by an Order in Council. That follows the standard pattern for the application of employment law such as the Employment Rights Act 1996 to employers and employees in the offshore industry.

It is not a unique process. The Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992, extended protection to offshore workers under the employment protection (offshore employment) order 1976. We are seeking to use processes that have been tried and tested by previous Administrations and found to operate effectively in what can be, as the hon. Member for Daventry said, complex areas of activity.

The clause applies to employment in UK territorial waters but it also applies to employment connected with the exploration of the seabed or exploitation of natural resources in the UK sector of the continental shelf, as designated by the Continental Shelf Act 1964, and to employment connected with the exploration or exploitation of, for example, an oil or gas field in a foreign sector of the continental shelf, where that field extends across the boundary between UK and foreign sectors.

My interpretation of that in layman's terms--the interpretation of the hon. Member for Daventry might be different--is that there is a line across the floor of the continental shelf which an operation occasionally straddles. We want to ensure that if that happens, it is not to the detriment of people's employment rights.

Subsection (4) is therefore a good and uncontroversial safety provision which follows standard legal practice. It ensures that any new jurisdiction conferred by subsection (3)(c) concerning enforcement through the courts or employment tribunals for offshore workers does not prejudice exisiting rights. It is about the avoidance of doubt and ensuring cover in the court or in a tribunal.

Mr. Ian McCartney: You will have to excuse me, Mrs. Dunwoody, but I think that I have the flu coming on.

All the questions asked have been reasonable. On the question of rural employment and whether people are covered by the agricultural wages board, the regime specifies which workers it covers.

The Bill's purpose is to define people as workers. As a consequence, all workers, irrespective of the nature of employment or the task being done, will be covered by the national minimum wage. In practice, that means that the national minimum wage will include everyone in agriculture and, therefore, that it will become the floor below which people cannot be paid. That is all it means. That will be of great benefit to many occupations in agriculture.

For example, it will ensure that gang workers earn a minimum wage, which is a matter of great concern. Indeed, the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food is about to complete an investigation into the misuse of gang workers by gangmasters and others, which leads to poor employment practices, pay and conditions.

Packhouse workers undertake a mixture of agricultural and non-agricultural work and are paid piece rates. Again, the minimum wage will work in unison with the Agricultural Wages Board regime to stop the exploitation of packhouse workers by gangmasters and others. They will be special categories of workers under the minimum wage.

Mr. Hammond: Will the Minister accept that it might be difficult to define whether a packhouse worker is an agricultural worker, given the difficulties to which my hon. Friend the Member for Ashford referred?

Mr. McCartney: The point is that there will be no such difficulty because those workers will be covered by the national minimum wage, which will be the basic floor. There will be no such problems of definition. The purpose of meshing the regimes is to provide an absolute floor--the national minimum wage.

That is also true for those agricultural contract workers who undertake a mixture of agricultural and non-agricultural work, for whom determining whether they are entitled to regional levels of pay is a constant problem. With the bringing together of the two regimes, the national minimum wage will provide those workers with a basic floor. Such examples show that the intention is to mesh the two systems to ensure that individuals of whatever designation in the agricultural sector are paid at least the national minimum wage. That is a fair way to proceed.

The hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge asked whether the operation Agricultural Wages Board will lead to sectoral rates. The answer is no. The minimum wage will apply to all employees, including agricultural workers. Therefore, it will provide no more and no less a floor in agriculture.

I hope that I have answered all the questions asked by the hon. Member for Daventry. If not, I shall write to him. He asked why powers are being sought to amend pay in agriculture. Those powers are needed on a contingency basis. We are bringing together two blocks of legislation--that on agricultural wages and the minimum wage--and the honest truth is that there might be certain frictions. It will not become clear whether agricultural wages legislation needs to be changed until the national minimum wage has been set.

Again, in an intervening on the hon. Member for Daventry, the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge asked an interesting question about the consequential amendments. I understand that amendments might be consequential on the regulations setting up the national minimum wage as well as the agricultural wage. We do not know, as of now, what they will be, so they cannot be included in the Bill. However, under clause 44(2) and subsection (3), we have powers to deal with consequential amendments. Such matters will come before the House for consideration.

Again, the hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge asked whether the national minimum wage will include benefits in kind in agriculture. I can give him another straightforward answer: no. Final decisions have been taken and we shall review the situation in the light of the Low Pay Commission's advice before dealing with the regulations. Industrial relations such as pay and conditions will be reserved matters. We therefore intend that in exercising its power to legislate on wages and conditions of services of agricultural workers in Scotland, the Scottish Parliament will not be able to act in a way that undermines or conflicts with the national minimum wage. The Bill will therefore not undermine agricultural workers or the non-agricultural workers who work in conjunction with that sector.
