There are several ways in which schedule 13 will damage the secondary market andthe whole principle of reinvestment relief. Several amendments have been tabled that try to mitigate that damage.

Amendment No. 137 attempts to limit that damage by raising the limit from £10 million to £20 million. I shall deal now with the defects of that specific £10 million limit. It is faulty for three reasons. First, it is entirely arbitrary. It has been conjured out of the air; it does not exist anywhere else in the Bill. The parallel with venture capital trusts cannot be justified. Secondly, it is extremely poorly defined: the gross asset tests discriminate against companies that are involved in manufacturing and are in favour of companies that are involved in services. The definition of "relevant assets" is left completely vague. I cannot find it in the schedule. I have read to the end of the schedule and ploughed through the various definitions. Relevant period and relevant shares are defined, but I cannot find a new definition of the phrase "relevant assets" . Perhaps the Economic Secretary can help the Committee. The problem is that this definition of the gross asset test will discriminate against manufacturing companies that have substantial assets such as capital equipment and in favour of service companies that can be floated on the alternative investment market with virtually no assets at all.

The third problem with the £10 million limit is that it is too low. As my hon. Friend the Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton) explained, a limit of £10 million leaves a huge funding gap for companies that have asset values of £20 million, £30 million, £40 million or £50 million. They, too, urgently need investment and have high risk stocks, which were the original purpose of reinvestment relief. It was designed to compensate investors because of the risky nature of the investment involved. Such risks apply to companies with assets of £20 million or £30 million just as they do to those with assets of £10 million. If the limit is fixed at only £10 million, that leaves a huge funding gap for companies with asset values of more than £10 million but less than £75 million.

That may be seen as the second half of the argument in favour of AIM shares. They have been hit by two factors first, by the limitation on new issues and, secondly, by the fact that when investors have gone into these shares they cannot, unlike in the past, subsequently sell them on to another investor who would enjoy the same tax incentive. That is of great concern to those in the industry. One such has written to me warning that there is the potential for a major disaster for AIM which should not be discounted lightly. That is because AIM has done very well under the bull market that started under the previous Government. It is a great success story. Part of that success springs from liquidity. Liquidity is partly related to size, as we discussed earlier, but it also relates to the knowledge that the attractiveness that the initial investor has in a share can also be passed on to others because they get the tax relief on capital gains, if not the relief on income.

Before the Economic Secretary pre-empts our amendments, I should like to expand my argument. Why just shares on AIM? Why not small quoted companies as well? It is an anomaly that, under the new rules, companies on AIM, some of which are larger than the small quoted company sector, can continue to enjoy the tax benefits for which the Bill provides, when small companies, which may begin to grow, but opt for the small quoted market, are excluded from the benefit.

The Economic Secretary may be able to give us better advice, but I am not sure whether the amendment covers small quoted companies. That is its aim. To cite information from experts in the market, the average size of a transaction in a small quoted company is below £7,500. Deals that exceed that figure drive the market price down. Liquidity is important. If we remove that, and the free flow of shares, we also knock the price of shares and make it harder for companies to raise money initially.

The Government assume that people will invest in highly speculative shares, receive tax deferment rather than tax relief, and, in the long run, after the taper relief provisions take effect, enjoy a modest reduction in tax liability. But even the system for that is far from perfect.

People who invest in such shares are strong, brave investors, but they are not necessarily 10-year, or long-term investors. Their skill is in spotting opportunities when companies emerge, and passing them on to more prudent, cautious investors when they prove the value of their judgment. They then redeploy their capital in other new companies. Tying down that skill and investment to their first choices, or first punts, for want of a better word, through fear of a massive capital gains tax bill, will affect not only the liquidity of the market, but the supply of funds to those new companies that the Government claim they wish to help.

The Economic Secretary has given a rather arduous performance this evening because she knows that she is on extremely weak ground. The Government have not done their homework. The clauses are a mish-mash, and many members of the investing public, as well as their professional representatives, have written to me to point out anomaly after anomaly in the scheme.

If there was only one reason for the view in the financial press that the AIM market was hit by the Budget, it would be the Government's decision to deny the right to any incentive to invest in shares that had already begun trading.

It is evident that the scheme that the previous Government introduced has enabled a great deal of money to go into shares, and has also enabled the AIM market to succeed. The provision poses a danger to the market perhaps not tomorrow or the next day but when the Government's failures and mismanagement of the economy hit wider sectors of the economy. They have already hit the manufacturing sector. When the bullishness of the market is blow apart, the unquoted sector in particular may suffer catastrophic decline. If that happens, the memory of that catastrophic decline will prevent the re-emergence of such a market for a long time. The Government have the opportunity to make a small but important change to this ill-considered and ill-drafted set of measures. If they do not do so, they will be most short-sighted.
