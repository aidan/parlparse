Mr. John Whittingdale (Maldon and East Chelmsford): We have already heard a number of good speeches from Opposition members of the Committee in support of the amendments, so I shall not go over those arguments again. However, I should like to touch on a few points that have yet to be made fully.

The key issue is that the differential between the price of tobacco products in this country and on the continent is encouraging smuggling on a huge scale. It is true that the previous Government implemented a policy of increasing the price by means of an escalator. However, there comes a point when the profit that can be made as a result of that differential increases dramatically, and I believe that we have now passed that point.

A few weeks ago I visited Adinkerke, in Belgium, with the Tobacco Alliance. [Interruption.] I should tell Government members of the Committee that the Tobacco Alliance represents 26,000 independent cigarette retailers. As a result of the Government's policy, many such retailers are suffering. Indeed, many are being driven out of business by the current extent of smuggling.

With some independent retailers, I visited Eastenders, a warehouse store with which some hon. Members may be familiar, run by Dave West and his wife Kathy. Eastenders specialises in the selling in bulk of cigarettes and hand-rolling tobacco, and its customers are exclusively British. During my visit I saw a steady stream of vehicles, all of which had British registration plates, loading large quantities of tobacco. The journey to Eastenders is remarkably easy one crosses the channel on Eurostar and drives a short distance across the Belgian border and takes no longer than a couple of hours.

I saw a man place £1,000 in cash on the counter at Eastenders to buy hand-rolling tobacco. In Belgium, hand-rolling tobacco costs £2 a pack; in this country it costs £8 a pack. Therefore he paid £1,000 for a quantity of hand-rolling tobacco that would retail at £4,000 in the United Kingdom. He duly loaded the tobacco into the boot of his car and I asked him whether that was something that he did regularly. He replied, "Oh yes about once every three months."

As the Minister knows, it is perfectly legal to import tobacco into this country for one's own consumption, but it strains credulity to imagine that that man intended to consume £4,000-worth of hand-rolling tobacco in three months. He was merely one of hundreds of such visitors to Eastenders, which is open 24 hour a day.

Mrs. West, a delightful lady, told me that she and her husband celebrated when the Budget was announced they were probably the only people in the world who did. For them, it represented a real opportunity to increase sales and make more profit. Mr. Dave West said:

    "There's brands of cigarettes on sale here in Belgium that Belgians don't smoke, so it's obvious that these cigarettes are destined for British people and are going back to England. It's very clear, the amounts of cigarettes that are sold for example, we sell now I think it's one third of all the cigarettes and tobacco that's sold in the entirety of Belgium. Now, we don't have any Belgian customers."

That is what is happening at the small-scale end of the business. People are filling up transit vans, Land Rovers or the backs of their cars, for themselves and their friends, and to make a few bob selling the cigarettes in the pub car park.

Unfortunately, that is not the only aspect of the business. There is now a great deal of involvement by organised crime. There are worrying reports that the IRA has now moved into the business in Ireland. I should like to draw the attention of the Committee to a recent television programme, "Tonight with Trevor McDonald" , which looked into the matter. The reporter said:

    "It's Wednesday morning in Dover docks, and a massive load of six million cigarettes has been discovered inside a container lorry, all disguised as an innocent consignment of kiwi fruit. Cigarette smuggling is now a major underground industry; it's run by organised crime on an international scale. The techniques they use are those of the drug smugglers and the attraction is that if you get caught, cigarettes, unlike drugs, carry low penalties."

The reporter set up a deal with a smuggler. He contacted someone named David through another gentleman known as Torquay Paul, and arranged to purchase 200,000 cigarettes at less than half the legal price. He said that David gave them a rendezvous point near the M25 in Essex, where they were to meet another gang member, Geordie Denis. For the purposes of the programme, the deal was set up, and at the end, the reporter made himself known to Geordie Denis. I shall not quote what Geordie Denis said I shall leave that to the imagination of members of the Committee.

That was a graphic demonstration of the hugh scale of the business now being conducted. We are heading rapidly towards the same problem that we began to experience a few years ago with hand-rolling tobacco. Eight years ago, nearly all hand-rolling tobacco sales were fully duty-paid. The proportion is now 25 per cent. Seventy-five per cent. of hand-rolling tobacco sales are now non-duty-paid the vast bulk is illegally imported. The Chancellor recognised the problem to some extent and did not increase the duty on it, although he did increase duty on other things.

The amendment would cut the duty on hand-rolling tobacco to what it was when the Government took office. There is no doubt that the hand-rolling tobacco market has been lost. Very few legal hand-rolling tobacco sales are taking place. The retailers, and the Tobacco Alliance, now say that they do not sell it. No one buys it any longer, because they can get it so much more cheaply through the black market. That is happening not only here but in Sweden, which, because of its accession to the European Union, was required to increase the duty on cigarettes dramatically. The first increase was 25 per cent. in January 1997, followed in August 1997 by a further 29 per cent., which resulted in an overall excise duty increase of 62.5 per cent.

The result was a huge increase in smuggling. The Swedish customs board estimated that 39.3 million cigarettes were smuggled into Sweden in 1997, compared with an estimated six million in 1995. A senior customs official stated:

    "organised crime is focusing more on cigarettes than narcotics today because the profit is better and the risk is minimal."

The parallel with events in this country is clear.

When the Swedish Government were confronted by the problem, they took dramatic action. They cut the rate of duty in the 1998 budget by approximately 30 per cent. In the budget proposal, the Swedish Government said:

    "Since cigarette prices on the Swedish market are so high compared with neighbouring countries and the prices of cigarettes sold illegally, the profitability of cross-border trade and smuggling will continue to be great enough so as to necessitate additional measures. It is therefore important to reduce the prices on the legal cigarette market."

We are not proposing to cut the duty today, but there is no doubt that dramatic measures are needed. I hope that the Government will look closely at what happens in Sweden as a result of its decision. Preliminary evidence shows that the proportion of the market taken by legal sales has started to increase following that cut in duty.

This is a serious problem and it will get worse. I draw the Financial Secretary's attention to information provided by Customs and Excise, for which her Department is responsible. The Public and Commercial Services Union, which represents Customs and Excise employees, said in its briefing paper:

    "The published estimate for 1996 stated that 1.5 per cent. of the market in cigarettes was lost through cross-channel smuggling. By 1998 the figure had risen to 10 per cent. The Department now estimates that present trends suggest that smuggled cigarettes will soon comprise 30 per cent. of the UK market."

I have already pointed out that 75 per cent. of hand-rolling tobacco is smuggled. The PCSU also said:

    "There is evidence that the Italian Mafia and organised Eastern European gangs are involved as well as UK criminals."

I conclude by drawing the Committee's attention to an excellent speech which made those points well. In the 1995 Finance Bill debate on tobacco duty, the current Paymaster General said:

    "As they did during the debate on alcohol, the Government completely close their eyes to the implications of their tobacco taxation policy and the way in which it will provide opportunities for tobacco smuggling. Smuggling not only undermines the industry here, and therefore will eventually weaken what tax base there is, but undermines the Government's health policy, because the contraband goods are unregulated and available to the very groups for whom the Government's health policy, "The Health of the Nation", sets targets to reduce smoking the 11 to 15-year-old age range.

    By pursuing the tobacco industry, through taxation, the Government are undermining that. We have made clear our views on the health aspects, on curtailing smoking and encouraging people not to smoke, but taxation is not the way to do that."

That was what the Paymaster General said just four years ago. She concluded by saying:

    "The industry itself has produced substantial and convincing arguments. I say that as a committed anti-smoker. The information provided by the tobacco industry shows only too clearly the disastrous route down which the Government's taxation policy is taking us. We will lose control of tax. We will lose control, through tobacco products, of our health policy and we will undermine considerably the country's industry." [Official Report, 23 January 1995; Vol. 253, c. 106-7.]

If that was the Labour party's view in 1995, surely it must be its view today, because the problem is far worse. There is evidence that the proportion of people in this country who smoke has started to increase again. In 1994, the general household survey put that proportion at 27 per cent. and in 1996 at 28 per cent. Latest research shows that in 1997 it stood at 28.2 per cent. and in 1998 at 28.5 per cent. The proportion of people who smoke is beginning to increase. It must be apparent that a major determinant of those figures is the increase in smuggling and the ease with which it is possible to obtain cut-price cigarettes illegally.

The policy of increasing tobacco taxation year after year is failing. The proportion of the population who smoke is increasing, businesses are being driven under by illegal imports and the Government's revenue from tobacco tax is forecast to fall. If the policy is failing to that extent, surely it makes sense to stop and reconsider it. That is why we propose a freeze on tobacco duties and a full re-examination of the whole issue. Unless that is done, the problem will simply get worse.
