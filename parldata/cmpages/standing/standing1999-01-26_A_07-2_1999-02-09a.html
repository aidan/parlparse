Clause 73

Calculation of basic amount of tax

Mr Forth: I beg to move amendment No. 351, in page 39, leave out lines 28 to 30.

This is a highly technical matter. I shall therefore on this occasion stick to my script extremely closely--the Committee will realise why once I am about two sentences into it.

In certain circumstances, the clause would give rise to a negative amount as a result of the calculations. In this event, as the clause is currently worded, it would mean that the negative amount calculated would effectively be written off, as the only possible amount to be stated other than a positive one is "nil". Using the word "nil" does not allow for any negative requirement to be reported. The result of not reporting or writing off a negative requirement--here comes the point--is that if the funds to which that related were not available for use in any other manner, they would effectively be lost to the taxpayers of London. The amendment would allow for the reporting of a negative requirement, and the subsequent redistribution of such funds to the benefit of the taxpayers of London.

I hope that that is as clear to the Minister as it is to me. It raises an important point, which at the very least deserves clarification, if only to satisfy the Committee and those outside that in no sense in such, perhaps unusual, circumstances would the money be lost, but that it would be retained and available for the beneficial use of Londoners.

Mr. Edward Davey: I am surprised that the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst needed to refer to his notes--members of the Committee have become used to learning the skills of debate from him because he is normally so clear and precise without the assistance of notes. However, I shall try to put his words into plain English.

The amendment would give the authority power to notify the billing authorities that they should levy, on the GLA's behalf, a negative council tax precept--that is, reduce the tax burden on London's council taxpayers. The beneficial aspect of that process is that no extra billing would be required for the refunds. The negative council tax precept could appear on the council tax bills that are sent out to Londoners, and the council tax demand that they receive from their local authorities would he reduced by the negative amount of the precept. That would ensure that the GLA would become super popular and win the support of the people of London.

The Government have told us many times that they intend the authority to be efficient, lean and unbureaucratic. If that turns out to be the case, it is likely that it will make savings and will therefore be able to propose a negative council tax precept. I anticipate that the Minister will reply that a negative precept is unlikely to occur. That may be because Ministers intend to ensure, through the Bill's other provisions, that they control the GLA's finances in such a way that it is almost impossible to imagine a situation in which it would be possible to pay back a negative precept to London's council taxpayers. However, let us cast our minds ahead five or 10 years into the future. The House of Commons may by then have given the GLA different powers by which it is able to raise revenue of its own and may be in a position to pay back revenues if it has, through efficient management, generated a surplus. The Government should not, through clause 73, strike that power from the Bill.

Ms Glenda Jackson: Yet again, an amendment seeks to change a key aspect of the local government finance regime of the GLA, which--in common with all local authorities--cannot set a negative amount of council tax. Despite the contributions of the right hon Member for Bromley and Chislehurst and the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton, I cannot conceive of a case for treating the GLA differently from local authorities in general.

The amendment would allow for the GLA's basic amount of council tax for non-police services to be negative. However, it is surely right that local taxes should contribute towards the maintenance and upgrade of vital local services--services that are in dire need of improvement in London. I would find it perverse to agree to the amendment. The hon. Members who tabled it apparently intend that the GLA should slash much-needed services and give money away. Despite the interpretation that was given by the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton, I do not think that that would make the GLA particularly popular with Londoners. The amendment is therefore unacceptable, and I urge the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst to withdraw it.

Mr. Forth: I am grateful for what the Minister has said thus far, but I would like her to say one further thing to reassure us. Can she guarantee that there are no circumstances in which money that has been gathered from Londoners in the form of tax, or which has been earmarked through the normal procedures for expenditure by the London authority, could be lost through an accounting mechanism such as we have discussed? I would like an assurance from the Minister that in all circumstances the moneys that were intended to be spent by the authority on behalf of Londoners will be so spent. if she can give us that assurance, I will gladly seek to leave to withdraw my amendment.

Ms Jackson: I am delighted to give the right hon. Gentleman that assurance; the only guarantees that I can give him are death and taxes.
