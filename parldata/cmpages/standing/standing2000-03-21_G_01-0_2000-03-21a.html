Clause 1

Park trading offence

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport (Mr. Alan Howarth): Before addressing the clause Mr. Sayeed, I welcome your chairmanship, under which I have every confidence that our proceedings will develop harmoniously and fruitfully to an expeditious conclusion. It is important that the details of the Bill, which we discussed in general principle in a Second Reading Committee should be considered carefully. I am pleased that we have moved so quickly to the Committee stage-the Bill is important and was strongly supported by all parties on Second Reading-and I hope that its progress will continue to be uncontentious.

I shall briefly set the context. Illegal trading in the royal parks is a nuisance. It is dirty, smelly and unhealthy and undermines the legitimate businesses sanctioned by the Secretary of State. The public must be protected from unprincipled traders, and the powers available to do so are inadequate. The Bill will provide powers of enforcement similar to those exercised by the City of Westminster, which Parliament approved in the City of Westminster Act 1999. The Bill will increase the fines that magistrates courts can levy, and introduce a power of seizure, which has been used effectively by the City of Westminster. We have equally sought to provide safeguards that protect the rights of the owner of any object seized.

The clause allows the Secretary of State, as one of the successors to the commissioners of works, to designate provisions of regulations made under section 2(1) of the Parks Regulation (Amendment) Act 1926 as park trading regulations. That designation will, in turn, be achieved by regulations made under the 1926 Act. Any failure to comply with a park trading regulation will subsequently be a park trading offence. I commend the clause to the Committee.

Clause 1 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 2

Maximum penalty.

Mr. Howarth: In view of our discussion on clause 1, and the fact that I feel suitably admonished by the hon. Member for Richmond Park (Dr. Tonge), I shall be extremely brief. I hope that my comments on the clause will fill not more than six lines of a column in Hansard.

The clause provides that in the case of park trading offences, failure to comply with, or contravention of, regulations made under section 2(1) of the 1926 Act will involve a maximum penalty not exceeding

    level 3 on the standard scale

which carries a fine of £1,000. The current maximum penalty involves level 1 on the standard scale, which carries a fine of £200. Magistrates courts generally impose much lower fines but even the maximum fine of £200 is no deterrent. The courts need to have much higher penalties available to them.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 2 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 3

Offence by body corporate

Mr. Howarth: The clause deals with the commission of park trading offences by officers of bodies corporate. In a case in which a park trading offence proves to have been carried out by a body corporate, and a director, manager, secretary or other similar officer or person purporting to act in any of those capacities is proved to have consented to, connived at or caused by neglect the commission of the offence, that person and the body corporate will be guilty of the offence. Our intention is to prosecute the person who sells goods illegally, the owners of such businesses and those who profit from such acts.

Clause 4

Seizure of property

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Howarth: The clause will give my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State and the Royal Parks Constabulary the power to seize items that appear to have been used in the commission of a park trading offence. The scope of this power will not apply to anything of a perishable nature.

Subsection (1) requires that a park constable must reasonably suspect that a person has committed a park trading offence before the power of seizure becomes exercisable. Subsection (2) adds the rider that a constable may exercise such a power only in the park in which he has jurisdiction. If those two conditions are met, subsection (1) gives the parks constable the discretion to seize anything that the trader has in his or her possession or under his or her control, which the constable reasonably believes to have been used in the commission of a park trading offence. All members of the Royal Parks Constabulary and all members of the Metropolitan police will have the authority to enforce this power in all the royal parks.

Mr. Brooke: We have not tabled an amendment to the clause, but I want to ask the Minister a probing question. The words in the Bill and the Minister's words emphasise goods of a non-perishable nature. It is reasonable for us to ask the Minister what will happen to perishable goods. Will they simply be dumped in the royal parks? The constables will be aware that their powers do not extend to perishable goods, so we can envisage that they will simply be deposited by the stall owner and left for somebody else to clear up.

It would be helpful if the Minister could give a more graphic account of what will happen to perishable goods. Unless there is a strong reason why they have been left out of the Bill, there would seem to be a case for affording the power to destroy them as a way of bringing them to an end, but there may be a legal objection to that.

Mr. Howarth: The right hon. Gentleman has asked an understandable question. The Royal Parks Constabulary will follow the same procedures as City of Westminster council officials, who offer the unlicensed trader the opportunity to take the perishable goods away. Almost all traders accept that invitation, probably because the goods have a monetary value to them: traders tend to be charged for their stock by the people managing or running them. The trader is asked to sign a form acknowledging that he is either taking or refusing to take the perishables, whichever applies. If he refuses to take them, it is on the understanding that they will be destroyed by the enforcing authority to protect environmental health and the general amenity of the surroundings.

Mr. Brooke: I am grateful to the Minister for his clear reply. I am embarrassed to say, as the local Member of Parliament, that I am not familiar with every word of the Westminster legislation. Is what the Minister just described in the statute, or is it simply an agreement, which is already hallowed by custom?

Mr. Howarth: My understanding is that it is in the statute.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 5

Retention and disposal

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Howarth: The clause deals with the retention and possible disposal of items seized pursuant to clause 4. Subsection (1) allows the Secretary of State to retain anything seized pursuant to clause 4 until the end of the period of 28 days beginning with the date of seizure. Subsections (2) and (3) specify the circumstances in which the Secretary of State may retain seized items beyond the 28-day period. That can happen only if proceedings for a park trading offence have been commenced against the person from whom the items were seized, in respect of his or her activities at the time of the seizure. If such proceedings have not begun within the 28-day period, the power of retention will no longer apply. If proceedings have begun within 28 days, the Secretary of State may retain the items seized until the conclusion of the proceedings, including any appeal. In the event of the court making an award of costs to be paid by the accused to the Secretary of State, the Secretary of State may retain the items until those costs have been paid.

Subsection (4) ensures that the powers of retention are subject to any order for forfeiture of the seized items made under clause 6. Subsection (5) provides that if the award of costs in the Secretary of State's favour is not paid in full within a further 28 days following the conclusion of court proceedings, including any appeal, the Secretary of State may sell the item at the best price that can be reasonably obtained, in order to pay such costs. Any balance should be paid to the former owner. Subsection (6) provides for seized items to be returned to their owner or owners if the Secretary of State ceases to be entitled to retain them, subject to any order of forfeiture made under clause 6.

Subsection (7) details the procedure that will be followed if the owner of seized items cannot subsequently be identified.

Subsection (5) gives the Secretary of State the power to sell any seized item if it is still in police possession 28 days after the conclusion of any court proceedings. As the Minister said, the Secretary of State must pay any balance, net of costs, to the person whom he believes to be the rightful owner. Gentleman, to ensure that anything I might say would be acceptable to him.

However, he was careless to indicate that that was his intention in May last year, as his office did not contact me until towards the end of May, which suggests that acceleration was not the principal stimulus. However, that is another matter.

I referred to the Select Committee on Northern Ireland because it conducted an inquiry last year into a similar issue-that of the sale of smuggled fuel oil across the Irish border. The vehicles in which that fuel is smuggled are sometimes conventional, but frequently not-as is the wont of Irish affairs. The most extraordinary vehicles can be reconfigured in order to carry fuel oil illegally, and the Select Committee report recommended that Customs and Excise's selling of such reconfigured vehicles back into the market at auction was not the swiftest way to bring that trade to an end. If they were taken out of the market and destroyed, any new vehicle to be used for the purpose would have to be reconfigured and could not simply be picked up ready made.

I acknowledge that the example of vehicles used in the Republic and in Northern Ireland is not an exact analogy, because they have been reconfigured to suit that trade, whereas a hot dog stand is a hot dog stand. However, it raises the question whether it makes sense to sell off hot dog carts if our purpose is to stamp out illegal trading. It could be argued that selling them at auction will simply perpetuate the trade that the Bill aims to prevent. Mr. Howarth: The right hon. Gentleman raises an excellent and practical point. I offer him a reassurance that I hope that he and the Committee will find satisfactory. The Northern Ireland case that he cited is not a bad analogy, and we must avoid causing the kind of sequence of events that he has drawn to our attention.

I shall cite the current practice of City of Westminster council in this regard, to illustrate that we do not anticipate creating a secondary market in hot dog stands. It will be within the power of the courts and of the Secretary of State to ensure that the hot dog carts and the burger barrows are destroyed. It is City of Westminster council's practice to have such barrows crushed and sold to a scrap metal dealer. It prosecuted about 3,000 illegal traders in 1999, and in about 700 cases it seized barrows, of which about 70 per cent.-about 500-were burger barrows. In 95 per cent. of those cases, the court awarded forfeiture.

In almost all the cases, the council was unable to ascertain the identity of the barrows' owners. It always asks, when a barrow is seized, but the traders will not give the owner's name. That being so, Westminster has the barrows crushed and sells them to a scrap metal dealer. The practice of putting them up for auction does not occur. We shall have the power, and the will, to ensure that that does not occur in circumstances arising from the seizure or forfeiture of barrows in the royal parks.

Clause 6

Forfeiture

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Howarth: The clause deals with the issue of forfeiture and will apply when a person has been convicted of a park trading offence. Under subsections (1) and (2), the court in which the offender was convicted may order forfeiture of anything which

    (a) was seized under section 4,

    (b) is retained by the Secretary of State under section 5, and

    (c) the court believes to have been used in the commission of the offence.

    Subsection (3)(a) will require that

    Before making an order for the forfeiture of a thing a court shall-

    (a) permit anyone who claims to be its owner or to have an interest in it to make representations.

    Subsection 3(b) will require the court also to consider the value of the item in question

    and the likely consequences of forfeiture.
