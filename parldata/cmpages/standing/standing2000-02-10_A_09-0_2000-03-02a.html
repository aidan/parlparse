Motion made and Question proposed, That further consideration be now adjourned.—[Mr. Pope.]

Mr. Christopher Chope (Christchurch): It is rather odd for that motion to be moved, given that people have assembled here in the expectation that proceedings are to continue all day, with sittings in the morning and the afternoon. Does the Government's desire to adjourn the Committee have anything to do with a story that appears in today's Financial Times under the headline ``Utilities regulation bill to exclude telecoms''? It states:

    Stephen Byers, trade and industry secretary, will tell the Commons today that telecommunications is to be dropped from the utilities bill, after a revolt by telecoms companies. Water is also expected to be removed.

I shall not continue with the rest of the text. However, if any part of the House is to be told of the withdrawal of substantial parts of the Bill, the Committee should be told first. I hope that we shall receive the courtesy of such a statement from the Minister before the Committee adjourns.

This is a humiliating day for the Government. It shows that their legislation is in a state of chaos, and is a great endorsement of the vigorous way in which my hon. Friend the Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) has pursued the Government on these issues.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): With permission, Mrs. Roe, I shall make a brief statement, which follows the text of the statement that is to be made in the Chamber later today during Trade and Industry questions by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State.

Mrs. Eleanor Laing (Epping Forest): On a point of order, Mrs. Roe. How can a statement be made during questions?

The Chairman: Order. What happens in the House is nothing to do with the Committee.

Dr. Howells: Thank you, Mrs. Roe. When any of us open our mouths we make statements, and I meant the word in that sense. I shall rephrase my remark. What I am about to say is based on what my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State will say later.

As I have informed the Committee on previous occasions, the Government keep under review all measures that may increase the burden of regulation on business. As a result, we have been considering the views that have been expressed by the telecoms operators—and, indeed, the opinions that have been voiced in Committee—on the Bill. We intend to publish a communications White Paper later this year. My right hon. Friends and I believe that there is a powerful argument for tackling the necessary reforms in one vehicle, and have therefore decided to remove the telecoms provisions from the Bill.

In addition, we shall remove the provisions relating to water, and the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions will instead bring forward measures in a draft water Bill, covering those matters, which it intends to publish later this year. That will enable Parliament to consider proposed changes to the regulatory regime for water alongside other proposals resulting from the review of competition in that sector.

Mr. Norman Baker (Lewes): That was an interesting statement.

I welcome back the Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe, who has been unwell, and hope that she is fully recovered. We have missed her. I am sorry that she has come back for an adjournment, and hope that she does not feel that her time has been wasted this morning.

It is odd for a Bill to be filleted in this way after such a short time. Has the Minister only just found out about the telecommunications White Paper that is to be published later this year? It has been known about for some time; indeed, I have raised it in Committee and with the Department for Culture, Media and Sport. It is obvious that telecommunications needs to be dealt with through that process, and it is bizarre that no consideration was given to that. I would have expected the Government either to decide that the inclusion of telecoms regulation in the Bill was consistent with the White Paper to be published later this year, or to have analysed the matter and decided that it was inconsistent. It seems odd that they have not analysed it, but decided a short way into the progress of the Bill that it does not fit. That is not joined-up government but joined-up chaos.

In respect of water provisions, I made a suggestion early on in this Committee, which was pooh-poohed by the Minister, that there might be a difference of opinion between the Department of Trade and Industry and the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions on whether the regulation should proceed under this Government. I suggested that the DTI was in favour of light-touch regulation and the liberalisation of markets, whereas the DETR was rather more conservative in terms of regulation, and that the Deputy Prime Minister's view on regulation might be different from that of the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry.

It now appears that the Deputy Prime Minister wishes to take the matter back to the Government and to deal with water under a separate Bill, as and when time is available for such legislation. There is no guarantee of such time in the near future. We do not know what kind of legislative pressure the Government are under. The Fur Farming (Prohibition) Bill has been withdrawn; we do not know where that has gone. We were promised a ban on hunting; we do not know what has happened to that. All sorts of Bills are being stacked up in the Government's list of legislation, and there now seems to be another one from the DETR.

Why was the Bill not more carefully analysed by the Government before being brought before the Committee? It is an extremely complicated Bill, and my hon. Friend the Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell) has expressed the view that a degree of pre-legislative scrutiny would have been appropriate. Why was that not done?

Given the Bill's complexity, the Government must listen to the views of the telecoms operators and analyse the position in relation to the proposed communications White Paper. It would probably be the right decision at this point to pull telecommunications from the Bill. If the DETR is not going to play ball, it is probably sensible to let it take responsibility for its own legislation, if it so wishes. We are in a mess, and we must ask ourselves how we arrived there. One wonders whether the Bill can survive at all, or whether the Government should go back to the drawing board and re-examine the whole issue. When one dismantles what is supposed to be a cohesive unit, do the threads that are left hang together in an integrated way? The Government should not only adjourn the Committee, but return to the drawing board and consider whether it is worth our while proceeding with what is left of the Bill.

Moreover, factors relating to the environment and other matters are also significant.
