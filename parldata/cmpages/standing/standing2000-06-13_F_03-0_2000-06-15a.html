Limited Liability Partnerships Bill [Lords]

Clause 10

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

2.30 pm

Question again proposed.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): There has been a brief interregnum. Nevertheless, I think that I have answers to most of the questions that have been asked. The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) asked two questions that revolved around transparency on conversion between limited liability partnerships and ordinary partnerships. I can confirm both his points.

The hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett) asked about disincorporation. The provisions allow partnerships to become limited liability partnerships without any tax charge arising. In the jargon, the conversion is tax-neutral. I think that he also asked about companies becoming limited liability partnerships. The Bill provides that limited liability partnerships will be taxed in the same way as ordinary partnerships. A company does not enjoy tax neutrality if it becomes a partnership, and the Bill reflects that treatment.

The hon. Gentleman's other questions, are linked. The phasing out of the cash basis will be covered in an article in the Inland Revenue bulletin. The transitional period for phasing out the cash basis will continue. Interest on loans taken out to buy interests in limited liability partnerships is allowable against total income, subject to the clear limits set out in the clause.

The hon. Gentleman asked about transparency if a limited liability partnership ceases to carry on trade or business. In that case, it becomes a body corporate and is taxed as such, by corporation tax instead of income tax. During an orderly winding up, as opposed to liquidation, it is likely that a business will continue, so the transparency will remain.

The hon. Member for Eddisbury (Mr. O'Brien) turned the question on its head by asking about limited liability partnerships that convert to limited companies. The position is the same as for a partnership that becomes a company, which means no tax neutrality.

The hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon asked about liquidation and rollover relief for business assets. That is highly technical stuff. If the Committee bears with me, I shall try to answer the question.

Proposed new section 59A of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 applies only when the limited liability partnership is carrying on a business. In liquidation, the limited liability partnership ceases to carry on a business, and the assets are in the hands of the liquidator. When the limited liability partnership goes into liquidation, it will be taxed under normal corporate rules. The liquidator will be taxed on disposal of limited liability partnership assets by reference to the original acquisition cost and date of the asset by the limited liability partnership. The limited liability partnership members each have only one asset, which is their interest in the limited liability partnership. They will be taxed as individuals on any gain or loss that arises on the disposal of that interest during liquidation.

We were asked to confirm that a limited liability partnership member who realises a limited liability partnership asset on the sale of a business and acquires an asset in another business will not be charged on that gain. We can confirm that rollover relief will be available in the usual way.

Mr. John Burnett (Torridge and West Devon): I am extremely grateful to the Minister for mentioning those matters. I shall turn to what I call disincorporation. The Minister will know that one suffers significant tax problems, particularly with capital gains tax, in, for example, winding up a limited company and transferring the business into a limited liability partnership. That is a penalty of disincorporation. I do not seek a detailed answer from the Minister, but would be grateful if he simply started the ball rolling by saying whether the Department of Trade and Industry and the Inland Revenue can come to some arrangement on or start the process of facilitating such action. It has caused a problem for many years.

The Chairman: Order. I asked this morning for brief interventions. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will now make his point.

Mr. Burnett: In that case, the best thing that I can do is wait for the Minister to finish his speech. Then I shall return to make various points.

Dr. Howells: I take it that the hon. Gentleman was referring to what happens to transparency if the limited liability partnership ceases to carry on a trade, profession or business. The transparency cannot continue, because the limited liability partnership becomes a body corporate and is taxed as such. It is no longer a partnership operating to provide a service.

Mr. Burnett: I shall not labour the point much more. I hope that the DTI and the Treasury will examine facilitating disincorporation, especially now that individuals can transfer from trading through a limited liability company to doing so through a limited liability partnership.

The Minister answered my question on rollover relief. I believe that he said that if the qualifying rules for rollover relief had applied to an asset that a limited liability partnership owned and that was sold and reinvested in another business, the rollover relief would not be clawed back if the other business continued to function and the limited liability partnership was wound up.

The Government should explain how the tax provisions impact on existing limited partnerships under the Limited Partnerships Act 1907, the provisions of which are to some extent carried over to limited liability partnerships that carry on trades. The rules for 1907 limited partnerships mean that they can have loss relief on the amount of capital that the partner contributes and on the amount of undrawn profits. As there is some confusion in business and in the professional world, I hope that the Minister will confirm that the Government intend to allow limited liability partnerships that carry on trades to benefit from loss relief on the capital contributed and on undrawn profits.

Dr. Howells: I can confirm that the limits set out in new section 118ZC of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 mean that, subject to the terms of the membership agreement, relief will be available in respect of any capital that the member has subscribed, plus any undrawn profit and any further amounts that the member has undertaken to contribute if the limited liability partnership is wound up. It is important to bear in mind that existing Taxes Acts provisions were designed for limited partnerships under the 1907 Act; in particular, a limited partner's capital is registered, as are any changes in the amount, and certainly does include undrawn capital or amounts committed in the event of winding up. By contrast, a limited liability partnership member's capital can be provided for in the membership agreement. It follows that if the agreement provides that sums of members' undrawn share of profit stand as capital, those sums will count with the members' capital towards the amount of loss relief that can be claimed against their other income.

Clause 14

Insolvency and winding up

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): We now come to the clauses on the regulations. I repeat what I said earlier—key regulations, which should have been in the Bill, and which would have made it easier to understand, have been relegated to secondary legislation.

Clause 14 relates to the application of insolvency law to limited liability partnerships, and anxiety has been expressed about the clawback of members' drawings in the period two years prior to a limited liability partnership becoming insolvent. It was felt that new section 214A of the Insolvency Act 1986 was less likely than the equivalent provisions for companies to encourage a business to try to trade from an insolvent position.

Since the original consultation, the Government have amended their drafting of new section 214A, and appear to have taken note of the anxiety expressed, but I should like to know if the Minister has consulted interested bodies about the revised wording, which was published only in May. A huge volume of regulation has emanated from the Bill, and the view of the Select Committee that gave the Bill its pre-legislative scrutiny, was that it should also scrutinise the recently published revised draft of the proposed secondary legislation. Will the Minister submit the draft regulations to the Select Committee for consideration?

 

Mr. Burnett: I reiterate that we deprecate the fact that so many of the proposals are in regulations, many of which have not yet been published. There is a commercial expedient for the Bill being in statute as fast as possible, but as a rule, regulations should be in primary legislation and part and parcel of statute. I hope that the Government will heed what I say because it applies to other measures, as well as to the Bill.

Dr. Howells: I take the points made by the hon. Members for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton and for Torridge and West Devon. We consulted carefully on the matter, took cognisance of the responses and as the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton said, changed things, especially in new section 214A. In the end, it is a matter of judgment. We carefully considered whether the draft regulations, which are intended to prevent members from siphoning off funds in the event of insolvency, should appear in the Bill. We concluded against that approach because it would cause a muddle of primary and secondary legislation: the remainder of the insolvency provisions would remain in secondary legislation, but this part would not. Furthermore, the intended insolvency regulations would not rewrite the Insolvency Act 1986 for all purposes; they would make amendments only insofar as the Act was applied to limited liability partnerships. Inserting section 214A into the Bill would amend the Insolvency Act for all purposes, which is odd because none of the other amendments to the Act for limited liability partnerships covers all purposes. It is, in any case, more sensible to keep the insolvency provisions together.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton asked about the scrutiny of the Select Committee on Trade and Industry. When it undertook pre-legislative scrutiny, it paid close attention to the effect of the clause and argued that the attractions of limited liability were unlikely to be outweighed by the distant prospect of clawback in the event of insolvency. It believed that, although the provisions might require some adjustment in detail, some provision along the lines of section 214A was required. I stress that the provision will be subject to affirmative procedure, and with that assurance, I hope that the Committee will accept the clause.

Clause 15

Application of company law etc.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Gibb: Clause 15 introduces all the regulations that incorporate vast chunks of the Companies Act 1985 into limited liability partnership rules. We have two particular concerns. The first is about merger and acquisition accounting. As hon. Members will know, merger accounting is different from acquisition accounting: merger accounting works by adding up the assets and liabilities of the two bodies; acquisition accounting is more complicated because all assets, including good will, have to be valued, put into the books and amortized.

When a company acquires another company, acquisition accounting is important to ensure that the shares issued in exchange for that company fully reflect its value and that any consequences if the managers of the acquirer have paid too much to the acquiree are fully reflected in the profit and loss accounts so that the managers can be held to account to shareholders for their decision. Limited liability partnerships do not have shares, so the most appropriate method of accounting when two limited liability partnerships merge is the simpler form of merger accounting. However, because the regulations incorporate the Companies Act provisions, when two firms merge and one is smaller or larger than the other, the rules will require acquisition accounting—cumbersome, expensive and, frankly, unnecessary.

The Government's response is to encourage the Consultative Committee of Accountancy Bodies to draw up a statement of recommended practice. Is the Minister sure that that is an adequate solution to the problem? Will it take precedence over the regulations that will be incorporated by the clause?

My second concern is about professional partnerships paying ex gratia annuities to former partners or their widows. Such annuities would be paid only if firms had funds to pay them. If profits were insufficient, firms simply would not bother paying the annuity to the widow or partner. The accounting rules that will be incorporated by the clause require any such annuities to be capitalised as a liability. That would mean examining the present net value of the payments and projecting for the likely future—in other words, entering a large capital liability into the accounts. In most cases, that would render the partnership technically insolvent. I have seen no references to this problem in consultation responses. How does the Minister propose to deal with it?

Mr. Burnett: I wish to make a point about solicitors. Can regulations be made under clauses 15 or 16 to enable solicitors' firms to become limited liability partnerships? Are the regulation-making powers in the clauses sufficient to enable such firms to practise as limited liability partnerships?

Dr. Howells: If the Committee will bear with me, I shall come on to the question of solicitors' firms becoming limited liability partnerships when we reach new clause 1, which was tabled by the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon.

I should like to thank the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton for identifying a matter—the effect of the Bill on annuities—that was not dealt with in consultation. I shall deal first with his question about merger and acquisition accounting. The structure of limited liability partnerships is different from that of other companies, so we believe that a different statement of recommended practice, which will provide specific accounting guidance, is required. The statement of recommended practice would supplement the statutory requirements contained in the regulations. It would also supplement, but not override, the requirements for accounting standards.

With the approval of the Accounting Standards Board, the Consultative Committee of Accountancy Bodies has set up a steering group to monitor and advise on the drawing up of the statement of recommended practice for limited liability partnerships. A separate working party will be responsible for its preparation and it will involve representatives of the main groups likely to be affected by it. A draft will be made available to interested parties. We propose to amend the provisions on merger and acquisition accounting set out in the July 1999 consultation document on the draft regulations by removing all references to shares. The chairman of the working party preparing the statement of working practice supports the proposed changes.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 16

Consequential amendments

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Gibb: The House of Lords Delegated Powers and Deregulation Committee says in its report that the clause is

    a Henry VIII provision which allows regulations to amend or repeal primary and secondary legislation.

That is a serious concern and in drawing attention to it I can do no better than quote subsection (1):

    Regulations may make in any enactment such amendments or repeals as appear appropriate in consequence of this Act or regulations made under it.

There is a long-established rule of company law that a subsidiary company cannot purchase the shares of its parent company because although a demand for shares in the parent company is created by a technically separate legal entity, it is wholly owned and controlled by the company from which it is buying the shares. Ultimately, that leads to disaster.

When an Act of Parliament passes through the House, we do not expect it to be amended other than by our full procedure. Passing an Act that gives the power to create secondary legislation, with its much simpler procedure, in order to amend primary Acts—even the primary Act that gave the very power to change an Act by regulation—is potentially dangerous. What is to stop such a regulation changing clause 16 and giving a named individual the power to change the Bill and other enactments? I hope that that measure would be spotted as it progressed through the negative or affirmative procedure, but it might not be, because those procedures involve little scrutiny. My view is that we should never pass Henry VIII clauses, and I urge the Committee to oppose clause 16. Henry VIII clauses are extremely dangerous and the House's procedures were not developed to deal with them.

Mr. Brian White (Milton Keynes, North-East): The hon. Gentleman is not a member of the Select Committee on Statutory Instruments, which thoroughly examines all statutory instruments. Gentleman has raised a fair point and that he trusts the process, but it is also fair to recall that Henry VIII clauses have become a habit. It would be lax of us not to make the point that they should be reviewed.

Dr. Howells: As the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton said, clauses 14 and 15 contain the Bill's main regulation-making powers. Clause 14 permits regulations to be made about insolvency and the winding up of a limited liability partnership, and clause 15 permits the application of the law relating to companies or other corporations. However, there is legislation that does not fall exactly within the powers contained in clauses 14 or 15, but which must be applied to limited liability partnerships or their members to achieve the overall policy aim of parity of treatment between a limited liability partnership and a company.

A significant example is the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986, which will apply to members of a limited liability partnership. That Act is directed mainly towards company directors and is important to insolvency and company law. However, it is arguable that the powers in clauses 14 and 15 do not provide sufficient vires to ensure the Act's application to the members of a limited liability partnership. Therefore, the Act will be applied to limited liability partnerships by schedule 4 to the draft regulations, using the powers in clause 16.

The Delegated Powers and Deregulation Committee in another place examined the Bill's powers. If members of that Committee had had serious concerns, they would have made them known. They commented that the clause was a Henry VIII power. Such a power is perfectly acceptable when used appropriately. The Government would not use it in any other way, and nothing in the Committee's report suggests inappropriate use.

The concept of pre-legislative scrutiny by a Select Committee or a Standing Committee that can take evidence from outside bodies and individuals is a useful way in which to scrutinise new law. New clause 1

Solicitors

    `The Lord Chancellor may make regulations amending or modifying any provision of any enactment or instrument having effect in relation to solicitors and their practice, with such additions or omissions as appear to the Lord Chancellor to be necessary or expedient, for the purpose of enabling solicitors to practice in limited liability partnerships or for the purpose of regulating the provision of solicitors' services through limited liability partnerships.'.—[Mr. Burnett.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. John Burnett (Torridge and West Devon): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause is intended as a probing measure. I hope that the Minister will confirm that it is unthinkable that firms of solicitors should be unable to become limited liability partnerships—and quickly. A large body of legislation making reference to solicitors will need to be amended in the light of the Bill. Solicitors and their practices are referred to in various Acts as partners, partnership firms, members of a firm, members of a partnership and, in the context of solicitors working through a company, directors and officers of a recognised body.

For limited liability partnerships to be a viable option for solicitors, all that legislation must be applied to limited liability partnerships and to their members. For example, sections 9 and 10 of, and schedule 2 to, the Administration of Justice Act 1985, under which solicitors can practise through incorporated bodies, need to be amended. For some time the Department of Trade and Industry led the Law Society to believe that these regulations could be achieved by making regulations under clauses 15 and 16. I have already referred to that.

The Minister's Department recently changed its mind and informed the Law Society that it believes that it lacks the power to make the regulations under those clauses. The Law Society believes that the Minister's Department is incorrect and that the powers are adequate. We need clarification of the DTI's position and some certainty about how we can ensure quickly that the limited liability partnership form is made available to solicitors' firms. It is imperative that they can take advantage of limited liability partnership status as soon as it is open to other firms so that they can continue to be a leading force in the provision of global legal services.

We have every reason to be proud of the role that our leading international law firms and other law firms take in global business and commerce. I understand that the Lord Chancellor's Department is well aware of the issue and is trying to find a solution. However, because we need to move speedily to a solution, one must be found in the Bill, and the DTI may need to take additional powers. New clause 1 is not perfect, but it is a starting point and the Government may wish to table their own amendment on Report.

 

Dr. Howells: New clause 1 would introduce a wide-ranging power for the Lord Chancellor to make regulations amending or modifying any piece of legislation affecting solicitors to enable them to practice through the limited liability partnership vehicle. While I shall urge the Committee to resist the change, we intend that limited liability partnerships should be available to solicitors. Nothing in the Bill prevents that; the possible difficulties that have been raised are in the separate legislation governing solicitors. As I mentioned on Second Reading, my officials are in discussion with the Law Society and the Lord Chancellor's Department to consider what changes to that legislation might be necessary and how best to give them legislative effect.

The volume of legislation governing solicitors makes this a complex and time-consuming matter. The legislation is being examined to consider whether primary legislation is unavoidable and, if so, to determine what specific changes need to be made. For example, we now know that the Law Society's power to recognise bodies corporate, which it uses to recognise companies, is already sufficiently wide to embrace limited liability partnerships. That is a good start. What is less certain is how existing references in legislation to terms specific to companies, such as ``officer'' or ``director'', would read across to limited liability partnerships.

My Department is working with the Law Society and the Lord Chancellor's Department, which has the expertise on this matter, to look at the detail of the legislation so that a considered view can be taken on the most appropriate way forward. Once it has been determined what amendments are necessary to the raft of legislation affecting solicitors, we can explore whether existing powers allow for those amendments to be made by regulation. Should primary legislation prove to be the only option—it is not certain that it is—we will consider with the Lord Chancellor's Department how best to achieve that. I understand that a regulatory reform order is planned to make amendments to a number of areas of legislation affecting solicitors. That may be a suitable vehicle for changes with respect to limited liability partnerships. I recognise that the Law Society is concerned about timing, but we do not expect that people will be able to incorporate as limited liability partnerships before early 2001. In the meantime, we will work as quickly as we can to reach a satisfactory conclusion and, if necessary, to remove obstacles in the way of solicitors making use of limited liability partnerships.

Returning to the new clause, we do not believe that the case has been established for introducing a power to amend other primary legislation by regulations, as we cannot say either that the power is necessary or how it might be used. For those reason, I resist the new clause.
