Ms Hodge: I am sorry that there are so many amendments, but I hope to take the Committee logically through them. They fulfil a commitment that we gave in the other place to consider further amendments tabled by Baroness Blatch and Lord Ashley and I am pleased to have tabled them.

Clause 4 on non-discrimination notices currently contains a regulation-making power to allow the Secretary of State to define the scope of non-discrimination notices. Baroness Blatch and Lord Ashley were keen to ensure that the scope of non-discrimination notices was defined in the Bill. Amendments Nos. 4 and 5 demonstrate the Opposition's desire to pursue that issue.

Having considered the matter further and taken account of the strong views about it in another place, our amendments will define the scope of non-discrimination notices. The relevant amendments are Nos. 20 and 21. Amendment No. 26 to clause 12 and amendment Nos. 27 to 30, 33 to 37, 40 to 42, 44 and 45 to schedule 3 are consequential to the amendments to clause 4. Amendment No. 39 to schedule 3 sets out the detailed procedure applicable to action plans.

It may be helpful if I outline the key elements of our proposals, as they make fundamental changes to the process of issuing and enforcing the requirements in a non-discrimination notice. We developed our proposals with a view to addressing the shortcomings that the Equal Opportunities Commission and the Commission for Racial Equality have identified in the existing process. In short, they found that although, under the existing process, one has to stop discrimination, nothing is said about what one can do to deal with it. In the amendments, we are attempting to pinpoint what is wrong and how it can be put right. That will require an action plan, which will need to be vetted. If the action plan is not agreed to, or is not implemented or adhered to, the matter should then be taken through the courts. We are suggesting an alternative and more effective way of putting it right than that suggested by the EOC and the CRE. I shall expand on that if the Committee wishes.

Mr. Tom Clarke (Coatbridge and Chryston): I accept immediately my hon. Friend's offer to expand on her proposals. The EOC and the CRE have tremendous experience. What advice have those commissions given her?

Ms Hodge: Their advice was that we should strengthen the process for dealing with non-discrimination notices. We should not simply say that discrimination must cease; we should lay out a procedure for the organisation to follow that will ensure that it does cease. There is a slight difference between what we propose and what the CRE and EOC said in their representations and submissions, and I shall deal with that when we come to it.

We have differed a little from the EOC and the CRE in that we have taken on board the concerns expressed by the Confederation of British Industry and the Institute of Directors. The CRE and the EOC wanted us to give the Disability Rights Commission the power to prescribe, in detail, the plan of action that an organisation would need to pursue in order to deal with the discrimination of which it had been found guilty. It is our view that the plan of action should be devised by that organisation, so that it owns it. However, I shall describe the processes by which the commission can vet the plan and ensure that it tackles the discrimination.

I turn to the key elements of our proposals. The amendments would give the commission the power to identify in a non-discrimination notice the policies, practices, procedures and other arrangements that it believes have led to or contributed to unlawful acts being committed, and to recommend what changes need to be made to address those unlawful acts. It will also be able to set the time scale for making the changes, which is important. The commission will also be able to require an organisation to produce an action plan within a prescribed time. That action plan will need to address the unlawful acts that have been or are being committed again, within a reasonable time. There are time limits throughout the process. The action plan would be legally binding once final and the commission would be able to enforce its implementation through the courts.

I now come to the matter raised by my right hon. Friend the Member for Coatbridge and Chryston (Mr. Clarke). It will be up to the organisation to produce the action plan, although it will have to take account of the commission's recommendations. We believe that that is the right approach. If an organisation has to change the way in which it operates, and to start acting in a non-discriminatory manner, we believe that it must have ownership of the action plan. However, we believe that we have built sufficient opportunities into the process for the commission to comment on a daft action plan and to make further recommendations if the commission thought it appropriate. Ultimately, the commission would be able to challenge whether an action plan would adequately address the unlawful acts identified in the non-discrimination notice through the courts.

As I said, I believe that the amendments strike the appropriate balance between giving an organisation ownership of its plans, and strengthening the commission's powers as requested in comments by the EOC and the CRE, and in some amendments discussed in another place to ensure that effective changes are made by an organisation.

I shall assist the Committee by quickly giving details of the amendments. Under proposed paragraph 12B(2) of schedule 3, if an organisation fails to produce an action plan required under a non-discrimination notice, the commission will be able to apply to a county court or sheriff for an order directing the organisation to do so.

Under proposed paragraph 12D(1) of schedule 3, if an organisation produces an action plan that the commission considers inadequate, and if the commission fails in its negotiation attempts to make the plan adequate, it will be able to apply to a county court or sheriff for an order requiring the organisation to revise its plan.

In both cases, the commission will be able under paragraph 15 of schedule 3 to enforce any orders issued to produce or revise an action plan through the courts. An organisation that fails to comply will be liable to a fine. The Commission will be able to apply for repeated orders to enforce compliance if the organisation continues to fail to comply. Each order could be followed by a fine.

Ms Hodge: Again, these amendments deal with written agreements. Amendments Nos. 22 and 23, taken together, will allow the Disability Rights Commission and an organisation to enter into a written agreement earlier than originally provided for. They clarify the revocation, termination or varying of agreements, and define the scope of written agreements in the Bill.

I am proud that our proposals represent another innovation in British legislation; it is the first time that enforceable written agreements will be part of a Bill. I strongly support that process because a written agreement between an organisation and the commission will be far less confrontational than enforcing non-discrimination notices. It will be much more effective in encouraging employers and service providers to tackle discrimination positively. It will enable us to move much more swiftly towards eliminating the discrimination that is bound to exist in an organisation. I also hope that it will limit the need to conduct lengthy and expensive formal investigations. We have learned that lesson from the CRE and the EOC.

The proposal will provide employers and service providers who accept that an unlawful act has been committed with an opportunity to agree with the commission the steps that need to be taken to put matters right. Given those provisos, the commission will be able to enforce the written agreements. It is important that the arrangements are seen as a better option for those who discriminate, not as a soft option.

I apologise to members of the Committee that the amendments have been tabled rather late. Therefore, I shall deal with them in detail. Under amendment No. 22, we shall set out the conditions under which the commission can enter into a written agreement in place of a formal investigation. To be consistent with clause 3(2) on formal investigations the amendment specifies that, if the investigation had been ordered by the Secretary of State, he or she must give permission for such an agreement to be entered into.

Amendment No. 22 deals with the stages at which it will be possible for the commission to enter into a written agreement with an organisation. Under clause 5(1), as drafted, the commission will have the power to enter into a written agreement only after a decision has been taken to embark on a formal investigation. However, in practice we know that discussions take place between employers and service providers before any decision is taken to begin a formal investigation. That is the experience of the other commissions.

If the organisation facing an investigation is willing to enter into a binding written agreement at an early stage, it will enable the commission to proceed more quickly to a solution, and to provide benefits for those affected by active discrimination.

It would be artificial to require the Disability Rights Commission to decide to start a formal investigation before entering a written agreement, only to stop it immediately afterwards.

New subsections (1) to (4) will make it possible for the DRC to enter a written agreement, not only at a preliminary stage before deciding whether to mount a formal investigation, but at any stage up to the point at which the DRC issues a non-discrimination notice. That is both practical and desirable; it will give the commission and the organisation full opportunity to take advantage of the clear benefits of a written agreement.

Opposition amendments Nos. 6 and 7 would enable the DRC to enter into a written agreement before deciding or being directed to conduct a formal investigation. I hope that hon. Gentlemen agree that the Government amendment meets their purpose, so will not press their amendments.

Subsection (5) deals with the scope of written agreements, which will be enforceable; we should therefore be clear about their scope. In practice, they will be the same as the non-discrimination notice and the action plan. The agreement will apply to unlawful acts, which the DRC has reason to believe have been or are being committed, and changes to any practices, policies, procedures or other arrangements that have caused or contributed to the commission of unlawful acts or are liable to do so. Subsection (5)(a) makes it explicit that a written agreement may include terms providing for the termination of the agreement. That means that the DRC or the organisation can agree a range of alternatives, including starting or resuming a formal investigation if a written agreement breaks down.

The White Paper, which was subject to consultation before the Bill was introduced, proposed that written agreements should comprise two elements: compliance, which would be enforceable through the courts, and good practice, which would not. The regulation-making power in subsection (5)(b) will give the Secretary of State the power to provide for the good practice elements which will not be enforceable under subsection (6).
