Mr. Robert Syms (Poole): I beg to move amendment No. 168, in page 4, line 18, at end insert—

Clause 6 sets out many terms and conditions that would apply to any normal licence, but one would expect most normal applications, whether for a fishing licence or for an air traffic service, to specify a time period. The draft licence sets out that a 30-year licence is likely, with a 10-year notice period for giving up or revoking the licence and a minimum of 20 years before that can happen In effect, that is a 30-year licence. Many of the Bill's provisions become clearer if they are considered in the light of that time span. When I first read the Bill, I thought that a licence would be granted for five or 10 years. I did not understand why so many checks, balances and other mechanisms had been included, especially relating to competition, until I realised that licences would be granted for a much longer time. As was said in this morning's debate, if the licence is for 30 years—especially if the first licence is granted by the Secretary of State—it could mean that it is 30 years or longer before the Civil Aviation Authority gets round to issuing a licence itself.

The Bill provides that the CAA must keep a register, which will contain the licence and any variation of it. On my first reading of the Bill, I thought that that would be a small, simple document, but I am beginning to think that it will be a large and growing document with attachments, ``Post-Its'' and goodness knows what else added as time goes by. It is inevitable that there will be a lot of changes in 30 years.

We need a much clearer idea about the Government's thinking. There are certain conflicts between economic regulation and best price. Often, if one sets a longer licence period, it affects the price that one is likely to receive from a strategic partner because there is much greater certainty about the period for which the licence is likely to run. Thirty years is a considerable length of time. We need to know whether the Government have decided on a 30-year period because of the long-term nature of the investment in the industry or because they are anxious to get the best price from the strategic partner and a longer licence will inevitably result in a better price.

Set against that issue are concerns about economic regulation. If the licence period were much shorter, so that the licence had to be renewed on a five or 10-year basis, it would produce a more rigorous regime, even with all the checks, balances and modifications in the Bill. If the licence extends over a 30-year period, 20 years will have to elapse before one can even think of revoking the licence and giving 10 years' notice. Ten years is probably the equivalent of two and a half Governments. I could not bear to think about how many Governments we could go through in 30 years. We would like the Minister to give us the thinking on having a 30-year period rather than a shorter one.

Why has the period for the ending of a licence been set at 10 years? That would seem a considerable period if one were dissatisfied with the licensee's service. There is no alternative arrangement aside from the emergency provision for the Secretary of State to take over in a problem such as a bankruptcy. We need answers. The only way to have a nice 10-year period would be if, looking 10 years ahead, the Government thought that there would be a treaty or policy change. However, I cannot see why a Government dissatisfied with the delivery of air traffic control services should decide to give notice to the company and yet sit for 10 years and allow it to work the notice.

We are concerned that the provisions will have to be amended because of the long period proposed. Harold Wilson said that a week was a long time in politics; 30 years is a considerable period. Looking around me, I wonder how many hon. Members will be here in 30 years' time.

Mr. Raynsford: As I was saying, a licence falls into two parts, usually called terms and conditions.

A licence in itself is a permission to do something, not an obligation. The permission extends to what the licence holder is authorised to do, where he is authorised to do it, for how long the authority is to last, and the circumstances in which the permission may be withdrawn. These constitute the terms of the licence; they are fixed and unchanging over time in order to provide security for the licence holder and absolute certainty about the provisions.

The licence also sets out the conditions to which the permission is to be subject: in other words, the price the licence holder has to pay for the privilege of having the permission. The conditions are designed to be modified, with proper checks and balances in the regulatory process, such modifications to reflect changing economic or technical conditions, changes in the nature of the business, or necessary developments in the legitimate requirements of the regulator and the public interest.

The main purpose of the conditions of a licence is to ensure that the licensee arranges its affairs so that adequate volumes and levels of services are available to users; that the business and assets are maintained in a state fit to meet those needs; that appropriate incentives to efficiency are provided; and that there are safeguards against abuse of a monopoly position by the licensee.

The initial licence will authorise NATS to provide air traffic services in respect of the two major segments of en-route airspace in which it currently provides such services—in the United Kingdom and Shanwick oceanic control areas. Further, it specifically requires the licensee to provide the core air traffic services and enshrines its commitment to provide specified other services listed in schedule 5 to the licence. The licence does not extend to air traffic control at airports, which is already carried out under contractual arrangements between the airports concerned and NATS and other providers. That and other certain activities around the margins of NATS' en-route business, would be exempt from the requirement for a licence, hence the separate provision for exemption.

It is envisaged that NATS would be the only en-route licensee initially, and probably for some time to come, in relation to the airspace for which the UK currently has responsibility. If the UK acquired responsibility for another tranche of international airspace, the licence holder would need a licence for that new airspace. They would be committing an offence under clause 3 if they provided air traffic services without one, hence the provision for an additional licence, if appropriate.
