Mr. David Davis (Haltemprice and Howden): I welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Butterfill. It is a pleasure to serve under your chairmanship.

I welcome the Minister's comments on amendment No. 72. It is pleasing that the Government have recognised that the appointment of accounting officers to Departments is an important constitutional obligation on the Treasury rather than just a duty that the Treasury may choose to take.

Amendment No. 27, and an amendment in the next group, relate to subsection (6), which imposes only two statutory requirements on accounting officers: to prepare the Department's resource accounts and to transmit them to the Comptroller and Auditor General.

The Economic Secretary rebuked us earlier for speaking at length, which I thought was unfair because our debate this morning was useful and informative. She must forgive me if I talk at length on the amendment because the position of the accounting officer is an important aspect of parliamentary oversight and accountability and I want to elaborate on that so that the Committee understands the need for amendment No. 27.

The accounting officer's role is much wider than that to which the Bill refers. He or she will have the personal duty of signing the accounts prescribed in the letter of appointment and, by virtue of that duty, the further duty of being a witness before the Public Accounts Committee, to deal with questions arising from those accounts or, more commonly, from reports made to Parliament by the Comptroller and Auditor General under the National Audit Act 1983. Further responsibilities are associated with those duties—there should be a high standard of financial management in the Department as a whole, financial systems and procedures should promote the efficient and economical conduct of business and safeguard financial propriety and regularity throughout the Department, and financial considerations should be fully taken into account in decisions on policy proposals.

The essence of the accounting officer's role is a personal responsibility for the propriety and regularity of public finances for which he or she is answerable—the keeping of proper accounts, prudent and economical administration, avoidance of waste and extravagance and efficient and effective use of all available resources. As a result, the accounting officer must first sign accounts assigned to him or her, and, in doing so, accept personal responsibility for their proper presentation, as prescribed in legislation or, mostly, under this Bill, by the Treasury. Secondly, he or she must ensure that proper financial procedures are followed, and that accounting records are maintained in a form suited to the requirements of management, as well as in the form prescribed for published accounts. Thirdly, public funds for which he or she is responsible should be properly and well managed and safeguarded, with independent and effective checks of cash balances put in the hands of an official. Fourthly, assets for which he or she is responsible, such as land, buildings or other property, including stores and equipment, should be controlled and safeguarded with similar care and checks as appropriate. Fifthly, in the consideration of policy proposals relating to expenditure on income, for which an accounting officer has responsibilities, all relevant financial considerations, including issues of propriety, regularity or value for money, should be taken into account and, where necessary, brought to Ministers' attention.

An accounting officer has a particular responsibility for ensuring compliance with parliamentary requirements in relation to controlled expenditure. A fundamental requirement is that funds should be applied only to the extent and for the purposes authorised by Parliament. The attention of Parliament must be drawn to losses or special payments by appropriate notation of the relevant account. We touched on that issue on Tuesday, in relation to the words ``service of''. On voted expenditure, any payments made must be within the ambit and amount of the vote, and parliamentary approval must have been sought and given. In cases not covered by the original estimate—for example, if a service had not been contemplated when an estimate was presented, or a temporary advance from the contingency fund had been sanctioned by the Treasury—the accounting officer must ensure that parliamentary approval is sought and given at the earliest opportunity, through a supplementary estimate, or, if necessary, an excess vote.

An accounting officer will be expected to furnish the PAC with explanations of any indications of weakness to which the Comptroller and Auditor General has drawn attention, about which it might wish to question the accounting officer. The accounting officer is also required to appear before the Public Accounts Committee on value-for-money issues. An accounting officer has particular responsibility to see that appropriate advice is tendered to Ministers on all matters of financial propriety and regularity, and, more broadly, on considerations of prudent and economical administration, efficiency and effectiveness. The accounting officer needs to determine how, and in what terms, such advice should be tendered, and whether to make specific reference to his or her duty to justify to the PAC transactions for which he or she is accountable. Any hon. Member who has served as a Minister will be familiar with that requirement.

If the Minister in charge of a Department is contemplating a course of action involving a transaction that an accounting officer believes would infringe the requirements of propriety, regularity or value for money, the accounting officer is required to set out in writing objections to that proposal and the reasons for the objections, and his or her duty to notify the Comptroller and Auditor General should advice be overruled. If the Minister decides, none the less, to proceed, the accounting officer is required to seek a written instruction to take action. Having received such an instruction, he or she informs the Treasury of what has occurred and communicates the papers to the Comptroller and Auditor General without undue delay—the letter of direction procedure. That might sound terribly dry, but, were it not for that responsibility on the accounting officer, the Pergau dam issue, for example, would never have been brought to Parliament's attention.
