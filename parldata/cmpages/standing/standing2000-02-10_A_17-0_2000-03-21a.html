Mr. Stunell: If members of the Committee have studied the amendments tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Lewes (Mr. Baker) and myself, they will see a common theme, perhaps even a sameness. The intention is to ensure that the authority is not simply given powers to carry out functions, but has positive duties to perform. The amendments relate to clauses 42 and 70, which march side by side for electricity and gas. Our amendments would ensure that the parallel approach continues so that the authority's proposed powers for gas and electricity are enhanced and solidified into duties.

The first group of amendments under clause 42 would ensure that the authority regulates standards of performance. The clause maintains the standards of service that is offered to consumers and empowers the authority to act and regulate to ensure that. The authority's principal objective is to protect consumers. The regulations must be watertight. The Minister may mention the subtle differences between powers and duties, but in establishing an authority with a specific purpose, it is sensible to ensure that the duty to carry out its principal purpose is clearly set out. That is the background to the amendments.

Line 7 on page 42 amends the Electricity Act 1989 by imposing a duty on the authority. No doubt we shall hear why the Government want to remove the consultation process, but our amendment would not do that. Instead, it would ensure that the authority never loses sight of its main job, which is to protect consumers and to ensure that standards of service-and, in later amendments, standards of performance-are in place. It is a straightforward, simple amendment.

 

Dr. Howells: If I may, I shall start with Government amendment No. 639, which is technical. We want to delete nine lines from new section 39A, which the clause inserts into the 1989 Act. They provide that the authority may set performance standards only after consulting various people and arranging for appropriate research. The provisions duplicate new section 40B, which is established in clause 44. I hope that that answers the query of the hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell). The equivalent overlap was avoided in clause 70, so that does not need to be amended.

The amendments tabled by the hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friend the Member for Lewes would require the authority to set performance standards instead of using a discretionary power to do so. I understand the motives behind the amendments, but they are not necessary or desirable. The authority will be under a general duty to promote the interests of consumers and it should decide whether setting performance standards for particular groups of customer will be the best way to fulfil its duty. If competition developed to such and extent that the authority considered it unnecessary to make relevant regulations, it would be wrong for it be compelled by primary legislation to do so.

The gas and electricity Acts contain powers to set performance standards for suppliers, and currently only domestic gas customers and tariff electricity customers are covered. However, under the Bill, there will be no limit on the type of customer who may be covered. That is to avoid enshrining in primary legislation an arbitrary cut-off point such as the amount of electricity that someone would need to consumer to be considered a small customer. In practice, we do not expect the authority to set standards for very large customers and we expect coverage to be much as at present.

Given the expansion of the scope of the powers, it is even more important that the authority should have the discretion not to impose standards. Very large consumers will not benefit from receiving, for example, £50 of compensation for being without supply for 24 hours. Their relationship with utilities is likely to be based on contract, and in those cases it would be wrong to insist on the authority's intervention in the market.

I invite the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.
