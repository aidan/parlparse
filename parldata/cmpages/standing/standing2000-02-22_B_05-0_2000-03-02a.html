Clause 33

Review and information

Mr. Alan Duncan (Rutland and Melton): I beg to move amendment No. 15, in page 18, line 29, after `Kingdom', insert

Mr. Duncan: It is a unique and great pleasure, Mr. Hood, to welcome you to the Chair of this splendid, productive Committee. I am amused that your first contribution should be to allow us to take off our jackets. I take the view of the former President Reagan, who always refused, out of respect for his office, to take off his jacket in the Oval Office, so I shall keep mine on.

The amendments are simple ones that might at first sight appear a little bizarre, but they were drafted for a particular purpose. I am not harking back to the old EEC or the European Coal and Steel Community by deliberately using the phrase ``European Community'' in the amendments. Rather, that is the proper and distinctive labelling. The clause deals with reviewing and information-gathering on the part of the regulator. The amendment would include express reference to reviewing and collecting information about the provision of postal services within the European Community.

We all know how irksome it is when we seek information through a parliamentary question and are told that that information is not centrally held. It is—to be more relevant to the amendment—equally frustrating when we ask a question about the United Kingdom, for example on our health service, to which the proper answer can be given only by comparing our situation with that of, say, France or Germany, and are told that information about other countries is not held and that Ministers have no responsibility to give such comparative information to the House.

The scope of the clause is broader, which I encourage and welcome. It states that the commission shall

    keep under review the provision (in the United Kingdom and elsewhere) of postal services

and shall

    collect information about the provision (in the United Kingdom and elsewhere) of those services.

The amendments would make the comparative parameters within which the information is to be gathered more specific, so that instead of reading

    in the United Kingdom and elsewhere

the clause would read

    in the United Kingdom, the European Community and elsewhere.

The most valuable comparative setting for the collection of such information is at pan-European level. A precedent for that has been set in debates and statements. Indeed, the White Paper of July 1999: ``Post Office Reform: A world class service for the 21st century'' emphasised the need for the regulator to pay particular attention to developments in the rest of Europe in discharging its duties, noting at paragraph 11 on page 21:

    The Regulator will, of course, need to take fully into account actual and prospective developments in the EU, WTO and the Universal Postal Union; and to ensure that its own actions and proposed policies are compatible with those of these organisations.

Article 22 of the directive also provides that

    The national regulatory authorities shall have as a particular task ensuring compliance with the obligations arising from this Directive.

The amendment would ensure that there was an overlap between the clause and all the other directives and statements so far issued.

Postal services are of pan-European significance and subject to on-going review at European level. It is probably at pan-European, although possibly also transatlantic level that some of the most obvious partnerships between a freer Post Office and other established commercial concerns in a similar sector are most likely to occur. The communications business is global, but at the early stage of the sector's development there is likely to be more concentration on the European area than anywhere else. The amendment would provide the focus necessary to achieve the stated goals of the White Paper and the directive.

The amendment refers to the European Community, not the European Union. The European Community is one of the three components of the European Union. Pursuant to Community legislation made in accordance with the treaty that established the European Community, Community institutions undertake measures relating to the single market, including the continuing review of postal services. It is therefore correct for the amendment to refer to the European Community, not the European Union. To many people that is techno-babble, but I am not trying to play games. I am simply trying to make sure that the Bill clearly provides that the focus of the review and the collection of information entrusted to the Commission is well directed and places proper emphasis on the pan-European market in which comparative studies will most usefully be made. There is adequate provision in the Bill for the Commission to make studies of the States, or further afield, in the far east or the Gulf or wherever other partnerships might arise. The word ``elsewhere'' would cover that, but it would be a pity not to make specific reference to the primary area to which the Commission should look. The amendment would enhance the Bill and give it a better focus.

Mr. Johnson: Indeed.

I would not quarrel with the hon. Gentleman's argument that the European Community should be a special focus for the Postal Services Commission. The clause refers to the exercise of the Commission's functions. It states that it shall

    (a) keep under review the provision (in the United Kingdom and elsewhere) of postal services,

    (b) collect information about the provision (in the United Kingdom and elsewhere) of postal services

``Elsewhere'' covers the European Community as well as the rest of the world.

The problem with the amendment is that it introduces an air of confusion. The Commission will need to ensure that it is informed about the pan-European requirements in the European postal services directive, such as that on service standards. However, the current drafting is adequate for that purpose and the amendment may confuse the issue by suggesting that the Commission has powers in the European Community that it would not have elsewhere. Its powers, including those to insist that information is provided—this relates to amendment No. 16—are limited to the United Kingdom. To suggest that it should have special powers in the European Community but not elsewhere is extremely confusing and a little dangerous.

There is a good argument for the Commission requiring information from outside the European Community, rather than inside, in a particular element of its work. In what is called remail, mail is shipped out of the country, mainly to east European countries that are not members of the European Community at present, and then shipped back in as a way of getting round the universal service and uniform tariffs, as the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire (Mr. Page) knows. The system is called ABA remailing, because it goes from country A to country B and back to country A. It is legal to send mail from country A to country B to be processed and delivered in country B—ABB remailing—and to send mail from country A to country B to go to country C—ABC remailing. However, going from country A to country B and back into this country is not acceptable and it is an issue to which the Commission must pay particular attention. The main problem is outside the European Community.

I do not want to make a big issue of the problem. The clause ensures that the Commission can tackle service quality and the requirement for information within the European Community and elsewhere. The amendment would cause confusion by giving the impression that the powers extended to the European Community, which would be dangerous and misleading.

Clause 36

Power of the Commission to require information

Mr. Duncan: I beg to move amendment No. 17, in page 20, line 13, leave out `any person' and insert

    `the Council, a universal service provider, or any licence holder under Part II who is not a universal service provider,'.

We come to an important part of the Bill. It delineates the power of the courts or the Secretary of State to enforce certain provisions in the Bill and contains a description of information that can be demanded and the penalties that will apply for default. The Opposition's view is that the way in which the Bill is drafted at present and the penalties that can apply to the parties defined here are too draconian and sweeping. Genuine arguments about civil liberties and commercial freedom apply to this part of the Bill and the amendment addresses those concerns.

Clause 36(1) states:

    The Commission may, for any relevant purpose, serve notice on any person requiring him . . . to produce any documents . . . at a time and place.

The sweeping category of ``any person'' is too wide, and I question how it can rightfully fall within human rights legislation. The Bill is about regulating postal services, granting licences, protecting universal service and allowing other competitive organisations to compete in the field, subject to certain provisions. However, the clause will allow the state to intrude on the lives and commercial freedom of people who may never seek to participate in the business.

2.30 pm

One might logically assume that the opinions might at least be allied to the business. For instance, one might ask for the accounts of a courier company even though it is not applying for a licence or trying to compete with universal service provision. The Minister might tell me that the company might violate such a licence, but the powers are too broad and allow too much leeway to the state.

The White Paper states:

    The UK postal market outside the statutory monopoly is a vibrant, competitive market with over 4,000 competitors. It is therefore the Government's intention that regulation should focus on those who are operating within the monopoly area, leaving the rest of the market free to operate as they do now in a largely unregulated environment . . . The Government proposes that the Regulator's role will be confined to ensuring the general ability of the Post Office network to support the USO on letters and parcels; and to monitoring levels of access to the network, reporting any concerns to the Government.

In addressing the issue of the power of the regulator to require information, it continues:

    The Regulator will have the power to require information relevant to the exercise of its functions from the Post Office and other operators licensed to operate within the monopoly.

The clause goes outside those bounds and grants dramatic powers to the state to demand information and to fine if it is not given. Since the White Paper, the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry has consistently maintained that extension of the regulator's powers outside the reserved area is unnecessary. In a letter dated 31 July 1999 to the Chairman of the Select Committee on Trade and Industry, the Secretary of State said:

    I do not believe that it is either right or necessary for the Postal Services Commission to have powers to require information from the commercial sector where it is operating outside the monopoly.

    I do not believe it is right because such powers would extend regulation into a highly competitive market (with over 4,000 businesses of all sizes) which has not shown any need for regulatory intervention. I do not believe it is necessary because the kind of information the Commission will tend to require of the broader market, for example on prices, will either be readily available; or is of a nature and type that I am sure businesses would be willing to provide voluntarily if asked.

A more limited power would also be consistent with the limited power of the council to require information as set out in, clause 47(2), which states:

    The persons on whom a notice under subsection (1) may be served are—

    (a) the Commission,

    (b) a universal service provider,

    (c) any licence holder under Part II who is not a universal service provider.

The amendment echoes that, word for word.

Amendment No. 17 exactly reflects the specific and detailed words of the Secretary of State when he formally submitted comments to the Select Committee; it is also in line with the White Paper and, indeed, with other parts of the Bill. I am trying to confine potentially draconian state powers so that they cannot stretch beyond the ambit of those who are directly involved with what the regulator is legitimately entrusted to regulate. As both sides of the Committee admit, the commercial sector is a vibrant, thriving and competitive part of the communications sector.

Mr. Page: I do not propose to gallop over the same ground as my hon. Friend the Member for Rutland and Melton, who made a compelling case. I wish to make just a few quick points.

If the Minister accepts the amendment—as I hope he will—some consequential changes to line 18 may need to be made. The amendment would remove any temptation for the Commission to mount fishing expeditions. I may be becoming fixated on the draft guidance, which states under paragraph 4.14:

Mr. Johnson: We strongly oppose the amendment and with good reason. The hon. Member for Rutland and Melton argued about civil liberties and claimed that we are breaching human rights legislation: we are not. As to the level of fines proposed later in the Bill, they have been approved by the Home Office and are entirely consistent with human rights.

The amendment would change the ability of the Commission to require information from any person. The phrase ``any person'' has a wide definition, but the purposes for requiring that information are specific, as set out in the Bill, and do not include the time that people go to bed, prying into their bank accounts or anything of that nature.

The Commission can require people to provide information if it is investigating the offence of operating without a licence in the reserved area, under clause 6; exercising its functions in relation to breach of licence conditions under the new licensing regime, under clauses 14, 15, 16 and 21; providing advice and information on public post offices; or collecting information on standards of performance achieved by licence holders and universal service obligation providers, or on behalf of the council so that it can carry out its functions. It can require information only for those specific reasons.

The problem is that, under the amendment, the Commission would not be allowed to police the reserved area properly. Under the Bill, we shall remove the Post Office's right to police the reserved area and place that obligation on the Commission. If it is to undertake that role, it must be able to require information from other postal operators outside the licensed area or the universal service provision. It must also be able to require information from those who may have just set up in business.

The purpose is to protect the universal service obligation, so we shall require the Commission to police the reserved area. The only reason why we want a reserved area is to protect the universal service obligation. To protect the USO, the Commission must be sure that it can stop those who want to deliver mail, intra-city or inter-city, cream-skimming, which would totally undermine the Post Office's ability to provide a universal service.

If we accepted the amendment, the Commission could not police the reserved area properly; it could not ask those outside the reserved area, such as new companies or unlicensed operators who might breach the conditions of the reserved area, to provide information. Unlike in telecommunications, the entry costs involved in setting up postal services are low—the proverbial man and a van—but the Commission could not require those people to provide information under the amendment. That would make its job extremely difficult.

The hon. Member for Rutland and Melton mentioned what the Secretary of State said. My right hon. Friend made it clear that, unlike other utilities, the whole industry would not be regulated. That was welcomed by the industry. The 4,000 postal operators who do a tremendous job outside the reserved area and merrily go about their business will not be affected. We decided—it was a significant step—that they should not come under the scrutiny of the regulator.

The regulator will be confined to scrutinising the reserved area and the universal postal service. If we had made the regulator responsible for the whole industry, no such drafting problems would have arisen. We have used the term ``any person'' and applied it to strictly defined specific purposes, because we have an unregulated market, apart from the reserved area that the Commission will be obliged to regulate.

Mr. Duncan: The Minister may be able to satisfy me sufficiently so that I can withdraw the amendment.
