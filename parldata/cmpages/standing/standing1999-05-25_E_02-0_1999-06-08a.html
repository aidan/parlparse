Clause 1

Referral of young offenders to youth offender panels

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 2, in page 1, line 8, leave out

    ``youth court or other magistrates''.

I sincerely trust, Mr. Stevenson, that you enjoyed the fortnight's break since the Committee last met. The amendment is largely probing but it would none the less improve the Bill. It might help hon. Members to follow the train of our argument if I briefly explained our approach to clauses 1 and 2.

Essentially, we have adopted a twin-track approach. First, we anticipate that the Government will seek to reverse the decision taken in the other place at Third Reading about the current discretionary nature of clause 1, particularly subsection (2)(b) which states:

    ``the court may order the offender to be referred''.

Although our preference is to retain the discretionary nature of the referral order, we anticipate that the Government will seek to change the discretionary back to mandatory, so we feel that we should endeavour to make other amendments to the Bill which would improve a mandatory referral order.

Although when we come to the third group of amendments I shall marshal our arguments about the discretionary as opposed to the mandatory aspect, amendment No. 2 and some of the other amendments tabled to clause 1 would either reflect a mandatory referral order or generally improve the Bill either way. Amendment No. 2 would improve the situation either way although the matter is covered in our new clause 1.

Essentially, we cannot understand why the referral order is restricted to the youth or other magistrates court and appears to be prohibited from the Crown court. The Government's thinking is clear from paragraph 35 of the notes on clauses, which states:

    ``The order-making power is available in the youth court and the adult magistrates' court. It is not available in the Crown Court''.

The Government apparently intend, however, to make the power available to a Crown court when determining appeals from the lower court. A youth court could have imposed a custodial sentence on a first-time offender who appeals against it in the belief that he should have had a referral order. If the referral order is thought by the Crown court to be more appropriate it can say so, but the case must be remitted back to the youth or magistrates court for sentence. That seems nonsensical.

If the referral orders were to be discretionary--which is our preference--it would be bad enough because it would hardly be a vote of confidence in our judiciary if magistrates can impose a referral order but judges cannot. If, as we anticipate, the referral order returns to being mandatory, the court will have no option but to refer a young offender to a youth offender panel, because a custodial sentence will not be imposed. Therefore, we do not understand why the Crown court that has considered the matter should send a case back to a youth or magistrates court for the referral order sentence. That would involve another hearing and more delay and expense.

I have tried to imagine what the Government's rationale might be. We know that they would prefer young offenders to plead guilty and to admit their guilt, because that would mean that they are mentally prepared to face up to their offending behaviour. In youth and magistrates courts dealing with such cases, there would be a general discouragement on the accused young offender from using his option to take an either-way triable offence to a Crown court.

However, as I pointed out on Second Reading, the opposite might occur in practice. In order to avoid a youth offender panel, a young offender might be advised to take the case to a Crown court. He would plead not guilty to avoid the youth offender panel. [Interruption.] The Minister looks a little surprised, but an offender can be referred to a youth offender panel only if he pleads guilty before a magistrates or youth court. If, on an either-way triable offence, he elects for trial at a Crown court and pleads not guilty, he cannot be subject to a referral order. As I said on Second Reading, that might provide a perverse incentive for people to plead not guilty if they wish to avoid the disposal of appearing before a youth offender panel.

If the accused elects to go to Crown court, he might, for reasons that the Home Secretary has recently elaborated, plead guilty. Some offenders plead guilty; there are several reasons for doing so. An offender might plead guilty to avoid a custodial sentence. He and his advisers might believe that a youth or magistrates court might be more inclined to impose a custodial sentence. Therefore, many young offenders might plead guilty in a Crown court for their first offence, and I do not understand why the Crown court should not have the power to impose the referral order on the offender. When the Minister replies, I am sure that he will tell us that the magistrate might be an important member of the youth offender panel and might seek to create a relationship with the accused. However, the Crown court should surely have the power to impose a referral order.

Other factors may determine why a first-time young offender appears in a Crown court rather than in a youth or magistrates court. For example, he may be charged with others. At the time of election of court of trial, the accused may be undecided about what to plead or unsure of what sentence to expect if he pleads guilty, or is found guilty. Associates with whom he is charged may be older than 17--perhaps 18, 19 or 20--and may make it clear that they intend to plead not guilty. Some may have previous convictions, and a further conviction would create difficulty.

Having come before a Crown court, a young offender may be advised that pleading guilty would be the best way of avoiding a custodial sentence. So it is perfectly feasible that in many cases a young offender of 16 or 17 will enter a guilty plea before a Crown court, and that all the circumstances that apply in respect of a referral order will then apply. We merely question why a Crown court cannot make the referral order. Why must such a case be remitted back to the youth or magistrates court?

There are other reasons why a case may be heard by a Crown court. For example, the offence may be an indictable-only offence. It would be a serious offence which in many instances would be likely to incur a custodial sentence, although not necessarily always. We know from constituency cases that an automatic custodial sentence is by no means a certainty if a youngster is charged with an indictable-only offence in a Crown court and it is his first appearance before a court.
