Turning to amendment No. 153, as I have already explained, the significance of a ``condition'' is that it is intended to be enforceable and modifiable. Those provisions that are not expressed as conditions—usually described as ``terms''—are neither modifiable nor enforceable. That is why it is important to include the provision in subsection (4) about conditions. I understand the Opposition's objectives in drafting the amendment, but believe that they have not recognised the importance of the distinction between a condition and a provision.

A licence is a permission to do something, not an obligation. The permission extends to what the licence holder is authorised to do, where he is authorised to do it, for how long it is to last and the circumstances under which the permission may be withdrawn. Those constitute the terms of the licence, which are fixed and unchanging over time in order to provide security for the licence holder.

By contrast, the conditions are the price to be paid by the licence holder for the privilege of having the permission. They are designed to be modified, with proper checks and balances in the regulatory process, to reflect changing circumstances, changing economic or technical conditions—for example, changes in the nature of the business—or necessary developments in the legitimate requirements of the regulator and the public interest. Their purpose is to ensure that the licensee arranges his affairs so that adequate volumes and levels of service are available to users, that the business and assets are maintained in a state fit to meet those needs, that appropriate incentives to efficiency are provided, and that there are safeguards against abuse of monopoly power.
