Competition Bill [Lords]

Clause 18

Abuse of dominant position

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 61, in page 10, line 38, at end insert--

    `(2A) Nothing in this section prevents--

    (a) offering bulk discounts or promotional discounts,

    (b) the operation of existing contracts that have been cleared under previous competition legislation,

    (c) specifying the manner in which a brand is to be supported by retailers, or

    (d) franchise agreements.'.

I have set out my general reasons for opposing the clause as a whole. I should now like to turn to some more specific reasons. I tabled amendment No. 134, which was not selected, possibly for technical reasons. However, that amendment related to a matter that I wish to raise with the Under-Secretary. The amendment would have inserted in subsection (3)

    ``the relevant product market, provided that such market consists of, or extends to''

the United Kingdom. However, the point would have been covered more expeditiously by stating in subsection (1) that the prohibiton related to

    ``abuse of a dominant position''

in a relevant product market, inasmuch as

    ``it may affect trade within the United Kingdom.''

We shall not debate at this point what the phrase

    ``may affect trade within the United Kingdom''

means. I note that, in the master guidelines, the director general has promised us a separate document on that subject, but we shall constrain ourselves at present. However, I am worried because

    ``may affect trade with the United Kingdom''

in subsection (1) is qualified by subsection (3) which states that

    ```dominant position' means a dominant position within the United Kingdom.''

Subsection (3) continues:

    ```the United Kingdom' means the United Kingdom or any part of it.''

I am worried that those who wish to implement the prohibition might be guided by that conjunction of definitions to treat

    ``dominant position in a market''

that

    ``may affect trade within the United Kingdom''

in terms of a geographic market. That geographic market might be much more local than the United Kingdom, or it might take no account of the fact that the relevant product market is much wider than the United Kingdom.

I am guided by the remarks of the Director General of Fair Trading who, on 20 March, published a guide to the provisions of the Competition Act 1998. Those were not the master guidelines, but the market defintion ones. Paragraph 2.12 of the document states:

    ``A market definition should normallly contain two dimensions: a product and a geographic area.''

There we have it. The matter is perfectly straightforward. In clause 18, market, in this context, is assumed to be geographic market, not a product market.

Will the Under-Secretary give the Committee a categorical assurance that the interpretation of ``market'' in clause 18, with reference to some other part of the Bill, will be that it is a ``relevant product market''? I hoped that my amendment would ensure that the exact meaning would be ``relevant product market''.

When we were trying to bring competition to the forefront of the examination of merger references, one of the difficulties was the way in which people wanted to construe public interest, as it applied to mergers--to narrow it down to much less than the actual market within which that merger took place. The classic case was to constrain the public interest to refer to the market in only one area of the United Kingdom: Scotland was the typical example.

In bringing competition to the forefront, we wanted to examine the whole relevant product market, not merely that market within the United Kingdom. If there was a Community-wide market, we considered it on a Community-wide basis. Abuse of a dominant position should relate to the relevant product market and not merely to a geographic subset of it.

Mr. Nigel Griffiths: I want to explain yet again what the clause seeks to achieve.

Clause 18 introduces the chapter II prohibition, which acts against abuse of a dominant position that damages competitors and consumers. It is modelled closely on article 86 of the treaty of Rome and must be interpreted so as to avoid inconsistency with it. In interpreting the chapter II prohibition, the authorities and the courts will be required, by virtue of clause 60, to act so as to avoid inconsistency with the jurisprudence under article 86.

As a result, conduct that is an abuse under EC rules will be an abuse under our domestic law, and what constitutes dominance in the EC system will constitute dominance under the Bill. Businesses and their advisers will be able to look to EC jurisprudence for guidance on the interpretation of the domestic law. More important still, they can be confident that particular conduct will be viewed consistently under the two systems.

For too long British companies trading in European markets have had to deal with two completely different sets of rules--one at domestic and one at European level. That puts our companies at a disadvantage to many of their competitors elsewhere in the Community. The Bill will level the playing field and help to make it a truly single market for our firms.

There is wide agreement that the prohibition approach is the most effective means of deterring abuses of dominance. Many other Organisation for Economic Co-operation and Development countries--for example, the United States, France, Canada, Australia, Italy, New Zealand and Spain--have prohibition-based legislation in this area. The United Kingdom is behind the times. Our current regime for dealing with anti-competitive behaviour by monopolists badly needs radical overhaul. It is slow, cumbersome, sadly lacking in any real deterrent and absolutely lacking in any provision for recompense for those at the receiving end.

Under the existing regime, it takes far too long to halt anti-competitive practices. There has to be a lengthy period of investigation by the Office of Fair Trading and then the MMC before anything can be done. In the meantime, consumers and competitors suffer. Worse, competitors can be driven out of business. There are no powers to provide them with interim relief while investigations are completed. My right hon. Friend the President of the Board of Trade has already referred to the Southdown Motor case where such problems occurred. I could also mention bus services in the north-east of England, on which the MMC reported in 1995--once again after the damage had been done and a weaker business had been driven to the wall.

I entirely agree with the point made by the hon. Member for South Cambridgeshire that the relevant, geographic market should not be limited to the United Kingdom. However, his unsuccessful amendment was also unnecessary. In Britain as abroad, there is no excuse for letting dominant companies get away with bullying their smaller competitors and exploiting their customers. Just as I followed your strict instructions, Mr. Atkinson, not to take a tour of the Polish coalfields, I do not intend to visit the supermarket.

Clause 18(3) specifically provides that a dominant position may be within the United Kingdom or any part of it. That means that the relevant market may be a local one, and that is how it is defined in the Bill. The prohibition approach is right for a modern, dynamic economy. It will be a far more effective deterrent against anti-competitive behaviour. Abuses of dominance will be unlawful, in stark contrast to the current regime, where nothing is unlawful unless a lengthy investigation has taken place, followed by orders made through secondary legislation. Financial penalties and third party claims for damages may follow.
