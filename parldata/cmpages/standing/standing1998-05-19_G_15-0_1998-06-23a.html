Clause 73

Amendments, transitional provisions, savings and repeals

Mr. Griffiths: I beg to move amendment No. 203, in page 40, line 35, leave out `Schedule 13' and insert

    `Schedule (Transitional provisions and savings)'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments 46, 206, 204, 205 and Government new schedule 1 Transitional Provisions and Savings.

Mr. Griffiths: The new schedule replaces schedule 13, which deals with transitional arrangements. The effect of the schedule 13 amendments that were made in another place is to prevent the restrictive practices court from considering resale price maintenance on over-the-counter medicines. The impact of that would be, as one commentator put it, to throw local chemists on the mercy of the European Commission which community pharmacists regard as hostile to resale price maintenance.

The Government's position on over-the-counter medicines is clear. We do not seek to abolish resale price maintenance for such medicines. As my right hon. Friend the President of the Board of Trade told the House on 11 May:

    "There is no doubt that the Government strongly support and care about community pharmacies My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State has made it clear how important community pharmacies are to his vision of health in the next century." [Official Report, 11 May 1998; Vol 312, c.34.]

Independent pharmacies are small businesses that provide important services to the community. Local shops encourage community life and reduce the use of private transport and local pharmacies provide invaluable advice as well as medicines. My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Health has spoken of his plans to make greater use of pharmacies. The Government also want to support and encourage small businesses. When the restrictive practices court considers the director general's application under the 1976 Act, it will undoubtedly take full account of the role played by local pharmacies and of the harm that would be caused if, as they fear, many were unable to survive without resale price maintenance.

I would expect the court to consider fully the issues relating to the retention or abolition of RPM in the sector, including the effect on local community of possible pharmacy closures. As the court said in 1970:

    "Ease of access to a chemist shop is a matter of public importance, particularly having regard to the fact that many members of the public who make most use of the chemist shop are old, infirm or mothers of young children... A plentiful and well distributed supply of such shops is therefore essential."

In 317F/G, the court stated:

    "While the availability of popular proprietaries would not be adversely affected, the number of establishments in which other proprietary medicines less freely in demand by the public... can be bought would be substantially reduced, and this... would be a detriment to the public as consumers of those brands of proprietary medicines."

It continued:

    "perhaps more importantly, the disappearance of those retail chemists who fell casualties in this competition"

it meant from supermarkets

    "would result in a reduction of the number of points at which the public could get prescriptions dispensed... The disappearance of these dispensaries... would be a detriment to the public as consumers of prescribed medicines".

Those words are taken verbatim from the conclusions of the restrictive practices court in 1970, when there were more than 10,000 pharmacies with an NHS contract in England. In the intervening years, the number has shrunk from five figures to four figures. If the restrictive practices court was so supportive of a large number of community pharmacists in 1970, the case that community pharmacies have to put in the 1990s is even stronger.

The origins of the matter are to be found in a complaint made in 1995 by the supermarket chain Asda to the Office of Fair Trading and to the European Commission. Asda announced a decision to reduce prices on 82 brands of over-the-counter medicines. High Court injunctions were served by four drugs manufacturers and Asda was forced to increase its prices. The complaint has therefore been the subject of OFT consideration, but it has also been the subject of dialogue between the OFT and the European Commission. Therefore, I must emphasise that it would not be of any help to pharmacists if the court's proceedings were stayed. The Commission has made it clear that it was not pursuing its own investigations on the basis of action initiated by the Director General of Fair Trading. Moreover, article 85 is directly applicable to the United Kingdom. Aggrieved companies such as Asda could argue on that basis that restrictions on pricing are void.

Commissioner Van Miert has written to the President of the Board of Trade stating, in effect, that if Parliament blocks the restrictive practices court from considering resale price maintenance on over-the-counter medicines, the Commission would be likely to take up complaints that it has received from Asda under article 85 of the EC treaty. However, the restrictive practices court proceedings are allowed to continue on the understanding that the Commission will not pursue the complaints, provided that the court's decision is acceptable.

The Commission has said that it is committed to decentralised enforcement of competition law. It has issued a notice on co-operation between the Commission and national competition authorities, which sets out how cases in which both national authorities and the Commission have jurisdiction should be allocated and how both are to co-operate in resolving a case.

The Commission is content for the matter to be dealt with by domestic authorities. If it subsequently takes up a complaint, it would be bound to give due consideration to a national court's findings if it is argued that they are relevant to the assessment under article 85.

The Resale Prices Act 1976 regime is not dissimilar to the regime proposed in the Bill under article 85. The Act also provides for a prohibition regime, in this case more narrowly focused on resale price maintenance, subject to the possibility of exemption. The consideration for exemption of a class of goods under the Act might be relevant to a decision under article 85 in respect of an agreement. I expect the view of the restrictive practices court to be an important factor in any Commission consideration under article 85. In the light of that, I would want to take a robust line with the Commission if, following the court's decision, the Commission took action that flew in the face of that decision.

On the other hand, if the proceedings were to be stopped and the Commission were to take action, it might be more rapid than some people imagine. A Commission investigation into the Italian tobacco sector took only about two years. The recent decision on Volkswagen took about three years. A multinational company with extensive legal resources and competition advice was found to be in breach and fined £60 million.

In the case of RPM and OTC medicines, the Commission would have a head start. One element of the understanding between the Commission and the Office of Fair Trading on the handling of that case is that the OFT would make all information obtained during the course of its proceedings accessible to the Commission, so that the Commission could step in with no loss of time if the UK procedure encountered any obstacles.

It must be remembered that article 85 is directly applicable to the United Kingdom. If an interested party thinks that an agreement for resale price maintenance for OTC medicines is in breach of article 85, it can challenge its enforceability in a UK court. Such a challenge might be prompted by a Commission statement of objections. Supporters of resale price maintenance should recognise that in such circumstances, the practice could unravel very quickly. We have only to remember how the net book agreement collapsed under commercial pressures within 18 months, before the court case was concluded.

By contrast, we have provided in the schedule that if the restrictive practices court reaches a similar finding to that reached in 1970 that resale price maintenance for OTC medicines is an acceptable practice the new director general should not be able to intervene under the chapter I prohibition for a period of five years. That transitional protection has been widely welcomed by pharmacists.

The other amendments are technical, and I commend them to the Committee.

The dire prognosis is based on a calculation that 20 per cent. of the customers of those small pharmacies would shop instead in supermarkets in the event of the abolition of resale price maintenance.

No one could accuse the community pharmacy action group of overstating its case. The 20 per cent. estimate is substantially lower than that made by the Offic e of Fair Trading, which estimated that 36 per cent. of customers would shift to large supermarkets if resale price maintenance were abolished. The community pharmacy action group's fear that 3,000 could go under may indeed prove to be a very conservative estimate. The damage wrought by abolition could be much greater.

This country is not excessively endowed with pharmacies about 10 per cent. of rural parishes have them. The two small towns in my constituency, which otherwise consists of over 120 villages, have invaluable pharmacies. Community pharmacies in the small market town of Buckingham which is an admirable place that hon. Members are welcome to visit at any time and in the even smaller town of Winslow, want those pharmacies to be preserved. They support the retention of resale price maintenance and over-the-counter medicines.

I can assure the hon. Member for Milton Keynes, South-West that community pharmacies will be deeply unimpressed by her pusillanimous approach to this important subject. The margins enjoyed by small pharmacies are among the lowest in the European Union, as are the over-the-counter prices for medicines in that sector. Many low-income households are nevertheless prepared to pay a little extra because of the excellence and the convenience of the local small pharmacy service.
