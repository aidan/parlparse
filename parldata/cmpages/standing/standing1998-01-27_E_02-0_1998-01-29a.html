We think that we have the right number of regions. Opposition Members have argued that some regions are too big and that we may need to add to the proposed nine regions. The Government do not agree. We believe that we are right to develop on the present economical regional boundaries and to build on established and developing relationships within them.

It has been argued that the arrangements proposed in the Bill do not properly take account of the needs of the south of England and that a case can be made to increase the number of RDAs in the south. I do not accept that case.

In some areas in the south, regional identities may be less strong than in other parts of the country. For a variety of reasons, including the proximity of the capital, regional groupings may be less well established. That is not, to my mind, an argument for an increase in the number of regional development agencies. Rather, it argues for each region devising its own method of working, taking account of its own regional, sub-regional and cross-regional needs. RDAs will need to ensure that cross-regional working is efficient and effective and that proper sub-regional arrangements are in place.

The hon. Member for South-East Cornwall argued that his area of the south-west was a separate region. The hon. Member for South Suffolk (Mr. Yeo) argued in debates on previous amendments, to which he referred today, the case of Devon and Cornwall. There is therefore no consensus across such areas about where the boundaries should be. We can either have endless arguments about boundaries, or we can move forward to create a system that will help regional economies to grow. The Bill as it now stands contains flexibilities to allow sub-regional and cross-regional working. We think that that is sufficient flexibility.

The south-east region is a large region geographically, with a large population of more than seven million people. Any proposals to divide it, or to merge part of it with other regions, would create difficulties by disturbing the balance that has been reached in other adjacent regions. As we have already found in our earlier debates, it is difficult, in some cases, to find answers that are unarguably right, or that would command a better consensus than the proposals in the Bill. The danger of a power to increase the number of regions is that it might be used to create smaller and smaller entities, in an attempt to respond to local concerns, with the result that many of the advantages of a regional perspective would dissolve as matters were dealt with on a local level. That defeats the purpose of the Bill.

The key will lie in the working arrangements that RDAs adopt. Within regions, there will need to be effective arrangements for the delivery of services and for the exchange of information.

Cross-regional working, as we discussed earlier with respect to the Thames gateway initiative, will also be important. The south-west and the south-east may want to put in place arrangements that deal with the special needs of south coast towns. Similarly, the eastern region, the south-east and London will need to continue with the close working relations that are necessary to take forward the Thames gateway initiative and to deal with the economic interdependence of London and the surrounding area. Under clause 29(4) the Secretary of State is required, before making an order to alter the extent of a region, to consult every regional development agency affected by the proposed order, every local authority whose area is affected by the proposals and such other persons as he thinks fit.
