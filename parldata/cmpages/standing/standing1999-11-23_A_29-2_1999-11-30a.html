Amendment proposed: No. 681, in page 224, line 13, leave out `Part' and insert `Act'.—[Miss Melanie Johnson.]

Mr. Tyrie: It would be helpful if the Minister could briefly explain what the amendment consists of because I have not had a chance to examine it carefully.

Miss Melanie Johnson: That is a shocking revelation. I shall help the hon. Gentleman out—I just need to find my glasses because they will help me to help him out.

Schedule 14 makes further provision for the structure of the scheme that is established under part XV. It creates the framework around which the constitution of the scheme operator can be built and puts in place several requirements to which the scheme must adhere.

The role of the chief ombudsman is not spelt out in detail in the schedule, which simply requires the scheme operator to appoint a member of the panel of ombudsmen as chief ombudsman. His duty will be determined by the scheme operator and that body corporate's constitution.

The schedule makes additional provision for the panel of ombudsmen. A panel must be appointed by the scheme operator, and the chief ombudsman is taken from the panel. The ombudsmen on the panel must be independent of industry and the parties to complaints. The provision does not mean that the ombudsmen should be independent of one another.

The amendment is a minor drafting point. As paragraph 2(1) is drafted, it is not clear whether the word ``Part'' refers to part II of the schedule or to part XV of the Bill. The amendment uses the word ``Act'' to avoid that problem. That does not widen the provision; the only provisions that relate to the scheme are those under part XV and schedule 14 to that part.

I hope that all hon. Members will be happy to accept this simple amendment.

Miss Johnson: I beg to move, amendment No. 685, in page 228, line 18, at end insert—

    `( ) The standard terms may include provision to the effect that (unless acting in bad faith) none of the following is to be liable in damages for anything done or omitted in the discharge or purported discharge of functions in connection with the voluntary jurisdiction—

    (a)the scheme operator;

    (b)any member of its governing body;

    (c)any member of its staff;

    (d)any person acting as an ombudsman for the purposes of the scheme.'.

The amendment refers to the scheme's liability in damages in respect of the voluntary jurisdiction. Schedule 14 does not need to refer in detail to voluntary jurisdiction. For example, paragraph 19 simply provides for complaints to be dealt with and determined on standard terms which are fixed by the scheme operator and approved by the FSA and which can be agreed with participating firms and consented to by complainants who wish to use the scheme.

Standard terms for the voluntary jurisdiction may cover, albeit not on a statutory basis, procedural and other matters, such as fees, investigation powers, determination and enforcement of awards, which are covered in the Bill and in procedural and scheme rules under the compulsory jurisdiction. Indeed, the scheme operator and the FSA recently announced in their joint consultation paper No. 33 their intention that the standard terms under the voluntary jurisdiction should, as far as possible, mirror procedural and scheme rules under the compulsory jurisdiction.

However, there is uncertainty about the effectiveness of standard terms that purport to exclude liability of the scheme in damages. That uncertainty arises not just in relation to firms and complainants, who might otherwise argue later that it was an unusual, unfair or unreasonable exclusion to be contained in standard terms, but in relation to third parties who might not otherwise regard themselves as bound by the terms.

The schedule provides in paragraph 11 for the scheme operator and the ombudsmen to have statutory immunity in damages for anything done or not done in the discharge of their statutory functions under the compulsory jurisdiction, except when that is done in bad faith or is unlawful under section 6(1) of the Human Rights Act 1998. That immunity will allow the scheme to carry out its functions effectively and without constant fear of legal challenge.

Without immunity the scheme would be forced into a defensive position and would have to weigh the consequences of taking what it thought to be the correct action against the prospect of being sued.

As the relationship between the scheme and its voluntary jurisdiction members will be consensual, the scheme is not automatically afforded the same protection as under the compulsory jurisdiction. Nevertheless, we believe that the scheme should be able to provide effective immunity for the scheme operator, its staff and the ombudsmen, provided that those terms are contained in the standard terms agreed by the voluntary jurisdiction participants.

The amendment provides a statutory basis for standard terms which confer immunity on the scheme, with the agreement of the participating firms. As the voluntary jurisdiction is consensual, there is no need to provide the same safeguards in respect of section 6(1) of the Human Rights Act 1998. I urge hon. Members to accept the amendment.

That is a key hallmark of an ombudsman scheme and how it is likely to work. The provision is consistent with current banking and insurance schemes. The ombudsman scheme is intended to be fairly informal and quick, especially compared with the courts. We are all agreed that it is highly desirable to achieve that result. The court-based process, as the right hon. and learned Gentleman knows, can provide an inherent advantage to the better-resourced party—usually the firm. It is important for consumers to be able to seek redress without fear or financial risk to themselves. They can go through the ombudsman, rather than an expensive court-based process. That is one reason why this scheme is attractive to consumers and other complainants in different circumstances. It is also important for consumer confidence that awards are binding on firms.

Sir Nicholas Lyell: I am keen on ombudsman schemes, which should be binding, but is it the case that the banking scheme is binding on one side only?

Miss Johnson: It is as I understand it.

Let me say more about how the scheme ombudsman must determine complaints under compulsory jurisdiction. The clause does not direct the way in which a decision will be reached; nor does it set out the process for mediation and determination. That detail will be determined by the Financial Services Authority and the scheme under their respective rules. The procedural aspects of the ombudsman scheme are set out in clause 199. In particular, the ombudsman must inform the respondent and the complainant of his decision and the reasons for it. The complainant is under a duty to accept or reject that determination within a set period. If a determination is accepted, it will be binding on the respondent.

There has been much talk about the fact that the ombudsman's decision is binding on the respondent while the complainant is free to choose whether to accept a decision. The scheme exists to enable individuals to settle cases outside the expensive arena of the courts. If the way remains open for a respondent to reject a decision and take the matter to court, the consumer could again decide that it is better not to pursue a complaint. That key hallmark of the scheme is important for consumer confidence. Decisions by the banking ombudsman and insurance ombudsman schemes are binding on firms only, which puts them in the same category as existing schemes.

It is perfectly possible to operate the scheme effectively while also protecting the parties' ECHR rights. Firms and complainants that bring disputes to the ombudsman will be able to exercise their right to a fair and public hearing. The option of a judicial review will be open to both firms and consumers once the ombudsman's decision has been taken, which ensures that all parties will be heard. Article 6(1) stipulates that in the determination of civil rights and obligations, everyone is entitled to a fair and public hearing by an independent and impartial tribunal. The scheme will provide for a hearing to be held if one is requested by a party to the complaint. We do not believe that the scheme will be legalistic. We expect the right to a fair hearing to be exercised frequently.

On the points raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Bedford (Mr. Hall), we expect the ombudsman to take account of existing voluntary codes when deciding what is fair and reasonable. The point of the test is that it is flexible. We do not want to hem in the ombudsman by stipulating the exact standards that will be set for firms. They will depend on the nature of the complaint. In addition, it is not for the ombudsman to improve the codes, although he can comment if he thinks that they are causing firms to make defective decisions.

My hon. Friend also mentioned the definition of small businesses. Whether small businesses can use the scheme depends on the definition of the eligible complainant, which is a matter for the scheme rules that are made by the authority under clause 198(6). That takes into account people who bring complaints to existing schemes.

Clause 206

Collective investment schemes

Mr. Tyrie: I beg to move amendment No. 707, in page 104, line 21, at end add—

    `(6) A company approved under section 842 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 as an investment trust shall not be a collective investment scheme.'.

Will the Minister explain why my amendment is unacceptable? It seems perfectly reasonable that a company approved under section 842 of the 1988 Act should not be part of a collective investment scheme. I look forward to hearing why the Minister finds that unacceptable.

Mr. Timms: In view of the hon. Gentleman's comments a few moments ago, I shall be glad to help.

The amendment provides that a company approved under section 842 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 as an investment trust should not count as a collective investment scheme. I am not convinced that that is appropriate. UK-incorporated investment trusts are not presently caught by collective investment scheme legislation and it is not intended that they will be under the Bill. That is why the amendment is unnecessary.

Subsection 206(5) provides that the Treasury may provide by order that certain types of arrangement may be excluded from the definition of collective investment scheme. We published a draft of that order for consultation in February and paragraph 20 of the schedule to that order clearly specifies that UK-incorporated investment trusts cannot be collective investment schemes unless they are also an open-ended investment company.

A standard investment trust is generally a closed-ended corporate body. Incorporation in the UK would take place under the Companies Acts. If a company were to redeem or repurchase its shares, it would do so under chapter 7 of part V of the Companies Act, which deals essentially with the maintenance of capital in the interests of shareholders. UK-incorporated investment trusts with those features are not open-ended vehicles, as clause 207, which specifies the definition of an open-ended investment company, makes clear.

I understand that section 842, referred to in the amendment, can apply to companies not incorporated in the UK but resident here effectively, if their central management and control is here. The amendment could provide that irrespective of where they are incorporated, bodies corporate that are incorporated overseas and resident here would automatically not be collective investment schemes. That could make the tax status of a company determine its regulatory classification, which I am not convinced is right in principle. In the light of that explanation, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment.
