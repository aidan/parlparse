Question again moved, That the amendment be made.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I was elaborating on the risks to the self-assessment system posed by the clause. The complexities signalled by the clause, which will come into effect in 1999, will impose higher costs on taxpayers by generating compliance cost increases. More taxpayers will have to consult professional advisers and that means more work for my profession so I suppose that I should not complain. From the point of view of responsible government, it is regrettable to increase the cost in that way because it is a cost that will be borne by the economy as a whole.

Government, of which the tax system is part, is the largest service industry in the world and often not an efficient service industry. I generally exempt our own institutions from that, but we should be wary of doing anything in legislation which increases costs on the economy as a whole. Increased complexity in the tax system does exactly that. With self-assessment, the chance of error is increased and if taxpayers make errors, they will face penalties.

For all those reasons, it is important to drive for more simplicity and transparency. The Red Book made great play of that and it is a desirable objective, but our charge is that the Government have not followed the objective through into deed. The abolition of ACT credit relief has brought in its train a series of additional complications which, under clause 31, will result in increased compliance costs.

The Economic Secretary may say that the problem is small and trivial and involves only a few extra computations, but I ask her to try to quantify the position. For example, will the computation worksheets prepared by the Inland Revenue have to be lengthened or added to to take account of the changes? That would be a fairly good objective guide as to whether there will be increased complexity for taxpayers.

The costs may be difficult to quantify but no less real because they are dispersed among the economy at large. That is why the amendment calls for a study to be made before the clause comes into operation. A study would take some time and cost something in the short term, but it would be well worth it if it could identify the real cost to the economy in the longer term. If the Government's response is that the costs are comparatively small and acceptable, a study would reveal that and then we would all be satisfied. For those reasons, I urge the Committee to accept amendment No. 45.

Mr. Geoffrey Clifton-Brown (Cotswold): It was George Bernard Shaw who said that genius was simplicity. I would add that any damn fool can make things complicated. The previous Government were absolutely right to introduce self-assessment. The problem is that ever since we initially consulted on the idea, it has grown more and more complicated. But it need not grow more and more complicated if one considers the systems in other countries, such as America and Australia.

It is a feature of our Finance Bills that they are unnecessarily complicated. Anyone can write a complicated piece of legislation; one studies the subject in great depth and drafts the legislation to cover every permutation and possibility. It is much more effective to write the legislation in a simpler way with more widely drafted anti-avoidance provisions so that the Government and Inland Revenue can challenge the individual taxpayer if some evasion takes place.

The test of whether self-assessment works will be what proportion of taxpayers are able to complete the self-assessment returns. If we find that a ridiculously high percentage of returns have to be completed by accountants, self-assessment will not have been a success. I urge the Economic Secretary--or the Financial Secretary, because I am unsure which of them will reply to the debate--carefully to consider the rewrite exercise and see whether we can simplify our finance legislation.

During the Committee stage of last year's Finance Bill, a comparatively short paragraph in the notes on clauses equated to a complicated two-page ramble in the Bill. When I asked the then Minister why the simple paragraph in the notes on clauses would not suffice, I did not receive a satisfactory answer. That proves that there is often a much simpler way of writing legislation, and I appeal to the Economic Secretary and to those involved in drafting financial legislation to make it simpler.
