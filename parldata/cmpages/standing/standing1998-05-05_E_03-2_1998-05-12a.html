Clause 15

Fiscal representatives

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. St. Aubyn: I shall speak only very briefly, Mrs. Dunwoody. [Hon. Members: ``Hear, hear.''] I am glad that my decision is popular with the Committee.

I am concerned about the proposal that fiscal representatives should provide security. In some cases, fiscal representatives may be dissatisfied with the amount of duty that has been levied on them. Will the clause allow them rights of appeal and, if so, how will their security be treated during the course of such an appeal?

Dawn Primarolo: The hon. Gentleman perhaps misunderstands the option that is offered in clause 15 with regard to passenger duty.

The clause will provide an additional degree of revenue protection and make it easier for certain foreign airlines to comply with the air passenger duty law by offering them choice, flexibility and a potential reduction in their compliance costs.

Under current law, any aircraft operator without a business establishment in the United Kingdom that operates chargeable flights from UK airports is required to appoint a fiscal representative who will be liable for the airline's duty debts. The Bill will not alter the provisions of the existing legislation in that respect.

A number of airlines have been unable to find representatives who are willing to take the risk of duty liability. In such circumstances, the clause will provide that the airline will not be brought under revenue control, the duty will not be secured and the airline will therefore be in breach of the air passenger duty law. It will be the fact that the airline cannot find a fiscal representative that puts it in breach of law, not the issue of whether it is prepared to meet its air passenger duty liabilities.

The clause will enable airlines to appoint ``administrative representatives'' who will be responsible for keeping records and accounts but will not be liable for the duty debts of their principals, on condition that the airlines provide security for the duty.

The Government are thus offering a choice to airlines that are experiencing difficulties in acquiring a fiscal representative. If the commissioners were to ask the airline for further security in such a situation, that request would be appealable. The Government are trying to ease the difficulty that some airlines experience when attempting to comply with the original APD law that was instituted by the previous Government. I believe that it will be a helpful measure.

Mr. St. Aubyn: I understand what the hon. Lady has said, which highlighted the reason why I asked my question in the first place. If an unfair assessment is made, as a result of which security is drawn down, I am concerned that the airline's right of appeal could come after that point. Will its right of appeal pre-empt the drawing down of security or, as the Minister's words seemed to imply, will the appeal only pre-empt a further demand for additional security?

8.45 pm

Dawn Primarolo: The current position is that security is calculated by the airline based on the commissioners' directions. There would be no right of appeal on that. If the commissioners asked for further security, that would be appealable. I understand the hon. Gentlemans's point. The new clause allows overseas airlines the option of paying security and having a fiscal representative responsible only for the records. They are required to do one or the other.

Security is calculated by the airline, based on the commissioners' directions, so there should be no difficulties there.

Mr. St. Aubyn: It is important that we clarify the point. If I understood the Minister correctly, she said that the original security may be seized by Customs pending an appeal, but that Customs may not demand additional security while the appeal is taking place. That begs the question, as the business carries on operating--which it will no doubt want to do while the appeal is being heard--what happens to further liability for duty during that period? Will the Government stay their hand, or will they invent a further demand for security outside the appeal process, which negates, in its own way, the comfort that the Minister is trying to give to airlines in that position?

Dawn Primarolo: The hon. Gentleman does not understand the position. The commissioners' directions are in a public notice. Security is based on duty, liability and past compliance. The company can agree on that security. Its fiscal representative will then have a lesser responsibility. The hon. Gentleman talked about drawing down security. The security would be the company's liability to pay its tax to release it from the requirement on fiscal liability, which is the current regime.

Airlines therefore have two options. They can operate under the current regime or under this regime. In both cases, the liability is calculated on the basis of their duty liability. I fail to see the hon. Gentleman's point. He is trying to make it sound as though the commissioners dream up a liability and draw down on the security totally unreasonably. That is not the way that APD works. The clause does not change the way it works or the calculations of liability.

 

Mr. Hammond: I understand the Financial Secretary's answer to my hon. Friend the Member for Guildford. I have a slightly different question. It is extremely technical, but I am sure that she will be able to put my mind at rest. It has been raised with me by people in the business.

If a fiscal representative is appointed under section 34A as an administrative representative only, and if that administrative representative gives security, at any time, for any reason and notwithstanding his appointment as an administrative representative, would he have the same rights of appeal to a VAT and duties tribunal that a fiscal representative appointed under section 34 has under existing legislation?

Dawn Primarolo: That is a good question. The commissioners issue the amount that an airline is liable to pay on its APD on the basis of its liability, and that will not change. The airline can have a fiscal representative with full liability, as currently determined, or an alternative, which would be somebody who accounts and holds the business. That person agrees directly with the commissioners the security that is necessary in the light of existing provision. Assessments are then made for liability. The previous Government put that legislation in place. Because of the calculation for liability for APD, the appeals procedure against liability remains the same.

Opposition Members are looking for complications that are not there. The Government are removing liability to pay the duty from the fiscal representative and substituting a security from the airline. The airline would have the same right of appeal as the fiscal representative. This is getting very complicated. The fiscal representative currently has the right of appeal under the full powers. If security was required, there would, therefore, be the same right of appeal.

Clause 16

Rates of duty where pollution reduced

Mr. Hammond: I beg to move amendment No. 21, in page 8, line 46, after `vehicles', insert `or types of vehicles'.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss amendment No. 22, in schedule 1, page 144, line 13, at end insert--

    `(3A) Without prejudice to the generality of subsection (1), regulations under this section shall provide that, once a certificate has been issued in respect of any one vehicle of a particular type, it will be sufficient for the keeper of any other vehicle of the same type to demonstrate that his vehicle is of the like type as that in respect of which the certificate was issued; and that in those circumstances no further proof shall be required that the reduced pollution requirements are satisfied in respect of the vehicle.'.

Mr. Hammond: This is a probing amendment in the genuine sense of the term. We seek to understand exactly what the Government have in mind in clause 16 and in schedule 1, which relates to it. We are slightly hindered in our pursuit of knowledge by the fact that everything hinges on regulations that have yet to be made. I understand, however, that negotiations between the Treasury and industry on the form of the regulations are at a relatively advanced stage. For the purpose of our discussion, therefore, I shall assume that the draft that is in play in those discussions indicates the final form of the regulations.

It is not clear to me whether the concession that will be granted under clause 16 will be available only to vehicles that are retrospectively fitted with a means of reducing their emission of harmful particulate material or whether it will apply also to new vehicles that are manufactured to a stricter emissions standard.

My attention was initially drawn to that possible problem by the wording of clause 16, which clearly refers to

    ``vehicles adapted so as to reduce pollution''.

I am not sure whether I should understand the word ``adapted'' in an active or passive sense. From my reading of the documentation that is circulating in the industry, however, I understand that the clause is seen as dealing specifically with the clean-up of vehicles that are in the fleet as of today.

The first point on which I seek clarification from the Financial Secretary is, therefore, whether the Government will allow the reduced rate for all vehicles that meet a specified emissions standard or whether it will apply, for example, only to vehicles of the pre-Euro I category which have been retrofitted with some means of reducing their particulate emissions to bring them up to the Euro III standard. The industry documentation often refers to such percentage particulate removals, which appear to be the way in which the improvement can be achieved.

Whatever the Financial Secretary's answer to that question, it brings the Committee directly to the point of the amendment. It seeks to provide a mechanism for allowing type approval of either new vehicles--if the concession will apply to new vehicles that meet specific stringent emission standards--or of commercially available catalyser systems or particulate traps that may be retrofitted to existing vehicles.

The Government's intention is clear. It is entirely laudable to reduce the particulate emissions of heavy vehicles. As I understand it, however, the cost of fitting a particulate trap to a goods vehicle is likely to be about £4,000. Having fitted it, the operator is likely to find that the vehicle's engine achieves a somewhat reduced level of performance in terms of mileage. The operator is also likely to face a somewhat increased routine maintenance requirement, in respect of both the engine and of the particulate trap.

It does not take a mathematician, which I freely admit that I am not, to calculate that a maximum £500 per annum duty rebate will be a modest incentive to the road freight industry to fit a £4,000 kit to vehicles that will often, almost by definition, be relatively old. Although the contribution is small, anything that reduces the cost and hassle to the operator of getting his vehicle approved for the lower rate of duty is to be welcomed. The requirement in schedule 1 for every vehicle to be tested and certificated, and for a fee to be payable for that testing and certification will be a further nail in the coffin in respect of the financial analysis that every operator will undertake to decide whether the proposition is attractive.

By proposing tight approvals either of vehicles or of commercially available means of reducing emissions, the Opposition seek to reduce the testing and certification costs and the off-road time of the vehicle, which will be an equally important factor to commercial operators. The amendment would make it marginally more attractive to fit such means of reducing emissions and to qualify for the reduced duty level offered by the clause. Whether that proposed duty level will ever be a sufficient incentive to motivate the economic actors in this equation to do what the Government want them to do is another question, which will be more properly addressed at the clause stand part debate.

Will the Government assure the Committee that they will seek to facilitate the development of commercially available standardised emission reduction systems by allowing type approvals, so that individual vehicles do not have to incur the time, cost and off-road implications of certification on a vehicle-by-vehicle basis?

That was clearly a good idea, and consultation was absolutely necessary.

Clause 17

Restriction of Exemption for old vehicles

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 10, in page 9, line 4, at end insert

    `unless such a vehicle is driven for fewer than 1,500 miles a year.'.

This is a mean little clause, and the amendment would improve it--or at least limit the damage that it will cause. My right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Rushcliffe (Mr. Clarke) introduced a concession in the previous Parliament to exempt from vehicle excise duty vehicles more than 25 years old. That has proved popular, well targeted and an effective way of addressing the circumstance in which classic and veteran cars are owned and used.

The University of Central England in Birmingham has conducted a survey. It estimates that there are 658,575 such vehicles, made before 1977. the real number might be higher because some vehicles are owned by clubs not affiliated to the sponsoring organisation--the Federation of British Historic Vehicle Clubs. About 64 per cent. of those vehicles are licensed and roadworthy, so we are talking about more than 400,000 vehicles, which sounds like a great many--as indeed it is--but it is only about 2 per cent. of the total number of vehicles on the road.

Some of those vehicles are part of our heritage; we should look after and cherish them. It may not be cool Britannia, because it harks back to a more industrialised phase in our national economic history. That does not appeal to the Government, who, as we have heard in other debates, are entirely unconcerned about the state of manufacturing industry and how it is suffering from the high pound. That debate will continue elsewhere, but all hon. Members should agree that the motoring past is valuable and should be preserved and that such vehicles should be kept in good repair and used.

Moreover, a surprisingly large number of people use such vehicles for everyday transport, especially those on modest incomes, who used to receive the sympathy and understanding of the Labour party. Those days are gone, but that category of people and vehicles should catch at least the passing attention of Labour Members. In recognition of that sector of the population, my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Rushcliffe exempted such vehicles from vehicle excise duty. That part of the Finance Bill of four or five years ago has stood the test of time. Although the national lottery awards generous grants to several heritage institutions, it cannot reach individual owners of such vehicles. That privatised sector of our heritage is not touched by lottery funding. It was, therefore, a perceptive concession on the part of my right and learned Friend to give such people that modest help.

A further feature of such vehicles is their low mileage. The study to which I have already referred estimates that the average mileage covered by such vehicles that are roadworthy and licensed is about 1,224. That compares with the average mileage for all vehicles, which is in excess of 10,000. Such vehicles therefore make few demands on the road network. That should be recognised in the licensing provisions, which we did through exemption.

The clause is mean minded. It will erode that exemption over time by specifying the date 1973, which will bring increasingly more vehicles into the vehicle excise net. Fewer historic vehicles will qualify as they are scrapped or become unroadworthy.

The amendment would restore the concession to vehicles that are drive for fewer than 1,500 miles a year. It should be possible to administer and enforce such a concession. If not, the Financial Secretary has another means at her disposal--to withdraw the clause. To do so would not be expensive, the clause is unnecessary, given the current state of Government revenue. I should like to hear the Financial Secretary's initial remarks about the amendment before we debate clause stand part.

Mr. Edward Davey (Kingston and Surbiton): Apart from his attempt to rewrite industrial history, I agreed with much of what the right hon. Member for Wells said. His description of the clause as mean was well put, and I shall support his amendment tonight.

For relief on the transfer of a going concern to apply, several conditions must be met. The principal one is that the business must be capable of being run as a stand-alone business. It would be a concession to business if Customs and Excise were to agree not to apply clause 22 to services that were pre-paid or invoiced before 18 March in the normal course of business. Will the Minister respond to that suggestion?

Dawn Primarolo: Clause 22 is an anti-forestalling provision that relates to the rules that set out whether a supply of services is made in the UK for VAT purposes. It will allow us to consult in advance of changes when we signal an interest, without compromising the pre-payment provisions.

The Institute of Directors raised a point about contracts in its representations to us. The clause will only operate in combination with a change to the place of supply rules. It is not retrospective, but will act as a warning. It means that we do not need to use the forestalling regulation on every occasion when we consider place of supply.

Suppliers are normally required to account for VAT on their supplies in accordance with the rules that determine when those supplies are treated as having taken place. In relation to services, that is in many cases linked to when the services are actually performed. However, if a payment is received beforehand, the supply is treated as having taken place at that time. That means that people could avoid VAT by making pre-payments prior to a change in the place of supply rules for services, which brings into the UK's VAT net services that would formerly have been treated as having been outside it.

The clause's purpose is to prevent avoidance. It applies to any order made after 17 March 1998 which changes the place where a supply of services is treated as having been made, so that it is treated as occurring outside the UK. It will ensure that where payments are made for services before the starting date of such an order, but relate to services that are performed afterwards, VAT will be chargeable on all or part of those services from the starting date of the order. Time of supply will be determined according to the time at which the services are performed. Compliance costs will have a limited effect because of the nature of the services. The clause gives signals to business, hence minimising compliance costs. The clause is designed to help Customs and Excise and business to deal with such matters.

Clause 24

Long leases in Scotland

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.45 pm
 

Mr. Hammond: The notes on clauses explain why it is technically difficult in Scotland to define a ``major interest'' for the purposes of the Value Added Tax Act 1994 in the same way as a ``major interest'' is defined for other legal purposes and for value added tax purposes throughout the United Kingdom. I understand from the notes on clauses that the pactice of Customs and Excise, under extra statutory concession, has been to treat disposals of residential buildings on 20-year leases in Scotland as though they were disposals of a major interest. The clause is designed to formalise prevailing practice. The practical and revenue effects will therefore be nil.

However, a point of principle is at stake. Notwithstanding the Government's valiant attempts to render it otherwise, we still live in a united kingdom, and a substantial body of opinion believes that it is unhelpful and unnecessary to introduce differences in the taxation system between Scotland and the rest of the United Kingdom. Conservative Members see no reason why the definition of a ``major interest'' for value added tax purposes could not be reduced to 20 years throughout the United Kingdom, thus preserving the principle of common taxation and overcoming the technical problem in Scotland that the clause is designed to address.

Mr. Fallon: The clause is important; I let my junior speak first on it. As he correctly points out, developers in England will be worse off than those in Scotland as a result of the clause. Major interest leases in England will still be defined by a term exceeding 21 years; it would be sensible to make it 20 years throughout the United Kingdom.

As I understand it, the press release was a revelation to the industry. It merely stated:

    ``Until now, Customs have treated Scottish leases of 20 years in the same way as 21 year leases in England on a case by case basis''.

That was never a published concession. I understand that it has not appeared in a Customs and Excise notice. Why is it done on a case-by-case basis? What criteria have been applied? If the clause will give effect to an existing concession, surely that concession should be deemed to have always had effect up to this point?

Dawn Primarolo: This simple change provides that 20-year leases of housing in Scotland can be zero rated for VAT purposes. The clause will end an anomaly that has disadvantaged some developers who let properties on lease in Scotland because of the difference between the Scottish legal system and that in the rest of the United Kingdom.

The hon. Member for Sevenoaks asked: why not change to 20 years for the whole of the United Kingdom? Requiring a lease to run for 21 years or longer in order for it to be zero rated causes no problems in the rest of the United Kingdom, where most property leases run for more than 25 years anyway, so there is no need to make that change. The hon. Gentleman also asked: will not that give Scottish developers an advantage over developers in the rest of the United Kingdom? In theory, it will give them a minor advantage, but in practice the change is necessary for them to be fairly treated in comparison with other UK developers. The clause is intended to regularise the practice inherited from the previous Government of recognising the difference between the legal system in Scotland and that in the rest of the UK.

Mr. Fallon: I know that the Minister has had to deal with amendments all day, but we asked two final questions. First, was the concession published? Why was it not in any official Customs and Excise notice? If it was introduced on a case-by-case basis, what criteria were applied?

Dawn Primarolo: I inherited the situation from the previous Government. If the hon. Gentleman insists on an answer, he can get one in two ways: he can either ask the right hon. Member for Wells why the previous Government acted in that way, or I can write to him with a specific response. I will be happy to write to him.

Question put and agreed to.
