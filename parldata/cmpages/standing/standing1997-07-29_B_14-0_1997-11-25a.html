New Clause 2

Apportionment of payments etc. made for more than one earner

    ` . After subsection (2) of section 3 of the Contributions and Benefits Act (``earnings'' and ``earner'') there shall be inserted the following subsection--

    ``(2A) Regulations made for the purposes of subsection (2) above may provide that, where a payment is made or a benefit provided to or for the benefit of two or more earners, a proportion (determined in such manner as may be prescribed) of the amount or value of the payment or benefit shall be attributed to each earner.''.'.-- [Mr. Denham.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

 

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. John Denham): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

With your permission, Mr. O'Hara, I should like to give details of an amendment that I intend to table at a later stage.

I am grateful to the Committee for its speedy acceptance last Thursday of the clauses that will introduce the new penalty regime for the Contributions Agency. The package includes the new criminal offence of fraudulent evasion of national insurance contributions, which will be a major weapon in our armoury against employers who wilfully misappropriate contributions instead of paying them to the collector of taxes or the Contributions Agency. A consequence of changing the legislation and decriminalising the majority of misdemeanours is that the Department will lose its current ability to transfer liability for a company's unpaid debt to a negligent director under sections 114 and 115 of the Social Security Administration Act 1992, except in the handful of cases where we obtain a conviction for fraudulent evasion.

The Government are determined to act against the problems caused by phoenix directors and their ilk, who put their companies into liquidation, having failed to hand over their national insurance contributions or those of their employees, but having made a personal financial gain. A new company appears shortly afterwards, often on the same premises, with the same employees doing the same work, and the process then repeats itself. The national insurance fund loses about £150 million a year when companies go into insolvency with national insurance debts. Up to one third of that is due to phoenixism. The end result is that contributions that have been collected from employees' pay have to be credited and benefits paid out on the strength of the contributions that the fund never receives. I am therefore loth not to replace our current ability to act against directors and officers with another in keeping with the new penalty regime. That is what the further amendment will do.

In brief, when a company fails to pay in full a contributions debt due to the negligent or fraudulent behaviour of any or all of the directors, consideration will be given to transferring the debt to those directors personally. A small, highly trained body of Contributions Agency staff, liaising closely with Inland Revenue officers, will investigate the facts. They will decide whether negligence or fraudulence is involved and, if so, which of the directors are culpable. A decision as to the degree of culpability in each case will be made. The total debt, including any associated penalty, will be apportioned between the culpable directors in proportion to their degree of culpability. As a safeguard, directors will have a full right of appeal against the decision that they have behaved negligently or fraudulently.

As I said, directors will have a full right of appeal against a decision that they have behaved negligently or fraudulently and can challenge whether the apportionment was reasonably exercised.

I will further reassure the Committee of the limited circumstances in which it is intended that the measure will be used. It is primarily, but not exclusively, aimed at the phoenix-type directors who have deliberately failed to pay NICs and usually PAYE tax and put the company into insolvency, having made a personal financial gain. Involvement in large NICs debts in relation to two or more companies would suggest a greater degree of culpability than in relation to a first company.

I hope that legitimate businesses will welcome the measure as a signal of Government intent to deal severely with the less scrupulous minority. The penalty regime poses no threat to honest, hard-working business men. There will, of course, be an opportunity for a full discussion of the proposal when the amendment is tabled, but I wanted to give the Committee notice of our intention. I am grateful for your indulgence, Mr. O'Hara, in allowing me to make that announcement. I hope that it will help the Committee.

The Government plan to use new clause 2 to assist in implementing the new policies to bring vouchers and payments into funded unapproved retirement benefit schemes into national insurance liability. Many of those policies can be introduced under existing legislation. The new clause is needed to ensure that employers cannot circumvent the national insurance charge by the simple expedient of making payments to cover more than one employee, in a manner that would frustrate the imposition of the charge.

To help the Committee to set the clause in context, I shall set out briefly the main details of our new policies and explain how the new clause will contribute to them, and how we plan to use the regulation-making powers that it contains.

I shall deal first with our proposals for non-cash vouchers. By these we mean in principle a voucher exchangeable for goods or services--for example, a store voucher. Non-cash vouchers are generally subject to tax but not liable for national insurance. The Government believe that that is unfair. It is unfair for low-paid employees who receive vouchers and who therefore do not pay contributions that would help them to earn benefits to support them in periods of unemployment, sickness or old age.

In the worst cases, payment in vouchers rather than cash can mean that people do not pay any contributions at all and so miss out completely on the protection of the contributory benefit system. It is more generally unfair to employees who are paid in cash, who pay more contributions than employees who receive non-cash vouchers. It gives an unfair competitive advantage to employers who pay in vouchers, compared to those who pay in cash.

The--by now infamous--Great Yarmouth case is a clear example. The manager of a nursing home paid her employees in cash to just below the lower earnings limit and topped up their pay with a substantial amount in vouchers, with the express stated intention of avoiding national insurance.

The Government want to take action to nip that kind of practice in the bud, and to ensure fairness to employers and employees alike. We propose to bring non-cash vouchers firmly into liability for national insurance. However, we intend to exempt childcare vouchers for the time being, so as not to cut off options in the development of our national childcare strategy.

Powers to charge national insurance on vouchers already exist in section 3 of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992, which defines earnings for national insurance purposes. Vouchers are currently exempted in regulation 19 of the contributions regulations.

We will end that exemption from April 1999, and bring non-cash vouchers into contributions liability at the point at which they are supplied to employees. As a general principle we will follow closely the Inland Revenue's practice when dealing with exemptions from the charge and valuation mechanisms, although some differences will be necessary to reflect the nature of the tax and national insurance systems. For example, employers will need to record contributions on a weekly or monthly basis, in line with normal practice for contributions, rather than on an annual basis as for tax. That is essential to ensure that contributions can feed properly into individual's benefit records.

New clause 2 is needed to deal with circumstances in which a voucher may be provided collectively to a group of employees, or those in which a block of vouchers may be bought by an employer and in which it is necessary to apportion the costs to each employee, who would then receive a voucher. Without the clause it would be possible to circumvent the national insurance charge by providing a single voucher to benefit two or more employees.

We intend to consult closely with business to ensure that we produce regulations that will provide a sensible balance between the need to provide a robust means of valuing collective vouchers in a way which cannot be circumvented, and our desire to impose the least possible administrative burden. In some cases, employers may be able to use the new PAYE settlement agreements to cover such vouchers and to reduce administration. However, settlement agreements are voluntary mechanisms and cannot necessarily cover all cases. We are sympathetic to the need to keep burdens low for employers, but I must emphasise that if employers choose to pay through this route, they must meet the administrative requirements which go with that payment method. That will include apportioning costs in certain circumstances.

I shall now deal with our proposals to bring funded unapproved retirement benefit schemes into national insurance. FURBS are retirement benefit schemes that are outside the scope of tax-approved retirement benefit schemes. Typical FURBS consist of an employer securing future benefits through an insurance policy or through the establishment of a separate trust. Insurance-based schemes are common in the case of so-called death benefit schemes, under which insurance policies are written into trust for employees. In practice, trustees apply the employer's contributions towards an insurance policy that provides a lump sum death benefit on the death of the specified person. The most common alternative model involves the employer establishing a trust fund specifically for participating employees and members.

FURBS started as a way to allow employers to provide a top-up for high earners who have reached the maximum that they can put into their tax approved personal or occupational pension. Employees are taxed on employers' contributions outside tax-approved schemes.

Some pension providers recommend FURBS as a way for companies to provide pensions whilst circumventing the restrictions required for tax-approved status. One of the attractions of these schemes is their flexibility. They are not necessarily used to provide retirement benefits, but they can be used to pay bonuses with a single payment from the employer passed on to the employee after just a few weeks. We propose to bring the tax and national insurance treatment of these schemes closer together by bringing FURBS fully into national insurance.

As with vouchers, very little new primary legislation is needed. Since we first announced our proposals to bring all FURBS into earnings, the Contributions Agency has received legal advice that has led it to take a more robust view of the current legislation. The agency this week has announced that most payments into FURBS in fact are caught by existing legislation. It has issued guidance to pension providers, accountants and other professional advisers to explain which schemes are caught.

Employers with schemes caught under current law will be required to include the payments in earnings for national insurance by April 1998 at the latest. The agency generally will not seek arrears of contributions for the past, as some employers may have found the previous guidance misleading. That would clearly be unfair. However, there is one exception to that for a small group of avoidance schemes that the agency has identified and against which it has always sought to enforce liability. In those schemes the employer has clearly never intended that the scheme should provide retirement benefits--the whole exercise is directed towards paying an annual bonus without paying contributions. The money is paid into the trust and out again within weeks or even days without there being a pension provided. The agency has always taken a firm stand against FURBS that in reality, do not aim to provide genuine pensions, and it will continue to pursue arrears from that type of naked scam.

    Take up
