Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: The clause deals with disciplinary powers and therefore is of great interest to market makers and participants, given that under the provision their livelihoods could be jeopardised and their reputation tarnished if the Financial Services Authority made a mistake. Against that, however, I and the whole Committee want such powers to be effective and wrongdoers to be brought to book so there is the usual balance to be struck.

The amendments would strengthen the obligations on the authority in several respects. Amendment No. 307 would clarify the standard of proof that the authority must meet to take action. In most professional discipline systems, the standard of proof applied is that of the criminal courts, which is one of beyond reasonable doubt. The Bill does not make clear exactly on what basis the authority needs to be satisfied of misconduct, but the amendment would make explicit in subsection (1)(a) that it must be satisfied beyond reasonable doubt.

Amendment No. 299 is a little more complicated than amendment No. 307. Under the clause, someone could face disciplinary action if he knew that another authorised person was doing something, even though he might not be aware that the second authorised person was contravening a requirement under the Act. If I were a dealer in the City, for example, I might know that a co-employee was dealing in certain shares and assist him with the paperwork, place a few orders or contact some clients. I could therefore be associated intimately with his actions.

It could then turn out that my co-employee was breaching the rules. Because I was knowingly concerned in those actions, I might satisfy the requirement of subsection (2)(b) and face disciplinary action—even though at the time I did not know that the action concerned was a contravention of the rules. It should be made clear not only that I was knowingly concerned in the relevant activity, but that at the same time, I was aware that that activity was a contravention of the requirements imposed on the authorised person. That may be the intention behind the clause, but to clear up any ambiguity, it would be useful if the Minister could accept the amendment or could put on record beyond doubt the Government's interpretation of the provision, which could be used subsequently in court proceedings. However, that would be less satisfactory than making matters explicit in the Bill, which we always prefer; that is how legislation should proceed.

One way or another, any possible ambiguity should be avoided because of the seriousness of the clause. People's livelihoods are at stake. People will look closely at the provision and we must be sure that those who face disciplinary action do so not only because they did something wrong but because they knew that they were doing, or were associated with, something that breached the rules.

Much of what I shall say, especially on amendment No. 307, applies equally to several other aspects of the regulatory regime that will be established under the Bill, but I shall begin by making some more general points.

Clause 63 will give the FSA the power to discipline approved persons—mostly employees—whom the FSA has approved to perform controlled functions in accordance with the requirements in clause 56.

As the Committee will know, the FSA has been consulting on various aspects of the operation of the regime under part V. Final decisions on the functions that will be made controlled functions are not certain, although the FSA's proposed general approach is set out in consultation paper 26.

It is clear that many people subject to vetting under current statutory or self-regulatory arrangements will need approval, will need to comply with the statement of principles and will be capable of being subject to disciplinary action under the powers in the clause.

I turn now to the points raised in amendment No. 299 and the circumstances in which the FSA will be able to take disciplinary action. Subsection (1) will impose a two-stage test. First, the FSA may take action to discipline an approved person only where it believes that he is guilty of misconduct. Subsection (2) defines misconduct as being where the person fails to comply with the statement of principle or where he is knowingly concerned in a regulatory breach by the authorised person for whom he works. Secondly, the authority must be satisfied that it is appropriate in all the circumstances to take action against the approved person.

The term ``knowingly concerned'' already covers the thought that a person had to be aware of the nature of the conduct in which he was involved. If he had been ignorant of its true nature, he would not have been knowingly concerned in the contravention. That principle has been established in criminal law cases. Although we are not talking about a criminal offence, the same principles would apply to the interpretation of the clause. Of course, it is not enough for an approved person to claim not to know about FSA rules—ignorance of the law is no defence. However, we are not creating strict liability. I hope that that meets the concerns of the hon. Member for Chichester (Mr. Tyrie).

A further point of reassurance for members of the Committee should be the fact that the FSA may take disciplinary action only where it is appropriate in all the circumstances. It will have to consider that additional test and will take no action if action solely against the firm is sufficient in all the circumstances— for example, because the part played by the individual was small or peripheral and it was unnecessary to pursue him.

I shall quickly clarify a point raised by the hon. Member for Chichester on amendment No. 293 on Tuesday, which is relevant. As I said then, the FSA stated in consultation paper No. 26 that it did not propose to use the disciplinary powers provided for in clause 63 unless the misconduct resulted from deliberate actions by the individual concerned or his standard of behaviour was clearly below that which the FSA would reasonably expect of a person who performed that function.

The FSA has, therefore, prepared its draft code of conduct, which is set out in consultation paper No. 26, in a way that makes it clear that intent would be an important factor in reaching a view on whether discipline was appropriate.

I shall move on to amendment No. 307, which will at least satisfy the right hon. Member for Wells. I have serious difficulty with the suggestion that the FSA should be able to take action against approved people only when it is ``satisfied beyond reasonable doubt'' that they are guilty of misconduct. It is not helpful to speak of standards of proof when referring to a body exercising a statutory discretion. The point is that the FSA should exercise its powers rationally.

However, I agree entirely that the FSA should be required to be satisfied of misconduct before it takes action. We believe that nothing more is needed to achieve that result. Clause 63(1) uses the phrase

    ``if ... it appears to the Authority''.

I should perhaps explain that, as it is important to be clear about the effect of warning notice procedures set out in the Bill.

The FSA will need to reach a view about whether a person has been guilty of misconduct before it issues a warning notice. Before the warning notice is issued, there will almost always have been some informal exchanges on the matter with the person concerned. The FSA may well have undertaken an investigation that would have informed its view of the case. When the FSA issues a warning notice, it will have reached a view in the light of the information available to it. The purpose of issuing a warning notice is to mark the beginning of a formal administrative process. Generally, the FSA will have been engaged in informal discussions on the matter before that stage; the issuing of the notice crystallises the allegation of misconduct against the approved person.

Clause 338(1)(b) requires the FSA to explain its reasons for the proposed action. In cases of misconduct under clause 63, the requirement would include an explanation of why the FSA believed the approved person to be guilty of misconduct, and why the FSA considered it appropriate, given the circumstances, to take action against that person. The FSA must also make available to the approved person and to interested third parties any evidence then relevant to its beliefs. That marks the start of a formal administrative process.

Having been given a warning notice, a person accused of misconduct may make representations to the FSA on the matter. In the light of any representations made and any further information that may then become available, the FSA will reassess the case. If it continues to believe that there is a case, it will need to issue a decision notice. At that stage, the FSA must inform the approved person and any third parties mentioned in a prejudicial manner of their rights to refer the matter to the tribunal. However, even at that point, the FSA must act on the basis of its belief.

As my predecessor in this post, the current Minister for Small Business and E-Commerce, my hon. Friend the Member for Leicester, West (Ms Hewitt), explained before the summer recess, the FSA must, as an authority exercising statutory functions, have reached its view on a reasonable basis. That point need not be spelled out in the Bill. I expect that the extent to which evidence available to the FSA would stand up before the tribunal would be included in the authority's reasoning.

The word ``appears'' is intended to capture the points that I have made—namely, that the authority's decision to proceed with the formal procedures will turn on its reasonable belief in the prevailing circumstances. After those administrative stages, the approved person has the opportunity to refer the case to the tribunal under part VIII. The tribunal, in determining the case, will apply the appropriate standard of proof according to the nature, severity and circumstances of the case. In the light of my comments on that point especially, I hope that hon. Members will agree to withdraw the amendments.

Miss Johnson: I shall place one or two further points on the record in connection with the right hon. Gentleman's final remarks and the comments of the hon. Member for Chichester. Whether the right hon. Gentleman will still wish to press the amendment I cannot say, but perhaps I can help him a little further.

In some cases, there will be direct evidence that the person involved was aware of wrongdoing, such as his having taken steps to cover his tracks. In other cases, the FSA will have to consider whether it can be shown that the person involved must have been aware that the action in which he was involved was improper. In doing so, the FSA's judgment may turn on the extent of the connection between the person's functions and actions and the nature of the breach.

For example, a pensions salesman may have no reason to suppose that his actions in selling policies at a discount contributed to the firm's breach of solvency requirements: he might well have supposed that the firm had adequate margins to cover his activities. However, if he knew that his action seriously threatened the firm's solvency, it would be easier to show that he was knowingly involved in a breach. Equally, if an approved person performed tasks that were superficially routine, such as compiling customer profiles, without knowledge of how they might be used, it would be difficult for the firm to show that he was aware that he was involved in improper conduct. Ultimately—this is important—the tribunal will determine whether the necessary standard of proof that the person was knowingly involved in a contravention has been satisfied.

Therefore, there is ample judicial protection for approved persons against whom the action is taken.

Miss Melanie Johnson: I need to make some comments on this clause because of changes, so I rise to my feet to do so—no doubt to the discomfort of my Whip.

This part of the Bill carries forward the provisions of part IV of the Financial Services Act 1986, which implement our obligations under European Community legislation on official listing—the listing particulars directive, the admissions directive and the prospectus directive.

The effect of this European legislation is that the admission of securities to official listing on a stock exchange in a member state is subject to several detailed controls. For example, the directives set out a range of detailed requirements for the information to be contained in listing particulars or a prospectus. They require information about the securities themselves—for example, the extent of voting rights or entitlement to share in profit—and about the issuer, its capital, activities and financial position.

The directives also impose several continuing obligations that issuers whose securities are admitted to official listing must meet.

The purpose of the legislation is to protect investors and to improve the working of the single market by harmonising the arrangements for the listing of securities in member states.

Whereas there is no obligation on issuers of securities to apply for listing, investors know that issuers of securities that are admitted to the official list of any member state are obliged to provide certain information and fulfil certain conditions if their securities are to remain on the official list. Investors can therefore have confidence that they will have access to information that is sufficient to enable them to make informed decisions. That is, of course, one of the main planks of investor protection.

We have largely carried forward the provisions of part IV of the Financial Services Act, but we have made several improvements. We have introduced a new power, as provided for in schedule 7 to which the clause refers, to allow the Treasury to transfer the functions of the competent authority to another body in certain circumstances.

A second change is the introduction of a new penalty power, which will allow the competent authority to impose a penalty on issuers who breach the listing rules. This power also extends to directors and shadow directors and to ex-directors and ex-shadow directors who are knowingly involved in a breach. Up to now the only sanctions available to the competent authority have been private or public censure or suspension or cancelling of listing. The new penalty power increases the range of options available to the competent authority and will help make sure that effective and proportionate action can be taken where listing rules have been breached.

The third change, which is more technical, is that in future all securities admitted to the official list will be admitted under the statutory provisions. At present the stock exchange admits some securities, such as gilts, under contract. This will simplify the arrangements and put all issues on the same footing.

The admissions directive, as hon. The Bill currently names the London stock exchange as the United Kingdom's competent authority. I come to the meat of the clause and why I need to put these matters on record this morning. It sets out the statutory basis for the making of listing rules which govern admission to the official list and on-going obligations.

The decision to continue to name the London stock exchange as competent authority was taken by the Treasury in February 1998. The London stock exchange is, of course, currently the competent authority and has been since the concept was first introduced into UK law in 1984.

Our reasons for continuing with the current arrangements were simple. They work. As the Treasury made clear when announcing the continuation of the current arrangements, the London stock exchange enjoys a substantial reputation throughout the world and it operates satisfactorily in creating and enforcing listing rules in a way which gives confidence to investors.

However, recent events have led us to reconsider the position. As hon. Members will know, on 31 July the stock exchange announced its proposal to demutualise.

While it remains the case that we are satisfied with the way in which the stock exchange has been and is carrying out the function, in the light of that decision, it is no longer appropriate for it to continue to exercise the competent authority function. Indeed, the stock exchange itself suggested that this would be inappropriate.

A demutualised stock exchange will be a for-profit organisation. We consider that it would create the possibility of conflicts of interest for the statutory functions of the competent authority to be exercised by a commercial for-profit company.

This is no slur on the stock exchange. Far from it. It is simply the right response to the change in circumstances.

We have, therefore, decided that the competent authority function should be transferred to the Financial Services Authority.

The Treasury announced this decision in early October, and said that we intended to bring forward the necessary amendments to the Bill to effect this transfer of responsibilities.

On the assumption that the Bill is enacted in the Spring of next year, the transfer will take place as soon as possible thereafter. The stock exchange will continue to exercise the function in the meantime. We will be working with the FSA and the stock exchange to ensure a smooth transition.

We are confident that the FSA will do a good job and will continue to carry out the function efficiently and effectively, meeting the needs of issuers and investors alike.

Hon. Members will appreciate that we are not in a position at present to make the substantive amendments to the Bill to cater for the transfer of function. There is more to do than simply cross out the words ``stock exchange'' and insert ``Financial Services Authority'' in clause 69(1).

I now want to say a little bit about the approach that we will be taking. The competent authority function, as I hope is clear from my opening remarks, is different from the FSA's general functions as a financial services regulator. This has been at the centre of our thinking in considering what changes are necessary to the Bill and it is worth expanding on.

First, although there is some overlap, the constituencies covered are different. The competent authority deals with all companies with an official listing—from banks to chemical companies to telecommunication companies—and not just with financial services firms.

Secondly, the duties of the competent authority are not as wide ranging. The competent authority is essentially concerned with ensuring that companies provide the necessary information for the market, with the bulk of the requirements laid down in the directives. It is not concerned with the conduct of an issuer's business in the way that the FSA is concerned with the conduct of authorised firms' dealings with their customers and each other.

It is not therefore a function which necessarily has to be carried out by a financial services regulator.

However, there are good reasons for transferring it to the FSA. Having the financial services regulator perform this job is in line with the practice in a number of countries including France, Germany, the US and Japan.

There are useful synergies. The FSA has a great deal of relevant knowledge and, through its oversight of exchanges, has a clear interest in the market in officially listed investments.

The FSA also has a strong interest in ensuring that the markets in officially listed investments are not abused and that investors in officially listed securities, which are of course investments, are protected. For example, if those with inside information make use of it before the rest of the market, or if information is given out in a misleading way, it can lead to losses for investors and damage confidence in the fairness of the market.

In policing the official listing requirements, the FSA will be in a good position to ensure that where the listing rules are not complied with and abuse occurs, it can take action, and it will be in a position to prevent as well as cure.

To summarise, there are strong synergies, but the function is a distinct one from the FSA's other functions. We believe, therefore, that we should not simply seek to align the provisions of part VI with the rest of the Bill without good reason.

As I have said, the stock exchange has done a good job as the competent authority, protecting the interests of investors while being sensitive to the needs of issuers and the importance of dynamic and efficient capital markets. We want the FSA to continue to operate in this way.

My starting point, therefore, is that since we were content with part VI when the stock exchange was named as competent authority, the transfer of the function does not mean that the current structure and arrangements have suddenly become inappropriate. We need to consider on a case-by-case basis whether the duties and powers which attach to the FSA generally should also attach to the FSA as competent authority. My conclusion is that we need to make some changes to part VI as a consequence of the transfer.

The Treasury is ultimately responsible for seeing that the competent authority gives proper effect to the directives, which are highly prescriptive. Therefore, we propose to retain the power to transfer the function to another body. Although I am fully confident that the FSA will do an excellent job, should circumstances change, we may want to transfer the function. It is appropriate to have that power, given the Treasury's slightly different relationship with the competent authority compared to that with the FSA generally.

The precise scope of the power for any future transfer will need to take account of the fact that the FSA is the competent authority. For example, we will no longer need the ground in paragraph 3 of schedule 7, which is appropriate only when an exchange is the competent authority. We also intend that the Treasury will continue to oversee the competent authority in the way that it does now, although that is not a matter for the Bill. The Treasury agrees the competent authority's objectives annually and holds regular meetings to assess progress.

There are general objectives, such as that of ensuring that the listing rules continue to meet UK obligations under EC law. There are also policy objectives: for example, one of this year's objectives is to prepare for consultation suggestions for change arising from the current market research into the views of smaller companies. There are also operational objectives, for example, on the timetable for processing applications for listing.

Given our intention that the competent authority function should be carried out in broadly the same way as it now is, and given the Treasury oversight role, we see no need to set out objectives for the FSA as competent authority in the Bill any more than we intended to do so if the stock exchange were to remain as competent authority. However, we intend to apply a number of principles to the competent authority, along the lines of those in clause 2(3). We intend to place a requirement on it to have regard to proportionality, competition considerations, the international position and the effective and economical use of resources.

We also think it appropriate to enhance the accountability arrangements for the competent authority. The FSA's annual report and annual general meeting will cover its functions as competent authority. We shall apply the Treasury's powers to commission enquiries into efficiency and economy to cover the exercise of this function. The complaints investigator will be able to look at complaints against the competent authority. We shall place the same requirement on the competent authority as on the FSA more generally to consult on rules and to produce cost-benefit analyses. Finally, we have already brought the competent authority within the scope of the power to commission independent statutory enquiries into the running of the regulatory system.

We, propose to put the competent authority's investigation powers on to a statutory footing. We think it right that those important powers are delimited in the primary legislation. Finally, we propose to make changes to clause 87, which requires the competent authority to make and maintain arrangements allowing someone to appeal against a financial penalty. Now that the function is to be transferred to the FSA, we think it right that, if a decision to impose a penalty is disputed, the route for challenging the decision should be by a reference to the tribunal established under the Bill. We shall introduce provisions to that effect.

I hope that, in the light of what I have said, Hon. Members will agree to accept for now the relevant clauses of part VI as they stand. We shall table amendments to part VI to take account of the transfer in due course.
