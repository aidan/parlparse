Mrs Caroline Spelman (Meriden): I did not get very far before lunch, so some of my arguments may be worth reiterating in case the minds of members of the Committee were elsewhere at the time. We welcome the establishment of the National Care Standards Commission. It will deal with the problem whereby several services are still unregulated. Currently, there is a split in the functions for inspecting services that are regulated among local authorities, health authorities and the Department of Health. Over the past 20 years, enormous changes have taken place in the way in which society is organised that put pressure on the services that the new commission would regulate. I refer, in particular, to the growth in demand for accommodation that reflects the changing social structure in which elderly relatives in their twilight years no longer live with their sons and daughters.

It is worth putting that point on the record because we are trying to regulate a dynamic situation to reflect changing structures in society. Some problems that have arisen, and some of the tragedies that have occurred, which we hope will be dealt with by the commission, have become more frequent because of family breakdowns, the increasing number of children in care and so on. It is against that changing framework that we must consider the very nature of the commission. Under subsection (2), the commission must, in the exercise of its functions, act

    in accordance with any directions given to it by-

and under-the Secretary of State.

It is because of how the clause is crafted that we seek to insert the word ``independent''. Stressing at the outset of the provision the importance of the commission being independent, would afford better protection. We are regulating against a background of failure to regulate correctly. Inspections have sometimes been carried out by the provider. The lack of such independent action has led to some well-publicised disasters in relation to children's service-hence our insistence on the need for independence. The commission should be an independent, corporate body to allow it to make objective and impartial decisions without political interference from the Government.

The matter brings us back to our debate on clause 1 about the need for a clear split between the provision and regulation of care. As the state will continue to be a significant provider of care through establishments that will be regulated by the commission, that body must have the complete freedom and integrity to act clear of direction by the Secretary of State. I readily anticipate that the Government will argue against the amendment, but I draw the Committee's attention to our debate at length of the degree of independence that the Food Standards Agency should have. The Food Standards Agency Bill has now been enacted, but there were some parallel arguments about the degree of public confidence in a body that was put in place to inspect and regulate. Public confidence is improved by the independence of such bodies and the independence of the Food Standards Agency was seen as important in the Government's attempt to restore public confidence in food safety. From the Government's perspective, an independent body has the added advantage of being easier to hold accountable under the inspection regime that has been put in place. The Government are therefore left at arm's length from that decision making.

We feel strongly that the National Care Standards Commission needs to be more independent than the clause provides. I ask the Minister to give serious consideration to the fact that the public will look to the body to make a better fist of dealing with the kind of situation, detailed in the Waterhouse report. They will want the new body, which we support, to have more teeth, and be quicker, more effective and more transparent in the way that it deals with poor provision, lack of provision or a misdemeanour in the provision of that service.
