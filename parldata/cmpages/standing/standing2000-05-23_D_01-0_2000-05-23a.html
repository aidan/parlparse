The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Charles Clarke): I beg to move,

    That during proceedings on the Criminal Justice (Mode of Trial) (No. 2) Bill, the Committee do meet on Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock and on Thursdays at half-past Nine o'clock and half-past Two o'clock.

I welcome you, Mr. Maxton, and your colleague, Mr. Sayeed, to the Chair. I am confident that under your guidance the Committee will be able to make rapid progress with full and proper discussion of the issues.

The Bill is an important part of the Government's programme to modernise the criminal justice system so that it is fair to defendants but also fair to victims of crime. The ability to elect jury trial has been used by many defendants in criminal cases to bring about delay in cases that were more suitable for hearing in a magistrates court. The Bill will benefit victims and the criminal justice system generally by ensuring that cases are not delayed and that the time of the Crown courts is not wasted.

I am aware that some members of the Committee are concerned about the effects of the Bill on the principle of trial by jury. Those arguments were put fully on Second Reading and I am sure that they will be reflected in the Committee's debates. However, I ask members of the Committee to give the Bill proper consideration. We listened to the concerns voiced about the original Bill in another place, and made changes. We responded to the fears about the potential effects of the provisions relating to reputation and livelihood in the original Bill. Under this Bill, the courts will consider the circumstances of an offence, not the circumstances of the defendant.

We have also improved the safeguards for mode of trial. Magistrates will be required under the Bill to give reasons for their decisions. We also intend that appeals should be determined by a resident judge or his nominated deputy. That would ensure consistency and raise the standard of decision making in the courts.

Our aim is to modernise the criminal justice system. We believe that it must belong to the whole people, not be simply the property of lawyers. It is important to acknowledge that police, magistrates, victims and local communities will gain from the Bill—something that many have acknowledged as they have moved forward. Although there have been legal concerns, we are implementing the views of the Lord Chief Justice and the royal commission. Legal opinion is divided and I am aware that many legal organisations are concerned about what we have to say. However, we must not forget that our duty is to build a criminal justice system in which the whole community can have full confidence.

The sittings motion proposes that we meet on Tuesdays and Thursdays in the morning and afternoon. We are demonstrating our determination that the Committee will have a proper opportunity to examine the Bill fully and in detail. I hope that the motion finds favour with members of the Committee.

The first and most fundamental issue is the liberty of the subject. The Bill places under attack the ancient freedom of the people of England and Wales. It is not just me saying that; history produces loud and clear warnings on that point. I wholly agree with what the Minister said earlier, and ask him to focus constantly on his words. He rightly said that justice in this country belongs to the whole people, not just the lawyers. That theme is central to my argument, to that which Lord Devlin put so eloquently in the famous book of his Hamlyn lectures on trial by jury, and to that made in the 1760s by one of the greatest legal commentators, Sir William Blackstone. It goes back to the Magna Carta in 1215, which is the foundation of many of our liberties.

I have taken the trouble to read a great deal around the Bill. It has frequently been said that the idea that the right to trial by jury is ancient is mistaken and that it actually developed from a compromise in 1855. Indeed, the Home Secretary says that today in The Times. The article is on page 3 in the law section, with an attractive photograph of the Home Secretary peeping around a door. He says that the right to trial by jury dates from a compromise reached in 1855. That is not correct. In 1855, the right to trial by jury—a right that no citizen could abrogate or deny—was modified to the extent that the citizen was, in cases of either-way offences, given the right to choose trial by jury.

Modern jury trial developed slowly out of the Magna Carta, and was beginning to develop well by the 14th century. It continued to develop, perhaps crucially, through the 16th and 17th centuries and into the 18th century at the time of Sir William Blackstone. Until 1855, trial by jury was for all serious crimes. Of course, crime has changed in modern times, but that would cover the vast majority of what are currently known as either-way offences. There was an absolute right to trial by jury. Indeed, a citizen had no choice: the only method of trial was trial by jury.

If the Minister thinks that I am wrong, he will correct me in due course. I am willing to learn, but I am pretty confident that I am right, because I have taken a lot of trouble to research the subject. Until 1855, there was an absolute right. All that changed then was that the citizen had a choice and an opportunity to say: ``No, I don't want this case to go to trial by jury and require a judge and an assize''—oyer and terminer as it was called—``I am happy for the magistrates to deal with this matter.''

Mr. Hawkins: I am grateful to my right hon. and learned Friend for giving way, because what he is saying is extremely valuable and important.
