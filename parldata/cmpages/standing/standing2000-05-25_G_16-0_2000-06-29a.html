Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss Government amendments Nos. 326 to 337.

I must inform hon. Members that had it not been for the fact that the amendments were selected with the stand part debate, we would probably not have had a stand part debate because of the amount of time that we have already spent debating amendments to the clause.

Mr. Philip Hammond (Runnymede and Weybridge): I understand your comment, Mr. Illsley, that we have spent a long time on clause 68, but it is an unprecedentedly long and complex clause. We have considered a large number of amendments to it, including Government amendments. When the Government find it necessary to table 50 amendments to one clause, it suggests that the Bill has not been as well thought out and honed as it might have been. Despite the fact that we have debated the specific points raised by those amendments, a case can be made for going back to consider the overall effect of the clause and questioning the Minister about it.

I find that approach to legislation somewhat baffling. Subsection (1) states:

    After Part X of the 1989 there shall be inserted.

That is followed by 14 pages of insertion; the last provision, subsection (5), states:

    Part X of the 1989 Act shall cease to extend to England and Wales.

Although that technically achieves the objective, it seems an extraordinarily cumbersome and tortuous way of legislating. However, we must consider the substance of new part XA of the 1989 Act.

The essence of our concerns revolves around the question of proportionality. Everyone is aware of the terrible cases of child abuse uncovered and described in graphic detail in the Utting and Waterhouse reports-inquiries that were set up by the previous Administration and reported to the present Government. I asked the Minister several times what evidence supports the child minding and day care regimes that the Government propose in clause 68.

It is clear from previous debates that the Government have had to wrestle with some difficult questions in deciding how far to carry the logic of their approach to the regulation of child care. I would like to be persuaded that the Government have acted rationally, on the basis of evidence that there is a serious risk, and that their proposals are proportionate to the danger that they have identified. Further, I would like to be persuaded that when the Government's approach is limited or has created a watershed-whether by age, by category of child or by category of minder-it should be logical and not based on expediency and convenience.

I have asked the Minister several times for such reassurance. She has said that she would write to me. Our previous sitting was before the weekend-[ Interruption.]-sorry, it was on Tuesday; time flies when we enjoy ourselves. I would be grateful if the Minister wrote not only to me but to all members of the Committee, so that the information could be shared. I am likely to receive the letter after the Committee has finished deliberating the Bill, and certainly after it has considered whether clause 68 should stand part. Members of all parties have expressed concerns and interest and have queried aspects of the way in which the clause has been put together. Many of those concerns have related to where the lines have been drawn and to where the watersheds have been created. If the Minister could adduce concrete evidence, many hon. Members might find their concerns allayed.

One area debated at length concerned the Government's intention to exclude baby sitters from the legislation. We understand the logic behind that position. In seeking to regulate child care, the Government do not want to go too far, finding themselves intervening in arrangements that are impossible to police effectively and looking unacceptably intrusive. The Minister, on reflection and having listened to the points made during debate, including those made by Government Members, might conclude that the proposed new section 79A(8) of the Children Act 1989 is inadequate. Although it excludes early-evening baby sitters, it will also exclude a category of people who, on any logic, could not be deemed appropriate for exclusion: those conducting normal child-minding activities, perhaps for people working shifts, between

6 pm and 2 am.

I understand that there is a technical difficulty and do not claim to have an answer for the Minister, but she has greater resources at her disposal than me. I hope that, in responding to the short debate, she will be able to tell the Committee that she recognises the difficulty identified regarding proposed new section 79A(8) and that she will consider further the way in which the Government can create a meaningful distinction between the informal baby sitter in the parental home in the early evening and the child minder who looks after children during the evening-perhaps for shift workers-and who is qualitatively no different from the child minder who looks after children during the day for someone who works from

9 am to 5 pm.

I turn to one or two general points that have already been touched on and which, having failed to amend to Bill, we must accept will continue as the Government have intended. I raised several concerns about the distinction between child minders who control the premises-those who look after children on their own domestic premises-and those who are merely employees. I am not convinced that the legislation makes that distinction effectively and thus does not risk injustice to those who are perfectly good, appropriate people to look after children. Because they do so not on their own but another's domestic premises they could lose their qualification to be registered-as a result of something outside their control. I hope that the Minister will consider that issue again. No issue of principle is at stake. The Minister clearly acknowledged that it would not be appropriate for individuals to be put at risk in the way that I have mentioned. Assurance is simply needed as to how the Bill would operate.

To digress a little, I asked on Tuesday about the tribunal that is mentioned in the proposed new section 79M of the Children Act. The Minister confirmed that it is the tribunal provided for in the Protection of Children Act 1999. I take it as read, therefore, that the Government will not bring the proposed new section into effect until the Protection of Children Act is fully operational and the tribunal is up and running.

I freely confess that I was confused about which tribunal the proposed new part referred, and, having had my attention drawn by the Minister to the relevant passages, I now understand that the tribunal created by the Protection of Children Act will be responsible for appeals provided for throughout the Bill. It seems odd, on the face of it, that an appeal against deregistration of a residential care home will be heard by such a tribunal. Do the Government intend it to become an all-purpose tribunal for social care issues? If so, where will the line be drawn between that and the tribunal that is created under health service legislation-if my memory is correct, under the Health Act 1999?

We have concerns, which have not been dealt with, about the role of the chief inspector in relation to training. I should like to think that the Government are creating an arm's-length inspectorate-a watchdog for standards. However, I believe that its role will be compromised, so as to undermine the inspectorate and parents' respect for it, by involvement in training provision. I hope that the Minister will think about whether she is comfortable with those proposals.

If the initial principle of the clause is accepted and the threatened mischief thought sufficient to justify the approach taken, we can concur with the Government on much of the detail. I have identified one or two aspects that still cause us serious concern, particularly with respect to fairness or equitableness of application. An important step is being taken in how we regulate and inspect establishments. For the first time, we are creating powers to enter not commercial or dedicated business premises, but people's dwelling houses. I am sure that the Minister will agree that we need to be absolutely certain that the powers are appropriate.

I make a plea for some evidence of work done by the Minister's Department that provides a basis for introducing such legislation; for some evidence of a risk of apparent mischief that justifies the creation of an enormous bureaucracy in response to it. As long as the Minister can show that the mischief has been properly evaluated and that the response to it is measured and appropriate, I am sure that the Committee will be reassured.
