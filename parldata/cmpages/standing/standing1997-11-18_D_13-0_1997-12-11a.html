Mr. Ross Cranston (Dudley, North): Can the right hon. Gentleman tell us of another Act where there had to be consultation before it came into force?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: Yes, such consultation occurs frequently, but the Bill is a rather good example. We referred to the consultation exercise in a previous debate, so the hon. Gentleman's observation was, as usual, extremely helpful.

It is particularly important in this case because of the radical changes being made to the Bank's responsibilities as against those of the Treasury. Indeed, as I mentioned, it is something of a swap. I hesitate to repeat myself, but if the Paymaster General was not listening I shall say again that the monetary policy responsibilities of the Treasury go to the Bank, and the banking supervision responsibilities of the Bank go to the Treasury. It is important that everyone is ready, that all the expertise has been redeployed and that systems are in place, and the Bank has an enormous responsibility to ensure that.

We know that tensions existed at an earlier stage about the arrangements. We therefore believe that it is appropriate that the Governor should be formally consulted as to whether he is ready. Otherwise, if something goes wrong, the Treasury will doubtless blame the Bank and the Bank will blame the Treasury--and the House of Commons will blame both. Explicit consultation before the Bill comes into effect would be highly beneficial.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): the real concern is that a similar problem arose in the United States over the thrift situation. New Clause

Resolution of disputes

    `.--On the passing of this Act there shall be established an office of Ombudsman to adjudicate independently on any disputes arising between an institution subject to regulation by the Authority under Part III of this Act and the Authority itself, without prejudice to any rights at law.'.--[Mr. Boswell.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Boswell: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

To borrow the analogy of my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory), but with suitable modifications, I think the fat lady is now about to sing because we have now come to the end of this process with only one clause on the amendment paper for consideration. I suspect that the reason why other new clauses were tabled but not called, although it is a matter for you, Mr. McWilliam, rather than me, is the strange nature of the legal proceedings.

We are effectively legislating for the Bank of England's regulatory powers over the banking system in a vacuum, which will be filled by the Financial Services Authority. That authority exists only in shadow form. I hasten to say again that that is not a criticism of those who have been selected for the important job of putting the authority together, either individually or collectively. I am sure that they will wish to do a good job. Indeed, the intention of the new clause is to strengthen their resolve and to foster and encourage their credibility with the financial services sector.

We are saying goodbye to the Bank of England's responsibilities in this area; they will go to a new authority, but the legislation setting up that authority does not yet exist. We are in both a valedictory and pre-legislative mode for the new FSA and some of our debates, which, in his more generous moments the Paymaster General might have acknowledged, have been about necessary safeguards that we need in the new FSA in the interests of building up a new relationship of confidence in the financial services sector, which we all agree is important.

Some of the points--minor and major--that we have raised have been about that, and we have had useful dialogues not only with Ministers but with the hon. Member for Dudley, North (Mr. Cranston), who is an expert in banking finance. If we are wise--Ministers and Opposition Members alike--we should treat it as a voyage of exploration on which we shall not reach the final answers, although we shall try to approximate to the ideal. It is in that spirit that we move the new clause. As the summation of what we have been saying about the whole process, I hope that whatever response--preferably friendly--the Government give to it they will ensure that the new shadow authority adopts those practices in shadow, as it begins to regulate the banking sector, and that those points are in due course reflected in the legislation setting up the FSA. One could say that it is a kind of first reading of a Second Reading debate that will no doubt take place in due course. The clause should be seen alongside a number of new clauses that were not selected for debate, I suspect because they were not tied to the Bill, including the one on matters to be covered on Report about the FSA, which makes a number of important points.

Mr. Cranston: Earlier today, I was very sympathetic to one of the hon. Gentleman's proposed amendments on publication of the memorandums of understanding, as I have had difficulty getting access to those in the past. Is there any precedent for that? Ombudsmen deal with the public's complaints about institutions: the banking ombudsman takes the public's complaints about banks; and the investment ombudsman deals with complaints about investors. But the mechanism that we are discussing is extraordinary. It purports to deal with the relationship between the Bank and the regulator and there is no precedent for that.

 

Mr. Boswell: I should have thought that coming from the supposedly radical party--although these days some of us have our doubts about that--the hon. Gentleman would not have felt that the argument that there was no precedent was in itself a sufficient reason for not taking action. In constructing the argument--which the hon. Gentleman will appreciate I have not yet done--I wish to say that there is a need for at least some procedure to run by those issues. I have no definite blueprint in mind, which is why the new clause is drafted rather generally, but, given the need, the point remains that Ministers should think about it. One or two of my hon. Friends may wish to make speeches; I am sure they would also wish to weigh their remarks and come out with what I called an approximation to the right solution.

I was saying that the contacts that I have had over recent months and during the passage of the Bill with representative institutions--they are responsible people and not ones who let their consciences out on the street--have shown that the financial services industry is concerned that the setting up of the new authority should be done with maximum consultation and not lip service, that its constitution of the new authority in its operation should be as transparent as possible--

Mr. Geoffrey Robinson indicated assent.

Mr. Boswell: I note the Paymaster General nodding at the principle, if not the detail. We heard in earlier debates about the importance of minimising charges and compliance costs and we also debated the importance of maintaining the international competitiveness of the financial services sector. I would be sorry not to refer to my hon. Friends' interest in the accelerated transfer of regulatory functions. We had the banking functions that are now going; one or two of the self-regulators may wish to move that on. My hon. Friend the Member for Guildford (Mr. St. Aubyn), who knows a lot about that, may want to make a contribution on the matter. It is important that when they are ready, they should be prepared to move over; if they are going to move over, they need to do so to something worth having.

The Paymaster General, perceptively as ever, drew a distinction--one that I made in a different context--between the customer end and the institutional end of banking. The distinction that I made, in Committee, was a slightly wider one: the difference between wholesale and retail. If one takes the whole piece, from international bankers or investment bankers in London at one end, there solely to lend to other institutions, through retail, clearing or even merchant bankers dealing with smaller institutions in the United Kingdom or venture capitalists in the middle, all the way down to people whose work is largely with the retail sector, that involves a huge range of people.

The purpose of the new clause, well-headed after its entertaining misprint in its first, blue paper print, is the resolution of disputes. We want a sensible way of saying that there is a result. Whether that is done by an ombudsman, it is important that the Government should show a commitment to thinking about the right system. I would immediately concede to the Paymaster General that one does not need exactly the same system for different types of case: Mrs. Snooks, for example, or Aunt Agatha, with a small deposit account for which the pass-book somehow got lost in 1937, or a dormant account, which might be even more contentious, are not quite the same as an international name who happens to have a spat with the Governor about some aspect of what he has been asked to do. That may indeed qualify the Government's response. We need to set down guidelines for a procedure for resolving such issues at the appropriate level and a commitment by the Government to do that.

I should like to touch on another point briefly, about the final phrase of the new clause, which I added myself to my no doubt imperfect drafting. Although it would be unworthy to suggest that the hon. Member for Dudley, North had an interest in the matter as a lawyer, he may wish to comment. I said that it should be without prejudice to any rights of law because the institutions, particulary at the professional end of the job, whose staff will read what will doubless become the bank of England Act, will have the staff, time and means of redress to deal with that.

I have muttered the words ``judicial review'' in Committee. There may well be cases where people will take the Government to law. I am not fishing for trouble and I hope that that does not happen, but if they did, institutions would no doubt have the means to deal with it. We are not anxious to take that away. However, there may be a run of relatively unsophisticated modestly-sized institutions, such as some of the smaller, local mutual building societies, which are still around, which may wish to get themselves into a dispute. There may be cases where the full might of the law could be invoked against the Bank for the conduct of its activities or for example, administrative procedures where, there has been unreasonable delay and they might not know where to turn.

A further extension to that is the retail end of it, where different customers are concerned, and here there may be a different perspective because some of the institutions involved will also want clarity in the way that the FSA will handle its new disputes procedure, whatever that is, so that it does not feel disadvantaged by having some opaque or anomalous system that applies to one fund, but not another.

The new clause is put in at the tail end of the Bill to give us an opportunity to discuss an important matter. The matter is already substantive, because the Bill will transfer banking supervision, and it will become even more important as the complexity of the FSA rolls out and is eventually embodied in new legislation.

The new clause was not tabled in a spirit of contumely or dissent. Despite the Paymaster General's slightly dismissive remarks to my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells, Conservative members of the Committee have sought to engage in the issues and to ensure, as is our job, that Ministers engage in them too.

As the fat lady quits the stage, the new clause is the final marker, enabling us to say that the Bill is extremely complex and, as yet, incomplete. It will inevitably involve some disputes and disagreements, although I hope that, through our efforts, there will be as few as possible. It will create a new structure, which will need a means of resolving disputes as effectively as possible. In that spirit, I commend the new clause to the Committee.

I am concerned that they have done so when there are many changes in the banking industry.

Some Labour Members realise that time scales have changed. We live in a world where a business that started from virtually nothing can be worth more than £100 million in a few years, but we also live in a world where a bank worth £800 million or more can be reduced to next to nothing in a few weeks. That is the scale of change that has taken place in the banking industry.

Although we have grown used to fluctuations in the markets, and the markets have prepared themselves for various trading conditions, there will always be freak circumstances and abnormal trading conditions. In today's world, banks are involved in trading shares, futures and other derivatives, and in dealing with foreign exchange, and are increasingly reliant for income on their success in that trading environment, rather than from old-fashioned credit analysis and lending on the strength of their capital. It is conceivable--I hope that it will not happen--that the wheels might start to come off.

It is 10 years since we last saw truly abnormal conditions in the stock market, and it is more than five years since we saw abnormal conditions in the foreign exchange market. There has been a remarkable period of stability. The Opposition do not presume that those conditions will continue. Indeed, our deep concern is that, in their handling of the transitional period, the Government will not have equipped the regulator with the means required to cope with such conditions.

The role of an ombudsman in such a situation might be to find a means of accelerating the merger of the old regulatory organisations into the new authority. Those who run the old SROs may welcome an acceleration of their integration into the FSA. Indeed, under the 1986 Act, and without any new legislation, it will be possible for the SROs voluntarily to give up that function if they deemed themselves incompetent. It is a tough test to ask SROs, who regard themselves as doing a pretty good job in the London market, to stand up and say, ``Because we want to accelerate the process, we have to tell you that we are incompetent''. There is a way round the problem, which the Government should consider. Before the 18 months is up, the realisation will grow in the FSA that an opportunity has been missed to provide for an accelerated procedure.

Friend have been acute and to the point. In the spirit of Christmas, I thank the Government for answering at least some of our questions. In the cases when they did not do so, we shall ask them again on Report and on Third Reading.

The Chief Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Alistair Darling): I join the shadow Chief Secretary, the right hon. Member for Wells, in thanking you, Mr. McWilliam, and your co-Chairmen, Mr. Butterfill and Mrs. Dunwoody, for chairing the proceedings. I also thank Hansard, who sometimes have a difficult task in following what hon. Members say. Hon. Members, especially new hon. Members, will find that what is written in Hansard often reads much better than the speech sounded when it was delivered.

For my own part, I thank the Economic Secretary and the Paymaster General for taking the Bill through its Committee stage. After the first week, pressing duties detained me elsewhere, but I followed the proceedings and know that my ministerial colleagues guided the Bill very well.

I also thank my hon. Friends who have sat on the Committee. Sitting on the Government Benches can sometimes be less exciting than sitting on the Opposition Benches, but, as all my hon. Friends assured me that they completely supported every line and word of the Bill, they obviously felt that silence was justified. They have put up with the long, sometimes interesting--sometimes not so interesting --contributions from Opposition members of the Committee.

I thank the Clerks, particularly parliamentary counsel. As far as the Treasury is concerned, the Bill was conceived on 2 May; to bring it to the end of the Committee stage in such a short period is no mean feat, as I know the shadow Chief Secretary, the right hon. Member for Wells, will appreciate, given that he was a Minister in the Conservative Government. The fact that the Bill is largely intact--although the Government have tabled a few minor amendments--is a great tribute to parliamentary counsel and the Treasury officials who worked long and hard on it.

The Opposition have acknowledged the Bill's importance. It is the first major reform of the Bank of England since 1946--that reform was introduced by the then Labour Government, of course. Not only does it give operational independence to the Bank to achieve the Government's inflation target, it paves the way for the first stage of what will be a major regulatory reform. As the Committee knows, the consultative Bill--the new financial services Bill--will be available next summer, and I dare say that many of my hon. Friends will even now be signing up to sit on the Committee that considers that Bill.

My only regret is that we have sat through Second Reading and the Committee's proceedings but still do not know whether the Opposition are for or against the Bill--perhaps we shall find out on Report or on Third Reading. The Committee's proceedings have been completed with great patience by you, Mr. McWilliam, and your co-Chairmen, and we are all grateful for that.

The Chairman: On behalf of my colleagues, the hon. Member for Bournemouth, West (Mr. Butterfill) and my hon. Friend the Member for Crewe and Nantwich (Mrs. Dunwoody), I thank the right hon. Member for Wells and my right hon. Friend the Member for Edinburgh, Central (Mr. Darling) for their kind remarks. Let me take this opportunity to thank the staff of the Serjeant at Arms, the police, my Clerk in particular, the Hansard Committee Sub-Editors and also the Hansard transcribers, whom none of us see but who are beavering away translating our gibberish into plain English or sometimes plain Latin--or even French.

I also thank all members of the Committee for being amazingly helpful and co-operative. There are many new Members of Parliament on the Committee and Committee procedure is not easy for those coming in from outside the House, as it is in some respects arcane. Our consideration of the Bill has been unique in that I have not been accused of anything particularly horrid, except by the Economic Secretary, who almost accused me of having a mid-life crisis. As she and I have been friends for more years than a gentleman would mention, I forgive her for that--she may well have been right. Thank you all very much indeed.
