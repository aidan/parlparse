Clause 1

Offences

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider new clause 3 Prohibition of hunting, &c.

    ". (1) A person commits an offence if he hunts a wild mammal with a dog.

    (2) An owner or occupier of land commits an offence if he permits another person to enter or use that land to hunt in contravention of subsection (1).

    (3) The owner or keeper of a dog commits an offence if he permits another person to use the dog to hunt in contravention of subsection (1).

    (4) It is an offence to own or keep two or more dogs for the purposes of their being used to hunt in contravention of subsection (1).

    (5) In this Act a reference to hunting a wild mammal includes a reference to searching for or coursing a wild mammal.".

And the following amendments thereto:

(a), in line 1, leave out "hunts" and insert

    "intentionally causes a dog to chase, pursue, harry, bait, attack or injure".

(b), in line 9, at end add

    "(6) A person commits an offence if for the purpose of hunting a wild mammal with a dog he obstructs an entrance to or exit from a structure or place used or likely to be used for shelter or protection by a wild mammal.".

Mr. Michael J. Foster (Worcester): With the introduction of new clause 3, we would not want clause 1 to stand part of the Bill. I shall point out the advantages of the new clause suggested by the Home Office.

New clause 3 is undoubtedly a simplification of clause 1. The purpose of the new clause is to point out clearly that the offence considered by the Bill is hunting. Hunting is a deliberate act and parliamentary counsel has made it clear that hunting cannot take place inadvertently. Reference to intention is therefore unnecessary; people do not accidentally hunt, they have the purpose of hunting in mind.

We must define clearly what hunting is so that people never forget the purpose of the Bill. Examples have[Mr. Michael J. Foster] been given at length in this Committee in the past few weeks; a fox is searched for and then chased to a point at which it either escapes or becomes exhausted and the hounds catch it. That is hunting; it will be stopped under the new clause. A fox escaping and seeking refuge possibly underground and terrier dogs being sent down to try to force the fox out also counts as hunting and will be stopped under this new clause.

It is important to state clearly that the new clause encompasses all such activities, including stag hunting. In the case of stags or deer, the dogs do not kill them but chase them to the point at which they are shot. That is also hunting, and will be banned under this new clause. Hare hunting and coursing will also be treated in the same way. I will deal with hare coursing later.

If I may remind members of the Committee, the former Attorney-General, the right hon. and learned Member for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir N. Lyell), made it clear during the debate on 28 November that in his opinion people could be found liable if they let their dogs off the lead and they chased a rabbit, vole or mouse. That is not hunting and it has never been the express desire of the Committee to include that in a definition of hunting. Parliamentary counsel acknowledge that that is not hunting.

People who walk their dogs in parks and fields will still be able to do so without fear of the consequences of being found guilty of hunting. I am glad that that can be put on the record once and for all, so that absolutely no doubt will exist in the minds of the British people about the Bill's intentions.

Subsection (1) of the new clause points out plainly and bluntly the offence that is being considered, which is hunting a wild mammal with a dog. Subsections (2) and (3) describe in more detail offences of a related nature, such as when an owner or occupier of land permits hunting to take place. Let us remember: hunting is an intentional act it is deliberate. Subsection (3) says that an owner or keeper of a dog is liable

    "if he permits another person to use the dog to hunt".

New clause 3 encompasses and simplifies the merits of original clause 1. Subsection (4) makes it

    "an offence to own or keep two or more dogs for the purposes of their being used to hunt".

It is appropriate to dispel some of the myths about what will happen to dogs involved in hunting as a result of the Bill. It has been claimed that 20,000 dogs will be put down. The Country Landowners Association claimed that 13,000 dogs will be destroyed, but 10,000 dogs a year are killed by hunts as a matter of routine.
