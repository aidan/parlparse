Mr. Charles Clarke: The purpose of the clause is to ensure that evidence of action taken or documents submitted for proceedings presented to the commission cannot be relied on in criminal proceedings involving proscription and related offences, except as part of the defence case. That is designed to cover the possibility, which the hon. Gentleman seems to acknowledge, that people might be deterred from bringing an appeal before the commission or from instituting proceedings under section 7 of the Human Rights Act 1998 because of the risk of prosecution for the proscription membership offence or another proscription-related offence. The clause does not provide general immunity, and it would be wrong to do so. If, for instance, it emerged during proceedings that an individual had been involved in a bombing attack or had incited an act of terrorism abroad, immunity in respect of criminal proceedings would not apply. That strikes the right balance.

Offences involving weapons, articles, information and so on are omitted from the immunity provided in the clause because they do not relate directly to proscription. The other offences in the other clauses that the hon. Gentleman read out relate directly to proscription. That is why we made that distinction. I hope that that is clear. On that basis, I hope that that the Committee will agree that clause 9 should stand part of the Bill.
