The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Angela Eagle): I hope that I shall be able to answer hon. Members' questions about clause 13.

The clause provides new terms of appointment for child support inspectors. The current scheme relies on an enormous amount of information being gathered to enable a maintenance assessment to be made. Where it has not been possible to obtain information from parents, a child support inspector may be sent out to gather the required information. The Bill will change the current system whereby inspectors are allowed to be appointed only on a case-by-case basis, rather than be generally available and sent off to deal with cases as they are required. That system makes it hard to maintain a dedicated group of inspectors who acquire expertise as well as training so as to get better at their jobs. It is a cumbersome part of the Child Support Act 1991 and has led to great difficulties in ensuring that inspectors can be successful.

An inspector may visit an employer's premises or the premises from which a non-resident parent conducts his business. He may also visit the premises of a person whom he has reasonable grounds for suspecting holds information about a non-resident parent acquired in the course of his own business, such as an accountant. He may question anyone he finds there and ask to see any documents. Obstructing an inspector carries a fine of up to £1,000.

The amendments would change the way in which inspectors are trained, the way in which their inspections are carried out and the basis on which they can take place. We want to make it easier and less cumbersome for the CSA to use its inspector service, and that is what clause 13 is intended to achieve.

I assure the hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar (Mr. Pickles) that no inspectors go out on behalf of the CSA without having had training.

Mr. Desmond Swayne (New Forest, West): Human rights training.

Angela Eagle: I shall deal with that in a moment. We do not send untrained people out to do potentially hazardous or difficult jobs. Clearly, inspecting is such a job. Inspectors receive full training. Currently, they are of at least executive officer grade and have a wealth of interviewing experience.

I shall give Opposition Members a quick flavour of the training that inspectors currently receive. They are trained in basic maintenance assessment calculation, which is clearly a complex issue requiring a fair degree of training, although it is hoped that less will be required once the Bill is on the statute book Inspectors are also trained in the use of lap top computers, which prove useful when they are inspecting. I doubt whether many members of the Committee require assertiveness training, but inspectors attend such courses before they begin working. On a more serious note, they receive potentially aggressive situation awareness training--expertise that we acquire, either informally or formally. They are also trained to deal with good cause situations when people may be upset and worried. They are fully trained to conduct interviews under caution, as per the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 and the Police (Scotland) Act 1967.

Mr. Pickles: What form does that training take? Is it a residential course that lasts for weeks, or a little chat over tea when they arrive?

Angela Eagle: Perhaps I should send the hon. Gentleman videos, so that he would have a better idea of what goes on. The current training programme is considered sufficient for part-time inspectors, but the additional powers needed to appoint full-time inspectors cast it in a different light. I can assure the Committee that, once the Bill is enacted, training requirements will be fully re-evaluated in the light of the evolving role of child support inspectors.

Amendment No. 222 would ensure that entry into the building that is to be inspected can be obtained only

    "in the presence of the occupier".

Inspectors do not possess the power to obtain entry by force, and access to carry out an inspection must be granted. Amendment No. 223 would change the basis on which an inspector could enter buildings and conduct inquiries. Under the clause, he will be allowed to carry out such inspections as he considers "appropriate". The measures in the clause and in the Bill in general form an overall picture. They strike a balance between the need to investigate and detect fraud and error, and the individual's right to privacy. It is important to look at that picture as a whole.

Angela Eagle: I hope that I shall be able to do so. The hon. Lady presumes that the existing law on testing is far more up to date than it is. The clause will bring that legislation up to date.

The clause makes changes to arrangements for court-ordered blood tests to determine parentage. This arrangement may seem odd, but clause 61 deals with how the courts, rather than the CSA, will determine parentage.

The present system, under which we still labour, was established by regulations made under the Family Law Reform Act 1969. I hinted in the debate on clause 12 that those arrangements were cumbersome and increasingly difficult to put into effect. That is because standards for testing and the arrangements for the supply of testing have changed so much since 1969. The Government are trying to introduce increased safeguards to ensure that testing is carried out properly and effectively, in line with newer methods of organisation.

The present law does not allow standards to be set for the laboratory conditions under which testers work. The Lord Chancellor can regulate individual testers, but not the laboratories in which they work. That arrangement causes problems, because quality standards do not apply to the laboratory in which the tests are carried out. I emphasise that there has not been a problem with the way in which individual testers do their job. There is no sign that they have fallen below standard. However, it is more appropriate these days to be able to licence a laboratory, rather than individual people, to carry out tests.

By moving to a system of accredited laboratories, the Lord Chancellor will be able to make regulations to prescribe conditions with which a body must comply to be eligible for accreditation, including ensuring that satisfactory laboratory conditions are in place, and to set minimum qualifications for individual testers.

I shall be brief. This is an opportunity to address the points that I made the last time I spoke. We must ensure that, when the process is phased in, priority is given to those who have paid nothing. The Government have made great play of those who have paid nothing, and, in the "hands across the Committee" spirit that we experienced earlier, I am trying to tempt the Under-Secretary to make the same concession. She may like to think about that and perhaps bring the matter back, or even agree with us.

Angela Eagle: The new clause seeks to influence the way in which the Child Support Agency will organise its resources on collection and enforcement activities. It seeks to require the agency to prioritise action against those non-resident parents who have made no child support maintenance payments--currently 30 per cent. of the case load--rather than the 23 per cent. who are partially compliant.

It is well known that the child maintenance formula is so complex that the agency has to devote 90 per cent. of its resources to collecting information and making assessments--only 10 per cent. is left for enforcing maintenance. In the new scheme, we will be tough on those who try to avoid their responsibilities. There will be greater emphasis on compliance and enforcement, including more efficient use of deduction from earnings orders. Our priority, in all cases, will be to get maintenance flowing quickly and regularly.

The new clause sends out the wrong message to non-resident parents. In effect, it says, "If you pay something, no matter how little, we will leave you alone." The agency will be able to spend more time concentrating on non-compliance, whether partial or total, once the changes to the system have made it more efficient in the calculation and enforcement of maintenance payments. All non-resident parents who owe child support maintenance will be asked to pay. If they do not, enforcement action will continue to be taken to make them pay.

The new clause is unnecessary. All non-resident parents who do not meet their responsibilities in full can expect to have enforcement action taken against them. We have no intention of introducing a legislative priority for collection and enforcement activities within the day-to-day running of the agency. I ask the Committee to reject the new clause.

Mr. Pickles: The Under-Secretary was careful to give no indication of what she intends to do. I think that she should have done so. Will the Government give priority to tackling the problem of people who have paid nothing? Will it be done on an alphabetical basis? The hon. Lady said that these are the people that she is after, and she has our full support in that crusade. She said that she did not want to put the new clause into the Bill. Her stock is very high on this side of the Committee, and if she says that she intends to sort the problem out, we shall be happy. If she will give us some indication of how she intends to approach this difficult task, we may well be satisfied.

Angela Eagle: We have great ambitions in this respect, which I hope are shared by all members of the Committee.

One of the aims of the Bill is to create a system that will allow quick, simple assessments so that maintenance can be put into effect quickly, and then regularised. That will give the CSA and its staff the time to concentrate on chasing those who have managed successfully to evade their responsibilities through non-compliance or partial compliance. The thrust of the changes that we have discussed today focuses on that aspect.

I am optimistic that, as a result of the changes, we shall be able to organise the CSA in such a way as to chase all non-compliant non-resident parents. That will be the CSA's task. It would not be helpful to prioritise who should be chased first, either in the Bill or in discussions, as that could be of help to those hoping to adopt a strategic approach to evading their responsibilities. When we have a more effective and efficient system, we will be chasing all non-resident parents who seek to evade their responsibilities. There will be no hiding place for them. Their liabilities and responsibilities will be much clearer, and it will be possible to work them out in advance by looking at a table. We expect to improve compliance considerably.

Mr. Pickles: I appreciate that the Under-Secretary has gone a little further. I put this point to her, although I do not require a response at this stage--no doubt we shall return to it on another occasion. I understand the worries about people taking a strategic view, and saying, "I'll pay a few bob and that'll get them off my back." That is not what this is about. It is about saying that there will be no hiding place: I approve of that phrase. There is a dividing line between a person who makes a strategic decision to try to get the CSA off his back and a person gets into a regular habit of making a contribution. The Under-Secretary has made frequent reference to such people over the past couple of weeks. She can do no wrong, as far as I am concerned--not only today, but well into the weekend, and perhaps a little beyond. I am a generous person.
