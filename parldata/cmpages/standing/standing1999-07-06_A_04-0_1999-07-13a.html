Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): Unfortunately, I missed part of our debate on the important subject of the complaints procedure, but I have carefully read the Official Report. I am aware that the Economic Secretary has contributed to the debate, but before we hear from her again, I should like to summarise some of our objections to the Bill as drafted and to set out the reason for our amendments.

We have frequently observed that the Financial Services Authority represents a concentration of power over people's livelihoods, which may well be justified, but which needs to be offset by the ability of traders and practitioners to obtain redress when things go wrong. In any human institution, mistakes are made, and can often be made at a low level in the organisation--not under the direct or continuing control of senior managers. Such mistakes can then be compounded by obstinacy, so that traders, some of whom may be small and precariously balanced, may feel substantially disadvantaged. In a competitive market--which we are all endeavouring to create--traders of all sizes are vulnerable to the vagaries of the market. They accept that, but they cannot accept that they should also be at risk from the negligence, recklessness or oversight of the FSA, especially when they know that if it were they who made such mistakes, they would be penalised. They want the same discipline and the same rules to apply to the FSA. There should be a readily available mechanism for such mistakes to be corrected and for financial compensation to be paid in appropriate circumstances.

I should say a word or two about the importance of independence. The philosophy underlying the Bill is that no one should be regulator in his own interest. The market is not to be self-regulating; an independent body--the FSA--is being authorised by statute to regulate it. The FSA should therefore also be subject to an independent complaints authority or investigator. The Bill is deficient in that regard. Although it is true to say that when complaints are well-founded, they will end up in the hands of the investigator, it would help greatly if the investigator were more clearly seen to be independent, so that complaints would be diverted to his office automatically and he, rather than the FSA, would do the filtering. We, as Members of Parliament, all know that complaints may not be well-founded--they may be frivolous, vexatious or whatever--and we do a great deal of filtering before forwarding complaints to a Government Department, or, in some cases, the ombudsman. However, in this case, it would be better if the investigator were to perform that filtering to decide in the first instance whether a complaint merited investigation. The investigator should also be obliged to inform the complainant in due course whether action was to be taken.

I ask the Economic Secretary to reconsider whether improvements can be made to the Bill. Our amendments may be defective in a number of technical respects, but we are certain of the thrust behind them, which is that we need to bolster the perceived and actual independence of the investigator.

The FSA will operate against a background of a great deal of statutory immunity, as we shall discuss later in our proceedings. It is important to bear that in mind in the context of the amendments, because that statutory immunity could, in some cases, prevent traders and practitioners from seeking redress through the courts. That is a powerful reason for setting up an investigator who can award financial compensation.

In the Economic Secretary's response to amendment No. 9, she gave three reasons for rejecting the case for financial compensation. I am not especially convinced by any of them. Taken together, they certainly do not make the case for the status quo. First, the Economic Secretary said of possible powers to award compensation:

    "That is not a feature of existing legislation and we do not see a good case for changing that".

However, the Government are introducing many other provisions not in existing legislation. Indeed, the whole Bill is an exercise in change. We are here this morning precisely because the status quo and the existing provisions are inadequate. Therefore, an appeal to what is in existing legislation hardly constitutes an argument.

Secondly, the Economic Secretary said of the role of the investigator as a mechanism for investigating alleged FSA shortcomings that

    "It should not be a route to additional redress for aggrieved firms and consumers."--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 8 July 1999; c. 83.]

However, it would not be a question of additional redress, because there is no redress at all at present. We want to incorporate a provision for redress, which would not duplicate existing conditions. It is true that there will be an opportunity to appeal against enforcement decisions by going to a tribunal, and in those circumstances a complaints procedure might be seen as an additional form of redress.

However, we have been advised that cases will not always be referrable to a tribunal. Decisions about enforcement could be heard by a tribunal, but other matters to do with procedure and the way in which decisions are made, which might not be referrable, would still require a means of redress. I therefore reject the Economic Secretary's assertion that we are proposing an additional mechanism.

Thirdly, the Economic Secretary referred to compensation schemes and to the ombudsman, pointing out that they will make awards to consumers. However, we are dealing not with the question of consumers, but with companies and regulated individuals. It would not be appropriate, or possible, for them to be awarded compensation or redress under those schemes or through the ombudsman. I therefore reject the third of her reasons because it is inadequate.

I return to the point that cases of negligence, incompetence or oversight could be perpetrated by the FSA, which would have serious financial consequences for regulated firms. In such cases, there is a need for financial awards.

Of course, they should not be made automatically, but, when an investigator comes across a case of serious oversight, negligence or incompetence, he should be able to make a financial award against the FSA. Whether that award should be paid out of FSA funds or through the Treasury is a matter that we might debate elsewhere.

It is important to prevent the asymmetry whereby firms can be fined for behaviour which, if performed by the FSA, it could not be sued for or for which an investigator could not award compensation. That is wrong in principle. Our amendment would change that.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Ms Patricia Hewitt): This debate has been extremely interesting and important.

I shall begin by recapping and reminding Committee members of what the Government have already done to strengthen the investigation of complaints against the FSA and to put the complaints procedure on a statutory basis. Under insurance company arrangements, complaints may only be made against the regulator through Members of Parliament under the Parliamentary Commissioner Act 1967. Arrangements in schedule 1 represent a pretty significant step forward, as they strengthen the complaints system and ensure that authorised people who feel that the FSA has not treated them well have access to a proper complaints procedure.

We have responded to the concerns of the Joint Committee. For instance, it wanted to ensure that the investigator had a permanent position and a continuing role. We have done that. The Joint Committee was also anxious that the investigator should have adequate resources. We have done that, as paragraph 7(3) states that the investigator must be appointed on terms which, so far as possible,

    "are reasonably designed to secure ... that he will be free at all times to act independently of the Authority".

Of course, adequate funding will be a key component in ensuring the independence that the investigator needs to do the job properly.

Last week, I told Committee members that I would be prepared to consider further whether the Treasury's power to approve the appointment of the investigator should be exercisable instead by the Lord Chancellor's Department, should Opposition Members wish to press the matter. Last week, the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) said that who should appoint the investigator or approve his appointment is a fine matter. Like the hon. Gentleman, I hope that Opposition Members will accept that an argument can be made for both sides and that they will not press the matter to a vote. However, I shall certainly reflect on the issue.

Before turning to the other amendments, it may be helpful for Committee members if I draw a comparison with the existing position, which has just been mentioned by the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton). The Financial Services Act 1986, which--if I may say so--was passed by the previous Government, merely requires the FSA to ensure that there are effective arrangements for investigating complaints against recognised bodies underneath it, including self-regulatory organisations. In other words, under that Act, there are no requirements for the FSA to maintain arrangements for investigating complaints against itself. There are no provisions relating to the investigator's security in office, his powers to investigate complaints that the FSA has declined to investigate, consultation on the investigation arrangements, or the publication of reports concerning completed investigations.

The Banking Act 1987 has no provisions relating to complaints against the Bank of England. So we have progressed a long way from the existing arrangements under the current legislation. I hope that, despite their concerns, Opposition Members agree that we have significantly improved the position that exists under the current measure.

The arrangements in the schedule create a system of investigation for the FSA that is analogous to the ombudsman arrangements that apply to Government Departments and other public bodies--the issue raised by the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton). Members of the ombudsman scheme are expected to have satisfactory arrangements for dealing with complaints, which are to be examined externally only if the complaint is not satisfactorily resolved.

In complaints to the ombusdman regarding maladministration by Government Departments, cases can be referred only by Members of Parliament, which means in practice that the Government Department has already examined the complaint and failed to deal with it to the complainant's satisfaction. The same applies in cases to the local government ombudsman. As a constituency Member of Parliament, I know that my local authority has a procedure for dealing with complaints by dissatisfied constituents who, if they are still not happy with the outcome, will come to me and the matter will go to the local government ombudsman.

That is right and proper, as it is for the FSA to deal with its own complaints in the first instance. It would be wrong, however, if complainants were left at the mercy of the FSA and if it could dismiss their complaints, fail to investigate them adequately or refuse to investigate them at all. That will not be the case under the Bill. Schedule 1 already provides that the FSA must refer complaints to the independent investigator who will have the power to conduct a full investigation. That is made clear in paragraphs 8(2) and 8(4).

Under paragraph 8(3), the Bill requires the FSA to tell the investigator if it decides not to investigate the complaint. If the FSA decides that a complaint is manifestly ill-founded, it must nevertheless tell the investigator that it proposes to do nothing more, and the investigator can still proceed with an investigation. That is an important independent safeguard for the complainant. There is no possibility for the FSA to stop the investigator from dealing with a complaint against it if the investigator wants to proceed and the complaint falls within the terms of the scheme.

We have responded fully to the Joint Committee's suggestion that the investigator should be able to investigate cases directly, as well as those referred by the FSA itself. Furthermore, we have done that in a way that is similar to the ombudsman's arrangements, thus giving the FSA the initial responsibility as well as the opportunity to account for itself. I hope that that deals with the status of the investigator and his powers vis-a-vis the FSA. The second main point is covered by amendment No. 9, relating to the ability of the independent investigator to award financial compensation. I stress again how committed we are to ensuring that consumers and firms have a real voice in the new regulatory regime. Where appropriate, that voice is backed up by rights--to be consulted, to be listened to and, if something goes wrong, to complain.

We must also ensure that the regulatory framework allows the FSA to get on with its business effectively and efficiently without being hindered by uncertainty and the constant potential for complaints. Otherwise, the system becomes unworkable and everyone--consumers and practitioners as well as the FSA--could lose out as a result. Just as we are determined that the industry is not made uncompetitive by being tied up in red tape, so we have to ensure that the regulator is not tied up in red tape or hindered by a constant prospect of claims for damages and compensation.

At this stage I do not wish to anticipate, even in response to the right hon. Member for Wells and the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham, the forthcoming debate on statutory immunity. During that debate, we shall explain our belief--with which the Joint Committee agreed--that an appropriate degree of statutory immunity is essential.

The hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham said that the complaints system must be a counterpart to statutory immunity. That is right, but it must not be a contradiction to statutory immunity. Whether in relation to compensation for complaints or damages for negligence, the FSA needs to be protected from actions that could otherwise undermine its ability to regulate in an open and effective way. People may well be upset by being regulated and prefer to get on with their business wholly unhindered, but we cannot set up a system that encourages them to take constant pot-shots at the FSA. The FSA would constantly be looking over its shoulder, and would be subject to the threat of claims that could undermine its administration and operation.

As I have said, the complaints mechanism is designed to ensure the open and transparent investigation of any alleged shortcomings in the FSA's administration. It is not designed to provide additional recompense for firms and consumers. The tribunal will make provision for regulated persons, and the ombudsman and the compensation scheme will make provision for consumers.
