On a more mundane note, I turn to the amendment. It provides that plans should be reviewed each year. Under subsection (1), plans need to be kept under review. In response to the important question asked by the hon. Member for Poole about democratic accountability, the Bill provides for that sort of reassessment to be carried out within the individual local transport plan. If there were a change of political direction at local level, requiring a radical reassessment of the plan, there is scope to allow that. Having said that, we place great emphasis in the legislation, and in the guidance that we have circulated on provisional plans and that we shall circulate on local transport plans, on consultation, participation and consensus. That is the spirit in which we hope the plans will be developed.

Our approach places much greater emphasis on authorities developing robust monitoring of performance indicators and setting appropriate targets, so that we can measure progress. We expect to see that in the annual progress reports produced by local authorities.

Local transport plans are five-year plans. In July, authorities will submit plans that cover the financial years from 2001–02 to 2005–06, and on that basis we will allocate resources for 2001–02 and give an idea of how much authorities can expect for the rest of the plan period. Greater certainty about future funding for local authorities and, in turn, for their local partners, is a key benefit of the new system.

Authorities will need to produce annual progress reports setting out how previous financial allocations have been spent and reporting on progress on performance indicators. That will allow authorities to consider the need for a more fundamental review of the plan and to instigate a review, if necessary, so our proposals provide some insurance. If a plan is delivering, it does not make sense for authorities to go through the motions of a formal review. Nevertheless, they should keep their plans under review. The amendment would place an unnecessary and unhelpful restriction on the review process, but I emphasise that the possibility of review and re-„assessment, if it is required locally, is implicit in our proposals. It is best to retain the general requirement for plans to be kept under review, and I invite the hon. Gentleman to withdraw his amendment.

My experience tells me that there is a tendency for professionals to reinvent the wheel and to make guidance locally if they can do so. I am sure that many of my fellow professionals in that important area of British life will welcome the fact that the guidance informs them that there is no need to reinvent the wheel when consulting on and designing those important local transport plans.

When I first studied the Bill, it was clear that the clause did not focus on one of the important factors in delivering effective bus services for people, communities and bus operators. Paragraph 7 of the draft guidance on provisional transport plans states:

    ``In many places, the role of local authority since bus deregulation has been limited to filling the gaps in the market through supported services, and operating concessionary fares schemes.''

In my experience as the Member for Scarborough and Whitby—a rural constituency, which includes significant urban areas—the local authority does not always choose to consult bus operators when planning street furniture and traffic management schemes to pull together important parts of the infrastructure and transport policy in my part of the world. For whatever reason, it forgets that bus operators have to go through its patch.

As someone who has worked with many local authorities, I have great sympathy for bus operators, because I was involved in trying to deliver a transport service, too. The tendency for local authorities to reinvent the wheel and perhaps have slightly different policies and design criteria from those of a neighbouring authority means that bus operators and transport operators in general have to adjust and make changes as a particular route or strategy goes from one local authority area to another. Obviously, that problem is compounded in the greater metropolitan areas, which is why I have always been a strong supporter of passenger transport authorities and passenger transport executives. There are many parts of the country in which slight changes in design strategy and approach mean that bus operators fail to convey to local authorities their important contribution to the debate about delivering an effective transport strategy for the community.

Many members of the Committee will be aware that my constituency is the home of Plaxton's, one of the country's major bus manufacturers. About 40 per cent. of the new-build buses that come on to Britain's roads come from that factory. Plaxton's were at the forefront of developments in low-floor technology and the design criteria that allow disabled people, young mums and other people with mobility difficulties to get on to buses. Obviously, the bus industry and operators are keen to buy into the new technology as they replenish their bus fleets. However, that great professional expertise offered by bus operators is often not utilised or absorbed when local plans are being developed.

Imagine that a bus manufacturer sells £40 million-worth of new-build buses—I hope that Plaxton's would be the company fulfilling such an order—which then come on to the streets. It would be nonsense if, as a result of the planning system, the street furniture and the routes from homes, work places or businesses to bus stops were incompatible with the needs of people with impaired mobility. However, I am afraid that that often happens in Scarborough and Whitby. We have the new buses with low-floor technology, but there are real impediments to passengers making their way to the bus stops. Local bus operators tell me that if they had had a formal opportunity to advise the local authority on infrastructure planning for the bus system, that fragmentation, and therefore those impediments, would have been avoided.

The local authorities are missing a tremendous opportunity, but I understand why. They have all sorts of other things on their minds as they try to pull local transport plans and transportation policy together. However, the amendment would ensure that local authorities had greater regard for bus operators and could use the in-house expertise of operators to plan a more effective transport system for the communities that will benefit from the important proposals for bus strategies.
