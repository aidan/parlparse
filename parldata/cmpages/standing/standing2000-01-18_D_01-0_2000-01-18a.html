Mr. Hughes: The Government have been sensible in proposing a Bill that codifies anti-terrorism legislation and in proposing that it start with a definition of terrorism. It is sensible to know what we are dealing with before reaching the detail. Many of us have considered far too many Bills in which the definition section is a considerable way into the Bill. I make no criticism about either of those aspects.

As we start with clause 1, the interpretation clause, we immediately face a major debate about the Bill's parameters. That debate is about the definition of `terrorism'. The various measures placed on the statute book in recent years contain different definitions. Broadly speaking, two definitions are currently in use. Against the background of a Bill to place terrorism legislation on a permanent, rather than temporary, footing—a point to which I shall return—the Government propose a more broad definition.

The Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989 and the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 contain the same definition:

    `` `terrorism' means the use of violence for political ends, and includes any use of violence for the purpose of putting the public or any section of the public in fear''.

That definition is limited, in that the powers and offences apply only to terrorism connected with Northern Ireland and to international terrorism. The Government are introducing two changes. They are widening the definition and they are widening its remit—where the definition applies. I do not want to concentrate now on the second of those changes, but I shall return to it. It is a reasonable starting point to say that terrorism should be considered wherever it takes place. Like hon. Members who represent Northern Ireland constituencies, one of my complaints about previous legislation that the Conservative Government introduced and that, later, the Labour Opposition supported, was that it contained exclusion clauses and effectively treated different parts of the United Kingdom differently. As I said on Second Reading, I have never voted for that legislation. My party did on various occasions, but I never did. I voted against it on some occasions and abstained on others. One is either a citizen of the United Kingdom or one is not. I believe that one should be treated similarly wherever one is in the United Kingdom. The Government are getting rid of that distinction, which is welcome. We are now considering a geographical definition that relates to the four countries of the United Kingdom and an area that is not limited by national boundaries. The definition that I have just cited from the PTA was used in the EPA.

The other definition is the one that we have proposed in our amendment. It dates from only seven years ago, from the Reinsurance (Acts of Terrorism) Act 1993, which is used for determining compensation for acts of terrorism. The definition is not an invented alternative definition; it comes from UK statute.

We must ask ourselves how far we should go. I shall not repeat verbatim what I said on Second Reading on 14 December, starting in column 185. The Bill seeks to widen the definition of terrorism in two ways that are fundamentally unacceptable to those who want to ensure that legislation both deals with crime and protects individuals.

I declare an interest in that I am a human rights lawyer. I also have family who have served in Northern Ireland and friends who have been injured there. Both my parents were in the services and I hope that I understand the balance that is required between the need for a proper law and order structure to deal with terrorism and the need to protect civil liberties. Terrorism has traditionally been defined in ways that limit the activity. It is not violence against anybody but a specific activity that is usually directed at the Government. The ``New Oxford Dictionary'' definition is

    ``the use of violence and intimidation in the pursuit of political aims.''

The definition in ``Brewer's Politics'' states:

    ``The use of violence or intimidation to achieve political ends.''

All the books on politics describe and define terrorism. For example, the ``Oxford Companion to Politics of the World'' states:

    ``The concept of terrorism has been a category of political discourse since the late eighteenth century. Its central meaning is the use of terror for the furthering of political ends''.

That has been the traditional definition. We must, therefore, be extremely careful if the Government propose legislation that absolutely rejects that traditional ground and gives additional powers to the state to deal with wider issues.

The definition of ``terrorism'' in clause 1(1) is

    ``the use or threat, for the purpose of advancing a political, religious or ideological cause, of action which—

    (a) involves serious violence against any person or property,

    (b) endangers the life of any person, or

    (c) creates a serious risk to the health or safety of the public or a section of the public.''

It is notable that no one in the public domain is advising the Government to go as far as they propose in the Bill. Have the Government received submissions from reputable bodies in support of that definition? If not, why have they chosen a definition that is wider than anyone has previously recommended?

Lord Lloyd headed the inquiry that preceded the Bill. It is perfectly proper to proceed by asking someone to review what is needed. That is one of the things for which the Government are criticised. Generally I do not criticise them for trying to set up commissions and inquiries before introducing legislation. It is always best to take advice before legislating.

Lord Lloyd, having weighed the matter carefully, proposed the following definition of terrorism:

    ``the use of serious violence against persons or property, or the threat to use such violence, to intimidate or coerce a government, the public or any section of the public.''

He then gave the definition of objectives:

    ``in order to promote political, social or ideological objectives.''

That was the widest that any proposal to the Government went. The Government, in their consultation paper, proposed the following definition of terrorism:

    ``the use of serious violence against persons or property, or the threat to use such violence, to intimidate or coerce a government, the public, or any section of the public, for political, religious, or ideological ends''.

The fundamental debate is whether one should extend terrorist legislation to actions that are not aimed at the Government—which would go further than the traditional purpose of terrorist legislation—and whether one should add a list of political, religious or ideological causes to the definition of terrorism. The hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone mentioned the issue on Second Reading. Widening the definition is important because it would allow a huge range of actions to be included within it.

Under the definition of terrorism in the Bill, if someone makes a threat in pursuit of a fundamentalist religious belief and by his own definition—even though he may not be sane—his action is religiously driven, it can be construed as ideologically motivated. That is nothing to do with the state, in contrast to the situation in Ireland, for example, where there are opposing ideologies surrounding whether the six counties are the rightful property of the United Kingdom or of the Republic of Ireland. The view that everyone should be armed, or allowed to carry a gun, or allowed to use any drug without restriction, could be claimed to be an ideological view. There is no view that could not be claimed by someone to be an ideological view. To be blunt, every Member of Parliament receives regular communications from constituents with fairly far-out views. They see their views as ideologically based and justify them to themselves and others as arising from their profound, philosophical or religious understanding. We might say they are mad, or madder than we are, but they define themselves as ideologically driven.

We get into dangerous territory once we start pulling into the definition of terrorism any action carried out in the ideological pursuit of a cause. I gave the examples on Second Reading—examples that have been debated outside the House—of someone who opposes the killing of whales, which is an ideological cause, and of someone who opposes all animal experiments, which is also an ideological view because it is held on the grounds of animal welfare. All such views are swept in by the definition in the Bill. I find it surprising that the Government wish to include such views and I emphasise that to the Minister, who will remember that we are old university sparring partners. That is a little unfair because he was a greater political mortal and I a lesser one.
