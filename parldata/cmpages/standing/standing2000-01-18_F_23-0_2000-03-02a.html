The Chairman: With this we may take the following Government amendments: Nos. 357, 358, 360 to 362, Government new clauses 31 to 33 and Government new schedule 2.

Angela Eagle: New clause 31 and new schedule 2 would align the arrangements for local authority decision-taking on housing benefit and council tax benefit claims with those in other social security benefits. They would also provide the recipients of those benefits with rights of appeal to an independent appeals tribunal if they were unhappy with a decision taken on their benefit claim. We propose to introduce the changes from April 2001.

The measure is an important part of our aim to modernise delivery of social security benefit. Although administered by local authorities, housing benefit and council tax benefit are similar in many ways to income support and jobseeker's allowance. About two thirds of people getting help with rent or council tax by means of these benefits also receive income support and jobseeker's allowance. At present, the arrangements for challenging local authority decisions on housing benefit and council tax benefit claims are different from those for all other benefits. Harmonising the arrangements would therefore be sensible.

We have successfully completed a phased introduction of new decision-taking in appeals procedures across the rest of social security benefits. The provisions would simply extend them to council tax and housing tax benefit. Taken together, the proposals will provide clear and consistent rights across the benefits system, with easy access to the appeals procedure and at the same time streamline decision-making services to people who apply for, or receive, benefits.

Amendment No. 358 and new clauses 32 and 33 are consequential on the amendments which provide for a new appeals structure for housing benefit and council tax benefit decisions. Those benefits are determined in accordance with the legislative framework which sets out the rules of entitlement.

The amendments enable local authorities to provide additional financial help to recipients of those benefits. They give local authorities discretion to consider making additional payments. The amendments enable a flexible arrangement but provide the framework within which local authorities should operate when considering whether, to whom and for how long to make discretionary payments, and the level of such payments. As they are close to their clients and aware of local housing conditions, local authorities are best placed to judge the circumstances of a request for further financial help. They are also in the best position to offer advice on rents and the availability of local accommodation.

The clauses provide a framework within which local authorities should consider these discretionary payments. For example, to ensure that local authorities act prudently in exercising their discretion, a financial limit will be set on the overall amount that they will be able to spend in any one year.

The Government will contribute towards the overall limit; regulations will also specify, among other things, the way in which people should apply for discretionary payments, how local authorities should deal with those claims and how local authorities should gather information that they need to take decisions. They will specify the maximum amount of such help to ensure that additional help does not exceed the rent or council tax liability.

The provisions also enable regulations to provide for decisions to be reviewed. They provide a framework for a scheme in which local authorities may decide to make payments to people who, because of circumstances beyond their control, cannot afford their housing costs. As a matter of course, we shall consult local authority associations and the Council on Tribunals on the necessary regulations and on implementation of the proposals.

Amendments Nos. 357, 358, 362 and 364 are technical amendments covering commencement, transitional arrangements and consequential repeals. I commend the proposals to the Committee.

Section 49 of the Welfare Reform and Pensions Act 1999 is concerned with the creation of state scheme pension debits and credits when a pension sharing order or agreement—which is purely voluntary—is made. A pension debit is simply the reduction in the SERPS rights of the member whose pension has been shared. The pension credit represents the part of the member's pension transferred to the former spouse. Section 49(4) currently provides for the calculation of the value of a credit or debit in accordance with regulations made by the Secretary of State.

As I said, it had always been our intention to sub-delegate the responsibility for calculating the cash value of SERPS rights to the Government Actuary. However, the general regulation-making power that we have taken in section 49(A) is not sufficient and we need a specific sub-delegation power.

In the absence of such a power to sub-delegate responsibility to the Government Actuary, we have been advised that the regulations will need to contain detailed actuarial tables, which would themselves have to be amended each time the methods, and/or assumptions, underlying the tables needed to be changed. To avoid that, it is necessary to provide a sub-delegation power in section 49(4) of the 1999 Act. Similarly, a sub-delegation power is needed in relation to other provisions in the Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 that are concerned with the valuation of SERPS rights in pension sharing cases—hence the symmetrical amendments to section 45B, 55A and 55B of that Act.

Mr. Rooker: Essentially, we are debating new clause 35. I shall not take long, but several points need to be made, if only because this will probably be the last time today that I shall speak.

Pension forecasting is important, and I alluded to it earlier. It is essentially a matter of data protection. The new clause will permit state pension information to be passed to employers and pension scheme providers for the purpose of providing statements of private and state pension rights, unless employees indicate that they do not want such information to be disclosed.

The policy announcement on this matter was made in the Green Paper in December 1998. There has been no legislation since because none was required to allow pension forecasts to every worker. However, because we want to get it right—we do not want to rush it—it has been piloted. Indeed the pilot schemes are being piloted. We are piloting the pilot scheme with various companies. We started the pilot schemes with companies that were prepared to co-operate and become involved because we want the pension forecast to be delivered by the employer, not the Government.

For the employer to print a form showing the state pension provisions of employees would require employees' permission. Indeed, that is what is happening in the pilot schemes. Pre-pilots are being run by the Prudential and Sainsbury's. Tens of thousands of their employees are involved, and they had to give their approval. Not all of them will have second pension provision—that issue will be dealt with elsewhere—but there is potential for the working population of about 27 million to be involved. The plan might not work if only one or two schemes seek approval. The legal question is not about people opting in but allowing people to opt out, so that we can conform fully with data protection legislation and European convention requirements.

We want to encourage the inclusion of all pension provision details at one point for employees, and there might be a problem if the state did not give information. It has been realised that getting people to opt in is more onerous than allowing them to opt out. The information is theirs and it will probably come through a computer printout. There is no question of employers prying, although they will transmit the information. We have safeguarded people's rights. The right will be clearly given—it will not be hidden.

Pension providers are also involved, albeit as a third party. They will be given state pension details. Some are developing comparative financial databases, which would give financial information to help individuals to identify the most appropriate pension or saving arrangement. Financial products could be targeted to an industry or segment of the population if its provision was shown to be deficient. The measure will be important.

The databases would also enable individuals to access their state pension details electronically. I will probably get rapped over the knuckles for advertising a little-known service that the Department provides, which allows anybody to ask for a pension forecast at any time. Remarkably few people do that—600,000 a year—and most do it late in their working life, close to retirement, when there is not much time to act. We provide only basic pension, graduated pension and SERPS information in the forecast.

Currently, 18.5 million members of private pension schemes in the working population could benefit from a combined pension forecast. About 17 million people of working age do not have rights in a private pension. The system will be voluntary, but the whole industry is behind it. The Government intend to promote the policy vigorously and we shall, in due course, target the self-employed because they suffer a pension deficiency.

The pre-pilot is under way; the first combined forecasts will be issued by the Prudential in March. Other companies, including the principal civil service pension scheme, are co-operating in the pilot. The parliamentary pension trustees issued a forecast relating to the parliamentary pension for the first time last year. That was a real plus.

I am modestly ignorant of pensions and everything else. The issue of pension forecasts to the working population will probably have more effect on making people pension wise than Ministers' speeches and the Department's fancy advertising leaflets. The effect on pension information and education will be dramatic, if only because people will say, ``Hang on, this is not enough. Why isn't it enough and how can I increase it?'' That will be an extremely valuable resource for 20-year-olds, 30-year-olds and 40-year-olds, allowing them to get more control over their pension arrangements. It will not make pensions politically sexy, but it will have a tremendous impact when people start to see how information is provided.

Careful testing in the pilots is necessary. We want the information to be easily understood. The scheme will not work if we make people opt in. We live in the real world. Although we must protect people's rights by providing a proper opportunity to opt out, our proposal is the only way to make a reality of this important policy.

Angela Eagle: New clause 30 is a necessary technical amendment to restore the legislative basis for child benefit payments for a small group of disabled children in Scotland. Amendment No 356 allows the new clause to commence.

The amendments restore the statutory basis for the provisions.
