Clause 27

Payments to companies under section 687 of the Taxes Act 1988

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): Clause 27 is an anti-avoidance clause. We understand the need in Finance Bills from time to time to take measures to ensure that the intention of Parliament is respected and not circumvented too blatantly or persistently by schemes seeking to undermine that intention. But, a word of caution must be struck. The measures taken against avoidance must be proportionate to the damage that has been caused or is in prospect. It is wrong to pass large and complex chunks of anti-avoidance legislation when the damage to the revenues may be incidental or transitory.

The clause must itself be clear and unambiguous. It must be focused clearly on the supposed abuse; it must not go unnecessarily wide. That is very important. There is always a temptation for the Revenue authorities or the Government of the day to pass rather sweeping and general anti-avoidance legislation--almost to try to force the intention behind legislation--whereas it is the British legal tradition to be precise. We start from the position that the taxpayer is entitled to keep his or her income or capital until there is clear legislative authority to take that money away from that person. The coercive power of legislation is one that the House, and certainly the Committee, should grant the Executive only on certain carefully defined conditions.

If we do not respect those anti-avoidance principles, we could end up not only hitting the innocent, but creating a good deal of confusion and ambiguity among compliant taxpayers. A frequently expressed wish of successive Governments is to try to make tax legislation--indeed, the entire tax system--comprehensible so that people can see what taxes they are paying, at what rate, how much and the purposes to which those taxes are to be applied. In a complex, advanced economy, people are entitled to make complex arrangements to secure their income and capital. None of that need be illegal. It is not necessarily avoidance of the scale or type that any Government would wish to undermine.

The fact that schemes or complex arrangements are in place is itself no evidence of avoidance of the type that the Government would wish to curtail. What worries me about the clause is that it seems to go too wide. I can give an example of a scheme that might be hit which has been in the news recently. Several large companies have perfectly legal avoidance schemes to shelter from taxation some dividend income and shares in capital form. The BP scheme to shelter specific shares from tax for the eventual benefit of directors and chairmen recently caught our eye. In the case of Lord Simon, who is a Labour Minister, the figure of £2 million worth of shares has been mentioned. Would the scheme be affected by the clause? In previous debates, it has been pointed out that, as far as we know, there is nothing wrong with the scheme. However, we are a little surprised by the large sum. Last week, it was observed that BP's work force had not done quite so well.

During the past five years, the number of BP employees world wide has fallen from 115,000 to 53,000--a reduction of more than half. The United Kingdom work force have also almost halved--they have decreased from 30,000 to a little under 16,000. The employees have not done so well, whereas the Lord Simon has done very well. It is not up to me or the Committee to advise the Government--

Clause 28

Arrangements to pass on value of tax credit

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 44, in page 21, line 23, at end insert--

    `(13) On an application by any taxpayer, the Inland Revenue shall provide advice and, if appropriate, pre-transaction clearance for any prospective arrangement under this section.'

Again, this is an anti-avoidance clause. I shall not repeat my general observations about the need for such measures in some cases. The clause must be extremely carefully focused and unambiguous so that it does not hit people who are compliant and have no intention of ordering their affairs to avoid tax as a primary motive or to an extent that might catch the Treasury's eye, ever-vigilant as it is to protect its revenue.

Clause 28 goes too wide. I refer especially to proposed subsection (6) which imports a motive test into avoidance. That is always a controversial area; it means only that the tax authorities are to judge whether someone enters into an arrangement to avoid tax or not. It is a principle of English law that we do not usually attempt to judge motives, but to gauge effects. We look at the literal clauses and at how the wording affects actual arrangements.

My problem is compounded because proposed subsection (6) is extremely vague. The clause generally--it is outlined in the summary--seeks to restrict or disallow certain transactions if at least one of the parties entered into them for an unallowable purpose. An ``unallowable purpose'' is broadly one that is not commercial so a shareholder might have to show that receiving shares under an arrangement was for a commercial purpose unless the distributing company was getting rid of some of its shares unconnected with its main activities for a commercial purpose. However, it is possible that the company holding some shares as an investment unconnected with its main operations may decide to distribute them to shareholders. That is not a commercial purpose, therefore the shareholder would have to show that he or she had received such shares for a commercial purpose. That may be difficult to show because most shareholders take only a fleeting or incidental interest in the companies in which they invest. If a shareholder receives shares it would be difficult to show that he has a commercial motive. More than that, the recipient may not even be a shareholder, but a nominee. How can a nominee receive such shares as part of a commercial purpose? I understand the reasoning behind the clause, but it is much too vague and general. I should like the Minister to prove to me that an individual or nominee can, in all the circumstances, be shown to the satisfaction of the tax authorities to have the required commercial purposes. If he has not, he could be said to be entering into an arrangement for an unallowed purpose and be caught by the clause.

Mr. Damian Green (Ashford): I share the concerns of my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells about the clause. They are best illustrated by the change of wording between the explanatory notes on clauses from the Revenue and the actual wording of the most offensive subsection, the proposed subsection (6). The Revenue's notes state:

    ``The arrangements are only covered by the restriction if at least one of the parties enters into them for an unallowed purpose. Broadly, that means a purpose other than a business or commercial purpose.''

That does not give the full flavour of the proposed subsection (6), which mentions arrangements that

    ``include a purpose which is not amongst the business or other commercial purposes of that person.''

The subsection as drafted puts too great an onus on one of the parties engaged in such an arrangement, who is almost being asked to prove a negative. It therefore gives too much power to the Revenue, and, eventually, presumably the courts, to interpret an allowable purpose under the clause.

Clearly, some classes of people may change their business or commercial purposes and may legitimately want to re-order their affairs, so that some such arrangement which would otherwise be covered by the clause could be regarded as part of a genuine business purpose. But wider classes of people would find it extremely difficult to prove that they have a commercial purpose, and specifically a commercial purpose as covered by the proposed subsection (6)--an existing commercial purpose.

An obvious group who would be badly hit by the proposal would be nominees, who may have a set of commercial purposes, but not purposes covered by the clause. The same applies to individuals: if the Revenue were minded to play hardball with the clause, it would be possible at least to argue that no individual or no nominee can have a commercial purpose as such, and that therefore any such arrangement would be caught by the clause.

I phrase my comments in a slightly questioning tone because I am genuinely unsure about how widely the Government wish the clause to be drawn. Is it to be an all-embracing anti-avoidance clause that will catch almost any such arrangement, or is it meant to be properly targeted at those who effectively try to fiddle the system? The drafting does not make clear which way the proposal will fall. Therefore, with perhaps unjustified cynicism, may I say that if allowed to stand as currently phrased, in practice it would be too widely drawn and a number of innocent parties would be caught by the clause for the entirely legitimate arrangement of their tax affairs. I seek reassurance from the Paymaster General that the clause can be interpreted narrowly so that innocent parties will not be caught by the current wording.

Mr. Gibb: Clause 28 is effectively the general anti-avoidance provision regarding the abolition of repayment of tax credits on dividends. Like all general anti-avoidance provisions, it is too widely drawn. Like my hon. Friend the Member for Ashford (Mr. Green), I refer to proposed subsection (6), which relates to the sole or main motive for entering into certain transactions. That test is often used in tax legislation, and the motive behind that wording is to detect whether the primary purpose of entering into a commercial activity was to avoid tax, rather than the avoidance of tax being simply a beneficial side-effect of the activity. The provision seeks to outlaw those instances where the sole or main benefit of entering into a transaction is to gain a tax advantage.

The problem with the clause is that the definition of tax advantage, which we can find in proposed subsection (12), refers to chapter I of part XVII of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988, which defines tax advantage according to the dreadful provisions of section 709 of that Act. Those sections are anathema to industry and the tax profession because they are the nearest thing in tax legislation to a general anti-avoidance provision. Despite the fact that that provision applies only to interests in securities or shares, it causes huge alarm to companies when they are about to undertake any corporate reconstruction such as acquisition, disposal or merger.

The provision is so vaguely drawn, as is any general anti-avoidance clause, that companies are forced to seek clearance under sections 703 and 709 whenever they undertake any major transaction in securities. There are 30 days in which the Revenue is required to respond to such a clearance. The measure adds a cumbersome element to any dynamic activity by companies. They have to set out for the Government full details of their transactions and then wait so many days before they receive a response. The whole effort can hamper many worthwhile plans that companies have for expansion, acquisition or merger. It is unfortunate, therefore, that the clause refers for the definition of tax advantage to those cumbersome provisions in the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988.

I am also alarmed that the Government stated in the Budget statement that they are considering bringing in a general anti-avoidance provision. Here we almost have such a provision regarding tax credit. It would be a disaster if the Government were to take the tempting route of introducing a general anti-avoidance provision. It would give too much power to the Inland Revenue and there would be too much resulting uncertainty in tax affairs. It would be cumbersome and would result in a plethora of litigation in the courts as taxpayers sought to clarify the law. I urge the Government--as, I suspect, will industry generally--not to introduce a general anti-avoidance provision.

Proposed subsection (6) comes in for severe criticism from the Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales, of which I happen to be a member. Its representation says:

All such procedures are cumbersome, but there cannot be a general anti-avoidance procedure without a clearance procedure. The institute of Directors is equally scathing about the clause. It states:

    ``This clause is too widely drawn. Clause 28 could apply to deny tax credit repayments when some tax advantage unrelated to tax credit repayment is a main purpose of the arrangements.''

The clause is a general anti-avoidance measure, albeit focused on tax credits. It is cumbersome, unfair and will create enormous uncertainty in the law as people will be unsure whether their particular transaction is caught by the Bill. General anti-avoidance legislation would not have been necessary had the Government not abolished tax credit payments in such a way. Altogether, it complicates and further inconveniences the commercial world. Without a clearance procedure, the provisions are unworkable.

Clause 29

Unauthorised unit trusts

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I shall make a few brief points on the clause, which forms part of the clutch of anti-avoidance measures. We have already debated the principle and the Government's approach to it, so I do not intend to add to what has already been said.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton suggested, however, the proposals have been put together in an extraordinary way. As a by-product of that, the clause is the first to rehearse the concept of foreign income dividends by saying, in effect, that where a qualifying distribution

    ``is made on or after 2nd July 1997 but before 6th April 1999 by a company resident in the United Kingdom''

it should be treated as a foreign income dividend for the purpose of this clause. I am not sure how many companies resident in the United Kingdom would be pleased that it was so treated when it clearly was not, or need not necessarily be, a foreign income dividend. That is an instance of the way in which the concepts are put together adds to the complexity.

I have been trying to carve through the complexity and through the much clearer explanatory notes from the Inland Revenue, and should like to ask the Paymaster General three points. The first arises from the representations made to the Economic Secretary by the Association of Unit Trusts and Investment Funds about the importance of the impact of this clutch of changes on the association. It makes the point that there is a growing interest in the use by pension funds of authorised unit trusts as well as unauthorised ones. We are not here to determine the decisions of pension fund trustees. However, I am anxious to elicit from the Paymaster General confirmation that the clause will neutralise the position for that decision. In other words, will the pension funds that invest in unauthorised unit trusts be in the same position as they would be investing in authorised unit trusts during that period? As I understand it, the clause is necessary to prevent them from avoiding the gap before April 1999, when they might otherwise have used unauthorised unit trusts. It would also be fair to say as a rider that those who invest in them will be in substantially the same position as people paying the basic rate from 1999, as they will get credit. There are two other small points.
