Mr. David Chidgey (Eastleigh): I should like to return to the key issues behind the amendment--under-18-year-olds in training and low-skilled adults or those with obsolete skills.

There is a clear case for a training rate for under-18-year-olds. All 16 to 18-year-olds should, in any case, be in training as a matter of right. It is widely recognised internationally that a lower rate should apply for under-18s. The House of Commons Library has produced some interesting work on the issue, which shows that lower rates apply for under-18-year-olds in training in Belgium and France. The Scottish Low Pay Unit, too, believes that a percentage of the national minimum wage should be paid to workers under 18, provided that they are not simply used as an alternative form of cheap labour and that they undergo training. The greater Manchester Low Pay Unit says:

    ``There is a case to be made for young people to be paid a proportion of the minimum wage if they are receiving training leading to a recognised qualification.''

The Confederation of British Industry agrees that there should be a lower rate for people in training. It notes that 48 per cent. of all 16 to 18-year-olds are paid less than £3 an hour and that 60 per cent. are paid less than £2.50 an hour. The impact of a national minimum wage on those young people in training would be quite dramatic. The key issue is that we must never forget that equal work should mean equal pay, regardless of age. I do not subscribe to the view that young adults should not be entitled to a national minimum wage purely because of their age.

The crux of the amendment and of the problem is low-skilled adults. How can we treat employees and employers fairly within the national minimum wage structure? How can we provide a mechanism to get the low skilled or those with obsolete skills, who are often the long-term unemployed, off benefits and into permanent work? How can we provide them with high-quality training to impart the skills that they need and which are essential if they are to become part of the permanent labour market? How do we enable employees to earn a living wage, free of the benefit trap and with an entitlement to a national minimum wage? At the same time--this is equally important--how do we protect employers from the unsustainable costs that they could incur while their employees are training and before their productivity increases to a level that provides added value to the national minimum wage and recovers the training costs that employers have supported?

It is as though we were trying to solve a complex algebraic equation without enough formulae to cover the many unknowns in it. It is, therefore, difficult to arrive at a solution. However, the CBI believes that new employees who receive training while they develop the skills necessary for their job will be less productive during training. That is fairly well understood, and it is recognised internationally in lower training rates.

The problem that we face when trying to solve such a seemingly insoluble equation is how to match the unknowns. When we face such a problem, it is always best to return to first principles. In this case, the first principles are that employees should not continue to be paid poverty wages and be supported by in-work benefits that are paid for by the taxpayer. The national minimum wage should therefore apply to all adult employees.

The low skilled, who are often the long-term unemployed, must have access, as of right, to high-quality training to increase their productivity. At the same time, employers must be protected from unsustainable training costs while paying the national minimum wage. Training must therefore be supported by state finance. That could be achieved through the transfer of unemployment benefits into a training fund for high-quality, recognised vocational training while the employer pays the going rate for the job. That measure would represent a net saving to the state because new employees would invariably pay back money in their income tax contributions.

We should consider an example that has already been used in this debate to justify the amendment. The view has been expressed that if training rates paid to craft apprentices, compared with the wages paid to qualified craftsmen, were up-rated to the national minimum wage, that would upset employer's staff and other costs. That presupposes that a national minimum wage would be at least close to the hourly rate paid to qualified craftsmen, but I can find no example in industry of craftsmen being paid at a rate as low as even the most optimistic forecast for the national minimum wage.

At the heart of the amendment there is a need for the Low Pay Commission to look beyond the immediate impact of the national minimum wage, which will be to take workers out of poverty, at the complex interrelationship that exists between training provision and costs. It needs to consider what will be the impact of the national minimum wage on the welfare-to-work programme and the costs that will apply to responsible employers. I therefore urge the Government to consult the Low Pay Commission on those essential factors if we are to have a sensible national minimum wage that does not undermine training and provides the skills that the low skilled and the young need to get into profitable, productive employment.

The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Mr. Ian McCartney): The debate has been reasonably short but it has raised issues of principle relating to training, its purpose and the role of the state, employers and employees, including potential employees. Much adult learning takes place in the workplace and the balance must be struck between the interests of the labour market, which is susceptible to change, and those of the work force, who must therefore be adaptable.
