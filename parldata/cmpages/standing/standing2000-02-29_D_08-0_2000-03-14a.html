We must consider whether the Bill should include more repeals. I am especially anxious about the NATO Status of Forces Treaty 1951 and the Visiting Forces Act 1952. The matter is fairly complex. The Status of Forces Agreement in London, which was signed by NATO members on 19 June 1951, sets out the legal arrangements for the exchange and basing of military personnel from one military state to another and provides for the sending state to exercise extraterritorial jurisdiction over members of its armed forces and their dependants in the receiving state's territory.

In the United Kingdom, SOFA provisions are enacted by the Visiting Forces Act. Separate agreements govern the status of NATO international military headquarters. United Kingdom service personnel remain subject to service jurisdiction wherever they are serving, but the extent to which that may be exercised varies according to the receiving state.

With the exception of exercises and other deployments to countries where SOFA applies or to Commonwealth or former Commonwealth countries whose visiting forces legislation is similar to ours, it is necessary to negotiate terms that can be incorporated in a memorandum of understanding. That will be increasingly important if our armed forces' prime purpose is to engage in peace enforcing or peacekeeping in nations that are not signatories to the European convention on human rights.

There is a further impact, and we need to go back some years to understand its significance. During the second world war, the Allied Forces Act 1940 enabled visiting allied forces to conduct courts martial, but did not provide immunity from ordinary criminal law. There was a single exception, as the United States of America (Visiting Forces) Act 1942 gave members of the United States naval and military forces immunity in United Kingdom courts. That remained the position until the aforementioned Acts were repealed by the Visiting Forces Act 1952.

Several extraordinary things happened in that period which gave rise to those Acts. There was a large American base in Shepton Mallet in Somerset and, as a colleague reminded me, the Americans meted out fierce retribution to those of their service men who were involved in rape cases in the local community.
