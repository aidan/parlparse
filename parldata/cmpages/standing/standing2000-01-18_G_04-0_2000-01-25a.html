Clause 30

Financial structure of existing registered party

Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): I beg to move amendment No. 19, in page 17, line 40, leave out `weeks' and insert `months'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss the following amendments: No. 20, in page 18, line 5, at end add

    `, except that notification given in relation to any accounting unit of annual expenditure or gross income of less than £5,000 shall be sent to the Commission within nine months.'.

No. 21, in page 18, line 13, leave out `nine' and insert `twelve'.

Mr. Stunell: The amendments are exploratory. We want to ensure that the Minister is aware of the likely impact of the provisions on small accounting units in political parties. The amendments seek to give a little more latitude in the reporting procedures and to ensure that the requirements in the Bill bear some relationship to reality.

At a previous sitting, we talked about the Hazel Grove action group. I would like to move on to the Littledon village branch of the Liberal Democrat party. Mrs. Brown has been its treasurer there for about 23 years, and she has just reached her eighties. The branch has about 15 members and it has two or three coffee mornings a year. The account is held in the local post office because it is too far for Mrs. Brown to get to a bank. The idea of the branch receiving a donation that exceeds £200 is more likely to induce a stroke in the treasurer than it is be used for a flight to South America.

Mrs. Brown will be subject to the provisions of clause 30 in its entirety. That requires that the report that she has to submit arrives at the commission within six weeks. The accounts must be approved according to the commission's scheme, in accordance with the same timetable as that which applies to the principal political parties. It will mean that the period in which the branch has to report is the same as for the main parties.

The purpose of the amendments is to draw the attention of the Minister and the Committee to the fact that, in many cases, reporting requirements that are highly necessary for whole parties and major accounting units are not sensible for small accounting units. There are ways to get round that, however, and the amendments seek to explore some of them. For instance, we could have a de minimis category of accounting unit that had separate accounting rules. Later amendments deal with that solution.

We are thoroughly committed to ensuring the most rigorous inspection of central party accounts. We are equally committed to making sure that the parts of the parties known in the Bill as accounting units are also responsible, and we think that rules should be drawn up that reflect the complexity and severity of the mischief that might follow a unit stepping over the line.

I also want to draw the Minister's attention to the parallel problem for schools and school governing bodies. If one increases the burden on volunteers and relies on them to run the system, there comes a point where the hypothetical Mrs. Brown says, ``That's it. I can't go on. I can't take the worry or stress.'' We would find that it became more difficult at the lowest level of volunteer organisations to find people willing and able to become involved.

The Littledon village branch probably does practically nothing. Perhaps once every four or five years, it puts someone up to the district council elections if it can find anyone to stand. When that happens, it has to appoint an agent, who will be subject to all the controls of the Representation of the People Act 1983 and, if the Bill becomes law, of additional requirements as well. That is proper but, before a second person in Littledon becomes subject to the full force of law in every respect as though he or she ran a major national party, will the Minister reconsider the restrictions and barriers that will be put in place of genuine volunteer efforts at the grass roots of politics?

The Parliamentary Secretary, Privy Council Office (Mr. Paddy Tipping): May I welcome you to the first proper sitting of the Committee that you have chaired, Mrs. Michie, and say that the hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell) is pursuing an issue that we talked about in some depth? It relates to ensuring that the Bill is not too onerous for smaller parties, and especially for people in large parties--for example, treasurers such as Mrs. Brown of the Hazel Grove Liberal Democrats.

I shall try to reassure the hon. Gentleman that there is a misunderstanding about the nature of the clause. It is a transitional arrangement that takes the registration of political parties under the Representation of Political Parties Act 1998 and transfers it to the Electoral Commission's new register. We are clear in our mind that that must be done quickly. I do not know when the general election will be. People with more authority will make such judgments, but I know that all political parties want to ensure that the Bill is in place by the next general election so that we can conduct it under a more transparent and accountable system.

If we accepted amendment No. 19, which in effect would change ``six weeks'' to ``six months'', it would not be possible to have the legislation in place within the timetable. Again, I want to stress that that responsibility of a transitional scheme being put in place within six weeks applies to the national treasurer--the national officer--not to Mrs. Brown. The existing 1998 legislation will be transferred to the new legislation, and the commitment will be national and not on Mrs. Brown.

Mrs. Brown will be delighted when the consequences of the Bill become clear to her. The hon. Gentleman made a strong, important point. There are worrying aspects to the Bill. He talked about the need for, and the difficulty of getting, school governors. We are putting constraints on voluntary action and, if we are not careful, we will put people off the notion of citizenship.

Mrs. Brown will have the ultimate responsibility for submitting her accounts in six weeks. I reassure the hon. Gentleman about what Mrs. Brown will have to do. The clause will have no effect on her whatsoever. Once the Bill is enacted, Mrs. Brown will have to submit accounts at annual intervals in a simple form--which will be negotiated with the commission--if the income of a party is more than £25,000, a figure that we will discuss later. If Mrs. Brown receives a large donation--one can speculate about what a large donation would be; I understand that a figure of £200 might cause bubbles to come out of people's heads and questions to be raised--she will have to report it. The figure of £200 is not a de minimis figure. I accept entirely the point made by the hon. Member for Hazel Grove that it is important that the legislation works at local level.

There are concerns that the amendments will not do the job that the hon. Gentleman anticipates. I hope that important people such as Mrs. Brown, who are the lifeblood of the party--people whom we do not praise or comment on enough--will not be under the obligations that the hon. Gentleman mistakenly believes exist under the clause. I hope that he will reflect on the matter and withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Stunell: I thank the Minister for dealing with me so gently. I appreciate his points and welcome his reassurance on the Government's approach to the Bill and on the operation of the lowest level--I do not mean that in a derogatory sense--of our democratic system. Without such volunteers in many small locations, in all our political parties, there would not be a political structure on which to build the rest of the elaborate framework that we have.

I understand the points that the Minister made. I am pleased that he accepted the generality of my argument. Perhaps when I pursue other amendments, he will bear in mind his own words and we will make progress. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

In relative terms, smaller parties might have more to do, and the converse is also true, as larger parties might have more to do in a different sense. At this stage, I can only listen to the debate and try to ensure that any points raised separately by political parties are drawn to the attention of officials, so that as the Electoral Commission emerges it will hear the views that have been expressed in Committee this morning.

Clause 36

Duty to keep accounting records

Mr. Stunell: I beg to move amendment No. 22, in page 21, line 19, leave out `six' and insert `five'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss amendment No. 23, in page 21, line 35, at end add

    `, and to different accounting units.'.

Mr. Stunell: This amendment brings us back to Mrs. Brown again.

I shall deal with the second amendment first, because it perhaps illustrates my point sufficiently. In the light of discussions at the drafting stage, clause 6(b) was amended to allow different accounting periods for different parties. That is a wholly reasonable amendment that reflects reality. Our amendment would also enable clause 6(b) to reflect different accounting units. As drafted, every unit of a particular party would be required to have the same accounting period.

Commonly, at least two obvious accounting periods are used in the commercial and real worlds--1 January and 1 April. In politics and in particular in the Littledon village branch, matters may be dealt with on a basis that has more to do with the local government electoral cycle. Therefore, 1 June or 1 July may well be seen as the start of an accounting period, thereby allowing election accounts to be dealt with in a more complete way from the branch's point of view.

Like me, Mrs. Michie, you may have found that the accounting period is of greater importance at branch level than, for example the abolition of the NHS or the privatisation of the railways. Such matters are vital to the practical working of the units of a party. The amendment would in no way weaken the safeguards that are incorporated in the Bill and would not affect the need to give proper account. However, it would allow existing, sensible and entirely legitimate practices to continue as necessary at a local level.

Amendment No. 22 draws attention to the record-keeping provisions in the Bill. Six years is a substantial time for the village branch to preserve accounting records. It is perhaps appropriate to require a national party to preserve records for a period spanning at least two general elections. That would allow for inspection and, if necessary, revelation over a prolonged period. However, I doubt whether all Committee members could put hand on heart and say that all branches and units of their local parties can reliably find their financial records for the previous six years. One must consider what is sensible, practical and necessary in terms of inspecting the public record.

Given that, under the Bill, such accounts will be reported to someone else, the six-year preservation requirement is perhaps somewhat over the top. Amendment No. 23 is a probing amendment that seeks the Minister's reaction to that point, but I none the less believe that it would give significant variation and flexibility.

Mr. Walter: I have some sympathy with the purpose of amendment No. 22, which would reduce the requirement from six to five years. However, I hesitate to say that I support that suggestion, because the provision is somewhat consistent with the Companies Act 1985 and the practice of the Inland Revenue and other bodies. Nevertheless, as the hon. Member for Hazel Grove has noted, in a small accounting unit, the treasurer will not necessarily want to keep records for up to six years if they cease to hold that job. A large degree of responsibility would therefore fall on the constituency organisation, or even on the party nationally, in that respect.

I believe--the Minister may correct me--that the way in which the clause is drafted means that an accounting unit with a turnover of less than £25,000 will not have to comply with it. In the interests of transparency, we have tabled an amendment to a later part of the Bill that would lower that limit. We shall explain that in due course.

11.15 am

I am concerned about the proposition by the hon. Member for Hazel Grove that there should be different accounting periods for different accounting units within the same party. The essence of the Bill, which is very much in the spirit of Neill, is that an outsider can look at the accounts of a political party and feel that he has a picture of where the money is coming from and how it is being spent. I appreciate the argument for two different accounting periods. However, if different accounting units had different accounting periods, the commission could approve a whole series of such accounting periods. If one accounting unit received money in one year and transferred that money to the central organisation in another year, we would be unclear as to exactly where the money that was being spent in the second year had originally come from. Therefore, although I have a great deal of sympathy with amendment No. 22, I do not support amendment No. 23.

Mr. Tipping: I turn first to amendment No. 22, which is the simpler of the two. In effect, it would allow records to be destroyed after five years, rather than six. If the hon. Gentleman does not mind me saying so, there is not much difference between six and five. The figure of six is in the Bill because it brings the Bill into line with companies and charities legislation. There are well-known precedents for six years in legislation that covers both those sectors.

Amendment No. 23 is more interesting. When I read it, I thought that it took us back to an earlier discussion about federal parties and different accounting years in different parts of a federal party. The Government intend to table amendments so that national parties can be independently registered. If that were the case, they could of course have different accounting years. The hon. Member for Hazel Grove has taken us back to Mrs. Brown and the real issues that surround ward and constituency parties.

In effect, the hon. Member for North Dorset was doing my job for me. The Bill specifies one accounting year for each party for the reasons of transparency and accountability that he outlined. It is also important that parties are well organised, so that they can meet the demands that the Electoral Commission will make upon them. I accept that there is a great deal to be done.

I believe that all hon. Members have received a copy of the letter that was sent out to political parties. For the legislation to be in place for the next general election, there must be progress now. I know that some parties have strong views about independence and are determined to do things in their own way, but it is not unreasonable for all parts of a political party that agrees the scheme through negotiation with the Electoral Commission to use the same accounting year. I accept that it may cause difficulties and tension in some political parties, but it is in the interests of the commission, the party and, most important, accountability and transparency, that we have each unit of every political party has the same accounting year. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will reflect on the matter and withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Stunell: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 37

Annual statements of accounts

Mr. Stunell: I beg to move amendment No. 24, in page 22, line 14, leave out `£250,000, or' and insert `£5,000'.

The Chairman: With this we may take amendment No. 25, in page 22, line 14, at end insert--

    `(ii.a) exceeds £5,000 but not £250,000, or'.

Mr. Stunell: The amendments would introduce four zones or bands of gross income and total expenditure for a party and its constituent parts. It is right that there should be sub-divisions and that the oversight of a party's performance should increase as its gross income or total expenditure rises. Subsection (4)(a)(i) makes de minimis provisions for small accounting units, which can do little damage. It is right that there should be increasing steps, however, there seems to be a middle band that does not take full account of the range of size and the mischief that might be created on the one hand by a political party with a gross income of a little over £1,000 and, on the other hand, by a political party with an income approaching £250,000. The amendment seeks to address that by inserting a category dealing with incomes between £1,000 and £5,000.

The Committee will want to know why we have chosen £5,000. There may be a better intermediate figure, but in drafting the amendment I had in mind a category for an accounting unit, or a political party, that was up to funding contesting one general election seat, but was not in the flat-out mode required to pay the current maximum election expenses. In other words--and this is not a political secret--at the last general election it would have covered most constituency parties that contested seats that were not fought tooth and nail, hammer and tongs, where there was, to varying degrees, a pro forma campaign. It seems sensible to ask the Minister to consider such an amendment as constituency parties should have greater oversight than Mrs. Brown and her village branch, although they do not need the same depth of examination as constituency parties that fought hammer and tongs to the last halfpenny of expenditure or national political parties that may have fought only a few seats but have a much wider impact.

Therefore, the intention is more important than the precise numbers. The first category catches what one might call the normal branch, which perhaps contests local government elections and gently runs along on its own track. Our new second category would catch parliamentary constituency parties performing essentially the same job. Then the new third category would cover organisations where real mischief might occur.

Committee members well understand the issues that I have brought to light, so I shall not deploy my argument at greater length.
