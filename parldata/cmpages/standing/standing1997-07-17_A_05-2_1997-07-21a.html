Mrs. Liddell: The clause will bring about a necessary change to prevent beneficiaries of estates from reclaiming tax credits to which they would not be entitled if the distribution was received direct. The clause, in effect, amends the treatment of company distributions that do not carry a tax credit and which are received by the estate of a deceased person in the course of administration. It is needed to ensure that the beneficiaries are taxed on payments from the estate in the same way as if they had received the distribution directly. From Budget day, payments to beneficiaries of estates funded out of non-qualifying distributions will be treated as made under deduction of non-repayable tax at the lower rate.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I should like to linger just long enough to get an assurance that executors handling the administration of an estate will not be put in an invidious position due to the changes. I refer particularly to the apparent absence of transitional arrangements. As the Economic Secretary helpfully outlined, the clause provides for amending the treatment of non-qualifying distributions received by the estate of a deceased person. A typical example of a non-qualifying distribution is bonus shares, which do not attract tax credits.

From Budget day, the payments to beneficiaries of such an estate, which are funded out of such distributions, will be treated as being made under deduction of non-repayable tax at the lower rate. That is clear, but what is not clear is the connection between payment out of the fund after Budget day and possible receipt by the fund in administration before Budget day. In the absence of a connection between the two--or, at least, guidance--to see how the payments flow through, executors could be put in a difficult position. Indeed, the tax authorities could claim disallowance of tax arrangements made after Budget day.

I shall take that illustration further. An estate, before 2 July, may have been receiving a number of incomes. In particular, a large or complex estate may have been receiving rental income, dividend income and interest on investments, some of which could be taxed at different rates and treated differently. Some of the income could be non-qualifying distributions of the sort to which I alluded--bonus shares for example.

The distributions received by the fund administration would normally be used to fund various distributions made out of the estate on to numerous beneficiaries under the terms of a will or suchlike. Such beneficiaries will typically have various tax status--some will be paying tax at a higher rate, some at standard rate, some at lower rate. It is important for them to know whether they can reclaim tax credits. If they are higher rate taxpayers, it does not matter. They do not have to receive back the tax credit--it will simply be set against their tax computation in due course as a credit. However, if they are lower rate taxpayers, it is important for them to know whether they will be able to receive the tax credits.

Along come the Labour Government, and from 1 July they change the law in respect of certain of these payments made out of the estate to beneficiaries--namely, the non-qualifying distributions. They are now to be treated as received by the beneficiaries as non-repayable tax. This is clearly an important change, and I understand the logic for it. I do not dispute it, and we shall not be voting against the clause. But the question is how are the payments out of the estate after Budget day to be linked to the earlier payments into the fund? There is no way in the clause of allocating elements in what I call the pot, which has received earlier payments into it, to the specific distributions to beneficiaries made out of it.

If they are dutiful and wish to pay minimum tax, executors may believe it right to allocate their distributions in such a way as to obtain the most favourable tax treatment. For example, they will not pay out funds to those beneficiaries paying tax at the lower rate if the tax cannot be recovered in the hands of those beneficiaries. Yet someone may claim that the payments they are making to this class of beneficiaries are not connected to the earlier receipt of non-qualifying distributions into the estate and administration before Budget day.
