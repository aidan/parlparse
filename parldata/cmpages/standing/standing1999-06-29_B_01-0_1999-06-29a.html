Mr. Maclean: The amendment is designed to define the Food Standards Agency's principal functions. Subsection (2) states:

    "The main objective of the Agency in carrying out its functions is to protect public health from risks which may arise in connection with the consumption of food (including risks caused by the way in which it is produced or supplied) and otherwise to protect the interests of consumers in relation to food."

That is a rather vague definition, perhaps intentionally so. It is difficult to specify the main food safety risks with which the agency will be involved, which results in a rather wide definition. What are consumers' interests in relation to food? The Bill deals with consumers' food safety interests and food standards in relation to microbiological risks and chemical dangers to food. In some ways those aspects the split between the two departments involved. The Department of Health previously concentrated on microbiological dangers, and the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food concentrated on chemical and other dangers. By pooling those functions both principal dangers in the consumption of food should be dealt with.

The Bill includes the phrase

    "and otherwise to protect the interests of consumers in relation to food."

One of consumers' main interests is price. Is it suggested that the agency will be interested in the price of food and consumers' interests in relation to that aspect? The agency is intended to deal with risks associated with food safety. Nutrition and the dangers of excessive indulgence in the wrong types of food are different matters on which I shall touch, and with which we shall no doubt deal later. Although nutrition should be either included or not--the Minister has said that the agency will have a role in giving advice on nutrition, which I may oppose--I have no qualms about the clauses covering aspects relating to nutrition. However, I am worried that the phrase

    "otherwise to protect the interests of consumers"

is far too wide. Consumers' interests in relation to food go much wider than food safety. One of consumers' principal anxieties is the price of food. Many consumers are worried that food is overpackaged, or, in some cases, underpackaged. Consumers have many interests that have nothing to do with food safety.

During food safety scares, when one asks 100 people what they are most concerned about in relation to food, the prevalent answer will be food safety. However, the Minister's advisers may confirm that when food scare levels are not in the news, often most people state that they are worried about price. Whether that is rational or not is another matter. All studies show that the average amount of time that it takes people to earn the cost of a daily shopping basket is getting less and less in real terms every year, yet the price of food is a major concern for consumers.

Consumers have other interests in relations to food that are nothing to do with the agency. As long as the phrase

    "otherwise to protect the interests of consumers"

is present, the agency has far too wide a brief. It would have the legal right to stray into matters that are not its concern, such as the environment. The interests of consumers in relation to food cover not only price, but packaging, presentation and, in my area, distribution outlets. My constituency is the largest in England. If some of my constituents were asked what was their main concern about food, it would be that they could not buy food unless they travelled 15 miles, because the little rural shops and delivery vans had gone. Consumer interest in food does not relate to standards only.

The Minister of State may say that the group of amendments is not technically legally correct, and I suspect that all other amendments that I shall table will also be legally imperfect. I hope that the Minister of State will explain that problem once only and not on every amendment. I am interested in exploring the principle.

My drafting in the amendment is much more straightforward than that in the clause, and I hope that it does not have large lacunae in it that the Minister of State will say would make the agency feeble. The amendment is more specific in dealing with exactly what we were led to believe that the Government wanted to do. It states that the

    "objectives of the Agency in carrying out its functions are . . . so far as is reasonably practicable".

I do not think that that is a limitation. At the moment, the agency has been set the impossible task of

    "carrying out its functions...to protect public health from risks which may arise"

If that is the objective, the agency will fail within 10 seconds of being set up. No matter how good it is or how brilliant the MAFF and Department of Health scientists and Ministers are, it cannot meet an objective of protecting public health from risks associated with food at all times. It can try its utmost and may succeed 90 or 99 per cent. of the time, but there should be a sensible qualifier such as "so far as is reasonably practicable", or "so far as practically possible" that would not diminish what the agency will do. I know that the terminology of BATNEEC--best available technology not entailing excessive costs is used for environmental protection, but that may not be appropriate in this case.

The public expect the agency to protect the public so far as it reasonably, sensibly and practicably can. If the Government go ahead with the words in clause 1(2), I suspect that the agency will quickly be subject to criticism because it will be perceived as having failed. My suggestion would not weaken the agency's purpose and is not designed to undermine the agency. It is designed to add some common sense, which is the standard that the agency will have to operate under. I suspect that the Minister of State's reply will be that we do not need such a phrase in the Bill, but common sense tells us that the agency will protect the public health only "so far as is reasonably practicable". The amendment also states the objective of

    "so far as is reasonably practicable, the protection of the public from microbiological and chemical dangers arising from the consumption of food".

I appreciate that one could add other dangers to complete that list, such as radiological dangers. I presume that the agency will also deal with radiological dangers, such as the few radioactive lambs left on the high fells in Cumbria

I have divided the possible risks into microbiological, chemical and associated risks, so that we avoid the clause's general and over-wide terminology of

    "and otherwise to protect the interests of consumers".

The agency needs more specific guidelines on its core functions of dealing with microbes, chemicals, veterinary products, residues and so on. There are better experts than me in the agency and in the Minister's team who can tighten up my inept outline.

The final function proposed by the amendment is that of

    "advising the public on the microbiological and chemical risks associated with the consumption of foodstuffs".

I deliberately left out nutrition. We may cover that subject under another clause, but my definition covers advice to the public on food safety risks, such as bugs or chemicals, inherent in foods, rather than general nutrition and diet advice. Some of the most dangerous foods, such as mushrooms and toadstools, are natural; some mushrooms, which I collect and bottle, are delicious, but others will kill quickly. As I said on Second Reading, I believe strongly that apart from certain foods that have toxins built into them, the food on our shelves is generally safe to eat. The problem is that too many British people and even more in the United States and other wealthy western countries, eat too much of some foodstuffs, such as sugary or fatty foods, do not take enough exercise and do not have a balanced diet. The agency should confine itself to problems inherent in food--microbiological, chemical, radiological or other contaminants--and not get into the business of nutrition advice, which should be lift to the Health Education Authority, an organisation that will continue to be funded by Government.

Giving advice on nutrition will give the agency a bad reputation early on. The Minister will know from his experience that as soon as the Government start advising the public on what they think people should eat, howls of anguish rightly emerge from the press that the Government are nannying and nagging. The press and the public are always keen to hear the mythical, so-called, independent experts, which the Government would love to have and perhaps one day will, give advice on food safety. Yet as soon as they give unwelcome nutritional advice they are no longer regarded as independent experts and are to be treated as no more than the Minister's poodles. The press and the public love Ministers to say that something is dangerous, if contaminated and is being taken off the shelves, but as soon as they start saying, "We think you should have such and such a diet; we think you should have such and such a lifestyle; this is how you should cook beef, without too much fat on it" and so on, they will be accused of poking their nose where it is not wanted.

An organisation such as the Health Education Authority may give such nannying, nagging advice on food but, provided it is coupled with advice to take exercise and do all the other things that are supposed to be good and healthy for us, that is legitimate. However, if the Food Standards Agency does that, it will give itself a bad name, whereas if it restricts itself to dealing with food poisoning outbreaks and food contamination it can develop a good reputation, and will build on the already excellent work done by staff at both MAFF and the Department of Health. If it brings advice on diet and nutrition into its remit, the media--which, whether we like it or not, are powerful--will start turning against it. The agency could also be proved wrong ifit gives such advice and that would further undermine its case.

I shall not reminisce, but much of the advice that I and others issued in the 1980s about how much wine was good for one was contradicted three or four years later by the same eminent scientists who gave the original advice. If the agency starts to advise on diet, nutrition and what is good for us, it will certainly contradict its advice in a few years. That will be seen to undermine the credibility of the agency. The public and we as politicians will say, "They have had to admit that they have got the amount of wine that we should drink in a week and the effect of salt or sugar in the diet wrong. If they cannot get that right, why should we believe them when they say that blue-green algae, or whatever, are contaminating shellfish? Why should be believe their risk assessment on beef on the bone?" That will undermine the scientific advice based on microbiological or chemical contamination, about which the quality of research and advice in this country is second to none.

In amendment No. 89, I suggest that the agency's nutrition advice tasks should be removed, but nothing would stop the agency working with the HEA. Let the HEA give advice on nutrition; let the Food Standards Agency concentrate purely on microbiological, chemical and, if necessary, radiological contamination and advise the public on those risks so that the public can make up their own minds. I hope that the Minister will find my suggestion satisfactory--or, at least, find some elements of it to be non-contaminated food for thought.
