Amendment proposed [25 January]: No. 67, in page 71, line 32, at the end to insert the words—

    `(3) The regulations shall in any event provide that the net weekly income of the non-resident parent shall be reduced in the circumstances mentioned in sub-paragraph (4) and by the method mentioned in sub-paragraph (5), but subject to the limit mentioned in sub-paragraph (6).

    (4) The circumstances referred to in sub-paragraph (3) are where the parent with care has a gross annual income from all sources for the most recently completed financial year of more than £25,000.

    (5) The method referred to in sub-paragraph (3) is that for every pound by which the gross annual income of the parent with care exceeds £25,000 the net weekly income of the non-resident parent shall be reduced by one penny.

    (6) The limit referred to in sub-paragraph (3) is that the net weekly income of the non-resident parent shall not be reduced by more than 50 per cent.

From the point of view of not taking into account the income of the parent with care, one can certainly adduce the urgent need for simplicity. I accept that in a case load of 1 million people, when only 6,000 cases might be relevant to such a proposal, we are placing a great burden on the CSA, but I can think of some ways to circumvent that difficulty. I understand where the Government are coming from because they are trying to manage the CSA. That is their problem. The CSA is clearly not working; it spends too much time on assessment rather than enforcement. In all these debates, the Government are looking for ways to simplify the system. From their point of view as managers, I understand that. I suppose that if I were a manager, responsible for ensuring that the CSA worked better, I would approach the argument differently and say that the problem was far too complex.

However, I am not managing the CSA, but trying to adduce arguments that introduce an element of fairness and natural justice into the system. If we stop thinking in terms of managerial efficiency and open our mind to justice, it must be fair to take into account the income of both parents. It may be true that there are relatively few cases where that matters. The money that the non-resident parent pays is not supposed to go to the parent with care; it is supposed to be a contribution to the upkeep of the children. I understand all that. It is also true that simply considering the income of the parent with care may not adequately assess the costs that are placed on the parent. The parent with care may have to employ expensive child minders, while the non-resident parent does not have that problem.

I understand all that, so I do not think that there is a hard and fast case to make. The evidence leads us to the inescapable conclusion that there will be absurd cases—admittedly, relatively few—when the income of the parent with care is never taken into account. Such cases may be relatively few, but they would be so absurd and lacking in any sort of natural justice that they would call into disrepute the whole system.

We need not follow the argument that someone may win the lottery, become a Spice Girl, or any of the other things that we have talked about today. That is taking the argument to absurd lengths. In the modern world, many parents with care will have substantially greater incomes than non-resident parents. Although the Spice Girl case is very rare, if we have a hard and fast rule that we should never address the problem, we shall be building up possible resentment. There will be non resident parents who have a reasonable income, but because they know that the CSA never takes into account the income of the parent with care, their general view of the fairness of the system will be affected. If there were a mechanism by which the income of the parent with care could be taken into account, which may be triggered only in certain circumstances, that would increase confidence in the system. People would be confident that the system examined both parents' incomes.
