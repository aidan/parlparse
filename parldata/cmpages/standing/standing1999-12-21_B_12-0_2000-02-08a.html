Freedom of Information Bill

Clause 48

Recommendations as to discretionary disclosure

10.30 am

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 159, in page 26, line 7, at beginning insert—

    `(A1).—Whether on application from a public authority or from any person who has made a request for information to that authority the Commissioner may review any decision made by the public authority under section 13.'.

One of the idiosyncrasies of life on the Committee Corridor is that, although hon. Members may be shuffled into other jobs, ceasing to perform one set of duties and beginning anew another set almost instantaneously, that procedure does not extend to Standing Committees. I am delighted still to be in the Committee under your chairmanship, Dr. Clark, not least because we have reached one of the most important parts of the Bill—the enforcement provisions. Having reached this late stage, I hope to be able to persuade the Under-Secretary to see the sense of the argument that will be paraded by me and by other members of the Committee in respect of the provisions.

I do not wish to be uncharitable, but the current provisions are a bit of a dog's breakfast. Admittedly, on the Richter scale of dog's breakfasts, it is by no means the worst that one has come across.

Mr. Ian Cawsey (Brigg and Goole): Small bones.

Mr. Greenway: Yes, small bones. Nevertheless, individual members of the public, and campaigning groups, will have to use the provisions to press for information that public authorities are reluctant to release. It is crucial that we debate the matter thoroughly, notwithstanding our general desire to make progress.

Clause 48 is the first clause to relate to enforcement. The amendment is, in one sense, a probing amendment, intended to find out what the Government think the provisions mean, but it also has substance. Neither the reason why the clause appears in part III instead of in part IV, nor the way in which the mechanism to provide for a commissioner to issue a discretionary disclosure recommendation under clause 13 may be triggered, is clear.

Under clause 50, a complainant may apply to the commissioner for a decision. It seems odd to have the clause proposing the mechanism for a discretionary disclosure decision—which would follow a review of a public authority's refusal to provide information—two clauses ahead of clause 50. In fairness, clause 48 was inserted during the period between the appearance of the original draft Bill and public scrutiny, and the eventual publication of the Bill before Parliament. The Under-Secretary will, no doubt, tell us why the clause is in the right place. However, because of its positioning, we are confused about the way in which the commissioner might have a review.

At the heart of our amendment is a second question. From reading the clause, it would appear that not only the applicant might trigger the mechanism. Subsection (3) provides for what should happen when

    ``the Commissioner gives a discretionary disclosure recommendation in a case where an application has been made to him under section 50''.

That seems to suggest that the Government intend that someone other than the person who is looking for the information might seek a decision from the commissioner.

Is it intended that a public authority could apply to, or seek advice from, the commissioner, because of uncertainty about whether, under clause 13, it should withhold or publish information? We should welcome that, and unlike some campaigning groups we have not suggested removing clause 48 from the Bill. The general concern about the commissioner's being able only to make a recommendation—a debate that we shall reach this morning—does not mean that there is anything wrong with a mechanism open to a public authority; in fact, there is everything to be desired in it. That might not be a Government Department but perhaps part of the quangocracy dealt with in the Bill, such as a body dealing with health or medical services. There might be uncertainty in such a body about its powers and it might not have access to the finest Treasury legal advice. It might well want advice from the commissioner.

The amendment is intended to establish whether we are right about the Government's intention, and I acknowledge the nods and winks from the Under-Secretary. More importantly, we need to clarify whether the review procedure could be triggered not just by an applicant but by a public authority. I shall not stray on to the argument about why the commissioner's powers should be greater. If the Under-Secretary and his officials have studied the train of thought behind the amendments and new clause 1, they will understand that we want to clarify whether the commissioner would provide a way to review, and make a recommendation on, whether a disclosure should be made. We might need to discuss further powers of review and enforcement for the commissioner and even the tribunal, should the recommendation not be followed.

Mr. O'Brien: The hon. Gentleman said that the amendment sought to probe the Government's intentions. There is not much distance between us on a number of the issues that it raises, so I hope to reassure him.

We decided that the clause should be included in part III rather than part IV of the Bill because it does not deal with an enforcement function. The commissioner will be able to review any issue put to her, and many requests for consideration will be made merely for clarification. Arguably, the clause could have featured in part III or part IV, but we felt that part III was more appropriate for the sake of clarity.

The commissioner will have power to review decisions taken by the authority pursuant to clause 13. I confirm that such a review can be initiated either by the applicant or by the authority. The hon. Member for Ryedale said that a public authority could be unclear about the meaning of the legislation or about the circumstances that apply to a request. Such an authority could initiate an application under the clause in order to obtain clarification from the commissioner, who would make the decision more straightforward.

An applicant can complain under clause 50 in order to prompt a recommendation under clause 48, but such a complaint is not required to prompt a recommendation. The commissioner could reach a decision under clause 48 on her own initiative or on the prompting of any other person. A third party could therefore seek such an indication from her. If an application obviously relates to an issue about which a public authority is unclear, it can seek the commissioner's assistance at any stage, so it will not have to wait until it has made a decision or is about to make a decision.

Those are the intentions of the clause. The hon. Member for Ryedale said that the amendment sought to probe, and I hope that I have reassured him on his concerns. If the amendment were adopted, however, the clause would be less clear. It allows the commissioner to review a decision under clause 13 on her own initiative or at the request of a third party as well as at the request of an applicant. The amendment could raise questions about that autonomy and put in doubt whether a review must be conducted at the request only of the authority or the applicant. Perhaps that is what the hon. Gentleman intends, but I am not entirely clear about whether he wants a third party to be able to request clarification from the Information Commissioner. Our view is that it should be straightforward for the commissioner to accept a request made under the clause, provided that she thinks that clarification is necessary.

Mr. Greenway: The Under-Secretary has confirmed the Government's position. I hear what he says about why the provision is included in part III and agree that that confirms that it deals with a reviewing process. However, his remarks lead neatly into the Opposition's next concerns about unclear intentions in the clause, which will be best dealt with during consideration of subsequent amendments. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

    ``and has not done so'',

has triggered the concern that the clause is part of the enforcement process. When reviewing a decision not to release information that was taken in respect of clause 13 provisions, the commissioner might decide that she can make a recommendation only about release. It is important to consider what would happen if the public authority chose to ignore the commissioner's recommendation. In light of what the Under-Secretary said about amendment No. 159, we are right to suggest that the clause needs some attention if it is to have the intended effect.

 

Mr. O'Brien: Clause 48 enables the Information Commissioner to take action when she believes that a public authority should have disclosed information or informed an applicant that it holds information under clause 13 provisions. The commissioner may issue a discretionary disclosure recommendation in writing, specifying information that should be disclosed.

The effect of the amendments would be to enable the commissioner to issue recommendations specifying information that should be disclosed, without having to wait for the authority to make a decision under clause 13. In effect, that would mean that the Information Commissioner could try to pre-empt an authority when a decision had not yet been made and the authority had not yet made a request. To allow the commissioner to issue a recommendation about what information should be disclosed under the discretionary disclosure procedure before a public authority has even considered whether the information should be disclosed would be to put the cart before the horse. The commissioner's role is to review decisions and recommend disclosure. An authority will then have to consider the recommendation and decide whether to follow it. That does not mean that an authority may not seek advice from the commissioner before then, just that the formal recommendation procedure may not be invoked until after the decision under clause 13 has been made.

There are two issues here. The first is whether the public authority can ask the commissioner to clarify its obligations, and the answer is yes. The second is whether a third party or an applicant can go to the commissioner and say, ``Before the public authority has made its decision, I want you to make a recommendation on what that decision should be.'' That would create an entirely different situation, so that an applicant would be able to prejudge the decision of the public authority, and the decision would, in theory, be not that of the public authority but that of the Information Commissioner. That is not what we want. There will be an option to apply to the Information Commissioner just to seek advice, if that is appropriate, which, I believe, will mainly be used by public authorities. However, if a decision is made with which an applicant does not agree, that applicant will be able to seek a discretionary disclosure recommendation from the commissioner, and it will then be up to the public authority to determine how to react to that recommendation.

Mr. Greenway: So that we can be clear, is the Under-Secretary saying that, if a public authority were to go to the commissioner and say, ``We are not quite sure what to do here. We would like your advice.'', and if the commissioner were then to advise the authority to release the information, by implication, the public authority would form the view that, if it did not release the information, it would be likely—even without an applicant making an application—that the commissioner would issue a discretionary disclosure notice if the decision then goes against the release of the information?

Mr. O'Brien: For entirely different reasons, a decision might be made not to disclose, so the public authority might have to ask whether it could interpret the law in a particular way with regard to the information that it was being asked to disclose. It may then seek a view from the Information Commissioner on that point, and the commissioner would give a view. The public authority would then examine the request for information and may conclude, on a different point, not to disclose the information. That is still a matter for the authority, but it may be that, having received a discretionary disclosure recommendation under the clause, the authority would then decide not to release the information on a totally different basis.

We do not want to put the cart before the horse. Our aim is to ensure that the process operates with maximum flexibility and enables a public authority to make a request to the Information Commissioner. It might even mean that a third party will seek advice on a particular point, if that is appropriate, but not a formal recommendation, until the public authority has made a decision, but at that point, a formal recommendation may be made.

Mr. Mark Fisher (Stoke-on-Trent, Central): The example given by my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of a public authority trying to decide whether to use its discretion seemed to rest on a technicality, but surely he would accept that the occasions on which an authority's decision not to disclose will be contentious will be when disclosure would embarrass that authority because it has opened itself up to public criticism. Does he think that it is satisfactory that an authority should have the right to judge itself in that way?

Mr. O'Brien: I am not sure how my hon. Friend's point relates to the issue that we are discussing. He makes a broader point, which he may wish to address later, but I am unsure how it relates to the amendment. We are dealing with the issue of whether the Information Commissioner may prejudge the decision of a public authority. My answer is clear; no, the Information Commissioner does not prejudge, although she may offer advice on a narrow point.

In considering whether to disclose a piece of information, several issues may arise. It will be for the public authority to determine whether it should disclose the information under the right to know that the Bill will create. If it decides not to disclose the information, it will be for the applicant or third party or, indeed, for the public authority—although I suspect that a public authority would do so before the decision is made—to decide whether to refer the matter for a discretionary disclosure recommendation. The procedure seems to be straightforward. It merely creates a procedural device to enable everyone to receive information about what they should be doing. The Information Commissioner may initiate a decision, if she thinks it appropriate, but I suspect that she will be busy making all the other decisions that she must make. If a third party, an applicant or a public authority wants to seek her intervention, the clause provides for that.

The amendment would complicate the matter because it does not make it clear how the commissioner would determine which cases are suitable for a recommendation. When a recommendation is made after the authority has made its decision, the position is clear. The commissioner is likely to exercise the power to make a recommendation if she disagrees with the decision taken, but if she is to act before that decision, how is she to know in which cases to make a recommendation? The commissioner would be forced to guess which decisions would go against the applicant under clause 13. That does not strike me as being a sensible use of the valuable resources of the Information Commissioner. We do not want to create an Alice in Wonderland world, in which judgments should be reached and recommendations issued in a void because an initial decision has not been made. If the freedom of information regime is to have credibility, it is important that actions, decisions and recommendations should be based on an objective and informed assessment of individual cases.

We should not lose sight of the fact that the public authority is the primary decision maker. The commissioner can issue a recommendation if she feels that an authority has made a wrong decision. Allowing her to issue a recommendation on disclosure before the authority has made a decision would confuse the commissioner's role as a reviewer of decisions with that of a maker of decisions. In view of those difficulties, I invite the hon. Member for Ryedale to withdraw the amendment.

Clause 50

Application for decision by Commissioner

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 5, in page 26, line 30, at end insert—

    `(1A) The complainant may also apply to the Commissioner for a decision in cases where a public authority has not complied with a discretionary disclosure recommendation given under section 48.'.

It is difficult to discuss the issues in isolation because the amendment is part of a scheme of enforcement procedure including amendments Nos. 6, 7, 8 and 9 and new clause 1, to which we shall come. Our narrowest point regards the Government's insistence on having the commissioner recommend whether a public authority should release, or should have released, information. The previous debate was important because, notwithstanding the possibility of persuading the Government to include a power for the commissioner or the tribunal to require the release of information, there was at least the sensible option of a review procedure to make a recommendation.

What would happen if the public authority ignored the recommendation and decided not to release the information? The question has caused concern because the power of decision currently rests not with the commissioner or the tribunal as independent arbiters but with the public authority, the Minister or one of the quangos listed. That is not good enough. A public authority's decision not to release information but to exercise its discretion to withhold it under clause 13 might be reviewed. What would happen if the public authority ignored a recommendation made by the commissioner? The amendment is intended to clarify whether the applicant would be able to ask for the matter to be considered again. At present the alternative under the Bill would be for the applicant to obtain through the courts some judicial review of the public authority's decision to ignore the recommendation.

I anticipate that the Under-Secretary will say that no Minister likes a ruling from a parliamentary ombudsman that he and his Department are guilty of malpractice or maladministration and that, equally, no Minister would feel comfortable ignoring a recommendation to release information. In reality, that would be seen as the lesser of two evils of embarrassment. The Minister concerned might be less embarrassed to face media wrath at his ignoring a recommendation than he would be to release the information, supposing that that would involve huge embarrassment to those with the highest authority in government.

Our aim, as a prelude to subsequent amendments, is to argue that if the Government believe there is merit in a recommendation procedure—notwithstanding the possibility of improvements to the powers under the Bill for requiring the release of information—the Bill should make it clear that an applicant could challenge, through the commissioner and, eventually, the tribunal, a public authority's decision not to follow a recommendation.

The kernel of the matter concerns the question whether, as amendments Nos. 6, 7, 61 and 62 would provide, the commissioner should have the power to require the release of information. In that case, the Minister would have to decide whether to include clause 48 at all. That is why our earlier discussion was so important. We see sense in retaining clause 48 as a prelude to an ultimate requirement to release information, whereas we all know that it has been included instead of such a requirement.

Clause 48, if it remains, gives rise to a need for a review mechanism, involving first the commissioner and ultimately the tribunal, for decisions not to abide by recommendations. That process should provide for a requirement to release the information, if appropriate. We should bear it in mind that if that stage were reached, a commissioner would have recommended the release of information and the public authority would have refused. A second opportunity would then be given for the public authority to demonstrate its reason. That would create a significant additional pressure to favour the release of information over secrecy.

The fact that a public authority declines to abide by a recommendation that is made under clause 48 should not be the end of the matter. The Bill should go further. The first step would be to extend the powers that require a decision to be reviewed by the commissioner to include a decision not to abide by a recommendation. The Under-Secretary made it clear that clause 50 triggers clause 48. An applicant could say to the commissioner, ``I wanted this information. They haven't provided it. Please will you review that decision?'' What happens if the commissioner finds that the public authority was wrong to use its discretion under clause 13 and makes a recommendation that is ignored? There should be a further process. I hope that that is the Government's intention.
