Postal Services Bill

Clause 5

Other duties of the Commission in the consumer interest

9 am

Mr. Alan Duncan (Rutland and Melton): I beg to move amendment No. 49, in page 3, line 35, at end insert—

    `(e) individuals who are homeless'.

To some Labour Members, it may seem curious that I, of all people, am to be cast in the role of a caring Conservative but, as one of the most misunderstood politicians in British political history, this is my moment to prove that I am not who they think I am. Serious intent lies behind the amendment. It would add much to the social purpose of the Bill, which the Government regard as a high priority. I hope that the proposal is consistent not only with what purports to be their policy but with the common decency that crosses party lines when it really matters.

Under clause 5, the Commission is instructed to exercise certain functions in certain areas, which I think is sensible given that the Government want to inject a serious social and environmental purpose into the way in which the Post Office is constructed and how the Commission goes about its duty in overseeing and regulating it. The Bill must take into account individuals who are disabled and chronically sick.

It will be of great importance to people who are not mobile and who need access to a post office, as it will be to pensioners. Similarly, we all know that the ability to collect a pension is very much at the root of the debate about the rural or, as I prefer to call it, the community Post Office network. The clause also refers to ``individuals with low income'' and allied to the groups to which I have referred are ``individuals residing in rural areas''.

My amendment would add a fifth category, ``individuals who are homeless''. Someone who is just glossing over such matters might think that the amendment was a little silly in that it is impossible to deliver a letter to someone without an address. ``You can't put a post code on a cardboard box,'' someone has quipped to me. There are many other practical ways, however, of including those whose plight is homelessness. A proper system of communication can assist the exclusion that the homeless face.

Homelessness is not a small issue. When the Labour party was in opposition, it made serious comments about it, as did we, in opposition and, indeed, in government. I am sorry to say that the number of homeless people is growing. The figures for the fourth quarter of 1998 to the fourth quarter of 1999 show that there were about 105,900 homeless people. That is a higher figure than when the Conservative party was in office, which illustrates that the scale of the problem is growing, not declining. It is a high priority for the Prime Minister to do something about it. He said that Labour would

    do everything in our power to end the scandal of homelessness, to tackle the spectacle of people sleeping rough on the streets and to end the waste of families sleeping in bed and breakfast accommodation.

That is not happening.

Above all, the plight of the homeless rings true in the words ``of no fixed address''. For someone who is in trouble, rootless and unable to insinuate himself back into society and a stable life, being of no fixed address is a great problem. Including such people in a proper system of communication that enables them to establish a recognisable record of consistency in their habits and show that they can be reliably contacted and contact other people, can only be of benefit.

The backpacker travelling around France has access to a well-established system through which people can send a letter, or even a cheque or money order, to a post office. The letter has ``poste restante'' written on it and remains at the post office until it is collected by the person backpacking through France or tasting the wines of the Loire.

Such a system may exist in Britain, although I do not often hear about it. The Minister may know better than me. If it does, we should make more of it and include it in the Bill. The Post Office already takes account of people of pensionable age, people on low incomes and people who live in rural areas. If those categories can be specified in the Bill, why not specify the homeless, too?

I advocate a much better established and recognised system that includes homeless people and enables them to be contacted for the purpose of collecting benefits, for example. If they have a reliable pattern of movement and can be reliably contacted, that can only help in tackling fraud, which is an issue about which the Government feel strongly. Enabling such people to establish a well-documented and well understood reliable link would help their job prospects.

Homelessness is an evil that should not exist in a modern society, but it always will. The poor will always be with us, and so will the homeless, but we can do our little bit this morning to help them by including them in the Bill as a category of people to be included in the Post Office network and the system of communication, the nature of which may change dramatically over the next few years. Homeless people may be able to have an e-mail address, which, because costs are falling, they could use despite their poverty. It would not be a geographical address, to which I am mainly referring this morning, but it would be a point of contact, and the service could be provided through the Post Office network.

The ability to receive and send physical deliveries via a recognised route for homeless people would be of great value. I urge the Minister seriously to consider this simple amendment. It is well intentioned; I do not want to score any political points. I would like it to be accepted so that in looking to the future we can add to the purpose and effectiveness of the social exclusion policies that the Government are genuinely trying to implement.

Mr. Duncan: It is pity that the best part of three years into office, and having had 18 years to think about it, the Government do not have these criteria properly defined and ready for us to study. If they thought that they were so much better, they could have taken time out to design the document that we are champing at the bit to read. We will not oppose this but we express sadness that the real detail and meat of the clause remain unavailable to the Committee as we scrutinise the Bill.

Mr. Johnson: Well, I have been called worse things than tantalising in my time. To a certain extent all Bills have hope rather than substance. Clause 32 talks about a commission that has not been established and that cannot be established until the Bill receives Royal Assent. We are talking about a council for the consumer that has not been established yet and about licence holders who have not had an opportunity to apply for their licences. The access criteria will be in the final guidelines, but the guidelines can only be in draft at the moment because until the Bill has received Royal Assent, we cannot go out to consultation.

The hon. Member for Rutland and Melton pointed out some wording in the draft guidance and it is as well that he did. He has up until 31 July to comment and that will obviously be taken into account. He said that the PIU study was ready and in a garret somewhere. I can assure him that it is not. I obviously have to meet the PIU study group from time to time as it undertakes its work. If anyone is desperate to get the information out and into the public domain, we are. We would like it to inform the debate in Committee, but the PIU study is very comprehensive and will be completed in due course.

Finally, in his usual entertaining way the hon. Gentleman asked why we have waited nearly three years to do this. In 18 years in government, the Tories failed to implement a manifesto commitment made in 1992 to introduce an independent regulator. They failed to act on their Green Paper. The hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire was the DTI Minister responsible for the Post Office. In the two years from 1995 to 1997, they did nothing. Once they had decided that they could not privatise the Post Office, they tried to plunder it.

This study was commissioned as a result of the concerns when the benefit payment card went belly up. We would have liked that benefit payment card project to succeed. It was a well-intentioned project. The private finance initiative project that launched it was, as the Trade and Industry Committee said, blighted from the start. It was a problem before we came into government. We rescued it and put Horizon back on the platform—[Laughter.] We put it back on the tracks. We could not get down to the nuts of bolts of what is in the PIU study until recently. With those few, tantalising remarks, I ask the Committee to accept that clause 32 stand part of the Bill.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 32, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.
