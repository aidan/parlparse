Mr. Eddie McGrady (South Down): I beg to move amendment No. 291, in page 33, line 40, at end insert-

    `(1A) Save in Part IV and section 62 of this Act and in section 51 of the 1998 Act, in the Police Acts ``efficiency and effectiveness'' includes impartiality and having regard to international human rights standards on policing.'.

We have all argued that the policing service in Northern Ireland should be efficient and effective, but we would like to ensure that it is also impartial. It is especially important to emphasise impartiality in the Bill in the context of our divided society and its divisions on policing. The inclusion of such criteria will enable those who are served by the police-the public-to make a proper judgment and assessment. Indeed, it would be useful for the police to assess their own performance through a power to judge efficiency, effectiveness, impartiality and regard to international human rights standards. The amendment would add the word ``impartiality'' in most places where efficiency and effectiveness are mentioned. Clause 2(2) is a good example of a provision into which the term ``impartiality'' should be inserted, as it deals with the general function of the Policing Board in relation to the police, support staff and traffic wardens.

I accept that the term ``impartiality'' is not applicable or does not make sense in some circumstances. Having considered the matter, we accept that it would not be appropriate to make changes such as those that are proposed in part IV, which deals with efficiency and effectiveness provisions in relation to the Policing Board, or in section 62 of the Police (Northern Ireland) Act 1998, which makes the Secretary of State responsible for ensuring ``efficiency and effectiveness''. In addition, such an amendment would not be appropriate in section 51 of the 1998 Act, which relates to the police ombudsman.

I emphasise the phrase

    having regard to international human rights.

because it is most important that such standards should be incorporated into Northern Ireland domestic law. They have not been, but they certainly should be incorporated into police law.

Many people have expressed concern that the introduction of those matters would give rise to court proceedings and so on, but we doubt that it will. The relevant words of the amendment are:

    having regard to international human rights standards on policing.

The best counsel that I have received indicates that it would not lead to an enormous amount of litigation, but it would enable the police service to take measures to apply appropriate international standards.

There may be some surprise at this, but I refer the Committee to the implementation plan and to recommendation 4, under which the Chief Constable instructs his officers about the implications of the Human Rights Act 1998 and

    the wider context of the European Convention on Human Rights and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights.

The UN declaration is not part of domestic law, but the Chief Constable will be instructing his officers, in the wider context, about its implications. It is a minor but important step to apply that principle generally, so that all the policing agencies have regard to international human rights standards on policing. That would place human rights at the centre of the police service. It is a reasonable, proper and important amendment.

New clause 1 concerns the naming of the new police service in Northern Ireland. It may have been inevitable, but it has certainly been unfortunate, that the publicity and media attention about recent change in Northern Ireland have concentrated almost entirely on the name of the police service. I do not want to deny or diminish the importance of that issue, but it has clouded the reality that the great majority of Patten's recommendations on structural change were what most of the population wanted. The failure fully to recognise or embrace that fact has enabled those who want to rewrite or remake Patten to escape from the criticisms and requests for justification of their attitudes by concentrating their anti-Patten propaganda on what is, properly, an emotive issue-the RUC name.

The structural changes are, in our mind, not a threat but an opportunity for a new beginning of a new service for all-not an old service for some. I still fear, because of the attitude that has been taken to many parts of the Patten report, which have been omitted or amended beyond recognition, that there will be difficulty over it. In concentrating on the issue of the name, an attempt has been made to remake Patten in many ways. It will be a terrible pity if this opportunity to set up a comprehensive new police service is missed. It could mean missed opportunities for police and citizens.

I must speak cautiously: the issue of the police service raises the deepest emotions and has been used opportunistically by some. Furthermore, many of those who most value the RUC and many of those who feel connected with it through family, by suffering and grievous loss, feel that to change the name is to deny or diminish all the accumulated pain suffered over the 30 years. My intention in commending the new clause is not to do that.

What informs my party's approach, and the purpose of the new clause, is the desire to learn from the pain that has been suffered. Aeschylus, the Greek philosopher, said:

    In our sleep, pain, that cannot forget, falls drop by drop upon the heart, and in our own time, against our will, comes wisdom through the awful grace of God.

After the pain of the past 30 years, I hope that wisdom has come from the lessons learnt. We who live in the north of Ireland can see a lot of it all around us-not all the time, and perhaps not so much in the headlines of the past week, but generally, we perceive a thrust forward in learning from past mistakes and sufferings. We have learnt the wisdom of negotiation, the wisdom of entering Government, the wisdom to end violence and, I hope and pray, the wisdom to change the policing service. I think that that has been accepted, if not agreed. People, when asked, may say that they do not agree with change, but accept it, because of the importance of what it will give. A growing number of people in the communities and in the police know that change, both symbolic and structural, is needed and that the police should be known by a name other than the RUC.

The international commission's report outlines the arguments for the change and states:

    Many people from the Irish Nationalist and republican traditions regard the name of the RUC as associating the police with the British constitution and state. This contributes to a perception that the police are not their police. On the other hand, many people in Northern Ireland from the Unionist tradition consider it perfectly natural that a service provided and funded by the state should signal its provenance. The problem is that the name of the RUC has become politicised In Northern Ireland, where the constitutional aspirations of the inhabitants conflict, the use of words to associate the police with one side of the constitutional argument must inevitably go some way to inhibiting the whole hearted participation in policing of those who espouse the other side of that argument.

The report was specific. It said that the new beginning for policing

    cannot be achieved unless the reality that part of the community feels unable to identify with the present name

is addressed. It explained:

    Our proposals seek to achieve a situation in which people can all regard the police service as their own,

and went on to say,

    While we have not accepted the argument that the Royal Ulster Constabulary should be disbanded, it should henceforth be named the Northern Ireland Police Service.

That is the argument advanced by Patten. It is unambiguous and unqualified. That is the reasonable Patten-compelling, clear and certain. The Government should be equally clear, certain and unambiguous. We accept the Government's preferred name-the Police Service of Northern Ireland-but we can accept no less than that.

I have outlined the principles that informed our decisions and the Patten report, but what are the politics of the issue? What does the name Police Service of Northern Ireland say to my community and the wider community of Northern Ireland? It says that the police service, for all its individual diversity, needs to be corporately neutral, especially in a divided society in which the division has developed to the point of conflict and beyond. It says to me that the police service needs to be symbolically and substantially impartial and independent, with a requirement to uphold the law and human rights. It is an invitation to my community to give its allegiance and offer its membership. It is a declaration of intent that the practice of the police force of the past, some of which heavily and happily identified with the constitutional position of the Union and with the political practice of Unionism, is not how a police service of the future should conduct its affairs. It says that this police service is in the ownership of all and not just the majority.

I want to take the politics of the issue further. I know that many people who defend the name Royal Ulster Constabulary do so genuinely-I have said that before-but it is wrong and irresponsible aggressively to position the issue of the name in such a way as to fail to address the wider concerns of the nationalists or to recognise our strong argument and the need for principled change. By concentrating on that one issue, it is possible to avoid tackling the need for a broader and deeper change in policing and related issues.

Amendment No. 156 and new clause 9 are in the same group. My remarks relate mainly to new clause 9 and incidentally to amendment No. 156, so I shall detain the Committee but once on the two measures.

Patten's proposals to rid the Royal Ulster Constabulary of its proud name and cap badge are the most controversial of the entire report. They are wholly without justification and have caused significant hurt and anger. As the Chief Constable of the RUC, Sir Ronnie Flanagan, said:

    No-one should underestimate the great hurt that altering the title of the RUC will cause to my members, to past members, to injured members, to their families and to bereaved families. I understand the feelings of my members because I share those feelings.

We profoundly disagree with the assertion that the police service should be entirely free of any association with the symbols of the state, especially when acceptance of the Belfast agreement means acceptance of the legitimacy of Northern Ireland's constitutional status as part of the United Kingdom.

As the Northern Ireland Affairs Committee put it in its report on the ``Composition, Recruitment and Training of the RUC'' in 1998:

    Until there is a change in the status of Northern Ireland, inevitably many of the symbols of Government will be British.

The name of the Royal Ulster Constabulary is one of which those who have served in the force are justly proud. It is a name granted by royal charter. The Patten report offers no convincing evidence to support scrapping the royal title, because no such evidence exists. As one of the police authority's most recent surveys concluded, it

    will cause major offence in the Protestant community but will not lead to significant improvements in support for the Police among Catholics.

That fear was echoed recently by the Chief Constable:

    If we are to endure this great hurt proposed then I hope the gains envisaged are demonstrable and achievable. I don't see the concrete evidence that that will be so.

In other words, the proposal is all pain with very little gain.

Why abolish the name, when all the evidence suggests that it is not the overwhelming problem that critics of the force claim? As an opinion poll in the Belfast Telegraph in April showed, 61 per cent. of Catholics in Northern Ireland had no difficulty with the name of the RUC. When the Northern Ireland Affairs Committee examined the issue two years ago, it recommended that the name be kept. As it convincingly argued:

    There is no clear reason to make a special case for the RUC

by changing its name, without changing the names of other organisations which are also either ``Royal'' or ``British''. The official symbols associated with the force are not central to the status of the RUC in the eyes of the community. They attract the loyalty of many in Northern Ireland. There is no good reason to change them.

The inescapable conclusion is that the decision to drop the royal title is intended to appeal to a minority of a minority who have never supported the police and who, in all honesty, are unlikely ever to support any police force other than one that they themselves control.
