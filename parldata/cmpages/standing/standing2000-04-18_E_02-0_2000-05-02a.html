Mr. Greenway: I hope, Mr Illsley, that you had an enjoyable and relaxing Easter break-it was all too short. I hope that we can make progress on the Bill today.

Amendment No. 4 stands in my name and that of my hon. Friend the Member for East Surrey (Mr. Ainsworth). The amendments in the group are probing amendments, but they raise important issues, in particular, that of the way in which the social security information that is to be provided will be determined. In the Bill, the definition of such information is widely drawn and is subject to secondary legislation that will prescribe the information that is to be made available to the BBC and its contractors.

That is a sensitive matter. As has been pointed out before, the information will not go only to the BBC-if, indeed, it goes to the BBC at all; it will go to the companies that supervise both the issuing of licences and the granting of free licences. It is not unreasonable to seek reassurance as to how the information will be determined, and an assurance that its quantity and type will be strictly curtailed. That goes to the heart of the concerns that were expressed on Second Reading about supplying social security information in the way set out in the Bill, which is a significant departure from normal practice.

The Minister for Tourism, Film and Broadcasting will recall that, on Second Reading, it was suggested that the information to be supplied should be specified in the Bill. Amendments Nos. 4 and 5 are designed to achieve that. Encouragement for such an approach comes from the notes on clauses. It would be helpful for the Committee to have them to hand, as they specify the information that the Government intend should be released. The Committee must ask the Minister why the Government chose not to specify in the Bill the quality and type of information that is to be made available, and why they decided instead to set out a wide definition that is subject to an order-making power. If the information were specified as amendments Nos. 4 and 5 propose, there would be no need for an order-making power; that is why amendment No. 14, which relates to clause 4, fits into the debate. Other issues relate to the order-making power, and I hope to raise them during clauses 4 and 5 stand part. However, amendment No. 14 is of interest in the context of the current debate only because, if the information to be released were specified in the Bill, there would be no need for an order-making power.

The Opposition are prepared to accept that the Government are betraying no sinister motive or unexpected intention in choosing to deal with the issue in the way that they have chosen. I expect the Minister to say that the Government are attempting to cover every eventuality, and that we should bear in mind that there might, in future, be unforeseen changes in the type of social security information that is kept and the way in which it can be used to identify the over-74 population. However, such matters could be addressed by an order to vary the information specified in the Bill. It would not require the wide powers that clauses 4 and 5 provide.

We want the Minister to give the Committee and the House the explanation we deserve. Why have the Government chosen the process they have when it might have been preferable to specify the information in the Bill, given that the latter option might have reassured those who were concerned about the release of such information?

The Minister for Tourism, Film and Broadcasting (Janet Anderson): I repeat the hope expressed by the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) that you had a pleasant Easter break, Mr. Illsley. Like him, I wish that it had been longer, but at least another break is coming up soon.

Janet Anderson: We have had extensive discussions with both the BBC and the Department of Social Security about the Bill, so I cannot accept the hon. Gentleman's assertion that they have not been sufficiently consulted. Neither can I accept his allegation that the Government have acted out of political expediency. It is important to note that the list of information that the amendment would put on the face of the Bill is incomplete. It omits the name of the person and the fact, where appropriate, that the person named has died. Both peices of information are vital to the verification of claims for free licneces. In addition, the proposed wording relating to the information that may lawfully be passed to the BBC is less precise than we intend.

I cannot accept the assertion made by the hon. Member for Ryedale that the definition of the information is loosely drawn in the Bill. The amendment mentions information ``about'' the age, address, date of birth and national insurance number of persons. We intend that the relevant order should provide that the information in question should ``consist of'' the age, address and so on. That is far more specific. The DSS would, under the terms of the amendment, be able to release information about, for example, what type of property was involved, if they held such information,

    as as result, or for the purpose, of the exercise of functions in relation to social security or war pension.

That would be ``about'' the address, but would not ``consist of'' the address. Surely, it is better to have tight and restrictive wording in an order than to have a looser formulation in the Bill.

The hon. Gentleman referred to amendment No. 14 and said that it is consequential to amendments Nos. 4 and 5. If, as the Government propose, the information that can be transferred to the BBC is to be specified by order, the definition of the term ``prescribed'' must remain in clause 4. It is required for the purposes of defining information of a prescribed description in clause 1-in other words, the information that the DSS may supply. However, even if the information that the DSS may supply to the BBC were specified on the face of the Bill, a definition of the term ``prescribed'' would still be required in clause 4 to give meaning to ``prescribed'' categories of reduced fee licences referred to in clause 2(2)(b), in connection with which the information may be used.

I hope that I have been able to reassure the Committee that the Government's intentions are genuine. We believe that we have set out the best way in which to administer the scheme in order to ensure that all pensioners who are entitled to a free television licence can get one easily and with as little trouble as possible when the provision is implemented on 1 November. I ask the hon. Member for Ryedale to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Greenway: These are probing amendments, which provide a peg for a debate about the offences relating to wrongful disclosure of information. The clause provides much-needed reassurance that the provisions of clauses 1 and 2 will be strictly applied. We seek further clarification as to the persons and bodies to which social security information will be supplied, because those bodies will be liable if information is wrongly supplied or used in ways that are beyond the scope of the Bill.

Clause 1 makes it clear that only the BBC-or, more likely, the BBC's contractors, who supervise TV licence fee collection-will receive the information supplied. Clause 3, however, in creating important offences in respect of the misuse of the information, introduces the concept of ``a recipient''. We appreciate that it is mainly a drafting issue to decide exactly who will be liable for the offences.

Our amendments are offered in the spirit of helpfulness. As I said, we understand that only the BBC and its contractors or staff will have the information, but instead of ``the recipient'', the words ``the BBC''-clearly defined in clause 1 to include all the relevant contractors-would be more appropriate. We also want to focus attention on the importance of supervision by the BBC and contracting companies at the most senior level in respect of the need for proper security about the information.

Another important matter is that the current wording-``the recipient''-it does not make it clear exactly who is liable. Who would be prosecuted for an offence under clause 3? Is the recipient the clerk or the administrative assistant who might inadvertently have released the information to the wrong source-we shall return to that theme in connection with amendments Nos. 6 and 7-or is the BBC as a corporation the recipient? Surely the corporation should be made to answer for any inappropriate use. Contractor companies will receive information regarding the supply of free television licences; that, too, raises the question of who would be prosecuted.

The Royal Parks (Trading) Bill, which is awaiting Report, refers to individual directors being liable to prosecution for offences committed under that measure. The Committee must consider whether the definition of ``a recipient'' makes it sufficiently clear that the directors of the companies that supervise the issuing of licences will themselves be liable for the wrongful use of information.

The Committee has expressed concerned about whether the definition of a recipient relates to an individual or a company, and about vetting contractors, The purpose of clause 3 is to protect information supplied to the BBC by the DSS against further disclosure. By ruling out employees of contractors, amendments Nos. 8 to 11 would narrow the range of persons who could be guilty of an offence if they disclosed information. As drafted, the effect of clause 1(1) and (2) is that DSS information can be supplied either to the BBC itself, or to persons supplying services to the BBC.
