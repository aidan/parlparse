Mr. Clappison: I gather that a frisson of excitement passed through the Committee before the adjournment for lunch. That frisson concerned the prospect of debating defendants' consent to television links. I hope that Committee members have been able to contain their excitement during the short adjournment, for we have now arrived at the anticipated discussion.

Amendment No. 194 would make the use of television links subject to the consent of the accused. It is very similar to amendment No. 422, tabled by the Liberal Democrats. However, their amendment is slightly different, because it would give defendants the absolute right to prevent television links from taking place. We do not go quite as far as that. Our amendment would require defendants to have a good reason for withholding consent and is as sensible as the rest of our amendments. In view of the security considerations that we have already touched on, we think that it is important for defendants to have a good reason to stop television links taking place. Defendants who are security risks cannot facilitate their removal from prison to court for a hearing by witholding their consent, unless they have a good reson for doing so.

As we adverted to earlier, defendants have escaped while in transit and have gone on to commit serious offences. However, that must be weighed against fairness. Provided defendants have a good reason for withholding their consent, they should be able to do so. We wish to explore that issue now. Defendants may be able to show that they would not get a fair hearing through a television link. That brings us back to the issue of access to legal advice. We note that Liberty, from whom we have had a representation, opposes the use of television links unless defendants give their consent and unless court buildings have television link facilities for defendants to have private consultations with their lawyers.

We discussed access to legal advice before lunch, and we have heard about the Government's plans for pilot schemes. I refer to Liberty to show that the two matters are part and parcel of the question of the fairness of proceedings.

I turn the amendments Nos. 195 and 196, which concern another aspect of these of television links. Our amendments require the bench chairmanship committee to give its consent to the arrangements for television links. We think that magistrates should be satisfied with such arrangements before links are deployed.

The Bill does not make provision for magistrates to be consulted about the use of television links. We imagine that the Government intend to have such consultations, and we hope that magistrates' views are taken into account fully. We look forward to the Minister's reassurance on this point.

I deal now with amendment No. 242. Clause 54(3) requires magistrates to give reasons for not using television links when such links are possible. That might put rather too much pressure on magistrates. They share everyone's wish to see justice administered efficiently, but they may have reasons for being unhappy with television links in particular cases or in particular courts and may not be willing to disclose those reasons in public.

We wonder whether subsection (3) takes an element of discretion away from magistrates. This morning the Minister told us how much he values the discretion of magistrates, so we hope that he will retain a little of that consideration this afternoon. In any event, magistrates will be only too willing to take full advantage of the technological opportunities that television links bring, subject to their usual considerations of fairness and the proper administration of justice.

A defendant might want to appear in court for a variety of reasons, each of which need to be addressed by the Government.

The first reason is that it is the defendant's right to be present in the court to hear and see what is going on. That is rather nebulous and it might not be enshrined in statute, but it is a long-standing practice.

Secondly, it is often an occasion for some social contact between the defendant and his family, who might have come to the public gallery to watch the proceedings. To conclude my remarks on the amendments, I apologise to the Minister because I do not know the definition of ``the start of the trial''. We are discussing what can happen ``before the start of the trial'' and, therefore, it is important for us all to know what that means. I jump to the Crown court for that purpose because I assume that the long-term aim is to have a pilot scheme in a Crown court, perhaps even in several Crown courts.

People in the Crown court sometimes get a little uncertain about the meaning of the phrase ``the start of the trial''. There are certain procedural steps in the Crown court. One of the key steps occurs when the arraignment of the prisoner has taken place. Another key step has caused questions to arise in the minds of those in the judiciary and at the Bar; it is that of empanelling or swearing in the jury, which is the moment when the defendant is put in the hands of the jury. Until that moment, the defendant is not in the hands of the jury.

As the Minster will know, the current arrangement is that plea and directions hearings take place at the Crown court. Often, 20 or 30 cases are listed for a particular morning. Defendants are probably arraigned by then, so what I ask next may be irrelevant. However, I feel slightly uneasy if there is a prospect, albeit slight, of defendants not attending their plea and directions hearings. They are normally bailed from the magistrates court to the first plea and directions hearing at the Crown court. The key issue is that, being present at the plea and directions hearing is sometimes the first available opportunity to get in one's guilty plea. It is enshrined in statute that there is credit to be given for an early plea. Counsels say, ``My client's pleading guilty'' at the first available opportunity at the plea and directions hearing. Judges are duty bound to take that into account.

I can see a difficulty if such a scenario arises. I do not fancy the idea of a guilty plea being entertained on the television link. I query with the Government whether such an outcome is possible on a plea and directions hearing. Has the arraignment taken place? Can an arraignment actually take place? Can the defendant be arraigned in his absence? They are key questions, and those issues will need to be studied

If a trial means the empanelling of the jury, we move onto another difficult area. It is not infrequently the case in the Crown court that there are preliminary trials before the trial takes place, which are not held in the presence of the jury. I refer to that regular system described as a voir dire. A voir dire is normally a trial within a trial and, quite often, evidence is called. It usually relates to the admissibility of evidence. If evidence is ruled inadmissible, it does not go in at trial. If it is ruled admissible, it does.

For example, there may be a dispute about whether an indentification parade fell within the correct guidelines or whether an interview should have been allowed as evidence. Let me explain what often happens in the case of these voir dire. The defendant is in court; he has been arraigned, and no jury is empanelled. I stand to be corrected but in my experience, if a jury is not empanelled there will be argument--leading to a trial within a trial at the Crown court on the voir dire. Some of my colleagues may have experience of them taking three, four, five, or six days. If that takes place before the start of a trial, it is even more important for the defendant to be present. Normally voir dires cannot take place with the defendant being present.

I have outlined some concerns. I return to my first words which were that the principle is quite attractive. There can be no doubt about how useful and sensible television links will be at certain times. We all agree about that.

The clause allows for both defence and prosecution to make representations, but leaves the decision whether to grant a hearing using a live television link to the full discretion of the court after it has heard from the parties. The Government believe that our approach strikes the correct balance and that the court is best placed to make the decision. We do not believe that defendants will be prejudiced by use of this technology or, therefore, that a defendant should be able to veto a decison made by the court.

Amendment No. 196 would require those setting up the televison link to seek the prior approval of each bench chairmanship committee to the arrangements and to the use of the technology in its area and formally to consult the local Law Society. The practical arrangements associated with introducing television links into magistrates courts will be tested in the pilots that we propose to undertake. They will be tailored to other courts if the evaluation of the pilots leads to national roll-out.

Subsection (5) spells out the standards that must be met so that the accused is able to communicate properly with the court. We believe that those standards will ensure that a fair hearing can be conducted.

The clause introduces the principle of television links for pre-trial hearings, but the court retains discretion over whether to use the link in a particular case once the approved facilities exist.

Amendment No. 195 which refers to line 43 not line 35, is consequential. It will be unnecessary without the addition of new requirements under subsection (2).

The amendment would make the Bill defective because it would remove the courts' justification for imposing extended sentences. A court cannot be required to extend a sentence when it has no reason to do so. If the court has no indication that a person whom it is convicting for a sexual offence needs a longer period of supervision than normal, it would have no justification for imposing an extended sentence and would have nothing by which to assess how long the extension period should be.

The Government intend that courts should have the power to impose extended sentences only in those cases in which the period of supervision that the offender would normally receive is not considered long enough to prevent him committing further offences and from securing his rehabilitation. The court will decide how long the period of extended supervision should be, based on its assessment of what is necessary for those specified purposes. However, if the court were to be required to pass an extended sentence for no reason other than the fact that the offender is a sex offender, it could not possibly decide how long the extension period should be.

A benchmark is needed to determine the length of an extended sentence. That benchmark is the period that is necessary to prevent re-offending and to secure rehabilitation. Amendment No. 246 would remove that benchmark in the case of sex offenders, leaving the courts with no criteria with which to determine what period of extended supervision would be appropriate.

The length of a custodial sentence is usually determined by what is commensurate with the seriousness of the offence. That is an important principle and courts are required by section 2(2)(a) of the Criminal Justice Act 1991 to abide by it. However, it is recognised that in the case of sexual and violent offenders it may be appropriate for the sentence to be longer than is commensurate with the seriousness of the offence, if the court considers that necessary for the protection of the public. Mr. Mike O'Brien: The new drug treatment and testing order provides a new community order for the courts. That meets our manifesto commitment to take action to break the link between drugs and crime. It will replace and improve on the current provision under schedule 1(a) to the powers of the Criminal Courts Act 1973, which allows courts to make it an additional requirement of a probation order that an offender undergo drug treatment. That provision has been little used and, outside certain isolated examples of good practice, it has manifestly failed to tackle the link between drugs and crime.

The drug treatment and testing order will be a community order. It will sentence the offender to treatment for his drug dependency. The new order will apply to offenders aged 16 or over and will be available to the courts in cases where the offence is neither fixed by law, such as murder, nor carries a mandatory or minimum custodial sentence under the Crime Sentences Act 1997. A community order will, furthermore, take its place in the sentencing framework that was laid down by the Criminal Justice Act 1991 and will, therefore, be combined with other community sentences.

Clause 57 provides that the duration of a drug treatment and testing order may be between six months and three years, which mirrors the length of a probation order and gives scope for a variety of different stages to the order, including relapse prevention counselling.

We envisage that the supervision that the hon. Member for Hertsmere requires could be put in place by means of a probation order that would run alongside the drug treatment testing order. It would be for the courts to satisfy themselves in each case that the offender was dependent on or had a propensity to use drugs, and that treatment would be beneficial and potentially effective. There is no necessity to establish that there is a link between drug misuse and the commission of the offence, which is an important point. That has been one of the major disincentives to using the current provision, to which I referred earlier.

Clause 57 nevertheless provides that the court may order the offender to undergo a drugs test to establish whether he is dependent on or misuses drugs. He can be ordered to undergo the test and, if he is to be the subject of the drug treatment testing order, he is required to give his consent. He may choose not to do so, in which case the court can proceed to deal with him by way of another sentence. If he gives his consent, however, he is likely to be dealt with under the order. To get to that stage, the court might have to order a test.
