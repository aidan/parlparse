Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I beg to move amendment No. 54, in page 2, line 35, at end insert--

    `(9) Subsection (1) does not prohibit agreements between companies with revenues below £10 million per annum, or such greater figure as may be from time to time prescribed by the Secretary of State.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss amendment No. 80, in page 2, line 35, at end insert--

    `(9) Unless the Director determines otherwise, a small agreement as defined in the European Commission's Notice on Agreements of Minor Importance which do not fall under Article 85(1) of the Treaty establishing the European Community is not subject to the Chapter I prohibition.'.

Mr. Boswell: I welcome you, Mr. Atkinson, to the Chair, and to an interesting group of amendments. The amendments concern small businesses. Having run a small business, I can declare to the Committee an interest that I perhaps should have declared earlier, but its turnover never reached anywhere near the ball park figure of £10 million per annum. Indeed, it continues with a turnover smaller by about two orders of magnitude. [Interruption.] The Under-Secretary invites me to expand on that, but he knows that the business is in agriculture and, in the context of the common agricultural policy, he may have second thoughts about that. I hastily return to the substance of our debate.

One of our worries has already been well articulated--I shall not dilate on that now. For the ordinary business--a not particularly complimentary term--a run-of-the-mill business, that is not large or sophisticated and which has the good or ill fortune to employ a significant number of competition lawyers, there may be some difficulties in the Bill. The Under-Secretary, with characteristic modesty, may concede that clause 2 does not leap lightly off the page.

Our first concern is that a large number of ordinary businesses, ranging in turnover from £50,000 a year to the median of between £5 million to £10 million a year, may stumble into circumstances in which their agreements are voided or they are made subject to penalty. It is important to bear in mind that there are two elements of sanction--the penalty and the voiding of agreements. As some of my hon. Friends suggested this morning, and it is certainly my view, the voiding of agreements, particularly at that level, may be even more worrying than the penalty.

Mr. Andrew Lansley (South Cambridgeshire): I would not dream of interrupting my hon. Friend when he is just getting into his stride, but may I add to his list the threat of third-party actions?

Mr. Boswell: My hon. Friend is entirely right.

The first element of our concern is the potential for what might loosely be called trouble for ``ordinary'' businesses going about what they would see as ``normal'' business. Our second worry is that if those businesses are responsible, if they read our deliberations and ministerial assurances and listen to the advice of the Director General of Fair Trading, or if they are well advised by in-house lawyers or have purchased their own legal services, they may decide that they need to do something about the problem, or at least to go through the process of deciding whether to do something about it. All that will impose costs on such businesses.

Our two elements of concern for businesses are, first, the element of sanction, whether by penalty, third-party action or voiding agreements, and, secondly, the cost of compliance. Although only time will show whether I am right, I have a certain scepticism about the Under-Secretary's figure on compliance costs. I emphasise that we are covering new territory. We understand fully that the present and previous Governments, with the Director General of Fair Trading, have considered ways in which those compliance costs can be relieved.

The guidance notes issued by the Office of Fair Trading state that the primary test is whether anti-competitiveness arises from the clause. If it does not, there is no need to register. That is fair enough if it is clear in advance to the business that no anti-competitive action could have taken place or could be alleged subsequently to have taken place. There will be much covering of one's back involved in compliance with that.

The second test will be the market share test, however it will be applied. It is designed to sift out or filter, both in European procedure and, ultimately, in ours, a proportion that will be taken off by the test. I refer to this morning's interesting debate on the consequences of local commercial action. Without malice on their part, individuals with a comparatively small global market share, with no definite view of whether their actions are anti-competitive, may find themselves caught by the provisions.

It is fair to say that the amendment tabled by the hon. Members for South-East Cornwall (Mr. Breed) and for Eastleigh (Mr. Chidgey) has the same broad intention as our amendment, which is to cut through the tests that are difficult and are transmitted to the business through lawyers and advice. It would produce an additional test that would strike out the smaller businesses.

Amendment No. 54 contains a provision for a turnover of below £10 million a year--``revenues'' is the word used. I do not hold a brief for the wording of the amendment nor do I have a precise commitment to the figure, but the amendment would cover the turnover of the majority of businesses. It would confine the way in which the Bill bites to a limited number of cases which, in their nature and because of their scale, are likely to be most appropriate for action on the anti-competitiveness front.

We also debated earlier whether parts of the United Kingdom should be sub-divided. It may be difficult to capture a small, local situation through the anti-competitive mechanism, in general. There would always be the possibility of clawback if a particular situation arose that we had not thought about. However, a basic presumption of a minimum turnover level for businesses designed to exclude the great majority numerically of businesses and to concentrate the Bill on the bigger players for which it is intended, and which are better equipped to deal with it given their resources and the advice that they receive, would be sensible.

Such a proposal would reduce considerably the compliance costs to smaller businesses. The Government certainly pay lip service to the need to do that. Occasionally, they claim that they have been able to better the work of their predecessors. If they do that, on one will be more pleased than me; it is an area that we all need to work on to give small business a chance.

Mr. John Bercow (Buckingham): Would it not be a supreme irony if the Government failed to accept our amendment, as a result of which many small businesses were damaged--even if only inadvertently as the Government would believe--and, consequently, competition in the market, far from being enhanced, decreased?

Mr. Boswell: That is a fair consideration. We do not want people to be unable to enter the market--the chilling effect--or to be driven out of the market because they are terrified of being caught in the coils of the Bill, although their broad intentions and commercial actions are, and have always been, designed to be reasonable, to borrow a phrase.

We are therefore faced with the impact on small businesses, compliance costs, and the danger that people may be inadvertently caught or may be driven out of the market so that they are not inadvertently caught. We also face the problem of the ability of the Government, the Office of Fair Trading and the director general to resource the process.

As an inevitable result of our activity as Members of Parliament, we are all familiar with the concept of what I call ``snow''--when the number of incoming messages destroys the ability of the poor mortals that we are to handle them. We all struggle quite hard. I suspect that some new Members may have been surprised by the amount that they have received.

If the director general and the OFT are beset with huge quantities of bumph from people who think that, despite their attempts to get it right, and because they have over-fussy lawyers who are determined to have everything in black and white, they need to apply for guidance and clearance on their activities, the OFT will ultimately not be able to concentrate on its main job, which is to pursue genuine actions of anti-competitiveness. It will be too busy ensuring that the burden of paperwork is clear, without discharging the objective of that paperwork. That is an equal anxiety.

The amendment contains those two parallel objectives, which are entirely reasonable. I hope that the Minister will reflect on them sympathetically. It is an attempt, first, to relieve the burden on smaller business of potential problems, which are not likely to involve the major objectives of competition legislation. Secondly, it is designed to clear some of the paperwork for the director general and the OFT, to enable them better to apply themselves to their duties on major cases.

In due course, I am sure that the hon. Member for Eastleigh will want to catch your eye, Mr. Atkinson, to speak to his amendment. His will be a differently expressed but broadly similar approach. I advise the Committee of another anxiety that is not embodied in an amendment, but appeared at the last moment from an august source--the joint working party of the Bar Council and the Law Society on competition law. Its members do not come cheap, and should know what they are talking about. It suggests that clause 2(4) should.

    ``not apply to a small agreement until such time as immunity is withdrawn by the Director under subsection (4).''

That question strictly arises on clause 40, but I raise it now because although, under clause 37(1), small agreements might be immune from the imposition of a penalty fine, they might remain open to actions by third parties. As I have already suggested, the danger is that parties to small agreements are likely to be less worried about penalty fines, especially if they have acted reasonably, and much more worried about their agreements being deemed void, because that is the major sanction on them. If we believe in a de minimis provision along the lines suggested in the Bill, we should allow minor agreements that fall within the scope of chapter I to operate without fear of sanctions. That is a sensible principle.

Concern is shared widely among members of the Committee that we should do our best to clear the decks of small businesses, which are unlikely to have a major impact on the objectives of the Bill, in both their interests and those of smooth administration for everyone.

We have no hang-ups about precisely how that should be achieved. It could be achieved by a turnover limit, by a better articulation of the immunity rules or by some importation from the European analogy. The Government need to address that issue a little more thoroughly in the interests of small business, which we all favour, and in the interests of making the Bill more effective. I commend the amendment to the Committee.

Given that assessment, I want to make one or two points that support the argument that we must do all that we can to reduce the risk of a substantial number of companies incurring costs under the chapter I prohibition.

The compliance cost assessment is interesting. I shall confine myself to the chapter I prohibition, with which we are dealing. Annex A of the compliance cost assessment deals with the number of agreements that have been notified under the current system, and that figure is compared with what is assumed to be the number of notifications of agreements under the new system. Built into the assessment are several assumptions, which I shall not question. For example, the Department has estimated the cost per agreement on the basis of correspondence with legal firms. Let us hope that that estimate is accurate.

It is assumed that the cost of business managers' time within the businesses involved--not the legal firms--will be 50 per cent. of the legal charges. I have no reason to question that assumption. However, some of us might well think that the Department's estimate of compliance cost per agreement is modest compared with our own estimate of the real cost to firms, but I shall leave that aside.

Let us consider the one assumption in annex A that seems arguable on the grounds that my hon. Friend the Member for Daventry gave when moving the amendment. If I summarise it correctly, it is assumed in the compliance cost assessment that, whereas between 50 and 60 per cent. of agreements were notified under the Restrictive Trade Practices Act 1976, only 20 per cent. will be required to be notified to the Director General of Fair Trading under the anti-competitive prohibition. That assumption substantially affects the recurring compliance costs--not the non-recurring costs, which are acknowledged--that businesses will incur as a consequence of the introduction of the new system.

I have done some calculations and it may help the Committee if I were to run through one or two numbers. For example, if the percentage of agreements notified under the new system were to increase from 20 per cent. to 60 per cent., and if the notifications were distributed between the two types of notification in the proportion that the Department has assumed--that is, one notification for individual exemption for every three that are notified on a precautionary basis--using the same compliance cost per agreement as the Department, I calculate that changing that assumption will increase the potential compliance costs under type F--that is precautionary notifications--by between £3 million and £4·5 million on a 50 per cent. to 60 per cent. estimate, and by between £750,000 and £1 million for notifications for individual exemptions.
