Mr. O'Brien: Welcome to a day in Committee, Mr. O'Hara.

The Government's primary objective is to ensure that we preserve the role of the criminal court as the sole forum for determining guilt, while keeping the exemption from section 19B as narrow as possible. That is in accord with the findings of the Phillips royal commission in 1981, which stated:

    publicly calling into question a decision not to prosecute could amount to a trial of the suspect without the safeguards which criminal proceedings are designed to provide.

I shall address the Government amendment first and then turn to amendment No. 21 and new clause 5 tabled by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes).

The Government amendment is necessary to ensure that our policy objective is fully met. As it stands, section 19D exempts a decision not to institute criminal proceedings. It also exempts an act taken for the prupose of making a decision not to institute criminal proceedings. However, it does not exempt a decision to terminate or not to continue proceedings once a decision to institute proceedings has been made. It needs to do so if the policy objective is to be fully met.

The Crown Prosecution Service, for example, rarely institutes proceedings. Instead, it decides whether to continue proceedings that have been instituted by the police. I understand that the Crown Prosecution Service decides not to continue about 12 per cent. of all criminal cases that have been instituted by the police and passed to them for prosecution. We want to treat those similarly to decisions not to prosecute that have been taken by the police. The amendment therefore ensures that a decision by the CPS or other public prosecutor to discontinue proceedings will be exempt from section 19B. Also, in line with the current exemption, it provides that, where a decision to discontinue proceedings has been made, an act done for the purpose of enabling that decision to be taken is exempt. It further provides that acts done for the purpose of securing that proceedings are not continued are exempt. That is necessary to deal with the fact that in many cases the court, not prosecutors, discontinues proceedings. I commend the amendment to the Committee.

I understand the intention behind the amendment and new clause tabled by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey, which were suggested by the Commission for Racial Equality. They seek to find a way to square a circle, to reconcile two conflicting and laudable public interests. On one hand is the public interest to ensure that individuals have a right to a remedy for discrimination-that is what the Bill is about. On the other is the public interest to ensure that those accused of committing crimes are tried not in the civil courts or by the media, but in the criminal courts, with the safeguards that criminal proceedings are designed to provide.

The Government resist the amendments, not because we do not wish to reconcile those competing interests, but because we do not think that the amendments achieve that reconciliation. I am also unconvinced that putting the CRE in a position to second-guess the criminal courts is a wise move for the CRE or for justice.

The amendment and the new clause would make racially biased decisions not to prosecute unlawful. Individuals would be given no remedy against such unlawful acts, but the CRE would be able to mount a formal investigation on the basis of, for example, patterns in section 95 data. The Government's concern is that, in exercising the power, the CRE would find itself conducting objective assessments of individual cases, which could have cast doubt on an individual's innocence, while leaving them without recourse to a criminal process.

There are additional concerns in relation to Scotland. To allow the CRE to conduct formal investigations would detract from the independence of the Lord Advocate as head of the systems of criminal prosecution and investigation of deaths in Scotland. To give the CRE the power to investigate decisions not to prosecute would give it stronger powers than those of the Scottish Parliament or the civil courts.

I do not oppose the intention behind the amendment and the new clause, which is to search for a way to narrow the exemption from section 19D, but I believe that we are all agreed that the preservation of the role of the criminal court as the sole forum for determining guilt is a proper and necessary objective. The Government believe that the amendment and the new clause would undermine that, and we must, on that basis, oppose them.

Friend the Member for West Aberdeen and Kincardine (Sir R Smith). As the Minister said they all seek to deal with the same issues. I am grateful that the Government have responded to the debate in the Lords on Opposition amendments. Ministers said then that they would come back with a proposal, and it is reasonable to assume that the Government amendment is the result.

Although on the face of it this set of issues looks small, behind it is one of the key issues that prompted the Bill. The Minister will be aware that, because the Bill was a proper, rightful and applauded response to the Macpherson inquiry, and if it is to do the job properly, it must address, among other things, the whole process of criminal investigation and prosecution, including the way I which the courts act. I am concious that, as the Minister said, we must try when addressing the problem to act according to the public policy objectives that we hope to achieve.

It must be right that the decision not to prosecute is taken on the grounds of the evidence before the prosecuting authority, whether in England and Wales, Northern Ireland or Scotland-my hon. Friend is much more qualified to talk about the position in Scotland than I am. That process must be independent. I agree entirely with the Minister that decisions cannot be second-guessed or subject to review that might open them either to civil proceedings or media campaigns, which may suggest that decisions can be overturned. The criminal law would be nobody's friend or defender if that were to happen.

In that context, we are aware that the Government and other are rightly thinking about whether cases that have resulted in a decision not to proceed on a technicality might be brought back if new evidence arises, as is happening in the Lawrence case and other. I have always taken the view that the old legal principle that one makes a decision only once has to be able to reconsider as a matter of public policy if new evidence and circumstances come into the domain of the public prosecutor subsequent to the original prosecution. We would all want that in the proper process in response to colour-blind, race-blind, gender-blind or religion blind decisions about guilt or innocence. The two public policy thresholds that are rightfully always followed by the Crown Prosecution Service when it does the job properly are whether there is a better than average chance of securing a conviction and whether it is in the public interest to institute criminal proceedings. The CPS is right to retain those two criteria.

If there is prejudice and discrimination in the collection and investigation of evidence, cross-examination of suspects and all the procedures that precede a decision whether to prosecute-rightful cause complaint, as there was in the Lawrence case-we must ensure that remedies are available. The Minister is right that amendment No. 21 and new clause 5, which are meant to be read together, were originally suggested by the CRE and have been tabled by my hon. Friend and I to probe the combination of language to deal with the issue.

New Clause 5 would disapply the enforcement part of the Race Relations Act 1976 to a later act of discrimination that consists soley of a decision not to institute, so removing from the challenge decisions not to institute proceedings. Amenndment No. 21 would omit the original exception, which Ministers now accept was drawn too widely. We would be left with a provision that although narrower does not preclude debate.

Although I do not pretend to have thought through all the implications of the Governemnt's amendment, it certainly seeks to make the decision and any decision on going ahead with prosecution inviolate for the purposes of challenge. I am cautious about saying that the Government amendment is perfect, but I am not saying that it does not go in the right direction. In such legal territory, we need to get the terminology right. Amendment No. 28 proposes that (b) where criminal proceedings are not continued as a result of a decision not to continue them, the decision and, where such a decision has been made-

    (i) any act done for the purpose of enabling the decision whether to continue the proceedings to be made

should be exempted. That is potentially still quite wide. I seek nothing more than the right wording. We need to ensure not only that the evaluation of evidence leading to the decision is inviolate and that evidence can be considered entirely on its merits, but that

    any act done for the purpose of enabling the decision whether to continue the proceedings to be made

cannot be interpreted too widely?

On first examination, the wording seems okay; it seems to suggest that consideration of whether proceedings should continue is to do only with the CPS-the prosecuting authority, to which the police hand over their evidence. If that is so, that is much better than at earlier stages of an investigation. The one thing that we on the Liberal Democrat Bench are keen to ensure is that we can get at the sort of complaint that was rightfully upheld by the Lawrence inquiry.

As the inquiry found, from the first reporting of the attack on Stephen Lawrence and his death, through several stages of that investigation, things that should have been done were not done and others that should not have been done were done. Those two general complaints, sometimes about the response of individual police officers and sometimes about the failure to pursue lines of inquiry, were clearly upheld by the Macpherson inquiry. Some of the conclusions-in that case, as in others-were wrongly drawn.

One of the bigger mischiefs that emerged from the Lawrence case was the completely justified complaint that judgments were sometimes formed on who was the victim and who was the assailant. The real horror of the case was that, at times, the assumption was made that Stephen Lawrence or the friend who he was with must have been the party to the attack-the aggressor and the assailant. That was the unacceptable attitude displayed by some of those involved in the case. Subject to a definition being limited to the protection of the prosecutorial assessment process and a review of earlier processes, we are going in the right direction.

On its own, new clause 5 may be too narrow. Therefore, having only that in the legislation may not go far enough. However, I want to ensure that we do not preclude people examining activities of the police or other agencies that could be prejudicial in the collection, assessment and passing on of evidence.

In conclude with an example that extends beyond issues to do with race relations. Over the years, there have been enough cases in which evidence has not been followed up by the police and certain corrupt police officers have decided not to pursue lines of inquiry or concealed or got rid of evidence. The lessons that we all need to learn is drawn from one of the sad truths of the Lawrence inquiry: Stephen's parents, with whom, like others, I have met and talked, felt that they had nowhere to go to put the case that they were unfairly discriminated against. There was no proper forum for them to do so. The intention of the Bill is to ensure that that does not happen when people feel that they have a justified complaint. We need to make sure that this part of the Bill addresses that concern as fully as any other part.

The hon. Member for Aylesbury asked me whether Customs and Excise was covered in the same way. The answer is yes, because the provision is for the decision to prosecute. The collection of information is one thing and the decision to prosecute another.

The hon. Gentleman also asked me how the amendment applies to the balance-of-evidence test by the prosecution and the public interest. He was concerned about high evidential thresholds for one ethnic minority group in comparison with another. The code of practice for Crown prosecutors, when making decisions, is clear. It says:

    prosecution is likely to be needed if the offence was motivated by any form of discrimination against the victim's ethnic or national origin.

The CPS prosecution manual provides guidance on that as follows:

    `When considering the public interest test, you must balance the factors for and against prosecution carefully and fairly. However, cases in which there is a racial motivation are almost invariably serious; prosecution will usually take place unless there are very powerful public interest factors tending against prosecution which clearly outweigh those tending in favour'.

Prosecutors have a positive duty to bring evidence of racial motivation to the attention of the court. That is reflected in the prosecution manual. The achievement of that is is specifically measured, and information gathered during the CPS racial incidents monitoring scheme has assisted us. The Home Secretary's action plan that followed the Stephen Lawrence inquiry showed that, in 1997-98, prosecutors met the objectives in 85 per cent. of cases. So, there is still quite a lot of work to be done, but the CPS is working on it.

The hon. Member for Aylesbury asked about a high evidential threshold for one ethnic minority group as against another. That will not occur. The matter is not one of the ethnic minority groups concerned, but that racism may be involved in a case. Of course, racism may be prepetrated white against black or black against white.
