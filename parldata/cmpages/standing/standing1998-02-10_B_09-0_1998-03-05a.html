Let us be clear about the purpose of the sections in the Police Act (Northern Ireland) 1970 and the Police Act 1996, which are equivalent to the clause. My firm legal advice is that the power on the statute is intended to provide an independent check on the propriety of the means by which the Chief Constable has reached his decision in a given case, and on the propriety of the negotiation process leading to the final settlement amount. In other words, the authority must ensure that the Chief Constable takes appropriate legal advice on whether to settle any claim against one of his officers out of court, follows that advice and then takes proper account of the need to conserve public funds in the quantum of any settlement.

The authority’s approval function should not amount to statutory second-guessing of the Chief Constable’s decision to settle a claim—or to fight it, as has happened in the past—in a particular case, nor to decide whether a liability exists; nor is it intended to have the effect of deciding that a particular case must be heard by a court.

The authority’s approval function is more akin to that of a court in proceedings by way of judicial review, ensuring that proper procedures have been used but not substituting its judgment for that of the relevant authority. The Police Authority recognises that, and in its document, “Listening to the Community—Working with the RUC”, sets out its interpretation of the legislation, which, I would argue, confirms what I have said. Under compensation—the issue that we are discussing—it states:

    “The Chief Constable is legally liable for the actions of members of the RUC under his direction and control.”

I hope that there is unanimity about that statement. It continues:

    “Where civil claims arise as a result of police action, the Chief Constable decides whether to contest the action or seek an out-of-court settlement. In either case, the Police Authority is financially liable for legal costs incurred and any damages payable by way of compensation.”

The section—remember, this is from a booklet produced by the Police Authority—continues:

    “If the Chief Constable decides to fight a claim, the Authority provides the necessary financial resources to do so and pays any damages awarded by the courts. However, if the Chief Constable decides to settle a claim out-of-court, the Authority must satisfy itself that the correct procedures have been observed in reaching the decision. Separate Authority approval must then be given for the amount of the settlement which must be reasonable in the circumstances of the case. This staged approval process is designed to provide an independent oversight of the settlement system by the Authority.”

The Police Authority is saying in that document that it has an oversight of the procedures being applied; it does not second guess the legal advice on which the Chief Constable works.
