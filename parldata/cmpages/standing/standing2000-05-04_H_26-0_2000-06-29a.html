New clause 1

Volunteer expenditure

    `(1) After section 83B of the Taxes Act 1988 there shall be inserted—

    ``Volunteer expenditure

    83C (1) Subsection (2) below applies to where an individual volunteers to assist in the work of a charity and in the course of providing such assistance incurs expenses attributable to the work for which the individual volunteered.

    (2) On a claim made in that behalf to an officer of the Board the relevant amount shall be allowed as a deduction in calculating the individual's income for the purposes of income tax for the year of assessment in which the expenses are incurred.

    (3) The relevant amount is the amount of the expenses incurred, after deducting any reimbursement for those expenses received by the individual from the charity for whose benefit the expenses were incurred.

    (4) In this section a `charity' has the same meaning as in section 506 and includes each of the bodies mentioned in section 507(1).

    (2) This section has effect in relation to expenses incurred by an individual on or after 6th April 2000.''.'.—[Mr. Flight.]

Mr. Flight: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

As we draw to the end of our deliberations, I hope that the Government will see fit to implement the substance of the new clause, if not its drafting, for two reasons. One is that it is a good and fair proposal. The other is that the Government's measures already enable its substance to be achieved, but by a heavily administrative and unnecessarily complicated route.

As hon. Members will know from their constituencies, many people volunteer to drive people to hospital, for example, on behalf of various charities. In such circumstances, subject to keeping records, we propose that they should be able to offset what they spend on petrol against their income for tax purposes. Under the new gift aid rules, the same can be achieved. If an individual were to give, as gift aid, 80 per cent. to the charity, and the charity paid for the petrol, the 18 or 10 or 22 per cent. that the charity gets back would be roughly equivalent to allowing such an individual to claim such expenses for tax. The net effect is already achievable by that cumbersome route.

The same thing would happen for higher rate taxpayers. If a donor donated 80 per cent., the charity would receive its 18 per cent. and the donor 22 per cent., so those concessions could be used towards the cost of that petrol. The Government have made it clear that charities may reimburse people for such out-of-pocket expenses—that is the other bit of the equation.

The complicated route that I have described could be cut through along the lines that I am suggesting. People should keep records when they incur such expenditure to be able to claim it as a gift to the charity for which they have incurred it. Those who give to charity in cash should not be in a different position from those who spend cash on something used by the charity.

I am referring, in part, to those who transport others free to hospital. That is a necessary adjunct to our inefficient health service, with the failure of ambulances to take old ladies to visit their husbands, and many people having to travel further as more hospitals close down. It is crucially important that lots of people volunteer to drive others to hospital when they want to visit relatives or their illness is not serious enough for them to command an ambulance.

The cost of the proposals would be zero, and the net tax effect would be the same as the original provision because the charities could organise themselves in the way that I described. Therefore I urge the Government to take up the matter and sort it out in a straightforward way, for the sake of the worthy citizens involved.

The Government have made clear their wholehearted support, and I am grateful for the hon. Gentleman's acknowledgment of the help that we have given to charities and to the whole of the voluntary sector. We recognise the time and effort that volunteers give to their communities, and that those who do hospital driving runs form a key part of the service by helping people get to hospital by means other than an ambulance.

We launched the active community initiative to promote and encourage voluntary and community involvement. We want to make it easier for people to get involved and remove the real or perceived barriers to volunteering. I say ``perceived'' because, for example, it is not always understood that organisations can reimburse volunteers' out-of-pocket expenses without tax consequences. No returns have to be made to the Inland Revenue in such circumstances, so the system is simple.

I have listened carefully to the comments made by the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight). Such comments have been made before, and to an extent I agree with them. However, the problem with the clause is not as much with its principle—although the general rule of our tax system is that only expenses incurred in earning a source of income are deductible for tax—as with its practicalities. In making it work, a substantial burden would be imposed on everyone involved, especially the claimants and the voluntary organisations. The hon. Gentleman said that his proposal would reduce the burden but, in fact, it would substantially increase it.

For many, the sums involved would not be worth the candle, and for others, who might otherwise not have to deal with the Inland Revenue because they were retired or on small incomes and did not have to fill in self-assessments, the sums to be claimed would be small. Gentleman that the Inland Revenue and the Department of Social Security will work with the active community unit at the Home Office and with voluntary organisations to identify the issues causing problems and how the Government can best tackle them. Mr. Davey: New clause 2 and new schedule 1 attempt to take on the Government's programme of encouraging savings, particularly among those on low incomes, by trying to raise the relevant limits for friendly society policies. Currently, friendly societies are exempt from tax on profits from life or endowment business arising from policies under which premiums payable in any year do not exceed £270. The new schedule would increase that limit to £1,000. That is rather a large increase, but it is in line with the quite generous limits for tax-free savings in individual savings accounts.

The new clause and the new schedule also contain a few technical changes, which I shall try to explain briefly. I should say from the outset that I have been helped by the Association of Friendly Societies. I have no vested interest. I have a savings plan with the Family Assurance friendly society, but I gain nothing from tabling the amendments. They are designed to probe the Government's thinking on their future policies towards friendly societies.

The technical changes aim to improve the way that a rise in the premium limit would be taken up by savers. The current legislation requires a friendly society policy to have level premiums to qualify for tax exemption. In other words, it must have either one annual premium or payments of equal value throughout the year. Previous amendments to increase the tax-exempt limit provided a window during which existing policies could be increased to the new limit, without being considered to have broken the requirement for level premiums.

The new schedule again makes such a provision in paragraph 3, but with two relatively minor changes. On earlier occasions, the window has been less than a year, with the result that for some policies, increases could not wait for the annual renewal date, which has been administratively inconvenient and expensive. The new schedule provides for a longer window to run for one year from the passage of the Finance Act 2000. However, even if a policy qualifies for tax exemption, tax is potentially payable on surrender of the policy or discontinuous premiums within a 10-year period or three-quarters of the term, if sooner. That is sometimes known as the quarantine period.

Section 540 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988 starts a new quarantine period whenever there is an increase in the rate of premium, thereby seriously limiting the attraction of taking advantage of any increased limits. Paragraph 3(4) of the new schedule disapplies that section to enable a premium increase to be made during the window without triggering a new quarantine period. Effectively, policies are treated as running from their original commencement. Those two technical amendments would ensure that, if the Government were minded in a future Finance Bill to increase the limit in a way that the new schedule proposes, the changes would be more effective. They would be easier for the friendly societies to administer and more attractive to people who are currently saving through these schemes to take up. I recommend them to the Government as they look at this important area.

One other important issue is not directly obvious and I gave the Minister notice of this. Friendly society savings schemes could be used to promote savings particularly directed at paying for a child's education and training after they have reached the age of majority. The Association of Friendly Societies put this proposal to the Government before the Budget and I am taken with the idea. It seems to me that increasingly we want to encourage people to focus on their children's studies as they leave full-time education and to make financial provisions for them by saving, so that later in life they can take advantage of further education or training. A tax-efficient scheme designed to assist in that respect, and directed towards relatively low levels of saving so that some equity is maintained, would be desirable.

The Government have considered individual learning accounts, which were suggested by the Labour party-inspired Commission for Social Justice in 1994, when the idea of a learning bank and individual learning accounts came into common parlance. I support those ideas, which were promoted by the commission. Such a scheme, linked to tax-exempt savings for a child's education and training, would take it a stage further. There are tax-exempt schemes for specific purposes, such as pensions and retirement savings and, as we move to the knowledge and information-based economy, it would be sensible to have a parallel scheme for education and training later in life.

Such a scheme could be designed to ensure that it was not simply a means of allowing the rich to escape tax, which my party, like the Government, is not keen on doing, but was tailored to ensure that it promoted investment in education and took account of genuine educational needs.

I recommend the idea, which was floated by the Association of Friendly Societies, to the Government. I want the Minister and her colleagues to imagine how the friendly societies would promote such a scheme if it was tailored to education and training needs. They would promote the idea of saving for children's futures in all groups in society. Friendly societies have much greater contact than institutions such as banks and building societies with people on low or modest incomes. They are better placed than any other institution to promote such saving and the idea of self-help and mutuality, which will encourage people to invest in the future of their offspring. I hope that the Government will consider the proposal favourably now and, perhaps more important, in the run-up to the Budget.

Miss Melanie Johnson: I thank the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton for his explanation of the thinking behind his rather detailed new clause and schedule. At first, I found it difficult to understand why it had been tabled, and I am grateful to him for writing to me and explaining the proposal.

A similar measure was rejected in proceedings on the Finance Bill 1998 and I did not think that anything had changed since then, but the hon. Gentleman introduced a new twist to the arguments. I am happy to respond to what he described as a probing measure.

The proposal is that an increase in the friendly society tax-free limits should be available only to those setting up an account to fund a child's further education needs. The Government wholeheartedly endorse the ideals that underlie the friendly society movement of thrift and providing protection and savings for those on modest incomes; many members on both sides of the Committee share that view. However, increasing the tax exemption, even when it is targeted in the way suggested, is not the right approach for several reasons.

First, an account to encourage saving for the cost of further education cannot be the preserve of friendly societies. The life assurance products offered by friendly societies would not always be the best way of saving in such circumstances. We want all sectors of the financial services industry to be able to market such accounts to ensure that savers get the best possible deal and choice.

Secondly, to introduce an account specifically aimed at a child's further education would be complex. Rules would be needed to ensure that it was used for the purpose intended and not simply as an extra tax-free savings vehicle. It would be difficult to find rules that were easy to apply yet sufficiently focused on the purpose of the account. Also, policing complex rules would be costly and undermine the value of the account to savers.

Thirdly, a successful account—the ISA—is already available to help people save for such an eventuality. The first year of ISAs has been a great success, with more than £28 billion placed in more than 9 million accounts. ISAs have proved to be a more attractive savings vehicle than the arrangements that they replaced, with an increase of around a third on the sums put into PEPs and TESSAs in 1998–99.

People are attracted to the flexibility and choice that ISAs offer. I am pleased that several forward-looking friendly societies, including some of the smaller ones, offer ISAs. It is disappointing that more societies have not risen to the new challenge, and I urge the societies that have not done so to respond to the changing environment. Seeking imaginative new ways to increase tax breaks is not the way in which they should want to proceed.

ISAs offer investors a choice in how they can save. There is no stipulation about how people use them or for how long people save. ISAs can be used for any of the events of a lifetime, such as marriages, births and retirements, as well as making lives more comfortable by assisting in the purchase of assets such as cars and houses. Of course, they can be used to save for a child's further education as well.

I accept that people under 18 cannot open ISAs. That issue has been raised as part of the Inland Revenue's review of the operation of ISAs. Children have a personal allowance. Therefore, for the vast majority of children, income or capital gains arising on investments other than those derived from their parents will be exempt from tax. Extending ISAs to children may not be worth while as it may simply complicate the savings sector, but the matter is being considered in the review.

No response on this topic would be complete without mention of how the Government are encouraging lifelong learning by individuals. The hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton remarked on the subject at some length. William Pitt the Younger was responsible for laying down the bones of our modern tax structure. By the time that he died in 1806, the main elements of our tax system—the income tax, the rating system and most of the excise duties—were in place. All Governments have revenue demands. Pitt had to finance a war, so he looked for new sources of revenue. The search was always for ways of raising the maximum amount of revenue while causing the least damage to the economy and without making the Government too unpopular. I think that it was the French Finance Minister Fouquet who, when explaining taxation to the King, said that the aim was to pluck the maximum amount of feathers from the goose with the minimum amount of hissing. That phrase is probably written above the desk of all modern Treasury Ministers.

Mr. Flight: My right hon. Friend alludes to a matter of great historic importance. We won the Napoleonic wars because we were able to finance them. The French ran out of money. At the end of those wars, we had a national debt of 150 per cent. of GNP, and it has never been higher.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: My hon. Friend is right. Pitt set in train a sinking fund to try to reduce the national debt.

I shall mention one or two failed taxes to show the problems that all Governments face, from which we can draw lessons. Property has always been a most tempting target. An early attempt to tax fixed property was the hearth tax, which was extremely unpopular and intrusive, because officials had to enter people's private property to assess it. That tax became the window tax, which could be more easily assessed by simply walking round the outside of the property. Bachelors and widowers were taxed between 1695 and 1706. It is not clear why bachelors were targeted. Perhaps it was to encourage marriage or because they were assumed to have a higher taxable capacity. The fact that the tax existed for only 11 years suggests that it became unenforceable. Clocks and watches were taxed not at the point of purchase, but on ownership. Gloves, hats, cards and dice were also taxed.

We can perhaps all think of newer and better examples for the list of consumption taxes. Salt was taxed; spirits were, and still are, taxed; tobacco pipes and leather were taxed; and there were riots in the west country when cider was taxed. The tradition of hostility to new taxation still lingers among my constituents. Famously, tea was taxed at threepence in the pound, and the attempt to extend that tax to north America led to the war of American independence. One could say that the United States was founded on a tax revolt.

My last example of a failed tax was introduced by William Pitt, so it slightly undermines his reputation for good taxation. In 1796, he gave in to a suggestion by a Mr. Dent for a tax on dogs. Mr. Dent was the Member of Parliament for Berkshire, and he quickly became known as ``dog Dent''. His aim was to prevent the multiplication of dogs and his idea was that the tax would become self-liquidating. Perhaps he thought in the same way as Treasury Ministers, who always advance the idea that the tax on tobacco is designed to stop people smoking, but get down on their knees every night in hopes that that declared aim is not a success because it would create an enormous hole in the Revenue if it were. The dog tax was not a success and was quickly withdrawn. Governments must therefore be careful about what they tax and how they tax it and be aware that taxes that are tolerable and enforceable at low levels can become intolerable and unenforceable at high levels. The efficiency and effectiveness of a tax system is just as important as the gross sum that it tries to raise.

A good tax system is a national asset and has always been seen as such. I end my brief and anecdotal trip around the British tax system by recalling the words of Edmund Burke, who studied the matter. In 1767, after studying the state of revenues of many of the principal nations of the continent, he asserted that, if called upon to do so, he could prove beyond possibility of dispute that England was the most lightly taxed of any of the great states of Europe and that we had the best constituted and best managed revenue that the world had ever beheld. That was a proud boast, and I hold it out as an aim—one that I am afraid that the Government have not achieved. A good and efficient tax system is an asset that the whole country enjoys. Taxes should be widely spread, modest in their incidence and properly enforced.

In conclusion, I turn to the initiative of the Institute of Chartered Accountants, which I heartily endorse. It published a document called ``Towards a Better Tax System'' in which it sets out 10 principles of good taxation. By way of illustration, it examined a piece of new legislation, the IR35 sections of the Bill, and tested it in the light of its principles. It gave the legislation marks under each heading; I regret that its overall conclusion was that the tax scored only 30 out of 100.     the Government gives its considered view on the options for simplifying the tax system . . . and sets out its own proposals for a systematic programme of simplification.

Our new clause tries merely to encode that recommendation by an all-party Committee, and I urge it on the Government.
