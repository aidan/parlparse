The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Keith Hill): We are beginning to approach the end of part II and as the terminus hoves into sight it might be appropriate to go a little further along the route in my famous guided bus, and to describe the purposes of clauses 130 to 135. [Interruption.] If I am further provoked by the hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Don Foster), I shall take us along in a tourist bus.

Those clauses cover several important financial arrangements for the support of bus services and other public passenger transport services. They recognise the role that funding provided by central Government and local authorities plays in ensuring that local authorities have the bus services that they need. The clauses also seek to ensure that the funding is used to best effect.

The Committee will know that we have already substantially increased provision for the support of bus services, most notably through new grants for rural services and by ensuring that the fuel duty rebate paid to bus operators is increased in line with fuel duty increases. Both those steps have been widely and justifiably welcomed. As it is important to continue to get value for money from the resources, we have asked the Commission for Integrated Transport to examine the whole subject.

Among other things, this group of clauses provides flexibility for changes to the grant arrangements, should they appear necessary in the light of changing requirements and the commission's report, which we expect later this year. We have no intention of changing or removing the arrangements for any current grant before we are fully satisfied that change would benefit the delivery of our wider transport objectives, or before necessary alternative arrangements are in place.

The clauses also provide a broad power to make grants to local authorities in support of public transport services and facilities. That will, for example, enable us to put grants in support of rural services, which are covered only by annual special grant reports, on a permanent statutory basis. I think that the Committee would on the whole welcome that.

The clauses also recognise the importance to bus users of measures to ensure that operators run local bus services reliably. When an operator registers the route and timetable for his service with the traffic commissioner, as he is required by law to do, that is in effect his promise to the public that the service will run according to the details in the registration. The Transport Act 1985 provides for the traffic commissioner, where he is satisfied that the operator has failed to fulfil the terms of his registration, to levy a financial penalty on the operator based on the repayment of 20 per cent. of the fuel duty rebate he has been paid in the preceding three months.

There are two purposes behind the changes to that penalty which these clauses introduce. First, they provide for a new form of penalty to be introduced on the replacement, should that occur, of fuel duty rebate under clause 131. Secondly, they give the traffic commissioner greater flexibility to match the amount of the penalty to the particular circumstances of the case so that instead of a set figure in every instance he can determine a penalty to suit the seriousness of the operator's failure up to a certain maximum provided for in the clauses.

Clause 130 introduces a new best-value test for subsidy by local authorities of bus services. It will require authorities, in making decisions on which services to subsidise, to have regard to economy, efficiency and effectiveness, and also to their bus strategy and environmental considerations.

The clause also tackles what has been seen as a hindrance to local authorities in using their subsidy powers to secure the provision of increases in frequency in bus services. Section 92(1) of the Transport Act 1985 requires that in exercising subsidy powers authorities must not act so as ``to inhibit competition''. It has been argued that that is too inflexible a test. Some authorities have been reluctant to buy additional frequency in circumstances that might prompt complaints from a bus operator that it inhibited his ability to provide a service in competition with the one on which frequency has been increased. Clause 130 therefore also introduces a new, more flexible requirement designed to make it easier for authorities to use the powers to subsidise increased frequency on an existing service The new formulation will simply require authorities to have regard to the interests of the public and transport providers.

Clause 131 is an enabling clause. It enables the introduction of new arrangements for central Government or National Assembly for Wales grants to operators of bus services. When introduced, the clause will replace fuel duty rebate, currently paid under the Finance Act 1965, with a more flexible power to make grants to operators on a changed basis. The clause provides for the making of regulations on eligibility for the new grants and the method of calculation.

Clause 132 enables a new form of penalty to be introduced for use by the traffic commissioner where he determines that a bus operator has, without reasonable excuse, failed to operate a registered service or has failed to register a route. Section 111 of the 1985 Act provides for a penalty of repayment by the operator of 20 per cent. of the fuel duty rebate he has received for the previous quarter. In the event that fuel duty rebate is replaced under clause 131, the new penalty under clause 132 will be in the form of a payment by the operator of a sum determined by the traffic commissioner according to his judgment of the seriousness of the case. The penalty will, however, be based on the number of vehicles that the operator is licensed to run up to a maximum of £550 per vehicle. The maximum figure may be amended subsequently by regulations.
