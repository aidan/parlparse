Clause 40

Cancellation of enforcement notice

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 212, in page 23, line 19, leave out from `notice' to end of line 22 and insert

    `subject only to his having the grounds to seek judicial review of that notice.'.

I trust that you had an enjoyable and relaxing recess, Mr. Welsh. In fairness to the Minister, his point of order made it clear how hard we work in this place. We welcome him back.

This morning, I will speak to several amendments on matters that the Data Protection Registrar has raised with Home Office officials. The registrar's concerns have been put on the record as a result of correspondence with members of the Committee and information that has been made available, even on the Internet. We are not privy to officials' answers, and it is right that we have some brief, sharp debates on those concerns, which have been so publicly expressed. That will give the Minister the opportunity to put on record his response to them.

Clause 40 provides a mechanism for the cancellation of an enforcement notice. It provides an opportunity for a data controller on whom an enforcement notice has been served to apply for cancellation or variation of the notice. The Minister should clarify the circumstances in which that would operate.

Under the Data Protection Act 1984, the commissioner may withdraw an enforcement notice for any reason, if there are grounds for judicial review. Clause 40(1) allows the Data Protection Commissioner to vary a notice. That is a welcome additional power. However, under subsection (2), the Data Protection Commissioner can cancel the notice only on the specific grounds of a change in circumstances. That requirement is far narrower than that in the 1984 Act, which stipulates merely that there must be grounds for judicial review.

Will the Minister tell the Committee why the Government made that change? Perhaps it is an oversight, and the Government did not intend to narrow the requirement in such a way. If that is so, amendment No. 212 would address the concerns that have been expressed by the Data Protection Register and her officials.

Mr. George Howarth: Under the 1984 Act, the Data Protection Registrar may cancel an enforcement order by means of a written notification to the person on whom it is served. No express limitations exist regarding the grounds on which she may do so. Her exercise of that power is subject to the general rules of judicial review.

The 1984 Act contains no formal provision, backed by a right of appeal, for controllers to ask the registrar to vary or to cancel enforcement notices. Data controllers may make an informal approach to the registrar. She then has the power under section 10(8) to cancel, but not to vary, the notice. We believe that more flexibility is needed. That is the purpose of clause 40(2).

The Bill contains, at clause 40(1), a general power for the commissioner to cancel enforcement notices on her own initiative. However, it also adds a power for her to vary a notice so as to relieve a data controller of some, but not all, of its requirements. That important and useful new option will be available to the commissioner as part of her enforcement regime. It extends the registrar's powers under the 1984 Act.

The Bill introduces, at clause 40(2), a completely new facility for data controllers to take the initiative in submitting a formal request to the commissioner for cancellation or variation of an enforcement notice. The hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) asked why that is necessary. Clause 47(2) provides for a right of appeal against refusal of such a request. That is a significant new right for data controllers. It represents a direct route to the lifting of regulatory restriction, with the option of a complete review by the tribunal of the merits of any case that the commissioner refuses. As that is a new method of challenging an enforcement notice, it is essential for it to be limited. A challenge will be successful only if a genuine change of circumstances makes compliance with the notice unnecessary. That is a right and proper safeguard against abuse.

When an enforcement notice is issued in the first place, a right of appeal already exists. We cannot allow the new right of cancellation or variation to become nothing more than a means of challenging further the issue of an enforcement notice if an appeal has failed. It is not merely an opportunity for a second bite at that particular cherry. Something must have genuinely changed so as to require the basis or extent of the notice to be properly reassessed.

The hon. Gentleman's amendment appears to seek to remove all restrictions on controllers' freedom to seek the variation or cancellation of an enforcement notice and to place it on a par with the commissioner's general power in clause 40(1).

For those reasons, the amendment, though well intentioned, is misconceived. Clauses 40(1) and 40(2) perform different functions, and I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Greenway: Before we began this morning's deliberations, Mr. Welsh, we agreed that the Bill's provisions resembled the clues of a crossword puzzle--their meaning depends on what one reads into them.

We acknowledge that the clause could cover a change in circumstances, but that would require a judicial review in any event. Our question was whether the Bill provided, a right of appeal. We wanted to ensure that it did not represent a narrowing of the provisions of the 1984 Act, but rather provided additional flexibility. The Minister has confirmed that that is the case.

I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.45 am

Mr. Greenway: I wish only to ask a question about clause 40(1). I did not want to be provocative and table an amendment, but the subsection begins with the words:

    ``If the commissioner considers that all or any of the provisions of an enforcement notice need not be complied with.''

Surely the words ``all'' and ``or'' are superfluous, as the word ``any'' must include ``all''. It seems strange that the commissioner might think that an enforcement notice was wrong throughout. If she did, why would she have introduce it in the first place?

I shall not labour the point, as we have already debated the clause, but I should be grateful for an explanation from the Minister about the necessity of the use of the words ``all'' and ``or''.

Mr. Howarth: Clause 40 provides the commissioner with the power to vary as well as cancel the conditions of an enforcement notice. It also allows controllers to request cancellations or variations of such notices, on the grounds that the relevant circumstances have changed.

The questions asked by the hon. Member for Ryedale hinge on flexibility, and the clause will give that to the commissioner and to data controllers. The Committee has already discussed the circumstances in which data controllers would be able to request the cancellation or variation of an enforcement notice. The flexibility in the clause strikes the right balance in enabling data controllers and the commissioner to have the necessary arrangements in place to make sensible decisions about any matter that arises.

Clause 42

Information notices

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 282, in page 24, line 24, after `purpose', insert

    `or in a case falling within either subsection (a) or (b) such information as the Commissioner may require for the purposes of the notification process.'.

The Chairman: With this we may take the following amendments: No. 214, in page 24, line 26, at end insert--

    `(3A) The Commissioner may serve an information notice on a data controller notwithstanding that the controller reasonably believes that the processing in which he is engaged is in whole or in part exempt from the data protection principles.

    (3B) An information notice may also be served on any third party whom the Commissioner reasonably believes has possession of any information relevant to his determination of the matters contained in the information notice.'.

No. 283, in page 24, line 31, at end insert--

    `(5A) A person who without reasonable excuse fails to respond to an Information Notice is guilty of an offence'.

No. 270, in page 25, line 6, at end insert--

    `(9A) A refusal to furnish the Data Protection Commissioner with any information may not be used or relied upon as a material fact in any criminal proceedings against the Data Controller other than an offence under this Act.'.

Mr. Greenway: These four amendments were suggested by the Data Protection Registrar--the commissioner elect, so to speak--to clarify some important questions.

Amendment No. 282 would allow the commissioner to require information to assist the notification process. Relevant information may be in the hands of third parties or data processors, but there does not appear to be a provision allowing an enforcement notice to be served on anyone other than a data controller. The need to compel is important in preventing reckless or false statements.

Amendment No. 283 would add an important additional offence of failing to respond without reasonable excuse. That is a fairly standard requirement in legislation, which we have already debated.

The Data Protection Registrar feels strongly that there must be a legal duty to respond to an information notice. If that is coupled with the requirement that an information notice could be served on a third party that had information, which would be important to the process of notification, that requirement should be in the Bill.

Amendment No. 270 to clause 42(9) provides an exemption from the requirement to furnish any information that might reveal evidence of criminal conduct by the controller. Obviously, it is important that individuals do not lay themselves open to prosecution for offences by supplying information to the Data Protection Commissioner, other than--it is an important caveat--the offences that are created under the Bill. A similar provision is needed for any refusal; such a refusal to supply information should not be a material fact in criminal proceedings, other than for data protection offences.

Amendment No. 214 provides that an information notice may be served when the commissioner has received a request under clause 41 in respect of any processing, or when the commissioner reasonably requires information for the purpose of determining whether the data controller has complied, or is complying, with the data protection principles. When a data controller claims an exemption from the principle in whole or in part relying on one of the exemption provisions, he is under no obligation to comply with the principles and it is not clear that the commissioner can serve an information notice in such a case. If she cannot do so, she may be unable to fulfil the obligation to check on a claim for exemption in fulfilment of article 28.4 of the directive. That is why we suggest the slightly different wording for subsection (3).

These are complex matters, but I hope that I have explained the purpose of the amendments. They bring out into the open the concerns that the registrar expressed, of which I am sure the Minister and his officials are aware, and they give the Minister a chance to respond.

 

Mr. George Howarth: The power to serve an information notice under clause 42 is, as the hon. Gentleman said, new to data protection law. Indeed, it is one of the ways in which the Bill gives the commissioner greater powers than the registrar currently has. That power may be exercised when the commissioner has received a request for an assessment under clause 41--we discussed a few moments ago the arrangements that will exist in such cases--or when she reasonably requires information for the purposes of determining the data controller's compliance with the data protection principles.

The purpose of amendment No. 282 is to clarify whether an information notice may be used by the Data Protection Commissioner to seek the information that she needs in connection with the notification arrangements.

The short answer is that an information notice under clause 42 is not needed for that purpose. The arrangements for notification will be established under part III of the Bill. In particular, notification regulations will specify in detail the information with which data controllers will need to provide the commissioner in notifying her, and the form in which they must provide it. If the commissioner identifies a data controller whom she thinks should notify her but has not yet done so, or who has notified her but done so incompletely or incorrectly, she will be able to seek the necessary information under the arrangements in part III.

Mr. Greenway: Will the Minister confirm that the regulations--which the Minister and his officials still have the opportunity to consider--would extend to third parties and in particular to data processors, who would have a contractual obligation to a data controller?

Mr. Howarth: I understand the point that the hon. Gentleman makes, and I shall certainly think about it before any regulations are made. Further action is probably unnecessary, but I shall consider it.

Amendment 214 would widen the power provided by clause 42. It would do that in two ways. First, new subsection (3A) would make specific provision for the commissioner to serve a notice where a controller claimed an exemption from the principles. Secondly, new subsection (3B) would allow her to serve a notice on third parties whom she believed had relevant information. I shall deal with each part of the amendment in turn.

We do not believe that new subsection (3A) is necessary; it does not add anything. The commissioner may already serve an information notice where the controller claims an exemption. In such a case, the purpose of the notice is to seek information to allow the commissioner to consider whether the controller is in fact exempt from the principle or principles in question. To put it another way, it enables her to consider whether the processing in question complies with the principles as modified by the exemptions.

Turning to subsection (3B), we do not believe it right to give the commissioner power to serve an information notice on a third party. That answers the point that the hon. Member for Ryedale has just made. It is the controller's activities that the commissioner is investigating. It is right that she should be able to require him to provide her with the information that she needs. However, to extend that to third parties would, in our view, be to give the commissioner an unjustifiably wide power. It would effectively permit her to demand information from anyone at all, whatever their connection to a controller, on threat of criminal sanction. That would be excessive, intensive, and quite inappropriate to a regulatory authority of this nature, however general its remit.

Clause 42 is already a much stronger power than that in the 1984 Act, or, indeed, in the Bill as it was first brought forward. The Government brought forward amendments in another place to extend the grounds on which an information notice may be served. We accepted the view of the registrar that the provision as originally drafted was too narrow. We believe that it now strikes the right balance.

I hope that I can give the Hon. Gentleman a brief but helpful response to amendment No. 283. It is not necessary, as failure to comply with an information notice is already an offence by virtue of clause 46(1).

With regard to amendment No. 270, clause 42(9) allows a data controller on whom an information notice has been served to withhold information from the commissioner where that information would reveal evidence of the commission of an offence--other than an offence under the Bill--and expose him to proceedings for that offence. The amendment would add that a refusal to supply the commissioner with information may not be used as a ``material fact'' in any proceedings against a data controller, other than proceedings under the Bill.

I assume from the explanation given by the hon. Member for Ryedale that the amendment is aimed at cases in which the information is properly withheld, on the ground of the privilege against self-incrimination. I assume that is what he means. The Government believe that the amendment is unnecessary. Clause 42(9) is similar in effect to the first part of section 34(9) of the 1984 Act, although that is concerned with subject access. We have no reason to believe that the section has not worked well or that it has not provided sufficient protection for data users. No further protection such as that suggested by the hon. Gentleman has been shown to be needed in practice.

Furthermore, data controllers are not required to articulate reasons for withholding information when they are entitled to do so. Information may properly be withheld on grounds other than self-incrimination, such as legal professional privilege. Data controllers must supply required information, but may refer to information properly withheld--if they refer to it at all--in general terms. For example, reference may be made to one of the exemptions under clause 42.

Little could therefore be read into general conduct of that nature. It is highly unlikely to be capable of material evidence of the commission of a crime. There are other reasons for withholding information. Even if a data controller told the commissioner that he was relying on the self-incrimination exemption, it would be very difficult to use that in proceedings against him. There would be no indication that the information withheld related to the specific offence for which he was being tried or, indeed, to any offence. In any event, the information would have remarkably little value as evidence.

I hope that my explanation in respect of all the amendments has persuaded the hon. Member for Ryedale that they are not necessary. I understand that they are intended to test the way in which the arrangements will work. I hope that he will feel able to withdraw them.

Mr. Greenway: The Minister is exactly right--the Opposition have tested whether the provisions mean what we think they mean. I am grateful for his explanation.

I may have failed, with no reasonable excuse, to explain that amendment No. 283 sought to clarify the burden of proof, but we will let the matter pass. I am especially grateful for the Minister's assurance that he will consider our points about notification regulations. Clause 43

Special information notices

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 284, in page 25, line 23, after `information', insert

    `about the personal data being processed'.

This is a short amendment to seek clarification of the word ``information'' in line 23, in respect of special information notices. Information is required under a special information notice for the purpose that is mentioned in subsection (2). That begs the question of whether the information includes details of the data that are being processed.

Subsection (1) refers to information that is

    ``specified for the purpose specified in subsection (2).''

Subsection (2) states that that purpose is to discover

    ``whether the personal data are being processed only for the special purposes, or . . . with a view to . . . publication . . . of any journalistic, literary or artistic material which has not previously been published''.

That refers to the wide exemption in clause 31, which we discussed at length before the recess. Given the importance of the arrangement in clause 43, we must be sure that details of the data that are being processed are caught within the provisions.

Dr. Nick Palmer (Broxtowe): First, I apologise to the Committee for being late--I was stuck in a lift with six other people for 25 minutes.

The amendment does not deal with the questions that would usually arise under clauses 31, 41 and 43(1) on the processing of data. It is unusual for data controllers to be asked about data; they are usually asked about the uses to which data are put. The amendment would restrict the registrar to asking about data, but it is more likely that she will want to quiz the data controller about how he or she uses the data.

Mr. George Howarth: Clause 43 is a crucial part of the arrangements that the Bill makes in connection with processing for the special purposes. It provides an essential safeguard against abuse of the arrangements for such processing.

The Committee will recall--my hon. Friend the Member for Broxtowe (Dr. Palmer) referred to this--that clause 31 allows data controllers who are processing for the special purposes to make a claim to that effect in order to stay judicial proceedings that might be under way in connection with their processing.

There needs to be a mechanism for determining how long the stay on proceedings should operate. That turns on whether the controller's claim is justified. Clause 43 is, therefore, a crucial part of the mechanism.

The power that it provides may also be used where the commissioner has received a request for an assessment of processing for the special purposes. The commissioner has no power to issue what might be termed an ordinary information notice under clause 42 until she has determined that the same criteria that would justify a continued stay on proceedings are not satisfied. That partly answers the point that my hon. Friend the Member for Broxtowe made.

The power is especially important in cases where a claim has been made under clause 31. It allows the commissioner to issue a special information notice for the purposes of determining whether the criteria justifying the continuation of the stay are satisfied.

It is a very closely focused provision. It relates only to processing for the special purposes; and it gives the commissioner a very specific power. It is, in fact, already so closely focused that it achieves the purpose suggested by the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Ryedale. That would require the special information notice to specify that the information that the data controller was required to provide was about the personal data being processed for the special purposes. It would do so by adding the relevant words in subsection (1).

The additonal words are not needed, however, as they are already covered by the wording of subsection (2). That makes it clear that the information that the commissioner may seek in a special information notice relates to whether the personal data in question are being processed only for the special purposes; or whether those particular data are being processed with a view to the publication of special purposes material which have not previously been published by the data controller.

In that way the clause already provides that the target of a special information notice must be the particular data being processed for the special purposes, and whose processing has triggered the issue of the notice.

Although the perspective of my explanation may have been slightly different from what the hon. Member for Rydale expected, I hope that I have allayed his fears.

 

Mr. Greenway: Let me narrow the matter down to one point. What is important is that the special information notice does not allow the data controller to say that, because he wanted to claim the special exemption, he would not release details of what he was processing. The Data Commissioner must be able to obtain information about the data: otherwise, she will not be in a position to assess whether the special purposes requirements are met. That is the key element of the debate.

Mr. Howarth: That issue is covered by the clause, which provides that a special information notice must be relevant to the particular data being processed. It must be justified in terms of the special purposes. That is the principle so the commissioner will have the power to examine any other data involved to ensure that they are consistent with the special purposes. There will be no barrier to any inquiry into whether the special purposes are justified or relevant.

I understand the point made by the hon. Member for Ryedale, but I do not think that there is scope for the sort of abuse that he implies could take place.

Mr. Greenway: I am grateful to the Minister. The clause is an important one, and I suspect that we shall return on Report to some of what it contains, especially in relation to clause 31.

I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Rights of appeal

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 285, in page 27, line 25, after `notice', insert

    `setting out the grounds for the appeal'.

This is another amendment that deals with a point that has been raised by the Data Protection Registrar. Clause 47 provides an important right of appeal for data controllers and, potentially, for data processors on whom an enforcement notice, information notice or special information notice has been served. The Data Protection Registrar has expressed concern that there appears to be no requirement for the controller to specify the grounds for appeal. Data controllers should not be required to respond to oppressive or unduly onerous information notices. However, the registrar is worried about the practical effects of the appeal provisions if the data controller does not have to specify any particular grounds for the appeal. That has particular relevance to clause 28, under which such processes could be long and drawn out. The amendment would ensure that the Bill makes it clear whether grounds for appeal are required.

Mr. George Howarth: As the hon. Member for Ryedale said, clause 47(1) provides a right to appeal against an enforcement notice.

That is not an open-ended provision: it merely states that a right of appeal exists. The hon. Gentleman's amendment would require such an appeal to spell out the grounds on which it was made. The Government do not think that that is necessary. The details of the form and content of a notice of appeal are best dealt with by the Data Protection Tribunal rules, which the Secretary of State has the power to make under paragraph (7) of schedule 6. Clause 47 follows the corresponding provision in the Data Protection Act 1984 that relates to appeals against enforcement notices.

The current requirement for applicants to state their grounds for appeal appears in the Data Protection Tribunal Rules 1985 at rule 3(2)(c). Those arrangements have worked well so far and we see no need to change them in the Bill. However, we shall be drafting new tribunal rules and we shall bear the hon. Gentleman's points in mind in doing so. It is likely that a provision similar to the present rule 3(2)(c) will be needed in the new rules. In the light of that indication of our intentions, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will agree to withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Greenway: I am grateful to the Minister. He clearly appreciates that this is an important matter and intends to deal with it under paragraph (7) of schedule 6. I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Clause 50

General duties of Commissioner

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 271, in page 29, line 14, leave out from `(a)' to `encourage'.

We are moving at such a pace that I need to pause for breath. This is a probing amendment, because I do not entirely understand the logic behind the provision--doubtless the Minister will try to persuade me that it is logical. The clause does not state that there must be codes of practice. One takes as read the Secretary of State's involvement via order, although we may come back to that point when we discuss amendment No. 272. It is left to the commissioner to decide whether it is appropriate to have codes of practice.

There ought to be codes of practice and I cannot think of grounds on which the commissioner might disagree. If, however, the Minister says that the wording is necessary to give flexibility because in some situations codes of practice would not be necessary, while in others, they would, I will be content. The clause seems to introduce an idea without providing certainty that it will be implemented.

Mr. George Howarth: The commissioner's general duties, as set out in clause 50, include the promotion of the following of good practice by data controllers. As part of that general duty, subsection (4)(a) requires her to encourage trade associations--that is, bodies representing data controllers--to develop codes of practice and to disseminate those to their members. The clause expressly says that that duty applies only where the commissioner considers it appropriate to do so. The amendment would remove that discretion.

It is difficult to see what value there would be in requiring the commissioner, in all circumstances--even those where it was inappropriate to do so--to encourage the promotion of codes. For example, to require her to encourage codes where they are unnecessary, or where such an intitiative was unlikely to succeed, would seem to be an unproductive use of her time and resources. The Committee should consider the fact that that could also lead to a lot of unnecessary work for trade associations, which might have to repond to requests for codes that were neither necessary nor useful. The commissioner needs to be in a position to determine for herself, in the light of all the relevant information and circumstances, whether encouraging a code would be appropriate at that particular time.

The Bill's current approach--that the commissioner should encourage codes where she considers it appropriate to do so--continues the approach in section 36(4) of the 1984 Act. The words that the amendment would delete are drawn directly from that Act. The commissioner's duty to encourage codes must also be considered in the context of her other duties in respect of codes. In addition to encouraging trade associations to issue codes, she must consider and give her opinion on codes drawn up by trade associations and prepare codes where she considers it appropriate to do so, or is directed to do so by the Secretary of State.

Taken together, the different provisions provide for considerable involvement by the commissioner in the development of codes.

As the hon. Member for Ryedale rightly said, codes have an important potential role to play in developing and fleshing out for particular sectors the general rules that the Bill establishes. I am sure that the commissioner will take full advantage of the opportunity that they provide, but there is no advantage in making the changes suggested by the hon Gentleman. The flexibility which the commissioner will have is sufficient to avoid unnecessary bureaucracy, while keeping a watchful eye on matters. With those assurances, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will seek leave to withdraw the amendments.

Mr. Greenway: I am grateful for the Minister's explanation, and I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendnent No. 272, in page 29, line 42, leave out ``the Secretary of State'' and insert ``Parliament''.

This is a brief amendment. Subsection (8) allows the commissioner to charge such sums as she may, with the consent of the Secretary of State, determine for the services provided by the commissioner in respect of all the sections under part VI. It is for the Committee to consider whether the consent of Parliament, rather than of the Secretary of State, might be more appropriate. We shall discuss later the appropriate procedure for a number of order-making powers in the Bill; I believe that Parliament should consent to the fees that are charged, because it is an important power for the commissioner.

Mr. Howarth: Clause 50(8) permits the commissioner, with the Secretary of State's consent, to charge for the services that she provides under part VI of the Bill. It is an important new power which the registrar has been pressing for. At present, she has no power to charge for any of the services provided by her office.

The commissioner believes that it is desirable that she should be able to charge for many of the incidental services that she provides, and the Government agree. For example, the commissioner has no power to recoup the costs of expenditure involved in organising conferences or in speaking at conferences organised by others. In the circumstances, it is reasonable that those powers should be granted.

The power would also enable the commissioner to charge for publications and other promotional material. In many cases she may not wish to charge for them; she may take the view that it is better to ensure that they are available rather than to inhibit their dissemination by charging for them. For example, single copies of promotional material which involve comparatively little expense nevertheless might have an important information role. It might be appropriate not to make a charge in those circumstances.

However, the commissioner may wish to charge for the bulk orders of videos or other promotional material, which can be a drain on what is often a tight budget. Being able to charge in such circumstances helps limited resources to be stretched further, which I am sure the Committee will agree is important.

Whether the commissioner should have the power to charge for services is, as the hon. Gentleman said, properly a matter for Parliament: it is an issue of principle. However, whether the commissioner should seek the consent of Parliament for the detailed charging arrangements is a matter of a different order. It is doubtful whether parliamentary oversight at this level would be an effective use of parliamentary time. We are discussing ancillary services which currently consume only about 10 per cent. of the registrar's budget of some £3.7 million. That is not a major amount of money for Parliament to deliberate on, although it is important.

It is essential that there should be proper and rigorous control over the charging regime. However, the system of control needs to be sufficiently flexible to permit timely responses to changing circumstances. Requiring the commissioner to seek the Secretary of State's consent satisfies the safeguards that are necessary because of the circumstances and the sums involved.

With that explanation of the scale of the operation and of the issues about which we are concerned, I hope that the hon. Member for Ryedale is happy with our proposal and will not press his amendment.

Clause 51

Reports and codes of practice to be laid before Parliament

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 273, in page 30, line 3, at end insert--

    `(1A) Each House of Parliament shall debate the reports laid before it by the Commissioner on an annual basis and within four months of each annual report being laid.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take new clause 3--Reports and codes of practice to be laid before parliament--

Unlawful obtaining etc. of personal data

Mr. George Howarth: This clause creates offences concerned with obtaining, disclosing and procuring the disclosure of information without the consent of the data controller. Amendment No. 274 raises the issue of how that consent is to be signified, and the hon. Member for Ryedale gave examples of the confusing messages that might be involved.

To set the amendment in its context, it may be helpful if I say something about the background and purpose of the clause. It has its origins in subsection (6) and subsequent provisions of section 5 of the 1984 Act. Those provisions made it an offence to procure the disclosure of personal data in contravention of the data user's register entry. It is also an offence to sell of offer for sale any data obtained. The provisions were included in the 1984 Act by the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994. They responded to concerns about the activities of private investigators who made a business out of obtaining personal information. Parliament decided that that was undesirable and outlawed such activities.

The principle intention behind the clause is to preserve those very important and worthwhile provisions from the 1984 Act. We have, however, had to set about dealing with the matter in a different way. Under the 1984 Act, the offence is based on breach of the data user's register entry. That approach would not work in the Bill. As the Committee knows, notification under the Bill is very different from registration under the Act. They are totally separate. The notification arrangements cannot be used in the same way, for example, to create an offence of disclosing data. It was necessary to start from scratch.

As the register entry can no longer be used as the base point for determining what is lawful, the Government have had to find an alternative. We have adopted the approach of basing the offence on the absence of the data controller's consent. In other words, if the data controller consents to a particular disclosure, it is lawful. If he does not, it is an offence, subject to the defences for which the clause provides.

Among other things, the offence will cover cases such as those in which a person calls up information on a computer and reads it without doing anything more. The Committee will recall that in the case of Regina v. Brown, such activity was not found to be using personal data under the 1984 Act.

12.15 pm

The amendment would broaden the offence by making it unlawful to disclose personal data without the controller's written consent. That is not a practical approach. It may be relatively straightforward to provide for written consent to disclosures by a data controller's employees, provided that their processing activities are fairly routine and predictable, but in every organisation the unexpected sometimes occurs. Will people confronted by unique or unusual circumstances, in which immediate response is necessary, be required to delay dealing with those circumstances while they seek written consent to make a specific disclosure?

The clause will also make obtaining information about the controller's consent unlawful. In many organisations, obtaining personal data within the requirements of the data protection principles is a routine occurrence, and may, indeed, be central to the organisation's activities. If the amendment were accepted, every person would be required to have the controller's written consent to obtain information. It is difficult to see how that would work in practice.

Requiring written consent is not a sensible way of dealing with the problem, and it is not desirable. Any additional protection that written consent may offer is more than offset by the additional burdens that it would place on data controllers. Contrary to the amendment, we believe that persons obtaining or disclosing someone else's data should be able to rely on the general law of consents, which the courts are very used to applying, for example, in the law of theft of property, which has many parallels with the offences under discussion.

Amendment No. 217 raises the issue of the scope of the offence and asks whether defence should be made on the ground that the obtainment, disclosure or procurement is in the public interest. The Government are firmly convinced that such defence should be possible. Subsection (2) deals with a number of defences. They cover matters such as the prevention or detection of crime, statutory or other legal authority, and the reasonable belief of the person in question that he was acting with legal authority or with the consent of the data controller.

Those provisions do not, however, cover all circumstances in which it might be justifiable to obtain or to disclose data without the consent of the data controller. Let me give some examples, as requested by the hon. Member for Ryedale.

A ``whistle-blowing'' employee might want to draw attention to the proper authority about the behaviour of his employer, which is so seriously unacceptable, without necessarily being a criminal offence, that it should not continue. Unauthorised obtaining or disclosure may be justified, for example, when environmental safety is at stake or when public health is at issue. There may be circumstances in which someone would want to pass on, or obtain, information and it is of great importance to the public and to other agencies to know about it.

The Committee has had important debates about processing for special purposes; both sides of the Committee agree that it is necessary to ensure that the Bill does not inhibit freedom of expression by the media. Without the public interest defence in clause 54, journalists might find it difficult to obtain the information that they need to do their investigative public interest work. No doubt there are many other examples about the need for this defence; that is what makes it difficult to include an exhaustive list of situations in the Bill.

The hon. Member for Ryedale has hesitations about the defence, which I understand. I agree with him that many share his reservations, but I hope that I have been able to persuade him that it is necessarily so for good reason. Moreover, I draw his attention to the inclusion of the word ``justified'' in the provision. It is not sufficient that the obtaining, disclosing or procuring should be in the public interest; it must also be justified as being in the public interest in all the circumstances of a particular case.

That makes the threshold for the test being satisfied a little higher than the simple public interest test: the clear undesirability of unauthorised obtaining or disclosure must be outweighed, of course. We have included it expressly to help to narrow the scope of the provision, to limit reliance on the exemption to cases in which there is a genuine justification for what is being done. Of course, even if the unauthorised obtaining or disclosure does not fall within the immediate criminal sanctions of the provision, a civil liberty liability remedy may be available or it may engage the commissioner's enforcement powers if they are carried out by the data controller.

It is a complicated matter and important issues are at stake. I appreciate that a fine judgment must be made, and we have probably got it about right. In those circumstances, I invite the hon. Gentleman to reconsider his amendment and perhaps seek leave to withdraw it.

Mr. Howarth: I shall speak first to new clause 5. It introduces a new offence to deal with what is known as enforced subject access, which we have debated in the past. That layman's term covers the circumstance when one person forces another to hand over his data protection subject access record as a condition of being considered for, or obtaining, some form of benefit, such as a job.

That happens mainly with criminal records. Some employers tell job applicants to hand over a data protection subject access printout obtained from the police if they want to be considered for a job. Sometimes they ask for Department of Social Security contributions records, believing that gaps in contributions show a period in custody. They may or may not be right in that belief, but they do it nevertheless. Also, some foreign embassies ask for convictions records from visa applicants. The police and the DSS believe that of the approximately 100,000 subject access applications they receive each year, the vast majority are enforced. Often the letters come in standard form, for example, as prescribed by employers.

It can be argued that, subject to the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974, convictions should be made known and that data protection subject access is just the means of providing that knowledge. Against that, it can equally be argued that forcing people to use a right originally given for their own protection is improper. Both points have some force, each within their own frame of reference. Reconciling them is extremely difficult, because so much depends on the circumstances--the information and the purpose for which it is wanted.

12.45 pm

In the case of convictions, Parliament has taken an important step towards such reconciliation. The Police Act 1997 provides new arrangements for criminal record checks. Three types of certificate will become available from a new Criminal Records Agency--the basic ones covering only unspent convictions, and the others including spent convictions. Who should be entitled to what will be set out by order? Once in place, the new arrangements should not be circumvented. In particular, we think it wrong for employers who are not entitled to see a higher level certificate to obtain information about spent convictions through the data protection route.

We issued a consultation letter in May last year discussing a range of possible approaches to enforced subject access--a criminal offence; a civil tort; Data Protection Commissioner enforcement notices; the Data Protection Commissioner filtering subject access applications; and allowing subject access only in person, not in writing. We considered the responses and the practicalities with great care. We believe that only an offence would work. The other possible solutions would variously weaken normal subject access, be burdensome or not sufficiently effective.

We do not propose an offence lightly. Criminalising behaviour places a special duty on the Government and Parliament to focus carefully on significant social problems. The offence is therefore tailored in several important ways. It is limited to certain types of records--criminal records, prison records and DSS records. Under amendment No. 256, the list is extendable by order subject to affirmative resolution--to which the hon. Member for Ryedale referred earlier--in case the practice is diverted elsewhere. The offence is committeed only where the data subject is required to provide the specified data. We do not want to criminalise straight requests for information. It is perfectly legitimate for a potential employer to ask a job applicant for details of any criminal record. It should be a crime only if he requires provision of that information through the applicant's subject access right.

We are confining the offence to the types of situations where systematic use is most likely to occur, and where people are least likely to be acting in their private, as distinct from their professional, capacity. Those are employment and appointments--which would include recruitment, continued employment, and preferment or promotion--placing contracts, and providing goods, facilities or services to the public. We propose defences where it can be shown that the requirement was authorised by law or court order, or was justified as being in the public interest. But bearing in mind the planned dedicated channel for the supply of criminal records under the Police Act 1997, the public interest defence will explicitly exclude crime prevention or detection. Anyone who has that as a genuine motive will have an alternative route for obtaining appropriate information.

Bearing in mind the close link we see with the introduction of criminal record certificates, amendment No. 257 provides that new clause 5 cannot be brought into force before they are available. As with other offences under the Bill, by virtue of clause 57 only the Data Protection Commissioner or the Director of Public Prosecutions could prosecute. The punishment on conviction would be a fine, and amendment No. 255 would allow a court also to order the forfeiture, erasure or destruction of documents.

I hope that that gives the Committee a clear picture of our proposals.
