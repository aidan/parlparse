Mrs. Spelman: There is some similarity between the subject of reasonable powers of inspection that we discussed in previous debates and the inspection of adoption and fostering agencies. The amendments would introduce an element of reasonableness. The Association of Directors of Social Services has advised me on current practice for rights of entry to private fostering and adoption agencies. The Children Act 1989 and related guidance do not make the situation clear and the association thinks that we have an opportunity to clarify matters. It advises inspectors to try to persuade private foster carers to allow entry when it has been refused, but if they are very worried that a child might be at risk of harm, section 48 of the Children Act gives them an ultimate right of entry. The consensus is, however, that social workers do not have the right to enter foster carers' homes without consent. The Bill extends the powers of inspectors to obtain entry to adoption and fostering services.

Amendment No. 228 is a probing amendment. It would set a limit on such entry and inspection so that it could take place only at a reasonable time of day. If inspectors are worried that a child is at risk, a knock at the door in the night would be disturbing for everyone, including the child. However, there would have to be a safeguard for inspectors who need to know what is happening at night.

Amendment No. 230, which is also a probing amendment, calls on the Minister to specify reasonable notice time in regulations. It is designed to introduce some reasonableness into the power of inspection by the registration authority. We are strongly of the view, as expressed in amendment No. 231, that inspectors should report their presence to someone in a position of authority when they arrive, at the very least to comply with health and safety regulations. It is important for someone who is intrinsically a stranger to the set-up formally to record his or her presence on the site. When I enter such an establishment, I am required, as a member of the public, to sign in and sign out. That allows the manager to know how many people are present if there is an emergency. It is not an unreasonable request.

Initially, the amendments seem analogous to the amendments on clause 29, but we think that it is worth repeating some of the discussions about fostering and adoption services, not least because in current practice there is a consensus that social workers do not have the right just to barge in. They are mindful of the need to obtain consent before undertaking an inspection. It would be helpful to clarify, in debate, how things stand. The amendments present an opportunity to establish what is a reasonable power of inspection by the new registration authority.

The amendment deals with one of the growing number of instances in which there is an interface between the courts and the Executive that works fine in one direction but not at all well in the other. It is a fact that about three fifths of children in care are or have been the subject of court proceedings. Obviously, the rest have not been the subject of such proceedings. Many of the children in both categories have care plans for adoption, which is what interested me in the key point, which is that the children in whose lives the court has been involved—whether through a formal care order or not—tend to do better. The 1998 study ``Someone Else's Children'' has been referred to several times. It shows that children who have been the subject of court proceedings benefit from better record keeping, assessment, decision making and, critically, care planning. As the Minister knows, the study took 27 local authorities into account. Judges are specialists in such cases, as are the lawyers who participate in them. They build up a huge amount of expertise and are able to make a considerable contribution to child welfare cases. They take a hands-on approach in planning for children and are a considerable national resource.

The problem that the amendment would address is that the court is unable to have any further involvement after the children leave it. An experienced family barrister who works in London was kind enough to give me a briefing about the matter. She said that she was amazed that a judge could summon a director of social services to court, perhaps more than once, to discuss a care order. Judges know which are the bad local authorities, yet they still have to listen to the social services director make pledges in regard to the 12th, 13th or l4th case and then watch them walk out of court in the full knowledge that nothing will be done.
