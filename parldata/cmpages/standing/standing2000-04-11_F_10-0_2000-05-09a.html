Clause 21

Guidance to local councils

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 130, in page 9, line 26, at end insert

    `and in doing so must confirm the proportion of a local council budget which may be allocated at the discretion of the local council'.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Mr. Malcolm Wicks): In many ways it is too early to quote the words of London mayors but, as I was saying before I was interrupted, between 10 per cent. and 15 per cent. of local learning and skills councils' funds will be outside the national formula approach for activities where such a funding approach is not appropriate. That will include work force development and funding to support improvement in the standards and quality of local providers. For those funds, local LSCs will make decisions about pricing as well as allocations. Within that percentage band, there will also be a significant local initiatives fund. That will enable local LSCs to support important local learning and skills initiatives that would not otherwise be funded because they do not fit easily into the categories of activity that attract mainstream funding.

In January, we proposed, as a further area of local LSC discretion, the power to vary national prices within limits set by the national council. It is worth emphasising that, in terms of our discussion on local discretion, the ability to vary national prices means much more local discretion for the local councils than might at first sight appear to be the case. A local LSC might want to exercise discretion if, for example, it thought that that would help to tackle local shortages of good-quality training for skills that are in particular demand.

The second technical consultation paper on funding will be published shortly. It will set out in much greater depth than my brief overview the Government's proposals for the LSC's funding and allocations system and the flexibility and discretion that will rest with the local LSCs. I hope that, given those assurances, the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) will withdraw the amendment.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Mr. Michael Wills): It will not surprise the hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell) that we agree entirely that the LSC should consult representatives of education providers. It is eminently sensible for it to do so when it prepares its guidance and it is vital that the sector is fully involved in the process. The requirement for the LSC to consult local education authorities, with their statutory responsibilities for education, gets us some of the way towards the amendment, not least as LEAs will also play a leading role in consulting education providers for their own education development plans and take a central part in local learning partnerships. However, there are, as ever, problems with the definitions that are necessary in respect of such a duty to specify which representatives should be included and excluded.

The objective that the hon. Gentleman and I share can be achieved sensibly by including such a requirement in the guidance that we offer to the national LSC. It is probable that the council would in any event wish to consult representatives of education providers with the aim of improving the guidelines. It is also probable that it would wish to extend consultation on the guidance to representatives of training providers.

The hon. Gentleman seeks in amendment No. 131 to remove RDAs from post-16 matters, just as his noble Friends did in another place. In their place, the amendment would require the national council to consult organisations that represent business, including small business, on the guidance that it intends to give to its local LSC. Whether the hon. Gentleman supports RDAs in principle or not, the fact remains that they have a statutory responsibility to produce regional skills strategies, so it is vital that the LSC's guidance must include their input. As RDAs are chaired by people with substantial business or commercial experience, they will be well placed to reach out to the business world and to reflect its views in commenting to the local LSC on the national council's guidance.

Although RDAs are new organisations, they are already developing an influential role in driving forward regional strategies for sustainable economic development, regeneration and social exclusion. They have contributed to the success of the new arrangements by making recommendations to the Government on the number and boundaries of local LSCs proposed in their respective regions, which the Government were pleased to accept in almost every case.

In view of the RDAs' statutory responsibilities and their achievements to date, we believe that it is right that their role should be explicitly recognised in the Bill. That is why the Bill stipulates that RDAs should be consulted by the national council on its draft guidance to local LSCs, by the local LSCs on their draft annual plans and by the national council on whether it should approve the final annual plans of local LSCs in their regions.

As a body that is chaired by a business leader, and as 40 per cent. of its membership will come from a business background, the LSC will be well equipped to ensure that the annual guidance reflects a business perspective. For the sake of providing clarity to the hon. Member for Daventry and my hon. Friend the Member for Blackpool, South (Mr. Marsden), I remind the Committee that the guidance will inevitably include the perspective of small businesses. We have already discussed the close links with the Small Business Service and with other business organisations at a national level that will inform the guidance. As with representatives of education providers, we want to reflect those principles in the guidance that we give to the LSC on its consultation. I do not see the need for the national council to go as far as to consult local business representatives before it gives its guidance to local councils at the beginning of the planning process. The local LSCs will work closely with business representatives when they prepare the local plans.

I do not see the need for such consultation to be a factor in the Secretary of State's direction either. Amendment No. 138 would require the Secretary of State to consult business whenever he decides to issue a direction to the Learning and Skills Council under clause 25. Naturally, if a direction were considered necessary and it was appropriate to consult business-or, indeed, any other group-before making it, we would do so. However, in some contexts it would be a nonsense, or even improper, to consult business-for example, if the direction concerned the LSC failing in its statutory duties with respect to a person with learning difficulties, or if mismanagement had been found at a college. Such consultation would only delay the direction and the resolution of the problem, and speed is often of the essence in such matters. It is difficult to see what interest the wider business community would have in those matters that could not be expressed through the representatives on local councils.

I am sure that the hon. Gentleman would not intend to impose such a constraint on the Secretary of State's powers to direct the council, as these are an important safeguard. I hope that the reassurances that I have given about our commitment to involve the business community and others will allow him to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Boswell: The bedside manner is out with a vengeance this afternoon. The Minister did well in committing himself to consultation all round, which I welcome. That is sufficient, and I would even go so far as to say that I can see certain technical problems with amendment No. 138 with respect to consultation at large on matters that should not be the specific remit of business. That is the kind of untidiness that arises when the Opposition draft amendments.

The debate has to some extent been foreshadowed by earlier debates about individuals' needs for education and training as opposed to the demands of employers in the local labour market. The Minister may argue that the existing wording covers the skills needs of the local labour market as well as the needs of the local population.     The local LSC is required to prepare a plan that includes a ``statement of the needs regarding education and training of the population of the Local Council's area. We submit that there is no harm in that, if that meaning is implicit in the existing wording. The amendment would give the Government an opportunity to spell it out even more clearly.
