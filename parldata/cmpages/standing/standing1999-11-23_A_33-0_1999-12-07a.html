Mr. Tim Loughton (East Worthing and Shoreham): I come in at the tail end of consideration of this clause with a query. Subsection (6) empowers the authority to

Subsection (6) would enable the FSA to publish the record or to sell information contained in it. Clause 305

Restrictions on disclosure of confidential information by Authority etc

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson): I beg to move amendment No. 947, in page 154, line 27, at end insert—

    '( ) a person appointed to make a report under section 136.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments: No. 900, in page 154, line 23, after `employed', insert

    `or engaged'.

No. 901, in page 154, line 29, at end insert

    `or who has been appointed by the Authority or the Secretary of State to carry out a function under this Act'.

No. 902, in page 154, line 34, at end insert

    `, Chapter VI of Part XVI or Chapter II of Part XVII.'.

Miss Johnson: As a mixture of Government and Opposition amendments have been tabled to the clause, it might be helpful if I first outlined its purpose and background. The FSA, together with the Secretary of State, will need access to a great deal of confidential information to carry out its functions under the Bill. Other parts of the Bill provide it with the powers it will need to obtain that. This part of the Bill imposes the corresponding safeguards to reassure businesses that confidential matters will be kept confidential subject only to being disclosed when that is necessary for a proper regulatory purpose.

The present separate regulations for insurance and banking are already subject to a statutory obligation of confidentiality which, however, allows the disclosure of information when that is necessary for certain specific purposes, for example for the investigation and prosecution of crime. Those exemptions are often referred to as gateways through which information may be passed. The restrictions give effect to a number of EC directives which cover the different financial sectors of the financial services industry. The provisions of these directives are quite detailed in their effect and, in order to ensure that the UK continues to conform to them, it is proposed that this part of the Bill, together with the regulations that will be made under it, will be used to create a confidentiality regime, which is similar in effect to those that already exist in the different areas of investment business and banking.

The creation of a single regulator means that there will be an opportunity for some rationalisation of the existing structure. It establishes a basic principle regarding information about a person's business or other affairs that is disclosed to the FSA—the competent authority, which hon. Members will recall is a role that will now be taken on by the FSA—the Secretary of State, FSA employees, or an auditor or expert appointed to assist the FSA, known collectively as primary recipients of such information. The information will be regarded as confidential, subject to the qualification that it is not confidential if it has already been publicly disclosed, or it is in the form of a summary or collection of data from which it is not possible to ascertain information relating to a particular person.

When the information is established as confidential, the primary recipients, together with anyone who has directly or indirectly obtained it from a primary recipient, are prohibited from disclosing it further unless the person from whom it was obtained gives his consent. If the information relates to a third party, the consent of that person must also be obtained before disclosure may take place.

Government amendment No. 947 will ensure that a skilled person who is appointed to produce a report under clause 136 is included in clause 305 as a primary recipient. Although subsection 6(a) of clause 305 makes express provision for ``a competent person'' appointed under part X to be included as a primary recipient, doubt has arisen as to whether it is clear that the provision covers persons appointed to make a report at the request of the FSA or the Secretary of State.

I do not know whether you want me to respond to Opposition amendments, Mr. O'Brien, or whether it would be more helpful for Opposition Members to speak to them first.

Mr. Flight: Clause 306 will allow the Treasury to make regulations that will provide exemptions from the obligation of confidentiality imposed on primary recipients under clause 305. Amendment No. 904 is a probing amendment intended to determine the limitations on the Treasury in making those regulations. Clause 306 will not limit the scope of those regulations under clause 305.

Amendment No. 905 relates to clause 308, which will give the Treasury the power to make regulations that permit the disclosure of information by persons other than primary recipients or persons obtaining confidential information directly or indirectly from a primary recipient. The amendment makes the point that those regulations should not apply to information in privileged communications.

Miss Johnson: As I have said, clauses 305 and 306 relate to the gateways for disclosure. We must be guided by European directives in that regard. We propose to make regulations that provide the widest possible gateways, consistent with our directive obligations. We believe that that is right because all the gateways are intended to assist domestic or overseas regulatory or law enforcement bodies. Clearly, it would be undesirable to create unnecessary impediments to the work of those agencies.

In contrast, clause 308 deals with the disclosure of information not by the FSA but to it, and to other persons carrying out functions under the Bill. Such information is not subject to the directives, but it might be subject to other restrictions on disclosure. Clause 308 would allow the Treasury to make regulations removing such restrictions.

Amendments Nos. 903 and 905 seek to make explicit the provision preventing the FSA from disclosing privileged communications, either through the gateways provided for under clause 306 or as a result of regulations made by the Treasury under clause 308. The only privileged communications in the FSA's possession will be those voluntarily handed to it by authorised persons, because the FSA does not have the power to require a person to produce a privileged communication.

As in parts I and X, when a person volunteers a privileged document to the FSA, he must make clear that he is waiving that privilege only in respect of the FSA. If he does not, he risks losing that document's privileged status. If he makes it clear that he is not waiving privilege more generally, then, to the world at large, the document will remain privileged and the FSA will not be able to disclose it further without his consent. If he has not specified that he wishes the document to retain its privileged status, it could be argued that nothing would prevent the FSA from disclosing that document through its gateways, just as it can disclose other confidential documents.

I am satisfied that no privileged document could be further disclosed without the consent of the authorised person who volunteered it. That is achieved with the current wording of the clause. Although I agree with the intention of the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight), the amendments are not necessary.

Amendment No. 904 would limit the gateways that we could open under clause 306 to those concerned with the purposes of the Act and functions of the FSA, including its power to prosecute certain criminal offences. We cannot agree to that. We intend to open up the most appropriate gateways in a wide range of circumstances and consistent with the directives. The amendment would unnecessarily narrow the scope for those gateways, and would thus create impediments to interdepartmental co-operation. For example, the amendment would stop the FSA passing on to the Department of Trade and Industry information that it found out in the course of an investigation about possible breaches of the Companies Act, which is allowed by the directives.

Amendment No. 904 would also stop the FSA passing on information about an occupational pension scheme to the occupational pensions regulatory authority. If the FSA had information that could help prevent another Maxwell problem, should it be prevented from passing it on? We believe not.

In the light of what I have said about the implications of the amendment, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw it.

Mr. Flight: If I understood the Economic Secretary correctly, she said that, unless a person made it clear that he wanted the information that he provided to the FSA to be treated as privileged, it would not be so treated. That, I believe, was the net meaning of what she said. If that is so, it should be made clear in the Bill. The industry's general view is that, if it is given in an attempt to help, information provided to the FSA should be privileged. If the Bill is about to change the present situation—I do not know whether it will—it could cause considerable upset, even unintentionally. I believe that such information should automatically be privileged unless the FSA asks for it not to be, but if the position is to be as the hon. Lady described, it is important to draw attention to that fact.

I understand the Economic Secretary's comments about amendment No. 904, but I am somewhat uncomfortable that clause 306 does not limit the scope of regulations that the Treasury can make. I do not know the answer, but if the clause does not contain at least some indication of the territories covered, it will smack of the Stasi lurking in the background.

We shall probably not press the amendments to a Division, but I feel that the clause is too wide, and I ask the Government to consider whether it might be tightened up on Report. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 310

Authority's powers to participate

in proceedings

Miss Melanie Johnson: I beg to move amendment No. 948, in page 156, line 30, after `company' insert

    `or insolvent partnership'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 949 and 950.

Miss Johnson: The Insolvency Act 1986 provides that the directors of a company or partnership that is experiencing financial difficulties may propose that the company come to an arrangement with its creditors whereby the creditors agree to receive a proportion of their debts in full and final settlement rather than petition for the company to be wound up. In some cases, creditors may feel that such an arrangement is in their interests, for example, if it seems likely that the costs of an insolvency will consume the greater part of the company's resources.

The proposed arrangement must be put to meetings of shareholders and creditors. If both meetings produce a decision in favour of the arrangement, all the creditors are bound by it, regardless of whether they attended the creditors' meeting. That condition is subject only to the creditors' right to ask the court to take action when their interests have been unfairly prejudiced or some material irregularity has occurred in the conduct of one of the meetings.

The clause gives the FSA the same right as a creditor to petition the court in opposition to a proposed company voluntary arrangement as a creditor and the right to be heard by the court at a hearing of an objection made by another person. That is in line with our general policy of giving the FSA the same rights in insolvency procedures involving authorised persons as creditors so that it can stand in the shoes of consumers, as it were, to represent their interests.

The amendments reflect the fact that company voluntary arrangements can, despite their name, apply to partnerships as well as companies. The amendments make it clear that the FSA's powers extend to arrangements involving partnerships.

 

Mr. Flight: This may seem a rather simplistic point. I assume that the arrangements would apply when the FSA has become involved in taking over a business that it regulates and that is experiencing difficulty or has become insolvent. However, I am not clear about the context in which the FSA is stepping into shoes.

Miss Johnson: I do not think that that is the context in which the FSA would be in the consumer's shoes, as it were. It would become another party to possible insolvency proceedings and would represent the interests of people in such circumstances as though it were on a par with one of the creditors.

Mr. Flight: Does that mean when the FSA acts on behalf of consumers?

Miss Johnson: Yes.

Clause 318

Winding-up petitions: EEA and Treaty firms

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I question whether the clause is necessary. It prevents the FSA from presenting a petition to wind up a European economic area or treaty firm unless the permission of the home state regulator has been obtained. This is different from the present law as we understand it. Under part V of the Insolvency Act 1986, it is possible to petition for the winding up of overseas companies—for example, for non-payment of debts. This is an important protection for United Kingdom creditors, so should not be given away lightly. I am a little puzzled as to why the Bill withdraws the authority's power, or circumscribes it in the way that it does. We can find no provision in the investment services directive requiring such a clause. Perhaps the Minister has information about which EU directive specifies the need for it.

Miss Melanie Johnson: The clause reflects the principle that insolvency is of necessity a prudential matter which is the responsibility of the home state regulator rather than the host state regulator. An unrestricted right for a host state regulator to apply for the winding up of a passported firm would be inconsistent with the single market principle of national regulation. Clause 318 therefore prohibits the FSA from applying to wind up passporting firms, except in instances when it has been asked by the home state regulator to take action on its behalf. Of course, that restriction does not apply to other UK creditors of incoming firms, nor does it mean that the FSA is powerless to intervene in relation to an EEA or treaty firm without the co-operation of the home state regulator.

Clause 322

Petitions

Miss Melanie Johnson: I beg to move amendment No. 954, in page 161, line 38, leave out from `rules' to end of line 39.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 955 to 957.

Miss Johnson: The clause gives the FSA an entirely new power to petition for the bankruptcy, or, in Scotland, the sequestration, of the estate of sole traders carrying on financial services business. This is because there is no reason why the FSA should have powers in relation to companies and partnerships that it cannot exercise in relation to sole traders. We announced in our July 1998 consultation document our intention to put matters on all fours.

The clause provides that the FSA may present a petition for bankruptcy or sequestration in relation to any individual who is, or has been, an authorised person, or who is, or has been, carrying on financial services business without authorisation. Such a petition may be presented only if the individual appears to be unable, or to have no reasonable prospect of being able, to pay a ``regulated activity debt''—a debt incurred in the course of doing financial services business.

The key difference between the clause and clause 317 is that it does not provide for the bankruptcy of a sole trader on any ground other than inability to pay his debts. There is no equivalent of the power to wind up a company or partnership on the ground that it is ``just and equitable''. The reason is that it is not meaningful to apply such a concept to sole traders. A sole trader's business is not legally distinct from the proprietor's personal finances, and thus there is no separate entity to be ``wound up''. So long as a person can pay his debts, there is no way in which he can be declared bankrupt.

The amendments are technical changes to ensure that the powers that we are taking are consistent with the general law of bankruptcy as it applies in England and Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland.

Clause 334

Power of Authority to require restitution

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: Here, the authorities and not the courts will have the significant power to make a restitution order. I would prefer such an order to be subject to the normal disciplines of disclosure, procedural fairness and scrutiny that obtain in a court. If the FSA did that, it would be a markedly different route for the person at the sharp end, who would not have the same procedural defences as they would if the matter were subject to a court procedure. I realise, of course, that they could appeal against such an order and, presumably, take it to the tribunal, but that seems to be a secondary defence and it would be better if these cases, too, were subject to court proceedings. Why is the authority to be given these powers?

5.30 pm

Miss Johnson: The clause provides for the FSA to have the power to require restitution from authorised persons directly, without having to apply to the court—although those persons would, of course, have the right to refer the matter to the tribunal. It is a new statutory power, but the existing rules on self-regulating organisations already include the power to require redress. It will allow the most appropriate route to be followed in a particular case, as well as avoiding unnecessary duplication.

If the FSA is applying to the courts anyway for an injunction in relation to an authorised person and it believes that that person should pay restitution, it makes sense for the application to the court to cover both the injunction and any restitution. However, where the FSA uses its internal disciplinary procedures in relation to a particular contravention, it makes more sense for it to have a direct power to require restitution to be paid, rather than having to pursue concurrent disciplinary and court-based proceedings in relation to one set of facts. If a reference is then made to the tribunal, it will be appraised of the overall picture and therefore better placed to reach a just decision. In that sense, the power complements the FSA's range of disciplinary powers in relation to authorised persons. In many ways, securing appropriate consumer redress and discipline in an authorised firm are two sides of the same coin. The clause does not apply to unauthorised persons.

If this power were removed, as the hon. Member for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory) appears to argue for, it would be more complex for the FSA to secure redress for those who had lost out as a result of a contravention by an authorised person. It would also mean that where a person exercised their right to refer the disciplinary case to the tribunal, two separate judicial bodies would be considering the same set of facts. That is clearly an unnecessary duplication and could lead to complications between the court and the tribunal—they could have conflicting opinions on the case or about which body was more expert in the area. I can see no benefit in removing that power.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I thought that the Minister presented that beautifully, so I shall not pursue the matter.
