Mr. Andrew Robathan (Blaby): I beg to move,

    That during proceedings on the Referendums Bill the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

I find myself in the unusual position it may be unique in my parliamentary career of sitting on the Front Bench. I am not troubled whether it is unique, but it is unusual for me to have the opportunity of introducing a Bill to a Committee. I hope that the Committee will bear with me if I occasionally seem less informed than the Minister with his phalanx of civil servants in this case four sitting behind him.

I do not want to reiterate all the points made on Second Reading, but it is worth reminding the Committee of some of the most important issues in the Bill. First, whether we like it or not, referendums are now part of the political scene in the United Kingdom. The Neill committee clearly said that referendums, along with other electoral practices, were in need of regulation. I know that the Government agree because they set up the Neill committee.

I have said all along that I would be happy for the Government to take over the Bill if I had an absolute assurance that they would introduce similar provisions as per the Neill committee's recommendations, before any other referendum were called in this country. At least two referendums may be called in the near future one on economic and monetary union and another on the voting system.

The Bill is simple. It sets up an electoral commission called the referendums commission. Neill referred to an electoral commission because he was dealing with elections in the wider sense. My Bill deals specifically with a referendums commission, but I am not bothered about the name. If the Government want to introduce a Bill later, they can change the name of the commission, if they so wish.

There are two main provisions. The first is to ensure that both sides in a referendum campaign are given the same amount of money from public funds. that should prevent what happened in the Welsh referendum, when one side had a great deal of money and the other side none. In a democracy, both sides must be heard. The second major provision is that all sides have the same access to the media particularly the broadcast media, which is all that the Government can realistically legislate on.

As I said, the Bill is simple and has support across the House. It enacts the Neill committee's recommendations. It is important not to establish commissions Nolan or Neill and then ignore what they say. The Bill should have the support of all democrats.

I have already said that I would welcome a commitment from the Government to introduce these measures before another referendum is held, but the commitment must be made on the Floor of the House by the Secretary of State. Nudges and winks are insufficient. I have been a Member of Parliament for seven years some members of the Committee have been Members for a great deal longer and I have to say that I have a deep distrust of "not quite" commitments made by Governments.

I look forward to hearing what the Minister has to say. I am concerned that one of the amendments would take out one of the major provisions of the Bill, but we will come to that later.

How we move from that principle into practice is less clear cut.

We have had 11 recommendations from the Neill committee on referendums and many others in the report overlap with them, including those on the electoral commission, the reporting of donations by political parties, the ban on foreign funding and the registration of campaign organisations at elections. There is also the possibility of going beyond Neill to provide for generic machinery for the conduct of referendums. Those are all serious issues that need serious consideration.

    "been conceived as part of an overall integrated scheme for the reform of the funding of political parties" .

    "a direct linkeage between all the recommendations, which should be viewed as a whole" .

As a Government we wholeheartedly concur with the Neill committee's approach: this should be dealt with as a whole rather than a series of small parts. What is to be gained from setting up a referendums commission now when there is no immediate prospect of another referendum?
