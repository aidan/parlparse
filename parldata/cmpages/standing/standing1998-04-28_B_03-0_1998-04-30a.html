Mr. Clappison: The grouping of amendments is quite a mouthful, and so I will try to be brief in speaking to it. Amendment No. 294 addresses the procedure that should be followed on anti-social behaviour orders.

The Government stressed that such orders will be civil orders. The Minister has made that point repeatedly. The Government's consultation paper set out arguments for and against hearing the orders in county courts. Why have the Government decided against using county courts as venues for the order? It is not enough to describe the orders as civil orders. Tribunals will not look at the label that is pinned on the order, but they will consider its effect when they come to make decisions.

I turn to the matter of procedure. We have received guidance notes that reveal more detail about the procedure that the Government wish to follow. However, we seek clarification on a number of points. Applications for the order will be made by complaint to magistrates courts. Those of us who are not familiar with the technicalities of magistrates courts assume that defendants will always be given notice that application has been made for an order. As with other hearings in magistrates courts, defendants make the choice to attend.

We are anxious to learn whether defendants will have a full opportunity to put their case and to test the case made by applicants. That is why we tabled amendment No. 34. The Government must guard against possible challenges being mounted through the European convention on human rights, as we mentioned earlier. Procedure will be examined closely in the course of such challenges.

We have been told that such matters will be governed by the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980. The Act's provisions on procedure and rules in magistrates courts specify that defendants have the opportunity to be heard. We know that courts must hear evidence from both parties. But how far will the procedure extend in giving defendants the opportunity to test applicants' evidence? Will defendants be able to put questions to witnesses brought by applicants in contested hearings? Naturally, witnesses who are victims of the alleged anti-social behaviour may be vulnerable. What will happen in such cases?

Amendment No. 34 sets out our ideas on achieving an appropriate balance. We must give defendants the opportunity to make their case and to test the case made by applicants, but we must also safeguard the interests of witnesses. Some witnesses, especially those who are victims, will be afraid of defendants. We are alive to the possibility of witness intimidation. Amendment No. 34 would protect a witness who is the victim on whose behalf an anti-social behaviour application is made. It would ensure that questions cannot be asked of such a witness except with the leave of the court. Moreover,

    ``no question shall be asked of any person who has suffered harassment, alarm or distress or who is to be protected from harassment, alarm or distress, and the identity of any such person shall not be disclosed''

With regard to Amendment No. 10, we would like clarification of the Government's position on children and young persons. We are mindful of the need to protect young people against whom an order might be sought, and who might be in a particularly sensitive position. The Bill should grant magistrates courts the power to apply to a priest, or someone similar, for a report on the family circumstances of the young person in question. The person might then breach the order, get into further trouble, and gain a criminal conviction. At that stage a report would be prepared as part and parcel of criminal procedure, because the young person would be facing a serious criminal sentence of five years' detention. The young person's family circumstances would no doubt come to light at the time of the report's preparation. However, by that point he or she would have acquired a criminal conviction. It would be helpful if the Bill dealt with that issue. The Government do not envisage many such orders being sought against young people, but this would be a useful safeguard in the event of the few that do arise.
