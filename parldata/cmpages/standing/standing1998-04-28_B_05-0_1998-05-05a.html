Mr. Humfrey Malins (Woking): Amendment No. 86 relates to a matter to which the Committee should return. I appreciate that we have had this argument once on an earlier clause and it is not right to detain the Committee for too long on it. I remain concerned because the proceedings we are discussing are quasi-criminal. It is perfectly right that an order is made in a civil case on the balance of probabilities, and if it is breached there is still imprisonment. Prison is prison. Whether a person goes to prison at the end of injunction proceedings, contempt of court, or at the end of criminal proceedings, it is all effectively the same. However, there is a fundamental difference for the person who is going to prison.

Under the Bill, proceedings begin as civil matters but turn into criminal proceedings. Anyone who goes to prison for breaching one of the orders that we are considering will have committed a criminal offence. That is very different from going to prison for breaking an injunction. In my career practising matrimonial law I have come across husbands who have been sent to prison for the breach of an injunction in matrimonial proceedings. There is no criminal record. That is a critical difference. The presence of the criminal record is a powerful argument to distinguish betweeen the two sets of circumstances and for introducing a standard of proof as high as the criminal standard.

When we discussed the matter earlier we were talking about the procedures in magistrates courts on the hearing of complaints. I took the opportunity of reading the House of Lords debates to see what would happen in proceedings in the magistrates court in terms of evidential matters. My understanding of what was said in the other place was confirmed by reading the speech by Lord Williams of Mostyn in Committee, when he dealt with the question of the standard of proof being beyond reasonable doubt on the very point that we are discussing this afternoon.

Other noble Lords had asked him about the procedural details that would take place on a complaint. He said:

    ``There is the specific question as to whether or not complainants have to attend. The complainants would not have to attend. Evidence can be given by the local authority witness or by the police witness . . . The Order stage is genuinely a civil process. There may well have been no actual offence.''--[Official Report, House of Lords, 3 February 1998; Vol. 585, c.560.]

Lord Williams of Mostyn was talking more in relation to the anti-social behaviour orders. But the point remains. If it is correct that evidence is given in hearsay form and accepted and if none of the normal rules of evidence in a criminal trial is to be followed, and translating that to what can happen in relation to a sex offender order, the following scenario is entirely possible: a sex offender who fulfils all the criteria, comes out of prison and is seen in a locality, acting not in a criminal way--that is very important--but hanging around in a park near where children play. That causes the parents of those children great anxiety. It is entirely possible that they could lodge a complaint with their councillor, who takes it up with the local authority, which says, ``Better to be safe than sorry; we shall seek a sex offender order, because we believe that it is necessary to protect the public from serious harm from this man.'' It is a belt-and-braces operation; the authority takes out the order; the complainant does not have to turn up, there do not have to be any witnesses other than members of the local authority and there is no cross-examination. The defendant may or may not have to be there, yet an order can be so framed as to prevent that man going into the park again.

I have given an extreme example, but it illustrates the fact that in the wrong hands--that is the point--these orders--this one as much as those we discussed earlier--can be rather sinister and over-used, especially in cases where much injustice could result. The Minister may say, ``Well, this will never happen,'' but who knows? At the end of the day, the policy decision to use these orders will be made by elected local politicians, and elected local politicians, whatever their political colour, although they must consult the police, are not required to take their advice. The local authority can act of its own volition and, at the end of the day, while we have elected local politicians, it is possible for these orders to be abused.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: The amendment would allow the police to appeal against the decision of a magistrates court not to make a sex offender order. That is attractive, but we think that it is wrong. The decision to grant a sex offender order rightly belongs to the magistrates court. In considering whether the order is justified the magistrates court uses the test that a reasonable man or the man on the Clapham omnibus would use. We are confident that magistrates will take this duty extremely seriously.

If magistrates err in law, the prosecution may go by way of case stated to the divisional court, or, if there is a procedural irregularity, it may go by way of judicial review. Where the police have been unable to make out a case on the facts to the satisfaction of the magistrates court, they should not be able to circumvent the court's decision.

In such circumstances, there is no reason why the police should not make a further application. These are not criminal proceedings--there is no double jeopardy.
