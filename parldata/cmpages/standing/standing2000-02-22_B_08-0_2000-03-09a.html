Mr. Johnson: We have a general policy of replacing the current regime with a system of licences under which anyone who operates within the reserved area needs a licence. Anyone who operates outside the reserved area who currently needs class licences will not need them any more. The clause specifies where people can operate within the monopoly area and not breach the law. We have suggested a definition of document exchange centres. They were introduced under the British Telecommunications Act 1981 as an exception to the monopoly area, but, after further consideration, it is necessary to amend the wording of the exception and the definition.

Document exchange is a system used by many professionals, especially lawyers and insurers, to exchange documents with other members of a document exchange A document exchange operator provides facilities for the exchange of documents, including the transportation between document exchange facilities. It is not intended that the system should offer door-to-door delivery. The system originated in provincial towns and around the Inns of Court to enable local lawyers and other professionals to have a pigeon hole system to exchange documents without relying on the postal system. Such centres exist elsewhere in Europe, but almost uniquely in the United Kingdom the document exchange system has become more sophisticated and now includes collection and delivery between document exchange by third parties.

For example, a solicitor in London can take material to the document exchange of which he is a member, which can then be transported to a document exchange in Liverpool for collection by a solicitor. The intention is not to permit end-to-end delivery and provide a potential viable alternative postal system to that of the licensed universal service provider outside the scope of the licensed area. Members must go to their own document exchange to drop off and collect mail. Document exchange is at present covered by licences in the United Kingdom. As the Committee knows, we are not planning to continue the use of class licences. The Postal services directive also deals with document exchange centres, requiring that they should not fall within the reserved area. That is why it is set out in the Bill as one of the exceptions to clause 6.

The reason why we seek to clarify the wording is that there has been difficulty with sham document exchanges being set up in an attempt to undermine the monopoly—for example, by creating a document exchange with a single member that is then serviced by a document exchange operator. It ends up as a door-to-door delivery within major cities in direct contravention of the reserved area and makes end-to-end delivery possible.

While it is recognised that it is extremely difficult to prevent abuse of the exceptions in clause 7, the amendments would limit the possibility of widespread abuse of the document exchange exception that could undermine the universal service.

Mr. Alan Johnson: My understanding is that the purpose of the amendments is to ensure adequate safeguards for the affairs of individuals. That is a worthy objective, on which we can all agree. However, we believe that the rights of individuals are protected sufficiently by the clause as drafted, and that the amendments would not increase those rights.

The amendments would impose an absolute duty on the commission and limit its discretion, without increasing the protection of individuals. For example, amendments Nos. 12 and 13, which propose, respectively, the deletion of ``so far as practicable'' and ``the Commission considers that'', would have the undesirable effect of leaving the commission with no discretion over entries in the register if a person were to object to an entry, on the grounds that

That would not achieve the objective identified by the hon. Gentleman.

Such a vacuum would result, inevitably, in delays, and the register would contain less information than would be desirable in the public interest. Mr. Duncan: I remain unconvinced. Simply to ``deem'' something to be acceptable for publication gives too much leeway to the commission and insufficient right to the person whose details are to be published to object. Clause 29(6)(a) states that if ``the person concerned consents'', they can be published; clause 29(6)(b) states that entering the matter in the register can be permitted if it is ``in the public interest''. There is not enough detail to instruct the commission not to publish. It is like the presumption of innocence or guilt: for the commercial facts of a company, which might emerge on the register, there should be a presumption of non-publication. In other words, the onus should be on the commission to prove that a company's accounts or history should be published. At present the commission may simply ``deem'' that the company has no legitimate objection. The presumption is the wrong way round.

I imagine that the details of a licence will appear on the register. It will contain commercial information that is essential to the publication of the licence details, such as the tariff charged, where the company may go and how many vehicles it may have. That information is relevant to the licence. It is commercial, but is not information to which a company seeking a licence could object. There may be other information about a company whose activities are broader than the performance of postal services which that company may not wish to see published by law on a register. The presumption is the wrong way round. It should be for the commission to prove that it has to publish the information, not for the commission simply to consider that it may include it. The emphasis is wrong, and we seek to press the matter to a Division.

Clause 30

Free services for the blind

Mr. Alan Johnson: I beg to move amendment No. 68, in page 17, line 24, after `Commission' insert

    `and the Council'.

This is a sensible and uncontroversial amendment, the purpose of which is to require the Secretary of State to consult the council as well as the commission before giving directions. Under the clause, the Secretary of State has power to give two directions, first, to impose as a condition of a relevant licence a requirement that the licence holder provide such free postal services to blind or partially sighted people as may be specified, and secondly, to direct it to bring that licence condition into force. The Secretary of State has the duty to consult the commission before making either direction. The amendment gives him the additional duty to consult the council before doing so.

The point of the clause is to provide a means, if circumstances require it, to ensure the continuation of the existing free services for the blind that we talked about on Tuesday. The Post Office currently provides those services on a voluntary basis; we need to ensure that its continuation is guaranteed.

The point of the requirement to consult the commission before making either direction is to ensure that it can express its views to the Secretary of State on what sort of services should be specified in the direction and whether the imposition of such a licence condition is necessary. On reflection, it makes sense for the Secretary of State to consult the council as well in order to receive its views. It represents the consumer—the user body—and the amendment will ensure that it is consulted along with the commission.

Mr. Duncan: We agree.
