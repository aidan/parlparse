Mr. Clappison: We deal now with provisions relating to the release of prisoners on licence, subject to home detention curfew. That is a matter on which the Opposition will have some observations to make.

Clause 91 provides for the release of prisoners on licence, with certain exceptions, and clause 92 provides for the inclusion of curfew conditions under such licences. It may therefore be convenient to discuss the two matters together.

Prisoners who may be released under such conditions are those serving short-term sentences, as set out in the Criminal Justice Act 1991. These are sentences of three months or more, but of less than four years. Prisoners will be eligible to apply for release on licence, subject to home detention curfew. If successful, they will be at liberty, but subject to a curfew, for part of the time that they would have spent in custody.

Amendment No. 200 would restrict eligibility for release on licence to short-term prisoners serving six months or more rather than three months or more, as specified in the Bill. Amendments Nos. 201 to 208 would restrict the periods that could be spent by prisoners on licence in home detention subject to a curfew The Opposition understand that that curfew will be operated electronically.

The amendments reflect our anxiety that the arrangements for early release through tagging will create too large a difference between the nominal length of the sentence and the period that prisoners spend in custody. Sentences will not mean what they say. Under the Criminal Justice Act 1991, prisoners serving sentences of three months or more, but less than four years, are automatically released on licence at the halfway point of their sentence.

Release on home detention curfew will be added to release on licence. Under the Criminal Justice Act 1991, a prisoner sentenced to six months' imprisonment which is the maximum sentence that the magistrates court can impose for a single offence is eligible for release after three months.

Under clause 91, a prisoner will be eligible for release after serving a quarter of his sentence. That means that a prisoner sentenced to six months' imprisonment will spend only about six weeks in physical custody. The Minister of State confirmed that in a letter, which was most helpful in disclosing the Government's intentions; but our opinion of those intentions is a different matter altogether.

We think the provision for early release is unlikelyto engender public confidence in sentencing. The Government profess to set great store by such confidence. The Committee will remember from earlier debates that confidence in sentencing is a great objective of the sentencing advisory panel to be created by the Government.

How will public confidence in sentencing be enhanced when the public discover that prisoners sentenced to six months' imprisonment will spend only six weeks in custody? The public will come to the conclusion that sentences do not mean what they say. They will not be impressed by arguments advanced by the Government in support of early release from sentences.

At Home Office Question Time, the Home Secretary spoke of giving prisoners time to prepare for their release. That is rather strange, when prisoners are already released at the halfway point of their sentences. As they spend such a short time in prison, they are more likely to spend time preparing for imprisonment. Is not half of the sentence long enough for prisoners to prepare for release?

Clause 102

Disclosure of information

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Michael: It is important to put a couple of points on the record about the clause. The approach which the Bill takes to tackling crime at its grass roots involves co-operation and partnership between agencies and partnerships both within and beyond the criminal justice system. That is particularly true in relation to crime reduction partnerships, youth offending teams and anti-social behaviour orders. Exchanging information is the life blood of these partnerships.

It is essential that there are clear powers to exchange that information, much of which is likely to be sensitive. It is important that the parties know how to share that information properly. There is a lot of misunderstanding about disclosure. Many bodies are unclear about how, or even whether they can do it. There are two stages to disclosing or exchanging information. Before public or statutory bodies can disclose information, they must establish that they have the power to do so and whether they have a responsibility to do so in the public interest. Once the question of power has been resolved, the disclosure must be carried out in a lawful manner.

The clause is about the first step establishing the power. The police have a general power of common law to disclose information for the prevention and detection of crime. Indeed, both the public and the Government expect them to use their powers and their knowledge to prevent and reduce crime and disorder. They also have statutory powers. The clause does not interfere with any of those important powers.

A number of public authorities collect information which will be central to the Bill's partnership approach. However, they may not have the powers to disclose this information to the police and others. The clause will provide those powers where they might otherwise be lacking. It is carefully drawn to provide a statutory authority for disclosure for those cases where none currently exists, so that the holder of the information has the say over what is disclosed and to meet the purposes of the Bill.

The clause empowers disclosure to and between the statutory authorities named in subsection (2): the police, local authorities, including social services, and the probation service. It does not give anyone a power to demand disclosure, to override or interfere with any statutory or common law duty of confidence, or the many existing and successful protocols for the sharing of information between, for instance, the police and the probation service; nor does it create a data free-for-all which would have severe implications for civil liberties.

In order both to facilitate the exchange of information and to ensure that it is done properly, the Home Office will provide guidance and draft protocols for use by the various agencies. We will work those out with the help of the Data Protection Registrar. All the agencies will appreciate the importance of following proper procedures and we are anxious to ensure that they are given the right guidance on how to do that. Equally, they must remember their responsibilities to work together and to act speedily to reduce crime and prevent offending.

If tempted to play safe and not to exchange or share information, agencies must ask what is in the public interest and what does common sense dictate. The sharing of information is a weapon against crime, and we must erode the position where the law is felt to shelter offenders with a convention of confidentiality, where the public is put at risk because agencies cannot be warned or cannot warn each other of the risk or where one agency cannot be confident that it can share what it knows because the law seems to be uncertain. The clause seeks to dispel that uncertainty.
