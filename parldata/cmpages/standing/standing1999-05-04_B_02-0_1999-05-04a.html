Mr. Woodward: The hon. Gentleman asks a perfectly reasonable question. The answer is that our proposal to hold the rates at their current level constitutes the beginning of a strategy. Clearly, the Government cannot ask the opposition to propose this country's policy on smuggling. The Government have got to tackle that worsening problem, which cost an extra £750 million last year. It is unfortunate that the hon. Gentleman is in a party that says that it wants to promote people's health, but pursues a policy that does nothing about that. An increase in smuggling is affecting some of the youngest and most vulnerable people. He should be aware that 82 per cent. of adult smokers start smoking in their teens and that more than 30 per cent. of 15-year-old girls smoke.

Mr. Derek Twigg: Will the hon. Gentleman give way on that point?

Mr. Woodward: Calm down. I will give the hon. Gentleman a turn in a moment.

Nearly 15 per cent. of 11 to 15-year-old girls smoke. The Government risk seeing those figures increase. The initiative to co-operate with legitimate retailers to curb the sale of cigarettes to children is welcome. I recognise that the Government are working on that. This is not a time to be trivial; we must be serious.

Mr. Twigg: Will the hon. Gentleman give way?

Mr. Woodward: If the hon. Gentleman can be serious, I will give way.

Mr. Twigg: Is the hon. Gentleman removing from the tobacco companies any blame for the 82 per cent. of young people who smoke? Mr. Woodward: Of course tobacco advertising plays a role, but it ill behoves the hon. Gentleman to make those remarks. Gentleman on how to deal with tobacco advertising.

We also need to consider the financial implications for the legitimate retailer, in particular the small trader or corner shop owner. Some 81 per cent. of retailers are worried about tobacco smuggling because it is destroying their livelihoods. As a result of smuggling, 90 per cent. have reported a decrease in sales and 45 per cent. have reported a dramatic decrease in sales.

Mr. Twigg: Will the hon. Gentleman give way?

Mr. Woodward: I shall not give way to the hon. Gentleman again.

Some 61 per cent. believe that smuggling and cross-border shopping is the most important reason for the decline. About 70,000 independent retailers are at risk, and some of them are having to close their shops. Labour Members may not be troubled by that, but they should be if their principal and fundamental strategy is not achieving the health aim that they desire.

Mr. Gardiner: Will the hon. Gentleman give way?

Mr. Woodward: I am sorry, but time is very tight.

It is clear that the policy is not going to achieve the end that hon. Members want. It is damaging the industry and the retailer, encouraging smuggling and not producing the income that the Government want. Evidence shows that it is not dealing with the crucial problem of discouraging young people from smoking.

The Government are fond of reviews. The time has come for a proper review of excise duty on tobacco and other products. It is clear that it is necessary to review the policy if we are not to take huge risks, not only with the economy but with people's health.
