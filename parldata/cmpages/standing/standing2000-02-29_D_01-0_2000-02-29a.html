Mr. Key: The Bill is of fundamental importance to the discipline system of the armed forces and will have a substantial impact on the chain of command, affecting not only senior officers, but non-commissioned officers and, of course, the vast law-abiding majority of service men and women. Far from being a tidying-up measure, the Bill is, in fact, of fundamental importance to the ethos and discipline of the military, and we heard much about that from all parts of the House on Second Reading.

I want to explore the reasons why we have a separate military system. The amendment brings us to the heart of the discrepancies between the systems of civilian and military law. That is important, because there is a body of opinion that suggests that there is no need for separate military law. We know that the British military has operated under systems of military law for good reasons over many centuries. That law was almost entirely derived from the practicalities created by the fact that the British military--two services for many centuries and, latterly, the Royal Air Force as well--served in all corners of the Earth. Clearly, it would have been unsatisfactory to have either no system of law or a British common law system, which would have been impossible to operate on, shall we say, the river Ganges in the 19th century. A system of military law was necessary. We accept that and reject the notion embraced by other countries in the European Union that there is no need for a body of military law.

The practical benefits of a system of military law are obvious in the heat of battle or in the theatre of war, where there must be a system of rules and regulations to ensure the maintenance of the discipline that has, as many hon. Members on both sides of the House have said in recent debates, been the foundation of the British Army's success down the centuries. We reject the idea that there should not be a military legal system, but we are concerned that, if we are to tinker with the system, we should do so in a holistic way. We should be concerned not only about the consequences of implementing the Human Rights Act 1998 in relation to the service discipline Acts, but about the Government's decision to omit from the Bill a significant number of areas in which we might have expected that they would wish to improve the lot of service men and women. That is the purpose of the amendment.

In the civilian world, we expect the police to operate within strict rules and guidelines, which have existed for many years. We consider it just, for example, that someone who is arrested by the police, taken off the street and placed in custody will have immediate access to a solicitor, and that a solicitor will be provided at the taxpayer's expense, for a long time and in complex cases, if appropriate. The purpose of the amendment is to discover why that is not the case in the military system.

It is important that the Committee should understand the reality of the situation. I will illustrate my point by referring to a case that sums up our concerns, although several outstanding cases would fall into that category. For example, nine RAF personnel at RAF Bruggen in Germany who are under investigation have chosen to be represented by civilian solicitors. There may be many reasons for that choice, and apparently there is nothing in the military regulations that precludes it, but the fact is that the Army criminal legal aid system is not universally seen as fair and unbiased. Many people want to have independent representation by civilian lawyers because they think that officers must be beholden in some way to the chain of command and are incapable of being independent.

There are many reasons why solicitors who assist military personnel are doubtful about the Bill. They are concerned about the terms of legal aid and about whether service personnel in England and Wales or elsewhere are getting a fair deal. There is also the question of a fair deal for the civilian solicitors involved. Army legal aid is capped, and answers that I have received to parliamentary questions confirm that the Government do not intend to increase it. I was told last week of one firm of solicitors that has an unpaid bill for £50,000 for four months work, and there are similar examples throughout the country.

The case with which I want to start occurred some time ago. An officer was arrested on 27 February 1997 and interviewed by the Royal Military Police five months later, on 2 July. He was charged on 22 December 1997, 11 months after his arrest, and it was not until then that he qualified for Army legal aid. Most citizens of this country would regard that as not only unacceptable but incredible--they would not believe it could happen.

What sort of man are we talking about? A brigadier described him, saying:

I am quite clear that he is without exception the best staff officer I have ever met in the Army. This assessment is based on the following characteristics: he is extremely intelligent and able; he is energetic and industrious. In short, I cannot speak too highly of his effectiveness and achievements as a member of my staff. From my own point of view, I trust him and he retains my confidence.

After the court martial, another officer wrote:

When he was acquitted we were all delighted at the outcome and we looked forward to seeing him back at work, not only because he clearly wanted to come back but also because we are a busy branch and there was much to do. The accused officer was suspended and, subsequently, administratively dismissed from the Army, having had a glitteringly bright career. That officer at no point had recourse to criminal legal aid from the Army, and it cost him almost £100,000 to seek his own representation.

The code of military honour must be consistent with the British way of behaving. The change that is occurring in the ethos of the service was highlighted by General de la Billiere in a Daily Mail article on Monday. I shall not bore the Committee by repeating it. This case, however, says it all and shows that there are real problems. Whatever else service law does, it must reflect the way in which the British do things: it must be demonstrably fair and proportionate; it must be applied sensitively and with common sense. Was it common sense and proportionate to court martial a colonel amidst a media frenzy of prurient reporting about his affair?

Was it sensible to subject the colonel, the Army, the Navy, the chiefs of staff, the naval officer with whom he had an affair and innumerable others to the embarrassment of a criminal trial for an offence that is not a criminal offence in civilian law? Was it sensitive to allow the forces to suffer the spectacle of a four star admiral giving defence evidence at a court martial for an army officer who was being prosecuted under military law for an affair conducted while the soldier and his naval lover were both serving under that admiral's command? Of course not. Was it fair to discharge the colonel--an officer with a fine and distinguished record--for the reasons for which he was tried, while the female naval officer's career prospered? Of course not.

If the services are seeking to justify and retain service law in the face of rapidly changing opinions, they must behave like sensible custodians of a code of jurisprudence that grants them far-reaching and, some would argue, anachronistic powers. The case that I have described illustrates why it is unfair that the officer did not receive criminal legal aid and why he should have been granted it. I shall explain the process that led to the enormous delay, which the amendment seeks to address.

One of the witnesses at the court martial was told to be the soldier's friend. He has described what happened during the enormous delay. He was a colonel and the accused man was an officer commanding. The soldier's friend was called to the general officer commanding's conference room and was informed that a female Royal Navy officer had lodged a complaint of harassment. There was an initial investigation: the colonel had clearly been a fool and behaved badly, and a serious sexual allegation had been made.

Everyone had the benefit of an official report, so the soldier's friend asked whether the colonel's side of the story had been given. He was told that it had not been considered at any point. The soldier's friend commented that the colonel and his wife were a good commanding officer team and that the Army should be careful to ensure that the colonel was treated properly and not condemned before the whole story was known.

As a result of the meeting, the soldier's friend went to Brize Norton to be present when the colonel was intercepted on arrival by the Royal Military Police. The soldier's friend later met the Royal Military Police in the arrival hall at Brize Norton, where he was shown into a side office and told that the colonel would be called to the reception desk on arrival and given a reason for going to the office where he would be interviewed with the soldier's friend present. Eventually, having been summoned over the public address system, the colonel--a commanding officer--was shown into the office where he was introduced to another officer who informed the colonel that he had spent seven hours with the naval officer who made the official complaint.

A discussion took place, after which the colonel's demeanour was pretty shattered. The impression was given that this was an unfortunate situation, but that it was not too extraordinary. It was explained that the procedures and rules had to be complied with. The colonel had to be escorted at all times and was not to be left alone.

He was not allowed to make telephone calls. That is not what happens on a Saturday night when someone has been arrested in my constituency. He had to travel by car from the office at Brize Norton to his station but was accompanied and a special investigation branch sergeant followed in another car. The colonel's liberty was, therefore, immediately constrained despite the fact that no charge had been made, and none would be for another 11 months. The interviewing officer said that the reason for that was that he believed that the colonel might try to contact his wife, or someone else, to have evidence removed before arrival at his station. They stopped at a service station on the M25, simply because they wanted to visit the loo.

Between arrival in the country and arrival at the station, the colonel's married quarters was searched without his wife's knowledge, who was out at a social function. His computer and several floppy discs were removed. On completion of the search, the colonel asked why the search record had not been signed by an authorising officer, or signed to show that verbal authority had been given for the search. That is not like the civil system of justice that we are used to in this country. The interviewing officer said that the search was duly authorised, but did not say by whom. The soldier's friend was required to sign a form and saw that the authorisation had not been completed.

The colonel was then released into the custody of the interviewing officer. A series of meetings was held, but the soldier's friend thought that the civil police should have dealt with the allegations included in Pearce's statement and that, if the Royal Military Police were to deal with the complaint, the information presented should have been confined to that which the naval officer was prepared to complain about and should not have included sensational and supplementary material. That view was subsequently supported at the court martial and clearly showed that the investigation and prosecution of the colonel was flawed from the outset.

The information that the interviewing officer presented to the decision makers was selective. If the information held by that officer and subsequently by the prosecution had been made available much earlier as required by law, the flaws and discrepancies in the investigation would have been exposed to those deciding on the course of action to be taken. For example, copies of all the letters that the colonel is purported to have sent to the naval officer, the true chronology of visits to her house in the run-up to the colonel's termination of the relationship and other visits that they had made were not made available. The inclusion in the general court martial charge sheet of the allegation that the colonel contacted the Navy was flawed. The evidence from the Royal Navy vetting officer is specific about date and time. Solid evidence is available to show that Pople was interviewing an officer throughout the period in question and could not have made telephone calls. That evidence was readily available, but it was not used.

If military legal aid had been available at that stage and the various parties had had access to legal advice from the start, much of that nonsense would not have happened. The investigation was handled in an extraordinary way from the start. The impression given to the soldier's friend was either that the civil police should have handled the investigation or that it should have been confined to those matters about which the naval officer complained. The investigation moved into an area between those two safe positions, and became unsafe as a result, which led to the fiasco that I have described.

It is therefore extremely important to consider whether, and to what extent, the fault in this and in other cases lies in the huge discrepancy between civil and military law. The amendment would help, first to secure justice by enabling any person charged to start the preparation of his or her defence with the assistance of a solicitor at an early stage when evidence and witnesses are more likely to be available than they are if the preparation has to be delayed for several months.

Secondly, the amendment would enable the preparation of the prosecution and the defence to proceed in parallel rather than in sequence. In the case that I described, the colonel who had not been charged was left in limbo for 11 months, while the military authorities were busy investigating and making their case.

Thirdly, the amendment would help ensure that a level playing field in the preparation of the prosecution and defence; and, fourthly, it would ensure that those facing court martial or trial at a standing civilian court overseas had the same resources for the preparation of their defence as those in England and Wales.

The current processes from the alleged commission of an offence to trial within the services operate unnecessarily to the detriment of the accused and in that respect they are different from the civilian process. The problem is that legal aid is not available to service personnel until much later in the process than in the civilian system. Typically, a civilian offence will occur and be investigated probably within days and at the end of the investigation, any charge is formulated. At that point, which marks the start of the judicial process, the accused can obtain immediate legal aid, advice and assistance, and the preparation of the defence, which may include the tracing and interviewing of essential witnesses while events are fresh in their minds, can start immediately.

In the case of the colonel, which I described, his defence could not interview anyone for 11 months. That is bizarre. A service person is simply told that the matter will be reported; there then follows a long wait while the Army prosecuting authority considers the matter and engages in various consultations. Papers are often served on the accused months later, and only at that point can he apply for legal aid, which frequently takes weeks to process. It is increasingly common for a court martial date to be fixed before any granting of legla aid is complete and it is by no means unusual for an accused to be left with a period of three months or less, for example, to prepare a defence of what may be a serious allegation. The police and prosecution may have had a good year in which to prepare.

It is interesting to note that the Bill, perhaps for the first time, identifies the moment at which service personnel are told that they are to be reported for an offence as equating to being charged at a civilian police station. Another anachronism is that the service police do not have the power to charge anyone; in the services system, that comes much later. The relevant measures are section 75(4) of the Army Act 1955 and the corresponding sections of the other discipline provisions.

Service personnel overseas are to be denied the opportunity to have a civil lawyer to help at the investigation stage; they are also at a further disadvantage as against their counterparts in England and Wales, in that the remuneration of the lawyer is capped, and not at a very high level. The fee would be more or less the same, irrespective of the seriousness of the allegation or the difficulty of the case. Preparation of the defence should be provided at an hourly rate, as it is in England and Wales, and not on the basis of a lump sum.

Codes of practice and the notification of rights of suspects at interviews are also important. The Ministry of Defence code of practice is perfectly reasonable from the service point of view. On the right to legal advice, section 3 on page 917 of the code of practice states clearly:

    Any suspect being interviewed by the Service Police in connection with a Serious Service Offence may consult or communicate privately, whether in person, in writing or on the telephone, with a Legal Adviser who may be a Legal Adviser of his own choice; the duty solicitor ; or a Legal Adviser selected by him from a list of Legal Advisers who have indicated that they are available for the purpose of providing legal advice; in certain overseas theatres, a Service Legal Adviser.

It is perfectly clear in the Army's own code of practice that that is desirable. There has never been any question of the military suggesting to service men and women that they may not have access to civilian lawyers. There is just one problem: last December, the Lord Chancellor's Department decided that that was no longer to apply. The rules were changed and the Legal Aid Board issued a directive stating that in future it would not meet legal aid bills for civilian solicitors who assisted in military cases overseas. That is of grave importance for service personnel, especially in Germany and Cyprus, because suddenly civilian lawyers are not to be able to take on such cases. The lawyers have never expected travel costs to be included; that was always part of a separate deal with their clients. It has always been important that when they get there they should be properly remunerated for providing legal advice, but this new Government diktat has thrown all that into disarray.

It is important that we probe the Government and get them to explain themselves on the matter. The amendment makes our position clear. We consider that service men and women should be eligible for legal aid in accordance with Queen's regulations and that the terms for legal aid should be the same whether the service person is in England, Wales or elsewhere.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Key: We have had a good debate on clause 1 although I regret that we did not make further progress with out amendments. However, there remains one issue on which I should be grateful for genuine clarification.

I am concerned that we are changing the custody arrangements for the armed services and the three discipline Acts without actually being sure exactly to whom they apply. I referred to section 211 of the Army Act 1955 which relates to the application of the Act to reserves and auxilliary forces. I am still not completely clear about it. I am sure that the Bill covers the Adjutant-General's Corps and service personnel and support--the Pay Corps. It covers education, training and support--the RAEC; the Army Legal Service, the Royal Military Police, the military police and the Military Provost Guard Service--the prison officers. I am equally sure that the military guard force is not covered by the Bill because the force is undeployable. I am not clear whether the Bill covers various other categories. I have in mind the central volunteer headquarters of the Territorial Army--the CVHQ. Is the CVHQ covered? What about the Royal Artillery, the Royal Logistics Corps, the Royal Engineers, and others such as the infantry and the medical services. What about the reserves--the former regular members of the armed services who have a reserve liability. Will they be covered? This is not a pedantic point but a practical one. Many people need to know who will be covered by the Bill.

Dr. Moonie: I cannot give a full answer now. As far as I know, the Bill involves all serving personnel and civilians who are covered by Army discipline Acts when they are overseas. I am not sure which other groups may be covered. I shall try to provide an exhaustive list--of course, I cannot promise to get every individual into it--and give the Committee the information.

Question put and agreed to.
