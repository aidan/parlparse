Mr. Oliver Letwin (West Dorset): I share a huge advantage over the Committee with you, Mr. Hood, because my paltry IQ has been raised by a haircut. Between us, we can try to cut through some of the difficulties. I shall not continue the fascinating discussion that has just taken place; we shall have time to return to it later. Having read some time ago and again during the recess the document to which the Economic Secretary referred, I am certain that my lack of eloquence has resulted in her failing to understand the problem of parliamentary accountability between parallel systems. As I said, we shall return to that later.

The Treasury's discretion to lay down standards and definitions in clause 5(2) is a crucial aspect of the Bill. Before lunch, I promised to explain why it did not provide a sensible method, even from the Government's point of view, let alone that of Parliament or the electorate at large. I also said that I would explain the merit of the chain of Opposition amendments.

We must return to what is the purpose of Government accounts in the first place. Clearly, if Parliament and the electorate intended the Government to spend whatever they liked and to decide the extent to which they should inform people about their expenditure and accounting, there would be no need for the Bill or for its predecessors. We would have a one-line Bill, which would say, ``The Treasury shall decide'' the extent to which it had to account for its expenditure. That is not, of course, a realistic situation.

We need Government accounts so that those who are not part of the Government—either in or outside Parliament—can ascertain how much is being spent on what. A set of figures organised generally in a rational spirit—clearly the intent of the Resource Accounting Manual—is not, of itself, enough without a degree of transparency. In this context, those reading the accounts, who are often not experts and do not operate within the Government machine, must know exactly what every figure means. With sufficient diligence, they should be able to read back from the figures to the physical and tangible correlates—in other words, the actual outputs. That translation exercise anywhere in the world—it applies to UK Government accounts—depends on the clarity and fixity of definitions. There are no armies of people clever enough to take a set of figures and work back to the outcomes that they represent without a clear and relatively unchanging set of definitions.

The first problem with clause 5(2) is that it offers no guarantee that there will be definitions, or that they will be announced or unchanging. In fact, it provides for the reverse of those propositions. It provides for the Treasury to direct, from time to time, that definitions be changed and whether to disclose them. In short, the Treasury shall be in charge of the question of definition.

The Treasury, I am sure, thinks of Departments as dangerous enemies seeking to spend public money and of itself as a guardian angel coming to the electorate's rescue, protecting the taxpayer by seeking limits. The Treasury has always thought like that. Ministers and officials in treasuries everywhere think of themselves in such a light. Alas, I must reveal something that Ministers would see if they were not in their current posts. Indeed, they may see it fleetingly within their own beings.

To the outside world, the Treasury is not a guardian angel or a protector of the electorate. Indeed, it is not the Treasury's constitutional position to be such things. The Treasury is part of the Government, so it is part of the problem, not its solution. The Treasury is the very body of which the accounts give an account. By extension, those accounts relate to every aspect of government. Parliament and, through its openness, the whole body politic, has an interest in pinning down the meaning of Government accounts. It is therefore no comfort to be told that the Treasury will decide all the definitions,

If we are to have clarity and at least relative fixity to guarantee transparency, we must be confident that a body other than the Treasury, and independent of it and the Government, will be the guarantor of those definitions. That will assist ordinary people—both within and without Parliament—trying hard to understand the accounts.

I may be wrong, but everything that I have said so far ought to be common ground. Certainly, as we shall hear in due course from right hon. and hon. Members and members of the PAC, the PAC made repeated calls in 1994–95, 1996–97 and 1998–99 for an independent body. It is unthinkable that there should not be an independent body defining standards in relation to the private sector. As the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton pointed out, the Comptroller and Auditor General drew our attention to that matter only yesterday.

It is strange that a Bill should be introduced without a provision, which provides for an independent commission such as that advocated in amendment No. 3. It is so strange, that it is bound to be an oversight. The internal evolution of such matters in the Treasury and Government, perhaps dating back to the previous Administration—I am not making a party political point—must have led to the lacuna represented by the sad state of affairs in subsection (1).

I therefore entertain a strong hope that, although our amendments may be rejected—they may be technically deficient—their general intent will be shared. Should the Minister give such a reassurance and persuade the Committee that the Government intend to move in that direction, we shall all seek the appropriate means of achieving that aim. However, if the Minister does not do that, she will be taking on not the Opposition or Opposition parties—actual and quasi—or even Opposition parties and the PAC, but Opposition parties, the PAC and almost every informed commentator who has commented on the matter or is likely to do so. There will be prolonged trench warfare over it and eventually the Treasury will give way, not necessarily in this Bill but in due course, because the proposition that we institute a new and entirely admirable principal regime for accounting and then say that the people being accounted for should settle the definitions according to which they are accounted is irrational and unsustainable.

If I may, I shall make one further point before going onto the precise mechanics suggested in our amendments. There is nothing even in the proposed subsection (2)—although I admit that later proposals might be held to have the same intent—that limits changes of definition within a year. Within the scope of subsection (2), it is possible that the Treasury could direct the use of different definitions for different Departments and different definitions for the same Department in the same year. That is clearly a mistake in drafting. Even on the most extreme analysis, it cannot be the Treasury's intent to give itself such discretion.

All that comes to our minds because no Government in recent history have been immune from definitional dexterity of a Macavity variety. The present Treasury has been the most accomplished of all prestidigitation experts. A great deal of attention has been focused on the remarkable dexterity with which the Government have defined expenditure as negative income—a process which, if taken to extremes, could eliminate the entire Budget. There are therefore great reasons for our concern, in current and future imaginable circumstances—I do not imagine that any Government, even, I regret to say, a future Conservative Government, would be immune from such temptation—and for our wish for an independent commission.

I should dwell a little on the detail of our amendments, which benefit also from the signatures of our Liberal Democrat colleagues. I will not be dogmatic about our precise articulation of the amendments, which may be susceptible to improvement or even radical change, but at least I hope to make a better job of explaining what I am on about than I seem to have done this moming in relation to cash and resources.

Amendment No. 3, which suffers from one deficiency that the PAC amendments pointed out—it refers to ``the Comptroller'' rather than ``the Comptroller and Auditor General'', although we took that from the Bill—is simple: it would mandate the Treasury to establish a national accounts commission. We could not see a way of getting such a body established other than by giving the Government—in this case, the Treasury—power to do so, although there is precious little use in having an independent body established by the Treasury unless that body's remit is either self defining or defined by another party—hence the suggestion in our amendment that the Comptroller and Auditor General should determine the commission's duties. That is of some importance. It need not be the Comptroller and Auditor General who does it—in fact, it sits slightly oddly with his normal activities—but some body other than the Treasury ought to determine the exact role of the independent commission.

The analogy is clear. If the Accounting Standards Board started falling down on the job, Ministers now present would be among the first to leap to the Dispatch Box and inform the House that they were taking steps to give the board new duties to ensure that it did the proper job for the private sector. We must therefore find an analogy, which is the Comptroller and Auditor General telling the independent commission what it should do about definitions of public accounting.

We do not want to specify in overmuch detail the scope of the commission's activities because all these are matters of extreme detail, and most of the important points arise at the interstices of one detail and another. We have simply suggested that the duties should include applying the definitions of standards of national accounting practice to all departmental accounts and to the whole of Government accounts. The Comptroller and Auditor General may wish to impose other duties on the commission, either from time to time or at the start, but the commission should at least have the duty of defining standards, and those standards should apply not just to departmental accounts but to whole of Government accounts. Departmental accounts are meaningless except in the context of the whole of Government accounts, and so the definition needs to be comprehensive.

Amendment No. 4 relates to a point that I made earlier and to which we shall undoubtedly wish to return. It concerns liabilities. One of the present failings of the manual and the whole approach to resource accounting is that it does not properly capture the major liabilities of the state. Indeed, the definitions currently chosen would exclude countering the major liabilities of the state. It is a pretty extraordinary state of affairs when those things that the Government are mainly liable to pay in future years, such as pensions to basic state pensioners and other such liabilities, are specifically excluded. Clearly we need to make sure that a NAC has standards that clearly describe the overall liabilities of the state so that Parliament can each year understand and access the long-term implications of policy for the fiscal balance.

Amendment No. 5 is consequential on amendment No. 3. It replaces the discretion of the Treasury with the discretion of the national, accounts commission. Amendment No. 6 takes us on a little pace simply by removing the reinforcement of the Treasury's discretion in subsection (3). Just before I move on, I should like to draw the Committee's attention to subsection (3) because it is very interestingly drafted. I can conclude only that it has been drafted without Ministers wholly attending to the words. It states:

    ``The Treasury shall exercise the power to issue directions under subsection (2) with a view to ensuring that resource accounts conform to generally accepted accounting practice subject to such adaptations as are necessary in the context of departmental accounts.''

One must then ask to whom should they appear necessary, the answer is of course the Treasury. As a result, it could fairly be said that subsection (3) adds literally nothing to subsection (2) in which case, as we suggest in amendment No. 6, it should be left out. Even if the basic proposition we are advancing were rejected, there could not be a reason for having in legislation a subsection that can lead to litigation and a lack of clarity because it adds nothing to the preceding subsection.

We then move to other amendments tabled by Liberal Democrats to which they will undoubtedly wish to speak. I hope that they will turn out to be part of a belt-and-braces approach, which would become unnecessary if the fully-edged approach in amendments Nos. 3, 4, 5 and 6 were adopted.

I have tried to imagine what the Economic Secretary might say in response. It may be helpful for me to outline the options. First, she may say that my interpretation is wrong and that, having given the matter deep consideration, Ministers have concluded that they do not like the idea of an independent body at all. If that is so, I fear that constructive dialogue on that matter and the Bill will fail, as there can be precious little discussion between a group advancing the proposition that it should be able to to monitor its own activity and another saying that someone else should do so. Indeed, the argument would move outside the House to a wider political stage. Eventually, the Treasury would lose the argument, so I hope that the Minister will not take that option.

Secondly, the general principle of an independent body that is not merely advisory may be acceptable. Our specific suggestion, however, may be rejected. Parliamentary procedures are ill adapted to such an outcome. We are passing from clause to clause, and our amendments would change clause 5. If our amendments are judged inadequate, but the principle is accepted, it will not be easy to solve the problem in Committee or before Report. It would be difficult to know how to argue for amendments later in the Bill unless we were aware of such an outcome.

Under such circumstances, it would be constructive to allow for cross-party discussions outside Committee, in an attempt to sketch a broad outline that would command consensus, and in the hope that suitable amendments could be tabled later. If we did that, we would know the background against which we were operating in the remaining Committee proceedings. I hope that that solution will prevail.

Thirdly, the Economic Secretary may tell us that the Financial Reporting Advisory Board is sufficiently independent. It is true that the FRAB is independent. Its independence is not especially concerning as, although its structure could be more independent, I would be prepared to accept it as a slightly inadequate substitute for a national accounts commission. However, I cannot speak for others who put their names to the amendment. The Economic Secretary would have to assure us that the FRAB would be given powers of definition and direction that are currently attributed to the Treasury. The FRAB's big disadvantage is not lack of independence, but lack of power. Doubtless, its independence could be strengthened over time. However, it will not achieve its objectives if it remains merely consultative.
