The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Defence (Dr. Lewis Moonie): I have an early start at this sitting. As the clause is closely linked to clause 5 and we went round the houses on that clause at the previous sitting, I will explain more fully what is involved. The hon. Member for Reigate (Mr. Blunt) spoke about the people who will be caught by the Bill. My direction at the previous sitting was correct, but I sowed some confusion through my misinterpretation of the amendment, which suggested to me that the hon. Gentleman wanted to reduce the required sentence. Reading the amendment again, I discovered that that was not the case and I am happy to accept that. However, that was not the substance of our later debate, and it might therefore be helpful to go through clause 6 in full and put our thinking on the record.

Confusion arose mainly in relation to officers in the Territorial Army or reserve forces. To ensure that there is no further confusion, I will describe precisely which categories of persons will be caught by the new offence. The first category comprises those being tried under the service discipline Acts for offences committed while those persons were subject to service law. Ex-regular soldiers are a simple example of persons in that category. Section 131 of the Army Act 1955 and of the Air Force Act 1955, and section 51 of the Naval Discipline Act 1957 provide that such former service personnel remain subject to service law only for the purposes of arrest, custody and trial, although they have ceased to be subject to it generally.

The second category of persons consists of those who are descirbed in the four relevant paragraphs of section 205(1) of the Army Act 1955 and of the Air Force Act 1955 and in section 111 of the Naval Discipline Act 1957. In the Army, that means every officer of the Territorial Army who is a special member when in permanent or full-time service or undertaking any training or duty, whether in pursuance of an obligation or not. I stress the word ``special'' because those officers constitute a specific category of Territorial Army officer and are to be distinguished from ordinary Territorial Army officers who are subject at all times to service law and do not fall within the groups of persons under discussion.

A further category of persons covered by the offence comprises every officer of the Army reserve in permanent or full-time service, undertaking any training or duty--whether in pursuance of an obligation or not--or serving on the permanent staff of the Army reserve.

Every warrant officer, non-commissioned officer and man of the Army reserve in permanent or full-time service, undertaking any training or duty--whether in pursuance of an obligation or not--or serving on the permanent staff of the Army reserve is also covered by the offence.

The final category includes every warrant officer, non-commissioned officer and man of the Territorial Army who is in permanent or full-time service, called out for home defence service, undertaking any training or duty--whether in pursuance of an obligation or not--or serving on the permanent staff of the Territorial Army.

Permanent service applies when staff are called out under the provisions of the Reserve Forces Act 1980. Full-time service is when they make a commitment to work full-time for the services. Home defence service applies to those members of the TA whose call-out liability is limited to the defence of the UK. Liability for deployment is limited to the UK. The phrase ``undertaking any training or duty'' refers to the minimum number of days that staff are obliged to undertake by virtue of their membership of a reserve force. Of course, they can volunteer for additional duties.

Persons on the permanent staff are known as non-regular permanent staff. They are similar to those of the reserves who have signed up for full-time service, but their terms and conditions differ slightly. I am trying to give as full a response as possible to show that the clause covers every possible circumstance; I hope that I am not creating more confusion.

The reason for a distinction between the types of volunteer reserve officer arises from the creation of a new category of reserve: those who enter employee agreements under part V of the Reserve Forces Act 1996. They are referred to as special members. The provisions in that Act enable civilians to work in support jobs which would require their services and operations. People taking those jobs agree as a term of employment that that particular post includes a liability for call out and training, and they become a special member of the reserve force. As I explained, because the call-out liability is related to the job, and they are not volunteer reservists in the ordinary sense, they are not subject to service law at all times as an ordinary reserve officer would be.

We are satisfied that regular soldiers and/or serving commissioned officers who are released from custody pending trial can be given a lawful order by the commanding officer to attend a future hearing. A subsequent failure to attend could result in such service personnel being charged with a service offence. However, individuals, who fall outside that specific, albeit large, category of personnel are not necessarily subject to a commanding officer's command and control at all times prior to the trial. Accordingly, in respect of the other individuals, it is necessary for the Bill to create an offence of non-attendance at a hearing after a person is released from pre-trial custody. Without a specific provision for those two categories of personnel, the Bill would be defective, in that such personnel could never be sanctioned for not attending a subsequent hearing.

I hope that I have given the Committee a sufficiently full explanation. I should be happy to give way if hon. Members wish to raise other points.

Mr. Moonie: That is a fair point. I accept that the amendment is intended to be helpful and to close a procedural loophole, but we are aware of the problem and when I explain what is covered by clause 6, the hon. Gentleman will realise that the amendment is unnecessary.

As drafted, clause 6 inserts a new section into each of the discipline Acts, under which either the commanding officer or a judge advocate can order an arrest if he is satisfied that there are substantial grounds for believing that the accused would otherwise fail to attend a hearing, commit an offence, injure himself, interfere with witnesses or obstruct the course of justice. The provisions are as follows: the new power of arrest is considered necessary because the powers currently contained in the services discipline Acts do not apply in these circumstances. It applies to two categories of persons who are charged with an offence or who are awaiting sentence for an offence, but who are not in custody. The first category comprises those who are subject to military law generally and the second, those who are subject to military law only by virtue of section 131 of the Army Act. Those persons have ceased to be subject to military law, except for the purpose of trying an offence that they allegedly committed while still subject to military law. The clause already covers those persons who fall within the paragraphs of section 205 that are listed in the amendment. Those people are either subject to military law because they have undertaken one of the activities described in section 205, or they are subject to military law only for the purpose of trying an offence, under section 131. Either way, they fall within the ambit of the provision and can be arrested during proceedings.

I will mention again that we are dealing with two categories. The first covers an individual who has left, for example, the regular Army, and who is later believed to have committed a service offence while in the Army. He can be arrested and held in custody pending trial, as if he were still a regular soldier. The second category is less straightforward. In relation to the Army, we identified four types of personnel that are subject to military law at only certain times. In both cases, we want to require those individuals to attend any future hearing. The penal sanction in the Bill fufils that requirement.
