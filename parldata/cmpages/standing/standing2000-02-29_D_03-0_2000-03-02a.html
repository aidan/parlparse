Dr. Moonie: I studied the amendment with interest and it seemed reasonable until I realised that the judicial officer will be involved in the review. The amendment would place only an additional duty on him to relieve the commanding officer of a burden. Currently, a member of the armed forces charged abroad and detained in custody is returned to this country within the eight-day period and remanded on base or at Colchester; he would not be left abroad. The 28-day provision would not apply to such a person.

Judicial officers will be allowed to authorise custody for up to 28 days, rather than the usual eight, provided the accused consents. If the accused does not consent, eight-day periods will be used. Consent will be acceptable only if given by the accused at a hearing where he is legally represented.

The provision is for the benefit of the commanding officer. It is designed to allow a reduction in the number of custody hearings required when an accused is being held in custody before trial. The judicial officer will use the same Bail Act criteria as is used in the civilian system.
