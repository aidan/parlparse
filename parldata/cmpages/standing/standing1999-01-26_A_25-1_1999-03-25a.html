Mr. Raynsford: I am afraid, Mr. Winerton, that your reference to Olympic prowess brought back an unhappy memory. As you rightly said, Mr. Winterton, we have come to the part of the Bill dealing with matters of culture. I am pleased to move a group of amendments that will strengthen the provisions establishing the cultural strategy group for London.

The clause and Government amendments provide for an appropriately sized body. We believe that the body should have executive functions if the mayor considers that appropriate. Corporate status for the cultural strategy group for London will avoid any potential problems over personal liability for members of the group—I believe that the change proposed by amendment No. 1231 would be necessary on those grounds alone.

The cultural strategy group for London, acting through the mayor, is to have broad discretionary powers consistent with the rest of the Bill. The amendments build on the foundations of the group as laid out in the Bill. For example, it will be able to acquire premises and employ its own staff for the purposes of carrying out its functions. The provisions on the mayor's funding of the group's expenses have been amended to give more flexibility. In the original draft, the mayor was to provide the cultural strategy group for London with staff and equipment; as amended, the clause will give the mayor broader power to fund the group for the exercise of its functions. Further provisions clarify the status of the group, providing that its seal can be used and presumed valid unless proved otherwise.

The size of the cultural strategy group has been set at between 10 and 25 members—as a body corporate, the upper and lower limits must be identified. The London Heritage Forum—the body that currently brings together the principal cultural agencies in London—has a membership of 14. It has done much good work already towards achieving an integrated approach to culture in London, and it seems logical that the expertise and experience of its members would be drawn on by the mayor in pulling together the new cultural strategy group. Nevertheless, the appointment of members will be at the mayor's discretion. I firmly believe that it is right that the mayor should have a good measure of flexibility.

By setting a lower limit of 10 and an upper limit of 25, we have accommodated both flexibility and manageability. The group should not be too unwieldy, but the mayor must have sufficient leeway to appoint extra members as he or she sees fit.

We have also made it explicit that the mayor will appoint a group member to chair the group and that its activities will not be invalidated by vacancies. The Government amendments allow the cultural strategy group for London to develop flexibly so as to accommodate changing circumstances and functions. The group parallels the initiatives launched by my right hon. Friend the Secretary of State for Culture, Media and Sport to establish cultural consortiums in the other English regions. The consortiums are part of a developing agenda that is still at a comparatively early stage. They will not be statutory bodies, however, London is different because the mayor is responsible for the cultural strategy group.
