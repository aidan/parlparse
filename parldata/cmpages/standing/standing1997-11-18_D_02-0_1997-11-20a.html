Functions of court of directors

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I beg to move amendment No. 4, in page 2, line 8, at end add--

    `(5) The court shall from time to time receive reports from the Monetary Policy Committee on the formulation of monetary policy.'.

The amendment would require the court to receive all the reports from the Monetary Policy Committee. I am aware that we shall return to debating that committee later in the Bill and that clause 16 also touches on the court of directors and monetary policy. However, clause 2 purports to provide a comprehensive list of the functions of the court of directors--there is nothing to suggest otherwise.

Although subsection (1) states that the court of directors shall not have a duty to deal with the formulation of monetary policy, we know from the Chancellor of the Exchequer that the court does have certain important duties relating to monetary policy more generally. Paragraph 19 of his letter to the Governor of 6 May states:

    ``The non-Executive Members will review the performance of the Bank as a whole, including the Monetary Policy Committee.''

That is an important duty. In reviewing the performance of a body, one needs to know what that body is up to and to receive any relevant documents or reports issued in relation to it. The amendment would put that beyond doubt and ensure that the court can discharge its functions by requiring reports to be sent to it.

We do not yet know exactly how the MPC will function. It presently operates in a non-statutory capacity but may operate differently when it has the force of law behind it. Statute law will be scrutinised by the court and the MPC to ascertain Parliament's intention in the division of responsibilities between those two bodies.

It is important for the non-executive directors to be kept fully informed. Jealousies can build up between organisations and information is power. One can envisage circumstances in which the MPC believes that information is confidential, price sensitive, should be held on to and not divulged to a third party or the outside world. That is understandable, but it is important that the court of directors is also given such information in the reports, because the court will presumably be bound by rules of confidentiality that are similar to those that bind it at present. If it is to discharge the duty--which is outlined in the Chancellor's letter--of reviewing the performance of the Monetary Policy Committee, full disclosure of the reports is essential.

To put the matter beyond doubt and to ensure that the clause sets out a comprehensive list of the court's functions, I ask the Chief Secretary to the Treasury to accept the amendment. If he cannot, will he tell us why that is impossible?
