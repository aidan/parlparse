Clause 118

Rate of CGT for trustees etc

Mr. Ruffley: I beg to move amendment No. 74, in page 107, line 37, after "settlement" insert

    "other than a settlement to which paragraph 1 of Schedule 1 to this Act applies".

The Chairman: With this we may discuss amendment No. 75, in page 107, line 38, after "person" insert

    "other than for a year of assessment to which section 3(7) of this Act applies".

Mr. Ruffley: The clause is designed to attack tax avoidance and, in that respect, it has some merit. However, it will hit those whom it was not designed to hit.

At present, the capital gains realised by trustees in possession under a UK trust and persnal representatives of deceased persons are charged to tax at the basic rate of income tax, currently 23 per cent. That remains the case, even though beneficiaries under the trust may be top-rate taxpayers, although the tax on capital gains charged to personal representatives or trustees in possession will be only at the basic rate. There is evidence that that differential is being exploited by those who set up trusts to allow higher rate tax beneficaries to avoid the marginal rate of capital gains tax.

The clause will introduce a new uniform rate of capital gains tax of 34 per cent., which will apply to all UK trusts and personal representatives of the estates of deceased persons. The problem arises in relation to the small estates of testators or deceased persons, which would never, throughout the lifetime of that person, have been liable to as high a tax rate as 34 per cent. Many of those people will have been basic rate taxpayers. However, as a result of the clause, there will be the palpable injustice that, in the year of administration after death, capital gains will be chargeable at the full rate of 34 per cent.

To counter that perceived unfairness, we have tabled amendment No. 75, which would exempt from the new 34 per cent. uniform rate those estates that would usually be able to take advantage of an annual exemption. Section 3(7) of the Taxation of Chargeable Gains Act 1992 sets out the period of exemption as being the year of administration and two years thereafter. We suggest that the 34 per cent. rate should not operate in that period. The 23 per cent. rate should remain operative for estates.

Amendment No. 74 relates to settlement trusts for disabled people and those who are in receipt of attendance allowance or disability living allowance. It would give relief to those trusts that are obviously not vehicles for tax avoidance.

The two amendments are sensible. They strike out in the direction of fairness, which the Government should find acceptable, and would enable the government to secure their objective of penalising trusts that have been set up for tax avoidance and that allow more relief than the Government believe should be available to higher-rate taxpayers, while giving sensible protection to those who should not be attacked by this otherwise worthy clause.

Mr. Nick St. Aubyn (Guildford): I support my hon. Friend the Member for Bury St. Edmunds (Mr. Ruffley), who clearly put the case for the amendments. The Government must demonstrate whether they believe in the principle of neutrality when dealing with trusts. As my hon. Friend said, many trusts have nothing to do with tax avoidance. If there is a principle that the taxation of trusts is comparable to the taxation of indivduals, there will be no incentive for anyone to regard trusts as tax-avoidance vehicles.

It is unlikely that those who receive disability living allowance face a tax rate above the basic rate. It is not only a matter of public policy that trusts and their beneficiaries should have their gains taxed at the basic rate and not at the higher rate, but unjust that their gains[Mr. Nick St. Aubyn]should be taxed at the higher rate when their other income is taxed at the lower rate.

The clause will impose an unfair penality on estates, that are distributed slowly. If the executors of an estate are quick off the mark and manage to distribute all the assets to the beneficiaries in a matter of months, many of those beneficiaries will be liable to tax only at the basic rate. Any subsequent uplift in the value of the assets that were distributed will suffer tax only at the basic rate. If the administrators of an estate are dilatory and, during the full two years that it takes them to distribute the assets, the value of those assets increases significantly, the increase in value will be taxed at the higher rate, even if the individual who left the estate and its beneficiaries have always paid tax at the basic rate. The current provisions recognise that, because estates receive full annual exemption from capital gains tax during the first two years, estates administation is a special case. It is therefore quite incongruous that they are not to be liable to tax at the basic rate only.

Mr. Tim Loughton (East Worthing and Shoreham): I hope not to wreck the chances of the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett) in that respect, but I wish to speak to both the amendments.

To accept the amendment would be to permit indexation allowance to apply from April 1998 to the date of disposal of the asset, in place of taper relief. That would cut across and undermine the Government's objective. Indexation allowance would also apply toassets treated as held at Budget day under paragraph 15 of schedule 20, or under the two new paragraphs that amendments Nos. 225 and 226 would add to the schedule.

The amendment would undermine what we seek to achieve through the taper, and I therefore ask the Committee to reject it. That applies as much to assets held at the commencement of the reforms as to those acquired subsequently. Allowing a taxpayer to substitute indexation at the time that the asset is disposed of would remove the taxpayer's incentive to retain that asset for a longer period, until the taper has outweighed the loss of indexation after April 1998.

In addition, the amendment would greatly complicate the CGT system for all assets held, or treated as held, after Budget day. For those assets two calculations which the taxpayer would have to make would be necessary. The first calculation would have to be made on the basis of the provisions as drafted in the Bill. Those provisions allow for indexation to April 1998, and for the taper from April 1998 to the date of disposal. The second calculation would have to be made on the basis of indexation over the whole period of ownership to the date of disposal.

It is not realistic to suggest that the two calculations would not be necessary, because the operation of the amendment would be dependent on an election by the taxpayer. In practice, the taxpayer would want to do both calculations to decide which produced the smaller tax bill. I presume that this is why the hon. Members tabled the amendment.

We want the new system to have a beneficial effect as soon as possible, both for existing assets and for new assets purchased after 5 April 1998.
