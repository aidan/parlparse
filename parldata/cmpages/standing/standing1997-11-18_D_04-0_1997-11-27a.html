Mr. Davey: My intention in moving amendment No. 16 with which amendment No. 17 is grouped, is to explore the extent and type of independence the Government intend to grant to the Bank of England, how full and proper that independence will be and how much political control they wish to keep.

There are several models of independence and they differ in many ways. In our previous debates we have heard how independence can differ in relation to a variety of factors: the appointment of members of decision-making bodies and their terms of office, the degree of contact between the Bank and the Government, and the reporting rights and responsibilities of the Bank to the Government, and vice versa. The clause and amendments Nos. 16 and 17 deal with the differences between models of independence in terms of who sets the inflation targets, how such targets are set and what their statutory objectives are.

Clause 12 would not have been necessary if the Government had opted for total target independence--the highest degree of independence that can be given to a central bank. Such independence has been given to the German Bundesbank, which, as I am sure hon. Members are aware, has a price stability objective in its statutes. However, the interpretation of that objective in relation to inflation targets--or other intermediary targets, such as money supply--is a matter for the bank and its governing body.

There are many arguments in favour of target independence. It gives the maximum degree of independence and is of great benefit for the credibility that is the rationale behind that institutional change. There would be greater benefits in terms of lower interest rates, as risk premiums for future inflation seen by the markets are reduced. Target independence gives the maximum degree of independence because the objective has been set by the Bank and need not be changed after an election; it would certainly not be changed between elections. It would be clear to the markets that the Bank's objectives would not change even if the electorate returned a different party to Parliament after an election, and that there would be a long-term, stable inflation policy.

Mr. Ross Cranston (Dudley, North): Does the hon. Gentleman concede that under our system the lifetime of a Parliament can vary? We do not have the American system of four-year elections. The amendment does not identify a definite period; it is a flexible period, depending on when elections are called.

Mr. Davey: The hon. Gentleman anticipates my arguments. I hope to cover that important point later. The amendment seeks to clarify how frequently the targets are changed.

As a backround, we need to understand the arguments against target independence, however. Liberal Democrats do not support target independence; indeed, we support the Government for not choosing that form of independence. The arguments against target independence relate to democratic accountability. It is right that the Government should set the objective for the Bank, to ensure that the overall economic policy framework is set by politicians, who are accountable to the people for that. Granting a central bank target independence may have other objectives. For example, a Eurofed--an independent central bank in Europe--would be designed to achieve the gains from the single currency. In such a case, granting the bank target independence might be worthwhile even though it would lead to some reduction in democratic accountability. However, the Bill does not have such aims. Therefore, we do not favour target independence for the Bank of England.

That leaves us with the Government's proposal for operational independence. However, there are alternative models for operational independence, which are based on the frequency with which the Government can change the inflation target. This clause will allow the Government to set the objectives as frequently as they like. Indeed, it is phrased in such a way as to allow them to change the target every week, or even every day. The clause will oblige them to set the target at least once a year, but they will not be prevented from setting it much more frequently. That provision will reduce the Bank's independence significantly.

The Government have said that they do not intend to change the target. However, they have said that if they change the target, they will do so only in a downward direction. Although those statements are welcome, they beg the question as to whether Government policy may change.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): Does not the hon. Gentleman's proposal mean that the Government are more likely to rely on clause 19 of the Bill? If severe external shocks to the economy have to be taken account of when delivering the inflation target, the amendment will prevent the Government from changing the target. It will be left to the Bank to do something. If the Bank were forced to stick to the target set by the Government before the shock to the economy occurred, we could face severe difficulties with high unemployment resulting from the Bank not relaxing the target in a period of economic shock. Therefore, the Government are more likely to rely more frequently on the exemptions in clause 19. In effect, that will make the Bank less independent than it would otherwise have been.

Mr. Davey: I thank the hon. Gentleman for that point, but his argument does not necessarily follow. Clause 19 might be the backstop, but how the target is set and the ranges that are given to the Bank will become as important--probably far more important--than the reserve powers. I believe in granting the Bank independence, but I am not sure that the hon. Gentleman does given his earlier comments. That might be why he is trying to disrupt the amendment.

For those of us who believe in the Bank's independence, the issue is the extent of its independence. If we reduce the frequency with which the Government can set the inflation target, that will create much greater independence and, thus, provide much greater benefit to the country.

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I am following the hon. Gentleman's argument with interest, but let us consider the logic of it. If the target can be set just once during a Parliament, the Government--unless, they were to use the override clause that we shall discuss later--will be able to override it only by calling a general election? Would the hon. Gentleman welcome that?

Mr. Davey: As I shall seek to argue, the amendment would retain democratic accountability. As the hon. Gentleman points out, the Government could go to the electorate and ask them for authority to change the basis of, and the targets for, their economic policy which was based on the mandate that they received at the previous election.

The amendment is designed to help the Government. It would take further their commmitment not to change the target. I wish to impress on the Ministers the importance of that. If we were to build an anti-inflation culture in this country and to create an institutional structure that will embed an anti-inflation bias in the system, we must build a framework that will prevent future Governments from manipulating targets between elections.

If the Paymaster Gengeral is impressed by that argument, will he consider the possibility--it may seem remote to him at the moment--of a future Conservative Government? As he will be the first to recognise, the previous Conservative Government were weak on inflation and had a poor inflation target. The targets used during their 18 years in office changed frequently. That was partly why their inflation performance was so weak. If the Government accept our amendment, they will enshrine in law a safeguard against a furture Conservative Government's mismanagement of the economy. I urge the Minister to support the amendment on that basis. It would limit the frequency with which the inflation target could be changed, while retaining accountability. It does not go as far as target independence.

The Government might say that they need freedom and flexibility, but there is a danger of abuse. If that freedom is abused, the benefits of independence will diminish or be lost. Even without abuse, the markets might anticipate the potential for abuse in the future and want to insure against it. The benefits of certainty and stability, which the Government are seeking to gain for our country, will be lost. The amendment is important and would help to ensure that the Government fulfilled their objectives.

Several other issues are important and question the wisdom of allowing Governments frequently to change their targets. One issue is how monetary policy works in practice. There is a degree of consensus--unusual among economists on these issues--that monetary policy takes between 18 and 24 months to work. There is a lag in the effect of interest rate changes on the economy. The long time lags suggest that the Bank should be given at least a chance to hit the targets. If those targets are to be moved frequently, we are asking the Bank to achieve the impossible. If we want to hold the Bank truly accountable, we must ensure that the targets are in place for a certain period to give it a realistic chance of assessing how its monetary policy is working.

Another argument is that accountability will be more secured if the targets can be set only once during a Parliament because the profile of inflation targets will be much higher during general elections when the kernels of economic policy are debated. It would be for the political parties to state the inflation targets--2 per cent. for one party, 3 per cent. for another and 4 per cent. for another, which, in government, they would set for the Bank. The higher profile given to inflation targets during elections would ensure greater accountability, with people being given the opportunity to choose between policies. That would strengthen the Government's mandate on inflation targets.

A final argument confronts head-on the Government's desire for flexibility. Targets can be set to incorporate flexibility. The Liberal Democratic party proposes a target of between 0 and 3 per cent.--a larger span than the Government's target of 1 per cent. either side of 2.5 per cent. It is possible to set targets while retaining flexibility with respect to policy and securing all attendant benefits, but if setting the target is permitted only once in a Parliament other benefits can also be obtained--there will be increased independence.

To conclude, the amendment is constructive and in line with Government policy, but would bring about a slight shift towards greater independence for the Bank. I hope that the Government will consider it seriously, because it will make their policy more successful.

Amendment No. 20 is intended to probe the Government's understanding of price stability. Students of English would have a ready answer to the question. Price stability surely means no price increases. If a shopkeeper put up a sign in his window, saying that he would keep his prices stable, and then put them up, his customers would agree that he had broken his promise. On the face of it, price stability means zero inflation; prices should not rise. Under clause 11 the Bank of England would by law have to pursue price stability. That objective would override subsidiary aims concerning growth and employment. Zero inflation is a pretty demanding objective. Clause 11 is, however, qualified in clause 12, under which the Government would tell the Bank from time to time what price stability means. It is therefore important that we should know now, roughly, the Government's aims in this respect.

When the Government took office in May one of their first actions was to loosen the inflation target. They inherited a target of 2.5 per cent. or less. The Conservatives were committed to that target and reached it during the previous Parliament. That was an achievement of my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Rushcliffe (Mr. Clarke). It is not true that inflation was accelerating at the time of the general election. It was falling slightly. Opposition Members have in other debates criticised the Government's loosening of the inflation target. It sends the wrong message to the markets, economic agents, wage bargainers and so on, to the effect that the Government will be softer on inflation than their predecessors. They now have a target of 2.5 per cent. or less, or more. It can vary by 1 per cent. on either side. Only if inflation rose above 3.5 per cent. therefore, would the Bank of England be required to write to the Government about remedial measures and with an explanation of its intentions.

The present situation is therefore rather dangerous. We have a loosened inflation target, which is a moving target, as it may be changed again. The Governor of the Bank of England speculated earlier this week that the inflation target might have to be reduced. He thought that 2.5 per cent. might be too high if we are really to proceed to economic and monetary union in Europe. Perhaps the target will be lower.

There is also a rumour that the Government will change the definition of inflation. The current method of measurement is by RPIX, which is the retail prices index without mortgate interest. However, the Government are under pressure to adopt the European Union's harmonised index of consumer prices. That is based on other factors and items in the shopping basket, which is assessed monthly. That is probably tempting for the Government because it would show lower inflation. I hope that the Paymaster General will explain the Government's aim. We need to know the Government's intentions on the inflation measurement, and whether there will be a moving target, which the Government will alter from time to time, probably without notice. The phrase ``price stability'' is already qualified, but such action would cause prices to become erratic, and create the opposite of stability, which the Government mention so often.

In speaking to amendment No. 13, I draw attention to the danger of monetary and fiscal policy diverging. Fiscal policy has a considerable impact on monetary policy; the policies cannot be completely separated. The quantity of money in the economy and the velocity with which it circulates is affected by the Government's budgetary and other decisions. It is frequently observed that low inflation can be achieved only if monetary and fiscal policy pull in the same direction. There are worrying signs in this country that that is not happening.

Mr. Boswell: I beg to move amendment No. 23, in page 5, line 27, after `Exchequer', insert `after open competition'.

We now turn from the clauses that will enact the Monetary Policy Committee to those that deal with the details of its composition, its rules of operation and the other provisions set out in this and later clauses and in schedule 3. The amendment would require what might be termed the four independent members of the Monetary Policy Committee to be appointed after open competition. It thus invites the Chancellor to ensure that the best possible people are chosen on their merits for the job.

I have no objections to the existing members of the Monetary Policy Committee or to how they have conducted affairs. They must decide matters according to the principles that the Government have set. For reasons that were outlined on Second Reading and elsewhere by hon. Members, including by Labour Back Benchers who are not members of the Committee, the Opposition are not surprised that reservations have been expressed that the Members of the Monetary Policy Committee behave like bankers, act like bankers and have kept interest rates rather higher than is strictly necessary for the overall conduct of the economy. I shall not rehearse those arguments again.

This, and later amendments, would provide an alternative approach to securing our objective--and I hope that of Labour Members--of providing the Monetary Policy Committee with a genuine degree of independence. The committee may have operational independence and the Chancellor may wish to distance himself from its activities on the ground that it is operationally independent. However, the Chancellor may rig the rules through his definitions of price stability or economic policy or may rig the membership of the committee. If he does, the committee will not provide the Government's monetary policy with the credibility that it requires. In any case, such actions would be discourteous to the House and would damage the Government.

If the Government are to set up a committee, it is important that it is reputable, freestanding and operates fearlessly. The difficulty will arise when, as so often happens, and which is why I produced a rather arcane case in the debate on the previous clause, the Government want to deal with a hard case in one way, and the European Community or the demands of the exchange rate want them to do something else. The Government would not be able to meet all their objectives and might be tempted to try to nobble the Committee or soften its conclusions because they found them unpalatable. When discussing the question of European monetary union, the late Nick Ridley once said that he did not want, three months before a British general election, to be faced with a central banker and to have to say, ``Who do you think you are, Herr Pohl?'' The purpose of this clutch of amendments is to get the Government to tell us how they see the membership of the Committee and the various other devices that might be adopted to ensure that it operates in an open and transparent manner, which will, on occasion, inevitably mean upsetting Ministers and not doing what they would wish. The amendment relates to an open competition for the appointment of what I described, somewhat limply, as independent members.

It is fair to rehearse here the constitution of the Monetary Policy Committee, which the Government have set out in the Bill. It should consist of the Governor and deputy Governors, making three. Two members are appointed by the Governor after consultation with the Chancellor. Those two members and their duties are set out rather more clearly in clause 13(3). One has the executive responsibility within the Bank for monetary policy analysis; the other has responsibility for monetary policy operations. Within what might be termed the home team we have a total of five. Then there are to be four outside, or independent members appointed by the Chancellor of the Exchequer.

I said that the Opposition do not complain about the choice, though there will be ways of looking at this. We have tabled amendments about whether prospective members of the MPC should come before the Treasury Committee. The suggestion in this amendment is that there should be an open competition. That at least would ensure that, if the rules and conduct of the competition were transparent and open, the persons chosen would be the most suitable people for the job. They would not be chosen because they happen to be Labour party supporters--perish the thought--or even Conservative party supporters. We are not seeking some kind of political balance here. They should not necessarily be residents of the United Kingdom or citizens of it. Indeed, there is one distinguished member of the MPC who is not. They should be the best people for the job.

I am sure that it would be possible, although I do not have a template for it at the moment, for the Government to draw up a job specification, to seek applications and to enable people to refer themselves or others, or to suggest names. They would all be considered on one basic set of criteria, and the best people would be chosen. It is not quite clear to me, even from the efforts that have been made by the Economic Secretary, that the Chancellor has completely specified the nature of the selection process for the initial members of that committee. There will inevitably be casual vacancies as people move on or retire.
