Mr. Oliver Letwin (West Dorset): I welcome you to the Chair, Dr. Clark. I doubt that you will be sterner in your guidance than your co-Chairman, Mr. Cook, who rightly reminded me to address my remarks to him, not to others in Committee.

We were treated this morning to many arguments about the method that was used for what the Government are pleased to call valorisation--in other words, indexation. The Financial Secretary will be sorry to hear that that issue arises in relation to hydrocarbon oil duties. I wish to declare a potential interest as some clients may have an interest in oil matters, but I do want to know how the calculation was made for hydrocarbon oil duties. Since this moming's sitting, the Financial Secretary will have had the advantage of being able to refer to the fountain of all wisdom in Great George street and no doubt he is now fully equipped with the figures. I hope that he will let us into the secret and tell us the market expectations for mortgage interest rate increases that are built into the calculation.

Beyond that, will the Financial Secretary explain the relationship between valorisation and escalations? Last year, the Chancellor told us with some flourish that the escalator was to be brought to an end. It is a term of art, in that it is an automatic mechanism for uprating something, but other mechanisms are used for such purposes, one of which is straightforward, so-called valorisation or indexation. I shall not tediously remind the Committee that we have explored the composition of the forecasts of the RPI all-prices index increases, which are being used as a basis for the so-called valorisation. We know that the set-up is asymmetric in that it could contain a its persistent bias without its being corrected ex post.

Can the Financial Secretary guarantee that, if the indexation turns out over time to re-create the escalator or part of the escalator that had been abolished with such a flourish, the error will be corrected? The Chancellor said specifically to the public that there will not be an escalator, so I take it that that means that there will not be a revalorisation that goes beyond revalorisation. In such a case, for the sake of consistency, surely there should be an obligation to produce a correction after the fact on a continuing basis. However, there is nothing like that in the clause and it would be helpful if the hon. Gentleman explained the provision. Is there to be such an exposed adjustment in the light of the Chancellor's statements and, if not, why not?

Dr. Nick Palmer (Broxtowe): The hon. Gentleman is suggesting, as he did this morning, that there should be some compensation if the reality turns out to be different from the prediction. Is he also suggesting that if an overestimation was made there should be a surcharge--or is he suggesting a one-way ticket?

Mr. Letwin: The hon. Gentleman asks a reasonable question which I might, in the manner of an e-mail, forward to the Financial Secretary. It would be interesting to know whether the correction is to be symmetrical or asymmetrical. As I mentioned in another context this morning, it was the practice of the previous Government to operate a forward-looking variant of indexation. The adjustment was made on an asymmetric basis, so that the gain to the recipients was banked when the indexation turned out to be less great than the actual. The recipients were not asked to hand back social security payments when the indexation turned out to be greater than the actual.

Whether that variand should apply in the case that we are discussing is an interesting question of public policy. I would not presume to insist that it should be one way or the other, but we need an explanation of a symmetrical adjustment at least; at best, we should have an explanation of an asymmetric one. If it turns out to be the latter, the Financial Secretary will have a persistent bias in his favour. As things stand, we can conclude that there is an implicit escalator, if the forecasts persistently turn out to be above the reality.

To know how likely that is to be the case, we desperately need the answer to the following question. What is the figure for a so-called market expectation of the interest rate rise component built into the RPI all-prices index, and how much like a market expectation is that?

While we are on the subject, will the Financial Secretary also tell us what he means by a market expectation? We have had an opportunity to think about that question in the interim, and I have had the inestimable advantage of talking it over with my right hon. Friend the Member for Charnwood (Mr. Dorrell) over a drink of tea. The Financial Secretary may be able to tell us that we are mistaken and that the standard measure, the discrepancy of bond yields--of indexed and nominal bonds--is what led the Govenunent to create the forecast. However, it was not verified by the National Audit Office as being consistent with the RPIX forecast--that was true two years ago but not this year.

I believe that he is so well endowed in the intellectual department that he could invent programmes for electronic devices. Between sittings, he may have invented a programme to discover the answer to the very question that we are asking.

I return to the seminal question, which is whether the market expectation to which the Financial Secretary referred as the basis for the interest rate rise component in the RPI all-prices index used to "revalorise" the hydrocarbon oil duty is a bond yield-based measure or a judgmental measure based on the forecast of someone in the Treasury. That is vitally important. To refer to the question by the hon. Member for Broxtowe (Dr. Palmer), whose constituency I temporarily forgot--

Mr. Graham Allen (Nottingham, North): Labour gain.

Mr. Letwin: The hon. Gentleman and I are fast becoming pals.

The hon. Member for Broxtowe rightly referred to asymmetry; it is probably true that if the Treasury, with or without the approval of the National Audit Office, builds those estimates into its forecast of the RPI all--prices index as a bond yield-based measure--even where genuine market mistakes, in the sense of impact on long-run bond prices, occur--they will probably turn out to be symmetrical over time. I say that without empirical proof but simply as a matter of theory. I take it that there is no inherent bias in the market and therefore that, over quite long periods, the taxpayer ought to end up just about even stevens on hydrocarbon oil duties, compared with genuine ex post inflation measures, if the market measure is used. If, however, the Treasury has simply made a judgmental insertion into the forecast of the RPI all-prices index, there may be--this comes back to the point raised by the hon. Member for Broxtowe--a systemic bias, making it all the more important to know whether there will be correction and whether we have an implicit escalator in the hydrocarbon oil duty increases. The Treasury is not just the body that sets the hydrocarbon oil duties through the Finance Bill; although no longer responsible for the setting of interest rates, it is also a main player. It is at least the second most important player in the land in determining macro-economic policy--and since the Chancellor sets the RPIX targets for the Monetary Policy Committee, it could be argued that it is the most important player in that. If the Treasury had put in a judgmental measure, that would convey--bizarrely in the case of indexing hydrocarbon oil duties, for example--a signal for the markets.

There is therefore a problem of feedback when talking of a market expectation and it is extremely important for the Financial Secretary to recapitulate several points. First, what is the figure for the mortgage interest rate rise component in the RPI all-prices index forecast that yields the 3.41 per cent. being used for that indexation? Secondly, what is the basis upon which that estimate of the interest rate rise has been made? Is it a judgmental measure or a bond yield differential measure? Thirdly, at which date was the forecast constructed? Finally, what measures will be taken in the light of the Chancellor's remarks that there will not any longer be an escalator in the hydrocarbon oil duties to ensure ex post that any over-egging of the rises will be removed?

He was clear about the calculation method and about the fact that the interest rates, as they stood on Budget day, did not justify a figure of 3.41 per cent. He said that the figure was arrived at because the Government included in the estimate of RPI for September 2000 interest rate increases that on Budget day had not come into force. He said that the quantum of those expected increases in interest rates on Budget day was "market expectations".

My hon. Friend the Member for West Dorset is seeking clarification of what that phrase means. As one would expect of him, he moderately asked whether the concept of "market expectations" focused on measurable figures in the bond markets, which is a conventional way in which to consider expected future movements of interest rates, or whether it represented an entirely judgmental question arrived at in the Treasury.

The matter is important, and affects everyone who pays a mortgage or pays any interest in the British economy. One might be a hard-pressed manufacturer. Perhaps I should declare an interest as a director and shareholder in a manufacturing business who is not, for this purpose, paying interest. The Chancellor of the Exchequer did not actually go to the Dispatch Box on Budget day to say that interest rates would go up, but he announced inflation forecasts that the Financial Secretary told us could be justified only if the Chancellor, when he announced them, expected interest rates to go up between Budget day and September 2000.

I echo the question asked by my hon. Friend the Member for West Dorset. Perhaps more importantly, I ask the Financial Secretary to follow the open approach that he adopted this morning and give the Committee one extra piece of information. What is the number that he included in his forecasts to justify the figure of 3.41 per cent?

The Financial Secretary has made a good start in being open with the Committee. We now seek the number that he acknowledged this morning was in the Government's working papers.

Mr. Edward Davey (Kingston and Surbiton): May I, from the Liberal Democrat Benches, welcome you to the Chair, Dr. Clark, and look forward to your presiding over our proceedings as we enter late May, June and, no doubt, the Government Whip notwithstanding, July.

I echo the comments of the hon. Member for West Dorset and the right hon. Member for Charnwood. Liberal Democrats want the Government to be upfront about the methodology and figures used in calculating the uprating index for this financial year. It is an important issue, and they should be transparent about it. They need not be worried if, as the Financial Secretary told the Committee before we adjourned for lunch, it is standard practice and has been used throughout this Parliament. If that is so, the Financial Secretary should have no political problem with telling us the methodology and giving us the figures, and when he does so, I hope that he will try to justify the methodology. Market expectations do not provide sufficient detail, so we cannot be expected to accept that justification. They are a subjective measure unless it is clear which market has been used.

Does the Financial Secretary feel that market expectations are a sustainable measure for such an important decision? They are notoriously imperfect on such issues. In the past two years, with the introduction of a new anti-inflation regime and the Monetary Policy Committee setting interest rates, mistakes have often been made in learning how that new body of nine members, each with a different attitude about how the economy works, votes on interest rates. We have witnessed volatility in the attempts by markets to second-guess what the MPC will do, especially in relation to short-term interest rates. Using market expectation of future interest rates may, therefore, lead to an inaccurate measure of future inflation as measured by indices that include short-term interest rates. I hope that the Financial Secretary will try to meet that anxiety.

The argument of the hon. Member for West Dorset was so persuasive that I almost felt that in this case we should perhaps consider using not the main RPI, because of the sensitivities involved, but the underlying measure of inflation, RPIX, because it might be more stable and might prevent the need for swings and roundabouts to ensure that one particular sector or group of our constituents is not deleteriously affected by changes in tax rates. Perhaps we should be looking elsewhere for the right uprating index. The RPI index might be a candidate, although, because of its political significance and economic and social effects, a wider debate is necessary to decide how and when to make that change. It would be a major change, but if problems such as those described are being experienced, there may be a case for changing the uprating index.

Mr. Letwin: I agree with the hon. Gentleman that there is a case to be made. However, does he agree that the most important point is consistency? Consistency and transparency are equally important principles that should apply to any index used for such an important matter.

I move from the debate about the interest rate and market expectations that the Treasury has used to set the index to the policy of uprating petrol duties. Last year, we had a long debate, because the Government were still using the escalator and shoving up petrol duties by significant amounts. It is therefore right to welcome the fact that the escalator has gone, even though the Government and the Chancellor will make the operating decision on a case-by-case and year-by-year basis.

I hope that escalators of recent years, under the Conservative and Labour Governments, have served their purpose, and that we can now look forward to much lower increases in petrol duty. However, Liberal Democrat Members want to reiterate the point that we made when the escalator was in place--it is necessary to compensate motorists, especially rural motorists, for the extra costs that they must bear as a result of increases in petrol duty. We have often argued for reductions in other taxes on motorists, especially vehicle excise duty, which is a tax on ownership and does not serve a useful purpose. It is better, environmentally speaking, to tax use, not ownership. We have therefore argued, on too many occasions to recall, that VED would be a good tax by means of which to compensate the motorist properly. The Government have made a few tiny steps towards graduating VED, as we will discuss later, but the two taxes have never been linked. That is not joined-up government, not holistic, and does not ensure that the interests of private and commercial motorists are looked after.

I welcome the freeze on duty on road fuel gases that the clause introduces. The use of such fuels has increased, and I hope that the Government will do more to encourage motorists to use such fuels, which are of considerable benefit to the environment. If such encouragement succeeds, many of the arguments that we have had previously about the trade-off between environmental and economic benefits will be superseded, as environmentally friendly fuels will be used by the vast majority of vehicles. The use of such fuels cannot be encouraged sufficiently through a small incentive in terms of duty. Clearly, such an incentive would be helpful--as was the introduction of lower fuel duties for unleaded petrol--but a different issue is at stake in relation to some of the new fuels. A critical mass in terms of infrastructure is required. Unleaded petrol was easy to deal with, because the infrastructure existed already, and the price incentive was sufficient to ensure a speedy uptake. However, new fuel gases will require a different infrastructure that fuel stations--to use the generic term--will have to introduce, operate and maintain. There would have to be a quantum leap in investment by the businesses involved.

The Government would have to do more than introduce a mere price incentive to promote new and environmentally beneficial fuels. Grants and support would be necessary to assist those companies that would supply the new fuel to consumers, and to make sure that vehicle manufacturers and the main vehicle purchasers are attracted to such environmentally friendly vehicles. So I hope that the Financial Secretary will assure the Committee that the Government's policy does not rely simply on a price incentive.

I urge the Financial Secretary to answer the central question. Which expectations have been used in the calculation of the index? We need to know before we can allow the clause to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 5

Ultra low sulphur petrol

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Timms: I think that clause 7, rather than clause 5, is relevant to the last set of issues to which the hon. Gentleman referred. A number of provisions have been made for the definitions to be changed by regulation. The hon. Gentleman makes a fair point, which I shall be happy to deal with when we reach the relevant clause.

I do not think that this is a technical clause. It is much more than that. As the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs said, it gives ultra-low sulphur petrol a duty incentive of 1p a litre over conventional unleaded petrol. European Commission legislation reduced the sulphur content of unleaded petrol to 150 parts per million from January this year. I am pleased to say that all UK refiners have met that requirement, so the time has come to look forward to the next change in specification, which will take effect from 1 January 2005.

The hon. Gentleman referred to the precedent of unleaded petrol, although, in many ways, the most impressive precedent is the duty incentive for ultra-low sulphur diesel, which already accounts for all of the UK road diesel market, five years ahead of the European requirement. We want ultra-low sulphur petrol to repeat that success story. It offers environmental benefits over conventional unleaded petrol, and rejuvenates--or cleans up--catalytic converters in the existing vehicle fleet. The duty incentive should encourage production and use of ultra-low sulphur petrol well before the 2005 deadline. It will also allow the introduction of advanced petrol engine technology, which can further improve fuel efficiency by up to 20 per cent. and is dependent on low sulphur fuel.

The specification for ultra-low sulphur petrol follows the current restrictions in the 2005 European petrol specification, which requires that the sulphur content is less than 50 parts per million, and that the aromatics content does not exceed 35 per cent. We shall, of course, keep that specification under review to reflect future changes. The target date for introducing the incentive is 1 October, and we are currently consulting the industry on the exact timing.

The hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs asked why the incentive was only 1p a litre. We think that that is sufficient to encourage manufacture and distribution of this cleaner fuel. Some companies are already selling ultra-low sulphur petrol, and will be grateful for the extra penny to offset development and production costs. We gave careful consideration to the representations that we received from oil companies in advance of the Budget, and concluded that an incentive of 1p a litre would have the desired effect. The selling price is a matter for oil companies.

Mr. Dorrell: Will the Financial Secretary make clear whether changes to engines are necessary, as in the case of unleaded petrol? Can the new fuel be put straight into a conventional engine?

Mr. Timms: Existing engines require no adaptations to use the new fuel. However, in order to gain the additional fuel efficiency benefits of the next generation of petrol engines, ultra-low sulphur petrol will be necessary.

Mr. Flight: I am trying to determine how quickly the new fuel will take off. Do the Government expect the new low-sulphur petrol to cost the consumer the same, less or more, net of duty? Mr. Timms: The selling price is a matter for the companies to determine. There is a clue in the fact that BP has been selling ultra-low sulphur petrol at the same price as unleaded petrol in Edinburgh without the duty incentive.

Clause 8

Penalties for misuse of rebated heavy oil

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

 

Mr. Flight: As I understand it, the purpose of the clause is to reverse a judgment last March by the Court Session in Edinburgh on the interpretation by Customs and Excise of the existing legislation on fines for breach of the rules for rebates on heavy oil as a road fuel. There are two different penalties of £250 each; the issue is whether two lots of £250 can be applied at the same time, or only one. Mr. Timms: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for his wholly accurate account of the clause, which extends the civil penalty provisions that Customs and Excise may apply when it detects the misuse of rebated heavy oil as road fuel. Some categories of user have access to rebated heavy oil, but ordinary road users are not permitted to use it.

The legislation currently provides for the imposition of alternative penalties when first-time misuse is detected, either in the taking-in of rebated heavy oil to a road vehicle or in using rebated heavy oil as fuel for a road vehicle. As the hon. Gentleman rightly says, on 5 March the Court of Session in Edinburgh determined that Customs and Excise's interpretation of the legislation was incorrect, and that the penalties for an individual were alternative rather than cumulative. The clause merely corrects that. Each penalty is in the amount of £250, which, if imposed alone, may not be enough of a deterrent to prevent misuse. The power to add penalties together to total £500, as was always the intention, will deter potential offenders to a much greater extent. The change will be especially welcomed by hauliers, to return to our earlier discussion, because it will deter the cowboy element of the haulage industry, which rears its head from time to time, from using cheaper "red diesel" when legitimate hauliers are using legitimate diesel.

Clause 11

Emulsions of water in gas oil

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Flight: This seems to be a further helpful measure, which exempts from duty the water content in emulsions of water in gas oil, which are used substantially by local pick-up delivery fleets, waste management vehicles and for urban short bus journeys. I should be interested to know how material that will be in terms of an incentive to the use of emulsions of water in gas oil. I assume also that such emulsions are environmentally more friendly than even the new forms of diesel and unleaded petrol. What financial incentive will the measure give to the usage of such environmentally friendly substances?

 

Mr. Timms: It turns out that oil and water can mix. Water diesel emulsion is a new, environmentally friendly fuel in which water is suspended in the oil, as opposed to normal emulsion, in which oil particles are suspended in water. The water is not used as a fuel but assists fuel dispersion in the engine. It lowers the combustion temperature and reduces nitrogen oxide and particulate emissions, both of which are of particular concern in urban areas--at which the use of this fuel is particularly aimed. It is targeted at centrally located fleets that are able to refuel from a central point--pick-up and delivery fleets, urban and school buses, and waste vehicles, for example--and early tests show that an emulsion fuel with a water content of 10 per cent. gives a 9 per cent. reduction in nitrogen oxide compared with ultra-low sulphur diesel and a minimum of 19 per cent. reduction in particulates. It therefore offers a substantial improvement.

To introduce the concession, we need a derogation under article 8(4) of EEC directive 92/81, to enable us to exempt the water content in such emulsions from excise duty. Unfortunately, that approval has not yet been received so the measure will come into effect not from Budget day but from Royal Assent. The French Government have applied for and been granted a derogation under that article, to allow them to do something similar. The product is already in use in France; at the end of April 1998, more than 70 buses and coaches were running on water-diesel emulsion there. No problems arose from those on-road experiments, which were operated by bus companies in cities such as Chambery and Lyon. Two UK companies, Elf and Lubrizol, are involved in the manufacture and development of those emulsions.

Once again, we are using the taxation system to encourage the development and use of environmentally-friendly fuels. The measure makes it economically viable for companies to develop and produce that emulsion. That is yet further evidence of our commitment to improving urban air quality, and I hope that the Committee will welcome it.

Mr. Flight: Does the Minister know what the innovation is worth in terms of money and what the financial incentive of exempting the water content will be?

Mr. Timms: I am sure that I do know that. If the hon. Gentleman will give me a moment to gather my thoughts on the subject--now I do know. Water is 10 per cent. of the emulsion, so that amounts to a 10 per cent. reduction in the duty due.

Mr. Flight: So it is 4p.

Mr. Timms: Roughly. It is 10 per cent. off.
