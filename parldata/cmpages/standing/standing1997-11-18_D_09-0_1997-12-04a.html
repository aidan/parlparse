Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I beg to move amendment No. 49, in page 9, line 36, at end add

    "(1A) In discharging the functions transferred to them under this section the Authority shall have regard to the principles of

    (a) accountability,

    (b) transparency,

    (c) the avoidance of duplication, and

    (d) information exchange.".

Alert Committee members will notice that the terms of the amendment follow closely the text of the opening paragraph of the memorandum of understanding between the Treasury, the Bank of England and the Financial Services Authority. That was deliberate, because we want to put into law the non-statutory principles that are set out in a signed memorandum, which has no legal force.

The Financial Services Authority may have been created, but it has not yet been backed by an Act of Parliament. Indeed, such legislation is some way off. Much consultation is being conducted at present. We have been promised that a Bill will be drafted next year that sets up and defines the FSA. It is anticipated that the Bill will come into effect in late 1999. Given that we are about two years away from having in place a full statutory system, we want to take the opportunity provided by this Bill to put into law some defining conditions and objectives governing the supervision of banks, which otherwise would fall into the interregnum and fail to be regulated by law.

Dangers can arise. My hon. Friends have alluded to several examples of the mistakes and problems that occur in other banking and financial systems during the transfer of responsibilities from one organisation to another. Different examples of that come to mind. I have noticed, for instance, that a rail crash often occurs after signals on a stretch of track have been upgraded. Before the supposed improvements are bedded in, tested and understood by operators, there is a period of danger. I foresee that occurring with banking supervision.

Until now, supervision has been undertaken by the Bank of England. It would be first to admit that it has fallen down on occasions when regulation was less than perfect. Opposition Members do not want to obstruct genuine improvements; nevertheless, the Bank has a great deal of expertise and experience, and it has a good vantage point for carrying out the task.

There is suspicion that the Government have opted for a single regulatory body, encompassing the entire financial spectrum, simply for tidiness and that there might be dangers in doing so.

We want to put those objectives into the Bill. The memorandum of understanding is an attempt to regulate the relationship between the Bank, the Treasury and the FSA. The principles referred to in the amendment accountability, transparency, the avoidance of duplication and regular information exchange should to be used not only to regulate relations between the institutions but to govern how the FSA discharges its functions. In the absence of the amendment, or something similar, no law will be approved by the House ensuring that the FSA pursues those principles until the financial services Bill arrives next year, when we can debate at greater length the duties, obligations and context in which the FSA will work.

Mr. Ruffley: As always, my hon. Friend makes a telling point and I ask the Economic Secretary to give her views on it. We have a duty to examine the logic that leads the Government to propose transferring the supervisory functions of the Bank under existing legislation to the newly formed SIB and to propose that, in the interim, the relationship be governed by a memorandum of understanding, which has no statutory force.

I want to take the argument forward. On grounds of logic and practice, why do the Government want to merge the functions of supervision and securities regulation in the newly configured SIB? One reason it is an understandable one may relate to the seeming conflict that occasionally arises between the macro-economic management of monetary policy and the obligation of Governments and central banks to deliver a stable banking system.

Tietmayer and Schlesinger, the distinguished German economists, bankers and politicians, are right to argue that the British experience has been to give excessive weight to the second of those perceived obligations that the maintenance of a stable banking system is the chief object of policy management. Their argument relates to the period when the former right hon. Member for Blaby, Lord Lawson, was in control of these country's economic management. They made the observation that while he was keen to have a liberalised banking regime, it was probably at the expense of a slackness in monetary policy, given the interest rate policy that he was running. This is obviously not a party-political point or, if it is, it is not one that helps my party. There is a perceived conflict or tension between the desire for a stable banking system and the desire for a tight monetary policy that will deliver the anti-inflationary discipline that most Governments seek.

The second example is the American experience of 1982. There is perennial concern about third-party debt and the exposure that American banks have to third-world countries. The high interest rates which the American domestic economy needed at that time led to a great deal of instability. That is another example of the perceived conflict between delivering anti-inflation objectives and at the same time providing an interest rate conducive to a stable domestic banking environment. As we know, Mr. Volcker decided to relax anti-inflationary discipline in pursuit of what he perceived to be a strengthening of the domestic banking system in the United States.

Those two examples illustrate the fact that such tensions are inevitable and will not be solved by the structural solution proposed in the Bill. The chief reason that the Government adduce for making the change is that it will resolve the policy dilemma that I described; the change will not achieve that result.

The amendment is important because we need to set down in statute a set of relationships that clearly define what the Bank, in its new form, is meant to be doing. The Bank will always be the lender of last resort. The problem with regulating the relationship through a non-statutory memorandum is that all sorts of complicated mechanisms will need to be put in place before the Bank can, under the new arrangements, properly supervise the banking system in this country.

Clearly, money is needed to bail out a failing bank that will always be the case. Does that mean, for instance, that large banks may ultimately require guarantees from the central bank or that larger banks that assist in the bailing out of failing banks may require extra liquidity? How is the Bank to deliver that in such a circumstance? There are all sorts of complicated mechanisms and relationships into which the Bank will have to enter in pursuit of its supervisory function. It is difficult to see how we can determine how those relationships will work without them being set in statute. It is in that spirit that I support the amendment, for the reasons that my hon. Friend and I have adduced.

In a strange way, the amendment may answer some of the points made by the hon. Member for Daventry in relation to schedule 4. It is a technical amendment that makes the necessary changes to the relevant statutes consequential on the transfer of banking supervision, which is the purpose of scheduled 5.

Inevitably, given the history of the Bank, there is a class of document that refers to the Bank in its current supervisory role that is not adequately covered by the Bill as drafted. Such documents, which are typically private contracts to which the Bank is not a party, refer to the Bank because it performs supervisory functions. An example would be the documentation relating to an issue by a bank of subordinated debt, where, if the debt is to count as capital for regulatory purposes, a provision will be included that the debt cannot be repayed early without the Bank's consent.

The amendment would insert two new subsections, 23(3) and 23(4), which ensure that, following the transfer of supervisory functions, such references will be read as referring to the Financial Services Authority. It is a purely technical arrangement to tidy up necessary parts of statute and I commend the amendment to the Committee.
