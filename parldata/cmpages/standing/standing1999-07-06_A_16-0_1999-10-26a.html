The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): Before we adjourned, I was commenting on amendments Nos. 285 to 287, which were moved by Opposition Members, and on Government amendments Nos. 336 to 340. I was making the point that the position of the directors of contractors of the firm in question will depend on the circumstances of the case. For the Financial Services Authority to require those individuals to obtain approval, they would need to meet one of the three conditions in subsections (5), (6) or (7). Either they will be performing functions that enable them to exercise a significant influence on the affairs of the authorised person, as set out in subsection (5), or they will need to meet one of the conditions in subsections (6) or (7), which relate to dealing with customers and their property respectively. If they do not meet any of those conditions, there will be no justification for requiring their approval, given that people who perform similar functions under different types of arrangement are not subject to approval requirements. Given that the question of directors is already covered, I hope that Opposition Members will withdraw amendment No. 285.

Amendments Nos. 286 and 287 try to address the second and third conditions. With regard to amendment No. 286, it seems to me that, if a person is dealing ``for'' a customer, he must also be dealing ``with'' a customer''. The point that subsection (6) tries to address is that the function will require the person to have contact with the customer, and in connection with the carrying on of a regulated activity. We are talking about salespersons, advisers and intermediaries, who are often the kind of people who are currently registered with the Personal Investment Authority.

Amendment No. 287 relates to the third condition, which is not dissimilar to the second in terms of the protection it seeks to provide. However, where the second condition is aimed at direct contact with the customer, the third is aimed at circumstances in which the employee of the authorised firm is dealing with his or her assets. I use the term ``dealing'' in a general sense. It might mean safeguarding, as amendment No. 287 suggests, but it might also include receiving money from a customer—whether directly or indirectly—and using it to buy shares. Equally, it might cover selling shares and passing the money back. Again, there is a link to the carrying on of regulated activities. Here, we are considering people such as traders, stockbrokers and fund managers, who are often members of the Securities and Futures Authority and the Investment Management Regulatory Organisation.

I hope that that explanation may prove helpful. To sum up, if they were to have any effect, amendments Nos. 286 and 287 would probably redraw the boundary between the two conditions. However, I do not believe that, when taken together, the amendments would materially alter the overall impact of the provisions contained in subsections (6) and (7). They would widen the second condition but correspondingly narrow the third. I shall illustrate that through reference to the example cited by the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton). He said that the example of an investment fund manager illustrates the need to amend clause 56(6), so that ``dealing with customers'' is changed to

    ``dealing with or for customers''.

However, such people will be covered under clause 56(7). As I said, where one amendment would indeed widen somewhat the second condition, the other would correspondingly narrow the third.

I shall now explain Government amendments Nos. 337 and 338, which also deal with the second and third conditions. Those conditions seek to address situations in which a person is dealing with consumers or their assets when what he is doing is, in effect, performing a function that gives rise to an authorisation requirement for the firm.

Our concern was that, as drafted, the second and third conditions were too wide. On the basis of the clause's current drafting, the FSA would be able to require the approval of an employee of an insurance company who gives advice on general—that is, non-life—insurance policies. That is because although giving advice on, for example, travel insurance, would not constitute a regulated activity, under current plans the advice is none the less given in connection with the regulated activity of effecting contracts of insurance which is carried on by the firm. That would be undesirable because if the same advice were given by a general insurance broker it would not fall within the scope of those arrangements.

Amendments Nos. 337 and 338 therefore seek to restrict controlled functions to those that are substantially connected with regulated activities, and they will remove the anomaly by pointing the definition of controlled functions to those functions that are part and parcel of carrying on the regulated activity. That is an important clarification of the Bill.

Amendment No. 336 simply removes a redundant word, ``directly'', which was originally intended to reinforce the degree of connection with regulated activities. There is a difficulty with that word because it would seem to preclude situations in which a person dealt indirectly—for example, if a solicitor obtained investment advice on behalf of his client from an authorised person, the authorised person may not deal directly with the customer, although he was clearly giving the customer advice and should therefore be covered by the provisions.

Finally, amendments Nos. 339 and 340 are minor amendments, which will tidy up the clause's definition of the word ``arrangement''.

I commend the amendments to the Committee.
