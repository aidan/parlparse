Clause 186

Financial assistance etc.

Mr. Robert Syms (Poole): I beg to move amendment No. 889, in page 101, line 39, after `may', insert

    `subject to implementation of an appropriate tendering process (where practicable)'.

This is an important amendment to an important part of the Bill. Clause 186 deals with financial assistance and what the rail authority may do to secure and provide improvements or in terms of developments by other railway authorities. We aim to find out from the Government precisely what that role involves. There is a suspicion that the provision will allow the Strategic Rail Authority to interfere rather more in the running of the railway and there may also be greater interference with franchise agreements. The briefing that we were given by Railtrack states:

    This clause gives very wide ranging powers to the SRA. Whilst Railtrack accepts that the SRA will have a role in making grants for particular projects we would hope that any direct funding would be at the margin and that the grants would not undermine the relationship between Railtrack and the Train Operators. Direct funding and direction of investment on a wide scale would make it more difficult for Railtrack to raise capital to fund our investment programme and would reduce the train operators' leverage on Railtrack. We believe this would make it harder for Railtrack to meet the aspirations of Government and passengers for an improving and growing railway.

There is concern among some outside bodies.

Our amendment No. 889 would ensure that any involvement by the SRA would have to be on the basis of competitive tendering. This country has a fairly good history of competitive tendering: of allowing people to submit tenders for services. We are not sure why this provision has been omitted from the clause. We shall be interested to hear what the Government are thinking on this part of the Bill and, in particular, why they do not think that there should be a requirement on the SRA, where it steps in, to conform to something like the competitive tendering process.

Mr. Hill: I will deal with the broader issues that the hon. Gentleman raised before dealing with the amendment.

During our proceedings we shall have further opportunities to explore competitive tendering. The Government have no anti-competitive tendering intentions for the SRA. In our discussion on amendment No. 881, we stated that in cases where the authority acts as a procurement agency, the value-for-money duty may drive it to a competitive process, which is a good thing. Franchising is one such example. Companies are competing for longer replacement franchises as part of the franchise replacement process. However, competition would not be appropriate where the authority was not securing services but responding to a request from the industry for specific financial assistance, such as an application for a freight facilities grant. The authority will judge such applications against criteria for assistance and it should not have to invent spurious competition.

In response to a question from the hon. Gentleman about the SRA's relationship with the train operators, as our exchanges unfold there will be further opportunities to examine that matter in greater detail. For now, I assure the hon. Gentleman that the provisions keep the existing basic structure of TOC and franchising director relationships.

The amendment would require the SRA to exercise all its financial functions under clause 186, subject to a tendering process, with the proviso of ``where practicable''. The amendment is both unnecessary and inappropriate. Of course, we expect the exercise of any power to make grants, loans or guarantees under the clause to achieve the best possible value for money. That is why clause 182(4) explicitly requires the authority to secure any grants, loans or other payments ``efficiently and economically''. Therefore, the amendment is unnecessary. The authority, in the commonsense application of its value-for-money duty, must decide whether, or to what degree, competition is appropriate to the facts and circumstances of each case and must disregard any dogmatic requirement for it. I hope that with those assurances the hon. Gentleman will withdraw his amendment.
