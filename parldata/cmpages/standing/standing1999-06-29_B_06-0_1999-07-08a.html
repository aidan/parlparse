Amendment No. 29 sets down the need to give a receipt for samples taken. That provision would tighten the Bill. Food products taken as samples may be of considerable value to those premises and it would be good practice for the visiting officer of the agency to give a receipt for them.

In the event of a need to enter premises, we would ask the Minister to consider the practice of Customs and Excise officers, who would normally be accompanied by the police when undertaking visits. It is important that the nature of the visit should be proportionate to the task in hand because the way in which officials conduct random visits to premises will dictate the response that they receive. We welcome the Minister's comments on the parallels between the way in which Customs and Excise officials might carry out their visits and those carried out by the staff of the agency.

It is also important that agency staff should not be given carte blanche to enter premises and remove what they want without any justification or explanation. Customs and Excise officers have to give receipts for anything and everything that they take; surely it is right to expect Food Standards Agency officials to do the same. It is reasonable to expect officials to provide a receipt for goods that they remove, if only to make them more careful about what they take. They may take items other than food that are worth a considerable amount, and that could result in the owner of the premises losing thousands of pounds worth of stock--only to find that, with no receipt for those items, any entitlement to compensation would be weakened because there was no evidence of the items having been removed in the first place.

Amendment No. 33 is designed to deal with the fact that the manner in which an observation is carried out and in which the agency officers conduct themselves may give rise to irritation on the part of the owner of the premises. If a visit is made without prior appointment and items are taken without explanation or receipt, it is not unreasonable to expect that the owner of the premises may be less than entirely happy. Adding the words ``without reasonable excuse'' strikes a better balance and only mirrors the provision in paragraph (b). It is reasonable to balance the clause in that way.

Amendment No. 30 concerns records. Agency officers will have the power to

    ``inspect and copy any records found on any premises''.

The records are not precisely defined. They could pertain to the financial health of a company. Outsiders cannot usually inspect and copy such records. Businesses are concerned about protecting commercial confidentiality. In the first group of amendments to the clause, we debated the qualifications that officers of the agency should have to define more closely who will be appropriate to enter premises and carry out such observations. We are interested in the level of those qualifications in relation to what will be found and understood from the records, and what follows from that.

Amendment No. 73 reasonably suggests that a receipt should be issued if copies are taken of records found on the premises. If any commercially sensitive information is subsequently misused--a subject that will be debated under a later clause--inquiries will consider it important to have evidence of when that information was first acquired and when it reappeared elsewhere.

The amendments are designed to tighten the powers of entry that officers will be granted under the clause. They are all well within the parallel of the guidelines that cover Customs and Excise. We hope that the Government will be amenable to amending the clause in a way that will provide safeguards for the owners of observed premises.

Not only the farms that my hon. Friend the Member for Meriden (Mrs. Spelman) mentioned will be affected--most of which are one-man or one-man-and-woman businesses, going flat out to keep the business going. In most cases, Customs and Excise officials and others who inspect farmers' books make appointments to ensure that the farmers are available--the same is true for other food businesses--reserving the power to enforce the law when they are there and to have a right of entry at any time if they suspect that an offence has been committed. In enforcement circumstances, I am happy, if the law has been broken or if there is a threat that it may be broken, for officers to have a right of entry without prior appointment, because the law must be enforced. However, when officials are not required to enforce the law, it is extraordinary to give them powers of entry without their having to make prior appointment. Thousands of small businesses cannot afford to take time off in the middle of an operation, whether it be making jam tarts, silaging or serving customers in the little village shop, to interrupt what they are doing in order to render all assistance to people coming unannounced to observe.

We do not know how long such operations may take. Time constraints will be placed on officers, because the agency will no doubt give them a quota. If 200 officers or contracted-out employees must collect 187,000 samples, they will have to collect a few hundred samples a day, and will, therefore, have to perform under time and efficiency constraints. Nevertheless, we have no idea how long a business might be interrupted if such people arrive unannounced at any reasonable hour, demand that everything be laid at their disposal to take samples, inspect and copy records, and require someone to render assistance to them.

In most small businesses, during working hours, which may be from 7 am to 7 pm, people work flat out, whether making jam tarts, silaging, mucking out the byre or processing food. In the evening, they work on the computer records. Most small businesses, along with larger businesses, complain that the Government have made them the tax collector. They must complete computer and value-added-tax records in the evening to ensure that they are not prosecuted. If, in the middle of the day, during the physical production work--food processing, preparation or transportation--such businesses must break off to dig out computer records and help people who have not made an appointment for a more convenient time, that will seriously disrupt the business, all because people want to observe at their own convenience.

In the case of small businesses in particular, the person coming to observe should do so by prior appointment at a time convenient to the business, and often that will mean in the evening. It is not a nine-to-five operation; I do not see why such people should work from 9 o'clock to 5 o'clock at their convenience, when the bosses--not the employees--of small businesses such as bakeries in my constituency work from 6 am to 7 pm.
