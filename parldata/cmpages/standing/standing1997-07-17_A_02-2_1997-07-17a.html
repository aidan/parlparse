Mr. Green: Before dinner, I was in the throes of explaining the inadequacies of some of the calculations in the schedule that which have contributed to what the schedule calls the

``Quantification of a privatisation windfall''. In particular, paragraph 2(1) states that the estimate of the value in profit-making terms of the disposal made

    ``on the occasion of a company's flotation is the amount produced by multiplying the average annual profit for the company's initial period by the applicable price-to-earnings ratio.''

That is one of the central problems with the schedule. Both halves of the equation that is used to come to that calculation, which leads to the estimate of the windfall tax that the utilities will pay, are severely inadequate.

As I was saying immediately before dinner, the annual profit for the company's initial period is an inherently arbitrary figure. It can take no account of the difference between a company's performance when it was nationalised and after it has been privatised. It would be extraordinary if the profit did not increase, simply because of the different pressures on a company that operates in a private sector environment and with private sector management. We have seen across every sphere of British industry since the previous Government implemented the privatisation programme that those industries that were so often the worst run have, in a relatively short time, become some of the best run. One of the problems with the idea of profits in the initial period is that they can take no account of that phenomenon--the problem is not that they do not take account of it, but that they cannot take account of it. A value has to have been placed on a company at the time of privatisation, but it would have been impossible sensibly to predict how much better that company would do in the private sector.

Such a valuation is only half the equation that the schedule uses to try to establish the amount of excess profit that can be subject to a windfall tax. The other half is what the schedule describes as the ``applicable price-to-earnings ratio''. We have seen several times that that is, to use the Paymaster General's term, perfectly simple. Indeed, he advocated the benefits of simplicity.

I put it to him that, in this case, it is simplicity to the point of idiocy to try to create a single applicable PE ratio for several companies across a wide range of sectors in different industries. At best, it must be very rough and, as it turns out, it is a completely arbitrary calculation. The Inland Revenue's notes on the Finance Bill explain that the relevant PE ratio is set at nine. They state:

    ``This figure approximates to the lowest average sectoral price-to-earnings ratio of the companies liable to the tax.''

That is about one stage up from sticking one's finger in the air to see which way the wind is blowing. It is an average sectoral PE ratio for companies in different sectors--I suppose that one can define them as being in a utilities sector--and in different types of businesses. There are different degrees of competition--some have no competition at all, others have competition in their own sector. For example, there are, and always have been, two completely different classes of water company. To try to establish a single figure to make that calculation cannot be fair to all the companies that the Government are trying to tax.

Furthermore, we have seen the wide range of different industries in clause 2. Earlier, we discussed whether the Bill was a de jure hybrid Bill--which it is clearly not--or a de facto hybrid Bill. The clause that announces all the different definitions of companies--which the Government have had to use to avoid making it a hybrid Bill--makes it clear to anyone reading it honestly that it is simply not possible fairly to produce a single PE ratio for all those companies that can be regarded as an adequate base on which to make the calculation.

I invite the Government to assess the matter impartially and to ask themselves whether they are treating the companies fairly and are seen to be doing so. Many of them work in central areas of the British economy; they are not only important in themselves, but provide important services to a wide range of other industries, on whose future the prosperity and jobs of millions of people in this country depend. It is vital that those companies believe that they are being treated fairly--visibly so--by the Government. I contend that the schedule is clearly inadequate in that it treats in them unfairly.

There are other important incoherencies in the schedule. Paragraph 6 makes clear that the initial period on which the calculations are based is arbitrary. The Paymaster General has explained that the four-year period which is used for the majority of the companies is related to the price cap that was put on them. But the game is given away by paragraph 6(2), which states:

    ``Where the initial period of a company privatised by means of a flotation would...include any time on or after 1 April 1997, sub-paragraph (1) shall not apply and the initial period of that company shall be taken, instead, to be the period which--

    (a) begins with the day on which the time of its flotation falls; and

    (b) ends with the end of the last financial year of the company to end before 1 April 1997.''

No doubt that is administratively convenient, but it gives the game away that the four-year period, which can apply to all early privatisations, is not regarded as a central part of the calculation. The Government are prepared to introduce for any company which happened to have been privatised fewer than four years before 1 April 1997 an arbitrary cut-off date which may or may not give a realistic view of the company's long-term profitability, let alone its long-term capital needs. Those are two illustrations of the unfairness of the tax.

I can reveal the source of the idea of a windfall tax. I was at college at the same time as the Chancellor's adviser, Mr. Edward Balls, who is known to Members of Parliament. I used the same micro-economic textbooks as Mr. Balls in my first year. A favoured textbook was written by an economist called Koutsoyiannis. Economists are keen on assumptions--ceteris paribus and so on. The assumption was that the tax would be a one-off measure.
