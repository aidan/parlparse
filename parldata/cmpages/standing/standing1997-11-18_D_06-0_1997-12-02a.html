Clause 14

Publication of statements about decisions

10.30 am

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I beg to move amendment No. 27, in page 5, line 41, at end insert--

    `(1A) A statement under subsection (1) shall include, in relation to any vote taken on any action other than intervention in financial markets, an indication of the number and names of the members of the Committee who voted in favour and those who voted against that action.'.

Clause 14 deals with the publication of statements about decisions made by the Monetary Policy Committee. It takes us into the realm of the public's right to know and the accountability of that super-quango to Parliament, outside interests and the electorate. Given the committee's power and influence over interest rate decisions, it is important that there is a regular flow of information on a timely basis. There should be a presumption in favour of complete openness and a case must be made for any restriction that is placed on the publication of information about decisions. When we reach clause 15, we shall debate the publication of the minutes of the meetings, which is to be done six weeks after the date of the meeting in question.

Amendment No. 27 would provide for immediate publication, not only of the decision on interest rates that was made at a particular meeting, but the number of votes cast, the names of the members of the committee who cast them and the names of those who voted for the decision and who voted against it. We shall not press the amendment to a Division, but it is important to explore with the Government their reasons for the delay in the names being published. The people involved will no doubt be of a high quality; they will have been appointed to the committee by the Government for short terms, so there should be a countervailing openness about what they do, what actions they take and how they vote. On the face of it, there is no apparent objection to publishing the information immediately if it can be made available six weeks later.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: The hon. Gentleman already behaves like a member of the Government. I hope that he will serve in the Government in the future but, at this stage in his career, he should be on the side of openness and the public's right to know.

We all know that it is in the interest of any Government--or any organisation--to keep information to themselves, but there should be a counter-influence that argues for the right to publish names. The hon. Gentleman is probably familiar with the United States, a country that I used to visit frequently. One of the refreshing things about America is its presumption of openness and the public's right to know. A clear reason must be given in that country why information concerning names and how people voted should not be released immediately to the public.

The Bill already deals with the problem of market disruption, and we shall be debating later this morning why intervention in the market should, in certain circumstances, remain secret at least for a time. However, given that the names will eventually be published, I see no good reason why we should not move rather more quickly, as suggested in amendment No. 27.

There is an instinct in all organisations to conserve information. Governments possess that instinct; one could call it an instinct to secrecy. That instinct to be less than candid can become overwhelming when a mistake may have been made. For example, let us imagine that the Monetary Policy Committee decides to intervene in a market during a period of turbulence and its action fails--it turns out to be not only ill-judged but expensive. It may be decided not to publish the information at the time because that might create further turbulence.

Under the Bill, although the committee will keep under consideration whether publication of that decision could cause further damage, it might find all sorts of reasons for never concluding that publication was justified. For instance, it might regard the fact that it had failed as itself undermining the authority and reputation of the Bank of England. So its market intervention could remain secret for all time, which would be damaging and wrong.

Given that under their short terms of appointment the members of the committee are essentially creatures of the Executive, it is doubly important that at some time--I concede the case for delay in particular cases--we should be told about the committee's decisions. It is therefore perfectly reasonable to place a time limit of 12 months on how long such information can remain secret.

Clause 15

Publication of minutes of meetings

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 29, in page 6, line 43, at end insert--

    `(4A) Minutes published under this section shall also record, in relation to the deliberations of the Committee, the views expressed by members of the Committee and by an Treasury representative attending under paragraph 13 of Schedule 3 to this Act.'.

The amendment relates to the publication of the minutes of the meetings. The clause follows a precedent set by the previous Government by stipulating that the minutes should be published within six weeks. The amendment would fill out the provisions for the minutes and it would ensure that they record not just the votes of the members of the Monetary Policy Committee, but provide more information on their views and those of any Treasury representative present.

The Bill provides that a Treasury official may attend meetings of the committee, so it is important that the minutes give a full account of the discussions that take place. That does not mean recording just the bald voting figures, but recording the views expressed by the Bank and the Treasury. That is important because, as we said last week, if we are to achieve a successful monetary policy it is vital that, as far as possible, everyone should be committed to the same objects and the same instruments.

11.15 am

We discussed the dangers of a split developing between monetary and fiscal policy; there is also a danger of divisions developing between the Treasury and the Bank. To some extent, it does not matter if they occasionally differ slightly--I believe that the term for that is creative friction--but any difference of emphasis should be known and reported so that the market-makers and the public can comment on and watch the development of that interchange. In the same way, the publication of the minutes in the last Parliament enabled outsiders to note any differences in approach or emphasis between the Bank and my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Rushcliffe (Mr. Clarke), as the then Chancellor. It is healthy that differences should be recorded in the minutes rather than being covered up.

There is currently a difference between the Treasury and the Bank. The Chancellor's pre-Budget statement differed markedly in its inflation forecast from the view of the Bank. Rather unusually, and perhaps only temporarily, the Bank was noticeably more optimistic about the course of inflation over the next two years whilst the Chancellor, perhaps for his own purposes, had a different view. If that difference is discussed in Monetary Policy Committee meetings, it is important that that should be carried forward into the minutes.

The accountability of the members of that super-quango is also an issue. Conservative Members believe strongly--as did the Treasury Select Committee--that its members should be subject to confirmatory hearings by the Treasury Committee, to ensure that they are untainted by any party allegiance. That request was refused and in the absence of that measure, another safeguard is for the views expressed in the deliberations to be recorded so that we can judge whether members are operating independently, as we would wish.

As has been said, the members of some other central banks around the world, including the Federal Reserve Bank, have occasionally aligned themselves with the policies of the Government of the day--a particular example of that is what happened under President Nixon. A safeguard against that developing here is best ensured through overseeing members' appointment and the term served, but the Government would not listen to us on that. As a modest substitute, therefore, we need to be sure that the discussions are fully and properly recorded so that we can judge members' independence and integrity--not only when they are appointed but in their continuing work on that important committee. The amendment would ensure that the views expressed in it are recorded in the minutes and I ask the Government to accept it in that spirit.
