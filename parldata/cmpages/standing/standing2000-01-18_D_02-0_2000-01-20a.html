Mr. Simon Hughes (Southwark, North and Bermondsey): I beg to move amendment No. 34 in page 2, line 3, at end add

Clause 2 deals with the non-continuation of existing temporary legislation, particularly that which relates to Northern Ireland. New provisions for Northern Ireland will be found in part VII. The amendment is a probing one. I wonder whether the new provisions should be a continuation of the existing temporary provision, but drawn up to allow for the agreement of both Parliament and, more important, the new democratic body--the Northern Ireland Assembly.

The amendment also raises the question whether temporary provisions should come to an end rather than be extended. The consensus of opinion in Northern Ireland over the next few years may be that temporary provisions should come to an end. The Government said clearly on Second Reading that they wish eventually to remove the temporary provisions. I do not argue that the provisions should not continue, but the reason for continuing the regular review is that we are keeping legislation that provides for it.

Until now, Parliament has had one debate every year and other debates periodically on whether to keep emergency and special powers under existing terrorist legislation. I shall discuss later something that we debated on Second Reading--whether the Bill as a whole should be reviewed periodically. Ministers were positive that they had not excluded from their minds the possibility that Parliament would be given the opportunity to review the Bill as a whole. I raise two other points. First, one of the benefits of having the proposals periodically return to Parliament--the amendment suggests an annual review--is that Parliament would have the opportunity to consider Northern Ireland matters and to be updated by Ministers. Broad, democratic debate would not be hidden away.

Secondly, although security matters are not devolved to the Northern Ireland Assembly, the members of the Assembly clearly have the greatest personal and local interest and are the ideal people to express a view. I do not pretend that they should have the final say, but the amendment suggests that they should be able to express their view. They should be permitted to contribute to the conclusion that emergency powers should continue, even if others make the final decision. If the Assembly says no to the continuation of emergency powers, those powers clearly should not automatically continue.

The amendment gives us the opportunity to consider whether the Northern Ireland Assembly should have a role in the discussion of such matters, together with Parliament. It could allow the Bill to be a marker between now and the time when Westminster ceases to legislate for Northern Ireland.

Mr. Ingram: Amendment No. 22 amends part V of the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 and puts that into force from 15 June. Amendment No. 23 is a substantive amendment that has two main purposes. First, it confers on the Secretary of State a power to impose a condition when granting a certificate for the provision of security services. Secondly, it introduces a new avenue of appeal to the tribunal established under the Northern Ireland Act 1998.

Part V of the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 prevents paramilitary groups from profiting from businesses operating in the private security industry. At present, the Secretary of State has a choice between granting a certificate and not granting or revoking it. The inclusion of the power to impose a condition on the granting of a certificate would permit a much more proportionate response to applications. That is one of the underlying purposes of the amendment.

Decisions not to grant or to revoke a certificate are likely to be based on sensitive intelligence information. With the only current avenue for challenge being by judicial review, it is difficult to make such decisions when, because of the sensitivity of these issues, they cannot be defended in open court. Access to the Northern Ireland Act Tribunal, which is set out in the provisions, will make the position fairer by giving a right of appeal and making it possible to apply the Act far more robustly in the knowledge that the intelligence information used can be scrutinised while still being protected. A further important consideration is that the introduction of such an appeals procedure is necessary to comply with the European Court of Human Rights. The amendments will continue in force until they are superseded by new schedule 2.

Before turning to the new schedule, I shall explain briefly the purpose of the related Government amendments Nos. 18 to 21, 24 and 25 and new clause 5. These are all minor and consequential provisions. Amendments Nos. 18 and 19 will ensure that proceedings relating to offences under the new schedule do not require the consent of the Director of Public Prosecutions in common with similar provisions elsewhere in part VII. Amendment No. 20 provides that an order under the new schedule to vary the duration of a licence shall be laid before Parliament. Amendment No. 21 provides that the provisions relating to the private security industry apply in Northern Ireland only. Government amendments Nos. 24 and 25 add certain offences under the private security industry provisions to the list of scheduled offences in schedule 8. New clause 5 is needed to give effect to new schedule 2.

The new schedule, headed "Private Security Services", re-enacts part V of the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996, including the amendments made to that Act by the amendment to schedule 1, to which amendment No. 23 refers. New schedule 2 provides for the advertisement, offer or provision of unlicensed security services to remain offences. It provides for the application for a licence, and for the issue, duration and, if necessary, revocation of that licence. It also provides the avenue of appeal, under amendment No. 23, referring to the Northern Ireland Act Tribunal. It requires the providers of security services to give notice within 14 days of any changes in their personnel since their licence was last granted and it allows a constable to enter premises and examine records of persons employed in the provision of security services. It also provides for the liability of officers of bodies corporate and requires all notice to be given in writing.

A comparison with part V of the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 will show only changes of substance relating to the imposition of conditions and the appeals mechanism. Some of the language has changed, but the draftsmen have made a conscious effort to make the legislation more user-friendly. I hope that my explanation has been user-friendly.
