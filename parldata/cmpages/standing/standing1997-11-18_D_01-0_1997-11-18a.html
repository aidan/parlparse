Clause 1

Court of Directors

 

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 10, at end insert

    `subject to the confirmation of those appointments by the Treasury Select Committee of the House of Commons.'

This modest amendment will improve the Bill and the future operations of the Bank of England. If the amendment is accepted, the composition of the court of directors will have the overtly expressed confidence not just of the Chancellor but of the House, which will have an important influence on the composition of the court.

The court is one of those rather mysterious British bodies. Its importance is acknowledged but few people know either its compostion or what exactly it does. I am more interested in what it will do in the future than what it did in the past.

In a memorandum to the Select Committee on the Treasury, Lord Kingsdown stressed that the court's role is vital. He said:

    ``The importance of Court in the management of the Bank should not be underestimated.''

He goes on to say:

    ``criticism in Court is taken very seriously, and one of the most powerful disciplines on the Bank's executive is the need to secure Court's agreement to major policy changes.''

The Bill builds on that and it is clear that the court of directors has a most important role to play, not just because the Monetary Policy Committee will in part be accountable to it--we shall hear more about that when we get on to later sections of the Bill--but because it will have an overview of the performance of the Bank in general. That is well set out in the report of the Select Committee on the Treasury.

That report notes that the court of directors will be responsible for ensuring that the resources of the Bank are properly deployed and that it will also have an explicit role in

    ``setting the strategic direction of the Bank''.

That quote comes from the Chancellor himself. The court will also act

    ``as a source of advice for the Governor on business and market conditions''.

It will therefore be an intelligence gathering body too. We understand that the Chancellor particularly wants the court to pay attention to regional matters, to the economic performance of other parts of the United Kingdom, and so to filter, scrutinise and monitor sectoral information which will be useful for the formation of monetary policy.
