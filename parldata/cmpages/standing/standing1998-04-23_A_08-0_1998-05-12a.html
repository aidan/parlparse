National Lottery Bill [Lords]

10.30 am

The Chairman: Members of the Committee may remove their jackets if they wish to do so.

New Clause 4

Overriding duties of the Secretary of State and the Director General

    ‘.— (1) The 1993 Act shall be amended as follows—

    (2) In section 4(2) Overriding duties of the Secretary of State and the Director General)—

    (a) the words “do his best to” shall be omitted; and

    (b) for the words “that the net proceeds of the National Lottery are as great as possible” there shall be substituted “the selection of a not-for-profit operator.”.’.—[Mr. Spring.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Richard Spring (West Suffolk): I beg to move, that the clause be read a Second Time.

Rarely, can there have been such times as now in the history of democratic society, when manifesto commitments have come under so much scrutiny within 12 months of a new Government coming into office. We saw an example of that last night when assurances that had been given on the future of community pharmacies were overturned. Considerable gaps have emerged between the rhetoric that was given about education and the national health service before the election and during the election campaign and what has happened subsequently and those gaps are being filled by the raid on the lottery.

During the past few weeks, we have had the incredible sight of Labour Members voting repeatedly against the manifesto upon which they were elected—rarely more so than under the Bill that we are discussing. We were told before the election, for example, that the New Opportunities Fund would be set up only after the money in the millenium fund had been used up. When we saw that great sticky finger arriving from the Secretary of State to raid the national lottery, it was along the lines of that wonderful advertisement for the lottery, “It’s for you.”

Let us consider the setting up of the National Endowment for Science, Technology and the Arts. Hon. Members welcome in principle the objectives of that organisation, which would have been a national trust for talent. However, what has emerged is nothing short of a Government-sponsored quango, with increased powers for the Secretary of State running unremittingly through the Bill. NESTA will not provide a basis for developing talent; it will be a mongrel involving neither a charity nor a venture capitalist. However, even at this late stage in our proceedings, perhaps Labour Members can be persuaded to honour a manifesto commitment, which is what new clause 4 is all about. Given the experience of the past few weeks, I cannot say that my hopes are high, but the heavens always rejoice at the conversion of a sinner and perhaps I can entice Labour Members to consider the basis on which they were elected and to act accordingly.

The success of the national lottery is due to the fact that the public have confidence in it and because it was attractively packaged, designed and sold. At the heart of its almost unique success is the profit motive. As in all spheres of national life, it has acted as a dynamic for innovation, for progress and for thinking up new and attractive ideas for selling a product to the consumer. That motive, which has been at the heart of the relationship between the Government and Camelot, has had the net effect of producing substantial sums of money for good causes. I shall not rehearse what those causes are, but they are traditional, under-resourced areas of our national life, including sports, arts and heritage. Recent innovations such as the mid-week draw continue to make the national lottery appealing and successful.

If we compare the remarkable success of the national lottery with lotteries in other countries, we can take some pride in it. We believe that the lottery should not be organised and administered by the Government; rather, its success depends on the underlying profit motive. Indeed, the fact that so many countries have sought advice from Camelot or wanted a relationship with it bears out that point extremely well.

In giving the Government the chance to accept new Clause 4, we are giving them the chance to honour their pre-election pledge. If they reject it, they will be admitting that they were wrong before the election to promise to appoint a not-for-profit operator. If the Government admit that they were wrong, they will have underlined the reason for the lottery’s success—the profit motive—which has resulted in the maximum amount of money going to the good causes so valued by communities throughout the country. The Government would be wrong because a not-for-profit operator could cut the amount of money going to good causes—that is certainly the experience in other countries—and an increased proportion of revenue could be allocated to costs. By any international comparison, the national lottery is presently run on a very low-cost basis, as we saw during the original bidding process.

Before the election, it was attractive for Labour to sing a particular line on their view of the lottery—and of profit, pay and businesses. They did it successfully in political terms, but it was treated quite disingenuously as a populist issue. In retrospect, it was a pathetic way of getting the tabloid press excited on that subject and looking at Camelot in a particularly negative way. The truly over-the-top way in which the Labour party in opposition sought to exploit those issues was totally to their discredit.

New clause 4 would give effect to Labour’s much-hyped and exaggerated promise, made in opposition, that a not-for-profit operator would replace Camelot after the expiry of its seven-year licence in 2001. That vein was richly tapped before the general election. It underlined the present Government’s exploitation of people’s distrust in the profit motive, yet it was the engine of success for the national lottery. Tabloid hysteria about the salaries of Camelot directors was ruthlessley expoloited by the Secretary of State in such a way as to cause maximum political advantage to himself, but it backfired.

Mr. Ivor Caplin (Hove): Will the hon. Gentleman confirm that he wholeheartedly supported the salaries that Camelot directors took out of the national lottery?

Mr. Spring: Once again, the hon. Gentleman so completely and profoundly misunderstands the arrangements within Camelot and the national lottery that his question is hardly worth answering. It is entirely a matter for Camelot if it wishes to pay bonuses of X or Y out of its profits. Whether it was a good or sensible business decision is beside the point. The question that I hope the hon. Gentleman will seek to address, is that pre-election hype on a not-for-profit lottery, which has now flip-flopped around and which created much political excitement on the way, was totally unprincipled.

Mr. David Pior (North Norfolk): Does my hon. Friend agree that that question from the hon. Member for Hove (Mr. Caplin) gives the game away? Behind it lies the assumption that somehow the Secretary of State knows what level of bonuses, wages or salaries should be paid. Labour Members cannot bear to leave the lottery to be run at arm’s length.

Mr. Spring: My hon. Friend is absolutely right. What the Secretary of State knows about the private sector could be written on the back of a postage stamp. Underlying the whole Bill is the Government’s desire to centralise and to accrete powers to the Secretary of State. Ultimately, that will be damaging because the Bill may undermine public confidence in the lottery and reduce the amount of money going to good causes.

The Labour party manifesto commitment demonstrates a clear understanding that there is a conflict between maximising the return to the good causes and providing the operator with the motive to make a profit. “new Labour because Britain deserves better”, published in April 1997, states:

    “Because the lottery is a monopoly intended to serve the public interest, it must be administered efficiently and economically. When the current contract runs out, Labour will seek an efficient not-for-profit operator to ensure that the maximum sums go to good causes.”

The Government have been flip-flopping around in an unprincipled way.

The best way to ensure that the lottery serves the public interest is to ensure that the operator has the incentive to raise as much revenue as possible—that will enhance its own profit, but money will of course also flow back to the good causes, which we are so anxious to support. That guarantees that the good causes will be supported to the maximum extent. The very process of Camelot seeking to maximise its own profit, which is only about 1 per cent. of turnover—remarkably low by international standards—has maximised the return to the good causes. The projections of Camelot and the Government when the National Lottery, etc, Act 1993 brought the lottery into being underestimated the amount of money that would be raised for good causes by between £1 billion and £1.5 billion. The dynamic of the profit motive has been the engine pushing the process to such success.

In its report on the process by which the licence was awarded, published on 7 July 1995, the National Audit Office endorsed the view that the best way to ensure the maximum return for the good causes—the common objective of all hon. Members—is to provide the operator with an incentive to generate as much revenue as possible from the lottery. It said:

    “The National Audit Office’s main conclusions were that the evaluation process was comprehensive, consistent, logical and properly controlled; that throughout the process the Director General acted in accordance with the statutory duties placed upon him; that following the competition the licence was awarded to the applicant who was shown by the Director General’s evaluation to offer the highest return to the Distribution Fund; and that the key commitments offered in the successful application were incorporated in the Section 5 licence issued.”

The report further states that any unexpected success for the lottery—we have talked about the global sums involved—would result in an increasing proportion of the proceeds being paid to the very good causes that we are all so anxious to support.

The National Audit Office found that

    “The way in which applicants were required to structure their applications effectively guaranteed that, as sales increased, an increasing proportion of the revenue generated accrued to the Distribution Fund, thus ensuring the fund benefited from any unexpected success in the Lottery.”

The Governments own analysis of the responses to the White Paper also demonstrated the absurdity of Labour’s commitment to appointing a not-for-profit operator on the expiry of Camelot’s licence in 2001. That analysis, prepared for the Department in December 1997, states:

    “Only 71 respondents commented on this chapter . . . These figures demonstrate the very low response to that part of the White Paper. Many more respondents ignored this chapter than any other. It was noticeable that even of those who alluded to it, it was likely that they would merely suggest that the question of who operated the Lottery and how was not appropriate to them. The others suggested that it was irrelevant as long as the most money possible went to the good causes.”

Sadly, under the bill, those good causes will be robbed of a substantial amount of money to pay for the Government’s own political commitments.

10.45 am

The Labour party should have needed no further advice on its dogmatic pursuit of a not-for-profit operator, despite the watering down of a firm commitment to appoint a not-for-profit operator, to a commitment to “seek”one. Labour’s original intention clearly was to appoint a not-for-profit operator, as stated in its draft manifesto of April 1997, which states:

    “The organisation itself”—

referring to the lottery—

    “as a monopoly”—

what a wonderful word Labour keeps using—

    “should be run efficiently, but not for profit.”

On Second Reading in the other place, Lord McIntosh of Haringey claimed that the Government were not abandoning their commitment to a not-for-profit operator, when he said:

    “The Bill does not explicitly mention a not-for-profit operator, but I assure the House that it has always been and still remains our intention to seek a not-for-profit operator, if such an operator will ensure the best possible return to the good causes. There is no need to change the legislation to make this happen; the existing framework allows a non-profit operator to bid. The Director General of Oflot and the Culture Secretary will be doing all that they legitimately can to encourage such applicants, but the key criterion for award of the licence is the money raised for the good causes, which is as it should be.”—(Official Report, House of Lords, 18 December 1997; Volume. 584, c. 731.)

The commitment that he gave was meaningless in the context of maximising the return for good causes.

Unless the Government choose to exclude profit-making concerns from the bidding, when the licence is to be renewed, they will have gone back on their commitment and, in all probability, a profit-making concern will win the bid. The Government have been doing some wonderful flip-flopping. After all the political capital in the run-up to the general election, including attacks on the profit motive and the efforts to embarrass Camelot by exaggerated hyperbole, a shift has taken place as reality has begun to dawn. Ultimately, the Government are not prepared to bite the bullet. That ambivalence to profit, as opposed to the outright rejection of a profit-making operator, has come with the responsibility of Government and the realisation of the need to get as much revenue as possible, especially as the clear objective of the Bill is to rob the existing good causes and to put the money into Government-sponsored and Government-supported programmes for entirely political reasons.

The Government’s commitment to their pledge is now so feeble that they even considered that there was no need to mention it in the Bill. On Second Reading, the Secretary of State said:

    “Before Opposition Members do so, let me draw attention to something that is not in the Bill. Look as hard as one will, one will not find any mention of a not-for-profit operator; nor will one find any mention for a for-profit operator. We said in our manifesto that we would seek a not-for-profit operator for the next licence, and we remain committed to that objective.

    There is no need to change the existing legislation to bring that about. We can achieve our aim entirely within the National Lottery etc. Act 1993. Let me stress that, in seeking a not-for-profit operator and assessing any other proposals, the National Lottery Commission will have one overriding aim: to ensure that the lottery delivers the best possible return for the good causes. I remain committed to our manifesto pledge, and that will be clear when we advertise the next operator’s licence.”—[Official Report, 7 April 1998; Vol. 310, c. 164.]

That is precisely the sort of sophistry or self-delusion that we nave come to expect from the flip-flopping adventures of the Secretary of State. The right hon. Gentleman says that there will be no reduction in money for good causes. We know how worried the distribution bodies are about that. We have already discussed the flagrant breaches of the additionality and the arm’s length principle that are inherent in the Bill. We have not had a satisfactory response. The right hon. Gentleman now claims that the Government hold firm to their pledge to appoint a not-for-profit operator. I do not believe that.

Both in opposition and in government, Labour Members have consistently delivered a series of weasel words, reacting in the way that is most politically advantageous at the time. When the history of this Government is written, detailing how they sought to exploit the national lottery for political advantage both before the election and in this Bill, they will be condemned by all who favour supporting under-resourced elements—the existing good causes.

If the Government are not prepared to enshrine in the Bill the not-for-profit principle, it is unlikely that there will be not-for-profit bids. What a contrast the Secretary of State’s words are to the clear pledge that the Labour party made in opposition, in the policy document produced by the Labour policy committee, of which the Minister for Arts was a member. That document states:

    “At the end of the current seven-year contract, the appointment of an efficient ‘not-for-profit’ organisation to run the lottery would have the benefit of retaining long-term public support. Alternatively, an effective cap on profits should be considered if a competitive not-for-profit organisation cannot be found”.

A total misunderstanding of the dynamics of the free-enterprise system lurks in the Minister’s soul. If profits are too high because people organise themselves effectively, to the advantage of good causes, the suggestion is to cap them. Such flawed involvement in the economy led us down the siren route of the 1970s to so much trouble. As he and other Labour Members demonstrated in that Labour policy document, an attack on the profit motive underlies his search for a not-for-profit operator.

No doubt the Minister will rise in due course to acknowledge the Government’s error in not including the provision in the Bill and to accept the new clause. Throughout the formulation of the Bill, before the election, during the election and now, the Government’s action on manifesto commitments and policy documents and their response to the plethora of arguments surrounding the national lottery have changed and are changing. Unless people are rooted in some sort of principle, history will judge them badly. The clause fleshes out the Government’s inconsistency on the matter and underlines how the enormously successful national lottery will be undermined.

When producing and supporting legislation, hon. Members should always be mindful of the need to consider the extend of ministerial powers and to ensure that the arm’s length principle is never undermined to this extent. This is an open door for future Governments to raid the lottery for party political reasons such as are already evident in the Bill.

Manifesto commitments should be taken seriously. Only in the most extreme circumstances should they be overturned. The thread running through the Bill is that Labour Members were, quite dishonourably, prepared to support one view before the election, which they are now rejecting. The new clause gives them the opportunity to square their consciences at the eleventh hour.

 

Mr. Robert Maclennan (Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross): The reality behind the rhetoric of the hon. Member for West Suffolk (Mr. Spring) is that substantially more unites the official Opposition and the Government than he would allow. Despite his talk of sophistry, of the desire to centralise, of the abandonment of the arm’s-length principle and of setting the principle of additionality at nought, the truth is that the Opposition set in train those processes when they established the lottery.

The lottery was a major centralisation of gambling, and it drew such matters into the hands of a monopoly. The hon. Member for West Suffolk used the word “monopoly” as though it were peculiarly inapposite, but the lottery is a state monopoly, which has been permitted to draw into its hands millions of pounds.

Mr. Prior: Does the right hon. Gentleman agree that the New Opportunities Fund will be more closely controlled by the Secretary of State than the previous distributing bodies were?

Mr. Maclennan: I will come to that point in a minute. The point that I am making is that from the beginning, lack of centralisation, the arm’s-length principle and additonality were honoured more in the breach than the observance. The reality is that from the moment the lottery was introduced, the principle of additionality was breached; the budget of the Department that is now called the Department of Culture, Media and Sport took a sharp downturn as soon as the lottery was set up.

The lottery has been used as a reason to diminish the taxpayer’s direct input through the Department’s vote, but the official Opposition have never adequately admitted that fact. Of course the Government have accepted both that fact and the outgoing Chancellor’s budgetary proposals in determining the Department’s expenditure. Money that had been made available for the arts, sports and heritage through a vote of Parliament to the then Department of National Heritage was replaced by the lottery. That specifically deals with point made by the hon. Member for North Norfolk (Mr. Prior).

In setting up the Millennium Commission—a major distributing body—they decided that it should not be independent, that the Secretary of State should be its chairman and that it should be dominated by the then Government’s political priorities, which are being carried on in the plans for the millennium celebration at Greenwich. Mr. Spring: The right hon. Gentleman makes a perfectly fair point about the Millennium Commission, but that represents only a fifth of the picture. The right hon. Gentleman is putting a gloss on the matter as if the whole package of the original Act were centralising. That is not entirely correct.

Mr. Maclennan: It is the hon. Gentleman who has made statements about the unimpeachable principle of additionality. It is he who has treated the arm’s-length principle as unassailable. Frankly, that principle has been more honoured in the breach than the observance by successive Governments.

Mr. Maclennan: The hon. Gentleman spoke for about 25 minutes when developing his views. I have not yet finished the exposition of my position. If I may say so, I hope not to take as long to do so as he did.

My personal view is that the time has come to recognise that there is not a great deal to be said for the arm’s-length principle. The arts, in particular, would be favoured by the recognition that Ministers should be accountable for the arts flourishing or dying, and that they should be prepared openly to answer charges that money has been misspent or misdirected or has not been forthcoming in amounts appropriate to sustain what the country perceives to be the needs of the arts. The time has come to abandon the arm’s-length notion.

I believe that that principle was first developed in the thinking of that great Liberal, John Maynard Keynes, when the Arts Council was established with him as its chairman at the end of the second world war. It was entirely understandable that the arm’s-length principle should have been espoused in the post-fascist, post-Marxist era, when Governments abroad had used the arts for the most improper purposes, and when it was feared that to give Governments power over the arts would be to run the risk of the power being abused. Those circumstances have long since gone.

The arm’s-length principle has been invoked by Government—notably by the previous Government—as an alibi to cover up the lack of public support for the arts and to allow them to say that the arts are looked after by the various distributing funds. I find the whole thrust of the hon. Gentleman’s attack on the Government’s position to be misconceived.

Mr. Prior: In the arts, does the hon. Gentleman see any distinction between tax revenues and lottery proceeds?

Mr. Maclennan: I see a substantial distinction. Tax revenues are a required payment, but lottery revenues are voluntarily given. The lottery’s ability to raise the funds comes about because of a decision taken by the Government and by Parliament. However, in the process of establishing such a great monopoly, some significant commercial interests were given a great deal of money.

I go thus far with the hon. Member for West Suffolk: a certain amount of humbug was heard from the Government about a not-for-profit operator. Impressions were given prior to the election that, for better or worse, seem to have served their purpose and are unlikely to be met in the near future. Those impressions were given because of the widespread feeling that it would be difficult to justify and against the public interest to give such huge sums of money to the lottery operators. That was particularly so as Mr. Richard Branson has proposed a not-for-profit operation, which would be substantially more in the public interest, and that the benefits being reaped by the consortium of companies that put together the successful bid might be better directed towards the good causes.

I do not think that Richard Branson entirely satisfied the scrutineers of the lottery bids that he was able to do that. In the event, however, the successful companies have distributed a lot of money—not only in bonuses to the well-salaried operators, but in the form of dividends. If one were to do a spot of arithmetic to determine how much money had been distributed to the shareholders as a result of their success in winning the management of the national lottery, one would run up a pretty big bill. It is therefore reasonable to ask whether the lottery could, at an appropriate time, be run for the public interest by a not-for-profit organisation.

I do not take a dogmatic view about that, but it is certainly worth considering because many of the initial uncertainties have disappeared. Many of the modes of operation are now well understood. A subsequent operator may take a great deal less risk than the entrepreneurs who put forward the successful initial proposals. Whatever the position may be today, and however justified it may be for the companies operating Camelot and the national lottery to reap their rewards for their risk—in so far as there are risks—that position will not obtain when the franchise runs out. I hope that the Government will not entirely resile from their position, which was struck more with an eye to how it might affect the public’s voting intentions than with the firm intention to put right what was conceived to be a mistake.

We are considering whether the private sector is best suited to run a public monopoly. Although the profit motive undoubtedly caused the companies to bid, the capacity to operate a project efficiently and effectively does not automatically, under the law of Medes and Persians, as it were, depend on the profit motive.

The wish to serve motivates rich people. I have been struck by how many of them have established highly efficient trust funds with the same purpose as that of the good causes, although they are on a smaller scale. Those people want their money properly distributed and directed. The efficiency of the distribution of such funds, which can run to millions of pounds per annum, depends not on the profit motive but on the motivation of those involved in the process. Profit is not an absolute sine qua non for the success of the management of the lottery.

Furthermore, the profit motive has not been the dominant driving force behind the Foundation for Sport and the Arts. That organisation, which is funded by pools companies, is notably successful. It has been run on a shoestring, and at the beginning distributed £60 million a year without a great bureaucratic centralising hierarchy. If the foundation can operate without the profit motive, I do not see why a lottery operator could not operate similarly. The expertise of the Foundation of Sport and the Arts is germane to the Bill, and I hope that its place in the sun will be secured by the Government’s action. It has shown how a great deal of money from the betting industry can be successfully, efficiently and effectively channelled into good causes. It has blazed a trail that the national lottery has followed.

It was a slightly cynical exercise for Conservative Members to table a new clause that their hearts were not in and to use it simply as a means of twisting the Government’s tail. However, I suppose that those in opposition act that way, and we learn to live with such devices for stimulating debate. I should like to know whether the hon. Member for West Suffolk will force a Division on the new clause. It would be interesting to have the Opposition’s position on the record, but I suspect that they will not go that far.

It is plain that the Government do not require the new clause and that the Minister will give the repeated reassurance that they could move along the lines of the 1993 Act. We shall see in due course whether they will do that, especially given the new provisions for advising the commission on the choice of the subsequent franchise holder. I do not know whether they will take an oath of fealty to the cause of the not-for-profit operators in appointing advisers—the panel—to the new commission. I rather doubt it. It seems that the prospects of a not-for-profit operator being selected are dwindling.

New schedule 1

Replacement of Director General by Commission: supplementary provisions

    Part I

    Transfers etc

    Interpretation

    1. In this part of this Schedule—

    “the appointed day” means the day on which subsection (4) of the principal section comes into force under section 26(2A) of this act:

    “the Commission” means the National Lottery Commission;

    “the Director General” means the Director General of the National Lottery;

    “instrument” includes orders, rules, regulations, schemes, licenses, agreements and other documents;

    “the principal section” (Replacement of Director General by National Lottery Commission) of this Act.

    Transfer of property, rights and liabilities

    2. On the appointed day, there shall by virtue of this paragraph be transferred to the Commission any property, rights or liabilities to which, immediately before that day, the Director General was entitled or subject in connection with any functions transferred to the Commission by the principal section.

    Transfer of Staff

    3.—(1) This paragraph applies to any person who, immediately before the appointed day, is employed by virtue of paragraph 3 of Schedule 2 to the 1993 Act.

    (2) Any contract of employment under which a person to whom this paragraph applies is so employed shall have effect as from the appointed day as if it had originally been made between him and the Commission.

    (3) without prejudice to sub-paragraph (2) above—

    (a) all the rights, powers, duties and liabilities of the employer under or in connection with a contract to which that sub-paragraph applies shall by virtue of that sub-paragraph be transferred to the Commission on the appointed day, and

    (b) anything done before that day by or in relation to the employer in respect of that contract or the employee shall be deemed from that day to have been done by or in relation to the Commission.

    (4) Sub-paragraphs (2) and (3) above are without prejudice to any right of an employee to terminate his contract of employment if his working conditions are changed substantially to his detriment; but such a change shall not be taken to have occurred by reason only of the change in employer effected by sub-paragraph (2) above.

    (5) In sub-paragraph (4) above, the reference to an employee’s working conditions includes a reference to rights (whether accrued or contingent) under any pension or superannuation scheme of which he was a member by virtue of his employment immediately before the appointed day.

    Construction of references to the Director General

    4. Any enactment or instrument passed or made before the appointed day shall have effect, so far as necessary for the purposes of, or in consequence of, the transfers effected by the principal section and paragraphs 2 and 3 above, as if any reference to the Director General were a reference to the Commission.

    Continuing validity of past acts

    5.—(1) Nothing in any other provision of the principal section or this Schedule shall affect the validity of anything done by or in relation to the Director General before the coming into force to that provision.

    (2) Anything (including legal proceedings) which immediately before the appointed day is in the process of being done by or in relation to the Director General may, if it relates to any of the functions, property, rights or liabilities transferred by the principal section or paragraph 2 or 3 above, be continued by or in relation to the Commission.

    (3) Any approval, authorisation, consent, delegation, direction, licence or appointment given, granted or made or other thing whatever done by the Director General for the purposes of any of the functions transferred by the principal section shall, if in force immediately before the appointed day, continue in force and have effect as if similarly given, granted, made or done by the Commission.

    Final report and accounts of the Director General

    6.—(1) As respects any time on or after the appointed day, the duties imposed on the Director General in relation to—

    (a) accounts, and

    (b) the making of a report under section 14 of the 1993 Act,

    in respect of his last financial year, or any preceding financial year for which those duties have not been discharged, shall be discharged by the Commission.

    (2) Any reference in this paragraph to the Director General’s last financial year is a reference to the financial year beginning with the 1st April last preceding the appointed day.

    (3) The duties imposed on the Commission by sub-paragraph (1) above shall be discharged by them as if the Director General’s last financial year ended with the day preceding the appointed day (if it would not in fact do so).

    (4) The property transferred to the Commission by virtue of paragraph 2 above shall include the records of the Director General.

    (5) For the purposes of this paragraph, the amendments of section 14 of the 1993 Act made by paragraph 13 below shall be disregarded.

    (6) In this paragraph “financial year” means the twelve months beginning with 1st April in any year and ending with the 31st March next following.

    Part II

    Constitution of the Commission

    7. After schedule 2 to the 1993 Act there shall be inserted—

    “Schedule 2A

    The National Lottery Commission

    Status and capacity

    1.—(1) The Commission shall not be regarded as the servant or agent of the Crown or as enjoying any status, immunity or privilege of the Crown.

    (2) The members and employees of the Commission shall not be regarded as civil servants and the Commission’s property shall not be regarded as property of, or held on behalf of, the Crown.

    (3) The Commission may for the purpose of enabling them to exercise their functions acquire and dispose of land.

    (4) It shall be within the capacity of the Commission as a body corporate created by statute do such things and enter into such transactions as are incidental or conducive to the discharge of their functions under this Act

    Membership

    2.—(1) The Commission shall consist of five members, all of whom shall be appointed by the Secretary of State.

    (2) Before appointing a person to be a member, the Secretary of State shall satisfy himself that that person will have no such financial or other interest as is likely to affect prejudicially the discharge by him of his functions as a member.

    (3) The Secretary of State shall also satisfy himself from time to time with respect to every member that he has no such interest as is mentioned in sub-paragraph (2).

    (4) Any person who is, or whom the Secretary of State proposes to appoint to be, a member shall, whenever requested by the Secretary of State to do so, furnish him with such information as the Secretary of State considers necessary for the performance by him of his duties under sub-paragraphs (2) and (3).

    Tenure of office

    3.—(1) Members shall hold and vacate office in accordance with their terms of appointment, subject to the following provisions.

    (2) Any appointment of a member shall be for a term no longer than five years.

    (3) A member may resign his membership by giving written notice to the Secretary of State.

    (4) A person who ceases to be a member shall be eligible for re-appointment.

    (5) The Secretary of State may by notice in writing to the member concerned remove from office a member who—

    (a) has been absent from three or more consecutive meetings of the Commission without their prior approval,

    (b) has become bankrupt, has made an arrangement with his creditors, has had his estate sequestrated, has granted a trust deed for his creditors or has made a composition contract with his creditors, or

    (c) is, in the opinion of the Secretary of State, unable or unfit to perform his duties as a member.

    Chairman

    4.—(1) The members of the Commission shall select one of their number to be the Chairman of the Commission.

    (2) A person’s term of office as chairman shall be no longer than twelve months.

    (3) A person may resign as chairman by giving written notice to the other members.

    (4) If the chairman ceases to be a member he shall also cease to be chairman.

    (5) If a member who ceases to be the chairman shall again become eligible for selection as chairman after the expiration of a period equal to that for which he was chairman.

    Remuneration and allowances

    5.—(1) The Commission may pay such renumeration, and such travelling and other allowances, to a member of the Commission as the Secretary of State may determine in the case of the member.

    (2) Where the Secretary of State so determines in the case of a member of the commission, the Commission shall—

    (a) pay to or in respect of him such pension, allowances or gratuities, or

    (b) make such payments towards the provision of a pension, allowances or gratuities to or in respect of him,

    as the Secretary of State may determine.

    (3) If the Secretary of State determines that there are special circumstances that make it right for a person ceasing to be a member of the Commission to receive compensation, the commission may pay to him such compensation as the Secretary of State may determine.

    Staff

    6.—(1) There shall be a Chief Executive of the Commission, who shall be appointed by the Commission as an employee of theirs.

    (2) The Chief Executive shall be responsible to the commission for the general exercise of the Commission’s functions.

    (3) Subject to any directions given to them by the Secretary of State with respect to the number of persons who may be employed by the Commission, the Commission may appoint such other employees as they think fit.

    (4) Subject to any directions given to the Commission by the Secretary of State, the Chief Executive and any other employees shall be employed on such terms and conditions (including terms and conditions as to remuneration) as the Commission thinks fit.

    (5) Services as an employee of the commission shall be included in the kinds of employment to which a scheme under section 1 of the Superannuation Act 1972 can apply.

    (6) Accordingly, in the Superannuation Act 1972, in Schedule 1 (kinds of employment etc referred to in section 1) the following entry shall be inserted at the appropriate place among the entries under the heading”Royal commissions and other commissions”—

    “National Lottery Commission”.

    (7) The Commission shall pay to the Minister for the Civil Service, at such times as that Minister may direct, such sums as that Minister may determine in respect of the increase attributable to sub-paragraphs (5) and (6) in the sums payable out of money provided by Parliament under the Superannuation Act 1972.

    Proceedings

    7.—(1) The Commission may regulate their own procedure and that of any of their committees (and in particular may specify a quorum for meetings).

    (2) The validity of any proceedings of the Commission shall not be affected—

    (a) by any vacancy among the members or in the office of chairman, or

    (b) by any defect in the appointment or selection of any person as a member or as chairman of the Commission.

    Delegation

    8.—(1) Anything authorised or required by or under any enactment to be done by the Commission may be done—

    (a) by any member or employee of the Commission who has been authorised for the purpose, whether generally or specially, by the Commission; or

    (b) by any committee of the Commission which has been so authorised and whose membership consists of—

    (i) members of the Commission; or

    (ii) one or more members of the Commission and one or more employees of the Commission.

    (2) In exercising their functions under sub-paragraph (1), the commission shall comply with any directions given to them by the Secretary of State.

    Duty to give reasons for decisions

    9.—(1) It shall be the duty of the Commission to give to any person affected a written statement of their reasons for any decisions which they may make in the exercise of their functions under sections 5 to 10 or Schedule 3.

    (2) It shall be the duty of the Commission to arrange for the publication, in such manner as they think fit, or a written statement of their reasons—

    (a) for any decision which they may make to grant, or not to grant, to any particular applicant a licence under section 5;

    (b) for any decision which they may make to revoke a licence granted under section 5; and

    (c) for any other decision which they may make in the exercise of their functions under sections 5 to 10A or Schedule 3 and which they consider likely to be of interest to the public.

    (3) Sub-paragraphs (1) and (2) do not apply if or to the extent that the giving, or (as the case may be) the publication, of reasons would involve disclosure of information in breach of—

    (a) a restriction imposed by or under any other enactment; or

    (b) an obligation of confidence.

    Finance

    10. There shall be paid out of money provided by Parliament such sums as are necessary to defray any expenditure of the Commission.

    Accounts

    11.—(1) The Commission shall—

    (a) keep proper accounts and proper records in relation to the accounts, and

    (b) prepare a statement of accounts in respect of each financial year.

    (2) The statement shall comply with any directions that may be given by the Secretary of State as to the information to be contained in such a statement, the manner in which such information is to be presented or the methods and principles according to which such a statement is to be prepared.

    (3) Copies of the statement shall be sent to the Secretary of State and the Comptroller and Auditor General within such period after the end of the financial year to which the statement relates as the Secretary of State may direct.

    (4) The Comptroller and Auditor General shall examine, certify and report on the statement and shall lay copies of the statement and of his report before Parliament.

    (5) The Secretary of State shall not give a direction under this paragraph without the Treasury’s approval.

    (6) In this paragraph “financial year” means—-

    (a) the period beginning with the coming into force of section 3A and ending with the next 31st March, and

    (b) each successive period of twelve months ending with 31st March.

    Application of seal and evidence

    12. The application of the seal of the Commission shall be authenticated by the signature—

    (a) of any member of the Commission, or

    (b) of any other person who has been authorised by the Commision (whether generally or specially) for that purpose.

    13. A document purporting to be duly executed under the seal of the Commission, or to be signed on behalf of the Commission, shall be received in evidence and, unless the contrary is proved, taken to be so executed or signed.

    14. Paragraphs 12 and 13 do not extend to Scotland.”
