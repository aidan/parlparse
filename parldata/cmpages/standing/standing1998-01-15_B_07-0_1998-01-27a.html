Clause 12

Control of alcohol at public processions

Mr. William Ross (East Londonderry): I beg to move amendment No. 173, in page 8, line 24, leave out `in uniform'.

The Chairman: With this we may take amendment No. 162, in clause 14, page 9, line 42, leave out `in uniform'.

Mr. Ross: Amendment No. 173 is a simple, probing amendment, to inquire why it is necessary for police constables to be in uniform whenever they are carrying out their duties. There are many situations in Northern Ireland in which police officers are not in uniform, but they are still policemen, Presumably, in such cases, they could operate under other legislation. Why must a police officer be in uniform before he can take action, under the Bill, against a person who is in possession of intoxicating liquor?

Amendment No. 162 relates to clause 14, which states:

    ``A constable in uniform may arrest without warrant anyone he reasonably suspects is committing an offence under this Act.''

That goes far beyond the concept of being in possession of, or consuming, alcohol. Clause 14 deals with the wide powers that will be given to a police constable in uniform, which seem not to apply to a police constable or a police officer out of uniform, or to a plain clothes officer. Given the speed with which events can move in Northern Ireland under the influence of paramilitary or terrorist organisations, there might be occasions when it would be reasonable for a police officer, who was not in uniform, to take action under this, or other, legislation.

In the cases to which amendment No. 173 refers, it is likely that there would be a crowd scene and the officers would need to be in uniform so that they could be clearly identified. However, in the circumstances to which amendment No. 162 would apply, there would not necessarily be a crowd of people who might be prepared to attack the officers who were present. Will the Minister explain to the Committee why police officers have to be in uniform before they can take action to arrest an individual who is breaking the law?

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. Tony Worthington): I understand that this is a genuine probing amendment. The hon. Member for East Londonderry (Mr. Ross) seeks to make it possible for any police officer, including those in plain clothes, to make an arrest under the Bill.

The Government appreciate the thought behind the amendment, but we shall ask the Committee to reject the amendment. The basic reason for that is that the citizen must know that the police officer is exercising his statutory function. That is especially important in the case of clause 12, which applies to circumstances in which drink is involved. It is possible that the situation could be misinterpreted when someone is trying to confiscate drink. There have been cases in which someone who was charged with assaulting a police officer has argued in defence that the officer was in plain clothes and therefore the person did not understand what was happening.

The Government believe that a police officer should be in uniform to identify himself or herself. The hon. Member for East Londonderry will appreciate that during a parade it is likely that there would be a significant number of uniformed officers present. The Bill reflects only those provisions that exist in all other Northern Ireland legislation. I therefore ask the Committee to reject the amendment.

Mr. William Ross: I understand the Minister's point in relation to amendment No. 173, but amendment No. 162 refers to clause 14, which is not limited to the time when a procession is taking place. The Bill applies six hours before the proposed starting time of a procession. At that time plain clothes officers would be likely to be in the street keeping an eye on what was going on, and they might have to take action. I understand the Minister's remarks about amendment No. 173, but those relating to amendment No. 162 were not as strong. I hope that he will take on board my arguments, but I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Ross: Many amendments have been tabled to the clause, all of which concern the controlling of alcohol consumption, or those who are consuming it, before, during or possibly after a procession. Amendment No. 174 is needed because clause 12 gives a constable powers in relation to a person who ``is consuming'' intoxicating liquor. However, a person who has already consumed some, and is drunk and in a fighting mood, would not be covered by the clause. People who became drunk in a pub and emerged, highly intoxicated, on to the street, could escape the attentions of the police under the Bill. The Government need to consider that provision again, because otherwise it would be a defence for someone to say, ``I was not drinking in the street; I got drunk in the pub.'' He could claim that though he had a bottle of whiskey with him, it was bought for drinking at home, and that the officer had no right to require its surrender. That is a hole in the Bill.

I tabled amendment No. 175 because I am not sure that the phrase

    ``with a view to taking''

is the right way to word the provision. The Bill refers to people

    ``who have assembled with a view to taking part''

in a procession, but surely the simple phrase ``to take part'' is preferable. I see no need for stating that a relevant person is present ``with a view'' to taking part. Such a person might not be acceptable to the organisers of the procession. He might be a hanger-on who wanted to go down the street, and might be outwith the organisers' control. I am curious about whether the Minister is certain that the wording is the correct one to accomplish what I think are the Government's intentions.

Amendment No. 176 would delete from the clause subsection (2)(c), which states that subsection (1) applies to a person

    ``who--

    (i) is otherwise present at, or is in the vicinity of, a place on the route or proposed route of a public procession; and

    (ii) is in a public place, other than licensed premises.''

I am not sure whether those two elements really hang together. A person who was at the end of a procession, at the assembly point in a field or similar place, which would be a public space, but who had not taken part in the procession, might well be intoxicated without causing anyone any trouble. Although the organisers might take a dim view of folk drinking in the field on the Twelfth, Black Saturday or 17 March, it would be difficult to take action against those drinkers unless they were becoming violent.

There is a difficulty with the phrase ``in the vicinity of''. Someone may, having bought a six pack, be carrying it along the street on his way home, and someone else may have bought a couple of bottles of wine for a dinner party in the evening. Neither of them may have anything to do with the procession: they are simply going about their lawful business, yet they could be caught by the Bill. It is no good the Minister saying that they should have taken another route.

The Minister should have considered the wording more carefully so that the Bill allowed an individual to prove that he was there for an entirely different purpose and was not connected with the procession. Then we come to the term ``in a public place'', by which I understand any place to which the public may have access, pavement or otherwise. As I say, an individual could be in a field, ``a public place'', but it is not clear whether he is going to cause trouble as a result of drinking.

I have always made it clear that organisers of loyalist--and, I am sure, nationalist--processions, are opposed to drinking either on the route or in the field. Sometimes, however, common sense has to apply, and I am unsure whether someone walking in a field may be caught by this provision.

Amendment No. 156 is interesting. Six hours is a long period before the proposed starting time of the procession and I cannot see the purpose of applying a ban for such a long time. We have long had byelaws against drinking in the streets anyway, so existing law is adequate to deal with the situation. Will the Minister give us some idea of the thinking behind this provision?

A procession will usually start at about 2 pm, or perhaps noon. That would mean people being prohibited from being in the street with alcohol from 8 am. With a very late procession, that period could be as late as noon. I cannot see why the Government have concluded that a period of six hours is appropriate--that is far too long for me. It also places an additional burden on the police. I should think that a couple of hours before a procession starts would be more than sufficient. So, as I said, I would be grateful if the Minister could explain the thinking behind the provision. At present, it could annoy many folk because six hours before a procession is an entire afternoon. Many people are walking the streets on Saturday afternoons taking alcohol home with them.

I should also like to deal with another crazy situation that is created by another part of the clause. Subsection (5) states:

    ``A constable may dispose of anything surrendered to him under this section in such manner as he considers appropriate.''

So could an officer confiscate six packs, a bottle of wine or a bottle of Black Bush and take them home with him, which is what the Bill states? Or would he pour it down the drain? Most police constables take the same general view of alcohol as I do. They would normally like to take it home with them. Given that, what restrictions will be placed on the police constable who seizes a six pack or other liquor from someone coming straight out of the pub? Can he take it to the police station and have a quiet beer with his friends over sandwiches at lunchtime and can he take seized whiskey home and enjoy a nightcap? The clause is open to abuse unless police regulations or other provisions in law prohibit an officer from taking possession of alcohol for his own use. However, under the clause, once an officer grabs alcohol, it is his--and what a temptation that would place before him.

For example, a lady may be walking in the street with half a dozen bottles of quality wine that she has bought for a fancy dinner party that evening. A constable may come along and say, ``Sorry ma'am. It is six hours within a procession taking place and it is evident to me that you are about to start drinking in the street. You will get intoxicated and consequently throw stones and petrol bombs at the police. I am therefore seizing this wine.'' That police constable could then have the dinner party. That is not the Bill's intention. I am sure that the Chief Constable or any of his senior officers would not be happy with such behaviour by a constable. However, one hears so much about the police these days that anything is possible. I do not see why temptation should be put in their way.

Far be it from me to criticise the Royal Ulster Constabulary because many of my relations serve in the force. One of my favourite stories was told by my uncle, who said that his hope in life was to draw his pension for longer than he drew his pay. He is 92, so he is nearly there. He told me that once when he was in the constituency of the hon. Member for South Down the police seized some poteen and poured 10 gallons of it down a drain. People in the village thought that that was a colossal waste.

I may have been a bit facetious about the matter, but the clause is wide open to abuse. It should contain a provision that would not allow a police officer to do what he considers appropriate with seized alcohol. It should be set down that the alcohol must be handed to senior officers for disposal in a way that a court may decide. That would be a far more sensible approach than the one in the Bill. I look forward to the Minister's reply.

 

Mr. Eddie McGrady (South Down): Despite the possible humorous consequences that have been outlined by the hon. Member for East Londonderry, we are debating an important and difficult clause. All Members of the Committee agree that the abuse of alcohol leads to the abuse of people and to the abuse of the right to parade and to peaceful assembly. We can all recount anecdotes, but on many occasions an otherwise peaceful scenario has been turned into a riot because of the over-consumption of alcohol by those in a procession and by bystanders.

We can all think of humorous situations that may arise, but we must try to prevent a recurrence of incidents in which the abuse of alcohol has led to serious problems. That has been recognised by the Bill's replication of parts of the Public Order (Amendment) (Northern Ireland) Order 1997 and the Public Order (Northern Ireland) Order 1987. It is also significant that recommendation 42 of the North report states:

    ``We recommend that the provisions in Great Britain for control of alcohol being taken to sporting events be extended to Northern Ireland in respect of those travelling both to parades and open-air public meetings.''

That is important because hangers-on often cause the greatest tension. Sometimes the resultant disorder does not occur on the parade route, or even alongside it, but in streets nearby. Therefore, it is important to get the measure right.

Amendment No. 76 is a probing amendment, which attempts to strengthen the hand of the police who are in charge. I am not sure if there is a difference in law between ``surrender'' and ``confiscate''. In my view, ``to surrender'' means to give up something voluntarily. I cannot envisage many people voluntarily giving up their load of liquor in the circumstances that we are discussing. Therefore, amendment No. 76 would allow the police to confiscate alcohol. However, in legal terms, that might mean the same as surrendering alcohol.

Mr. William Ross: The term ``surrender'' implies that the person can retrieve the alcohol later. ``Confiscate'' might preclude that. That is my only objection to the amendment.

Mr. McGrady: I thank the hon. Gentleman, who always lives in the hope that if he loses his liquor, he will get it back. Perhaps someone who has legal expertise can explain whether there is a difference between ``surrender'' and ``confiscate''. The amendments are intended to clarify matters and strengthen the hand of the police.

Amendments Nos. 74 to 76 contain contextual inaccuracies, which may make them difficult to incorporate in the Bill. I apologise for that, but I would like the Minister to respond to their aims. I would be happy to withdraw amendments Nos. 74 to 77 if the Minister can clarify the police's powers, and strengthen their position, perhaps through an amendment on Report.

Mr. Worthington: We think that clause 12 provides useful powers to deal with the problems that are often associated with alcohol at processions. The amendments would make the police's job impossible.

My first comment to the hon. Member for East Londonderry will be hurtful because it is that amendment No. 174 is defective. After years in Opposition, I know what it is like to labour long to produce an amendment only to be told that it is defective. But how can someone surrender alcohol that he has already drunk? That is a difficult challenge. Alcohol that is still being consumed is covered by the Bill.

The circumstances that the hon. Member for East Londonderry described, in which someone is roaring drunk, are already covered by legislation. It is an offence to be drunk in a public place under common law in Northern Ireland. The police already have adequate powers to tackle that, so the amendment is unecessary.

The hon. Member for South Down acknowledged that amendments Nos. 74 and 75 contained problems. They would make nonsense of clause 12. If the amendments were accepted, clause 12(1) would read

    ``Where a constable in uniform reasonably suspects that a person . . . is consuming intoxicating liquor, the constable (b) to state his name and address.''

It is difficult to think of reasons why the police constable should state his name and address. There is a drafting problem there but we think that the clause as it stands is perfectly adequate to pick up the problem of bystanders and those who are associated with processions.

The hon. Member for South Down suggested in amendment No. 76 that the police would be empowered to confiscate alcohol rather than requiring a person to surrender it. It is not possible to give powers to confiscate anything whose possession is not an offence. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman is thinking of the Confiscation of Alcohol (Young Persons) Act 1997 under which the police can confiscate alcohol as the persons are under 18 and possession of alcohol is an offence for them under that Act.

The Bill deals with adults and it is not an offence for them to possess alcohol. It requires a person to surrender alcohol and if he does not he shall be guilty of an offence. It is the same thing in the end. It gives the RUC sufficient powers to deal with the problem.

Mr. McGrady: I do not quite understand the Minister. It seems that if a person refuses to surrender his alcohol the police constable has the power to arrest him on the spot and thereby indirectly confiscate the liquor of he may allow him to continue to imbibe the liquor and cause possible mayhem.

Mr. Worthington: As I understand it, if someone disobeys the order of a constable the offence is disobeying that order. The objective is thus achieved but it is the definition of ``confiscate'' that causes difficulties.

Amendment No. 77, also tabled by the hon. Member for South Down, is technically complicated. We are not sure what he intended by it but it would greatly expand the powers of the constable to require a person to surrender alcohol even at some distance from the parade. The constable would have the power to confiscate alcohol from any person even if that person was not in the vicinity of the parade but was likely to be travelling to a parade. Taking an extreme example, it would theoretically allow a constable in Londonderry to ask someone whom he thought might be travelling to the vicinity of a parade in Bangor to surrender alcohol.

It would be strange if alcohol could be confiscated from someone who was merely walking down the road in Londonderry. Amendment No. 156 concerns the requirement that the controls on alcohol would begin four hours before the start of the parade instead of six. In this case we are simply following the GB legislation on the control of alcohol carried by those on the way to sporting events, and our information is that it has worked well. The Bill is drafted in that way to ensure that controls will start early enough for people to sober up before the parade. It is important that intoxication does not inflame passions during the procession.

Clause 13

Breaking up public procession

Question proposed, That the clause, stand part of the Bill.

 

Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): I ask the Minister for clarification of the first line of clause 13, which refers to ``a person''. Does that mean only individuals or does it mean a group of persons or a body, such as a group that comes together to hold an open-air public meeting or a counter-demonstration with the intention of breaking up a legal procession? If the clause refers only to individuals, does the Public Order (Northeren Ireland) Act 1987 have the same effect? How often are arrests made under that legislation?

Does the Minister intend that the amendments to the 1987 Act, as set out in schedule 3, should apply to those open-air public meetings that are organised by a body or a group of persons with the intention of opposing a procession or a legal march?

Mr. William Ross: This is an interesting clause, not least because it is pretty much the same as the Public Order (Northern Ireland) Act 1987. As that is so, and because it is clearly intended to accomplish the same purpose, why is it in the Bill?

My reasoning applies not only to this clause. If a section of the 1987 Act which to some extent consolidates existing law is transferred to the Bill, why are not all the other bits and pieces of public order legislation moved to this Bill so that we have not just a new Bill but a consolidation measure? That might be more satisfactory, because the body of law on the issue would be in one Act. That would make matters much clearer for those who have to deal with public order. The clause is a straight lift, with some different wording, from the 1987 Act.

There are a number of interesting features in the Bill. It will provide protection to people who are taking part in a procession or going to one. It calls into question the behaviour of the police in the past few years, when processions have been violently obstructed by people who clearly had the most extensive plans to obstruct the procession and to cause mayhem across the Province. After the 1996 riots in Drumcree, the senior officer in Londonderry said that it was the most vicious rioting that he had ever seen; but there were few prosecutions after those riots. I know that I am harking back to the matter of counter-demonstrations, which was mentioned earlier, but I want the Minister to tell us, if such a provision is already on the statute book, how comprehensively it has been used against those who are violently engaged in opposing processions that have received permission to go ahead.

Only a tiny number of people have been prosecuted under the appropriate section of the 1987 Act. The Unionist, loyalist and Orange population of Northern Ireland believe that the section is pretty much a dead letter, because it is not applied, and they resent that very much. The powers exist for the police to take action against those who oppose the processions, and far too often they fail to do so.

There have been many instances in Northern Ireland recently of buses carrying people travelling to a parade being thoroughly stoned and attacked. The Minister of State may giggle, but he would not do so if his 11-year-old daughter had glass in her face because a stone was thrown through a bus window.

The Minister should know that for many years buses going to processions in Northern Ireland have been banned from using roads in some areas and they have to make lengthy detours. Dungiven town is one such place; Glenshane pass is another. The Government, or the police, say, ``That particular road is a no-go area, we cannot allow you to pass through that town because you might come under violent attack''. If such a situation occurs, we should like to know what has been done about it; clear measures should have been taken to prevent such behaviour. The police have the potential to take action against folk who obstruct processions, who attack people en route to processions and who attack people taking part in processions. However, we do not see much being done about that.

The proposed measures appear to apply to behaviour that the hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire (Mr. Moss) and I have described as counter-demonstrations to processions. However, such behaviour is not treated as though it were a counter-demonstration. Ministers have suggested that a counter-demonstation would be treated as if it were a new and different procession. There seems to be some divided thinking on the issue. Will the Minister clarify that?

There are problems when people sit in the street, or when they gather together in a mob and throw stones. There are also occasions when there are really counter-demonstrations. The legislation is in place, but it needs clarification. Will the Government explain how the police should deal with such behaviour in future?

I note that the clause seems to be far more limited than the provisions under section 3 of the Public Order (Northern Ireland) Act 1951. Will the Minister check that earlier legislation and tell the Committee whether changes have been made to it, and whether the provisions that seem to be missing from the Bill have been included in other legislation? How much of the 1951 Act still applies? If section 3 of the Act is still in place, why can it not be transferred to the Bill?

The earlier legislation is interesting; its wording is simple, to deal with the situation that existed then, compared with the complex wording used in the Bill. Consolidation of the legislation dealing with public order and public processions is overdue.

The increasing complexity of legislation shows the capacity of those of evil intent to make things more and more difficult for law-abiding citizens. Clause 13 is the one part of the Bill that clearly protects the right of people to process peaceably, as the Minister pointed out earlier in our sittings. However, that right has been allowed to become a dead letter in many places, because, as the hon. Member for South Down said, the police have to bow to the greater evil. That is the primary evil, from which all other evils spring. It is the capacity and the willingness of the terrorist organisations--and in this case, only one terrorist organisation, the IRA, is involved--to use murder and other forms of attack to stop people exercising their right to process peaceably.

Clause 13 is one of the key parts of the Bill. Will the Minister assure the Committee that a serious effort will be made over the coming months to implement controls against those whose sole intention is to cause mayhem in Northern Ireland? When there is disruption, as there was at Drumcree and a number of other places, the great and the good lament the damage that is done to Northern Ireland. But the IRA wants to do damage to Northern Ireland and it wants to be able to claim that it is an ungovernable and oppressive place. It creates the conditions and gets its spin doctors on to the job. It then presents a case that often has no relation to reality. However, it gets away with that because the law is not applied against those who are prepared to use violence to stop legal processions.

Clause 16

Interpretation

Mr. William Ross: I beg to move amendment No. 164, in page 10, leave out lines 42 to 44.

The amendment applies to the interpretation clause. It refers back to the provison on alcohol and again raises questions about

    ``any place to which . . . the public has access''.

Does that cover a hall where people may assemble? The Bill states

    ``a public place, other than licensed premises.''

That could mean a hall where people assemble, a field where a demonstration takes place, or any open-air area to which people have access. How wide is the clause's remit? I should like an answer to that before I raise one or two small points in the stand part debate.

Mr. Ingram: The amendment would alter the definition of a ``public place'' to cover only roads, whereas in the Bill the term means

    ``(a) any road within the meaning of the Roads (Northern Ireland) Order 1993; and

    (b) any place to which at the material time the public or any section of the public has access, on payment or otherwise, as of right or by virtue of express or implied permission''.

That is a standard definition and accords with much existing Northern Ireland legislation. Changing it would set a precedent.

Mr. Ingram: The hon. Gentleman is working under a misunderstanding and has misinterpreted the Bill's intention. He has read schedule 2, paragraph (9) to mean that a new tribunal will be set up under the Bill. That is not our intention. The subsection relates to the admissibility of relevant instruments to a court or any other existing tribunal. That includes the code of conduct, the procedural rules and the guidelines. The provision will make such documents admissible in a court or tribunal.

For example, it is possible that documents might be called for at an industrial relations tribunal or in a fair employment tribunal. The provision will ensure that any tribunal sitting to adjudicate on matters that relate to the codes is able to call for documents in evidence. To remove the word ``tribunal'' from the Bill would deny external tribunals the right to documents that is possessed by a court of law.

I assure the hon. Gentleman that the Bill will not set up a new tribunal. The schedule deals with tribunals that already appear in legislation. For that reason, I ask him to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. William Ross: I am grateful to the Minister for that explanation. I have a clearer understanding of what is intended. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
