Mr. Flight: The amendment reflects some quite complicated territory. I understand that the Treasury lawyers currently feel that either part of it may be unnecessary or that it seeks to achieve what we should not want it to achieve.

The amendment would insert at the end of line 27,

    ``identifying the single market directive under which each relevant activity is authorised'',

which is an attempt to provide to UK banks wanting to passport abroad greater flexibility than they have currently. The general principle is that banks can obtain their passport under the second banking directive, and non-bank investment firms under the investment services directive.

4.45 pm

The scope of the 2BCD passport is different from the scope of the ISD passport--in some respects wider, in other respects narrower. The ISD passport covers arranging deals in existing securities and receiving and transmitting orders, which the 2BCD passport does not. The amendment would provide the ISD passport, as well as the 2BCD passport, to UK banks. The ISD contemplates that it may apply to banks as well as non-bank investment firms because the investment firms to which it applies are defined as firms that carry out any of the four particular investment services referred to by the ISD as core investment services. Generally, core investment services are receiving and transmitting orders, including arranging deals in existing securities, dealing with agents, discretionary investment management and underwriting new issues. The principle is that banks providing any of those services will be treated as investment firms to which the ISD passport applies. The ISD does not want to duplicate the 2BCD passport and therefore provides that only certain of its articles, particularly those about the imposition of business conduct rules and about membership of exchanges, apply to banks. Clearly, the ISD contemplates that its passport is appropriate for banks that are authorised as investment firms--instead of being the result of the banking licence under 2BCD. That is why certain articles of the ISD are excluded with respect to banks authorised for one or more core investment services by their banking licence.

We need to make it clear that authorisation for core investment services is not extended to banks simply because they are banks: they need to apply for it separately. That can be done now because authorisation is covered by the Banking Act 1987 rather than the Financial Services Act 1986. The UK implemented a regulation covering the acquisition of the full 2BCD passport, which stated that any bank authorised under the Banking Act was ipso facto authorised to do everything covered by the 2BCD passport. That was before the ISD and was a neat way of doing it. However, if banks could be excluded from the provision on ISD core investment services, the necessary authorisation under the Financial Services Act would entitle them to the ISD passport.

I look forward to hearing the Government's response. We are not convinced that the present drafting on this territory cannot be improved.

Mr. Timms: The hon. Member for Chichester asked about the purpose of the schedule so I set it out at the outset. The aim is to give effect in UK law to rights of establishment and to provide services that extend beyond those covered by the single market directives.

A person who is established in another member state of the European Economic Area and who is authorised to carry on a regulated activity under the law of that state has a right to be authorised to carry on that activity in the other states as well.

However, that right applies only as long as the relevant law of the home state provides equivalent protection to the provisions that apply in the host state or meets minimum European Economic Area requirements that may apply in that area of law.

In cases in which the activity and the person are covered by passport directives, the mechanisms for the home state to approve the passport and the split of responsibilities between the home and host states are the same as those in the directives. However, the passport directives do not cover the full range of financial services or financial service providers that currently fall within the scope of UK regulation or might fall within it in future.

5 30pm

Schedule 4 therefore applies in cases in which a person has rights that cannot be exercised through the directives because they do not fall within one of the classes of person covered by the directives or because the regulated activity concerned does not fall within the scope of the directives.

A mortgage provider would be a good example of that, if, as a result of consultation, we decided--hypothetically--that mortgage provision should be regulated. A mortgage provider that did not finance its business by accepting deposits from the public would not be a credit institution as defined by the second banking directive.

Lending on property is an activity that is passportable under the second banking directive, but not under the investment services directive. An investment services directive investment firm that was authorised for mortgage lending in its home state might therefore have treaty rights that extended beyond its directive rights.

We must provide an equivalent mechanism to permit persons from EEA member states to exercise their treaty rights. The schedule provides for that mechanism.

It is not right to regard the schedule as waiving our rights, to borrow the phrase used by the hon. Member for Chichester. We are giving effect to the jurisprudence of the European Court in relation to circumstances in which the general good may not be invoked. Schedule 4 follows the precedent set by sections 31 and 32 of the 1986 Act, which deal with such matters.

The hon. Member for Chichester asked about paragraph 3(4), which also reflects another provision in the 1986 Act. However, no certificate has been issued or sought under that Act provision.

Schedule 4 does not seek to define the extent of treaty rights, which are the subject of extensive and developing case law in the European Economic Area. However, it sets the conditions that must be met for authorisation by such a route in terms of the general principles that I have described.

Schedule 4 requires confirmation from the appropriate home state authority of the person's home state authorisation. It requires that home state arrangements provide equivalent protection for United Kingdom consumers and provides for the Treasury to determine whether the laws of a member state afford that protection.

The schedule provides a statutory mechanism for fulfilling our obligations under the treaty but it maintains the necessary protections and safeguards for UK consumers. It has received a warm welcome from industry bodies such as the London Investment Bankers Association and I hope that it will find equal favour with the Committee.

Mr. Timms: The purpose of clause 29 is, as the hon. Gentleman said, to ensure that a partnership, or some other form of unincorporated body that is an authorised person, does not need to reapply for authorisation simply because the association has reformed as a matter of law following certain changes--for example, a change in membership. In effect, the clause reproduces the provisions of section 29 of the Financial Services Act 1986. That is not a problem for a body corporate, such as a company or building society, because it is a separate legal person from its members. The Government's view is that the definition of a firm in subsection (4) is sufficiently wide to encompass any authorised person to which clause 29 applies, and we see no need to add to that or to include references to ``other persons''. It is an approach that was adopted in section 26 of the Companies Act 1989, which addressed a similar issue and which has worked well.

There is a danger about the formulation proposed by the hon. Gentleman, as it almost certainly would be deemed to refer to an individual. We would not want that to be the case. We would want to seek authorisation afresh if a new individual came along. Gentleman is able to withdraw it.

Mr. Flight: For the sake of clarity, will the Minister confirm that the Government understand that, under clause 29, a partnership that becomes a corporate structure is not required to re-apply for authorisation? Mr. Timms: In that instance, I understand that it will need to apply afresh. It is only if the members of the partnership change, or something of that sort, that application is not necessary. If there is a change in legal status, a new application will be needed.
