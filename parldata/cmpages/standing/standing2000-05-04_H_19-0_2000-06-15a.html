I now come to a serious matter. It could turn into a quibble, but the provisions that we shall discuss later under schedule 30 lead me to worry about it slightly. I refer to line 24 on page 476. Under paragraph 4 of the schedule, the prohibition on pertaining relief applies not to tax that has been deducted, but to tax that is deductible. In other words, it applies not to tax that was shielded, but 749 750 to tax that might have been shielded. Finally, I come to a much more important matter. If we can resolve it, we will assist taxpaying companies for a long time. I do not mean to be unkind to the parliamentary draftsmen, because the drafting of a Finance Bill is an incredibly arduous task, which I certainly would not like to undertake. However, on page 477 of the Bill, under subsection (6) of the proposed new section 403D of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988, is a masterpiece of ambiguity. I am sure that it can be cleared up by a clear reference to Pepper v. Hart by the Paymaster General. That alone would justify our entire proceedings. It might be said to constitute the only justification for our proceedings to date, as I do not think that we have changed ministerial minds yet.

I shall read out the sentence in question and explain its ambiguity. It is:

    So much of the law of any territory outside the United Kingdom as for the purposes of any foreign tax makes the deductibility of any amount dependent on whether or not it is deductible for tax purposes in the United Kingdom shall be disregarded for the purposes of this section.

That is not what I would regard as the most pellucid English. More important is the systematic ambiguity. In case the Committee believes that I am imagining it, I should say that the Law Society agrees with me, or rather, I agree with it.

There are two interpretations. One is that if another country's deductibility clause makes deductions dependent on a UK non-deductibility, the whole of it should be ignored. The second possibility is that one should simply disregard the fact that an overseas country's deductibility clause makes deduction dependent on the UK's non-deductibility. Those are crucially different interpretations.

I find it extraordinarily difficult to put the matter clearly. I kept trying to, and have written it down in the form that I hope will be most pellucid. I apologise; I shall read it out. If the sentence has the first meaning, I think that things are okay, as the UK company would receive group relief in the UK if the other country allowed the same deduction but restricted it to cases for which the UK had no deductibility. All would then be well, and I think that that is the intention.

If one takes the second interpretation, which is at least equally plausible as an interpretation of the words, there is an infinite regress. A UK company cannot receive group relief here because, in the absence of any reference to deductibility in the UK, it could be deductible overseas, but the UK company cannot receive deductibility overseas because the other country has a parallel clause referring back to here and so forth. The UK company would end up without a deduction anywhere, which I cannot imagine is the intention.

Recovery of tax payable by non-resident company

Question proposed, That this schedule be the Twenty-eighth schedule to the Bill.

 

Mr. Letwin: There is a tenuous connection of ideas between schedules 27 and 28. If group relief is to be global, I dimly gesture towards the Inland Revenue's logic in supposing that it should chase up any member of the group for tax unpaid by another member of the group. I think that that is what schedule 28 is getting at. We do not object to trying to collect unpaid UK tax. It is a proper activity for the Inland Revenue to undertake. However, there are problems with the way in which the schedule approaches that task.

I do not know how those problems can be solved, but something needs to be done.

I draw the attention of the Paymaster General and the Committee to paragraph 2(3)(a) and (b) on page 483--as I am conscious of my reputation for mumbling, I shall raise my voice--which widens the definitions of associated company. It is notable that, for group relief purposes, 75 per cent. is the definitional limit, whereas 51 per cent. is adopted here. That means that the Inland Revenue may collect from UK companies that are associated with the defaulting company, even if they are only thinly associated and have an interest of only 51 per cent.

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): That is quite wrong.

Mr. Letwin: My hon. Friend said from a sedentary position that it was wrong and, emotionally, I am on his side. However, I could understand the logic of the measure if it were not for certain matters, on which I have been advised and which I hope that I have understood correctly. The Revenue could attack a company for unpaid tax if it was, for up to 12 months previously, part of the same group as a defaulting company according to the wide definition of association in the schedule. That recalls a point similar in character to the problem addressed by Government amendment No. 337 to schedule 27.

The problem is a practical one for investment bankers, lawyers and solicitors when doing due diligence in acquisition, for example. I suppose that I should declare an interest here. It is important for the working of the capital market that due diligence can be done, but it is difficult if a company can be penalised after buying in good faith another company that, 12 months before, had been loosely associated with dozens of companies, one of which had not paid UK tax. Let us imagine that a major British company buys a minor one and sends in teams of lawyers and accountants, who do all the due diligence. Six months later, officials from the Inland Revenue arrive at the door of the large, rich UK company and say, "We would like £100 million because, unbeknownst to you at the time that you bought it, a company with which it was loosely associated 12 months previously, which had no commercial impact on it and of which you have never heard, failed to pay some UK tax."

The problem is not that the entire resources of the City of London could not be mustered to discover the answer to the question, "Is there any such company owing such a sum of tax?" Those resources can be mustered but, as a result of the measure, there will be a considerable increase in the costs of due diligence, the professional indemnity required by lawyers and so on. It will jam up the works in an unpleasant way.

Mr. Flight: May I ask my hon. Friend whether what can be collected from the subsidiaries is pro rata to the ownership or 100 per cent. of the tax liability?

For the Paymaster General's edification, I refer to paragraph 2(4), lines 34 to 40. My final point is on paragraph 4(1), which stipulates a long time limit--three years after the date on which the liability of the taxpayer is finally determined, and I understand from experts that there are six years in which to determine it. Dawn Primarolo: The hon. Gentleman's final points about the proposal being too loose and too tight reflect the fact that the extension of group relief being provided has been welcomed, but the counterbalance protecting the revenue under circumstances in which it is needed has not. We have discussed several times the balance between fairness and restrictions on companies and their commercial activities that are not undue.

The hon. Gentleman raised four points, which were about the 12-month period, the figure of 51 per cent., the consortium and the three-year period. Representations have been made to the Revenue on all those points. Our response on the 12 months before the beginning of the period to which the tax relates is that there is a clear need for protection against arrangements designed to ensure that the non-paying company can leave the company while remaining under the same economic control. For example, a foreign parent could de-merge the non-payer. Alternatively, a UK-resident company with the resources to pay the tax could leave the group while remaining under the same economic control. The 12-month period represents a reasonable response, and is similar to that in existing legislation of a similar nature, such as the company migration collection powers and the company gains recovery powers.

The second point was about an inhibition on the sale of companies and partnerships. It is already common for indemnities to be taken out when companies are sold because of the existing provisions that allow tax to be recovered from another group of companies. Again, the company migration collection powers and capital gains recovery powers are relevant.

The recovery power in the clause and the schedule should not add significantly to the current need for indemnities. As we have said in response to representations from organisations and professional bodies, that is a fair balance in terms of what is being given by the extension of the relief, the protection of the revenue, and the opportunities that could be available.
