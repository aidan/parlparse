I understand from reading the clause and from the helpful explanatory notes that it addresses a situation that I can explain by sharing with the Committee a piece of doggerel, which goes:

    ``Big fleas have little fleas upon their backs to bite 'em

    Little fleas have smaller fleas and so ad infinitum.''

It relates to a situation where a worker, person A, has an employer, person B, in situ, who is in turn employed by person C. Person C is, therefore, the employer of both person B and, indirectly, person A. It is possible to create, as the doggerel does, a process of infinite regress, in which there could be a series of superior employers. They would not be superior in the sense in which Lord Curzon was once memorably described as a very superior person, but they would be different grades of employer.

The only constraint to that process of regress is subsection (1)(b), which requires that

    ``the worker is employed on the premises of that other person.''

I set that out because it is important for the Minister to say a little more about his appreciation of the scale of the situation when he explains the clause to the Committee. I suspect that the situation will apply in what I might loosely call the home worker context, although we are not talking strictly about home working. It may apply in the textile, garment and similar trades. Will the Minister say a little more about the scale of the situation with which the clause deals? In which industries will the clause apply?

It will open up a whole new legal area, injecting this statutory provision into what would have normally been a contractual dispute between an employee who has not had his wages paid and his employer.

I raise that issue in relation to this clause because there is a real risk of a genuine intermediate employer being in dispute with the superior employer. For example, an intermediate employer may undertake that work will be carried out and engages subordinate employees for that purpose. The work may be done unsatisfactorily or not at all, and the superior employer may withhold payment to the intermediate employer, who is thus unable--perhaps even unwilling--to pay his subordinate employees. Surely it is not intended that in a genuine contractual dispute, the superior employer, as a contracting party, will find himself unable to exercise his legitimate right to withhold payment under the contract if the result of such action is that the intermediate employer is thus unable to pay his employees. It would be unfair if the superior employer were then placed in jeopardy and put at risk of action under the terms of this legislation.

The issue becomes more important because of the Bill's definition of employment. Subsection (1)(a) deals with a situation in which

    ``the immediate employer of a worker is himself in the employment of some other person''.

That might appear to exclude circumstances in which the immediate employer was a labour-only subcontractor to the ultimate employer. I have a little knowledge of, and have already declared an interest in, the construction industry. It is, sadly, one of the industry's features.
