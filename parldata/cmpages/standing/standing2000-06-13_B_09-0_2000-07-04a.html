Mr. McGrady: Clause 50 is difficult and sensitive, as it deals with cultural issues. There is a sensitivity about cultural issues in Northern Ireland that is sometimes real, sometimes imaginary. It should be understood that our proposals on the name, badge and flag are not intended in any way to diminish or deny the sacrifices made by the Royal Ulster Constabulary, particularly over the past 30 years, and the suffering endured by its officers, their families and members of the community that identified with them during that period. We must acknowledge that there are people in the RUC and the wider community who see the retention of the name, badge and flag as recognition of what they have endured over the years.

However, the Social Democratic and Labour party believes that many people in the RUC and the wider community, including the Unionist community accept that changes are needed in the emblems, flags and name of the police service to create a new beginning. Again, we have adopted an approach that ensures compatibility with the accepted Patten report and addresses the structures and cultural issues with which it dealt. We have tried not to be one-dimensional by focusing on one issue, but to deal with the broad canvas that was covered by Patten in his attempt to achieve a new police service.

If the new police service is to be accepted, there needs to be a satisfactory resolution of the issue of emblems and flags. The cultural proposals in the Patten report are both symbolically and structurally important. They are an expression of the new beginning to which I have referred. Much more importantly, they are and will continue to be an invitation to all sections of our community to accept and join the new service. The amendments express our hope of achieving a corporate neutrality, impartiality and independence in the new police service, albeit there will be individual diversity within it.

The Patten report dealt unambiguously with those matters. The legislation should be equally unambiguous. Patten was particularly straightforward on the name, flag and emblems:

    that new beginning cannot be achieved unless the reality that part of the community feels unable to identify with the present name and symbols associated with the police is addressed.

Patten made that statement on the basis that he outlined in the report:

    Many people in Northern Ireland from the Irish nationalist and republican tradition regard the name, badge and symbols of the RUC as associating the police with the British constitution and state The problem is that the name of the RUC, and to some extent the badge and the uniform too, have become politicised-one community effectively claiming ownership of the name of ``our'' police force, and the other community taking the position that the name

and the emblem and the flag are

    symbolic of a relationship between the police and unionism and the British state.

Some people here and in other places disagree with that. In an earlier debate, the hon. Member for West Tyrone (Mr. Thompson) quoted from the response of the Northern Irish bishops, who said:

    it is important to point out that the name, symbols and political ethos of a police service are not essential to its primary purpose of detecting crime and upholding the law in service of the whole community.

The hon. Gentleman gave the impression that his quote fully represented the opinion of the Catholic hierarchy in Northern Ireland. I know that he did not intend to mislead the Committee, but he did not continue to quote the response, which said:

    To our mind such de-politicisation of the culture and ethos of policing is essential if policing arrangements in Northern Ireland are to win the full support of the Catholic community and if the targets for Catholic recruitment set out in the Report are to have a realistic chance of being met.

The full paragraph has an entirely different meaning from that of the partial quote recorded in our sitting on 27 June at column 312. Lest that be taken as the view of the hierarchy, I think it important to read the full sentence into the record.

The members of the police commission knew of the difficulties and the nuances because they came from different traditions in Northern Ireland and different backgrounds. They accepted and promoted the idea of a substantive change in emblems and flags, and the strength of Patten's unambiguous and conclusive recommendations on the emblem and the flag must be acknowledged. The commission recommended:

    that the Northern Ireland Police Service adopt a new badge and symbols which are entirely free from any association with either the British or Irish states;

    that the Union flag should no longer be flown from police buildings;

    that, on those occasions on which it is appropriate to fly a flag on police buildings, the flag flown should be that of the Northern Ireland Police Service and it, too, should be free from association with the British or Irish states.

The SDLP amendments would turn those recommendations into law.

The Government say that they accept Patten's argument that the police service should adopt a new badge and new symbols free from any association with either the British or Irish states. However, they argue that the badge and symbols need not be free from any association from the British or the Irish traditions. We do not think that Patten intended such an approach. Some anxiety has been expressed about the difference in meaning between ``association with either the British or the Irish states'' and ``association with either the British or the Irish traditions'', and about how the Government interpreted those expressions. The wording of the Bill seems to be contrary to the spirit, if not the letter, of Patten.

Unfortunately, the final decision on such matters appears to lie with the Secretary of State. In his only public comment about the issue after the report's publication, Patten said that emblems and flags should not be linked to any political view either. That is another significant comment. We are opposed to the Secretary of State having the power to decide. The responsibility should be left to the Policing Board, just as decisions about the flying of flags from public buildings should have been left to the Northern Ireland Executive, rather than the Secretary of State.

Patten stated unambiguously that the Union flag should no longer be flown from police buildings. Under the Bill, the Secretary of State reserves to himself the power to determine which flags, including national flags, and which police flags, whatever emblem may subsequently be upon them, shall be flown from police buildings. The Government have argued that they need this power, ironically, to stop the national flag being flown from police buildings. But there is a real sense that it is to enable the national flag to be flown from the buildings that the Secretary of State is securing such a power. The terminology and ethos of the clause are contrary to the letter and certainly the spirit of what Patten intended.

It was a specific agreement that was intended to allow both major traditions and others in Northern Ireland to build upon a clear definition of Northern Ireland's constitutional position.

The agreement began by stating that Northern Ireland would remain part of the United Kingdom until such time as the majority of its people at the ballot box decided that it should be otherwise. Hence, it is important that everyone-politicians, police and others-support and defend that agreed position. It was not without some reserve that Unionists accepted that state of affairs. They accepted it as democrats but realised that if Northern Ireland's constitutional position was left open to question, even in terms of the future vote, it could provoke the sort of agitation that has been reflected in the contribution here this morning. If we are part of the United Kingdom, the citizens of Northern Ireland should take the United Kingdom's position of constitutional, legal and other issues.

The United Kingdom flag is the Union flag; it should not be abused, as it has been by a small minority. That adage could be applied to all flags, which tend to be abused in confrontations. The Ulster Unionist party stands firmly and resolutely against the abuse of any person or tradition in Northern Ireland. There cannot be a deviation, or those who have defended the rights of citizens across the political divide in Northern Ireland will be alienated.

I repudiate any attempt by an individual, party or group to create circumstances in which the reality is different from that in the hard-won agreement of Good Friday 1998. It is not a question of being an arm of the state, but in the UK-there are different rules in different countries-ultimately all power devolves from the Crown.
