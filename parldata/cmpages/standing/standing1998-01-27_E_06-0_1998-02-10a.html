Mr Breed: Although there are many amendments in this group, they all bear down on one feature. Chapter 11.4 on page 53 of the White Paper “Building Partnerships for Prosperity” states:

    “RDAs and the chambers must work together in a fair and transparent way within and between regions”.

We all agree that that is vital for the future success of the RDAs. The relationship between chambers and RDAs is at the heart of that working together, and the RDAs accountability to the regional chambers and the way in which the bodies consult, operate and, we hope, agree will be fundamental to it.

The amendments deal with the status and the powers of regional chambers and contain important arguments. The regional assemblies could play a substantial role, but the danger is that their meetings with local councillors may simply be to say what they do not like about the RDA and that it is not doing what they want it to do. The RDA could become divorced from the regional chambers, which would be powerless to influence the RDA board and its actions. In a sense, therefore, the RDAs and the regional chambers could work to different agendas, but they must work together to tackle the economic deficit.

The Bill says that the RDA must have regard to the chamber’ views and consult it. Both this clause and clause 18 do not reassure me that the chambers will be able to hold the RDAs to account. The RDAs could go off and do their own thing, instead of working with the regional chambers. Therefore, the Secretary of State’s ability to direct these matters needs to be stiffened, to ensure that the bodies work together.

The amendments give the regional chambers modest extra powers to ensure that they and the RDAs build up a relationship that is in the region’s best interests. The amendments seek to prevent future conflict between the two institutions. This is new territory. These will be exciting and often difficult times. There are likely to be differences of opinion and different emphasis on certain aspects of policy and strategy. Our amendments ensure that the bodies work together in the fair and transparent way flagged up in the White Paper, instead of working to different agendas.

Amendments No. 87 and 90 are unnecessary. Amendment No. 90 would add to clause 8(2) a reference to the RDAs’ functions set out in clauses 4, 5 and 6. The amendment would ensure that RDAs can be required to have regard to the views of the chamber in exercising these functions. Clause 8(2)(a) relates only to the economic strategy in this context. The different formulation used in clause 8(2)(a) arises because we wish to be able to require the RDAs to have regard to the views of chambers when they are drawing up their strategy—that is, before there is a draft on which to consult.

Amendment No. 90 provides that in addition to consulting the chamber the RDA should consult other bodies. To the extent that such bodies are already represented in the chamber, that could amount to unnecessary additional consultation. However, there will be individual groups, whether involved in the chamber or not, that might be particularly interested in or affected by a specific RDA. In those cases I believe it would be right for the RDA to consult directly with such groups. I hope that RDAs will develop their own procedures for involvement of those directly affected by their activities without Ministers having to tell them what to do and hold their hands.
