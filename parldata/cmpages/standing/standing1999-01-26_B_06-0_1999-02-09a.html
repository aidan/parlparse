Mr. Jenkin: Good morning, Mr. Gale.

Clause 7 concerns the audit of best value performance plans. It sets out how the auditor is to be appointed and what he will consider once such a report is prepared. The principal issue that I wish to raise is contained in amendment No. 10, which concerns who the auditor is to be.

In the private sector it is common practice to follow the Cadbury committee's recommendation and change company auditors on a regular basis. It is recommended that a public limited company should not have the same auditor for more than three years running. The auditor should then be changed because there is a danger of a cosy relationship developing between him and the company. Local government, however, is different because the Audit Commission and the district auditor are permanent features. The district auditor often audits local authorities ad infinitum.

Best performance plans should be considered by someone who is distanced from the authority's operation. If an authority's auditor was to become involved with auditing a best value performance plan, that would be too much like an auditor auditing his own work. Amendment No. 10 proposes that clause 7(1) should stipulate that the auditor should be different from the one employed by the authority.

Amendment No. 161 is a probing amendment and concerns provisions for the auditor's appointment in clause 7. We would like the Minister to explain the background to subsections (7), (8) and (9) so as to clarify the Bill's intentions.

Amendment No. 162 proposes that the auditor should not be a provider of consultancy advice, as that would lead to conflict. A situation may arise in which an auditor audits the authority's annual accounts and also provides consultancy advice on constructing a best value performance plan. If the auditor was then asked to audit such a plan, he would not bring the necessary freshness of view.

Experience demonstrates that district auditors, as a breed, can be reluctant to take up complaints about contracting and contracting out. However, auditors will be required to pursue such matters in the audit of best value plans. Complaints made to the district auditor about poor value in a local authority are often passed on to the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions. That is because such matters are regarded as political issues rather than audit issues. The Audit Commission is paid to carry out consultancy advice, and it is important that such responsibilities are kept separate.

One local authority went through the process of letting a refuse collection contract, the lowest bidder for which was a private sector contractor. However, the council kept the contract in-house for the direct labour organisation. When the contractor complained, the district auditor simply claimed that it was a matter for the DETR, not the auditor. I shall not name the authority. It would be invidious to name public officials in the district audit office.

The auditor should be independent if the person who holds that post is to assume a proper enforcement role. Amendments Nos. 10 and 162 would effect that, subject to clarification of clause 7(7) and (8).
