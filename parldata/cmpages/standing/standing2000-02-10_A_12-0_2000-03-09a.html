The amendments would place controls on the amount that would be spent by the authority in using its licence fees and put a check on the cost that the clauses would add to electricity and gas bills.

The figure of 2.5 per cent. may seem strange, and in many ways it is too high a figure. When Parliament was considering a tax on normal domestic fuel, the previous Government first announced that it would put a full 15 per cent. value added tax on it. In the previous Parliament there was a considerable rebellion among Conservative Members and in the end Parliament decided not to give permission for the second 7.5 per cent. increase, so VAT was limited to 7.5 per cent.

They are a signal that we are trying to keep prices down for consumers. The Government have clearly decided that they want mechanisms in place that allow the authority to fiddle around on the margins, transferring funds from better-off consumers to less well-off consumers, and the Bill should contain a limit to how much they can do that. As I read the Bill, it gives the authority an unlimited and unfettered power to change the amount that can be transferred from one set of people to another.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): The amendments and the clause relate to social and environmental guidance that will be issued by the Secretary of State and to which the regulator must have regard.

I shall begin by quoting from a recent, and critical, Electricity Association parliamentary briefing. It states:

    In the light of recent events—

by which it means the withdrawal from the Bill of telecoms and water—

    the electricity industry believes that there is a strong case for a much simpler Energy Utilities Bill concentrating only on those provisions necessary to implement the new electricity trading arrangements, business separation and essential reforms of regulatory structure.

Like the telecoms and water industries, the electricity industry is demanding that, apart from minimal elements, its segments be removed from the Bill. The brief continues:

    Other provisions of the Bill go well beyond the broad objectives of regulatory stability, fairness and accountability which the Government earlier set itself to seek. As drafted these provisions have the potential to destabilise the regulatory regime and this underlines the case for a fundamental review of the broader provisions of the Bill including the penalties regime, the powers of the regulators, and social and enviornmental guidance.

It is that last element that we are debating this morning. That is a fairly damning indictment of the Bill, and it echoes criticisms made by the telecommunications and water industries.

An Electricity Association press release, issued on 7 March, states:

    As the Utilities Bill returns to the House of Commons Standing Committee to be shorn of its telecoms and water provisions, the Electricity Association is seeking a meeting with DTI Ministers to raise industry concerns about ``over-regulation'' in some of the Bill's remaning measures.

Has such a meeting been held, and if not, when will it be held? Has the meeting even been scheduled, and if not, why not?

The amendment, which would add to the clause a phrase with which the Minister should be familiar, would ensure that guidance issued by the Secretary of State on social and environmental matters does not impose significant financial implications for consumers.

The passing on of costs associated with the regulatory offices and their staff to the consumer has been our constant worry. We have discussed in detail the likely level of those costs in the light of the expansionist tendencies, to which the regulators are not immune, of most state organisations. However, it is not merely the administration costs suffered by the utilities in responding to requests for information and other administrative compliance matters that will be passed on to consumers. The largest extra cost to the consumer will arise from the cost to the utilities of implementing the requirement set out in guidances, which will be issued by the Secretary of State to the regulatory authority. Those guidances are important because, as the Bill states,

    The Authority shall, in carrying out its functions ... have regard to guidances issued by the Secretary of State.

The draft guidance to the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority states:

    the Authority should have regard to the Government's objective of improving the health of everyone.

That seems a broad target for utility companies to meet. So far as the authority is concerned, those so-called guidances will amount to more than an indication of one or two factors. In effect, they will be orders, which the regulatory authorities will try to turn into guidances, and which they will then have to turn into specific licence conditions or regulations for the utility companies. The requirement on the authority to have regard to the Government's objective of improving everyone's health is very broad. I dread to think what effect it will have on the licence conditions of the electricity and gas companies.

The draft guidance reflects the rhetoric of the Government's social policy agenda. They clearly expect their social objectives to be addressed through the delivery mechanisms of the private sector utility companies and their competitors. The electricity industry does not object to the merging of the two regulators into Ofgem, the consumer council being one body for both industries or the new electricity trading arrangements. In fact, it is positively in favour of NETA, as are Conservative Members. What it objects to is the broader policy of seeking to make the utilities deliver the Government's social and environmental agenda.

However worthy the Government's objectives may be, this is not the correct delivery mechanism to achieve them. The wording of the guidance is vague and uncertain. Paragraph 5.2, on page 5, states:

    The Government is committed to the creation of a fairer society, through social progress which recognises the needs of everyone. To this end it has set five broad objectives. The task of regulating the energy sector appears to the Government to be relevant to three of these.

Thank goodness that it is only three. They are:

    to tackle poverty and social exclusion;

    to improve the health of the population overall;

    to reduce the proportion of unfit housing stock.

It is staggering that the utility companies should be given such broad targets. I do not know why the Government did not add ``to end world hunger''. [Hon. Members: ``Hear, hear.''] Labour Members seem to think that that would be worthy of inclusion in the guidance. Perhaps they would also include ``end world famine'', ``bring about world peace'' and ``end all known diseases''. Given that the utilities are faced with such open and wide guidances, it stretches credibility to the limit for the Minister to claim that the Bill increases the certainty of regulation.

The CBI is not clear about what it all means, which is why it has said:

    We look forward to the opportunity to comment on the development of this guidance, and feel that it is important that it is subject to affirmative procedure, to ensure all parties are clear about the full implications of the interaction between social and environmental guidance and the primary duty.

Of course, the primary duty is to protect the interests of consumers. It will be difficult for the utility companies to deliver world peace and the end of all known diseases—or, more seriously, the three objectives set out on page 5 of the draft guidance—while at the same time keeping control of prices and minimising costs to the consumer.

Last week, Ofgem issued its social action plan, which reflects much of what is in the Government's draft guidance. The press release that accompanies the plan states:

    A plan of action which Ofgem will undertake with others to tackle fuel poverty is published today, following wide consultation with consumer groups, industry and government.

So Ofgem has already consulted widely to produce the plan—which is a plan, not a draft plan. The Government's guidance is still in draft. It has still to be consulted on, let alone laid before Parliament, as required by subsection (5), after which there must be a period of 40 days during which it can be objected to. The Bill is still a Bill—it has not yet passed through the Committee, let alone received Royal Assent, and will not survive the Committee in its current form, even though the telecoms and water sections have been excised.

Ofgem appears, yet again, to be acting as a law unto itself. That is not surprising; it is in the nature of such organisations when they are given such wide remits. I am concerned about the cost implications of the extent of what will ultimately be required of utilities by Ofgem. Page 2 of its press release states:

    Ofgem has also appointed a 10 member review panel bringing together consumer groups, government and representatives of the energy and banking industries. Regular reports on progress against the plan will also be published.

The press release goes on to quote Callum McCarthy, the Director General of Gas and Electricity Markets, who said:

    There are many customers who either because of low income or poor housing find it difficult to afford heat and light for their homes. This is a big and complex problem, which requires many parts of government and the industry to contribute to solutions. Through the Social Action Plan Ofgem will concentrate on taking effective action to tackle this problem to make a difference for low income and disadvantaged customers.

Those words will have far reaching financial implications.

10.15 am

The concern about such implications was discussed in the Green Paper, which stated:

    If the Government concludes that its social and environmental policy objectives require action which would have significant financial implications for consumers or for the regulated companies, such action should be taken forward by means of new, specific provision in primary or secondary legislation rather than through guidance to the regulators.

We have used some of that clear wording in amendments Nos. 134, 314 and what was amendment No. 135, which related to the telecommunications industry and has been withdrawn. The amendments would add to the subsection enabling the Secretary of State to issue guidance the provision that

    In preparing guidance under this section, the Secretary of State shall ensure that such guidance does not impose significant financial implications for consumers.

That final phrase has been lifted word for word from the Government's Green Paper. I have every confidence, therefore, that the Minister will accept the amendments. If he does not, he will expose yet again the fact that the Government do not care about the financial implications of the Bill, despite the fact that the Green Paper—and other documents published during the preparation of the Bill—expressed concern about financial implications.

The guidance says that one objective should be to improve the health of everyone. If that does not have significant financial implications for consumers, I do not know what does. Consequently, such objectives—which the Government seek to place on the regulators, who would then pass them to the utilities—should be made through primary or secondary legislation, rather than through guidance issued to the regulator. I look forward to hearing the Minister's comments on those points.

In setting the legislative framework, the Government should put in place provisions such as those suggested by the amendment.
