Amendments Nos. 1 and 2 are intended to tease out that question.

The Protection of Animals Act 1911, which the Bill amends, seeks-in 1911 language-comprehensively to define ``animal'', ``domestic animal'', ``captive animal'', ``horse'' and, rather quaintly, ``knacker''. We need not concern ourselves too much with that, but as the Pet Care Trust points out, the world has moved on since 1911. Although definitions of animals may not have changed, society in the early part of this new century regards and treats animals in a way that could not have been envisaged in 1911, and we must satisfy ourselves that the Bill's wording is adequate for the purpose. The amendments therefore try to deal with the questions raised by the Pet Care Trust. In particular, they try to establish whether the Bill properly answers those questions, or whether they would improve it.

Amendment No. 2 tries to clarify subsection (2), which states:

    those sections only apply in relation to an animal which the owner keeps or has kept for commercial purposes.

Perhaps amendment No. 2 is not particularly elegantly worded, but its purpose is to establish whether the promoter and the Minister, working in harness, can satisfy Committee members that the Bill, as drafted, answers the questions raised by the Pet Care Trust. Would the amendment-or one with similar wording-do a better job?

I shall not elaborate further. I hope that I have got across the gist of the matter as I see it, and that I am doing justice to the Pet Care Trust, to whose letter I shall return briefly in due course. My aim is to probe, seek clarification, and ensure that the Bill leaves this Committee in the best possible shape.

Mrs Curtis-Thomas: I appreciate the right hon. Gentleman's concerns, and I hope that I can clarify the situation. The amendment would place an additional requirement on a court to have regard to veterinary advice when deciding on the type of order to make under clause 2(3). By virtue of clause 2(1), a court is already bound to take into account veterinary advice when deciding whether to make an order.

A court is already required by clause 2(3) to take into account all the circumstances of a case when deciding on the appropriate form of order. Such circumstances could include the welfare of animals. Clause 2(1) provides the first step in a cumulative approach to the granting of an order.
