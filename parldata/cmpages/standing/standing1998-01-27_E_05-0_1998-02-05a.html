The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Angela Eagle): It may help the Committee if I explain once again that the Government intend sustainable development to be at the core of regional development agencies' activities. The purposes of the RDAs are not mutually exclusive, as some hon. Members have suggested. Nor are they ranked in any way that is detrimental to sustainable development. In an earlier debate, the right hon. Member for Skipton and Ripon (Mr. Curry) rightly pointed out that there is some overlap in the purposes. We have made no attempt to rank them in terms of importance or to define each purpose completely separately.

Sustainable development can be looked at in two ways. It is one of the five purposes of the RDAs and will inform the agencies' actions on the other four purposes where sustainable development is involved. It will also enable the agency to act independently on sustainable development even where such action would not fall under any of the other four purposes. The provision was drafted as an enabling factor.

The two qualifications to the purpose of sustainable development, which have caused such suspicion, are that action should contribute to sustainable development in the United Kingdom and should be relevant to the agency's area. Those qualifications have worried the hon. Member for South Suffolk (Mr. Yeo), and I hope that I can reassure him.

Mr. Yeo: I am not concerned about the reference to the UK. That deals helpfully with an earlier worry. I am concerned at the remaining phrase,

    ``where it is relevant to its area to do so.''

Angela Eagle: In that case I shall concentrate my efforts on reassuring the hon. Gentleman about that qualification, which, in practice, will have an effect only in relation to the not-in-the-UK qualification. We shall therefore leave that aside.

Against that background, let us consider the effect of the purpose when it is used independently of the other purposes of RDAs. If we removed the qualification

    ``where it is relevant to its area''

as the amendment would, we would allow RDAs--indeed, give them a duty--to take action contributing to sustainable development that is not relevant to their area, which is not sensible. RDAs are designed to take action that is relevant to their particular area, rather than action that is relevant only to the rest of England, or of the United Kingdom, or indeed the rest of the world.

On the other hand, a qualification that would limit RDAs to action within their regions only would be too restrictive. There may be projects, such as the improvement of transport links between the west midlands and Wales, for which action outside the region would be relevant to sustainable development within the region. Limiting RDAs to action that is physically within their regions would jeopardise such projects, and make RDAs less able to fulfil their core duty of promoting sustainable development, because such development does not always stop at the regional border.

We are attempting to be as flexible as possible and to work out a way in which sustainable development remains relevant, but which allows cross-border activities among RDAs. Problems such as climate change and the hole in the ozone layer are relevant to the world as a whole; action anywhere in the world can be said to affect other places in the world. However, that is the international approach.

By including the qualification, we are attempting to give--and, I believe, succeeding in giving--RDAs flexibility both ways. We are not restricting them to their areas in some circumstances. We are not giving them a duty to behave with a sustainable development aim within their areas only.

We are therefore ensuring the opposite of what the hon. Member for South Suffolk fears: we are attempting to give RDAs the flexibility that they need to act across borders when necessary. I realise that that is a rather convoluted answer, but I hope that I have reassured the hon. Gentleman about our intentions.
