Clause 33

Formulation of government policy, etc

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 134, in page 18, line 16, leave out from `if' to end of line 21 and insert

    `its disclosure would harm the frankness and candour of internal discussion, including:

    (a) proceedings of Cabinet and Cabinet committees;

    (b) internal opinion, advice, recommendation, consultation and deliberation;

    (c) projections and assumptions relating to internal policy analysis; analysis of alternative policy options and information relating to rejected policy options;

    (d) confidential communications between departments, public bodies and regulatory bodies.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendments: No. 136, in page 18, line 24, at end insert—

    `(3) In seeking to claim an exemption under subsection (1) the public authority must at all times have regard to the overriding public interest in the publication of facts and analysis of the facts which the Government considers relevant and important in framing major policy proposals and decisions.'.

No. 137, in page 18, leave out lines 27 to 42.

Mr. Greenway: The Committee must seriously consider three issues in the clause, which is inadequate as drafted. In terms of marshalling thinking, the first issue is the exemption of the formulation of Government policy under subsection (1), which is too widely drawn. Amendments Nos. 134 and 137 would improve the clause considerably.

The second issue, with which we shall deal further in the next debate, is that the exemption needs a harm or prejudice test. It is implicit in amendment No. 134 that such a harm test would be included. In other words, it is not an either or situation, although either option would be better than the present drafting. Thirdly, we must include a public interest test for the release of facts that is stronger than that in clause 13(5), which we have already debated.

The Bill will provide a class exemption for policy formulation. That means that any information in that category will be covered by the exemption whether or not its release would be harmful. As public authorities or Ministers ultimately decide whether the release of exempt information is in the public interest, the exemption would mean that access to such information could be severely limited.

It is especially important that subsection (1) rows back from provisions in the code of practice and the White Paper. Under subsection (1), Ministers will effectively be given carte blanche to deny access to innocuous and non-controversial information. That could mean that information that is available now would be made secret. That is unacceptable. In fact, the Government's proposals will end the right to that category of information, rather than provide it.

The proposed wording of the code in amendment No. 134 is more limited. For example, subsection (1)(d) includes

    ``the operation of any Ministerial private office''.

in the absolute exemption, with no harm test. That is a scandalous proposal, given that there is no prejudice test. That point will be made more forcefully when we come to the relevant amendments.

Our key point is that information currently available under the code will become secret under the Bill. Subsection (l)(d) is a classic example of that. The code does not exclude the operation of a Minister's private office, and it exempts only confidential communications between Departments and Ministers. I stress the word confidential. There is no confidential nature to subsection (1)(b), which simply refers to ``Ministerial communications'', so any communications between Ministers will be covered by that blanket exemption with no prejudice test.

Worse still, a Minister's decision under the code not to release information that it suggests ought to have been released could be reviewed by the parliamentary ombudsman. I am sure that the Parliamentary Secretary will tell us that no Minister would relish the ombudsman's finding that some matter that related to information about the development of Government policy had been withheld when it should have been released.

In spite of it critics, the code has teeth. No one suggests that it is perfect, and we shall soon discuss how enforcement provisions should be structured. As there is no prejudice test in the clause and because the exemption is so wide, the Minister will decide whether to release information. There is no enforcement of any kind. Under the code, one can at least tell the parliamentary ombudsman that the code has been breached. There is no obligation on the Minister to release information, partly because of the prejudice test but, in relation to the amendment, especially because the exemption is far too widely drawn.

Many organisations have criticised the Bill's drafting. Under an amendment to clause 13 that was linked to an amendment to this clause, we discussed the need to provide for the release of information that advises Ministers on the formulation or development of policy, rather than of the advice itself.

The facts and figures or dates on which decisions were taken, which are freely available under the code, should be available under the Bill.

To try to rectify that shortfall and criticism, the Government included in clause 13(5) a requirement for a public authority to

    ``have regard to the desirability of communicating to the applicant factual information which has been used, or is intended to be used, to provide an informed background to decision-taking.''

I stress the word desirability—for whom? ``That is not a prejudice test; nor is it a public interest test. Amendment No. 136 seeks to add an additional caveat on the exercise of that provision. Its overriding public interest in the publication and release of such information is far stronger than the provisions of subsection (5), which talks only of desirability.

I want to keep our proceedings strictly in line with the order of consideration, so I shall not stray any further into arguments about section 33 requiring a prejudice test. Our primary objective is to narrow the material caught under that exemption to that caught under the code. The Government have widened that provision considerably, which is not acceptable.

Our debate on amendment No. 136 enables us to speak of a public interest test on the release of factual information that has informed decision taking, rather than relying on the rather weak provisions that the Government introduced at a late stage. Indeed, we believe that those provisions are in the wrong clause. The Bill should allow a public interest test, not only a test of desirability.

Finally, just for the sake of tidiness, amendment No. 137 would omit the definitions of law officers, ministerial communications and so on. That provision is consequential on subsection (1). Because our amendments do not refer to them, such definitions are unnecessary. The clause does not progress the cause of freedom of information. On the contrary, it is possibly the most glaring example in the Bill of the extent to which the proposed statutory framework rows back from the provisions of the code.

The clause makes more information secret, but it is information at the heart of Government, not in many of the more peripheral public authorities caught under the Bill. This is a fundamental matter. We are entitled to some give from the Minister. I have tried to be concise, and not to detain the Committee unnecessarily, but the Minister and his officials should recognise that our concerns are justified, and it is time that we saw some movement. We have moved a sensible amendment that would achieve what the Government say they want: the freeing of more information—not making it more secret, as clause 33 does.

Mr. Heath: Clause 34 widens the scope of the previous clause to non-exempt information in Government Departments and other public authorities. Again, it seeks to protect the internal deliberations of those bodies.

I do not propose—and I should be grateful if the Minister would not respond as if I had—that internal deliberations should not be exempt when that is necessary for the proper workings of government, whether national or local. However, the clause goes further than that by introducing two tests, which I wish to explore through the amendments.

First, there is the test of the

    ``reasonable opinion of a qualified person'',

The clause goes on to list, at length, those who would be deemed qualified in that respect. Secondly, there is test of whether something

    ``would otherwise prejudice the effective conduct of public affairs.''

That definition is capable of considerable extension by those who would want it to apply to material that Ministers or those who drafted the Bill may not have intended to incorporate. Something that is harmful to public affairs at large requires a rather more serious attempt at a definition than we have here.

That exemption may be derived from a similar provision in the New Zealand Official Information Act 1982, which allows information to be withheld to

    ``maintain the effective conduct of public affairs''.

There is a substantial difference, however, between the New Zealand legislation and this Bill. In the former, that phrase is qualified by four separate provisions. First, it applies only if the damage to the conduct of public affairs would result from harm to the frank expression of opinions or by exposing officials to improper pressure. Thus, there is a definition of what that harm might be.

Secondly—this is an extremely important point—objective evidence of such harm is required. In other words, the authorities cannot simply offer an opinion that it is harmful to the conduct of public affairs; it has to be shown to be harmful. Yet, under the Bill, opinions are going to be made by people who are inevitably parti pris and inevitably interested in whether disclosure is made.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: I am following the hon. Gentleman's argument as closely as I can, but I want to be clear. If the qualified person is removed, could not any public servant take the decision? The consequence is retrogressive. We suggest that a qualified person should take the decision, but the hon. Gentleman is removing that and allowing any public servant to do so.

Mr. Heath: The Under-Secretary refers to a separate part of the provision. I am dealing with the test of effective conduct of public affairs and the qualifications in the New Zealand legislation. I shall get to the Under-Secretary's point later, but I wish to finish my discussion of the opinion as such, as opposed to whether it should be qualified.

The test here is the assertion of opinion by a person who may or may not be qualified. The qualification is evident under the Bill. However, persons such as Ministers of the Crown also have an interest in the outcome. Depending on who is elected as the new mayor of London, strong opinions on various matters can be expected. Such people are inevitably, as I said, subjective rather than objective in their opinions.

The third test in the New Zealand legislation requires it to be ``necessary'' to withhold information—a stricter test than under the Bill. Fourthly, it requires that the information be disclosed if the harm is outweighed by the public interest, which is legally binding—unlike in the Bill.

The fundamental problem is that a shield is erected against the commissioner applying her judgment. She is required to apply a judicial review test on the reasonableness of the opinion or view taken. As we know from judicial proceedings over a wide area of public affairs, a reasonable test is a fairly substantial obstacle in challenging a decision. A decision has to be shown to be extraordinary, irrational, outrageous, in defiance of logic or verging on the absurd before it can be held to be unreasonable. If there is a scintilla of reasonableness in the opinion—it might be based on a misjudgment or a small ancillary factor—that could bring a qualified person reasonably to formulate such a conclusion, irrespective of whether it is right or wrong, the test will have been satisfied. That is a serious flaw in the Bill. It puts an obstacle between the commissioner and the correct decision, which is an objective assessment of whether material should be exempt. That issue lies at the heart of the amendments.

The Under-Secretary questioned whether leaving out the provision about a qualified person would make the test apply to anyone. He was right that to take one amendment in the absence of the other would be unsatisfactory. If we replaced the provision in the Bill for an objective test, there would be no reason to have a person's opinion, however well qualified he or she was, as that would no longer be a test of reasonableness.

I am not alone in feeling that that is a problem. The Public Administration Committee called for the reasonable opinion test to be dropped. I will not quote its findings at length, as I suspect that the Under-Secretary will be aware of them.
