The Minister for Local Government and Housing (Ms Hilary Armstrong): I was responding after a lengthy debate to amendment No. 180, which would remove parts of the clause. I was trying to explain why we wanted to retain them.

Clause 13 ensures that local inquiries that are conducted on the request of the Secretary of State are procedurally consistent. The removal of subsection (4) would mean that procedural consistency between different sorts of local inquiry could not be guaranteed. Providing for the inquiry process will promote public confidence in the transparency of decisions about best value failure. Provision to direct a local inquiry is important for local accountability, and to ensure that intervention is based on clear evidence of failure. Procedural inconsistency in inquiries would undermine their effectiveness.

Amendment No. 36 would require the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales to consult representatives of best value authorities on the action that was considered necessary or expedient to secure compliance with part I of the Bill before issuing a direction under clause 13(2)(d). I am well aware of the importance of working with authorities and their representatives when an auditor or inspector recommends intervention by the Secretary of State. Therefore, clause 13(6) gives best value authorities the opportunity to make representations about the report that proposes the direction, and about the direction itself, before the Secretary of State or the Assembly makes a direction under the clause.

Notifying local authorities of the basis and nature of any proposed intervention and allowing them to put their case is a fair and effective method of tackling failure. The Bill provides ample opportunity for authorities to make representations about any direction under subsection (6)(d). The protocol that we circulated to members of the Committee will explain authorities' involvement in any intervention process and how the LGA or the association of police authorities might assist. Amendment No. 36 is, therefore, unnecessary.

Amendment No. 146 is superfluous. Any direction under clause 13 will be designed to tackle an authority that has failed to secure best value. The amendment would ensure that a direction under clause 13 was issued to promote continuous improvement in the manner that an authority exercised its functions, with regard to economy, efficiency and effectiveness. The amendment would not make a practical difference to any activity for which the clause provides. Therefore, it is unnecessary.

My hon. Friends tabled amendment No. 198, and my hon. Friend the Member for Southampton, Test (Dr. Whitehead) spoke to it this morning. I thank him for his measured speech, and for reminding us that he has a reputation in academia for his knowledge and understanding of local government. That was very clear this morning.

The amendment is intended to ensure that the Secretary of State will act, under subsection (5), in a manner that promotes the achievement of best value, and that authorities will be obliged to offer assistance only so long as the function exercised by the Secretary of State is also subject to best value criteria. The Government recogonise that that is an important concern—as did the hon. Member for North Essex, who jumped on the bandwagon. I assure my hon. Friend the Member for Southampton, Test that the Bill already provides for what he wants.

When issuing a direction under subsection (5) that he or a nominated person should exercise a function on behalf of the authority, the Secretary of State is automatically required to make arrangement to secure best value, despite not being a best value authority himself. A Secretary of State or his nominees are, in exercising a function, by definition acting to ensure that best value be secured.

The amendment suggests that the authority's obligation to provide such assitance as is required for the exercise of the specified function would be conditional on the Secretary of State acting in a manner that ensured that best value was achieved. I am sure that my hon. Friend would agree that, the Secretary of State should always act to promote the achievement of best value with respect to any function that he was carrying out on behalf of an authority, and that no prudent best value authority that wanted to recover responsibility for that function would fail to assist him. That is already implicit in the Bill and I hope that my hon. Friend will no press the amendment.

CCT entailed powers of intervention similar to those in the Bill, and Conservative Members, when in government, did not think of the matter when exercising powers under previous legislation.

Government amdment Nos. 59, 60 and 61 allow for intervention in urgent cases. Government amendment No. 59 will do so by making clause 13(6) subject to new subsection (7A). Government amendment No. 60 makes clause 13(7) subject to new subsection (7A), and Government amendment No. 61 inserts new subsections (7A) and (7B) into clause 13.

Government amendment No. 60 makes provision for the Secretary of State to intervene in urgent cases, without having regard to any statement of action produced under section 9(2) of the Local Government Act 1972. Government amendment No. 61 will allow the Secretary of State or the Welsh Assembly to exercise intervention powers under clause 13 in urgent cases to address serious and persistent failure by an authority.

The White Paper, ``Modern Local Government: In Touch with the People'', made it clear that effective intervention will normally involve working with authorities and giving them the opportunity to make the improvements deemed necesary themselves. However, in some cases, failure may be so severe or persistent—or the risks to local people so great—that immediate action is justified. If there is evidence of serious harm to vulnerable groups, and the authority is unwilling or unable to act sufficiently quickly to protect them, the Secretary of State must retain the discretion to act quickly. Hon. Members will note that the draft protocol illustrates the type of circumstances which might justify urgent intervention.

New subsection (7A) allows the Secretary of State or the Welsh Assembly to give a direction without complying with clause 13(6) or (7), if he considers such a direction to be sufficiently urgent. That means that the Secretary of State or the Welsh Assembly can issue a direction under clause 13 without giving the authority concerned the opportunity to make representations about the direction proposed or the report—if one is produced—as a result of which intervention is proposed. The Secretary of State or the Welsh Assembly will also be able to issue a direction under clause 13 without having regard to any statement of action produced under section 9(2) of the Local Government Act 1972.

Under new subsection (7B), the Secretary of State or the Welsh Assembly will be required to tell the authority concerned and those representing best value authorities of any direction that does not comply with clause 13(6) or (7) and the reason why that direction was given without such compliance.

Some amendments in this group deal with inquiries, and the right hon. Member for Skipton and Ripon (Mr. Curry) raised that matter earlier. So it might be helpful if I said a little more about that. When the hon. Member for Taunton (Jackie Ballard) asked about democratic accountability, she seemed to imply that all intervention powers would automatically bypass local people. I assure the Committee that that is not necessarily so, although I accept that urgent intervention would do so. But section 250 of the Local Government Act 1972 gives the Secretary of State power to direct an inquiry. He has power to appoint someone to hold it and his appointee can then direct people to give evidence. Costs are met by either the authority or a third party, as the Secretary of State directs. In this context, inquiries will be used where it appears necessary to ascertain local people's views. It may be that consultations under the best value framework have already provided sufficient indication of local people's wishes and aspirations, but inquiries could be used to determine whether the clause 13(5) powers should be exercised. We are certainly not ruling out that possibility.

I understand why hon. Members are concerned about clause 15 and whether it offers the right mixture of parliamentary scrutiny to meet their concerns. It is true that, at present, the scrutiny powers referred to in subsequent clauses are not the same as those in the Deregulation and Contracting Out Act 1994.

The Bill, as currently drafted, requires the Secretary of State to consult on proposals for draft orders. Such proposals would have to be laid before Parliament, together with the consultation results and a full explanation of the proposed orders. Parliament will then have 60 days to consider them. That period will not include times when Parliament is dissolved, prorogued or adjourned for more than four days. When the 60 days have elapsed, the Secretary of State can lay draft orders before Parliament, together with details of representations made during the 60 days that the proposals are before Parliament. A report will be published on the changes made as a result of such representations.

The Secretary of State can make and effect the order only when both Houses of Parliament have considered and approved it. It is true that, under the 1994 Act, there is more detailed parliamentary scrutiny, through Committee procedure, because under that Act, the Secretary of State must report back to Parliament on the extent to which he has taken into account the Committee's report when framing the draft orders. I am prepared to consider adding that provision to this Bill. We shall take account of today's debate and other comments that are made.

The Government are anxious to ensure that local government has the flexibility to develop the vehicles that it needs in order to deliver best value to its citizens. But we also want to ensure that Parliament has the opportunity properly to scrutinise. We must ensure that there are safeguards, to which the hon. Member for Mole Valley referred. I cannot say that we shall always agree on the safeguards that the hon. Gentleman may want, but Parliament should have the opportunity to consider the mattter.

We have had a useful debate. I hope that hon. Members recognise that we are trying to achieve the necessary flexibility and to ensure that Parliament has sufficient powers of scrutiny of whatever steps are taken.
