Mr. Timms: The clause is about the treatment of national insurance contributions for members of stakeholder pension schemes.

As members of the Committee will know, people whose membership of a pension scheme contracts them out of the state earnings related pension scheme are liable for a lower rate of national insurance contributions. The way in which that happens varies according to the type of scheme. Some members pay a lower rate of contributions. Others pay the normal rate but have part of their contributions rebated to the scheme. In some schemes, there is an element of both. I propose to refer simply to rebates, although the mechanisms will vary according to the type of contracted-out scheme.

Stakeholder pension schemes will fall, in legislative terms, into one of the two existing general categories of pension schemes. They will either be occupational pension stakeholder schemes or personal pension stakeholder schemes. As a result, many of the existing legislative arrangements for contracting-out will apply to them automatically. So there is already provision, in the Pension Schemes Act 1993, for the arrangements for contracting out and for rebates to be paid.

However, existing legislation provides only for a single rate of rebate to apply to money purchase occupational schemes and for a separate single rate of rebate to apply to personal pension schemes. Currently, it does not of course make further separate provision for stakeholder schemes, because they are a novelty. The clause provides a power to set different rebates for schemes that are stakeholder schemes as opposed to those that are not.

Rebate rates are set by the Secretary of State in the light of advice from the Government Actuary. The rates of rebate reflect the value of the state benefit forgone by contracting-out. The Government Actuary takes into account, among other things, the expense levels of schemes, which affect the cost of providing an equivalent benefit to that which has been given up.

The expenses that are associated with a scheme are the crucial factor in determining the different rates of rebate that will be applied.

The clause will enable a different rate of rebate to be set for stakeholder pension schemes, if it turns out that it is appropriate to do so. The presence of that power does not mean that we will necessarily set different rebates. Decisions on rebate rates for stakeholder pension schemes will be taken later. We expect to lay the appropriate orders by April 2000 a year in advance of their introduction as is required under existing legislation. However, the clause ensures that a different rate could be set for stakeholder pension schemes if it was judged appropriate to do so.
