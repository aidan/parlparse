It seems to me that my constituent is on a perfectly reasonable and affecting quest. The purpose of the Bill, as outlined by the Secretary of State on Second Reading, is precisely to empower such people. Apart from the democratic importance of Government information, the Bill will be most effective in giving people control over their own lives in a way that they have never had before. In that, the Bill deals with an important problem, but in the case of my constituent there is a danger that the quest for information could be thwarted.

Leaving that example aside, the Data Protection Act requires that, in addition to meeting the general tests of fairness and lawfulness, at least one of the specific conditions in schedule 2 of the Act must be met. One of those is that the individual must consent to the disclosure, and if he or she does not, the disclosure must be shown to be necessary for at least one of a series of purposes. The other condition is to comply with any legal obligation on the authority. Here we move into tricky areas. Is the requirement to comply with the Freedom of Information Bill itself such an obligation? The Bill does not require the disclosure of information which breaches a principle, but this principle permits disclosures which are required by law. That forms a neat paradox, which turns in on itself.

That is the first of the data protection principles, and there are seven more to go. The second principle—I will not go through all seven—says,

    ``Personal data shall be obtained only for one or more specified and lawful purposes, and shall not be further processed in any manner incompatible with that purpose or those purposes.''

How does that leave people such as journalists? If information, including personal information, is collected to monitor the good working of a public body, and a journalist wants such information in a matter of public interest, but that is not the purpose for which the information was collected, will the journalist be thwarted by the relationship between the two measures? In order to decide whether the information is exempt, the authority will have to discover why the applicant wants the information and then assess the applicant's—in this case, the journalist's—intended use of the information and decide whether it is compatible with the purpose of the measure. These are difficult matters, but it is important to consider journalism in this respect and I should be grateful for the Minister's comments.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. David Lock): I am grateful to my hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central for tabling the amendment because it raises an important issue about the inter-relationship between the data protection principles and freedom of information.

The effect of amendment Nos. 148 and 149 taken together would be that the exemptions for personal information relating to a third party in clause 38 would no longer apply in relation to certain classes of people if the only grounds for such exemption were that any disclosure would identify an individual or related to an identifiable individual. It would deem that such disclosures are not contrary to data protection principles or to section 10 of the Data Protection Act. The classes of people covered by the amendment are public officials, public contractors and any individual who is communicating with a public authority on behalf of an organisation, described as a representative of an organisation. The terms ``public official'' and ``public contractor'' are defined to include any individual appointed by a public official to any body and any individual employed by or providing services under a contract to a public contractor in relation to such a contract.

I recognise the concerns that have given rise to this amendment, which echoes a concern raised by Elizabeth France in her evidence to the House of Commons Scrutiny Committee during consultation on the draft Bill published last year. She said then that she was concerned about what she saw as constraints within the Bill which could limit the effectiveness with which access would be made available to personal information as it related to individuals acting in their public capacities.

I explain that concern by referring to my hon. Friend's example of an unfortunate incident in which a constituent was allegedly sexually assaulted by a nurse in the course of his or her duties. Rightly, that individual seeks disclosure of the information. To what extent could the employee seek to block the disclosure of that information on data protection grounds and thereby stop the freedom of information principles applying to give that disclosure? We are alive to the issue, but I am not sure that the amendment, which was no doubt carefully drafted with that in mind, would deal with the problem, for two reasons. First, new subsection (5A) restricts the exemption to acts of public officials acting in an official capacity. It is arguable that, if a nurse sexually assaulted a patient on the ward, the nurse was not acting in an official capacity, but directly contrary to what he or she was supposed to be doing as a nurse. Therefore, it may not fall within the terms of the amendment.

Secondly, subsection (5B) rightly refers to the need to hold confidential issues relating to an individual's disciplinary record. Clearly, the vast majority of disciplinary records of individual employees are personal and should not be disclosed. However, if a complaint was lodged and a health authority investigated the conduct of a nurse in such circumstances, such information would be relevant to a disciplinary inquiry. Therefore, even within the terms of the amendment, unfortunately, such information would not be disclosed, although I can understand why my hon. Friend's constituent would want it to be disclosed. The nub of the problem is the difficulty of definition.

We are anxious about third party requests for access to personal information. At present, the Bill treats all requests for information in the same way; it makes no distinction between information that relates to an official's public activities and that which relates to his activities as a private individual. The amendment seeks to draw that distinction.

The law relating to the processing of personal information is laid down in the Data Protection Act. A request that is made to a public authority for personal data relating to a third party will be considered by reference to the principles set out in the Act, as my hon. Friend has said. They are contained in schedule 1 of the Act. As the hon. Member for Ryedale noted, the Act gives effect in the United Kingdom to the EC directive on data protection.

The Government believe that the amendment contains a problem in that respect. The distinction between public and private personal data, which the amendments seek to establish, is not explicitly recognised in the directive. Accordingly, if the Government accepted the amendment, the United Kingdom would risk finding itself in breach of EU obligations. It would not be sensible to base legislation on anything other than a firm understanding that it is consistent with our international obligations. To do so would be to invite an unwelcome challenge in the courts.

In respect of the example that I discussed with my hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central, it is unclear whether his amendment would cure the problem that he highlighted. He rightly asked whether recital 72 of the European directive on data protection provided flexibility, and he was right to say that it provides for some flexibility on the distinction between public and private information. The Government believe that it informs the interpretation of the principles and that it cannot be reduced to a simple formula such as that proposed by the amendment. Its effect on the interpretation of basic data protection principles may differ according to whether such protection concerns public officials or a private person.

During pre-legislative scrutiny of the draft Bill, the Government gave a commitment to consult with Mrs. France on that point. We discussed possible resolutions for the concerns expressed by her and others about the divide between personal and private activities. We have consulted, and we continue to believe that it would be inappropriate to write into the primary legislation a requirement whose legality would be doubtful to say the least, bearing in mind all of the circumstances in which it would apply under the Act. However, taking into account my hon. Friend's observations on recital 72, we agree with Mrs. France that there may be some room to interpret the principles of the directive differently from those of the Data Protection Act when dealing with information of the sort addressed by the amendment.

As the Information Commissioner, Mrs. France may want to consider giving authorities guidance on the considerations that they must take into account when responding to applications. We believe that the Bill provides the right way forward.

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I beg to move amendment No. 155, in page 22, line 25, leave out subsection (2) and insert—

    `(2) Information is exempt information if it is information—

    (a) which the person from whom it was obtained consistently treats as confidential, and

    (b) the disclosure of which would cause significant financial loss to that person.

    (2A) Information is exempt information if it is information which relates to the financial affairs of the public authority which holds it, and—

    (a) that authority consistently treats it as confidential, and

    (b) its disclosure would cause significant financial loss to the authority.'.

We now come to commercial confidence. The Liberal Democrats have some substantial concerns about the drafting of the clause, principally because it is so lacking in definition. The clause also lacks tests to ensure that what is described as a commercial confidence should remain confidential when that status runs contrary to the interests of consumers and the general public. I am indebted to the Consumers Association, which has said that the Government are widely out of kilter with public opinion. It has conducted a survey in which 86 per cent. of the people questioned thought that the public's right to know should outweigh the commercial interests of companies or public authorities. Only 5 per cent. of them disagreed with that view. There is widespread concern that commercial interests should not have a special exemption placing them outside the realm in which the usual rules on disclosure of information apply.

Exemption of commercial interests is an increasing problem. The number of matters about which consumers have genuine grievances against companies or commercial interests is increasing. Consumers need information if they are to take on what may be substantial vested interests. Although information relating to such interests may be in the hands of a regulator or a public authority, the clause ensures that it may not have to be disclosed as a matter of course.

There are five principal objections to the clause. First, I should mention the nature of the information dealt with and its source. The Bill covers information derived from any source and contains no sense that detected information is in the ownership of any commercial interest involved. One could ask whether other circumstances might apply, but there are many obvious examples. Public authorities undertaking test regimes or a variety of investigations could discover information about a product or a commercial activity that is prejudicial to commercial interests but which should be released. An authority that wishes to avoid causing embarrassment or damage to a commercial interest with a regional or local significance may be tempted to apply the exemption test. I believe that that approach is incorrect.

Secondly, the information need not even be confidential; it may have been supplied in confidential terms, but it may also have been supplied to others in a different context. Information that is common knowledge among members of an industry might not be so well known to consumer groups or members of the public. That could be considered exempt.

Thirdly, the exemption puts the avoidance of commercial prejudice above all other considerations. Companies or authorities that behave badly should be prejudiced. That is a simple argument. I am sure that it is not the Government's intention—it is certainly not in the interests of consumers or the general public—to allow information to remain exempt if it reflects badly on a company. There are many instances in which malpractice is improperly protected by the application of the term commercial confidentiality. I hope that the Bill will prevent that.

Fourthly, a binding public interest test is absent. It is not hard to imagine circumstances in which the collusion between a public authority and a company invokes the cloak of commercial confidentiality. The test is discretionary and can be used to prevent embarrassment. For example, it could be applied if the disclosure of a commercial business's continuing activity reveals that a regulatory body, which has failed to prevent a harmful situation from arising, is complacent or negligent. Another example is the approval of arms exports. Many hon. Members know that it is sometimes convenient for Ministers and Departments to collude with an arms exporter to avoid publicity on their activities. Indeed the Under-Secretary might refer to relevant material in the Scott report.

Freedom of information legislation in many other countries includes a public interest test as part of commercial confidentiality exemptions. Irish legislation contains two enforceable public interest tests. The first is a general test in which

    ``the public interest would, on balance, be better served by granting than by refusing to grant the request''.

The second provision requires disclosure where it is

    ``necessary in order to avoid a serious and imminent danger to the life or health of an individual or to the environment''.

That qualifies the general test on commercial confidentiality.

Finally, the test of prejudice is weak. American freedom of information legislation applies if a disclosure

    ``would cause substantial harm to the competitive position of the person from whom the information was obtained''.

What is the difference between that and the Government's proposal? First, the clause uses the White Paper's substantial harm test, which we believe is stronger than the prejudice test, although it would be foolish to embark on that debate. Secondly, it applies only when the harm is to a person's competitive position.
