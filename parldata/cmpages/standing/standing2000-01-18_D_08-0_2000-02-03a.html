Clause 74

Possession: onus of proof

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Simon Hughes: I want to raise a predictable question on the clause, which makes another reverse burden of proof provision. Lord Lloyd made the point in paragraph 16.24 of his report that

    ``Although this has not proved to be a particularly controversial provision (I have heard no complaints about its use) it should be possible to rely on the ordinary law to deal with any such cases once a lasting peace is established in Northern Ireland.''

The argument has again been put to me that the onus should always be on the prosecution to prove a case beyond reasonable doubt and that if one goes in the other direction, one may risk breaching article 6.2 of the European convention on human rights, which states that everyone charged with a criminal offence should in law be presumed innocent until proved guilty.

In our previous sitting we discussed the Kebeline case. The House of Lords implied that a reverse burden of proof alone might not be sufficient to create a risk in terms of the law. No one argues that the offences covered by the clause are not serious ones. I do not want to overrate the importance of offences under the Explosive Substances Act 1883 to do with the possession of explosives and petrol bombs and with the manufacturing and handling of weapons. There is no argument about the seriousness of the matters in question, but it would help if the Government were to put on record their case for the reverse burden of proof provisions being continued in part VII.

Mr. Ingram: Clause 74 makes provision about the onus of proof in trials on indictment for offences of possession of firearms and explosives. When an accused is charged with possessing firearms or explosives, proof that he was present in the same premises as those articles, or that they were on his premises, is sufficient evidence of possession, unless the person charged can show that he had no knowledge or control of them. The provision was introduced to facilitate proof of knowledge as well as proof of possession when knowledge is relevant. Defendants have been acquitted by claiming no knowledge of articles found in their possession. Those acquittals were the subject of comment, and the clause was introduced as a result.

Some people would contend that the clause puts an unfair burden on the accused. The hon. Gentleman's amendment was moved on that basis. The accused— perhaps only the accused—knows whether he had knowledge of the existence of the articles and, if he knew of their existence, whether he had any control over them. The judgment of the late Lord Lowry in the case of Crown v. Killen in 1974 was that the provision allowed a prima facie case of possession or knowledge of possession. However, the judge made it clear that the burden of proof remained at all times on the prosecution, which must prove possession beyond reasonable doubt. The law is applied on the back of that judgment. The clause is subject to scrutiny and examination, to decide whether it is justified, and reviews of the Emergency Provisions Acts have always recommended the retention of that provision.

Question put and agreed to.
