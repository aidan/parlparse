There is a clear distinction between requiring people to attend an interview as part of an initial or renewed claim for benefit and requiring them to take part in the options for training, work experience and so on in the new deal programme. There is compulsion on people to take part in the new deal for young people, but there is no compulsion on people to take part in the new deal for lone parents, nor will there be when the single work-focused gateway is in operation. So I trust that that clarifies matters for the benefit of the hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar and, indeed, for the public.

Government amendments Nos. 225 to 228 will improve the provisions that set out the consequences for failing to attend a work-focused interview. The amendments are needed to provide the right incentives to ensure compliance with the requirement to take part in interviews and to ensure that people on benefit receive help as soon as possible.

The Committee will be aware that the clause contains the requirements to take part in work-focused interviews in three main circumstances. The majority of claimants will be asked to take part in an initial interview at the point of claim. As has been said, an interview at the outset may not be appropriate in a small number of cases and the interview may be deferred until a later date. In addition, further interviews may take place throughout an individual's time on benefit, following the occurrence of certain trigger events that have an impact on a person's employability.

We are concerned that the clause as drafted fails to provide the right incentives to ensure compliance with the requirement to take part in work-focused interviews. In particular, it would have created an anomaly in that those required to take part in their initial work-focused interview at the point of claim who did not do so would receive no benefit, but those who had that interview deferred would suffer only a reduction in their entitlement.

Under amendment No. 225, a consistent approach will be achieved for all first interviews, whether or not they are deferred. Thus the claim of someone who fails to take part in a deferred initial interview will be terminated. In those circumstances, benefit will be reinstated only on the receipt of a new claim, for which participation in a work-focused interview will be required.

That is entirely consistent with the change in culture in the benefits system that we want to achieve through the single gateway. I am pleased about the amount of cross-party support in the Committee for that change. Before I attended the Committee, I addressed a conference for officials, advisers and others from single work-focused gateway pilot schemes. I was pleased to be able to tell them about the degree of bipartisan support in the Committee for what we want to achieve through the single work-focused gateway. That support is important for such a radical reform of the welfare system—the wider the consensus, the better its chance of working successfully.

I am sure that the staff that I addressed appreciated my communicating to them the support that we have received from Opposition Members. That reinforces from the outset the message that people have a right to state support, but also a responsibility to take reasonable steps to take advantage of the help that they are offered. For that reason, we need to ensure that a stronger incentive is in place to encourage people to attend their initial interview, whether or not it is deferred.

Once the new routine has been established, we believe that the requirement to attend a work-focused interview will be seen as a normal part of continuing entitlement. The requirement to fill in forms was mentioned in exchanges with the hon. Member for Newbury. That requirement is not seen as a threat, so there is no reason why the requirement to attend an interview, especially such a helpful one, should be seen as a threat.

Unlike the Opposition amendments, which were largely probing, our amendments will strike the right balance. Amendments Nos. 245, 255 and 256 seek to ensure that failure to take part in initial and triggered interviews should lead to the immediate termination of benefit. However, as I explained earlier, the single gateway is about achieving a culture change in the benefits system. We want to place the clearest incentives to comply in respect of all first interviews. However, we expect that most people, once they have had that first interview, will welcome the amount of support that can be provided and adjust to the new relationship that is required of them as part of the welfare system. Going further and ensuring that people face an immediate loss of benefit for non-compliance, throughout the lifetime of their claim, is therefore likely to be unnecessary and counter-productive.

Amendment No. 254 also attempts to strengthen the sanctions regime by preventing an individual who has failed to take part in a work-focused interview at the point of claim from making more than one new claim for benefit in the same calendar year. The hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar did not talk about that—

Mr. Pickles: I was broken off, but I spoke about it before lunch.

Mr. Smith: The hon. Gentleman got through a lot quickly in the brief period in which he talked; I apologise for having missed it. It is therefore all the more appropriate that I take some time to explain why the amendment is not a good idea.

An individual who failed to participate in two interviews would not be eligible to claim again until the next calendar year and would be left completely without benefit until that time. That could lead to real financial hardship, with no remedy available to the claimant to relieve it. There is a crucial difference between that and what we are proposing. Under our proposals, the claimant could at any time relieve any threat of hardship by undertaking an interview and legitimately establishing a claim to benefit. The amendment would have perverse effects because the penalty would be completely arbitrary, depending on which point in the calendar year the failure occurred. A second failure in January would lead to a much more severe penalty than a similar failure in December. That cannot be right. The amendment could also prevent someone who failed to participate in a first interview but who subsequently attended a second from claiming another benefit even if he or she became entitled to do so later that year. I shall not dwell further on the point because the hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar was probing and we do not need to expose every technical weakness.

The single gateway and our amendments will provide claimants with a better, more efficient and more integrated service, giving them access to a wide range of advice and support. People have a right to expect help, but in return they have a responsibility to discuss the help that is available. We believe that most claimants will welcome that opportunity.

I hope that the Committee will agree that our amendments will create the right balance of rights and responsibilities, while being flexible enough to deal with a wide range of clients in different circumstances. I commend them to the Committee.
