Mr. Tim Loughton (East Worthing and Shoreham): The Government would not expect me to let this part of the Finance Bill go by without referring to the plight of the English wine industry and, yet again, the discriminatory nature of the proposals. Your predecessors, Mr. Butterfill, will recall that last year we debated at length various subjects, including English wine brands such as Breaky Bottom. The Economic Secretary even chipped in with her own contribution about Irn-Bru. However, last week's reference to Lambrini bianco by Liberal Democrat Members surpassed that.

The matter is especially important this year because last year, when I mentioned the discriminatory nature of excise duty on wine and the way in which it affects the English wine industry, I referred to Lamberhurst Priory in Kent, one of the pioneering vineyards in the revival of the English wine industry. Since last year, the Lamberhurst vineyard one of the largest and oldest vineyards in this country has gone bust. It cites as a major contributory reason for its demise the high level of duty charged on wine, which set it at a disadvantage to foreign-made wines and imported wines from the continent. It is extraordinary that some clauses will give preferential treatment to British industries such as the Scotch whisky industry while the English wine industry is left completely to the devices of competition with producers on the continent who get much more favourable treatment on duty.

I shall give some examples of how continental countries treat their wine industries. In the United Kingdom, the typical duty on a litre of still table wine up to a strength of 22 per cent. is £1.44 which, in continental terms, is 1.75 ecu. In France, the duty on a litre of wine is the equivalent of just threepence, or 0.039 ecu. In Germany, the duty is nil, or nil ecu. In Austria, it is nil; in Italy, it is nil; and in Portugal, it is nil. Yet in this country we charge about £1.40 a litre, whether it is an English wine that is produced, grown and bottled in this country or whether it is imported from France, Germany, Austria or Italy. Some French wines sell for less than the duty charged on a bottle of wine in England. That cannot be fair competition. Yet spirits are certainly not banded at nil value in their continental partner countries. In France, typical duty on a litre of Scotch is £3.78, and in Germany it is £3.43. Only the wine industry is treated favourably by all the other continental Governments who have domestic wine industries. So there is an unlevel playing field and I fear that this provision in the Finance Bill, yet again this year, does absolutely nothing to level that playing field. This is a double whammy, a move against the English domestic wine industry, which increasingly harms the UK wine market.

My hon. Friends the Members for Witney (Mr. Woodward) and for Cotswold (Mr. Clifton-Brown) spelt out in detail the absurdity of the situation; the bought-in-France, drunk-in-Britain trend is reducing the amount of revenue going to the Treasury and, at the same time, giving unfair competition advantage to our partners on the continent. That was why the last Government froze duty on wine in 1995 we saw it as a diminishing return to the Revenue, which was counterproductive. Since then, there has been a reduction in the official level of wine consumption in the UK. So the clause will be harmful to the trade, harmful to the pubs and clubs that sell wine and contribute £11 billion per annum to the Exchequer, and certainly harmful to English wine producers.

Why are the Government singling out wine, particularly English wine, for unfair treatment? Is wine drinking too popular? Official figures show that the consumption of wine has been declining compared with consumption of other alcoholic beverages during the past few years. Consumption of wine per head in France is a lofty 62.5 litres a year. In Italy, it is 52 litres and in Portugal, 52.5 litres. Even in Austria, where they add curious substances to their wine, consumption totals 31 litres a year, but in the UK, only 14.7 litres of wine are drunk roughly a quarter of the equivalent wine consumption in France.

The UK wine industry should be encouraged, and wine drinking is a form of alcoholic consumption that should be encouraged. It is the only natural alcoholic drink, which does not require a heated fermentation process or distilling. It has health-giving properties, as we are frequently told by medical reports. To back that up, a recent survey of deaths from liver cirrhosis on the continent showed that, in deaths per 100,000 of the population, the top country was Austria with 29 out of 100,000 probably due to what they put in their alcoholic drinks. In Portugal, there were 27.2 deaths per 100,000 and in Italy, 26.6. But in England and Wales, only 5.6 deaths per 100,000 were caused by alcohol. So deaths as a result of liver cirrhosis in this country are well below average, and we can afford to allow them to increase a little without having a major health hazard.

Lest Labour Members are surprised at that information, I should add that the figures for Scotland are not quite such good news. In Scotland, the figure is almost double that in England and Wales 9.3 deaths per 100,000 of the population caused by liver cirrhosis but of course they do drink other things apart from wine in Scotland. Indeed, the manufacture of Irn-Bru, in the constituency of the Economic Secretary to the Treasury, may be a contributory cause.

If wine drinking is not a health hazard, does it contribute towards crime and disorder? Again, the research would suggest not. In this country, we have the highest level of non-abstainers from alcohol 89.5 per cent. in the western world. In Germany, 86 per cent. of the population are non-abstainers; in Spain, the figure is 85 per cent.
