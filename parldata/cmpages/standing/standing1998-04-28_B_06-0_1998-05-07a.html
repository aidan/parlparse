Clause 5

Appeals against orders

10.30 am

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I beg to move amendment No. 332, in page 5, line 41, at end insert--

    `(2A) In making an order under subsection (2)(a) above in the case of a sex offender order the Crown Court may make any order which could have been made by the Magistrates' Court and may vary any order which was made by a Magistrates' Court.'.

The Chairman: With this, we may take amendment No. 333, in page 5, line 46, at end insert--

    `(3A) Without prejudice to the power of the Crown Court to give leave for an appeal to be abandoned, in the case of an appeal against the making by a Magistrates' Court of an anti-social behaviour order an appellant may abandon an appeal by giving notice in writing at any time before the hearing of the appeal.'.

Mr. Clappison: Amendment No. 332 concerns what happens when there is an appeal to the Crown court against a sex offender order from the magistrates court. When the Crown court upholds such an order, will it be able to impose any additional terms that it thinks fit after hearing details of the case? Will it be in the same position as the magistrates court in the first hearing, or will it be restricted to confirming the terms of the order that the magistrates court made? We want the Crown court to be able to impose any terms that it thinks fit to protect the public.

Amendment No. 333 deals with a small point. Will the same rules of court for criminal cases apply to the Crown court against anti-social behaviour orders from the magistrates court? There are different sorts of appeals from magistrates courts to Crown courts; sometimes non-criminal cases are appealed. I shall understand if the Minister is unable to provide a considered reponse today, and wishes to write to me instead.

We are also worried about liability for costs. In an appeal to the Crown court, an appellant may be made liable for costs unless he abandons the appeal by giving three days' notice in writing. Amendment No. 333 would increase the time during which an appellant could abandon the appeal. There may be many appeals against anti-social behaviour orders, for reasons that we have already outlined and that I do not propose to repeat. In those circumstances, there should be flexibility for appellants who wish to abandon their appeals. Costs should not weigh too heavily on their minds. We wish to avoid appeals that appellants wished they had abandoned but with which they felt obliged to go ahead because they believed that they were already liable for costs.

We want to promote the most efficient use of court time. If an appellant to the Crown court has second thoughts, as sometimes happens, he should not simply plough ahead with the appeal and thus take up court time becuase he believes that he will be liable for costs. We invite the Government to consider allowing an appellant to abandon an appeal at any time up to the calling on of an appeal in court so that he does not feel constrained by costs. An appellant would be protected against a costs order by abandoning the appeal.

Perhaps the Minister will write to me if he does not wish to reply now. There may be some factors in my explanation that he did not apprehend, and I understand if he prefers to write.

Mr. Humfrey Malins (Woking): I shall set out briefly points that the Minister may wish to tackle in writing. Is legal aid available for an appeal to the Crown court? Can legal aid be granted by the magistrates court or the Crown court to an appellant? I am sure that that is so, but can the Minister confirm that important point of principle?

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): I am grateful to the hon. Gentlemen for the way in which they made their points, which we clearly need to consider.

I agree with the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison), who leads for the Opposition, that Crown courts need the powers that amendment No. 332 sets out. However, the amendment is unnecessary, because it is clear from clause 5(3) that the Crown court already has them. Under clause 5(2)(a) the Crown court

    ``may make such orders as may be necessary to give effect to its determination of the appeal''.

That would allow either a new set of prohibitions--provided that those fulfilled the criteria in clause 2(6)--or variation of existing ones, by amending an order or making a new one.

Section 48(2) of the Supreme Court Act 1981 applies. To enable the Crown court, on hearing any appeal, to confirm, reverse or vary any part of the decision that is being appealed against; to remit the matter, with its opinion, to the authority or court that made that decision; or to make any such other order as it thinks fit, thereby exercising any power that the other authority might have exercised. That provision would apply to both anti-social behaviour and sex offender orders. That is why the amendment is unnecessary. The hon. Member for Hertsmere tabled it quite properly as a probing amendment, and I hope that my response has dealt with his queries.

Amendment No. 333 is also unnecessary. An appellant would already be able to abandon an appeal by the means set out in the amendment, under rule 11(1) of the Crown court rules 1982, except that that requires notice to be given not later than the third day before the day fixed for the hearing of the appeal. That would apply to both anti-social behaviour and sex offender orders.

We expect that under the Crown court rules the normal provisions as to costs would apply. I understand the hon. Gentleman's argument that there might be some constraint on someone who wanted to abandon an appeal after the three-day limit. Some constraint is useful. The setting up of an appeal which is then cancelled entails costs, and when I and the hon. Gentleman practised law we no doubt were both aware that all too often appeals were cancelled at the last moment. We need to ensure that the court can decide whether costs should apply after considering all the circumstances. I should not want to prevent the court from imposing any constraint that it might want to impose in the case of anyone who could have written an appeal before the three-day limit but chose not to. Removing the three-day limit would be an encouragement to wait until the last possible opportunity before withdrawing an appeal.

I am not without sympathy with the hon. Gentleman's argument. The court's discretion as to costs would to some extent deal with the matter.

I shall consider the hon. Gentleman's points further, and shall write to him about any matters that seem to warrant that.

Mr. Clappison: What about legal aid?

Mr. O'Brien: Legal aid will be available as normal for appeals in this context, and we do not propose to place constraints on the normal legal aid that would apply.

Mr. Clappison: I am content with the Minister's response to amendment No. 332. He knows why it was tabled.

I agree, with respect to amendment No. 333, that the costs thrown away in bringing an appeal at all must be borne in mind. However, they should be weighed against the costs of taking up court time on appeals that might have been abandoned if an appellant had not felt constrained by the possibility of being made liable for costs in any event. The Minister indicates that he is prepared to think again about the matter. That was the purpose of the amendment. I look forward to his further consideration of the matter, and hope that, if necessary, he will write to me.

On that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: Clause 5 provides for an appeal against anti-social behaviour orders and sex offender orders. That is an essential part of the fair and proper operation of the orders in clauses 2 and 3.

The appeal is to the Crown court. When the Crown court makes an order on appeal, it will be treated as if it had been made by the magistrates' court for any subsequent application for variation or discharge.

The provisions of the clause are necessary to provide proper recourse for the defendant against any civil order made against him.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 5 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
