Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): Amendment No. 13 is designed to ensure, on the one hand, that the authority does not have an extremely dominant chairman, with the other two or more members being essentially rubber stamps, or, on the other hand, that we do not finish up with a body that suffers from inertia.

To tack on a few non-executive directors with an advisory role does not necessarily add much to the authority's impact and effectiveness. Even if a few non-executives with expertise and experience turn up part time, they will not necessarily be able to offer the commitment and the availability that ensure that the authority is genuine, plural and effective. Full-time executive directors would offer such commitment and could provide a balance to a chairman who was too powerful or too idiosyncratic.

We have seen cases in the past where the character, skills and impact of a chairman of a regulatory body have brought a distinct flavour to its operation. That may be the Government's intention, in which case we do not need to bother with the other people appointed, but if the authority is to have a coherent, balanced and well-rounded policy, the other directors appointed in accordance with the schedule must have the clout, the time and the authority to play their part effectively.

Important technical issues are emerging as the technology in the energy and telecommunications markets changes. It will be valuable to bring in people who operate in those markets in a variety of ways. Non-executive directors will bring a breadth of experience that will be invaluable given the scope of economic activity for which the regulators are responsible.

There are unlikely to be sufficient executive posts to accommodate the optimum number of members for each authority, in terms of the demand for their skills and perspective. Many highly qualified people who could give of their expertise to a regulatory authority may be unable or unwilling to accept full-time executive posts. Alternatively, their skills may be better suited to strategic matters—they may have a knowledge of strategic issues connected to the industry acquired over a lifetime. They may have reached a stage in their career at which they do not wish to hold a full-time executive post, but it would be a tragedy for an industry as important as this to lose those skills.

The industry requires the knowledge and experience of people who have an overview not only of the industry but of society. It would be wrong to bar such individuals from playing a part in the new regulatory authorities. The appointing of non-executive directors will give a more corporate flavour to decision making. The hon. Member for Hazel Grove spoke of the danger of the chairman being a dominant personality. I believe that non-executive directors would be a counterbalance to such a chairman, or any other executive or non-executive member of the regulatory authority. Strong, competent non-executive directors will provide that balance. Given the benefits of appointing non-executive directors, it would be wrong to adopt a measure that restricted the scope of the Secretary of State to do so.

I shall address the claim made by the hon.
