Mr. Paice: I was addressing the use of the word ``natural'' to describe features in respect of which landowners' liability is removed. Amendment No. 232 seeks to remove the word ``natural'' from the clause, and amendment No. 322 seeks to define the features in question without using that word.

I mentioned the historical issue involved in the use of the word ``natural''. The Country Landowners Association has stated:

    ``Natural features'' is not a recognised term and there will be difficulties for the occupier, the public and ultimately for the courts, in deciding what is and is not ``natural''. For example, lakes can be natural or man-made and have been in existence for generations—when does the artificial lake become natural?

That statement deals with the nub of our main concern, which is shared by others who are worried about implementing the Bill. What is natural? For me, it is something that has existed for ever and a day and in whose creation mankind has had no involvement. Man-made features include lakes, reservoirs, iron age forts, old or modern peat diggings, which are common in some areas, and grouse butts. They also include more obvious features, such as fences, stone walls, gates, stiles, dams and path surfaces that have been created specifically to minimise erosion. Those features are all man-made and are therefore clearly not included in the Bill's ambit with regard to the removal of liability that the Minister keeps professing. I challenge the Government on what they mean by the term ``natural''. One can think of all manner of examples in the hills and uplands which would cause difficulty under the Bill. Upland areas contain many reservoirs. Nobody could maintain that reservoirs are natural, but it seems daft that liability will not apply to falling into a natural lake but it will in relation to an artificial one. That approach seems inconsistent.

The huge power station at Llanberis is close to the locality of the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd). What was a natural lake has been massively enhanced and its water level rises and falls by many feet twice a day. Above the lake is another lake in the high mountains—I cannot for the life of me remember its name—which is the header tank for the power station, which is also located on the higher ground. That lake also rises and falls. It was natural, but it has been seriously changed by mankind.

That example illustrates the absurdity of using the word ``natural'' in the Bill and the huge difficulty that lawyers and courts will encounter in trying to ascertain what it means. I suspect that most people would think that the landowner should be liable in relation to a feature that has been established in the past few years and which clearly presents a risk. However, the term ``natural'' implies that anything done by mankind since the year dot is outside the removal of liability ambit.

That is why amendment No. 322 seeks to define types of features that the clause should cover. The list is not exhaustive, but it contains examples of features that are not put in place with any nefarious motive of trying to cause injury. Many such features have been in place for many hundreds of years. Indeed, some ancient monuments have existed for thousands of years. It is wholly improper to expect the landowner of today to be liable to people who injure themselves on those features. I suspect that the Minister will respond that such liability applies now. That is perfectly true, but the purpose of the Bill is to encourage more people to gain access to the countryside. If they are to do that, the prospect of many more landowners being sued by many more people will open up. That is inevitable if far more people are to roam the countryside than at present. However careful they may be, more will hurt themselves. Under the Bill they will virtually all be able to sue the landowner for having done so. That is a major impost on landowners for features that happen to be on their land but which they did not necessarily have constructed. Clause 13 needs a radical rethink, not least because the Government have so far failed to explain the central term, ``natural feature''.

Amendment No. 360 seeks to ensure that the rules governing charities' actions do not stand in the way of their dedicating land to public access, even if that is not one of their objectives. I am advised that charities already have some freedom in that respect. In general, a charity's trustees can already take actions that they believe are in the best interests of the charity, as long as those actions are consistent with the charity's constitutional objects and powers. Trustees can make a dedication if they can justify that it is in the best interests of the charity. However, I would be unwilling to go further by, in effect, changing the constitutional objects and powers of charities. To take an extreme and unlikely example, I do not believe that the Bill should enable a charity to dedicate land to public access when the charity's constitution expressly forbids it.

We sympathise with the objectives behind amendment No. 360, but cannot include it in the Bill. However, we will consider amendment No. 359 further.
