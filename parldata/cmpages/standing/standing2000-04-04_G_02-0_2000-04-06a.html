Clause 3

Functions of the Secretary of State

Mr. Nick Hawkins (Surrey Heath): I beg to move amendment No. 31, in page 2, line 24, at end add—

    `(5) The Secretary of State shall lay before each House of Parliament the conditions on which he makes any payment under this section.'.

I welcome you to the Chair, Sir David. We are glad to be under your chairmanship this morning. This is a probing amendment. It would require the Secretary of State to lay before Parliament the conditions under which he makes conditional payments to a person for the purposes of fulfilling the functions listed in clause 1. What sort of payments may be made under clause 3 and for what purpose will they be made?

When discussing taxpayers' money, it is vital to discover exactly how much the Government envisage might be paid out, as well as to whom it will be paid. Will it be paid only to local boards or is there a list of people to whom payments will be made including, for example, those in the voluntary sector? Given that subsection (2) allows for ``any payment'' to be made to ``any person'', we must know such details. It would also be helpful if the Minister could explain what conditions will be attached to such payments. Will they be permanent transfers of cash or will they be in the nature of an interest-free loan? Most importantly, what sort of public or parliamentary scrutiny will be given to the payments? It would be helpful if part of the conditions under which payments were made was subject to proper scrutiny. I am sure that the Minister can give detailed answers to such questions.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Paul Boateng): I join the hon. Member for Surrey Heath (Mr. Hawkins) in welcoming you to the Chair, Sir David, on this bright and sunny morning, especially as you bring such glad tidings about our future sittings.

This is a significant amendment in that it seeks clarification that I am only too happy to give about the funding arrangements. Clause 3 is important because it will change fundamentally the current arrangements under which area probation services receive a grant. At present, 80 per cent. of the services are funded by the Home Office, while the remaining 20 per cent. are funded by local authorities. Such arrangements have been cumbersome to administer and they significantly blur the lines of financial accountability. We want to allow the Secretary of State to make payments to whoever he considers appropriate to deliver the functions that are set out in clause 1 and which we debated extensively at our previous sitting.

Local boards will be funded. However, the hon. Gentleman was right that, under the clause, payments can be made to other organisations, including the voluntary sector to which work has been contracted out. The clause gives the Secretary of State the opportunity, if it is needed, to control how the money is spent by setting conditions, regulating the purpose for which sums may be used and requiring payments to be made in certain circumstances. Those powers could be used to ring-fence expenditure to ensure, for example, that local boards spend a minimum amount on staff training or research. The advantages are clear—it is a national service and we would not want undue and unhelpful discrepancies in relation to amounts of spending on staff training. Indeed, certain types of training might be regarded as important at particular times. The hon. Gentleman asked for examples of where a ring fence might be appropriate. In relation to drug treatment and training orders, for example, infrastructure development requires the application of specific sums of money. Where that is a priority, it will be necessary to ensure that money is spent on the purpose for which it is intended.

The hon. Member for Surrey Heath asked about repayment, which is most likely to arise where a significant amount is required to be spent on capital infrastructure. It is important that the probation service should develop information technology that will enable it to work effectively with other partners in the criminal justice system. Capital spending may be necessary, and it can be made clear that the organisation that received funding for the purpose, whether a local board or a contractor, will refund the cost of the equipment if it stops doing the work for which it was provided. That is an example of the circumstances in which repayment might be necessary.

On the level of funding and the sums to be expended, an extra £127 million was found for the probation service during the comprehensive spending review period, which is worth £159 million when grossed up to include the local authority contribution. Those are considerable additional sums, which are justified by the increasing work load of the service and its capacity to respond to it. It is important that funding arrangements are subject to proper accountability, which clause 3 provides.

I understand the probing nature of the amendment, but it would put a needless constraint on the Secretary of State's freedom to manage the new service effectively. Ring-fencing is eminently sensible where it is necessary to support initiatives to deliver specific Government policies, of which accredited offender programmes might be another example. The amendment would make that more difficult and cumbersome.

Mr. Hawkins: It sounded as though the Minister was about to deal with this point towards the end of what he was proposing to say. He will appreciate that the wording of the clause is remarkably wide. It gives the Secretary of State unfettered power and is much more open than is usually seen in statutes. Does he contemplate or will he leave the door ajar for any kind of parliamentary scrutiny? Might the clause be amended by a Government amendment in another place?

Mr. Boateng: No, I do not think so. I believe that the flexibility is necessary. Expenditure by Government Departments in that way is always subject to proper accountability to Parliament through the Audit Commission, the National Audit Office and so on. The NAO has proved very effective in the past. Civil servants and Ministers always have its strictures and supervision in mind and no special measures will be needed in this regard.

Mr. Hawkins: I am grateful to the Minister for his response to my intervention. This is a matter to which we may need to return on Report, and perhaps in another place. I repeat that I find it a remarkably widely drawn power when one is speaking of taxpayers' money. The power to make any payment to any person for any purpose is rather wider than one is used to seeing in statute. However, I shall not press the matter to a Division today because it was a probing amendment. We have heard what the Minister has said, but those who come to look at the proceedings may not regard his reply as particularly reassuring in the context of potentially large sums of taxpayers' money. To assist the Minister and his officials, we have pointed out that this is a subject to which we may return, and they may wish to think again. However, for today I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 4

Local boards

Mr. Hawkins: I beg to move amendment No. 32, in Clause 4, page 3, line 1, leave out subsection (6).

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 110, in page 3, line 2, at end add

    `, which may only be made if a draft of the statutory instrument containing the order has been laid before and approved by resolution of each House of Parliament'.

Mr. Hawkins: Amendment No. 32 seeks to delete subsection (6) of clause 4. It is in a rather different category from the previous amendment. We are not merely probing; we believe that these are very serious matters. Clause 4 provides that the boundaries of the local boards shall be the same as for police forces. Reference was made in our first sitting to the fact that there was some logic in having coterminosity, as it is called, for the boundaries. However, the subsection would allow the Home Secretary to redraw boundaries of probation service boards simply by order. Clause 58 makes it clear that that will be a negative resolution power.

Although the Bill has used the model of police force areas for the local probation boards, the Police and Magistrates' Courts Act 1994 dealing with the police does not contain a similarly sweeping power for the Home Secretary to alter the boundaries of police force areas, and we do not believe that the Home Secretary should be given such a power in relation to the probation service board boundaries.

The 1994 Act provided for an order-making power for the Home Secretary to alter police force area boundaries, but that power is only exercisable on the request of police authorities or if it appears to the Secretary of State of the day expedient

    in the interests of efficiency and effectiveness.

In that event, under the 1994 Act the Secretary of State would first have to give notice to local councils and others of the changes that he proposed to make in relation to a police force area boundary, and to set out his reasons. The power of the Secretary of State to change the boundaries in relation to police force areas on his own initiative is a negative resolution power.

We have looked carefully at the 1994 Act. It would be far better if the proposals put forward in the Bill in relation to probation service board areas were exactly the same as those in the 1994 Act. It is for the Minister to justify his reasons for a change that would appear to give the Home Secretary of the day, if the Bill become law, a much more unfettered power in relation to probation service boards.

It would, therefore, be unwise of this Committee to hark back to the 1994 Act as an example that we should follow in relation to this piece of legislation. What we are doing here is creating something new, something fit for the purpose that builds on the existing experience and the needs and requirements of the probation service and of the criminal justice system as a whole.

The amendments would make any rationalisation of boundaries that may be useful and felt to be necessary in future, difficult. It is not necessary to have a parliamentary debate on the changing of boundaries. It is not a matter of principle requiring a debate on every occasion. It is a matter of organising the service in the most efficient and desirable way, in terms of the particular need in the area and in the service as a whole. Local consultation would always be undertaken before such an order was made. Experience shows, as in Devon and Cornwall, that local involvement, when properly conducted, leads to change that has the ready acceptance and enthusiastic embrace of all parties. The Chairman: Order. I appreciate the spirit of the Minister's argument, but the Hansard reporters cannot hear what is being said.

Mr. Boateng: Perhaps Hansard will not be missing much after all.

I shall deal with the serious substance of the hon. Lady's argument. We are setting up a unified national service, which demands a level of consistency among its constituent parts. One way to achieve that is by requiring information to be separated in each board's annual report in a standard form and to be produced by a set date. That is not unreasonable. It will not inhibit the capacity of a board to present such information in an attractive way that responds to local needs and concerns. The board will be judge of how best to present the report, in terms of its design and its content.

However, there must be a certain minimum content. The report must be presented in a standard way to enable the public and the Secretary of State to compare the performance of different services. It is important to ensure that members of the public who read the information—perhaps for the first time, and who do not have the detailed information that is available to the Secretary of State—can judge the performance of their local board and compare it with the performance of other boards. I am talking about transparency and accountability, not control freakery.

Jackie Ballard: I am not convinced of the Minister's argument. I was amused, however, by the measure of support from the Conservative Front Bench because I often worry about the Government's control-freak tendency and feel that they may be building on an 18-„year legacy left to them by the previous Government.

However, I am delighted to accept support from wherever it comes, even though it is sometimes from surprising quarters.

The Minister said that he had better things to do with his time than to count the extra powers given to the Secretary of State under the Bill. He has made our point for us: there are so many powers that it would take him too long to count them. If there were only one or two new ones, it would take him only a matter of seconds.

Mr. Boateng: How many are there?

Jackie Ballard: I have not counted them.

Mr. Boateng: Why not?

Jackie Ballard: I have better things to do with my time. There are too many powers, but I suspect that, after our sitting, someone will count them.

Now is not the appropriate time to have an in-depth debate on such matters, but during our discussions on many other Bills, we have debated how a national service with national standards can meet the expectations and legitimate demands of the Government for common standards throughout England and Wales, yet still be responsive to local needs and provide a locally based service. I am sure that the Minister accepts that there is always an in-„built tension in any service, whether it be the health service, the probation service or the police service, but there is obviously a difference between us about where to draw the line. Giving effective powers to the Secretary of State to decide the form of the reports is drawing the line too close to central control. I am sure that we shall return to the matter but, for the moment, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
