Miss Johnson: As we have now reached an important part of the Bill, it might help the Committee if I gave a little background.

Clause 107 establishes the financial services and markets tribunal and gives the Lord Chancellor a general power to make rules for its operation. Schedule 11 sets out further details of the tribunal's constitution and operation.

The tribunal is a vital component of the new regulatory system. It is right and proper that the regulators should have effective powers. It is equally right and proper that we ensure that those who are subject to Financial Services Authority authorisation and enforcement decisions should enjoy their right to a full and fair hearing.

The tribunal will replace the existing fragmented provision for review procedures and will be fully independent. Its members will be appointed by the Lord Chancellor and its administration will be run as part of the Court Service.

Contrary to misleading reports in the press, the independent tribunal has been a key part of our plans to modernise financial services regulation since the draft Bill was published in July 1998. The first-instance nature of the tribunal was clarified last December, in the Government's progress report in March and in the proceedings of the Joint Committee, which Lord Burns chaired in the summer.

As the Minister for Small Business and E-Commerce, my hon. Friend the Member for Leicester, West (Ms Hewitt)ómy predecessor as Economic Secretaryóexplained to the Joint Committee, the tribunal will be a first-instance tribunal. It will be able to consider all the facts and all the merits of the case, in full compliance with the requirements of article 6(1) of the European convention on human rights.

The tribunal will also comply with current best practice, as laid down by the Council on Tribunals. The council will oversee the tribunal's workings in accordance with its role under the Tribunals and Inquiries Act 1992. Our amendments make it clear that the tribunal comes within the council's responsibilities under the Act.

The result is a single, fully independent tribunal, which will safeguard the rights of individuals and firms. A wide range of regulatory decisions will be subject to recourse to the tribunal. They will include: the refusal of an application for a new permission; the use of the authority's own-initiative powers to restrict an authorised person's business or to withdraw his authorisation; disciplinary measures against authorised firms; objections to controllers; the refusal of an application for or withdrawal of approval under part V; the making of a prohibition order; and the imposition of financial penalties for market abuse.

Of course, the right to refer a matter to the tribunal will generally come only after the necessary internal FSA procedures have been completed. As the Committee is aware, the Bill imposes several requirements on the FSA as regards the notice given, rights to make representations and to see evidence and the need to follow public procedures. We have come across the procedures several times in our deliberations, and we shall debate them fully when we finally reach clauses 338 to 340.

However, an important balance must be struck. It is in no one's interests for FSA procedures to replicate the tribunal's role. That would be costly and inflexible and would distance people from their recourse to the tribunal. Nor is it in the industry's interests for all cases to be referred automatically to a full tribunal hearing. The Joint Committee noted that that would impose unnecessary costs. We have, therefore, drawn a clear distinction between the tribunal's administrative internal procedures and its judicial procedures. The Joint Committee described the result as

    ``an elegant hybrid which we find attractive''.

It may be helpful to the Committee if I briefly outline the timetable for consultation on the draft procedural rules. The Lord Chancellor's Department expects to begin consulting the Council on Tribunals about the rules in early December. It intends that a draft should be circulated more widely in the new year, with a full public consultation in spring. I accept that Committee Members may wish to see a draft at the earliest possible stage, so I propose to make available to them the draft that the Government will send to the council in December.

For the purposes of our debate, I shall explain some procedural aspects that the rules will cover. Those include how references are commenced; how the FSA presents its case; rules on disclosure and inspection of documents, and rules of evidence, including the use of expert witnesses. As our amendments make clear, the tribunal will have broad discretion over the evidence that it hears.

Other procedural aspects covered by the rules are the holding of preliminary hearings and the procedure at the main hearing, including circumstances in which the hearing is not held in public. As long as that is consistent with the interests of justice and everyone's right to a public hearing under the ECHR, it is intended that that will generally be at the request of the person referring the case.

Post-decision matters, including publication of the decision and the award of costs are also covered by the rules. Our amendments to schedule 11 narrow the tribunal's discretion to award costs in line with the Joint Committee's suggestion.

The objective in drawing up the rules is to achieve a careful balance.

Further details of the procedure for obtaining permission may be set out in the procedure rules to be made under clause 107 by virtue of the power in clause 109(5). It is envisaged that permission would have to be sought from the tribunal first in the usual way, with the option of renewing the application to the appeal court if the tribunal refused permission.

Amendment No. 406 makes it clear that the only type of decision by the tribunal that may be appealed against in clause 109(1) is the final determinative decision referred to in clause 108(4). It is not intended that decisions in interlocutory matters such as the admissibility of evidence or the calling of particular witnesses should be capable of appeal. That would open up the possibility of the tribunal proceedings being endlessly disrupted and delayed by one or other party constantly seeking permission to appeal minor procedural issues to the higher court. That would not be consistent with the speedy and effective administration of justice.
