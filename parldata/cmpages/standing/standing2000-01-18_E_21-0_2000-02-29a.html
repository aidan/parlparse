Mr. Hill: Much water has flowed under the bridge since the previous sitting, most of which was entirely unexpected. The hon. Member for Bath (Mr. Don Foster)—who is my hon. Friend in spirit, if not in title—was referring to the six-month maximum period that will be allowed under the clause between approving a quality contract scheme and its being made. He was concerned about the procedure if the appropriate national authority requests modifications to the scheme. I hope that I can reassure him.

If a scheme is approved with modifications, clause 106 will require the scheme-making authority to be given prior notice, and it must then consult anyone likely to be affected by the modifications. The authority must inform the Secretary of State, or the National Assembly for Wales, of the consultation's outcome; only then can the scheme be approved under clause 106(6). The six-months referred to in clause 107(1) will begin on the date of that final approval. We believe that six months is sufficient time in which to arrange matters so that the scheme can be duly made in that time.

The hon. Gentleman asked what would happen if further issues arise as a result of the second round of consultation. In consulting on any modification, it would make sense for the local authority to aim to arrive at an accommodation with the parties. The time scale will be in its hands, and it can delay informing the Secretary of State until such time as outstanding issues are resolved. I hope that the hon. Gentleman finds that answer satisfactory. If he has further questions, I shall try to be of assistance.

I have explained why the amendments tabled by the Liberal Democrats are unacceptable. For the same reasons, I cannot accept amendment No. 517, under which the official Opposition would reduce the period from 21 to 12 months. I draw the Committee's attention to clause 107(7), which provides for the appropriate national authority to vary the period by order; it is not necessarily set in stone, although obviously there would need to be very good reasons either to increase or to decrease it.

Amendments Nos. 518 and 519 deal with the maximum period for which the scheme—not an individual contract—can remain in operation. The Bill refers to a maximum of 10 years. Under amendment No. 518, that would be shortened to 5 years, and with great ingenuity, as the hon. Member for Bath said during the previous sitting, it would be increased to 12 years under amendment No. 519. That makes me pretty sure that 10 years is about right. We want to provide scope for reasonable stability, partly in the interests of local authorities and local taxpayers, given the work involved in such schemes. However, a scheme should be re-examined occasionally with a wholly fresh eye— hence the maximum period. Time and travel patterns change and a fresh look will be appropriate from time to time.

Amendment No. 520 refers to the maximum length of a contract within a scheme. The Bill refers to 5 years, but 12 years is proposed under that amendment. Frankly, five years is long enough for anybody to have an exclusive monopoly contract to run bus services in an area. Of course, the operator needs stability to encourage proper investment, but after 5 years it will be time to consider whether somebody else could do better—to test the market, in fact. Amendment No. 521 deals with when the clock should start ticking for the maximum contract. In effect, the Bill states that the clock will start when the contract is signed, but it does so in legal language. Under that amendment, a gap of up to 12 months is proposed, but I am reluctant to accept that the time scale should be spun out any more than necessary. The completion of the contract would be spun out to six years after it was signed—an unnecessarily long time. I accept that the operator may need to prepare, but he will do so in the confidence that the contract will be definite, and I believe that five years is enough time for that. I hope that those explanations have helped the Committee and that, in the light of them, the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Foster: I am grateful to the Under-Secretary for that helpful response. Mr. Hill: Subsection (2) states that quality contract schemes must specify certain matters, including the area of application, the commencement date and their duration. None of that will be affected under the amendments, but the clause also requires the scheme to specify the local services to be provided and the features of the proposed invitations to tender. On reflection, that is thought too inflexible.

The policy intention is that local authorities should have the option, if they wish, to take a broad-brush approach in describing the service requirements and routes, but to allow detailed matters to be decided in the light of the bids received. That would give bus operators scope to bring their professional views to bear in determining how best to meet the local authority's requirements.

Therefore, under the amendments, we shall omit such matters from those that must be specified under subsection (2) and introduce a new subsection under which it will be necessary only to outline, rather than to specify, the local services to be provided and the features of the invitations to tender. I hope that the Committee agrees that that will improve the Bill.
