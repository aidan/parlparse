Angela Eagle: The amendments and new clauses concern the provisions of benefits in kind to employees by somebody other than their own employer--third party. That might arise if, for example, a manufacturer of cameras wanted to reward or give a greater incentive to retailers' sales staff who sell its cameras. Alternatively, a car manufacturer might wish to incentivise dealers' staff to sell particular models of car. Such incentive schemes are often short-term and reward the best sellers in the form of holidays, goods or non-cash vouchers.

The provisions are designed specifically to address the concerns third party incentive providers and employers about the difficulties that they anticipate in meeting class 1A national insurance contribution liabilities. They asked us to allow third parties rather than employers to pay the national insurance contributions, as in the case of tax. We listened to them carefully, and the amendment provides what they asked for. It moves the liability for class 1A national insurance contributions on such benefits from the employer, who has had no hand in their provision, to the third party.

From 6 April 2001, the third-party provider's liability for class 1A national insurance contributions will become compulsory. On Royal Assent, the provision will take effect from 6 April 2000. We recognise that there is little time for forward planning in the first transitional year. The third party may choose whether to meet the class 1A liability on benefits that he provides. He must notify the Revenue in writing whether he will be paying. If he decides that he will meet the liability, we expect him to tell that to his employer. That will help employers who would otherwise have to pick up the bill for national insurance contributions on benefits that were given to their employees, possibly without their knowledge.

Switching the liability to the third-party provider should mean that employers will be glad to hear that their employees have received incentive awards, and that they will not resent the cost and hassle for themselves. It will also make life easier for manufacturers who want to incentivise salesmen and saleswomen to sell their products, and it will benefit the workers who receive the awards.

Mrs. Lait: I seek clarification of a matter of fact. Will the third party have access to the employee's pay details in order to pay the relevant rate of national insurance?

Angela Eagle: No. Class 1A liability will not affect the situation of the third-party provider or the relevant liabilities. Class 1A national insurance will be paid on the benefits that are provided, as incentives, to the employee. There is therefore no need to give third-party individuals or companies access to an employee's pay details, which are, frankly, nothing to do with them. We need to establish how a third party can incentivise someone else's employee to sell their products.

To ensure that the national insurance fund receives all the national insurance contributions that are due in such cases, the new clause includes arrangements for the premises of third party providers to be open to inspection by Revenue officers. Late payment or non-payment will attract interest and penalties in the normal way.

New Clause 24 mirrors the UK provisions for Northern Ireland. Amendment No. 320 will merely add new clause 24 to the list of sections, which relate to Northern Ireland.

Moving Class 1A liability on to third parties who provide benefits is a sensible deregulatory measure that has been introduced at the request of the parties involved. I urge the Committee to accept the amendment.
