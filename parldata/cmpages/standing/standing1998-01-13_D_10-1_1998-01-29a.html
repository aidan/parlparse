It ensures that low-paid workers and those who are vulnerable and lack the capacity to seek advocacy rights on their own will be able to do so. The clause enables an enforcement officer to take fuller action if an employer fails to comply with an enforcement notice. He may bring a case to an industrial tribunal or a civil court to recover the difference between the national minimum wage and what has been paid to the worker covered by the enforcement notice. The fact that the agency is bringing such a case does not deny the individual the right to bring his own case.

I disagree with them that an employer would be taken to court by an individual and, simultaneously or subsequently, by the agency. That would not happen in practice. The agency is unlikely to take up a case that an individual is already pursuing. Clause 19

Mr. McCartney: I beg to move amendment No. 158, in page 13, line 16, leave out subsection (5) and insert

    "(5) A financial penalty under this section

    (a) in England and Wales, shall be recoverable, if a county court so orders, by execution issued from the county court or otherwise as if it were payable under an order of that court;

    (b) in Scotland, may be enforced in the same manner as an extract registered decree arbitral bearing a warrant for execution issued by the sheriff court of any sheriffdom in Scotland;

    (c) in Northern Ireland, shall be recoverable, if the county court so orders, as if it were payable under an order of that court.".

The amendment would simplify the procedure for the recovery of a financial penalty and make that process more effective. It is in everyone's interest employers and employees to ensure that the national minimum wage is implemented effectively. The amendment forms part of an effective enforcement strategy. It will also ease the burden on the courts by simplifying the process of recovery of the penalty if an employer fails to pay it.

The Chairman: Order. Only one person should be standing in the Committee.

Mr. McCartney: I was talking about the process for recovering the penalty if the employer fails to pay it. The amendment removes the requirement to take separate proceedings to recover the debt. I shall briefly clarify how the amendment fits into the Bill.

In clause 19(5) the financial penalty is recoverable as a civil debt by the enforcement officer. In practice, that would require the officer to prove the debt in civil proceedings before a court. Such proceedings, even at that late stage, could be open to challenge by the employer, which would require the debt to be proved by means of full proceedings before a court. That could potentially drag out proceedings and cause extra work for the courts. The amendment makes the debt recoverable, as if it were payable under a court order in England and Wales or the equivalent in Scotland and Northern Ireland. Recovery under a court order means that there is no need to prove the debt.

We want to ensure that delay does not pay. We also want to ensure that the Bill provides sufficient balance for employers to be able to appeal. I believe that the Bill already provides every opportunity for the reasonable employer to exercise his or her rights. First, the employer may appeal against the enforcement notice under clause 17(5). Secondly, the employer may appeal against the penalty notice under clause 20, in each case to the employment tribunal. When an appeal is pending, either against the enforcement notice or the penalty notice, the financial penalty cannot be enforced.

I believe that those appeal mechanisms provide a final and reasonable opportunity for employers to have their say. The amendment simply provides for a more effective, efficient means of implementing a penalty once the appeals process has been exhausted.
