Pensions are deferred pay, and form part of the contractual arrangements. The important point about a pension fund, especially a defined benefits scheme—which is what we are dealing with—is that it represents a promise from the employer to the employee. The pension fund is merely a means for the employer to fulfil that promise. In the final analysis, pension funds belong to the employer, not the members of the pension fund. That is because if the employer has put in too much money—or has put in money and the fund has grown so that there is a surplus and the promise can be more than delivered—the employer has no obligation to deliver more than his promise.

That was the case with the bus company pension scheme. When the actuaries looked at the figures, they saw that there was a surplus in the pension fund. Once the pension fund had been split up among the various privatised bus companies, the previous employer—the Government—had no obligation to release those surplus funds to the bus companies because they had every confidence, supported by actuaries, that their promise would be fulfilled. That is why the surplus was retained.

The clause deals with determining the book value of a company. It is not concerned with evaluation which would be made for a sale nor creative accounting—the latter matter is germane to the hon. Gentleman's inquiry.

Clause 45 deals with accounting provisions and provides for values or amounts to be ascribed for accounting purposes to property, rights or assets transferred under a transfer scheme. It is especially concerned with, for example, the opening accounts for the newly transferred NATS to the Secretary of State and any wholly owned subsidiaries of NATS. Those transactions will not be normal commercial transactions and it is necessary to provide certainty about how transferred assets should be accounted for. The object of the transfer scheme is to parcel out assets and liabilities between different entities as though they had always been held that way.
