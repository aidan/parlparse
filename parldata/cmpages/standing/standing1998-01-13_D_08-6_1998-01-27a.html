The point about clause 14 that the Committee should consider, and on which we require a series of reassurances from the Minister, is that the imposition of a minimum wage might affect people's basic liberties. The Opposition fully recognise that some sort of enforcement will be necessary and that enforcement officers will therefore be needed. It is important that the Committee should recognise the seriousness of the steps being taken under clause 14, which deals with the powers of enforcement officers.

Clause 14 creates a new set of law officers with extremely wide-ranging powers. Given this country's traditions, it is important that we should not regard the change as merely technical; it is worthy of the most serious consideration. It can be argued that there are already at least enough, if not too many, people with enforcement powers.

Clause 14 will create an entirely new class of enforcement officers, who will potentially have access to every business premise and home. They will not only cover employees working in a conventional work force; the notes on clauses explicitly state that officers will have enforcement powers over home workers, piece workers and the many other classes of worker that we have mentioned.

The Bill particularly affects home workers and among the powers of those officers is the power to demand records and to interview people in person. It is therefore extremely likely that they will want access to people's homes.
