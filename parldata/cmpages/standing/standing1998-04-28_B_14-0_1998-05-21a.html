Mr. Clappison: The amendments relate to the revocation of drug treatment and testing orders, which is part of the subject matter of schedule 4.

The schedule amends the Criminal Justice Act 1991. Without going into too much technical detail, it would appear that when a magistrates court or a Crown court revokes a drug treatment and testing order, the person to whom the order applied could be dealt with in any of the ways that were originally available to the court. He could be given a custodial sentence or sentenced to any of the non-custodial alternatives.

It is likely that revocation would be sought when a defendant was being sentenced for other matters. The amendments accept that a drug treatment and testing order may be revoked within the treatment and testing period if the defendant is taken into custody, which would prevent him from complying with the order. Obviously, we would hope that he would continue to receive treatment while he was in custody. However, Opposition Members can see little merit in a drug treatment and testing order being revoked within the treatment and testing period only to be replaced by another non-custodial sentence, such as community service or some form of supervision or probation. It does not make sense. In such cases, priority should be given to the continuation of treatment and testing.

That is our understanding of the matter, but we look to the Minister for guidance.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Alun Michael): The hon. Gentleman has tabled a reasonable, probing amendment. I think I can respond to it fairly simply.

People who breach a drug treatment and testing order are dealt with under other provisions. The paragraph applies to those who remain within the terms of the orders, not to those who breach them. We want to ensure that there is the maximum flexibility to deal with the offender in the most appropriate way.

The order can be revoked and replaced by another form of sentence only with the agreement of everyone involved. That would include the service providers, the probation officer and the judge. It could not be done casually.

In most cases, we would share the view of the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison): a period of treatment should continue, to ensure that the individual had not come off drugs only for a short time, but that his or her pattern of behaviour had genuinely changed. However, there might be circumstances in which the individual had fully complied with the order and had made progress. It would, therefore, be sensible to substitute something different to confirm that changed pattern of behaviour. For example, community service, to which the hon. Gentleman referred, can help to change people's behaviour for life.

The provisions for the revocation of orders are not intended to be used casually, and that is emphasised by the fact that agreement has to be given by the judge and others involved in the case. The provisions are not intended to be used when an order has been breached. If someone has committed another offence the court can make a judgment. Criminogenic matters might well be better addressed under a probation order, the drug problem being less than originally thought. The point would not be to lessen the emphasis on preventing continued drug use, but to increase the impact of a sentence on other elements of the person's needs or behaviour that presented more urgent problems.

The court would not be required to revoke a drug treatment and testing order after a further offence if a non-custodial sentence was imposed. The hon. Member for Hertsmere rightly said that where a custodial sentence was imposed revocation would be necessary, but one would hope and expect that the prison regime would include appropriate continuing treatment. My colleagues with direct responsibility for the Prison Service and prison regimes are keen to pursue that approach.

I hope that what I have said will reassure the hon. Gentleman. To some extent the matters are technical. I confess that I had to spend some time in discussion to be sure; however, I can assure the hon. Gentleman that the provision is intended simply to allow the courts flexibility. There is to be no lessening of impact or stringency in applying drug treatment and testing orders.

Mr. Clappison: I am grateful for the Minister's courteous and considered response. Mr. Michael: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for clarifying the amendments. He made a perfectly reasonable point.

In recent years, the former system of cautioning has fallen into disrepute with the police and with the public. It was felt that the pattern of repeat cautions gave the message that nothing would happen as the result of a caution. That view has been expressed to me by many police officers. Some cases in which a caution would have been appropriate were dealt with informally in order to avoid what was seen as a waste of time.

In recent times, the Home Office has given guidance to tighten the use of repeat cautioning, but that has not rescued the system. Uncertainty exists at the lower end of the scale. We want to avoid that because we want the system to operate consistently in local areas and across the country. The new system will make it clear where cautions and reprimands stand in the hierarchy.

An informal caution does not really exist as an arrangement. However, one needs to apply common sense. If a group of youngsters is causing a nuisance on the streets at night, police forces in some areas send a note to the parents. For example, they might tell the parents that their youngster is one of a group causing a nuisance on the green and they will ask the parents to take responsibility for their children's behaviour and where they are at night. Such advice does not need to become part of the formal system. I hope that it will provide the correction that will prevent youngsters from reaching the stage of receiving a reprimand or final warning.

I do not want to be so prescriptive as to say that there should never be any informal advice given to parents about their youngsters' behaviour. That would be so prescriptive as to be unworkable. We want to ensure that there is not a half-understood system of informal cautions, which are not really cautions. No account is taken of them.

If a youngster receives a reprimand, it should be known that that is the first step and that appropriate steps will follow. When a youngster commits one, two or three minor offences, that may provide an opportunity to bring him up short and avoid the development of further patterns of misbehaviour. The clause is designed to ensure that the system is clear, crisp and well understood by offenders and their parents as well as by parliamentarians and the courts.
