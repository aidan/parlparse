New Clause 10

Report by Low Pay Commission

    ``.--Within one year of this Act coming into force, the Low Pay Commission shall make an evaluation of the impact of any national minimum wage determined by regulations made under section 2 of this Act, as it affects workers covered by the relevant Agricultural Wages Order for each territory of the United Kingdom, and shall make a report thereon to the Secretary of State.''.--[Mr. Boswell.]

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

No. 259, in Schedule 2, page 35, leave out lines 40 to 48.

Mr. Boswell: The purpose of the new clause is to trigger a debate on the agricultural wages orders and enable us to voice our specific concerns. The new clause would require the Low Pay Commission to report after one year on the operation of the national minimum wage in the agriculture industry and in particular to produce a territorial report tailored to the various parts of the United Kingdom.

Amendments Nos. 258 and 259 are clearly probing amendments. We are deep into schedule territory and it is not always easy to unpick the Government's intentions, although they are helpfully set out in the notes on clauses.

I shall break down into two parts the analysis of the new clause. The first concerns the likely macro impacts of a national minimum wage being introduced into agriculture in parallel with the existing and continuing agricultural wages orders. The second relates to the continued operation of the agricultural wages board and how it will co-exist with a national minimum wage.

The new clause has given me an opportunity to study in more depth the National Farmers Union evidence submitted to the Low Pay Commission. I hope that the commission will carefully weigh that evidence in its considerations, as I hope the Government did in formulating their own evidence to the commission.

The National Farmers Union evidence confirms my knowledge of the industry, which is if nothing else based on long experience as it dates back not to when the first agricultural wages order was made in 1948 but to the early 1960s, when the agricultural minimum wage was first modified to take account of craft status. The evidence brings home more clearly than I would hope to be able to do the distinctions within agriculture and the likely impacts of the minimum wage on its different sectors and interests.

Agriculture is not a straightforward industry. Many parts, such as those that I mentioned earlier in our proceedings, are highly capitalised, highly skilled and rely on a small labour force that is satisfactorily paid even in comparison with industrial wages. That is not the issue here, except in so far as it relates to the future of the board, to which I shall return.

The NFU's concern is about those at the lower end of the wage scale such as casual workers, for whom a separate rate is prescribed by the agricultural wages orders, particularly in horticulture, which is a major employer of casual workers. It is difficult to find exact figures because the labour force survey is not undertaken in June when casual employment is at its peak. In round terms--the NFU believes this to be an under-estimate--some 70,000 workers are employed as casuals.

The distribution of casual workers in horticulture as against other sectors is difficult to estimate, but there may be 25,000 to 30,000 at any one time. We should bear in mind that total employment in horticulture is upwards of 50,000--it is typically the most labour-intensive sector of agriculture.

We should also make the point, as the NFU does in its evidence, that many of the casual workers who are employed in other areas, such as livestock, have significant responsibilities; they may, for example, be relief stock persons and are probably quite skilled. In horticulture, however, one typically still finds gangs and people with manual skills; such people are generally not highly skilled and do intensive seasonal work.

One interesting point needs to be made clear in relation to the agricultural wages order. We have debated continuity of employment periods. The order clearly specifies, however, that employment should not run for more than 20 weeks continuously. In other words, there is no question of quasi-permanent employment being dressed up as casual employment. The order is designed to fit--in so far as any order can--a seasonal production pattern.

The other points that I should make--perhaps more clearly than I have done so far--concern the sharp break between different rates. I have already mentioned that the order prescribes a minimum basic full-time agricultural wages rate of £4.12 an hour. The casual rate, however, is £3.06 an hour for a person of 19 or over. There is also a sharp differentiation by age, and the rate goes down at the age of 16 to a minimum of £1.84 an hour. The NFU understandably uses that to argue for the importance of a separate youth rate--not just for agriculture, but across industry generally.

To summarise the general pattern, there is a two-nation agriculture. We are not talking only about skills, but about the nature of the job and whether it is mechanisable. We must consider two different situations. On the one hand, there is the highly capitalised set-up that typically offers full-time employment and employs two or three people on 1,000 acres or with a dairy herd. Such a set-up has a higher output per unit of employment. Although it offers mainly full-time employment, that may be supplemented by casual work--at harvest time, for example. Work at such an establishment is fairly highly paid and rates are in the range of £4 to £5 an hour or even higher for skilled grades. On the other hand, there is the significant number of employees who are paid between £3 to £5. It is difficult to be sure exactly how many such employees there are; there might be 25,000 or 30,000 at any one time, although not necessarily the same 25,000 or 30,000 because people enter and leave employment. Quite a number of those people will be paid at rates that are towards the lower part of the pay range.

I do not intend to summarise the whole of the NFU's evidence to the commission, but it is important to pick out one or two points that reinforce my argument. The first is exactly consistent with what I have said. The NFU's evidence states:

    ``The use of casual workers is spread across the industry but is particularly concentrated in the horticultural sector where growers use a large amount of casual workers and are extremely vulnerable to increases in labour costs.''

The evidence refers to the fact that labour can amount to 50 per cent. of overall costs. It goes on to say:

    ``Given this we would like to stress to the Commission as strongly as possible that any NMW set at a level higher than £3.20 per hour could have drastic effects upon the agricultural and horticultural industries and lead to a significant loss of employment. It is important to appreciate that in many horticultural businesses the existence of full-time and part-time jobs often relies upon the employment of casual workers.''

Perhaps I did not make that necessary qualification clearly enough.

The NFU points out that employment, particularly that of casuals, is extremely sensitive to rates. It estimates the potential direct cost to the industry of setting the national minimum wage at various levels: £4.40, which is within the ball park of half national male median adult earnings, would cost £202 million, which would affect full-time, part-time and casual workers; £4 would cost considerably less--£93 million--and affect only casual workers directly, as would any lower rate; £3.50 would cost £43 million; £3.20 would cost £14 million. The cost escalates sharply as the rate moves up.

Mr. Philip Hammond (Runnymede and Weybridge): Does my hon. Friend accept that those figures do not allow for restoration of differentials between casual and permanent workers?

Mr. Boswell: Precisely so. I was coming to exactly that point. The next sentence of the NFU evidence states:

    ``Not only do these figures not take into account the restoration of differentials, they do not take into account the impact of other employment costs such as national insurance and pension contributions either.''

The NFU concludes:

    ``Restoration of differentials will have a significant impact on costs.''

Those are matters of concern.

Another point is relevant to how casual employment is carried out in the industry. I am not a significant employer of casual labour, but I used to run a fruit enterprise and employed a significant number of seasonal labourers. The NFU evidence points out:

    ``With some groups of pickers who come and go as they please (often for good reasons such as seeing children to school) it is very hard to know how many hours are worked by any individual.

New pickers are given the option of one or two days' work at the hourly rate while they acquire the skills, the amount they pick is recorded . . . if they don't look like making it, the company usually agree that that job is not going to be for them. Earnings for the lower 10 per cent. of some of the weekly paid and engaged pickers do sometimes drop below the AWB rate but usually the next day comfortably exceeds it so the weekly average is maintained.''

That point is germane to pay reference periods, which I shall discuss later.

Casual employment in horticulture and the calculation of the hourly rate equivalent will cause difficulties within agriculture. I am not making a special case for agriculture: my hon. Friends and I have already argued that the severe problems with that type of employment are not confined to farming, although it is a sensitive sector. If the new clause is not agreed to, the Low Pay Commission will not be able to test the impacts of the minimum wage. Strategic business decisions may severely curtail, if not wipe out, horticultural employment.

In best business school fashion, the NFU derives from an extended case study the strategic business decisions that would appear likely following the introduction of a national minimum wage rate above current agricultural minimum levels. It suggests what might happen:

    ``Increase productivity of overall workforce--most likely achieved by reducing workforce and retaining only the more productive workers.

Reducing the size of labour intensive horticultural enterprises and increasing the amount of land used for arable crops. This would lead not only to a significant drop in employment in the business, but to more general loss of economic activity and to increased imports of high value fruit and vegetables.''

I can confirm that from my own experience. When plums were harvested in my orchard of 10 acres--or four hectares--grain was harvested on the rest of the farm on an acreage that was 10 or 20 times larger, but which involved less employment than the intensive harvesting of horticultural crops in the orchard.

I am sure, Mrs. Dunwoody, that you share my distaste for French Golden Delicious. If English apples, pears, soft fruit or other English fruit were not available, everybody would feel the loss, whether or not they were employed in agriculture.

The Opposition are anxious about those issues, and it would be helpful for the Commission to consider them in the light of events. We hope that, if the national minimum wage is set at a moderate level, growers and horticulturalists will be able to plan their strategies sensibly, and will manage the situation. The second issue raised by the proposed new clause is the relationship with the board. It would not be within the Committee's remit to debate the desirability of continuing the agricultural wages order, or to draw a final conclusion about the matter, which should be examined partly in the light of experience. The Ministers have reminded the Committee that the order will be due for review in 1999. Presumably, hon. Members will then have some experience of the operation of the national minimum wage.

I have warned Ministers that, if the board continues, it is important for agriculture that it is administrated together with the order as cleanly as possible, so that rates are not altered every couple of months like a yo-yo, as one and then the other is changed. That would be a recipe for mistakes, and I am sure that Ministers will take that point on board.

I said that I have no permanent vested interest in the continuation of the board against the wishes of agriculture. It should be emphasised that, even if the two systems mesh in terms of the lower rates, there are important differences. I hope that the Ministers will comment on the matter when I come to the amendments that I have tabled. It would not be right to be a tidy mind and to say that, because one structure is in place, the other should be dropped.

I shall make two points about the matter that will give the Committee a sense of the sublime as well as the ridiculous, and which relate to actual situations. The Agricultural Wages Board has extended its wings during some years. It has moved from being the setter or protector of a minimum return to all farm workers to being concerned with the wider concept of professional development and with the acknowledgement of such development within agriculture.

That change originated with proficiency tests, which are still part of the order. If an employee passes such a test, perhaps because an employer has required it, he or she may receive a higher rate of pay. That higher rate can be defended through various craft and appointment grades, and through the acknowledgement that is now available of national vocational qualifications and so on.

I believe that no fewer than 10 minimum rates exist. I do not wish the Minister to say that he wants 10 national minimum wage rates for national vocational qualifications of otherwise, but the solution is agricultural, and is for the agricultural industry. Whatever happens at the bottom--and I have been emphasising that because where the two systems meet, potential difficulty arises--there may be a role, if the industry wants it, for a wider cover such as an agricultural wages board.

There is another aspect to this: it may sound ridiculous, but it relates to real situations. Many people, as I have in the past, employ shepherds who are required to keep dogs for livestock tending. There is a provision in the order that people required by their employer to keep a dog can be paid £4.65 a week for the dog. We do not have a national minimum wage, but we have a dog's minimum wage. These matters relate to a particular industry. Apart from their rather colourful impact--I speak as a Tory here--we should at least consider whether we should lightly throw away these colourful provisions.

Just because there is a meshing at the bottom does not mean that there needs to be monkeying about at the top. There is a separate Ministry which will continue to have responsibility for the orders. It will be answerable to the collective opinion of the agriculture industry. I hope that it will listen to the industry carefully, without being either unduly conservative and refusing to make changes--some administrative changes will be required for the two systems to harmonise--or too tidy minded, wiping things out simply because they appear different. The jobs are somewhat different.

That is not the purpose of incapacity permits. The Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food estimated in a recent written answer that in June last year, some 170,000 workers in England and Wales were covered by the agricultural wages order. The number of workers known generically to have a disability is not clear--although I presume that it is a significant number--but the number who are incapacitated is known, because at the latest convenient date only 102 permits were in force. That is a tiny proportion of the total. The permits are confined under the Agricultural Wages Act 1948 to a person
