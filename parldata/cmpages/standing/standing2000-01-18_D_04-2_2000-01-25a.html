Clause 12

Uniform

Mr. Lidington: I beg to move amendment No. 17, in page 7, line 1, leave out from `liable' to end of line 5 and insert—

    `(a) on conviction on indictment, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding one year or a fine or both, or

    (b) on summary conviction, to

    (a) imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months,

    (b) a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum, or

    (c) both.'.

The amendment, which is a probing amendment, would reinstate the possibility of a Crown court trial and, consequently, the possibility of the more serious Crown court penalties for the offence of wearing, carrying or displaying an article in support of a terrorist organisation. The provision for Crown court trials and sentencing powers exists within the EPA. The Government have chosen, in re-enacting that part of the EPA, to amend the provision so that only magistrates courts may deal with such offences. That will much reduce the maximum penalty.

Will the Minister explain the policy behind the decision? In earlier debates on the penalties for membership of a proscribed organisation, the Minister was rightly firm in saying that other courts required the tougher penalties that could be applied only by the Crown court. The offence being considered is akin to that of membership of a proscribed organisation and could be committed by a range of people, from a green recruit to a hardened terrorist. The Government seem to be adopting different approaches. The Committee would be grateful for explanation.

Mr. Charles Clarke: Although I would not wish to diminish the hon. Gentleman's point, the explanation is simple. Clause 12 replicates an offence that appears both in section 3 of the PTA and in section 31 of the EPA. The PTA version of the offence, which has effect in England, Wales and Scotland, is a summary-only offence with a maximum penalty of six months. The EPA version is an either-way offence, with a maximum penalty on indictment of a year.

In consolidating the legislation, we needed to ensure that the offence had the same penalty throughout the UK, and so had to decide which way to go. We could give the offence either a tougher penalty in Great Britain, or a lesser penalty in Northern Ireland. It is serious, but we decided, on balance, that it was right in principle to align the situation in Northern Ireland with that in Great Britain, rather than vice versa. That is for why we chose the form that is before the Committee, which I hope that the hon. Gentleman will accept.

Mr. Lidington: I shall want to reflect on the Minister's remarks before we reach later stages but they were adequate for this evening's purposes, so I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Charles Clarke: I shall respond first to the points of the hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone. A three-level hierarchy of language has been used. The lowest level of proof, under the PTA, provides that property ``may be used'' for terrorist purposes. The Bill proposes a slightly higher level—property ``is likely to be used'' for terrorist purposes. The higher burden of proof is justified because it is important that a case should be demonstrated clearly.

The proposal of the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey—that property will be used for terrorist purposes—wouId raise the level again. It is not terribly easy to get rid of stolen or tainted goods or money that is the profit of drug dealing or that has been used for terrorist purposes. A cache of money cannot simply be handed over to the next person, and we must reflect for a moment to ensure that we are not catching innocent users of the most common commodity of all. One point validly made to me was that everybody is likely to carry money and therefore that everybody can be caught by the provision. We must be careful that the clause is tightly drawn.

Clause 18

Disclosure of information: duty

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

9.15 pm

Mr. Simon Hughes: We have had more representations about this clause than any that we have debated so far. The most prestigious of those is from Lord Lloyd of Berwick, who says that the clause should not be in the Bill. Therefore, I want to challenge the Minister on the clause that has been most widely criticised for eating into civil liberties.

On page 93 of Lord Lloyd's ``Inquiry into Legislation against Terrorism'' he addresses the suitability of retaining the present law, as set out in section 18 of the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989. The section relates to the failure to disclose information about acts of terrorism, and is replicated in the Bill. Lord Lloyd states:

    ``It is an offence, punishable by up to five years imprisonment, to fail to report to the authorities information which might be of material assistance in preventing the commission of an act of terrorism connected with the affairs of Northern Ireland, or in securing the apprehension of a person for such an offence. The provision applies throughout the UK.''

Lord Lloyd says that the measure is one of the more controversial in the Act. He points out that when Lords Colville and Shackleton reviewed the legislation they recommended the repeal of that provision, although he also notes that Lord Jellicoe, in his review, recommended that it should stay. He goes on to analyse the argument, and writes:

    ``The offence clearly targets the person who finds him or herself on the fringes of terrorist activity. Such a person might, perhaps, have rendered assistance to someone whom he later discovers to be engaged on a terrorist enterprise. Or he might be close to the person concerned and thus have discovered what he is about. A public spirited citizen would have little difficulty in resolving that his duty lay in informing the police of his suspicions. The purpose of the offence is to bring home to the person who puts personal considerations above the public safety that staying silent is not an acceptable option where terrorism is concerned.''

Then, referring to one of the reviews of the law, Lord Lloyd says:

    ``In his admirable and well-balanced examination, Walker could find little evidence that the offence has been of much practical value in increasing the flow of information to the police, and he is concerned about the `chilling' effect on the reporting of terrorism by the media . . . It is undoubtedly true that the offence has been little used. In Great Britain only 28 people have been charged since the offence was introduced in the 1976 Act, of whom 10 were convicted. The figures for Northern Ireland are rather higher.''

Lord Lloyd sets out two substantive criticisms. First, he says that although we have

    ``a moral obligation to help the police, the state should be reluctant to transform this into a legal duty. The second argument is . . . that prosecutions''
