Energy Conservation (Housing) Bill

4.30 pm

Clause 1

Issue of guidance to registered social landlords and to registered housing associations

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it may be convenient to take new clause 1 Issue of guidance to registered social landlords and registered housing associations.

Mr. Clive Efford (Eltham): I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Atkinson. I hope that under your expert guidance we can proceed expeditiously. To help the Committee, I shall start by saying a few words on the Bill's background.

The Bill will improve the Home Energy Conservation Act 1995. That important Act required local authorities to draw up and act on energy conservation plans for housing stock in their areas. As a result of that legislation, much progress has been made in improving home insulation, in helping people to keep warm and in reducing CO2 emissions.

However, a problem has arisen in the flow of information between registered social landlords and local authorities. The Bill is intended to rectify that. In England and Wales, housing associations are now called registered social landlords, although the original term still applies in Scotland and Northern Ireland.

In many areas, there is good co-operation between RSLs and local authorities, which are the energy conservation authorities responsible for implementing the 1995 Act. I recently visited a project run by Hyde Housing Association in south-east London. It was an excellent example of good practice but unfortunately that is not the case everywhere.

Problems arise when local authorities have difficulties obtaining information on housing association stock, which hampers preparation of their plans under the Home Energy Conservation Act 1995. RSLs have also complained of difficulties for instance, when they have houses in several areas under different local authorities. They may be required to give information in various ways, leading to a burden of bureaucracy when dealing with energy conservation.

More than 1 million low-income households are RSL tenants. Dealing with the information problem in the context of energy efficiency and energy poverty is an important part of the Bill.

The amendments are in my name and that of my hon. Friend the Minister for Local Government and Housing, whose Department the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions has given me a great deal of help. That assistance has been gratefully received and, as a result, the Bill has been compiled by the Parliamentary Counsel.

New clause 1 will replace clause 1. It amends the Housing Act 1996, and in particular section 36, which deals with the Housing Corporation's supervisory role with regard to RSLs. The Housing Corporation currently monitors RSLs' performance and is empowered to advise them about maintenance, management and other functions. Similar arrangements exist in Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland.

However, no provision exists enabling the corporations to give guidance to RSLs and housing associations regarding the achievement of energy efficiency in their housing stock. The Bill will rectify an omission in the Housing Act 1996 and in corresponding Scottish and Northern Irish legislation.

Under the new clause, the Housing Corporation will be able to give guidance on energy efficiency matters in general and on how RSLs which are still called housing associations in Scotland and Northern Ireland should co-operate with local authorities to assist them in the discharge of their duties when the Bill is enacted.

Clause 2

Energy conservation reports

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this we may take new clause 2 Energy conservation reports and new clause 3 Guidance as to requests for information.

Mr. Efford: I am grateful for hon. Members' kind comments and am happy to be the vehicle for the Bill. I also thank people outside the House who have done a great deal of work on the Bill.

Clause 2 will be replaced by new clauses 2 and 3 as drafted by the Parliamentary Counsel. Unlike my hon. Friend the Member for Worthing, West I am not used to procedure, and so I bow to his superior knowledge and sense of humour.

New clause 2 makes provision for registered social landlords and housing associations to supply local authorities with the information that they require. Local authorities will be enabled to obtain that information under the 1995 Act and the relevant Acts pertaining to Northern Ireland and Scotland.

Subsections (3) and (2) deal with the properties for which local authorities may require information. Subsection (3) requires registered social landlords I cannot get my tongue around that term, I keep trying to say housing associations to provide all the information that they possess.

Subsection (4) is important because it deals with enforcement, should that be necessary. I hope that such a measure need never be implemented. It is merely a long-stop that enables the Housing Corporation, which is the monitoring body for registered social landlords, to take account of compliance under subsection (3) when deciding whether an RSL is guilty of mismanagement. Subsection (4) acts as a gentle nudge to registered social landlords or housing associations that may be acting in an unsatisfactory or dilatory manner.

Subsection (5) deals with definitions and is self-explanatory. I commend the new clause to the Committee.

 

Ms Armstrong: New clause 2, which has been drafted in the same manner as new clause 1, seeks to improve the 1995 Act. That Act requires energy conservation authorities to prepare reports setting out energy conservation measures that the authority considers practical, cost-effective and likely to result in a significant improvement in the energy efficiency of the residential accommodation in their areas.

I welcome the provisions. As my hon. Friend the Member for Eltham has explained, the Bill will require RSLs and registered housing associations to assist energy conservation authorities in the preparation of their reports by providing them with information about the energy efficiency of their residential housing. Our amendments retain the basic intention of the Bill, but tighten up the wording.

One of the strengths of the Home Energy Conservation Act is that, by making local authorities energy conservation authorities, it recognises the key role that they can play as catalysts for change. It does not require them to do everything themselves, but to act as coordinators and encouragers in partnership with others who have a role to play. They know their areas and the people who live there. They know local businesses and voluntary bodies and they will knowthe landlords with whom the Bill is concerned.Co-operation and partnership are at the heart of the 1995 Act, and it is welcome that the Bill will further that theme.

The provisions also amend the Home Energy Conservation Act to give the Secretary of State a more specific power to issue guidance to energy conservation authorities. Rather than the more general wording of clause 3, new clause 3 makes it clear that such guidance may cover the categories of information and form, and the timing of relevant requests. We envisage wide consultation about guidance, which will involve local authorities and the housing association movement. It will also involve other bodies that helped with the development of existing guidance, including the Association for the Conservation of Energy, which is advising my hon. Friend the Member for Eltham.

I am confident that the Government can in this way devise something that will help both energy conservation authorities and the landlords described in the Bill. I shall endeavour to ensure that our guidance will meet the aims of my hon. Friend the Member for Eltham by encouraging co-operation and reasonableness on all sides.

As with the Home Energy Conservation Act at present, when the Bill is enacted, separate guidance will be produced and issued for England, Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland. The Committee will discuss commencement shortly, but I assure it that the Government intend to issue guidance for each territory as soon as possible once the Bill has received Royal Assent. Our aim is to have the legislation in place in England in time to help local authorities to complete their 1999 progress reports.

Mr. Peter Bottomley: I am delighted to follow the Minister, and to echo much of what she said. The legislation is uncontroversial. The new clause, which will replace clause 2 in the same way as new clause 1 replaces clause 1, will mean that the legislation will extend to the whole of the United Kingdom, and that all four countries will be covered without a delay for Northern Ireland.

It is worth recalling that relevant tenants generally stay longer in the same premises. The same applies to other social housing, which is usually in the hands of local authorities or, in the case of Northern Ireland, the Department of the Environment Housing Executive. There is a tendency for such tenants to be older and poorer, and for them to pay more for heating and to have less comfort. That is a generalisation. The circumstances in the private sector should be at least replicated, if not improved on. As in the private sector, when homes turn over roughly every seven years, people, should get information, and the opportunity to replace their boilers, to improve their system of heating controls, to improve their cavity wall or roof insulation and to deal with draft proofing. Too many examples remain of people paying œ20 a week to be cold in winter, when they should pay œ5, œ7 or œ10 a week to be warm. The Bill and new clauses, which I am delighted to support, will make that possible by drawing attention to what is going on, and, as was spelt out, by giving powers to the Minister to step in if people are not taking action that is reasonably practicable and necessary.

Clause 3

Short title and commencement

Mr. Efford: I beg to move amendment No. 3, in page 2, line 10, leave out subsection (2) and insert

    "(2) This Act, except this section, shall not come into force until such day as the Secretary of State may by order made by statutory instrument appoint; and different days may be appointed for different purposes or different areas.

    (b) the reference to an order made by statutory instrument shall be construed as a reference to an order which is a statutory rule for the purposes of the Statutory Rules (Northern Ireland) Order 1979.".

The Chairman: With this, it will be convenient to consider amendment No. 4, in page 2, line 11, at end insert

    "(4) Subsection (1) of section (Issue of guidance to registered social landlords and registered housing associations) extends to England and Wales only; subsection (2) of that section extends to Scotland only; and subsection (3) of that section extends to Northern Ireland only.

    (5) Except as provided by subsection (4), this Act extends to Northern Ireland.".

Mr. Efford: The clause deals with the reasonableness of the relationship between registered social landlords and local authorities. Mr. Efford: I am speaking to new clause 3.

Mr. Peter Bottomley: I support the change that the hon. Member for Eltham proposes to the commencement date. He has explained a complicated issue in a way that is understandable to us all. It is worth noting that one reason for the difference in Northern Ireland is that the Secretary of State or probably a junior Minister currently exercises the power of the housing authority because the Department of the Environment for Northern Ireland controls the Northern Ireland Housing Executive. Nobody expects that to remain the case. In time, the 26 local councils, their successors or devolved government might take direct control of housing.

I congratulate the hon. Member for Eltham and the Minister on proposing arrangements so that we will not have to wait for Orders in Council, which would delay the introduction of the Bill's provisions in Northern Ireland.

Ms Armstrong: The amendments to clause 3(2) of my hon. Friend's Bill are the only ones that make a real change. I understand why my hon. Friend wanted the commencement date to be written into the Bill he does not want his Bill to go through the whole parliamentary process and then to languish on the shelves. I assure him and the Committee, however, that the Government intend to introduce orders promptly so that the Bill's useful measures can be implemented.

There are two main reasons for using the appointed-day procedure. First, it will allow greater flexibility for the different parts of the United Kingdom. As the hon. Member for Worthing, West said, that is particularly necessary in Northern Ireland, which has separate commencement arrangements. The corporations want to consult before issuing guidance to registered social landlords and registered housing associations. Departments will also consult widely before they issue guidance to local energy conservation authorities.

I hope that the Committee agrees that that is entirely sensible and reflects no wish on the Government's part to delay implementation of the Bill's provisions.

New Clause 2

Energy conservation reports

    ". (1) After section 2 of the Home Energy Conservation Act 1995 (energy conservation reports) there is inserted

    `Provision of information by registered social landlords and registered housing associations.

    2A. (1) For the purposes of preparing or modifying a report under this Act, an energy conservation authority may request from a relevant body information regarding the energy efficiency of any relevant residential accommodation, other than houses in England and Wales or in Northern Ireland let on long leases to persons other than other relevant bodies. (2) In subsection (1) `relevant residential accommodation', in relation to a relevant body, means

    (a) any residential accommodation in respect of which the relevant body owes an obligation to another person to repair any part of the structure, (b) any other residential accommodation in England and Wales or Northern Ireland

    (i) in which the relevant body has the freehold interest, or

    (ii) which the relevant body holds under a lease and in which no other relevant body holds any superior interest,

    except accommodation in respect of which one or more other relevant bodies are under an obligation which is, or obligations which taken together amount to, an obligation to repair the whole of the structure, or

    (c) any other residential accommodation in Scotland

    (i) of which the relevant body is a heritable proprietor, or

    (ii) in which the relevant body has the interest of a tenant under a lease and in which no other relevant body has an interest of a landlord under that lease or an interest of an over landlord, and of which no other relevant body is a heritable proprietor,

    except accommodation in respect of which one or more other relevant bodies or one or more other persons are under an obligation which is, or obligations which taken together amount to, an obligation to repair the whole of the structure.

    (3) A relevant body shall comply with a request made under subsection (1) so far as it relates to information which is in its possession at the time of the making of the request.

    (4) In considering whether action needs to be taken to secure the proper management of the affairs of a relevant body or whether there has been mismanagement, the Corporation may have regard to the extent to which the relevant body has complied with its obligations under subsection (3).

    (5) In this section

    `the Corporation' means the Housing Corporation, Housing for Wales, Scottish Homes or the Department of the Environment for Northern Ireland;

    `house' has the same meaning

    (a) in relation to England and Wales, as in section 183(2) of the Housing Act 1985, and

    (b) in relation to Northern Ireland, as in Article 3(7) of the Housing (Northern Ireland) Order 1986;

    `long lease' means

    (a) in relation to a house in England or Wales, a lease falling within paragraph (a), (b), (c) or (e) of the definition of `long lease' in section 7(1) of the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993, and

    (b) in relation to a house in Northern Ireland

    (i) a lease granted for a term certain exceeding 21 years, whether or not it is (or may become) terminable before the end of that term by notice given by the tenant or by re-entry or forfeiture, or

    (ii) a lease granted for whatever term in pursuance of Chapter I of Part II of the Housing (Northern Ireland) Order 1983; and

    `relevant body' means

    (a) in relation to England and Wales, a registered social landlord within the meaning of the Housing Act 1996,

    (b) in relation to Scotland, a registered housing association within the meaning of the Housing Associations Act 1985, and

    (c) in relation to Northern Ireland, a registered housing association within the meaning of the Housing (Northern Ireland) Order 1992.

    (2) In section 6 of that Act (supplementary provisions), in subsection (2) for `or' there is substituted `and nothing in this Act, apart from section 2A,'.". [Mr. Efford.]

Brought up, read the First and Second time, and added to the Bill.
