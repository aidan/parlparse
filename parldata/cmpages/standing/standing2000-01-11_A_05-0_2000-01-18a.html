Mr. Oliver Letwin (West Dorset): My right hon. Friend the Member for Haltemprice and Howden (Mr. Davis) has been reading the Committee's proceedings, and I was doing the same late last night. I must beg the Committee's indulgence because, perhaps as a result of inspecting the inelegance of my exposition or the incoherence of the Economic Secretary's logic, I developed a severe eye infection and had to go to hospital late last night to have it dealt with. I discovered that under the new Labour Administration, the accident and emergency unit at Guy's hospital has been moved and that I had to traipse across London, as a result of which I am somewhat lacking in complete coherence today. I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I take a little longer than usual to get to the point.

I was explaining why we must support amendment No. 33. I reflected on the comments that have been made and perhaps the best and quickest way of making my argument is to examine the contrapositive position. Let us suppose that amendment No. 33 had been proposed in the opposite sense and that instead of saying that

    "The Comptroller and Auditor General may examine any performance statement which he receives",

it had proposed the opposite and stated that

    "The Comptroller and Auditor General may"

not

    "examine any performance statement".

That would have been a remarkable amendment. All members of the Committee will agree that the Comptroller and Auditor General has, through the many tenants of that office, always performed the role of acting for Parliament in the public interest. It would be remarkable if such a person were prohibited form examining performance statements that he receives.

As I understand it, all that my right hon. Friend's amendment would ensure is that we enshrine in law the principle that we would not wish to see defeated in law: the Comptroller and Auditor General may examine any performance statement. It is an extraordinarily weak amendment. My right hon. Friend could well have tabled one suggesting that the Comptroller and Auditor General must examine any performance statement or that Departments must submit performance statements. The amendment would simply ensure that no one can interpret the Bill as in any way prohibiting the Comptroller and Auditor General from examining performance statements.

I cannot believe that it can be the intention of the Economic Secretary or the Government so to prohibit the Comptroller and Auditor General, so I hope that we will hear either that she and the Government accept the amendment or that, again, an amendment is not necessary. If the latter is the argument, I suppose that there is more merit in it in this case than in some others, because I can see that, in the absence of a prohibition, it could be argued that the Comptroller and Auditor General may examine the statements. However, my right hon. Friend, in his capacity as the Chairman of the Public Accounts Committee, has told us that he and his colleagues on the PAC and the National Audit Office believe that there is a doubt. I hope that the Minister will make an unequivocal statement that the Government would expect the Comptroller and Auditor general to examine such of the performance statements as he wants to examine and would be enabled to do so, and that, if they do not want to accept the amendment, they will clarify the Comptroller and Auditor General's ability to examine the performance statements in some other way. That is a minimum condition of acceptability.

I want to relay to the Committee the fact that, in another incarnation, the guru on these matters said something very interesting. I am not often in receipt of letters from those who are not constituents of mine, as nobody who is not a constituent of mine has ever heard of me, but I was in receipt of a letter from someone called Mr. Des McConaghy of 5, Glenluce road, Liverpool. The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson):

I am grateful to right hon. and hon. Gentlemen for the points that they have made during debate, which now spans both last week and this week.

The Government strongly believe in the importance of modernising our public services and continually improving their quality, efficiency and customer responsiveness. In order to do that, we have set each Department, and some cross-cutting areas and units, public service agreements explaining what targets will be achieved, and by when, with the resources allocated. If that is to be a force for accountability, as the Government wish, the information that supports those targets must be of high quality. It cannot be overemphasised that that is in the interests of everyone--front-line staff, Ministers, Parliament and the public.

Amendment No. 25, which was rejected when we debated it last week, suggested that performance information should be the subject of a report from the accounting officer. Amendment No. 33 suggests that such a report could in turn be audited by the Comptroller and Auditor General. The right hon. Member for Haltemprice and Howden said that that would be a straightforward matter, and cited the example of our target of

    "increasing the coverage of nursery places for three-year-olds from 34 per cent. to 66 per cent. by 2002, focusing on the most deprived areas of the country."

He went on to explain that the Comptroller and Auditor General would then

    "check the systems for collecting the information, the veracity of the conclusions and whether the outcome has been represented fairly."--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 13 January 2000; c.137.]

How straightforward a matter is that? The CAG would need to be able to go into local authority, and even private sector, nurseries and form a judgment about how many nursery places each could reasonably be expected to provide. I do not believe that nurseries would welcome such intrusions. Moreover, he would have to form a judgment as to which were the most deprived areas of the country, which is a matter of policy if ever there was one. That highlights the difficulties that would take the Comptroller and Auditor General away from auditing and matters of fact into a political arena which he has always rightly avoided.

I believe that the right hon. Member for Haltemprice and Howden agrees with the need to stay out of the policy arena in these matters. I remind him of his comment when questioning the Chief Secretary to the Treasury at the Public Accounts Committee hearing last Wednesday. He said:

    "I am entirely happy that the Government should define what performance measurement should be. After all, that very often arises straight out of a political party's manifesto."

Traditionally, the role of the Public Accounts Committee and of the comptroller and Auditor General has been kept out of the political arena. It is important for the credibility of their role that they do not become tied up in political debates, but concentrate on matters of fact that can be substantiated.

Ministers are responsible for the performance of their Departments, hence the system of published targets formally presented to Parliament and the annual reporting against them that the Government have introduced.

Mr. Letwin: Will the Economic Secretary respond to a point made by my right hon. Friend the Member for Haltemprice and Howden during our proceedings last Thursday? During a discussion on bringing the Comptroller and Auditor General into politics, he said:

    "Let me point out quite how ridiculous this argument is. The CAG is explicitly tasked with the assessment of value for money in Government activity. Time and time again, that takes him close to the effectiveness of policy."--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 13 January 2000; c.139.]

Does the hon. Lady agree that that is so?

Miss Johnson: The Comptroller and Auditor General has a role in a number of areas and has undoubtedly done valuable work on value-for-money studies. The hon. Gentleman made a point about taking the CAG close to the effectiveness of policy. Taking something close is different from taking it into an arena. In this case, taking something into that arena is very different from being careful to avoid it, despite going close to it on occasions.

I should like to say something more general about the Government's approach to guaranteeing the quality of the information that they collect and publish. This is important for ensuring the credibility of the new performance management framework that they have introduced. Ministers and officials are aware that they need to be clear from the outset about which measures they have chosen to monitor and target, and why. They have to be sure that those measures provide a strategic focus for work in an organisation without wrecking its balance. Above all, they recognise the need for the information that they collect to be owned at the most front-line levels of organisations, while being subject to some form of checking to ensure accuracy and consistency.

Quality assurance checks are already in place. Information from many measures is needed by local line management to make day-to-day decisions and set priorities. It is often in the interests of people near the coal-face to ensure that the data that they return to the centre is of high quality. Departments have analytical services and internal auditors who make systematic checks of data quality and advise on methodology and technical matters. Policy experts in the Treasury also hold quarterly meetings with Departments to go through returns against their PSA targets, and use them as an opportunity to raise any data quality issues. The Government are doing more than that. Our new arrangements for national statistics will provide a source of central expertise.

Mr. Letwin: The Economic Secretary says that the Treasury will be able to raise the question of data quality with Departments. How will it be able to do that without conducting the kind of checks that nursery schools, for example, might find obnoxious?

Miss Johnson: The Treasury is looking at the way in which information supplied by Departments is panning out against their PSA targets, and raising quality issues. There is no reason to believe that that raises the issue to which the hon. Gentleman referred.

The Government's new arrangements for national statistics will provide a source of central expertise to help Departments with the statistical validation of the measurement systems, and an independent and external check in the form of the new statistics commission. Discussions are still going on as to which measures will be covered by the national statistics banner, but there is a growing presumption that statistical measures used to support PSA targets will be included.

The Government have not ruled out considering other options as the new system settles down. Officials from the Treasury and elsewhere are working with National Audit Office officials on a series of relevant projects. Novel ways in which to draw on the expertise of the National Audit Office, the Audit Commission and other bodies will be considered.

I am grateful to the hon. Member for West Dorset for his even-handed observation that

    "The amendment is not essential to the Bill".--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 13 January 200; c.142]

That is precisely the point. The Comptroller and Auditor General already produces some very useful reports on performance measures, and no Labour member of the Committee wants to undermine that in any way. Our proposals for the statistics commission show that we are committed to producing reliable figures, in which the public can have confidence and against which our success can be measured. As my right hon Friend the Chief Secretary pointed out during the recent Public Accounts Committee hearing, a number of bodies have legitimate interests in this matter, and we do not want to rule out other options at this stage.

The Government are conscious that this new and challenging system requires enormous efforts from departmental public servants on the ground who collect, analyse and interpret information.

We want the system to improve public services and make them more accountable, and we believe that it will. If the Opposition do not intend to withdraw the amendment, I invite the Committee to oppose it.

Mr. Davis: I shall start by reminding the Committee that the key aspect of the resource accounting revolution is the measurement of outcomes, on which, as I pointed out last week, the Bill is completely silent. If the matter is not handled properly to Parliament's satisfaction, that policy revolution may be lost.

Secondly, the Economic Secretary has advanced many technical arguments on the various audit options. The central issue is the role, rights and powers of Parliament, but her suggestion--if it amounts to any form of audit option--would bypass Parliament. The Public Accounts Committee found that suggestion unsatisfactory, and so should this Committee.

Thirdly, the Economic Secretary offered the alternative of the new statistics commission. The proposal for an independent statistics commission was one of the most attractive in the Labour party manifesto, but what is proposed in the Green Paper--or White Paper, I cannot remember which--does not constitute an independent body. It is in no way as independent as the Comptroller and Auditor General, who is appointed by the Prime Minister and the Chairman of the Public Accounts Committee, and can be dismissed only by the House of Commons and House of Lords. The Comptroller and Auditor General has a separate budget that is provided by Parliament, not the Treasury. Within the limits imposed by the Bill, he is completely unfettered. That is true independence. The new statistics commission offers not true independence but an appeal to the Prime Minister, who is being regarded as impartial. Clearly, in this context, no Prime Minister is impartial.

The Economic Secretary, who offered an Aunt Sally in respect of nursery schools, has not been properly briefed by the Treasury on the extent to which the National Audit Office liaises with the Audit Commission and private sector auditors.

Such liaisons work, provided that power rests with the Comptroller and Auditor General, who can issue directions and requirements for which the Economic Secretary's proposals do not allow.

The Economic Secretary referred twice, in detail, to the oblique comments that the Chief Secretary made to the Public Accounts Committee last week, on 12 January. He said that there was a risk that as the Committee

    "came closer to some of these perfomrance and target issues... you get drawn closer to the political arena".

The idea that a Select Committee should be near to something important in the political arena is a terrifying prospect. Those who drafted the National Audit Act 1983 foresaw such a pack of excuses; I shall not put it more lightly, as that is what it is. The Economic Secretary did not repeat her incorrect assertion of last week. It was perhaps not incorrect in her terms but misleading for the Committee to say that

    "Legal advice suggest that such constraints"--

the constraints in the 1983 Act--

    "would also prevent him from critising the performance measures and targets that the Government, as included in its service agreement".--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 13 January 2000; c. 101.]

Clearly, that is not the case--the Comptroller and Auditor General already does that, as he acknowledged in his closing remarks. The Treasury foresaw the possibility of such an argument, which is dealt with in section 6(2)of the 1983 Act. It says that subsection(1), which empowers the Comptroller and Auditor General to carry out examinations of economy, efficiency and effectiveness

    "shall not be construed as entitling the Comptroller and Auditor General to question"--

these words are important--

    "the merits of the policy objectives of any department, authority or body in respect of which an examination is carried out."

In debate at the time, the then Chief Secretary, Leon Brittan, said:

    "broadly speaking, it is in accordance with the current practice that policy objectives can be questioned in the sense of saying what they are, but their merits cannot be questioned. One can question the instruments used to secure these objectives",

which is what would happen in a value-for-money study. The then Chief Secretary went on to say:

    "if the Comptroller and Auditor General is saying, "I do not understand what the policy objectives are' that is different from saying "I think that they are wrong'. The latter he cannot do, but I do not see how one can stop him doing the former".

The formulation, "merits of policy objectives", was inserted as an amendment, in place of "policies". Its importance was recognised by Mr. St. John-Stevas, as he was then known, when he stated:

    ""policy'is very imprecise, and could have been interpreted by departments to obstruct attempts by the Comptroller and Auditor General to examine the instrument by which the Government chose to achieve their objectives."--[Official Report, Standing Committee C, 25 March 1983; c.117-20.]

Therefore, such a line of defence was foreseen in 1983. Subsequently, the section has been interpreted to mean that the Comptroller and Auditor General can look at how policy objectives are implemented, including whether policy instruments are consistent with the Government's objectives, as long as the merits of those objectives are not questioned. In terms of assessing performance measures, the Comptroller and Auditor General has already done so in the Meteorological Office--which was perhaps not especially controversial--in the Defence Evaluation and Research Agency, which would be controversial, and in the Benefits Agency, which would certainly be controversial.

I cannot see how any of the Economic Secretary's arguments stand up. Yet again, the mandarin line has been taken. I do not hold her responsible for that--she has to defend the line that she is given. That line was foreseen in 1983, when the powers of the Comptroller and Auditor General were last updated, on behalf of Parliament, to ensure that the Executive can be held to account. As a result of the unsaid half of the Bill, a revolution in policy delivery is in process. It is vital that Parliament has the right to have its say on that, and the right to be able to trust the figures put before it. There is only one person whom Parliament can properly trust in that regard: the Comptroller and Auditor General. I intend to press the amendment to a vote.

Mr. Davey: I should like to reiterate some of the comments of the right hon. Member for Haltemprice and Howden.

The amendment is designed to help the Government to achieve their stated objectives. The Government talk about modernising public services, improving their performance and making them more accountable. The Prime Minister spoke on television of his frustration at the slowness with which Whitehall meets his Government's aims and objectives, which include transforming and modernising public services. To speed up that process, the Government rightly want to impose performance measures. The framework of the Bill contains the correct approach.

If the Government really are frustrated and want to modernise public services, they should ensure that the proposed framework is not captured by mandarins. Whitehall could mess about with the framework--mandarins could turn it around or upside down. They are highly intelligent people who are more than able to invent performance measures and ways of collating information that shroud the real situation.

That is the crux of my argument. I am trying to help the Government. If they want to promote efficiency and quality in the public services, they must ensure that someone outside the bureaucracy checks the figures. Otherwise, when reports reach the Economic Secretary's desk and civil servants tell her how well the Treasury is doing, how will she be sure that what she is told is the truth? She may move to another Department or be promoted to the Cabinet, and have to judge how well the Treasury is doing. If she wants to be sure that she can rely on what she is told, there must be external validation.

The amendment is so much in the Government's interest that it should be completely acceptable to them. I accept that the new data that will be produced under the framework, which will involve public service agreements and output and performance analysis, will be complex and difficult to collect.

We are at the start of a process that contains the right measures. It will not be easy to get full agreement about how to make the system consistent or how collation should be conducted. The process will be complex, but that does not mean that we should leave the matter to mandarins and to the internal workings of Government; such complexity is a strong argument in support of external validation. However, the Economic Secretary's arguments in this context appeared to go in the other direction. If her arguments are unpicked, she appears to be pushing in the opposite direction to that which is suggested by the conclusions that she seeks to reach.

The Government have made much of the need for greater transparency in public services. Those new arrangements will be necessary because the VAT elements of departmental receipts would otherwise have to be dealt with like any other receipt.

Members of the Committee will recall that departmental receipts must by law be paid into the consolidated fund unless they are appropriations in aid. In practice, the VAT element of departmental income would be appropriated in aid, but doing so would require it to be accounted for gross, thereby making compliance with SSAP 5, which requires the netting off of VAT, impossible. The Treasury therefore intends to allow the VAT element of the receipts arising from such income to be exempt from the requirement that they be paid into the consolidated fund. That approach would build on the treatment formalised by section 21 of the Finance Act 1999, which allows a partial netting off. Incidentally, section 21 will be replaced by the new clause and will therefore be repealed by Government amendment No. 87.

All transactions between Departments and Customs will, of course, be fully recorded in each Department's accounting systems and will be subject to audit by the Comptroller and Auditor General.

The important points about our policy for the treatment of VAT are that the change will enable the Government to bring their accounts more into line with those of other bodies and that Parliament will continue to exercise control over departmental activities.
