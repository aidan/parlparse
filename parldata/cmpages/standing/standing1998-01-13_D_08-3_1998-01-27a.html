I shall speak to amendment No. 96 and, in particular, amendment No. 97, which was the subject of the last contribution before the welcome break. In speaking to amendment No. 97, the hon. Member for Daventry referred to the Secretary of State's letter dealing with the remit of the Low Pay Comission. Public debate about the Bill and, beforehand, about the policy has been dominated by the worries of Opposition Members about the effect of the national minimum wage on levels of employment.

Those worries are misfounded and misplaced. They are based on the assumption--the hon. Member for Daventry repeated it--that the price of labour is determined by the supply of and demand for it. That is not wholly the case in the real world. If it were, there would be no unemployment in India, for example. There is a great deal of unemployment in that country, which discredits the argument. All members of the Committee know that other factors--skill shortages, transport, general education levels, sectoral issues and geography--play a hugh role.

The hon. Member for Daventry presented as a danger the fact that employers would restructure their labour forces in the light of the Bill. If they do, good, because the objective of the Bill is precisely to force companies and organisations in the private and public sectors to abolish the scourge of low pay.

The hon. Member for Buckingham considered other economic implications--inflation in particular--which have not featured in the public debate. There is a relationship between the cost of employment and inflation, but it is interesting to note that although similar arguments were made about the effect of the implementation of the Equal Pay Act 1976 on employment and the RPI, they were not realised either in its immediate aftermath or, indeed, the years after. That is because the supply and demand of labour in this country does not take place within the confines of a purely free market--if, indeed, it does in any country.

The argument that differentials affect RPI and inflation is also subject to that false assumption. The engineering industry and the electrical and engineering union were referred to in that regard. The engineering and electrical industries have operated a minimum rate of pay for many decades. The differential impact of that is taken into account when that minimum rate is agreed by the two sides, but it is not true to say or imply that the Amalgamated Engineering and Electrical Union opposes the concept of a national minimum wage. It does not--it simply argues about the sensible implementation of it. That is one of the reasons why the Government wisely set up the Low Pay Commission.

We have to debate competitiveness. We did not talk about levels of investment, although, to be fair, the hon. Member for Buckingham referred to the letter from the Secretary of State. The most important factor for investment levels, especially those of small firms, is the level of investment in human capital. Yet we do not argue that there should be exceptions for certain small firms, sectors and geographical areas in relation to skill levels and qualifications. We have national vocational qualifications--we do not make exceptions for GCSE levels in, for example, east Anglia or the south-west. We apply a level playing field to the measurement of investment in human capital--that is, qualifications.

Similarly, the Bill will apply a level playing field to pay. It will simplify that intervention in the labour market in order to meet the objectives of equality and effectiveness. Of course there is a relationship between labour price, investment and profit, and of course that will apply to small firms, but I argue that the need for simplicity and a level playing field in small firms--a common definition is companies employing fewer than 20 people--would be greater if more complications and exemptions were introduced into the National Minimum Wage Bill and not less, as hon. Members sought to argue. Experience abroad, especially in the United States, provides evidence to back up that view.

One cannot separate arguments about the costs to industry from those on costs to public funds. For Tesco to make up the rate of its lowest-paid workers to, say, £4 per hour--a figure that I choose merely for convenience--would, despite a profit last year of more than £700 million, cost the equivalent of only 0.07 per cent. of its turnover. We can all find examples where the cost would be greater, but one cannot argue that the cost to industry has a deleterious effect on companies in sectors such as security and clothing and textiles, where, in the main, people are employed on low wages.

The argument applies to savings to the public purse, where there is a point of agreement. There is a balance between the cost to the taxpayer of benefits to subsidise low pay--we now know that the Opposition want to see a minimum income, which would have to be based on some definition, albeit with regional variations--and the huge tax saving to industry, not just to individuals, which results if the Treasury no longer has to subsidise low pay. There is a relationship.

But the Low Pay Commission has been asked to look at those implications in most regards. We all recognise that good legislation is that which lasts--it stands the test of the real world and cannot be watered down by this or future Governments. Good legislation must be simple, effective and provide for equality. By making exemptions, as proposed, we would undermine the legislation's effectiveness and turn what is a good Bill into a bad Bill. Complications would undermine it.

The problem arises not from the motive behind the amendments, but because there is no shared concept about what the Bill is trying to achieve. It will not treat the minimum rate of pay in the same way as other matters and variables do when it comes to calculating pay. There are many hundreds of collective agreements in this country, and the questions posed in the amendments come up every day. One criticism of industrial relations in this country is that there is too much complication in calculating these matters.

Mr. Woolas: I will not, if the hon. Gentleman does not mind.

This intervention in the labour market--which the Bill represents--attempts in the same way as the Equal Pay Act to provide a universal playing field across all of industry and the public services. Not all low pay is subject to collective bargaining. If it were, there would be no need for the Bill, and some of the points that have been made would be valid. The danger is not that the Bill would be too restrictive either by sector or by occupation, but that the minimum could be seen as a maximum. Points about London weighting are often based on the assumption that all that low-paid people in London have to look forward to is the achievement of the national minimum rate whatever it is. No member of the Committee would want to say to staff who work nights in London's catering and service industries that all that they can look forward to is a minimum wage. The minimum wage sets a minimum, not a maximum.

If Labour Members' arguments are correct--only time will tell--and if the policy raises standards and quality without the bad effects on price and investment that are feared by the hon. Member for Daventry, the minimum wage will be used as a minimum.

To differentiate according to company size would distort the labour market in a way that would not be subject to supply and demand, but to other factors that impact on the labour market. That would be detrimental to some small companies. In short, a minimum must apply to all small companies, not just to some.

The arguments about area or regional differentiation, which are relevant to amendment No. 82, are not pragmatic and would have the same effect. Differentiation according to occupation would be a lawyer's delight. Nothing takes up the time of industrial negotiators more than defining a job and who it applies to.

Most of all, the legislation is intended to be effective. To be effective, it must be enforceable. Many employers and employers' federations--we could argue about their number, but there are many--have supported the concept of a national minimum wage, never mind its practical implementation. One major reason for that is that they recognise the truth of Sir Winston Churchill's words. To be enforceable, a minimum wage must be universal, otherwise Churchill's dictum falls out of the window. It must also be simple.

I do not wish to call upon old Labour too much. The words of a disciple of new Labour, John Kenneth Galbraith, are appropriate and pertinent. I shall quote--I think that I am allowed to quote in a speech.

 

The Chairman: Briefly.

Mr. Woolas: Very briefly, I shall quote a sentence, although Galbraith's sentences are long. At the opening of ``The Affluent Society'', he states:

    ``Wealth is not without its advantages and the case to the contrary, although it has often been made, has never proved widely persuasive. But, beyond doubt, wealth is the relentless enemy of understanding. The poor man has always a precise view of his problem and its remedy: he hasn't enough and he needs more.''

A minimum wage should not be set at different levels in different parts of the country, for different occupations within different sectors. Such a wage would not be enforceable, and it would not be understood.

The point is very important. If the minimum wage is not understood, and it will not be if it is set at different levels throughout the country, the genuine policy worries that come from the practical experience of Labour and Opposition Members about detrimental effects on the labour market, on inflation and on investment, would become reality. That would occur as it would have done if exceptions had been made in the Equal Pay Act 1976. A shift in female labour would have occurred, to the detriment of companies and industries in parts of the country.

If the Bill is to be effective, it must be universal. When the national health service was introduced, it was universal. When people talked about medical provision being free at the point of delivery, they did not say that that was except for people working in companies employing fewer than 20 people or for people working in the clothing and textile industry in Northern Ireland. They said that it had to be universally free. The same was true of universal education and suffrage. The national minimum wage can work only if it is universal.

I accept that the amendment would give powers to exempt, rather than to instruct an exemption. However, the Bill must make it clear that the national minimum wage has to be universal to be workable. If it does not, the national minimum wage will become a problem for industry and for people in low-paid work. It will not represent an opportunity to abolish the scourge of low pay.

For that reason, I invite the Committee to reject the amendment.

Underlying the structure of the Bill as it is presented is an assumption that people go out to work with one motive only--to earn money. That is perhaps not an unreasonable general assumption; selling their labour in the work force is indeed the primary motive for most people.

I do not want to refer in detail to future clauses in the Bill. However, the Government have made provisions within the Bill that would exclude those who work on a purely voluntary basis. That must be right; we welcome that exclusion.

There are many people who are active in the economy in a marginal but none the less important role who are willing to work under certain circumstances for a wage that is purely nominal and considerably less than any of the figures that have been suggested as a likely outcome for the minimum wage.

Charity shops are a now familiar feature of our high streets--alas. I say alas because their presence tends to reflect a downturn in retail trade in some of our less prosperous high streets. I will respond in a charitable way. I do not think that there is anyone in the Committee who has not personally been involved in work for charitable or voluntary organisations, either within the context of their family or as an individual, although we will leave out the hon. Member for Buckingham's Monday Club adventures for the purposes of this debate. In general terms, we are all committed to the concept and principle of volunteering in the community.

Many voluntary and charitable organisations were set up for the relief of poverty and to help people who had been isolated or marginalised. Some people might therefore find it difficult to understand why organisations set up for the purposes of relieving poverty should perpetuate it, which is what Conservatives call for when they say that charities should not pay their workers a national minimum wage.

There are about 320,000 full-time equivalent employees of registered charities in the United Kingdom. That not insignificant number of people are employed by charities to ensure that the voluntary part of the charity operates effectively. That is a marrying of arrangements; full-time resources are available to charities to ensure that volunteers operate effectively. Charities understand that, as should Opposition Members.

Issues relating to people with special needs have also been raised. I worked in special needs for many years before entering the House. I still have a keen interest in it and I am still the president of a charity that works in special needs. I will return to these issues in a moment.

The issues relating to volunteers are specifically covered by clause 41. In response to the hon. Member for Daventry, I say that I will reflect on our discussions tonight about volunteers so that when we debate clause 41 it will ensure that genuine volunteers, who work for no payment at all or for genuine expenses, are not included within the provisions of the Bill. I will ensure that the Bill is drafted sufficiently vigorously to ensure that no one who is a genuine volunteer is covered by the national minimum wage. The Government do not intend that they should be.

I shall now say why we will oppose amendments Nos. 28 and 30. Amendment No. 28 paves the way for amendment No. 30, and discriminates by sector employment against persons employed by registered charities. Amendment No. 30 is an arbitrary and unjustified amendment for a number of reasons. A charity worker is of equal value, and often it is said that he is of greater value, to the well-being of the community as other workers. Why should genuinely employed workers face the risk of being exempted from the minimum wage simply because they work for a charity? Opposition Members know that charities are extremely diverse in their functions: there are 180,000 registered charities and another 180,000 non-registered charities. Therefore charities form a diverse group of organisations. For example, a cleaner in a centre providing leisure facilities, a teacher working for an independent school and a care provider may all work for different charities. The amendment would achieve the absurd result that such workers could be exempted from the national minimum wage while their occupational counterparts, who do not work in charities, would be entitled to a minimum wage.

Conservative Members are always keen to ensure that market forces can let rip; they should therefore reflect that the amendment could also create a distortion in competition. Registered charities might compete with commercial bodies, or with a local authority, for the provision of local authority services, and would be exempted from paying proper rates of pay by the operation of this clause if the amendment were passed. Such charities would still be able to compete for the provision of services, many of which would be for vulnerable people in the community.

By contrast, one reason why the Government wish to introduce a national minimum wage is to create a level playing field. Some will claim that charities should be exempted to prevent them from going bust. We are not forcing charities to pay their volunteers the national minimum wage: they will be exempt. However, I see no reason why their workers should not be entitled to it. It is not just my view. My hon. Friend the Member for Basildon said that not only does the NCVO wish to dissociate itself from the amendments, but that in its submissions to the Low Pay Commission it supported the establishment of a national minimum wage. Therefore, charities support the concept of a national minimum wage in principle and will support its implementation.
