Pension Sharing Orders: England and Wales

    `(2C) No pension sharing order shall be made in favour of a person aged less than 40 years.'—(Mr Quentin Davies).

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. Quentin Davies (Grantham and Stamford): May I welcome you back to the Committee, Mr. McWilliam, to preside over our deliberations.

On the first day of our discussion of pension splitting, I explained that the Opposition are prepared to entertain the addition of pension sharing on divorce to the statute book and the range of instruments available to matrimonial courts, provided that there are safeguards and that the power is used only as a reserve power to ensure that justice is done. I made it clear that if it is not used as a reserve power, it would contribute to even greater injustice, would have some perverse consequences on people's lives and behaviour, and would be a source of additional costs. We tabled amendments, including amendment No. 162, which would have constrained the courts' use of the power, but the amendment was rejected. We have been forced to table a series of specific amendments to achieve the same purpose in a more crude but targeted way.

One of the great problems of pension splitting or sharing is that it is irrevocable. A capital sum is paid away and a pension is permanently foregone. That may cause injustice, but that injustice may not arise for a long time, so it may not be anticipated. The younger the parties to the pension sharing, especially the beneficiary of the pension sharing, the greater the problem, because the courts will have to take a view not on the position now or in the next few years, but on what may be a just solution in 30, 40 or even more years.

That is the background against which we tabled amendment No. 76, which would limit pension sharing to cases in which the beneficiary of the pension sharing—the recipient of the pension credit or the transferee—is over the age of 40 and would prevent such sharing when the beneficiary is under 40.

I have acknowledged that 40 is an arbitrary cut-off age. It is not an ideal way of resolving the problem. Hon. Members may be tempted to suggest that it is the wrong cut-off age and that 35 or 37 and a half may be more appropriate, but any figure would be open to the charge that it is arbitrary, so I shall say in advance that I have an open mind to suggestions on the cut-off age. We come back to Bernard Shaw and the actress. We are trying to establish certain principles in the amendment and we are less concerned with the degree to which those principles will be implemented or the extent to which implementation may be given to them. Clearly, pension sharing is more problematic when the parties are less than 40 than when they are closer to pensionable age. For example, at 58 it is easier to predict what the circumstances are likely to be when the pension share takes effect and is translated into an income stream for the beneficiary. That makes it easier for the court to determine what effect the pension share will have on the beneficiary, the member of the scheme who, as the transferor, is the loser, and other members of the family. The courts should be sensitive to the effect of pension sharing on families, which is dealt with in the next amendment. It is more difficult to predict circumstances when people are under 40.

Most of our experience of the sad issue of divorce confirms the adage that divorce is usually a disaster for one party. Indeed, it may be a disaster—or a liberation—for both parties. All too often, however, at least one party loses out badly on divorce, either psychologically or materially. If the party who loses psychologically is also the main loser financially, which, under pension splitting, is the person with the largest pension asset, the damage is enhanced.

Common human experience leads us to conclude that it is difficult at the time of a divorce to predict which party will suffer most or least, or whether the person who might appear to suffer the most will be able successfully to rebuild his or her life. Those decisions are hard to make for people in their 20s and 30s. It is especially difficult for the courts to make the just distribution that is required under the law when the effect of their decision will not, in the unique case of pension sharing, come on stream and take effect for 20 or 30 years. It is also impossible for those arrangements to be altered in the light of changing circumstances and if the courts have got it wrong.

In most cases where courts decide on the allocation of assets and distribution of cash flows under section 21 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, the arrangements can be varied in response to an application by the parties for changes to ancillary relief. Pension earmarking arrangements exist to allow for a particular form of ancillary relief that is related to pensions.

The Chairman: Order. I am finding it difficult to relate the hon. Gentleman's remarks to persons ``aged less than 40''. He is speaking in general terms.

Mr. Davies: I am speaking about the considerable differences in the application of earmarking, which is the alternative to pension sharing for people under 40. Younger people can apply to have an earmarking order varied. If a court has made an arrangement that seems sensible—

The Chairman: Order. Persons of any age can have a court order varied.

Mr. Davies: Yes, but no one can have a pension-sharing order varied. It follows that if a pension-sharing order is made before someone is 40, it will stand for a long time before it comes into effect. The income stream that it produces in favour of the beneficiary is a loss of a potential income stream that the pension scheme member, the transferor, would otherwise have expected. Circumstances might change substantially during that period. However, unlike other forms of ancillary relief—a contrast that must be drawn especially in relation to people below the age of 40—there is no scope for the courts to vary a pension-sharing order, however much they might think, if they were presented with the case, that a variation should, in justice, be made. That goes to the heart of the amendment, which intends to remove a potential source of injustice. If it is not a source of injustice to impose a rigid system of pension sharing, applied once and for all below the age of 40, and perhaps below the age of 30, when it is completely impossible to predict the life paths of the beneficiary or the donor of the pension credit over the subsequent 20 or 30 years, I do not know what is.

In section 25A of the 1973 Act, the courts are enjoined to take account of,

    ``the income, earning capacity, property and other financial resources which each of the parties to the marriage has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future.''

There are several unsatisfactory aspects to that. What is ``foreseeable''? That is not defined. At the age of 58, it is relatively easy, in normal circumstances, to foresee the financial position of the parties two years leater, at the age of 60. However, it is unreasonable to expect a court, or indeed anyone else, accurately to foresee the financial future of parties 20 or 30 years hence. It is unreasonable to require the courts to make such a judgment, but that will be their obligation as a result of the combined effect of section 25 of the 1973 Act and the Bill. When there is such a large gap between the time when the court makes its decision and the consequences of its determiniation for the parties, justice cannot properly be served and an unreasonable burden is placed on the courts.

We know that the courts already find it impossible adequately to carry out the injunctions of the law contained in section 25. That section is already a source of considerable abuse, and indeed merriment, to divorce lawyers. The issue arises of whether it is possible to predict that someone is about to inherit a large amount of money from a grandmother. Of course, the court cannot take that into account because the grandmother might change her will. That is an aspect of

    ``the income, earning capacity, property and other financial resources which each of the parties to the marriage has or is likely to have in the foreseeable future''

that cannot be taken into account. Are courts supposed to determine the course of people's careers—for example, that someone is about to be promoted to the position of partner at Goldman Sachs and take part in a flotation? No.

Mr. Davies: The amendment intends to distinguish clearly between people who are 20 years and more away from their prospective retirement and people who are less than 20 years away from retirement, which relates to people who are below 40 and above 40. Given that people who are considerably older than 40 are much nearer to retirement than those who are considerably younger than 40, it follows that my argument will apply a fortiori with even greater force to those who fall into the latter category.

The law requires the courts to predict the future income, property and other financial resources of parties, but it does not require them—as the Bill will—to look 20 years and more ahead. That is the significance of the age threshold of 40. As I said, the courts find it extremely difficult to carry out the task already required of them in section 25. We should be conscious of that when imposing even more burdens on them in future.

Will courts also be required to take account of an individual's re-marriage prospects? Will we have the absurdity of judges evaluating the attractiveness, or marriage prospects, of individuals? That prospect, which no one welcomes, is a source of great merriment in the legal profession. As it is, courts do not take seriously their obligation to take into account all the factors that might affect the income, earning capacity, property and other financial sources that each party has, or is likely to have, in the foreseeable future. If we place a further requirement on courts to take account of an individual's financial prospects more than 20 years ahead, we will open the door to inevitable and serious injustice.

It would be grotesque to allow courts to make an irrevocable pension split favouring a particular party to the marriage. During the succeeding 20 or 30 years, when the parties are aged between 40 and 60 or 65, the party who has a large part of his or her pension taken away may get into difficulties and be unable to rebuild that pension. He or she may develop a history of redundancy and employment problems, may experience marital and other personal problems, and retire in a state of penury. Such a person could have benefited from money that, after all, he or she had earned up to the time that the pension was split. On the other hand, the beneficiary of that pension split may have a successful time during the intervening 20 or 30 years. He or she may make a successful marriage, have a successful career, or win the national lottery.

Under the pension-splitting arrangement, courts will not be able to re-examine cases. Where a pension was originally split under section 21 of the 1973 Act, which allows for periodical payments, lump sum payments, or the division of property, or where a court decided that the pension asset should properly be taken into account, earmarking orders are subject to review by the courts on application by the aggrieved party. However, under the Bill, where a party in his or her 60s and 70s does not need a pension, regards it as an embarrassing surplus, has forgotten about a marriage contracted 20 years ago and cannot account for the additional source of income, and where the second party to the marriage needs money desperately and is applying for means-tested benefit, such a case will not be subject to re-examination. That is grotesque and would offend most people's sense of natural justice. However, that will be the precise consequence of an irrevocable pension split. Unless the pension-sharing power is regarded as a reserve power, only to be used explicitly in the interests of justice to achieve a just settlement, it will produce perverse results of this kind. I have no doubt that it will discredit itself in due course, but it may be a long time before we have the opportunity to look at the matter again. Indeed, section 25A has already been discredited in the courts, but it is still impossible to do anything about it. However, this would be a much worse provision, because it would not be revisable in the light of subsequent circumstances—that is the essence of the injustice.

However, if the Committee accepts our amendment—and I am prepared to bargain either side of the age limit of 40—that would at least limit the damage and abuse, and the extreme cases, such as those that I have described, would not occur. Of course, someone might say that a person aged 41 is still a long way from retirement, and that a settlement based on his or her circumstances at that time could be hideously inappropriate some 20 years later and could not be revised. It could therefore be said that the age limit of 40 does not give adequate protection, and that I should have said 45 or 50. We ought to have a discussion on that because the issue is of key importance.

We would have been happy to withdraw the amendment, had the Government accepted amendment No. 162. It is only because they pig-headedly refused to do so—confident, no doubt, in their substantial majority—that they now find themselves in this position, where the kind of cases that I have outlined could and will arise, unless something is done. This is Parliament's last chance, apart from Report stage. We have moved on past amendment No. 162, and it is only by limiting the potential damage by tabling amendments of the kind that we are now discussing that we shall ensure that pension splitting adds to the sum of justice and does not, on a net basis, detract from it.
