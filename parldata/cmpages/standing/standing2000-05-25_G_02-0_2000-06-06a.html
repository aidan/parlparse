The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 38, 40, 47, 48 and 50 to 55.

Mr. Hutton: This group of amendments relates to the definition of a children's home in the clause and makes a series of consequential amendments. The amendments make it clear that an establishment providing care and accommodation for children is not a children's home if it provides foster care as defined by the Children Act 1989, as amended by the Bill.

Amendment No. 37 is purely technical and has no effect on the meaning of the clause. No one would expect a home run by a parent or relative of a child to be regulated. Therefore, we do not intend the homes of foster parents to be regulated by the National Care Standards Commission. Amendment No. 38 ensures that that is the case. We define foster carers in amendment No. 40.

Amendments Nos. 47 and 48 insert the definitions of private fostering and local authority foster parents from the Children Act into the Bill. It is important to introduce the Children Act definition of private fostering into the Bill, because otherwise we could create a loophole that allowed unscrupulous people to avoid registering their establishment as a children's home by pretending to be private foster carers. That would be contrary to one of the Bill's main purposes of strengthening and improving the regulation of children's homes. Perhaps the risk is small, but we are not prepared to accept it.

All children's homes will be registered with the commission, so the Children Act description of registered homes as private homes with four or more children is no longer relevant. Amendment No. 50 clarifies the position in relation to section 63 of the Children Act. Amendments Nos. 51 and 53 ensure that the limits on the numbers of foster children set out in the Children Act also apply in the Bill. Amendment No. 52 redefines private foster carers as those who care for children in their own homes, to create a clearer distinction between private fostering and children's homes. It also provides powers to make regulations to define when a child is being cared for in a person's own home, so that we can adjust the new definition at the margins as required.

Amendments Nos. 54 and 55 are consequential to the changes that we are making to the relationship between children's homes and private fostering. By making those changes, we will ensure that there is no scope for confusion about when an establishment providing care and accommodation as a children's home needs to be registered as such. There will be no scope for people to manipulate the rules and claim to be foster carers to escape effective registration. The amendments do not reflect any substantive policy change. There has always been a need for a clearer distinction between a children's home and a foster home, and we have taken the opportunity presented by the Bill to provide that. It is particularly important to get the definitions right now, as the abolition of the concept of small private children's homes that need not be registered creates the potential for confusion. The amendments will prevent any such confusion from arising.
