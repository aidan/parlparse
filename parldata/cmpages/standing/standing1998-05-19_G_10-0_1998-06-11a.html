Mr. Andrew Lansley (South Cambridgeshire): I apologise for transgressing the rules of order earlier, and shall now endeavour to make the speech that I would have otherwise made, but shall remain in order.

I hope not to detain the Committee too long, but I have been prompted to speak by the Minister's reply and on reading once again the notes on clauses and article 88. It is clear, as the Minister helpfully explained, that the intention of the clause is to make certain that, when there is no corresponding EC regulation that deals with a sector, national authorities, by virtue of article 88, are enjoined to make provision for the control of anti-competitive agreements in accordance with the provisions of article 85.

That seems slightly curious. Is the clause necessary? In the absence of the clause, would article 88 not still have force, requiring national authorities to operate in relation to sectors that are not covered by EC regulations in the character of article 85? In the rest of the Bill, the prohibition is designed to achieve an article 85-style prohibition that applies within the United Kingdom in the same way as in relation to any other sector of industry. Therefore, although a sector such as the air transport industry may not be covered by the regulation, in so far at it is covered by the prohibition in the United Kingdom, it would clearly be covered by the article 85-style prohibition rendered in clause 2.

I am therefore at a loss to understand why clause 11 is necessary. In the absence of clause 11, would an article 85-style prohibition none the less be given effect in relation to any sector, including those that were not already covered by EC regulations? Stemming from that, if the Minister explains that, in the absence of clause 11, there would be a problem about jurisdiction, why is there no corresponding provision relating to article 86 and the chapter II prohibition? I do not see one.

If there is a difficulty in the application of article 88 in relation to the chapter I prohibition, why is there not a corresponding difficulty in relation to article 86 and the chapter II prohibition? Article 88 applies to the article 86 and chapter II prohibition in exactly the same way as it does to article 85 and the chapter I prohibition. I am not sure that Ministers can have it both ways. Question number one is, what would happen if the clause were not there, and why do we need it? Question number two is that if it is necessary, and if there is a justification for that, why is there no corresponding clause that deals with chapter II prohibition and article 86?

Mr. McCartney: The principal purpose of the amendments is to make it clear that if an agreement or course of conduct has infringed a chapter I or chapter II prohibition, as the case may be, but has come to an end, the director general can still penalise a person for infringement. Clearly, it would be no deterrent if an infringer were to escape penalties merely by bringing the agreement or the conduct to an end. Proceedings relating to past activities are by no means uncommon in the EC system. For example, the European Court of Justice is currently considering a major case on liner conferences in which the agreement in question came to an end some time ago.

That clarification is achieved by Government amendments Nos. 94 and 95, which substitute "has infringed" for "infringes" in subsections (1) and (2) of clause 37, which empowers the director general to impose penalties. When the director general is contemplating imposing a penalty, he must consider past infringement whether infringement is continuing will make no difference.

The remainder of the amendments are either directly consequent upon that change or as a result of the review that we carried out on the tense used in the Bill in relation to a prohibition being infringed. We concluded that we should make a few changes for the sake of consistency. For example, in clause 16(1) and clause 25(1), "does not infringe" should read "has not infringed" if the provision is to match that of clause 14(2), clause 23(2), clause 26 (1) and clause 32(1). That is achieved by amendments Nos. 89 and 90.

Amendment agreed to.
