Clause 19

Solicitation of gifts and investment of money
 

Mr. Spring: I beg to move amendment No. 40, in page 19, line 31, leave out "shall be under a duty to" and insert "may"

Clause 18(1) states:

    "NESTA shall be under a duty to seek gifts of assistance, money, land and other property, but in seeking or accepting any such gifts NESTA shall comply with any directions given to them by the Secretary of State."

The purpose of the amendment is twofold. I shall not detain the Committee for too long, because we have already had a comprehensive discussion on NESTA.

First, the amendment is intended to re-establish the arm's-length principle, which is under attack throughout the Bill. Labour's pre-election commitment was to set up an independent trust to fund creative talent we certainly agree with that but we find that the proposed body is different from that proposal, and that the role of the Secretary of State is different from what we envisaged. Secondly, the amendment would remove from NESTA a duty which is unlikely to be carried out in its current form.

As we have already argued, it is highly unlikely that NESTA will be able to meet its objectives as set out in the Bill, because of its inherent contradictions. My noble Friend Baroness Rawlings rightly described it as a mongrel. There is a contradiction between NESTA's charitable nature and its objective of seeking venture capital. That objective is incompatible with the independent trust promised by the Government.

The Government are in danger of setting up a body that will fail because it has been saddled with mutually exclusive objectives that make it impossible for it to carry out what it has been set up to do. NESTA will be a commercial body with charitable aims. Of course, it will be expected to attract money from the private sector, although it appears to be the Government's intention to continue funding it from the lottery.

NESTA is supposed to be an independent commercial organisation, yet responsibility for investment and for its endowment will be delegated to the National Debt Commissioners. In the language of the Government's pledge, NESTA will be an independent national trust for talent, yet the Bill provides it with the characteristics of a quango.

To speak specifically to the amendment, it is a moot point whether the Government will impose a duty on NESTA to seek outside contributions or whether it will simply be allowed to do so. Frankly, I doubt whether NESTA will be successful in attracting outside funds either way. That is the nub of our objection.

We accept that NESTA should be allowed to seek money from sources other than Government doing so would at least give it a limited additional measure of independence. The amendment would allow it to do so, rather than giving the Secretary of State the power to interfere and require it to do so. That is the distinction that we seek to make.

We have discussed the contradiction in the way that NESTA has been set up, but does the Minister envisage that NESTA's role and structure will be changed in future? He has mentioned his commitment that nothing will be added to the œ200 million endowment. Although he has given that assurance, we are still concerned.

If, as we fear, NESTA cannot attract additional funds because of the inherent contradiction, what does the Minister envisage will happen to restore its credibility? For example, would it be given an opportunity to adopt the more independent status that we have in mind which would enhance its credibility and make it easier for it to attract donations? In moving the amendment, we ask the Government to consider strengthening NESTA's independence without undermining its ability to carry out its mission. I seek assurances from the Minister that he will consider that important principle.

Mr. Fisher: This is a strange amendment for the Opposition to move. It would loosen NESTA's responsibility and duty to go into the marketplace and try to fend for itself. We hope that the Committee will reject the amendment.
