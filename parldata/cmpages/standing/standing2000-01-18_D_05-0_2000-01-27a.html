Clause 39

Arrest without warrant

Mr. Simon Hughes: I beg to move amendment No. 106, in page 18, line 9, leave out

    'reasonably suspects to be a terrorist'

and insert

    'identities as having committed an offence under this Act.'

The amendment raised the first important issue that comes under part V of the Bill, and is about the drafting of the qualification in the clause. Clause 39 is about arrest without warrant on terrorist-related issues. Subsection (1) states:

    "A constable may arrest without a warrant a person whom he reasonably suspects to be a terrorist."

My amendment would replace the last part of that sentence with the phrase

    "identifies as having committed an offence under this Act".

I shall explain why we are concerned about the current drafting.

There is a suggestion that clause 39 contravenes the European convention on human rights under article 5(1)(c), which refers to

    "the lawful arrest or detention of a person effected for the purpose of bringing him before the competent legal authority on reasonable suspicion of having committed an offence or when it is reasonably necessary to prevent his committing an offence or fleeing after having done so".

The question is whether that would allow the arrest of someone reasonably suspected of being a terrorist by nature of a previous conviction--someone who had a terrorist past might fall within that broad definition.

The proposed alternative would be a more specific trigger for an arrest because it would require someone to have been identified as having committed an offence under the Act. That is narrower than the general band of offences. It would be even tighter drafting if a new offence, not previously dealt with, were introduced. As drafted, the Bill has a retrospective element, so there may be a risk of the police arresting someone who may be alleged to have committed terrorist acts before the Act comes into force or has committed such acts that have already been dealt with.

This probing amendment raises important issues about double jeopardy and returning to matters that have been dealt with before. If additional powers are to be conferred, under the umbrella of dealing with terrorism, we must not draw them more widely than we need to.

Mr. Charles Clarke: This is a reasonable amendment on which to air the issues. However, we hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment because it would mean that only a person whom an officer had identified as having committed an offence under the Act could be arrested under the special terrorist arrest power. The ability to arrest if an officer reasonably suspects an individual of being a terrorist is an important qualification. It is in line with the current prevention of terrorism Act and it gives officers an arrest power that can be used when there may not be reasonable suspicion that an actual offence has been committed. The power is important because experience has revealed some difficulties in combating terrorism without it.

As to the wording, it is important to stress that the courts, not the police officer, will ultimately take a view on whether the offence has been committed. The Bill suggests that the police officer should make that judgment, but it is ultimately for the courts to decide.

We continue to take the view that the arrest power is necessary when an officer has reasonable suspicion that the person is, or has been, involved in the commission, preparation or instigation of acts of terrorism. That would allow the police to act at the point when they have a reasonable suspicion that a person is involved in terrorist activity, without waiting until they have reasonable suspicion about the offence that the individual is going to commit or has committed. Waiting until that point could mean that a bomb goes off and evidence of the incident is destroyed. That is why the power is so important.

I should like to explain a couple of points to the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey. First, the power does not give carte blanche to the police to round up people whenever they like. I share the hon. Gentleman's concern about that potential effect, but the wording does not allow it, as there must be a reasonable suspicion that a person is involved in an act of terrorism. That suspicion is reviewed by an independent officer regularly during the first 48 hours of detention, which will be extended only if a judge is persuaded of the case.

The arrest power may not be used for fishing trips or intelligence-gathering exercises. It has been most often used thus far in Irish cases, but not exclusively so. The reasons that I have set out for needing this special power apply just as much to some international or even domestic groups. The police regularly underline to us the importance that they place on this power in preventing and detecting terrorist crime. That is why the Government believe that we must keep it.

On the human rights point, I would observe--as I have previously--that we are of the view, having taken considered legal advice, that the Bill is consistent with the Human Rights Act 1998. In the Brogan case, the European Court confirmed that this arrest power was not incompatible with article 5.1 of the European convention on human rights, which talks of

    "reasonable suspicion of having committed an offence".

The court ruled that, although no specific offence was listed in conjunction with the arrest power, the way in which the power was constructed was analogous to an offence.

I hope that I have responded to the reasonable concerns raised by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey and that he will therefore consider withdrawing his amendment.

First, what is the justification for giving the Secretary of State power in this case? The second point relates to the word "possible", or, to use an alternative word, "practicable".

The schedule provides:

    "The Secretary of State may direct the place where a person is to be detained."

The amendment proposes:

    "A person arrested shall be taken by a constable to a police station as soon as possible after the arrest".

The obvious question is why the Secretary of State should make the initial decision, as opposed to the police deciding to take action and to take someone to a police station, as is normal. There is an argument that that allows more political interference in the case than is justifiable.

If the provision were replaced by the amendment, that would not enable a Home Secretary or a Northern Ireland Secretary to indicate to the police a view on where a detention should take place. However, once the Secretary of State is given the power to determine that, it has all sorts of potential consequences. It might give those investigating the case a view that might not otherwise have been in their minds. It would allow the Secretary of State total discretion and thereby effectively take the investigation out of the normal police process. As a general principle, the Bill does not do that; it retains the normal process of police investigation, but gives the police additional powers -- wider powers of arrest and of detention, and so on. It does not seek to replace the normal investigative agencies by governmental investigative agencies.

We have tried in Britain to keep separate the powers of the Executive and of those who act as the law enforcement agencies on behalf of the Executive. Over the past few weeks, the Secretary of State has on many occasions demonstrated that he has powers that are separate from and additional to the powers of the police. The power of extradition is a good example of that. After someone has been arrested in such a case, judicial and quasi-judicial considerations by the Secretary of State are then involved. It is important that, even in the area of terrorism, we keep the powers separate and do not give Ministers powers that override the normal powers of investigation and processing.

When that requirement was first introduced into legislation, the police were suspicious of it, but it had been made necessary by a series of well-publicised miscarriages of justice and botched or even, I fear, corrupt police investigations. The previous Government took that decision because of the special circumstances of terrorist inquiries and because of the fear that tape recording would indirectly lead to the drying up of the supply of information to the police because it would make it easier for terrorist organisations to threaten and blackmail those whom they suspected of being police informers. That point was discussed by Lord Lloyd in his report; his conclusion was that terrorist suspects should be brought within the statutory regime of tape recording. However, he did so after making the point that

    "intelligence is the most potent weapon in the counter-terrorism armoury"

and that terrorist organisations were well aware of that and would be, in his words,

    "energetic and ruthless in their efforts to choke off the flow of intelligence to the authorities."

Lord Lloyd expressed the concern that when a terrorist suspect was released following arrest by, for example, the RUC, the terrorist organisation concerned would make every effort to establish whether that man had given any information away, deliberately or otherwise. Lord Lloyd added that the task of the terrorist organisations

    "would be made easier if there were a taped record of the interview."

The detainee could then be ordered--on pain of violent retribution--to initiate an action against the police or to seek disclosure of a copy of the tape recording during the course of criminal proceedings.     "Such a state of affairs would put informants at risk and jeopardise the flow of vital intelligence".

From a reading of his report, what seems to have persuaded Lord Lloyd that the balance of evidence was in favour of bringing terrorist interviews into the provisions of general criminal law was that the view of the police service had changed. My next point relates to who should exercise the power to grant extensions of detention. Life starts to resemble "A Dance to the Music of Time" for me at this point. I remember being at the Home Office when the Brogan case came up and the Government were trying to frame their response to it. I again emphasise that the then Government and the then Secretary of State's decision to seek a derogation from the European convention on human rights in respect of terrorists in Northern Ireland was a decision taken only after the most careful reflection on international legal questions and on the practical question facing the Government of how to counter the threat from the IRA and other Irish terrorist organisations.

As members of the Committee will know, in November 1988 the European Court held that the detention of applicants under the PTA for more than four days in the Brogan case constituted a breach of article 5.3 of the convention because the applicants had not been brought "promptly" before a judicial authority. About a month later, Lord Hurd, the then Home Secretary, announced that the Government would derogate from article 5.3 in respect of Northern Ireland terrorism, as they were entitled to do under article 15 of the convention.

As the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey pointed out in an earlier debate, in the later case of Brannigan and McBride, the European Commission of Human Rights found by a majority vote that the convention had not been breached. That view was then referred to the European Court which, in a final decision of 1993, upheld the United Kingdom's derogation by a decisive majority of 22 to four.

When the then Government had to deal with the Brogan case in 1988, my recollection--I did not keep a diary at the time, so I rely on my memory--[Interruption.] My hon. Friend the Member for Solihull expresses, with the habit of an ex-Whip, relief that I was not keeping a diary. My recollection is that the key factor in the Government's decision was the attitude of the judiciary, especially the judiciary in Northern Ireland. There was a view in Whitehall that it might be possible to put together an arrangement in England and Scotland that would introduce a judicial element into the review of detention.

However, the question of Northern Ireland was the real stumbling block. The difficulty was grounded on two facts. First, the Northern Ireland judiciary were few in number, and they were living under the threat of terrorist attack. To give them that responsibility would expose them further. Secondly, there was the problem that because of the special circumstances in Northern Ireland, judges in the Province had the responsibility of trying cases without a jury and determining guilt or innocence in a way in which their judicial brethren in the mainland did not have to do.

Because of that, the situation in Northern Ireland represented particularly acutely the dilemma of how to involve judges who are used to hearing all sides making representations to them in court. They are expected to make executive decisions to which the normal rules of disclosure or the giving of an equal hearing to both sides may may not apply. The dilemma was particularly acute because the men and women who make decisions about the extension of detention for a terrorist suspect are drawn from the same body from which are drawn the judges who decide without a jury someone's guilt or innocence.

For a long time, the Government have signalled that they want to substitute for the United Kingdom's derogation from the convention a different approach, based on the incorporation of the convention into domestic law and the introduction of a judicial element. I still have reservations about the wisdom of doing that and the risk of blurring the distinction between the judiciary on the one hand and the Executive on the other. A number of points have been made in the wider debate outside the Committee in support of the Government's view, including reference to the fact that judges are already involved in hearings of public interest immunity certificates, or decisions to extend detention under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984.

There is a qualitative difference between those powers and what is being proposed under schedule 7. Public interest immunity is concerned with the rules of evidence, not with Executive acts that determine whether an individual should be detained for questioning in an investigation. In a public interest immunity application, the judge knows the issues from both sides. He has the prosecution statements and depositions, which will make the defence case clear. If he does not have them, he can ask the defence for them. In addition, the court has a duty to continue to monitor the issue of disclosure. If it becomes clear that the material that has not already been disclosed would assist the defence, the court must reverse its decision and order disclosure. That is materially different from the sorts of decision that we are considering in respect of the power to authorise the extension of detention. The judicial authority is being asked to act on the basis of material that would not be disclosed either to the suspect or to his legal adviser.

A similar point applies to the argument about the power to extend detention under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984. Under that Act, when a magistrate grants an extension, there is a full inter-partes procedure, at which the defendant is present, for which he is legally represented and when he is fully aware of the grounds for the application. That lies behind many of the amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey. The defendant is told the nature of the evidence on which he has been arrested, and why his continued detention is necessary. A magistrates court has no power to act, except in those circumstances.

That is completely different from an application under terrorist legislation when the detained person may not be given the procedural safeguards because of the sensitivity of the material in question. I differ from the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey in that I come down on the side of protecting sensitive information. Schedule 7 involves serious questions about the constitutional relationship between the Executive and the judiciary, and I hope that the Minister can give a full account of the Government's reasons for the change of policy.
