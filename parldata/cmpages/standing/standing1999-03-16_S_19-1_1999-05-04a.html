Amendment No. 351 would remove the power of the Secretary of State to make regulations prescribing the period in which an asylum seeker or his dependants are likely to become destitute. We need to take a perspective view of destitution and to establish a smooth, effective system of support to prevent people from falling into destitution. If a person turned up in the United Kingdom with £50 in his pocket, we would probably say that he was not destitute. We would also say that we anticipated that he would be within a very short time. We need to be able to anticipate destitution. The clause enables us to do so by allowing us to conclude from what a person has on him whether he is destitute. A person with £50 in his pocket may soon be destitute, but what about someone who arrives with £5,000, and who three days later turns up claiming to be penniless. Would that person be destitute? Who would know what he had done with the £5,000? An element of judgment has to be exercised. How do we deal with someone who makes himself destitute? The clause as drafted gives us a degree of discretion that must be exercised reasonably with an appeals system, but which enables us to deal with the variety of circumstances in which a decision may have to be made. It deals with the person who turns up with £50 and may shortly become destitute. We need to anticipate that and put in place support mechanisms to prevent him from becoming destitute. It also deals with the person who turns up with £5,000 and deliberately makes himself destitute in order to access the resources, possibly having salted the money away somewhere for a rainy day.
