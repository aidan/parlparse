As the hon. Member for Stafford (Mr. Kidney) said, paragraph 113 of the report states:

    "In the longer term we recommend that the posts of Chairman and Chief Executive should be separated and that a non-executive chairman should be appointed."

That is exactly what the amendments would do. The report also states that parallels with other models of corporate governance are not exact in this case. However, it runs against all current thinking on good corporate governance to have those two very powerful roles wrapped up in one person. Paragraph 1.2 of the Cadbury code of best practice clearly states:

    "There should be a clearly accepted division of responsibilities at the head of a company, which will ensure a balance of power and authority, such that no one individual has unfettered powers of decision."

It is slightly ironic that the FSA's first report and accounts, for 1997-98, states, on page 60 that

    "the Board considers that the FSA complies with the Code of Best Practice of the Cadbury Committee."

I am at a loss to see how that statement can tally with the Cadbury code--particularly two years on, now that we can see the details of the vast powers that are to be assigned to the FSA under the sole personage of the combined chairman and chief executive.

The FSA's response to the Joint Committee report was:

    "To split the role between a non-executive Chairman and Chief Executive would complicate accountability and serve no useful purpose."

Again, I am confused. Why would it complicate accountability if a Minister had to deal with two people in different roles rather than one person? That would not be the most enormous intellectual task. As to serving no useful purpose, one useful purpose would relate to people's perceptions. Many of those in the financial services industry are worried about the authority's overriding powers, and they will be even more worried if those powers are seen to be largely under the control of a single person, however good he or she may be at the job. Accountability is not helped by concentrating so much power in the hands of one individual.

There is, no doubt, a case to be made that in the interim period while the FSA is set up, the existence of such a joint role has been an effective way of establishing the new regime and bringing the self-regulatory organisations together. However, when the Bill has been enacted the FSA will be a fully-fledged organisation with very sharp teeth, and accountability and checks on the executive are absolutely paramount. That is reflected in many of the amendments that Conservative Members have tabled and will table during the Committee's proceedings.

There are three main reasons why we would like to see the roles divided. First, the situation affects the whole relationship between the head of the FSA board and the non-executive directors. If the non-executive directors are faced with a single head in the person of a combined chairman and chief executive, to whom do they go if they are unhappy with the way in which meetings and proceedings are being run? They will not necessarily have an independent check on the chief executive. Having said that, I realise that the current board of non-executives supports the combination of the roles.

Secondly, the focus on a single individual is not healthy for the industry. The FSA is becoming very much identified with the single figure of Howard Davies, and may continue to be identified with the single figure of his eventual successor. Perceptions in the industry should be borne in mind.

Thirdly, the chairman and chief executive would have complete control over the agendas of meetings--the weighty matters that can be discussed by the board of the FSA. There should be a non-executive chairman looking over the shoulder of the chief executive to ensure that the right matters come before the board of the FSA, and that they are put before it in the right way and prioritised accordingly. That is the obvious role of chairmen in industry.

It was suggested that splitting the roles would weaken the hand of the supremo of the FSA when dealing with other important institutions in the financial world at home and abroad. I do not buy that argument. The Committee should consider another organisation whose construction is different but which is also a powerful regulator--the Environment Agency. The agency is headed by its chairman, Lord de Ramsay, and has a separate chief executive, Ed Gallaher, as well as a recently appointed deputy chairman. No one would suggest that the hand of its chief executive is weakened when he has to negotiate with the Government or with other environmental bodies purely because a chairman is keeping an eye on him and chairing board meetings.

That thesis can be applied to British industry. All Britain's major companies have a split between the role of chairman and chief executive. I ask the Minister to consider the example of Chris Ginn, chief executive of one of our largest companies, Vodafone. Is she suggesting that Mr. Ginn's hand was tied behind his back when he recently negotiated what I think was the largest ever telecommunications merger--between Vodafone and Air Touch--because he had a chairman and had to look over his shoulder? I hope that she will be able to refute that, although I do not think that she will be able to do so. Are we being told that the chief executive of Glaxo Wellcome had one hand tied behind his back when he negotiated a large pharmaceutical takeover because a chairman was running the board?

The example of British Petroleum is rather closer to the hearts of the Government. Its chairman and chief executive jointly negotiated the largest ever oil merger involving a British company, and it was clear that their roles were absolutely split. The same practices apply in the financial services industry, as not many major banks, stockbrokers or other financial services providers have a combined chairman and chief executive. Why apply one set of principles to the FSA and another to British industry? Why is it appropriate for British industry, including the financial services industry, to have an entirely separate set of principles, which came through the Cadbury committee? This Government, like the previous one, rightly want to apply them to industry and to other regulators in the interests of accountability.

I do not buy the argument that the proposal would weaken the hands of the supremo of the FSA when dealing with outside bodies, and I fear that the Treasury's response to the Joint Committee in that respect was weak. Surely it is not beyond the wit of Treasury Ministers to deal with two people or to realise that one of them speaks on behalf of both, even if only one turns up at a briefing. We should make it crystal clear at the outset that we need the FSA in practice to be as accountable as possible and to be modelled on other best practice that we are trying to promote in British industry, including the financial services industry. Perhaps even more importantly, the perception that that is the case should be clear in the industry, especially as such an enormously powerful body is being established. Those are the clear and simple reasons why the Opposition support amendments Nos. 1 and 2.

I shall deal finally with amendment No. 29, although I am not sure why it has been grouped with the others. It seeks to add to the role of the non-executive directors the additional function of the non-executive committee. As it stands, the committee has a monitoring role in respect of whether the FSA is using its resources appropriately and operating cost effectively and of establishing the pay of the executive directors. The amendment would ensure that the non-executive directors have a role in monitoring whether the authority's legislative and other functions are being properly discharged. The reason for having non-executive directors is to provide an added check by people who do not have such a close vested interest in the way in which the FSA is working. It seems right and proper that they have powers to ensure that the FSA directors are carrying out their major functions and using the resources of that big body properly.

Thus, it is a simple amendment which would beef up the non-executive directorships. Given that the Government acknowledged that non-executive directors are an important part of the FSA--which they did by making them a majority on the main board--it seems only right to enshrine in black and white the principle that they should have greater regard to the FSA's overall work.
