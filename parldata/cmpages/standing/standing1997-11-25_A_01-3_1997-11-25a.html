The Minister has not yet addressed the fundamental point made by my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire earlier, and several times on Second Reading. The arguments can be summarised briefly. They are well rehearsed, and there is no need to explore them in detail.

Opposition Members believe that internment is undeniably an infringement of civil liberties. It would be folly to pretend otherwise. It would be equally foolish to deny that when internment was introduced in the 1970s, it was a disaster. It was entirely counterproductive, and it left the IRA stronger. The Committee should not spend much time discussing these issues. It should acknowledge that internment is an infringement of civil liberties and that it failed drastically in the past. It by no means follows from that acknowledgement, however, and it is an ultimate non sequitur, to argue that the power to intern should therefore be abandoned. The drastic failure of internment in the past does not mean that it is bound to fail in future. There is no logical reason for drawing that conclusion.

Internment failed in the 1970s for a variety of reasons. One of them was that the then Government suffered from woefully inadequate and inaccurate intelligence. It failed also because it applied only to republicans, and therefore accentuated the sectarian divide between communities. That compounded its negative impact. Internment was, I believe, seen predominantly as a weapon of persecution used by the state against republicans. Some people would argue that internment failed in the 1970s also because it was not deployed at the same time by the Republic of Ireland. I do not believe that that is especially significant. Internment failed above all because the mood and character of the times was very far from supportive.

Acknowledging those factors does not mean that such circumstances will always prevail, for the simple reason that times change. For example, the quality and quantity of intelligence gathering has been transformed since the 1970s. If internment were used even-handedly against loyalist and republican paramilitaries, the stigma of sectarianism that plagued it in the 1970s could not arise.

Selective internment--as opposed to mass internment--could render terrorist activity virtually impossible, if it was aimed at breaking the chains of command and of communication without which that activity cannot occur.

As my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire said, it is possible to envisage internment--especially selective internment--winning widespread support. There are no pragmatic, practical factors to prevent internment being effective. It would be foolish to deny that the principled objection--the civil liberties argument--has force. However, it no more carries the day than the practical or pragmatic objections.

We should therefore make a clear distinction between political concessions, as have been identified by some hon. Members, and points of principle.

It must remain a point of principle that people are innocent until proven guilty, and that is what internment is about. Once one accepts that internment is viable and possible, one steps away from that principle. That is something with which we should never bargain and never put at stake, whatever the perceived advantages of the past or of the American system, which I consider ephemeral. We should never barter with the principle of the assumption of innocence.

I have certain reasons for being committed to that view. I come from a long line of internees, going back long before the 1950s. People talk about internment as if it were something that was introduced in the 1970s, but it existed in the 1920s, the 1930s, the 1940s, the 1950s, the 1960s and the 1970s. Internment failed in each of those decades because of a common factor and if it had not done so, we would not be here debating it today. It is not a matter simply of failure but of cumulative failure, not in a negative but in a positive sense, however one views the matter.

I throw words off the top of my emotional head--the Argenta. Who will tell me which period of history that relates to? Most people here do not know what I am talking about. In the community in which I live the Argenta is a badge of honour for those who were interned in that prison ship. Many of our predecessors were there. Who remembers Ballykinlar? That was another step in the process of internment. We all talk about the Maze or, to update matters, Long Kesh, but the same principle was always at stake--taking away people's freedom without trial and experiencing political problems as a result. The same abject failure occurs each time.

How does that abject failure translate itself in political terms? I am probably better placed than most to say. It is part of nationalist folklore to say, ``My brother was on the Argenta'' or, ``My uncle was in Ballykinlar''. The failure has translated into a type of nationalist-republican folklore down the years, not in terms of the solution to the problem but the perpetuation of the refusal to solve it. That is what internment means to me.

I shall go to the grave remembering three things, in time and place. First, from the late 1960s, I remember exactly where I was, what I was doing and what I should have been doing on the day that John F. Kennedy was shot; secondly, from the early 1970s, I remember the day of internment; thirdly, I remember the day on which Diana, Princess of Wales, was killed. Internment is a failed experiment. It failed in the past, it would fail now and it would fail in the future.

It was suggested that internment should be used in the north and the south simultaneously. That will never happen. We will never see internment in the north of Ireland again. We will never see it in the Republic of Ireland again. We will never see it in the north and south again. Why? Because it is a failed process. It simply cannot work. The north of Ireland has changed radically, as has the Republic of Ireland. The relationship between Ireland and the rest of the world has also changed remarkably and fundamentally. Let us therefore stop talking about simultaneous north and south internment. The north and south should simultaneously try to deliver a political solution to the problems that internment was supposed to solve. Those are not the words of a politician. Lord Colville was making an analytical assessment of the provision.
