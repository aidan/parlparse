The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendment No. 583, clause 28 stand part, Government amendment No. 594 and Government new clause 6.

The amendments ensure that the Bill will achieve two purposes. Amendment No. 583 withdraws the repeal of section 18 of the Electricity Act 1989 in clause 28(2), and new clause 6 repeals that section instead. It is proper to repeal section 18 in a clause in its own right because it is very important. The effect of the repeal is to abolish tariff supply.

The abolition of tariff supply is a significant change to the regulatory regime for electricity. In future all electricity supply, like gas—the previous Government put everyone on to contract in 1995 by amending the Gas Act 1986—will be governed by a contract between the customer and his supplier. That is an important contribution to the development of supply competition in the interests of consumers. It opens the way to flexibility and diversity in electricity supply and removes the statutory distinction between tariff supply by the public electricity suppliers and contract supply by all other suppliers.

The change represents a significant shift in the way in which consumer protection is secured for electricity customers and will put them on a par with gas customers. At present, for customers of public electricity suppliers only, the terms under which they receive a supply are determined by a statutory tariff and certain other statutory provision. Disputes between a customer and his supplier may be determined by the regulator. That was appropriate in the days of monopolies, but competition is now at the centre of consumer protection. Supply contracts will be subject to the full range of consumer safeguards under the law applying to contracts. Ultimately, contract disputes can be determined by the courts and the authority will continue to control suppliers' behaviour through their licences under its principal objective to protect the interests of consumers. In addition, the Bill establishes the new consumer council as an independent champion of consumer interests with a specific role to investigate consumer complaints. We believe that this is the right package of consumer protection for our future energy markets.

The second purpose of this group of amendments is to make necessary consequential changes to the drafting of the Electricity Act 1989 which result from the separation of supply and distribution and the move to a single class of electricity supply licence. Amendment No. 582 is necessary to ensure that any reference to a public electricity supplier and supply in the 1989 Act or other legislation can be read in future as a reference to supplier, supply, distributor or distribution as appropriate. It also ensures that any reference to holders of second tier supply licences—licensees other than public electricity suppliers—can be read in future as a reference to electricity suppliers as defined in the Bill.

The amendments are a consequence of the separation of supply and distribution in the Bill. The concept of public electricity suppliers will disappear and the old meaning of supply, which covered both supply and distribution as defined in the Bill, must be adapted to those two distinct concepts. In future there will be only one type of supply licence.

Amendments Nos. 583 and 594 combined will withdraw the repeal of section 22 of the 1989 Act made by clause 28(2). We now need section 22, to which the Government will table amendments to allow for connection to a distribution system on terms to be agreed between customer and distributor. This will be an alternative to connection in accordance with the procedures and requirements laid down by the Act, as amended by the Bill.

The Chairman: With this we may take Government amendments Nos. 591 and 592.

Dr. Howells: Amendment No. 584 makes a number of changes to the clause. As this is quite a technical area, I want first to explain briefly the purpose of the clause.

I shall explain one piece of jargon. Current legislation refers to public gas transporters or PGTs. The word ``public'' is not appropriate in a competitive market with a number of players, so the clause changes the term to ``gas transporters''. Nevertheless, PGT is useful shorthand, so I hope that the Committee will forgive me if I continue to use it until we have debated the amendment.

Gas transporters are licensed to operate only within the area specified in their licence—their authorised area. At present any given place can be within the authorised area of only one transporter at any given time. This is sometimes called the geographic exclusivity of transporter licences.

If a transporter's licence includes an area that does not contain gas pipelines and a second transporter wins a contract to lay pipes and use them to convey gas to premises in that area, the second transporter can ask to have the area carved out of the incumbent's licence.

This geographic exclusivity derives from section 7(2)(a) of the existing Gas Act 1986, which defines the term ``authorised area'' of a PGT in a way that depends on exclusivity. The clause introduces a new regime that allows overlapping authorised areas, so the existing definition must be changed. The clause in the original Bill provided a new definition that did not cover the case when a licence is restricted—that is, when its geographic scope is reduced at the transporter's request—so the second part of the amendment is necessary.

The third subsection in the amendment creates the ability to restrict licences. That was always part of our policy intention, but, as we said when we introduced the Bill, we could not include all the policy at that time. Restriction of licence areas has always been allowed for gas suppliers and shippers, and it is sensible to offer that here so as to increase flexibility in responding to changes in the market.

The amendment deletes subsection (2) of clause 64. The change envisaged is consequential on the rest of the clause as amended, so it fits better in schedule 5 with the other minor and consequential changes. That is the effect of amendment No. 591.

The clause is highly detailed, but I hope that I have provided sufficient context for the moment. I commend the amendment to the Committee.
