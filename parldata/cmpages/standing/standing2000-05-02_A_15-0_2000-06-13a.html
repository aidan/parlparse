New clause 19

Consultation with Local Commissioner

    ``.—(1) If, at any stage in the course of conducting an investigation under section 55, an ethical standards officer forms the opinion that the matters which are the subject of the investigation relate partly to a matter which could be the subject of an investigation under Part III of the Local Government Act 1974, he may consult the appropriate Local Commissioner about the investigation and, if he considers it necessary, inform any person who made the allegation which gave rise to the investigation of the steps necessary to initiate a complaint under Part III of that Act.

    (2) If, at any stage in the course of conducting an investigation under Part III of that Act, a Local Commissioner forms the opinion that the complaint relates partly to a matter which could be the subject of an investigation under section 55 of this Act, he may consult the Standards Board for England about the investigation and, if he considers it necessary, inform the person initiating the complaint of the steps necessary to make an allegation under section 54.

    (a) the conduct of the investigation, and

New clause 2

Restitution order

    ``Where matters considered by an interim tribunal or a case tribunal are or become the subject of criminal proceedings in a court, it shall be open to that court, in considering any penalty resulting from verdict of guilty, to apply a requirement, as part of any penalty, that a restitution order be made so that any loss to the local taxpayer is repaid.''.—[Mr. Waterson.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Waterson: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

This is one of the Opposition's new clauses. We are having the debate the wrong way round. It might have made more sense to debate the abolition of surcharging first. The new clause is aimed at filling a gap that might be left by the abolition of surcharging, which we shall deal with when we reach clauses 84 and 85.

There is a consensus throughout local government that surcharging is a blunt instrument and that it should go. However, the principle underlying it—that wrongdoers should pick up the bill for their misdeeds and the problems that they cause for local taxpayers—is not wrong. It is a sound principle and I am open to the Minister's suggestions as to how to approach it more effectively.

The new clause would ensure that, when someone is found guilty in criminal proceedings, the court can apply a restitution order to reimburse local taxpayers. In a sense, the Government have already accepted the broad principle underlying this and a subsequent new clause—that a financial penalty or restitution should be applied. In another context, the Government tabled their own provision on paying compensation in the event of maladministration. That is right and it mirrors our new clause: the basic principle is the same.

The new clause would probably need to be invoked only rarely: because it would apply to criminal proceedings, a higher standard of proof would be required within the audit system, for example, so the risk of injustice or unfairness would be minimised. It would provide reassurance to local taxpayers that elected representatives who abused their position or were simply incompetent would not get away scot-free. A financial penalty may, albeit only rarely, be appropriate. It is right that a criminal court deals with such serious cases. I cannot imagine that the Minister would have any problem with the broad principle underlying the new clause, but with the battery of advice and expertise at her back, she may well find a better way of approaching the problem.

Ms Hughes: The new clause is interesting and many Committee members will agree with the spirit of the hon. Gentleman's remarks. When wrongdoing has led to criminal proceedings, the principle of having a mechanism for restitution—either to the council as a whole or to individual members of the pubic—is right.

I do not know whether the hon. Gentleman is aware of the recent case at Broadlands council, whose chief executive—with the support of the Conservative group on that authority—took actions, including opening her mail, against a Labour member of the authority, which were subsequently found to be unlawful. The council had to pay £20,000 to the aggrieved Labour member. That was a substantial loss of council income and, following the hon. Gentleman's line of argument, the Conservative members on that authority who approved the chief executive's actions should be held to account.

We must remember that the new ethical framework is not of itself aimed at tackling criminal issues. Where criminal misconduct is suspected or uncovered in a case referred to the Standards Board, it would be passed to the appropriate authority—usually the police—to deal with. As the new clause implies, that could arise in particularly serious cases in which an interim case tribunal considers suspension and it subsequently becomes a matter of criminal investigation.

However, when the actions of a councillor, councillors or political groups on a council have resulted in financial loss to the authority, the courts are already able to order restitution from the council, if they consider it appropriate. That does not prevent the authority from separately seeking to recover losses arising from misconduct, which they may do either through a compensation order in criminal cases or through civil action proceedings. With his legal expertise, the hon. Gentleman will be familiar with the powers that criminal courts possess—including compensation, confiscation and restitution orders—to recover losses in various circumstances. As I said, they are over and above the civil proceedings that councils may wish to instigate in non-criminal cases.

We agree with the spirit of the new clause and with many of the hon. Gentleman's comments. However, the powers open to the courts and the avenues open to councils through civil proceedings already provide sufficient mechanisms for councils to pursue compensation and restitution.

Mr. Waterson: I am still minded to withdraw the new clause, but I remain concerned about some of the Minister's remarks. She tried to make an uncharacteristically cheap party-political point about Broadlands council.

Ms Hughes: It is a serious matter.

Mr. Waterson: I am not aware of the details and I am certainly not going to comment on it. It would be relevant to the new clause only if there had been criminal charges in that case. [Interruption.] The Minister may refer to a court judgment, but I assume that she means a civil court. For the new clause to be appropriate, there has to be a successful criminal prosecution—not civil proceedings. In other words, there must be a conviction based on the standard of proof required in criminal cases in this country.

I hear what the Minister says about civil proceedings and it may be open to councils to start them for recovery, but the point of the new clause is that it would be more convenient and appropriate to deal with it at the same time in the same case by the same court. It could be more dangerous for the people involved if the case were dealt with by a wholly different court, which might reach a different conclusion based on the same evidence.

We disagree with the Minister that the circumstances are covered by existing law. In any event, the new clause is important symbolically to make it clear that the abolition of surcharge does not give a green light to anyone who might be contemplating corrupt actions. Nevertheless, I am relieved that the Minister accepts the principles behind the new clause and we may return to the issue later. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Ms Armstrong: In many senses, this is an historic occasion. We are repealing the surcharge provisions in the Audit Commission Act 1998 and the Secretary of State's power to sanction an item of account. Surcharge has caused much excitement over the years. We might best remember the issue from way back in the Clay Cross case. I will not mention the other case involving surcharge that people remember and about which they are concerned. All that I will say is that proceedings currently before courts will continue to be subject to the legislation that was current when those cases commenced.

The Nolan committee recommended the repeal of surcharge. The operation of surcharge allows financial penalties to be imposed that bear little relation to the gravity of individuals' misconduct. One of the many reasons for introducing the new ethical framework was to remove such anachronisms and eliminate the difference between local and central Government approaches to punishing financial wrongdoing.

Nolan recommended repealing surcharge because it was unfair for local government officers and members to be subject to surcharge when other public servants were not. The law was defective and although surcharge was in theory a means of restitution, that was not the case in practice. There were technical difficulties in calculating the relevant sums, which were often beyond the means of the individuals involved and bore no relation to people's ability to pay or their culpability.

We have considered those arguments and we agree that the surcharge provisions in sections 17 and 18 of the 1998 Act should be repealed. Removing the relevant surcharge provision from section 17 of the 1998 Act does not affect the auditor's ability to apply to the courts for a declaration that an item account is contrary to law. I have been deliberative about that point to ensure that the hon. Member for Eastbourne appreciates that the auditor will still have that power.

Under the new arrangement, the Standards Board and adjudication panel rather than the auditor will determine whether there has been misconduct and any issue subsequently will be pursued through them. These changes affect all the bodies to which surcharge provisions currently apply.

Mr. Waterson: I thought it was right for us to have a short debate on this because, to the untutored eye, these matters look fairly technical. We are, in a sense, drawing a line under a longish period of surcharging in English law and British local government. Not many tears will be shed at the wake, but it is important that we mark its passing in some way.

As I said a moment ago in a different context, it is common ground across parties and local government that the day of surcharging has gone, although the principle behind it is still important. There should be some mechanism for financial punishment of wrongdoing. The problem with surcharging, as the Minister said, has always been that it is a blunt instrument. Sometimes people can blunder into doing something and almost biblical punishment is rained down on them in the shape of surcharging.

It has also contributed to a large body of law on the issue of ultra vires. We spent much time on part I of the Bill debating the new power and the question of ultra vires. But the reason that it is engraved on the souls of most local councillors is, I suspect, mostly because of the dangers of surcharging. The Minister was delicate enough not to go into too many details about recent cases that have brought the concept into disrepute on grounds of procedure, cost and general inappropriateness to the needs of modern local government. We saw what happened in such places as Hammersmith and Fulham over interest swaps and so on, which seemed like a good idea at the time but went disastrously wrong.

At the moment, the district auditor has a duty to surcharge anyone who is responsible by wilful misconduct for a loss or deficiency. There is a great body of law also about the meaning of wilful misconduct and the Minister referred to the fact that the auditor will still have some power to deal with items that he or she does not find appropriate. However, clause 84 removes the ability to impose the surcharge. There remains the possibility of declaring an item of expenditure to be contrary to law but, according to the Library brief, it would be for the Standards Board and the adjudication panel to decide whether there had been misconduct and what, if any, action should be taken.

I am also informed that the amendment would remove the reasonable belief defence under the 1998 Act, which, again, many people consider is simply not sustainable in the modern world. It will be interesting to see whether the provisions that are being made in other parts of the Bill will fill the entire gap left by surcharging, but neither Opposition nor Government members of the Committee will shed a tear at its passing.

Question accordingly agreed to.
