Government new clause 3--Special provisions relating to child witnesses.

Government new clause 4--Extension of provisions of section (Special provisions relating to child witnesses) to certain witnesses over 17.

Mr. Howarth: The Government amendments would create a new category of witness: those who require special protection. All child witnesses who give evidence in sexual or violent offence cases would be deemed to require special protection, and there would be a high degree of certainty that they would give evidence in a particular way. That would mean that all child witnesses would give their evidence-in-chief by pre-recorded video unless it was not in the interests of justice for the recording to be admitted. Unlike cases of other types of offence in which children give evidence, it would not have to be argued that the measure was necessary to maximise the quality of their evidence.

Child witnesses in violent offence cases would give further evidence by live link if their video evidence-in-chief was admitted. I am sure that the Committee will find that acceptable--the matter has caused anxiety for some time.

When we are ready to implement the measure, children in sexual offence cases will be cross-examined on video pre-trial unless they tell the court that they do not want to be cross-examined until the day of the trial. We are prepared to commit ourselves publicly to that target. However, there are technical and procedural difficulties that agencies will work to overcome.

The Opposition amendments would give judges more discretion to decide when they should make directions that allowed children to give video-recorded evidence-in-chief and further evidence by live link. Those amendments would abolish the presumption that children should be able to give evidence in that way, and would enable the courts to decide not to allow the provisions on the grounds that their use would not be in the interests of justice.

We have listened carefully to the anxieties of the National Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Children, Childline and other children's organisations about the provisions in chapter I of part II of the Bill. They are worried that the Bill weakens the current provision for child witnesses to give video-recorded evidence-in-chief in cases that involve sex and violence. Under Section 32A of the Criminal Justice Act 1988, as amended in 1991, the court is required to admit video evidence-in-chief from children in such cases unless the witness is unavailable for cross-examination, the disclosure of the circumstances of the recording is unsatisfactory or it would not be in the interests of justice to admit the evidence.

Section 32A applies only to sexual and violent offence cases. The Bill extends eligibility for help to give evidence to all child witnesses. When we made the extension, we thought it right to ensure that the court would consider whether the witnesses needed help to improve their evidence. Not all the newly eligible witnesses will need such help. However, the representations that we received convinced me that we need to be sure that we do at least as much as we did previously to give children who are witnesses in sexual and violent offence cases--and thus need special protection--the confidence of knowing that they will have help to give evidence.

The amendments would mean that particularly vulnerable children would have a video recording of their evidence-in-chief admitted, unless it was not in the interests of justice for it to be admitted. The court will not have to consider whether the video will help to maximise the quality of their evidence. That will help practitioners working with children to have confidence that a properly made recording is likely to be admitted, while ensuring that courts can still exclude recordings that are deemed to be prejudicial.

The Bill already reflects our intention that certain particularly vulnerable children should ultimately be kept out of court altogether by finding other ways to put their evidence--tested according to the principles of the adversarial system--before the court. The Government amendments make it clear that children giving evidence in sexual offence cases will be our first priority for the use of this measure. We intend ultimately that all such children will be cross-examined before trial and the proceeding recorded on video, unless they have not already given video-recorded evidence-in-chief, or a child has informed the court that he would prefer to wait for the trail. That reflects the particular vulnerability of child complainants of sexual offences, such as those in child abuse cases, who might need to be kept out of court altogether.

I know that video-recorded cross-examination is a controversial provision. However, after considering it carefully, we see no reason why it could not work in practice as long as it is properly implemented. Judges will retain control of when cross-examination is held and they will have to ensure that the defence has had full prosecution disclosure and is able to cross-examine. Judges will also control cross-examination itself. The child will be corss-examined by the opposing lawyer and the defendant will be able to see and hear the cross-examination. If there is a problem with the way in which the hearing is conducted, the court will be able to exclude the recording under the provisions in clause 27(4).

I am not unaware of the difficulties that will have to be overcome before the measures can be implemented. We shall need to assess demand carefully; it is not clear what it will be. We shall need to find or equip facilities for these purposes throughout the country. That will require an audit of existing facilities to see where there are gaps. Each case involving such witnesses will need tight management to ensure that delays in disclosure, for example, do not make it impossible to fix a date for the proceeding and to get the necessary participants in the right place, at the right time and on the right day. In many respects, those problems are already wrestled with every day in ensuring that trials go forward. However, the extra variation of holding the cross-examination proceeding in a non-court venue will add to the pressure.

We are prepared to consider setting progressive targets for implementation if that is what it will take. Those are matters for the multi-agency implementation steering group, which is already hard at work on deciding the best approach to take. In anticipation, under clause 62(4), the Bill will give the Secretary of State the flexibility to make different provision for different cases, circumstances or areas as he thinks fit, on the basis of evidence presented to him about existing facilities and on what different areas can cope with.

If the working group decides that the best way to get the measure off the ground is to bring it in first for Crown court cases involving very young witnesses and the most serious sexual abuse, we shall be prepared to consider that as a starting point. If the group recommends that we wait until we are, as it were, ready to go for broke nationwide, we will be prepared to consider that too. We are committed to the ultimate goal--the total application of these measures.

The rest of the amendments simply preserve the position in clause 21 to ensure that children who have made a video recording when they are under 17 can have that video admitted. When a child has started to give evidence-in-chief or to be cross-examined in a particular way, he can finish his evidence in the same way, even if his 17th birthday falls in the middle of that procedure. It will be obvious to the Committee why that might be necessary: it will ensure that children are not confused and do not have to give the same evidence repeatedly.

I still believe that it is helpful to retain a presumption for how child witnesses in cases other than violent or sexual offence cases should give evidence. It helps to create a standard system for how children give evidence. The presumption can be disapplied if the court considers that video evidence-in-chief or live-link evidence will not maximise the quality of the child's evidence. The presumption can also be disapplied if the measures would tend to inhibit the other party's ability to test the child's evidence, or if other measures would be preferable for the child. Of course, it also includes the child giving evidence with no help at all, which may be appropriate for older children in cases that are not especially distressing. We are trying to have a system that measures the importance of the presumption in each case, given the child's circumstances.

We do not believe that there is any need for the court to consider both whether a special measures direction would be in the interests of justice and whether it might tend to inhibit the ability of a party to the case to test the witness's evidence or improve the quality of the witness's evidence. If the court considers the second question, there is no need to consider the first as well, especially given the requirement to consider a jury warning in clause 31.

Many children give evidence-in-chief on video already. The new capital cost of the Bill for the extension of video evidence-in-chief to other children and to certain adult witnesses, which will create a need to obtain more equipment, has been estimated to require up to another £1.5 million a year over the next three years. Video-recorded cross-examination is a new initiative, and costs will depend on the number of witnesses who are eligible for it and on what existing facilities can be used. First estimates are based on the expectation that, initially, some 900 witnesses a year will be involved.

On that basis, we expect that the cost of preparing for, appearing at and reviewing the results of a separate hearing, assuming that the whole process lasts three hours, in terms of time spent by practitioners and the judiciary, would be about £0.6 million a year. We have not yet costed the expense of training practitioners and the police about the effects of the new legislation, the development and publication of guidance on the new arrangements and, possibly, the cost of accommodation hire and appropriately expert technicians. Those costs are likely to be fairly substantial.

We are trying to get the balance right. The whole Committee will agree that these are difficult circumstances for children. Often they are being required to recount harrowing and difficult experiences. We want to create circumstances where they will be comfortable giving evidence and where the level of distress and the number of pressures on the children are kept to a minimum.

I hope that the Committee will agree that the amendments and new clauses are an appropriate and measured response to the problem that we are trying to resolve.

Clause 26

Video recorded evidence in chief

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 52, in page 19, line 42, leave out 'may'and insert 'shall'.

I was tempted not to move the amendment, when I tried to prepare for these clauses before last Thursday's sitting. However, I have looked at it again this morning and shall try to comment on what lies behind the clause.

The clause provides

    "for a video recording of an interview of the witness to be admitted as evidence in chief of the witness."

However, subsection (2) gives the discretion that a special measures direction may not provide for a video recording, or a part of such recording, to be admitted

    "if the court is of the opinion, having regard to all the circumstances of the case, that in the interests of justice the recording, or that part of it, should not be so admitted."

As the Under-Secretary knows, we are anxious to ensure that there is discretion, so we welcome that part of the clause.

Subsection (4) says that, where a special measures direction provides for a recording to be made,

    "the court may nevertheless subsequently direct that it is not to be so admitted",

and it gives two specific sets of circumstances in which it might come to that judgment. The first is if

    "the witness will not be available for cross-examination";

the second is if

    "the parties to the proceedings have not agreed that there is no need for the witness to be so available".

Our probing amendment questions whether that discretion is necessary. If the witness is not available for cross-examination and the parties have not agreed that there is no need for the witness to be cross-examined, almost inevitably the defence will have said that it wants to cross-examine the witness whose evidence has been taken by video in advance of the trial. The Under-Secretary will know--we shall come to it when we debate the next clause--that this measure has given rise to a great deal of anxiety and concern.

Clause 30

Status of evidence given under Chapter I

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Boateng: Clause 30 provides that evidence given by a witness with the assistance of a special measures direction will have the same status as it would have had if it had been given orally in court. Such evidence will be subject to the same admissibility rules, so it may not be used to corroborate other evidence given by the witness. Those giving it will be subject to the penalties for perjury--or, when the evidence is given unsworn, for wilfully giving false evidence--that would apply if they had given oral evidence in court in the usual way.

The jury or magistrate must have regard to all circumstances that can be reasonably considered when they come to assess the weight that can be given to evidence given with the help of a special measures direction. For example, they may consider that less weight should be given to unsworn evidence, or that it may be unreasonable to take into account the use of a screen when weighing the evidence.

The clause provides that video-recorded evidence is admissible as evidence if the witness would usually give sworn evidence but does not or is unable to swear an oath or make an affirmation prior to giving evidence on video.

The clause will put the person giving evidence in the same position as someone giving evidence in the ordinary way, and will enable the best evidence to come before the jury. Such evidence will not tell for or against the giver, and the court will not be prevented by any technicality from considering the full weight of the evidence before it and giving it the significance that it merits.

I hate the expression that I am about to use; nevertheless, it is sometimes useful. The provision is designed to put all the evidence on a level playing field so that the judge or jury assessing the evidence are in no worse a position than they would have been in assessing the evidence, had it been given without the benefit of special measures.

Clause 33

Complainants in proceedings for sexual offences

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.
 

Mr. Boateng: Chapter II of part II is designed to protect certain witnesses from the trauma of being cross-examined in person by their alleged attacker.

Clause 33 will prevent defendants who are charged with rape, or one of the other sexual offences listed in clause 60, from personally cross-examining their alleged victim about that offence or any other offence with which they are charged in the proceedings. This issue was the subject of considerable debate on Second Reading, and I appreciate the Opposition's approach to its consideration in Committee.

The clause was inspired by the cases of Ralston Edwards and Milton Brown in which the defendants used the opportunity of personally cross-examining their alleged victims to humiliate them. Judge Pontius, when sentencing Brown, described the suggestions that he had made to the complainants in his case as "outrageous and repulsive". He also said that Brown's questioning of them amounted to a

    "merciless cross-examination designed only to intimidate and humiliate them."

Members of the Committee who are aware of events at the Old Bailey during those two trials will have been appalled by the terrible ordeal that the witnesses were obliged to go through. We are determined that that should never happen again. The trial process was abused by those defendants. Following the Brown case, the Lord Chief Justice rightly issued guidelines encouraging judges to behave more robustly in future.

However, we do not believe that those guidelines go far enough. It is only of limited use for judges to step in and stop abusive questioning once it has started: often the damage has already been done. The problem with unrepresented defendants cross-examining sexual offence complainants goes further than the questions that they ask. The very fact that the complainant is being cross-examined by someone whom he or she alleges raped or abused them is intimidating in itself. No one should underestimate the impact of having to face one's alleged attacker when one is in the witness box. The victim is accused by the person in the dock and is obliged to answer questions that are designed to humiliate, degrade and intimidate. That cannot be right.

In some cases, there may be a history of an abusive relationship between the defendant and the complainant, which will make it particularly intimidating for the complainant to be questioned by the defendant. For example, they might have been previously married and that relationship might have been characterised by intimidation and abuse that reflected the disproportionate power relationship between the two. It is all the more important in such cases that the ban should apply, otherwise the unequal power relationship is taken into the courtroom and becomes a way of undermining the evidence given by the victim. The only way to ensure that the complainants are neither subject to that sort of treatment in future, nor deterred from pressing charges because they are frightened of it, is to impose a mandatory ban on defendants in such cases personally cross-examining complainants.

It was argued in the other place that judges should have the discretion to override the ban in certain cases, but that would not be appropriate. Whether the offence is alleged to have occurred following a relationship between acquaintances or strangers, the possibilities for intimidation and humiliation are obvious regardless of whether the defendant deliberately seeks to exploit them. It is hard to see in what circumstances judges could reasonably consider that allowing the defendant personally to cross-examine the complainant would not carry the risk of humiliation and fear. The very existence of a decision to override the ban would destroy complainant's certainty that there was no risk of their being cross-examined by their alleged assailant.

The two cases to which I referred did enormous harm to the confidence of victims of the criminal justice system; we cannot allow that to continue. It has been said that clause 33 may give rise to miscarriages of justice: that innocent defendants will be convicted because they have not been allowed personally to question the complainant, or that guilty defendants will be acquitted because the jury will consider the ban in clause 33 unfair. We do not accept or believe that. Since 1992, defendants have been banned from personally cross-examining child witnesses in cases involving sex, violence or cruelty. No one suggests that that has led to miscarriages of justice. No one would seriously suggest that that right should be restored to such defendants, nor do we believe that it will be a matter of concern to juries that they have not heard a defendant personally cross-examining his accuser.

It is important to bear in mind that clause 33 does not prevent complainants from being cross-examined; it simply prevents defendants from carrying out that cross-examination themselves. When a ban has been imposed under the clause or under clauses 34 and 35 the defendant will have three choices. He can instruct a lawyer to conduct his entire defence, which is what usually happens. He can instruct a lawyer to cross-examine the witness on his behalf and conduct the rest of the case himself--he will be eligible for legal aid and its successor under the Access to Justice Bill on the same means-tested basis as other defendants. If the defendant chooses not to appoint a legal representative there is a third option: the court will have to consider whether to appoint its own lawyer in the defendant's interest to test the witness's evidence. It will make such an appointment if it decides that that is necessary in the interests of justice. Court-appointed lawyers will have access to the court's and the prosecution's papers, statements made to the police by the defendant or anyone else, any medical evidence in the case and so on. In such circumstances, of course, a judge will have to consider warning the jury that the witness's evidence was not tested by a lawyer whom the defendant had chosen to instruct himself. Whenever the ban applies, the judge will have to consider warning members of the jury, if there is one, that they should not allow the fact that the defendant is prohibited from cross-examining the witness in person to prejudice their assessment of the evidence in the case.

In the light of those safeguards, I hope that the Committee will agree that clause 33 provides an important protection for the small but significant number of witnesses who might be subjected to an ordeal and the many who fear that they might be so subjected and would therefore be deterred from coming forward and giving their best evidence of an alleged offence against them.

For those reasons, and in view of the opinions expressed clearly and forcibly on both sides of the House on Second Reading, I hope that the Committee will accept the clause, although I appreciate that some aspects of the Second Reading debate gave Members on both sides of the House cause for concern, as I suspect that the hon. Member for Ryedale will point out later.
