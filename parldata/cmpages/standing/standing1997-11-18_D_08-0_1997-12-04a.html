Mr. Boswell: This is a nice sunny morning in which to change gear, if I may use that analogy--not to motor in the country, much as we would like to do that on a day like this, but to consider the Government's proposals for the transfer of banking supervision to the new Financial Services Authority.

The amendments are designed to probe several aspects of the changes and to stimulate a general debate on the Government's intentions and how they intend to execute them. I say at the outset, although it may be termed a reverse health warning, that nothing that I or my right hon. and hon. Friends might say should be interpreted as an attack--covert or disguised--on the competence of the Governor of the Bank, his staff, those who are transferring to the new authority or on Howard Davies who will chair that authority. They are people whom many of us know and respect. It is, however, right for us to tease out from the Government their intentions and thinking.

Amendment No. 47 is a paving amendment, and amendment No. 50 is a substantive amendment that would reinforce the Bank's duty to be responsible for the overall stability of the financial system. It is a little unfortunate that the Bill's geography has meant that we must debate those amendments before amendment No. 48, which would leave out from the clause the provisions relating to the Bank's functions under the Banking Act 1987. I am not suggesting that we do not regard this debate as important. It is most important, but it might be easier for the Committee if I save some of my more general remarks about the clause for our discussion on the next amendment.

The purpose of amendment No. 50 is to reinforce the point, which I think is conceded by the Committee, that the Bank shall continue to be responsible for the overall stability of the financial system as a whole and, in particular, that of the monetary system. We want to ensure that that responsibility is clearly reinforced by the Government.

Earlier in our proceedings, I expressed concern about the transfer of monetary responsibilities. We are now debating the regulation of individual institutions. That will take place elsewhere through the authority, but the Bank will still have an overview of stability. If circumstances arise when a bank or institution is failing in its functions, we are worried that the authority may initially perceive that to be a minor regulatory "retail" matter. However, things could get out of hand, which could pose a systemic risk to the overall system. I anticipate that the Economic Secretary will respond, "Ah, that is what the memorandum of understanding is for. We will talk to each other closely and make sure that nothing like that happens."

It is important to flag up our concern that the Bill divides responsibilities that were previously the Bank's in-house responsibility. If a bank got itself into trouble, the authority would have to take an interest as regulator; but the Bank would still have overall responsibility for the financial system's stability and, as the executive agency, it would have to lend its own money--public money--to prop up the failing bank if it were deemed necessary in order to maintain the stability of the system.

I do not want to stray out of order, Mr. McWilliam, but let me first refer to the Government's intention to establish a single regulator for the financial services industry. The Government decided that there was a need for a single regulator because of the hotchpotch of regulations that had grown up to suit circumstances that have now changed considerably. For example, some household-name organisations were building societies but are now banks carrying out a wide range of activities. There have also been changes in the activities of the banks themselves.

As Member of Parliament, we are all aware of the difficulties faced by our constituents when they have a problem relating to the financial services sector; they have to approach the alphabet soup of existing self-regulatory organisations, which is confusing. People whose lives are not consumed with the detail of financial regulation may feel that the wool is being pulled over their eyes and that the confusion will let the guilty off the hook. That was the reason for establishing a single regulator.

Banking supervision is being transferred from the Bank of England to a single regime to give the regulator cohesion across the range of financial services. That also gives us the opportunity to set out clearly the specific role of the Bank of England in its monetary policy and other non-statutory obligations.

The hon. Member for Daventry was right about my response to amendment No. 50. The amendment is unnecessary because the financial stability role of the Bank and the Financial Services Authority is fully spelt out in the memorandum of understanding. That memorandum is a public document and has been signed by the chairman and governors of the three relevant authorities; the Governor of the Bank of England, the Chancellor of the Exchequer and the chairman of the Financial Services Authority, who are the three key players, are comfortable with the Government's arrangement.

Our intention is to improve the quality of regulation available to banks. Let me reiterate the hon. Gentleman's point, which I take fully: in exploring these issues, there is no question that we are criticising the existing system of banking supervision. Indeed, we are anxious to learn from the experience of the professionals in banking supervision and to give them a specific and more public role within the single regulator. They will bring a much-admired professionalism to the Financial Services Authority; there is great professionalism in the existing self-regulatory organisations.

The Bill is a first step. It will not transfer overall financial services regulation to a single regulator; it is but one part of the process.
