Penalty interest is set at a higher rate to provide both commercial restitution for money outstanding and an element of penalty. If you will indulge me, Mr. Cook, I shall go briefly into history and outline the origins of the difficulties caused by the starting rate of savings vis-à-vis individuals and their savings. The first time that a lower-rate band was introduced was in the 1992 Budget, by Lord Lamont, the then Chancellor of the Exchequer. It posed an immediate problem, as, although the basic rate of tax was 25 per cent., people were being taxed on savings with a starting rate of 20 per cent. Those whose savings took them just into the 20 per cent. band had considerable difficulties in claiming back the over-deduction of tax. Tax was deducted from savings at 25 per cent., but those who were liable for tax only at 20 per cent had to claw back that 5 per cent. Higher rate taxpayers had to pay an additional proportion of tax up to the higher rate level.

The problem was remedied in 1995 when the 1995 Budget changed the system. Deductions on savings for basic rate taxpayers and those on the starting rate were set at 20 per cent. That solved the problem for those people who had crept into the starting rate band because the amount of tax that was deducted from their savings matched their obligation to pay tax. People in higher rate bands had to pay more. That was the situation from 1995 to this Budget—savings were taxed at 20 per cent. and deductions were made at 20 per cent.

Last year's Budget introduced a starting rate band of £1,500 at 10 per cent. That was an election pledge. It was no surprise and it has been uprated to £1,520 in this Budget. However, the old problem has returned. People who just qualify for the starting rate because they have a small amount of savings, will find that 20 per cent. is deducted from their savings, but that their liability for taxation is only 10 per cent. Therefore, they will have to claw back from the Inland Revenue the 10 per cent. to which they are entitled. That will cause a huge administrative problem. The changes in the 1995 Budget were introduced to deal with the difficulties that those on low incomes had in clawing back the sums that were due to them which, at that time, was only 5 per cent. They now have a considerably larger sum to claw back. The only way to do that is to submit a tax return.
