Clause 50

Obligation in connection with electricity from renewable sources

Mr. Norman Baker (Lewes): I beg to move amendment No. 246 in page 53, line 3, leave out ``may'' and insert-

    `shall, within one year of the entering into force of this Act,'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss amendment No. 747, in page 53, line 10, leave out ``may'' and insert ``shall''.

Mr. Baker: This morning's amendments on renewable energy have been tabled predominantly by the Liberal Democrats. I hope that other members of the Committee will join us in supporting them. I can hear a murmur of assent from Labour Members.

Amendment No. 246 is a standard amendment that is tabled to many Bills. The clause allows the Secretary of State to impose on an electricity supplier-or another supplier-an obligation to generate electricity using renewable sources, or to ensure that between suppliers they have done so. However, the use of the word ``may'' means that the Secretary of State is not obliged to do so. Further more, no time limit is specified. The amendment would impose a duty on the Secretary of State to give effect to the commitment.

I shall develop that theme. We have heard fine words from the Government about renewable energy, and they have published documents that state their commitment to it. However, a close reading of the Bill suggests that, once enacted, it may have no effect, because such commitments have become aspirations couched in modal verbs such as ``may'' rather than positive verbs such as ``shall''.

As the Government are clearly committed to the policy of developing renewable energy, which they have rightly espoused, they should have no objection to the amendment. It would give strength to their intentions and send a clear signal to the industry. If the word ``may'' remains, it is possible that nothing will change. I therefore hope that the Minister will be sympathetic to the amendment.

The Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe (Mrs Helen Liddell): I appreciate the desire of the hon. Member for Lewes (Mr. Baker), for the Government to set the new renewables obligation as soon as possible, and very much share his enthusiasm for that. The Government will lay a new renewables order before Parliament as soon as is practicable, and I wish that to be within one year of the Bill coming into force, as the amendment would require. It may assist the Committee if I put the matter into context, so that I do not have to delay our proceedings by doing so later.

In pursuit of our manifesto commitment to a

    new and strong drive to develop renewable energy

we are proposing a major and fundamental shift in renewables policy. That follows a far-reaching review with extensive public consultation. As the hon. Gentleman knows, we have moved away from the old non-fossil fuel obligation structure. The NFFO mechanism was not a failure; it was successful in bringing down the price of renewable energy. However, it had failings, including a poor implementation record on renewables projects and excessive Government involvement in the selection of projects and technologies-the Government were put in the position of having to choose winners, which, historically, Governments have not been terribly good at. Another failing was the intermittent nature of the NFFO bidding process, which did not provide the continuity that the renewables industry requires. As we were about to implement-under a clause that I hope that we shall discuss later today-broader changes in the structure of the electricity market, we had an opportunity to do something about the NFFO structure.

The Bill will provide the necessary powers to place an obligation on licensed electricity suppliers to supply a fixed proportion of their power from renewable sources. That firm obligation will allow generators to invest in renewable energy technologies with confidence that they will have a market for their product.

Mr. Brian White (Milton Keynes, North-East): Given the history of renewables in this country, some investors are cynical about the way in which the investments market has been made available to them. Will my right hon. Friend assure those investors that the Government's commitment to renewables means that they will have a marketplace in which to invest?

Mrs Liddell: I can certainly reassure investors. Committee members are discussing the policy of supplying 10 per cent. of power from renewable sources by 2010. Our view is that the programme should continue until 2025-a long way away-and place specific obligations on electricity suppliers.

I want to consult on the details before the order setting the 10 per cent. target for renewables by 2010 is made, to ensure that, in the light of the state and market readiness of much of the technology, the target is achievable and the cost to the consumer is acceptable. That will win consumers round to the idea of renewable energy-cost is an important element in that regard.

I intend to leave it to electricity suppliers to choose the renewables technologies that they use to meet their obligations. In that way, the Government's intervention will be kept to a minimum and ensure that we work within the framework of a competitive market to minimise the additional burden on consumers.

The obligation will sit alongside other measures that the Government are taking, including the exemption of renewables-generated electricity from the climate change levy, an expanded research and development programme, and the development of a regional strategic approach and targets to help overcome some of the planning obstacles that are associated with renewables projects. We are also examining the scope for limited additional support for longer-term technologies, such as offshore wind and energy crops.

Against the background of the Government's strong support for renewables, there is an exciting opportunity to develop the renewables industry and to turn it from a fledgeling industry into a mainstream business activity. That will be good for business, for the diversity and security of supply and for the environment. I want to consider the amendment in that context. It would not be wise to prescribe a time limit in the Bill or to introduce an obligation to do something that we shall do anyway-amendment No. 747 would have that effect.

We are already a long way down the road towards preparing an order, but more needs to be done. We have got to get our approach right, not least in co-operation with the industry. We intend to consult on a draft order before we lay it before Parliament. I hope to be in a position to do that within the next few months. We also have to implement the new framework of electricity licensing before we can impose the new obligations. In particular, we have to achieve the separation of the public electricity suppliers into separately licensed supply and distribution companies.

That wil be a complex process and it would not be responsible to legislate without allowing for the possiblity that the timetable might slip. Our intention is to present an obligation within a year but, as any rational person will realise, risks are associated with our approach. I am sure that the hon. Member for Lewes did not intend to complicate the situation further. Amendment No. 747 would preclude the possibility of the Secretary of State making further orders, but we might want to increase the obligations in future.

In view of my explanation, my firm undertaking to press ahead with the preparation of the order as quickly as possible and my desire to implement it within a year of the Bill reaching the statue book, I hope that the hon. Member for Lewes will withdraw his amendment.

A simple definition of renewable energy refers to those energy sources that for all practical purposes are continually replenished. It can be defined in other ways-as energy that is obtained from the continuous or repetitive currents of energy that occur in the natural environment; as energy flows that are replenished at the same rate as they are used, including, more broadly, the use of any energy storage reservoir that is refilled at a rate that is comparable to that of extraction; or as those energy flows that occur naturally and repeatedly in the environment and can be harnessed for human benefit-the ultimate sources of most of that energy are the sun, gravity and the earth's rotation.
