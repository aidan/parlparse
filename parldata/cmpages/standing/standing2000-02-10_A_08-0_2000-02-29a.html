Dr. Howells: Taken together, the amendments honour a commitment made in the 1998 White Paper that has been widely supported during consultations. Dr. Howells: The amendments are intended primarily to correct errors that were made in transposing the gas and electricity regulator's secondary general duties from the existing legislation to clauses 8 and 12. We have recast the regulator's general duties to put consumers at the heart of the regulatory process. In both the gas and electricity sectors, that has involved elevating the consumer duty, which is a secondary duty in the existing legislation, to ensure that nothing takes precedence over the interests of consumers.

Apart from those changes, our general approach has been to preserve the other existing secondary duties in terms both of their content and their position in the hierarchy of duties. In seeking to preserve the remaining secondary general duties in the Bill, we made two accidental omissions. The amendments correct those, and make an additional change.

The first omission relates to the gas sector. The secondary duty to protect the public from dangers arising from the conveyance of gas through pipes or from the use of gas was omitted from the Bill. That duty is currently located in section 4A(1) of the Gas Act 1986. Amendment No. 180 transposes that duty word for word, in the same position within the hierarchy, into the gas regulator's general duties in clause 8. The secondary duty will match its electricity equivalent, which is set out in clause 12, and sit alongside the new specific duty in clause 10 to consult the Health and Safety Commission on gas safety issues, and to take account of any advice that is given.

The second omission also concerns the gas sector. Clause 8 states that the gas regulator's secondary duty to promote efficiency and economy applies in relation only to licence holders. However, under the 1986 Act, that duty applies in relation to persons who operate under an exemption, as well as those who hold a licence. The amendments correct that omission by ensuring that the duty to promote efficiency and economy applies equally to exempt persons and to those who hold a licence.

The amendments go further by making similar changes to the electricity regulator's duty in clause 12 to promote efficiency and economy. That widens the existing scope of that duty, as it appears in the Electricity Act 1989, so that it too captures persons operating under an exemption, as well as those who hold licences. That change to the existing position in the electricity sector makes it conform to that of the gas sector, which is desirable given that both sectors are now to be regulated by a single authority, GEMA.

The appointment of a single regulatory authority reflects the convergence of the two sectors and the necessity, whatever possible, to subject the two sectors to a common regulatory regime. The authority's principle objective of protecting the interests of consumers extends to customers of exempt suppliers, and elsewhere in the Bill there are provisions that make it clear that conditions may be attached to the granting of exemptions. Promoting efficiency and economy to exempt persons is consistent with that approach, and I commend the amendment to the Committee.
