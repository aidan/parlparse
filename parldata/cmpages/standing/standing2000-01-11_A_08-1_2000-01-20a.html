Mr. David Davis (Haltemprice and Howden): Before we adjourned for lunch, I was halfway through a speech on amendment No. 40, during which the Government Whip, who is a former member of the Public Accounts Committee, became so excited that I am tempted to give the entire speech again. [Interruption.] He was almost falling off his chaisé-longué with excitement. However, I shall merely summarise my speech.

The White Paper, the Companies Act and generally accepted accounting practice argue for greater powers for the principal auditor than the Bill will give to the Comptroller and Auditor General. The national health service consolidated account is the only consolidated account with which the Comptroller and Auditor General deals. In effect, the Audit Commission handles most of the subsidiary work. It has the power to inspect, to intervene and to demand papers from subsidiary organisations on the Comptroller and Auditor General's behalf. The Audit Commission, national health service trusts and other bodies work on the basis that the existence of those powers is sufficient, and exercising them is virtually unnecessary. A habit of co-operation allows the Comptroller and Auditor General to produce summarised accounts.

Government accounts are a far more complex consolidation than NHS accounts. They include bodies that are audited by private firms and public corporations, with which, at present, the National Audit Office has no direct contact. Unlike the Audit Commission, the auditors of such bodies do not work closely with the National Audit Office. Without the leverage that the reserve power gives the Comptroller and Auditor General elsewhere, it is highly unlikely that he will be able to persuade auditors to do the additional work that is sometimes necessary to provide evidence on the consolidation to Parliament.

A potential consequence of the uncertainty of audit evidence is that the Comptroller and Auditor General will have to give a qualified opinion of Government accounts. Such an occurrence would not be in the interests of the Treasury, Parliament or the country. National Audit Office officials advised the Treasury several months before the Bill was introduced that, to avoid a qualified opinion, the Comptroller and Auditor General must be granted those rights. However, the Treasury simply ignored the Comptroller and Auditor General's view of his own needs. The Treasury feels that it knows best, and its approach is to disregard his independent view and substitute its own.

Perhaps the Treasury does not trust the Comptroller and Auditor General to use the reserve power sensibly in accordance with professional auditing practice, although such a power would not lead to a massive programme of visits to bodies whose affairs are reflected in the account. The power would be a reserve power, and that is what the amendment would provide.

Mr. Flight: I beg to move amendment No. 90, in page 8, line 7, at end insert

    `but the total financial assistance which the Treasury may provide, including that specified under subsections (1)(a) and (b), shall not exceed the sum of £500 million'.'

Clauses 15, 16 and 17 deal with different territory from the rest of the Bill: the establishment of Partnerships UK. Most of our amendments are drafted in an attempt to match the legal definitions of the scope of Partnerships UK and its activities to the Government's final statement on the matter in July. There has been some lack of clarity as to what Partnerships UK is supposed to be doing, and it may help the Committee if I try to clarify that.

The Government have made it clear that, in line with the Bates report, Partnerships UK is intended to provide a permanent base for the Treasury taskforce. Problems have apparently been perceived, in respect of so-called City levels of remuneration, in retaining the team in the Treasury. One might say that the objective has been to privatise—or PFI-ise—the Treasury taskforce. Partnerships UK is to continue the taskforce's roles of negotiating private finance initiatives with the Treasury and related Departments. Speeding up and increasing the volume of such projects has been one of the taskforces' key successes. Perhaps surprisingly, I have come to the view that that role would be much better performed if the permit stayed in the Treasury.

The taskforce's stated objectives and purposes include putting PFls into better shape more quickly, pump priming for feasibility studies for local authority projects, expanding the range of projects that can be undertaken, and seeking to make good the Public Sector skills deficit on putting PFI deals together.

The Government have indicated that they intend that only a minority of PFI deals should be handled by Partnerships UK. Figures of a maximum of a third have been leaked to the press, rather than overtly stated. That raises the issue of who will handle Treasury and Departmental negotiations for the other two thirds. Will that be an unpaid role of Partnerships UK?

It has been made clear that there are some matters that Partnerships UK will not deal with.

Mr. Flight: I thank you, Mr. Butterfill.

The Government and the head of the taskforce do not propose that Partnerships UK should be involved as leaders and organisers of the syndicates bidding for PFI deals, or of the syndicates providing finance for such deals. There would be enormous impropriety if a 51 per cent. private sector organisation were writing business on the back of privileged access to public sector business.

The problem of the second, simpler, conflict of interest has been widely acknowledged, Partnerships UK might advise a local authority or other public sector body on how to get things right. If it were then to take an equity stake, how would it marry acting in the best interests of its public sector client with its own best interests as a private sector body to make a profit? The Confederation of British Industry and the TUC have unanimously advised against Partnerships UK taking equity stakes.

The Government and the taskforce have responded by suggesting four ways round the problem. Partnerships UK could be paid for a feasibility study that it had financed either by the Treasury, by the local authority or other public sector body, or out of the total PFI deal, if the gross value included that amount. It has also been suggested that a public authority buying PFI deals should not decide how it would pay Partnerships UK for its advice until it has negotiated the deal, and that Partnerships UK could hold equity if it held it on exactly the same terms as the public sector body. That last suggestion has been viewed as unsatisfactory, and the other possible ways of disposing of the short-term interim equity stake acquired have been viewed as the only ones that would fully avoid conflict of interest problems.

In addition to that straightforward conflict of interest problem, another that is potentially as important—and that could damage the long-term reputation of Partnerships UK just as much—is that it could not be seen, as a private sector body, to have privileged access and to make money as a result. That would be seen as a considerable impropriety. Therefore, a slight conflict lies at the heart of the matter of what Partnerships UK should be legally empowered to do. It would exist to obtain the best deal for the public sector body that it is advising, while also endeavouring, as a majority private sector body, to get the best deal for itself and to make profits.

The clauses fail to define the scope and activities of Partnerships UK as announced by the Government. Clause 15, and especially subsection (3), to which our amendment relates, provide an open-ended commitment of Treasury funding, not only to establishing Partnerships UK and setting up operations, but subsequently. The amendment would cap the total amount that the Government could invest in Partnerships UK and any subsidiaries thereof at £500 million, without inflation escalation. I sounded out the taskforce to discover whether that figure was reasonable and it said that it was sufficient.

The key point is that, without a cap, Partnerships UK can do whatever it wants. It could become a major participant, lender or syndicate organiser, and could slip into the problems faced by similar vehicles that were set up by a Labour Government in the 1960s with—dare I say it?—the risk of ministerial intervention leading to unsuitable investments.

I rise with some hesitation, as my previous speech was so persuasive that I made my hon. Friend the Member for Arundel and South Downs vote the wrong way. Before I move on to the substance of the amendment, I want to touch on the delicious paradox of this and the subsequent clause.

We have heard from Ministers, time and again, that the point of principle—if I may grace it with that phrase—against introducing anything related to audit or accountability is that it has nothing to do with resource accounting. Partnerships UK, of course, also has nothing to do with resource accounting. The double irony is that the reason for putting time pressure on the Committee is Partnerships UK, not resource accounting. Resource accounting will carry on as it would if the Bill took years to be passed, but Partnerships UK is the necessity that drives us. Although I agree with the timetable, that is an interesting irony.

Partnerships UK will be an important body, which will exercise a key role in the development of the private finance initiative. That is an admirable aim. It will receive substantial assistance from funds voted by Parliament. The Treasury has spoken of £6 million for the cost of formation, including the cost of advisers—as always—and £20 million working capital. In addition to that seed capital, we have already heard, in part, that the Treasury plans to provide financial assistance to Partnerships UK, and/or any subsidiaries or joint venture companies, in a variety of ways, including guarantees, loans, subscription of share capital, and acquisition of debt instruments, which is made clear in subsection (4). Mr. Davis: The Committee may be relieved to hear that I intend to withdraw new clause 5 and new clause 7, although we may return to them on Report. That will depend on the Economic Secretary's response to the hon. Member for Swansea, West when he speaks to some of the later new clauses. I shall now focus primarily on new clause 2.

The audit arrangements for arms length public bodies are currently arbitrary and illogical. Consequently, there is a lack of consistency in the information that is provided to Parliament and an unnecessary waste and duplication of audit effort. It is important to be clear about which bodies we are talking about, as there is significant scope for confusion. Indeed, confusion has even been expressed by experts among members of the Committee. For the purposes of the new clause, the generic term is public service agencies, which are defined as bodies that exercise

    ``public functions of a governmental nature which might otherwise fall to be exercised by central government''—

in other words, executive non-departmental public bodies. Those are bodies directly funded by Departments to carry out public functions such as the administration of legal aid or the funding of higher education or student loans.

There are some 300 such bodies in the United Kingdom, and they are an increasingly important part of national government. Between 1979 and 1999, public funds spent by the bodies more than doubled from £8.5 billion to more than £18 billion at today's prices. About 100 now fall within the jurisdiction of the devolved assemblies, leaving just over 200 that should fall within the remit of the Comptroller and Auditor General. The bodies vary in terms of status. I have grouped them into four sets, which I shall outline.

Some bodies are audited by the Comptroller and Auditor General, and thereby subject to full parliamentary accountability. Some are established as companies—I shall return to those in a moment. Some are established by statute and audited by the private sector on behalf of the relevant Secretary of State. Many of those are small, but the Housing Corporation, spending £1 billion, also falls within that group. Finally, some are established by administrative means and audited by the private sector on behalf of the relevant Secretary of State.

I know that the Treasury has had some difficulty in recent days trying to work out which bodies fit into which category, and I was reassured to discover that the National Audit Office has been almost as perplexed. The Economic Secretary will no doubt say that the annual Cabinet Office publication, Public Bodies, is the comprehensive authority. However, that is compiled only after a lengthy process during which Departments scratch around trying desperately to decide which bodies they monitor. The NAO is often asked to assist in that process. That is not a recipe for clear-cut accountability.

The complexity and evident confusion go to the heart of the problem. We are talking about a single category of bodies, the executive non-departmental public bodies, being subject to a multiplicity of accountability arrangements. Leaving aside the matter of bodies established as companies, the new clause would provide a single accountability structure. The Comptroller and Auditor General would be appointed as auditor of all such bodies already in existence, and of any new bodies that are created in future.
