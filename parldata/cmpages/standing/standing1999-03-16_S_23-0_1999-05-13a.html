Mr. Richard Allan (Sheffield, Hallam): I wish to continue moving the amendment. The purpose of these amendments is to probe the nature of the parties or the groups of immigration officers who might be called upon to execute a warrant. They seek to ensure that when a warrant is issued under the powers that will be given to immigration officers, the person who issues it has some idea of the size and nature of the group who will use it. For every person named on the warrant no more than two additional persons should go out on the mission to arrest or to enter and search premises.

It is important that those who issue warrants have some idea of the nature of the operation because of the enormous sensitivity in terms of community relations, which we have already discussed. Some clarity is needed here and that is the view of ILPA too. The clause as it stands could allow a warrant to be issued in the name of a single immigration officer for what would effectively be a large-scale exercise. A search at a hostel, for example, could involve quite a large number of people who have broken their immigration conditions. We therefore seek a little more clarity about the process of issuing warrants and the extent to which the justices of the peace who issue them will have information on the nature of the operation they are authorising.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): The aim of the amendment is to prevent excessive numbers of immigration officers or police officers accompanying the officer who is to execute the warrant. I confirm that the restrictions that apply to the police in this regard will also apply to immigration officers. Indeed, the immigration service is not so flush with staff these days that it will send large numbers of people to do anything.

A warrant is issued on the understanding that the number of accompanying persons is necessary for the purpose of the search. If the number is unreasonable, the search may be deemed to be invalid. There are difficulties in setting a precise limit in certain operations. For example, some operations involve attending a factory where a large number of people are present, so a number of officers may be necessary.

If it was considered appropriate for a police officer, an immigration officer of the opposite gender and a person from another agency with an interest in a search to accompany the officer executing the warrant, who would be left out? I am not sure that we would want anyone to be left out in that situation.

The amendments are based on the assumption that all warrants will be issued with the intention of searching for persons. Clause 107 provides for the issuing of warrants to search for evidence. How would the amendments comply with that clause? What would happen if a large premises was to be searched, but the provisions did not allow sufficient officers to do it?

Those examples display the care that we must take before introducing new elements into legislation that has already been tested in the operational arena. The provisions in the clause are based on the existing provisions in section 16 of PACE. They are necessary not only to provide consistency between the police and immigration practices, but to avoid two sets of procedure for the justices of the peace and the sheriffs who will consider the applications. Justices of the peace are well used to signing warrants for such searches. They will make appropriate inquiries. In my experience the extent to which they make inquiries varies, but they will want to know what the purpose of the warrant is and how it is proposed that it should be executed. They will also have the right to ask any other questions that they deem appropriate.

Clause 116

Detention of persons liable to examination or removal

Amendment made: No. 804, in page 75, line 4, leave out from `If' to `pending' in line 6 and insert

    `there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that a person is someone in respect of whom directions may be given under any of paragraphs 8 to 10 or 12 to 14, that person may be detained under the authority of an immigration officer'.—[Mr. Mike O'Brien.]

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Allan: Can the Minister put on the record an explanation of the reasons for the changes that are being made here and their likely implications? He will be aware that I and my colleagues have grave reservations about the way in which detention is used and are unhappy about the way in which we believe it is used at present. The Bill seeks to make some improvements in bail provisions. Can he also give an indication of scope of the changes and say whether there is any relationship between the changes to the detention regime and the other provisions, such as the automatic bail hearing within a week of the person being detained?

Mr. O'Brien: We shall have a chance to discuss detention on later clauses, but let me explain the purpose of the clause.

There is a difficulty with the current detention provisions which the clause is intended to overcome. The present position under schedule 2 to the Immigration Act, 1971 is that a person may be detained if he is liable to examination or removal. In some cases, although there has been compelling evidence that, prima facie, a person falls into one or other of those categories, it has subsequently been determined that that is not the case. Even where a person has not assisted the immigration officer to determine the true facts, such a detention is currently unlawful and as such exposes the immigration service to legal action and damages, even though it may have been effected honestly and properly. Indeed, that issue has had to be addressed in a number of cases. We therefore seek to clarify the law so that, where there are reasonable grounds for suspecting that a person is in one of those categories, the immigration service can deal with it properly.

Clause 117

Use of force by immigration officers

Mr. O'Brien: I beg to move amendment No. 992, in page 75, line 15, at end insert—

    `( ) Any person exercising a power conferred by sections (Fingerprinting) or (Attendance for fingerprinting) may, if necessary, use reasonable force.'.

The Chairman: With this we may take Government amendments Nos. 988 to 990,

Government new clause 41—Proof of identity of persons to be removed or deported.

Government new clause 42—Fingerprinting.

Government new clause 43—Attendence for fingerprinting.

Government new clause 44—Destruction of fingerprints—

Government new clause 45—Other methods of collecting data about physical characteristics.

Mr. O'Brien: At present all asylum seekers are fingerprinted under existing provisions, a policy that has operated satisfactorily and sensitively for some years. Now we intend to incororate that power into the Bill and to ensure that fingerprinting can take place for other categories of person known to abuse our immigration system.

The categories where fingerprinting can take place now are limited. For reasons that I shall outline, it is necessary to extend the powers primarily for categories other than asylum seekers, although some of the provisions may have an impact across the board.

Where an immigration officer has apprehended and detained an offender under paragraph 16 of schedule 2 to the 1971 Act, he has the power under paragraph 18(2) and under paragraph 2(4) to schedule 3 to the 1971 Act to take all necessary steps to photograph, measure or otherwise identify the person. That implies the power to fingerprint only for purposes of identification. There is no power in law for the immigration service to retain those fingerprints once a person is released, even if only on temporary admission. In addition, the person may have been detained under paragraph 16. It would not cover those arrested for offences under other parts of the 1971 Act. In the majority of cases, fingerprint records are kept for a maximum of 10 years.

Amendment No. 992 would provide that anyone authorised to take fingerprints under the Bill—constables, immigration officers, prison officers, authorised officers of the Secretary of State and employees at detention centres—may use reasonable force if necessary when taking fingerprints. It is a necessary provision, although we hope that it will be used infrequently. The definition of what constitutes reasonable force in a certain circumstance is ultimately a matter for the courts, but it is a well-worn route and the courts are familiar with the concept. Any force used must be no more than necessary to achieve a lawful purpose such as, in this case, the taking of fingerprints.

I come now to amendment Nos. 988. Under paragraph 18(2) of schedule 2 of the 1971 Act, immigration officers, prison officers and the police have powers to take any steps that are ``reasonably necessary'' to confirm the identity of someone who is detained under that Act. We have always been firmly of the view that the powers to take ``reasonably necessary'' steps to confirm someone's identify include the power to take fingerprints. The purpose of amendment No. 988 is to make that clear beyond any doubt.

4.45 pm

Amendments Nos. 989 and 990 are technical. They make it clear that the power to fingerprint under the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984—PACE—and the Police and Criminal Evidence (Northern Ireland) Order 1989 do not affect in any way the power to fingerprint contained in the Bill. PACE and the 1989 order already contain a similar provision in respect of the existing power to fingerprint under schedule 2 of the 1971 Act, which provides the power to fingerprint those who are detained for identification purposes. The amendments are in line with that provision, and I invite the Committee to accept them.

Government new clause 41 will allow the release of fingerprint data to the authorities of other countries in circumstances where the individual concerned is to be removed from the United Kingdom, but does not have a valid passport or other identity document that perimts him to travel. The release of any personal information to another country is obviously extremely sensitive and should be done only in limited circumstances when there is a specific need to do so. We will, therefore, only use the power as a last resort when the authorities of the country to which the person is to be returned refuse to acknowledge that the person is one of its nationals unless a fingerprint is provided and when there is no other way of returning that person to his home country. For example, Morocco refuses to issue travel documents to its nationals without a copy of their fingerprints. However, that is rare and we expect to use the power infrequently.

I stress that no information about any asylum claim will be released to an individual's national authority. Of course, those provisions will apply not only to asylum seekers. Someone who is being removed for other reasons might be part of the provisions, so the other country would not know whether he had made an asylum claim.

I come to Government new clause 42. At present, with the exception of asylum seekers, our powers to fingerprint the individuals who may commit, or may have committed, an immigration offence are limited. That inability to fingerprint them, and more importantly to retain fingerprints, greatly reduces the scope for effective enforcement action to identify and to remove immigration offenders.

If we leave aside asylum seekers, the only power to fingerprint we have at present is the power to fingerprint someone who is arrested for the purposes of identification. That power does not permit us to retain fingerprints once the person has been released, even if only on temporary release. We are determined to strengthen immigration controls where necessary. Therefore, we need additional powers to fingerprint and to retain those fingerprints in order to achieve that aim.

It might help if I set out the scale of the problem. Every passenger arriving in the United Kingdom has to produce a valid passport, identity card or other document that satisfactorily establishes his or her identity and nationality. In the first half of 1998, 10,000 passengers arrived in the United Kingdom with inadequate identity documentation or no documentation at all. They were unable to satisfy the immigration officer at the port of entry of their identity. Such people fall to be refused entry to the country.

Most cases merit futher investigation before the individual concerned is removed from the United Kingdom. The immigration service has limited detention space, so the majority of people in that position are granted temporary admission—so too are most asylum seekers. There are powers to deal with asylum seekers, but the new clause is primarily concerned with others.

Persons suspected of being immigration offenders and apprehended in-country—that is after they have entered the United Kingdom—would normally be given temporary release, which is the equivalent of temporary admission. Unfortunately, many of them subsequently abscond. Absconders from temporary admission or release, failed asylum seekers, illegal entrants and overstayers are all likely to become the object of enforcement action. Without adequate powers to fingerprint such persons, they could make successful use of false identities to avoid identification and further action when apprehended and to repeat the process on subsequent occasions using a series of different identities.

It would obviously be disproportionate to fingerprint all those who are granted temporary admission, because many of them are genuine visitors whose immigration status is quickly resolved. We therefore intend to extend the power to fingerprint only in respect of specific categories of people. The first is inadequately documented arrivals—for example, people who arrive without passports. Another is people who have been refused entry and given temporary admission when an immigration officer reasonably suspects that they may abscond.

In the case of inadequately documented arrivals, instructions to those authorised to take fingerprints will make it clear that they should be taken only when there is no reasonable excuse for failing to produce adequate documentation. The persons against whom that power will be directed are, for example, those who have deliberately destroyed or concealed their documentation—not those who have an innocent explanation for the failure.

We also intend to extend the power to fingerprint people who are to be removed or deported so that we can identify them if they try to return to this country by adopting a new identity. Unfortunately, that is all too common an occurrence.

The amendment also provides for the power to fingerprint anyone arrested under the second schedule of the 1971 Act. That will help us identify someone, for example, who is fingerprinted as an inadequately documented passenger, subsequently absconds, is then arrested under the second schedule on suspicion of having breached temporary admission conditions and is fingerprinted again. The fingerprint comparison would enable us to make an immediate identification. Finally, we want to extend the power to fingerprint certain dependants.

However, I should stress that we have no intention of routinely fingerprinting children. We intend to fingerprint children under the age of 16 only when there are doubts as to their identity. Where such doubts do arise, it may be necessary to obtain the authority of a chief immigration officer to take the prints and, in all cases, the parent or guardian of the child—or an adult who has taken responsibility for the child for the time being—must be present.

The provisions mirror those relating to the fingerprinting of the dependants of asylum seekers. We recognise the sensitivities surrounding fingerprinting. However, we believe that there is a considerable need to enhance immigration control by these means. Asylum seekers have already been fingerprinted for several years and it has operated satisfactorily. We shall build on that experience. We intend to introduce safeguards to the system and we will publish the instructions to be issued to immigration staff before the provisions come into force, so there will be some time for representations to be heard.

New clause 43 provides a power to require those who are liable to be fingerprinted to go to a specified place within a specified time in order to be fingerprinted. There is also a power for a constable or immigration officer to arrest without warrant anyone who fails to do so. Similar provision exists in section 3 of the Asylum and Immigration Appeals Act 1993 in relation to asylum seekers only.

The new clause deals with the practicalities of fingerprinting those persons identified in new clause 42. Ideally, fingerprints will be taken immediately. For example, a person who arrives at a port of entry with inadequate documentation who cannot offer a satisfactory explanation is likely to be fingerprinted on arrival. Similarly, we would expect someone who has been arrested under paragraph 17 of the second schedule to the 1971 Act—referred to as ``D'' in subsection (7)(d) of new clause 42—to be fingerprinted as soon as he is arrested.

However, it may not always be possible to fingerprint immediately—perhaps because of something as simple as a finger wound or, in the case of someone under 16, the absence of a responsible adult, as defined in subsection (4). That is not ideal because the idea of taking a person's fingerprints is to ensure that we are certain of an individual's identity and have a record for future records before he is released.

On occasions when it has not been possible to fingerprint someone immediately, we would expect to use the power in the amendment to issue them with a notice advising them that they are required to return either to the same place or to somewhere more convenient where their fingerprints will be taken. The notice will also give a seven-day period in which the person must attend for fingerprinting and may specify a time of day or particular hours during which the person may attend. The period will not begin any earlier than seven days after the notice was issued. If the person does not attend as required, the power to arrest him is needed in order to take his fingerprints. The 1993 Act made similar provisions in respect of asylum seekers. If the arrest takes place at an immigration office—perhaps the person has returned for further interviews—his fingerprints may be taken there. It is possible that the arrest would take place as the result of a person being apprehended in the field. In such circumstances, he may be removed either to an immigration office or a police station where his fingerprints will be taken.

New clause 44 provides clear and specific guidelines on when fingerprinting records must be destroyed. It also provides safeguards so that the person whose fingerprints have been taken can be sure that the database records have also been destroyed. That mirrors the existing provisions on the destruction of asylum seekers' fingerprints in section 3 of the 1993 Act.

In the majority of cases, fingerprint records will be kept for 10 years. That is the essential counterpart to the power to take fingerprints; without it, that power is meaningless. For example, someone is fingerprinted because he is to be removed as an illegal entrant. He is successfully removed, but some years later he returns to the country and arrives at port without documentation. Under the new powers, his fingerprints could be taken and compared with previous fingerprints for immediate indentification. That would not be possible if we did not have the power to retain fingerprints.

There are exceptions, however. We will not retain fingerprints for 10 years if, for example, a person was fingerprinted because the immigration officer was not satisfied as to his identity. If it is later proven that he is a British citizen, the fingerprints will probably be destroyed. When an asylum seeker has been granted indefinite leave to remain in the United Kingdom, the fingerprint records will be destroyed as soon as practicable. That mirrors the provisions in section 3(6) of the 1993 Act. Similarly, when a person in any other category is given leave to enter or to remain in the United Kingdom, the fingerprint records will be destroyed as soon as practicable.

In addition, fingerprint records taken from a person who has been refused leave to enter and granted temporary admission will be destroyed when he has left the United Kingdom, unless he has broken the conditions of his temporary admission relating to residence or reporting requirements. That is so that the fingerprints can be used in any future enforcement action. There is a possibility that someone who has committed an offence once by breaking a condition of his or her temporary admission may do so again after removal, for example, by returning to the Unted Kingdom and entering as an illegal entrant. A fingerprint will help to identify such a person. Other exceptions to the 10-year rule are those in respect of whom removal directions cease to have effect and those who, having been arrested under schedule 2, are subsequently released.

Finally, if a person has been fingerprinted as the dependant of someone whose fingerprints are taken under the categories set out in new clause 42, their records will be destroyed at the same time.

We are determined to make the procedure as secure as possible and for that reason new clause 44 provides for all copies of the original fingerprint records to be destroyed. When those records are held in electronic form, it will mean ensuring either that they are destroyed or erased or that access to the information is blocked. The person to whom the data refers will also be able to request a certificate confirming that the records have been disposed of in that manner. The Secretary of State will be required to supply such a certificate within three months of a request to do so.

Taking anyone's fingerprints is a sensitive matter. The retention, and ultimate destruction, of those fingerprint records is equally sensitive. The amendment fulfils our obligation to treat the matter with the utmost care and security, while still allowing the operational requirements of immigration control to be met. I urge the Committee to accept the new clause.

New clause 45 would provide the Secretary of State with powers to make regulations to enable data to be collected about a person's external physical characteristics other than by taking fingerprints. New methods of uniquely identifying people are being developed all the time: for example, there are systems in development based upon the recognition of the iris of the eye, or of a palm print. Another likely technique may involve a sophisticated photograph.

The new clause would give the Secretary of State the flexibility to take advantage of new technology, as and when it develops. However, it is drafted so that the provisions of the regulations covering new technology would have to be equivalent to the provisions of new clauses 42 to 44. I invite the Committee to accept the amendment.
