In dealing with the history relating to my hon. and learned Friend's amendment, I will discuss what Sir Patrick Devlin said, because it goes to the heart of our objection to the Government's arguments for the Bill and particularly the crucial importance of considering the circumstances of the individual offender. My right hon. and learned Friend's amendment is directed towards that point. Sir Patrick said:

As my hon. The Bill was introduced in the name of modernisation, but in fact shows the Government's complete rejection of all the history and traditions of the law of England and Wales.

Sir Patrick went on to say:

    The English jury is not what it is because some lawgiver so decreed, but because that is the way it has grown up. Indeed, its invention by a lawgiver is inconceivable. We are used to it--

    and know that it works.

That goes to the heart of our objection to the Bill and particularly the Government's rejection of what my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for North-East Bedfordshire said. They understand so little of the importance of a system that works and has grown up by tradition. Nothing sticks in my craw more strongly than the Government's complete contempt for history and tradition.

Sir Patrick also said:

    The jury system developed that way simply because that was the way in which it was found to work.

He talked about British traditions and the British people:

    The virtue that makes them a law-abiding people makes them also good guardians of the law and gives them a sense of fairness that makes them happy judges. They put up with the inconvenience of

    it--the waiting and the hanging about and the interference with the daily round--not only because they assume it as a proper responsibility but because, once they get into the jury box they enjoy being there. . . they perform. . . an important service to British justice. They sit there as witnesses of the legal process. They see the way the accused is treated--

this is crucial to the point made by my right hon. and learned Friend--

    listen to the evidence of character, hear the sentences that are passed, and silently approve or disapprove. . . It reminds them that they are not engaged upon a piece of professional ritual but in helping to give the ordinary man the sort of justice he can understand. Upon what jurymen think and say when they get home, the prestige of the law, in great measure, depends.

We have had the rather unusual benefit of the experience of my hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Worcestershire, who spoke about what happened when he sat on a jury. In one case, he was also the foreman of a jury. It is unusual for us to have the benefit of that experience, because the secrets of the jury room are normally kept. However, my hon. Friend was right to give us the benefit of the experience of a nonlawyer or lay juror. He was impressed by the way in which the system operated.

It seems that--I am sure that those who read the record of our debate would agree--when talking about civil liberties, the experience of the ordinary lay juror is central. My hon. Friend the Member for Mid-Worcestershire gave a graphic example of someone whose whole career depended on the result of a case and on the common sense of a British jury. All the points made by Sir Patrick Devlin in his Hamlyn lectures back in the 1950s were reinforced in spades by my hon. Friend's experience. That is unusual, because I do not think that any Committee considering such issues has ever had the opportunity to benefit from a speech by a former lay juror. From the research that I have undertaken, I think that that is unique. Many of us on both sides of the Committee have plenty of experience as practising lawyers in the courts, but hearing what goes on in the mind of a juror and in the jury room has been enormously beneficial and has completely undermined the Government's case, particularly against the amendment.

Sir Patrick went on to say:

    The judge's task--

and one might add the lay magistrate's task,

    is not merely to expound the law to the jury, but also to offer them an approach to the facts that is based on the ideas that lie behind the law ... It is not with him, as it may well be with the jury, the only trial in which he will ever take part; he is perhaps less interested than they are in the fates of the individuals concerned.

In this amendment, we are talking about the fates of individuals and the crucial constitutional protection that they should have.

    The jury is the best instrument for deciding upon the credibility or reliability of a witness and so for determining the primary facts. Whether a person is telling the truth, when it has to be judged, as so often it has, simply from the demeanour of the witness and his manner of telling it, is a matter about which it is easier for a single mind (or even three justices' minds) to be fallible.

That is surely the point. The collective impression made on 12 minds has in this country always been regarded as more reliable.

    The jury hear the witness as people who are as ignorant as they are of lawyers' ways of thought. That is the great advantage to any citizen of judgment by his peers--

the whole foundation of jury trial.

In 1922, Sir William Holdsworth stressed the role of the jury as the touchstone of contemporary common sense. He said:

    The effects of the jury system upon the law are no less remarkable and no less beneficial. It tends to make the law intelligible by keeping it in touch with the common facts of life. The reasons why and the manner in which it thus affects the law, are somewhat as follows: If a clever man is left to decide by himself disputed questions of fact he is usually not content simply to decide each case as it arises. He constructs theories for the decision of analogous cases. These theories are discussed, doubted, or developed by other clever men when such cases come before them. The interest is apt to centre, not in the dry task of deciding the case before the court but rather in the construction of new theories, the reconciliation of conflicting cases, the demolition of criticism of older views ... The jury system has for some hundreds of years been constantly bringing the rules of law to the touchstone of contemporary common sense.

It is precisely because of the way in which that common sense view of jurors can affect the characters of individuals that the amendment proposed by my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for North-East Bedfordshire is so important. The jury provides a safeguard.

I want to remind those who cynically dismiss the importance of these issues of what was said as long ago as 1784 by Mr. Justice Willes when he spoke about looking forward into futurity. He talked about the danger that might arise if a Government casually tossed away this constitutional safeguard:

    For more than seven out of the 8 centuries during which the judges of the common law have administered justice in this country, trial by jury ensured that Englishmen got the sort of justice they liked and not the sort of justice that the Government or the lawyers or any body of experts thought was good for them.

I wanted to end on the conclusions of the report published by the Commission for Racial Equality. It says that its findings were based on a small sample drawn primarily for qualitative purposes. It said that the findings cast significant doubt not so much on the statistics being cited by the Government to support limiting the right to elect but on the interpretation of the statistics being put forward. It said that, as far as comparisons were possible, the statistics from its research sample relating to outcomes in either-way cases, almost precisely mirrored those drawn from Home Office research, particularly in relation to the number of defendants who elected Crown court and subsequently entered a guilty plea. It went on to say that in seeking to use the statistics to argue that there is widespread abuse of the right to elect, the Government overlook the limited basis of Home Office statistics, for example, including defendants who are acquitted or otherwise cleared, and ignore the circumstances that lead the majority of those who change their pleas to guilty in the Crown court to do so. There is a wider community interest in studying the statistics properly. The Government are foolish to ignore the serious conclusions of the research.
