Clause 65

Rates of short-term incapacity benefit

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Steve Webb (Northavon): I should like to make a brief observation about the clause. I ask the Minister to address an anomaly in rates of short-term incapacity benefit, which I should have liked the clause to have dealt with, so I hope that the Minister will amend the clause at a later stage to take account of it.

The short-term rate of incapacity benefit for the first six months of the claim is below that for income support, so recipients with no other income will also receive income support. After six months, the rate of incapacity benefit rises but the income support rate does not, so they cease to be entitled to income support. However, after 12 months, although the rate of incapacity benefit rises further, the rate of income support rises further still because entitlement to a disability premium is triggered, so at that point the recipients start to become entitled to income support again.

Welfare rights advisers have pointed out to me that bizarre situation--caused by the current structure of rates of incapacity benefit--which means that recipients have six months on, six months off and then an indefinite period on. I would be grateful if the Minister, when commenting on the clause, stated whether he intends to address that anomaly.

The Paliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. John Denham): It may be helpful if I begin by outlining the purposes of clause 65. It introduces a minor measure to deal with an anomaly in the workings of the tax and benefits systems which can, in a small number of cases, lead to some people effectively being taxed twice on the same income.

Section 44(4) of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992 sets out the rate to be used in calculating entitlement to short-term incapacity benefit for people over pension age. At present that is based on a rate set below the standard rate of retirement pension. The clause increases the rate to one equal to the standard rate of retirement pension.

A person who is over pension age and is not in receipt of retirement pension may claim short-term incapacity benefit for up to a year of incapacity. The higher rate of short-term incapacity benefit payable in that case is based on a person's notional entitlement to retirement pension. However, under existing legislation that calculation is not based on the full standard rate of retirement pension because of an abatement of 5 per cent. introduced in 1980 in lieu of taxation. However, after 28 weeks of incapacity, incapacity benefit becomes liable to income tax. That means that some people over pension age and receiving the benefit at the abated rate could effectively be taxed twice. To compensate for that, the Benefits Agency currently pays extra-statutory payments to make up the difference--currently £2.55 a week--between the two rates of benefit.

The clause will mean that, after 28 weeks of incapactiy, the Department will use the full standard rate of retirement pension to calculate the rate of incapacity benefit for a person over pensionable age. That will ensure that those whose income level makes them liable to pay income tax will pay only once, thus removing the need for extra-statutory payments.

On the question raised by the hon. Member for Northavon (Mr. Webb), the relationship between income support and incapacity benefit is not the subject of the clause, but I should be happy to write to him about the matter.

Clause 67

Budgeting loans: criteria for making awards

Mr. Denham: I beg to move amendment No. 302, in page 43, line 39, at beginning insert

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 303, 263, 304 and 264.

Mr. Denham: This group mainly serves to clarify the provisions of the clause in relation to the budgeting loans scheme. Amendment No. 302 makes clear that new subsection (1A) will be subject to the existing subsection (2) by relocating the words

    ``Subject to subsection (2) below''

to the beginning of subsection (1A). Amendment No. 303 simply places those words elsewhere in the clause. Amendment No. 263 makes it clear that the personal circumstances of an applicant, which will be taken into account in deciding budgeting loan applications, will be specified in directions issued by the Secretary of State. Amendment No. 264 simply ties the subsequent reference within the clause.

A more substantive change will be made through amendment No. 304, ensuring that the further considerative stage will be undertaken automatically when no loan is awarded rather than, as the clause currently implies, leaving it to the discretion of the appropriate officer.

To put the amendments in context, I should explain that the clause will simplify decision making on social fund budgeting loans. Currently, all budgeting loan decisions are based wholly on the discretion of the social fund officer. That means that applicants must provide a lengthy and detailed justification of every item for which they want money and may be subjected to intensive and arguably intrusive questioning so that the social fund officer can use his discretion on whether a loan should be awarded. That is inconsistent with the purpose of a budgeting loan and leads to unclear decisions, confusion for customers, disproportionate effort by staff and unnecessary expense in administration.

The clause replaces discretion-based decisions with fact-based decisions. That will involve testing all applications against a simple set of common factual criteria related to the customer's personal circumstances. Where applications would fail on those criteria alone, a further test involving a wider set of criteria will apply.

The clause as amended will enable decision making to be automated and become quicker, and decisions to be clearer and more comprehensible.
