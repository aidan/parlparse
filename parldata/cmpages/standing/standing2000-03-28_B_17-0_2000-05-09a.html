Clause 37

Order to remove obstruction

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I beg to move amendment No. 484, in page 21, line 5, leave out `may' and insert

    `shall'.

The amendment takes a familiar form of words. It deals with a curious state of affairs. In the scenario, one has an obstruction sufficiently serious that it cannot be dealt with by an unofficial approach by the access authority—a formal process must be entered into. No compliance has been forthcoming at that stage. Two or more access notices relating to a means of access have already been served and the person has not complied. The magistrates court has then been brought in under clause 37 and has ordered that the obstruction be removed. The landowner has still not complied with the request. At the end of that long process, the net result under clause 37(2) is that the person is liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding level 3 and the access authority may remove the obstruction.

Having gone through that process, there is surely a suggestion that the access authority will remove the obstruction and will charge the offending person the cost of removal. It seems extraordinary to allow that wishy-washy ending to such a long saga of punitive measures, not reaching the conclusion that might have been expected. That is the purpose of the amendment. I hope that the Minister will accept it in the spirit in which it is intended.

Mr. Meacher: I have much sympathy with the hon. Gentleman's exasperation. I hope that I can persuade him that we are taking account of the objective that he and I share.

We envisage that access authorities will only rarely need to make use of the powers given to them in clause 37. They are instruments of last resort. Before the powers can be used, an authority must go through several stages. It must have tried to reach agreement with the landowner or occupier with regard to the means of access. Only if it is unable to do that, or if the owner is in breach of such an agreement, may it serve notice to undertake works. The hon. Gentleman has made that point. It is unlikely that the authority will need to serve a second notice within three years regarding the same means of access and still less likely that it will need to apply for an order of the magistrates court to add weight to its endeavours.

The hon. Gentleman's argument is that the objective should be secured after that rigmarole has been gone through. Even if, in those rare circumstances, an access authority found it necessary to apply for a magistrate's order and an order was granted, a person might still avoid a penalty by removing the obstruction. It would be far better if that happened. However, if a landowner were so determined to flout the law that he failed to comply with the order, it would be right for him to risk being fined up to £1,000.

The circumstances envisaged by clause 37(2)(b) will arise rarely. I agree that, where there was such an obstruction and the authority had already gone to the considerable trouble of securing a court order, it would be surprising if it did not then remove the obstruction, especially given the fact that it could recover the cost. However, there might be circumstances in which, even at that late stage, it does not make sense for the authority to remove the obstruction, because the right of access has ceased to be exercisable in the area concerned since the court made its order. That is an unlikely circumstance, but it could arise if, for example, the land became cultivated or if a direction were made under chapter II for the long-term exclusion of access.

I do not for one moment believe that, in the great majority of circumstances, events will not occur exactly as the hon. Gentleman and I expect. However, to use the word ``shall'' when it would not be appropriate in a small minority of cases is none the less wrong. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will rest assured that, if the unlikely combination of events arises, the local authority will be pretty sure to act in the manner proposed by the amendment, although it would be wrong to compel it to do so if such action would be inappropriate. For those reasons, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will be happy to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Heath: I cannot say that I am entirely happy to do so. We have both recited the long sequence of events that leads us to our conclusions. It would be an extraordinary situation if a case reached the magistrates court after such a scenario or if circumstances had changed so substantially at the point of issuing the order that access was no longer required. If that occurred, it would suggest that the access authority had not been acting entirely intelligently in pursuing the case to that point. The term ``means of access'' is defined in the Bill, so if land ceases to be access land, the term will have no meaning in relation to it. Whether the word ``may'' or ``shall'' is used, the Bill will not require the access authority to do something that it cannot physically do, as, by definition, the offending landowner will already have removed the obstruction. There would be no difficulty in either case, because the access authority cannot be required to do something that does not exist.

The difficulties are, therefore, even less likely to occur than the Minister suggests. The change from ``may'' to ``shall'' would clearly assert what will happen and would make it clear to offenders that they would not only bear the cost of a fine—it will not exceed level 3, which may or may not be a factor in considering whether to maintain intransigence—but the cost of removing of the obstruction, which may amount to considerably more than the value of such a fine. That cost would act as a further deterrent from carrying on to the bitter end. The amendment would be helpful to access authorities in such circumstances, although the implied threat is already so clear that I do not think that it would change the minds of many people. Will the Minister reflect at his leisure on those thoughts? I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 43

Redesignation of roads used as public paths

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Chris Mullin): I beg to move amendment No. 493, in page 24, line 16, leave out from first `and' to `of' in line 17 and insert

    `sections 44 to 48, ``definitive map and statement'' has the same meaning as in Part III'.

We now come to part II and enter the magical world of byways open to all traffic and roads used as public paths. My eyes tend to glaze over when I see acronyms, but I realise that in some circles they talk of little else but BOATs and RUPPs.

Clause 43 creates a new category of highway, a restricted byway. This will replace ways presently shown on local authorities' definitive maps as RUPPs. RUPPs are an inheritance from 1949 when local authorities were first required to record rights of way on definitive maps. The rights attached to RUPPs are not set out in the National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949. Since 1968, local authorities have been under a duty to re-classify each of the RUPPs as either a footpath, a bridleway or a byway open to all traffic in accordance with the existing rights. Some authorities have failed to complete that task and some 4,000 miles of RUPPs remain. Those are valuable resources for walkers, horse riders and others, but the continued uncertainty over the rights existing over each RUPP is unsatisfactory.

The Bill removes that uncertainty for most users and reduces bureaucracy by removing the duty on local authorities to research each RUPP individually. The present legislation presumes that RUPPs carry at least bridleway rights. The new restricted byways that will replace RUPPs give statutory rights to walkers, cyclists, horse riders and drivers of horse-drawn vehicles. Those rights will not have to be researched and confirmed on a case-by-case basis and will be conferred by statute. That means that it will not be possible to downgrade a restricted byway to a footpath or bridleway.

It is possible that vehicular rights already exist over some RUPPs. Where they do exist, all vehicles, including motor vehicles, have a right to use the RUPP in question unless that right is subsequently extinguished by, for example, an order made under the Highways Act 1980. The Bill itself does not extinguish such rights, but evidence of their existence will have to be produced on a case-by-case basis, before the way may be shown on the definitive map as a byway open to all traffic. Current legislation enables people to apply to a local authority for an order modifying the map.

Government amendment No. 493 ensures for the purposes of clauses 43 to 48 that the term ``definitive map and statement'' covers all the types of map and statement that may be produced under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981, including any consolidated maps that are prepared under the new provisions contained in paragraph 6 of schedule 5. These new provisions will enable local authorities to consolidate into a single map any parts of maps that they have inherited following local government boundary changes.
