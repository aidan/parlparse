Mr. Andrew Hunter (Basingstoke): I shall not detain the Committee for more than a moment or two on the amendments. I was briefly encouraged by the Minister's comments on clause 37 and I particularly note, if I understood correctly what he said, that he is prepared to consider, at least in some circumstances, a consultation role for the Police Association as well as for the Police Authority. I very much hope that the Under-Secretary will apply the same sentiments as were expressed on clause 37 when he addresses these amendments. In clause 38 we detect that further lack of consultation and a further arbitrary power being bestowed on the Secretary of State, and we do not like what we see.

To set the amendments in their context, clause 38 provides for the Secretary of State to

    ``issue, and . . . revise codes of practice relating to the discharge by the Police Authority of any of its functions.''

We see here another manifestation of the imbalance and lack of fairness in the tripartite structure of so much of the Bill, to which we have previously drawn attention. Yet again the Secretary of State is being given what amounts almost to dictatorial powers, and amendments Nos. 87 and 88 seek to redress that position.

Amendment No. 87 inserts the words ``procedures for'' into subsection (1) and amendment No. 88 adds a new subsection (1A):

    ``Before issuing any code of practice the Secretary of State shall consult the Police Authority.''

If amendment No. 87 were accepted, subsection (1) would read:

    ``The Secretary of State may issue, and from time to time revise, codes of practice relating to procedures for the discharge by the Police Authority of any of its functions.''

This morning my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire referred to amendment No. 87 as a small amendment. I am not sure that it is as small as all that. Perhaps I am reading too much into it, but I believe that it has some significance and is worthy of merit. Amendment No. 88 self-evidently requires the Secretary of State to consult the Police Authority before issuing any code of practice.

The amendments have a common theme--they would enhance the status and operational independence of the Police Authority while correspondingly restricting the power of the Secretary of State. Amendment No. 87 would mean that the Secretary of State would issue or revise codes of practice relating not to

    ``the discharge by the Police Authority of any of its functions'',

but to ``procedures for'' the discharging of those functions. That is a significant distinction. The key consideration is that it is not for the Secretary of State to dictate in detail to the Police Authority how it should discharge its functions. We argue that it is more appropriate for the Secretary of State to be at arm's length. He or she should issue or revise codes of practice rather than having the power to intervene arbitrarily and directly.

Amendment No. 88 advances the same theme and would mean the Secretary of State having to consult the Police Authority before issuing any code of practice. It can be argued that it would be intolerable for the Secretary of State not to do that and instead arbitrarily to impose on the Police Authority a code of practice without consulting it first. Given that that would be intolerable, it is surely right for the Bill to specify that such consultation should take place--hence amendment No. 88.

The amendments are intended to help ensure that the authority has appropriate status and independence, not least in its dealings with the Secretary of State. They would therefore strengthen the authority appropriately.

Mr. Worthington: Clause 38 will enable the Secretary of State to set out publicly how key aspects of the relationship between the Police Authority and the Secretary of State are to be governed. I would point out that in referring to the Secretary of State, we are talking about the Secretary of State enforcing the will of Parliament. The accountability of the police force and everything that is publicly funded must lie with Parliament. I would therefore resist the assertion that they are dictatorial or pre-emptive powers; they are powers invested in Parliament through the Secretary of State.

Clause 41

Appointment of inspectors of constabulary

Mr. Moss: I beg to move amendment No. 90, in page 21, line 6, after `State', insert `and the Police Authority'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 91, in page 21, line 12, after `State', insert

    `or the Police Authority, with the agreement of the Secretary of State.'.

Mr. Moss: Clause 41 deals with the appointment and duties of Her Majesty's inspectors of constabulary. It is replicated from section 54 of the 1996 Act. Its contents are also in section 16 of the 1970 Act. There have been cordial and constructive working relationships over a long period between the various inspectors of constabulary and the Police Authority in Northern Ireland. The Police Authority has for some time been in the habit of receiving copies of HMIC reports.

Amendment No. 90 would insert the words ``and the Police Authority'' in line 6 so that subsection (2) would read:

    ``The inspectors shall at least once in every year inspect and report to the Secretary of State and the Police Authority on the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland Police Service.''

The inspectors are required to report on those matters at least once a year. One of the primary functions of the Police Authority is to secure the efficiency and effectiveness of the police service in Northern Ireland; I refer yet again to the authority's duties under clause 2(3). It is axiomatic, therefore, that the Police Authority should be informed as soon as possible about the inspectors' findings.

The police will be held to account by the Police Authority on behalf of the community. We have discussed that several times, especially in relation to the annual policing plan. It is important, therefore, that the authority should be seen to have the same power as the Secretary of State to require the inspectors to carry out the further duties referred to in clause 41(4). Amendment No. 91 would insert, after ``Secretary of State'', the words:

    ``or the Police Authority, with the agreement of the Secretary of State''.

Subsection (4) would then read:

    ``The inspectors shall carry out such other duties for the purpose of furthering the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland Police Service and make such other reports as the Secretary of State, or the Police Authority, with the agreement of the Secretary of State, may from time to time direct.''

The amendments are helpful and reasonable. They would strengthen the position of the Police Authority, not only in substance--the argument might well be made that the Police Authority would be consulted on those matters--but because the authority's role would be defined in the Bill. The authority already receives the inspectors' reports, but the amendments would ensure that it receives them by statute.

The amendments would strengthen the authority's hand in carrying out its task of securing an effective and efficient police service. As subsection (4) requires the inspectors to report on that issue, it is logical to include the Police Authority so that it can discharge its functions properly. The authority would have the power to ask for reports from the inspectorate as and when necessary. The Secretary of State has that power; there is no reason why it should not be afforded to the authority.

Mr. Hunter: My hon. Friend has drawn attention to the context for the amendments. Clause 41 makes provision for the appointment of inspectors of constabulary from among Her Majesty's inspectors of constabulary under section 54 of the 1996 Police Act. The clause closely reflects section 16 of the Police Act (Northern Ireland) 1970.

Clause 41(2), to which amendment No. 90 refers, requires the inspectors at least once a year to inspect and report to the Secretary of State on the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland police service. It is for the Police Authority to secure the efficiency and effectiveness of the police service.

Amendment No. 90 reasonably requires the inspectors to report not only to the Secretary of State but to the authority. The strength and merit of that are self-evident. The inspectors' annual reports into the effectiveness and efficiency of the police service will provide important and significant help in the achieving of those goals. It is right and proper that the reports be submitted to the Police Authority because it will want to know the inspectors' advice and judgment in order to do its work more efficiently. The arguments can be reversed. It would be hard to think of a reason not to submit reports to it--indeed, failure to do so would detract from and negate the authority's standing. Nor is there a compelling reason for reports to be sent to the Secretary of State alone.

Amendment No. 91 follows logically from amendment No. 90, and the arguments for it are similar. Subsection (4) enables the Secretary of State to direct the inspectors to carry out further duties for the purpose of improving the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland police service. The amendment enables the Police Authority also so to direct the inspectors. Again the reason is self evident; as the authority is responsible for ensuring efficiency and effectiveness, it is only reasonable that it should have the statutory power to direct the inspectors to undertake further duties, to assist it in securing those aims.

The amendment would not bestow an absolute power because it is rigidly qualified. The power could be exercised only with the agreement of the Secretary of State. That is an important qualification that acknowledges the Secretary of State's primacy in the tripartite structure. In the unlikely event of the authority wishing to exercise its power frivolously or irresponsibly, the Secretary of State effectively has the power of veto. Nevertheless, sensibly used with the Secretary of State's agreement, the power would enable the authority better to fulfil its responsbility for ensuring efficiency and effectiveness. After all, that is the authority's raison d'etre and one of the Bill's purposes.

Mr. Worthington: Having established our qualifications as reasonable people earlier by being so reasonable about consultations, I am afraid that I cannot agree to the amendment.

I go back to the analogy of the tripartite structure and the three-legged stool that we are erecting. Her Majesty's inspectorate of constabulary is part of the leg that includes the Government and the Secretary of State. To accept the amendment would have implications not just in Northern Ireland but in the rest of the United Kingdom, because it would change the nature of Her Majesty's inspectorate of constabulary.

When the inspectorate conducts an inquiry into the efficiency and effectiveness of the RUC, it also assesses whether the Police Authority has carried out its general duty. The Police Authority is part of that inspection and part of what is being scrutinised. To make the Police Authority part of the body commissioning the inspection changes the relationships. It would be inappropriate for the inspectorate to report to the Police Authority.

The inspectorate acts on behalf of the Secretary of State, who bears the full cost of an inspection of the RUC. The Secretary of State determines what an inspection should focus on, apart from the general inquiry, and meets the resulting costs. The Police Authority cannot be party to the final decision on how an inspection is to be conducted. There is a great danger in muddling the functions of each leg of the inspection. The Bill tries to clarify the respective roles.

The matter is clear-cut: on behalf of Parliament and of the people, Her Majesty's inspectorate of constabulary is part of the important existing accountability mechanism of the police and we should not muddy the waters by saying that one of the bodies that is inspected can commission that inspection. There is an analogy with school inspections. It would not be acceptable for the governors of a school to commission an outside inspection that would look at the configuration of relationships within the school, and as the RUC and the Police Authority are part of the inspection, the waters should not be muddied as the hon. Member for Basingstoke suggests.

Clause 44

Inquiries

5.15 pm

Mr. Moss: I beg to move amendment No. 92, in page 22, line 13, at end insert--

    `(2A) Before causing an inquiry to be held the Secretary of State shall consult the Police Authority unless to do so would be against the interests of national security or might jeopardise the safety of any person.'.

The Chairman: With this we may take amendment No. 93, in clause 45, page 22, line 22, after `State', insert

    `after consultation with the Police Authority'.

Mr. Moss: Amendment No. 90 applies to clause 44 whereas amendment No. 91 applies to the following clause.

Mr. Hunter: Those are the wrong numbers.

Mr. Moss: I got the numbers wrong, but the gist of what I said was correct because the lead amendment, No. 92, applies to clause 44 and the subsidiary one, amendment No. 93, applies to clause 45. They deal with a similar point relating to consultation between the Secretary of State and the Police Authority.

Clause 44 deals with the Secretary of State's power to set up inquiries under article 83 of the Police and Criminal Evidence (Northern Ireland) Order 1989. Similar provision for England and Wales is made under section 49 of the 1996 Act. However, although that section is explicit in providing that the costs should be borne by one or more police funds, the Bill makes no reference to the financing of inquiries.

I hope that the Minister, with adequate prompting from his officials, will provide me with further information about that problem. It could be covered by a reference in clause 44(3) to

    ``Schedule 8 to the Health and Personal Social Services (Northern Ireland) Order 1972''.

Perhaps the information about who pays for the inquiries is to be found there.

I have not been able, during my research, to discover whether any such inquiry has ever been held in Northern Ireland. Perhaps the Minister will confirm the facts of that. That should not of course mean that the Bill should not provide the Secretary of State with the wherewithal to call such inquiries. No doubt any such inquiry would attract considerable media and public interest. The Police Authority would be at the heart of that, as the body responsible for securing an efficient and effective Northern Ireland police service. Any hint or suggestion of inefficiency or ineffectiveness, or wrongdoing of any kind, would come firmly to rest at the Police Authority's front door. It would have no hiding place. Clause 2 sets out its task of securing effective and efficient policing for Northern Ireland. Any other outcome would be treated as its responsibility.

In view of what I have outlined, it is inconceivable that the Police Authority should not be consulted about a proposed inquiry. I can envisage its intimate knowledge of policing matters enabling it to carry out an instigatory role. We recently discussed the idea that, in the context of the regular contact between the Secretary of State, the Chief Constable and the Police Authority, the latter might instigate reports and inquiries, particularly by the inspectorate. My argument now is the same; that because of its knowledge of policing matters the Police Authority could draw to the Secretary of State's attention circumstances that would lead inexorably to an inquiry.

Amendment No. 92 would add a new subsection to the clause, requiring the Secretary of State to consult the Police Authority before setting up an inquiry. That would assist the Police Authority in maintaining its public credibility, and in discharging the important functions given to it under clause 2. To reverse the argument, it is difficult to see how the Police Authority could discharge those functions if it were excluded from discussions about setting up an inquiry. It might be positively hindered in its duties if that were so.

Amendment 93 would insert into clause 45 a reference to consultation, so that subsection (1) would read:

    ``The Secretary of State, after consultation with the Police Authority, may set up such bodies and take such other steps as appear to him to be necessary or expedient for the purpose of undertaking research into matters affecting the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland Police Service.''

Once again, we want the Secretary of State to consult the Police Authority--in this case on research into matters of efficiency and effectiveness. There are no similar provisions in the 1970 Act, which is the current legislation. The clause as it stands is probably based on sections 57 and 58 of the Police Act 1996--indeed, I think that clause 45(1) is identical to section 58 of that Act.

There is no direct link between clause 45(2) of the Bill and the legislation for England and Wales. Subsection (2) allows the Secretary of State to set up bodies to give advice. Section 57 of the 1996 Act refers to ``common services'' and subsection (1) states:

    ``The Secretary of State may provide and maintain, or may contribute to the provision or maintenance of, such organisations, facilities and services as he considers necessary or expedient for promoting the efficiency or effectiveness of the police.''

Under the same section, powers are given to the Secretary of State

    ``requiring all police forces in England and Wales to use specified facilities or services''.

The Secretary of State can direct police authorities in England and Wales to take up such services, but before making regulations requiring police forces to use those facilities and services, the Secretary of State must consult police authorities and chief constables. Section 57(4) states:

    ``Before making regulations under this section, the Secretary of State shall consult . . . persons whom he considers to represent the interests of police authorities, and . . . persons whom he considers to represent the interests of chief officers of police.''

Therefore, when dealing with matters such as research, advice, and special services or facilities that may or may not be provided, the Secretary of State must ensure that proper consultation takes place with the Association of Police Authorities and the Association of Chief Police Officers.

Amendment No. 92 refers to the inquiries that may or may not be set up under clause 44, and amendment No. 93 refers to consultation on the research and advice provisions in clause 45. The logic behind the amendments starts with the premise set out in clause 2(3), which states:

    ``The Police Authority shall secure that the Northern Ireland Police Service is efficient and effective.''

It seems to me, therefore, that the Police Authority has a direct interest in proposals for establishing bodies to undertake research into matters affecting that service's efficiency and effectiveness.

It is arguable that the duty placed on the Police Authoriity in Northern Ireland is more specific than that placed on police authorities in England and Wales. As the Committee has noted frequently, the working relationship between the Secretary of State, the Police Authority and the Chief Constable--the so-called tripartite structure--is much closer in Northern Ireland because of the scale and because of the way in which the system has been set up. That is not a matter of contention; it has been used in argument by hon. Members on both sides of the Committee. I recognise not only that that is the reality, but that it is a force for good.

The Bill prescribes that the Secretary of State shall, without actually talking to the Police Authority, set up bodies to research into the very matters that are the authority's prerogative, duty and responsibility. That seems to be at best an oversight; at worst, it is yet another slight to the authority's standing.

These two simple amendments deal with a similar issue: consultation. The first refers to inquiries that the Secretary of State may set up, while the other relates to clause 45, under which the Secretary of State may set up research bodies. If the Bill is enacted as published, it is my opinion, as well as the Police Authority's opinion, that to circumscribe the authority's functions in that way diminishes its status and credibility.

On page 44 of the previous Government's White Paper, ``Foundations for Policing'', under the heading of other responsibilities, we read that it was envisaged that the Police Authority for Northern Ireland would have responsibility for,

    ``in consultation with the Secretary of State and Chief Constable, sponsoring and publishing research into policing issues.''

As is clear from that White Paper, until May last year the previous Government intended to introduce legislation--had we had the good fortune to be returned--that would have had built in to it that link in research matters and the setting up of research bodies jointly between the Secretary of State, the Police Authority and, as the White Paper says, the Chief Constable.

The authorship of much of the Bill originated with the previous Government. Officials, some of whom may be here today, worked extremely hard on the White Paper and have worked equally hard in drafting the Bill. The advice that officials gave before last May was that the area of research should be shared between the various components of the tripartite structure. However, it does not appear that that has been carried across into clause 45. I am curious as to why it has changed--have circumstances altered in the intervening months and encouraged the Government to reword the provision? I am confident that the Minister will tell us why the changes have been deemed necessary, although I for one cannot think of a reason why.

The Police Authority should be included in and be very much part of research, particularly research into efficiency and effectiveness. In the end, even if we simply live with clause 45, the Secretary of State will seek its advice in order to research the right areas and it will tell her that it needs more help on this or that. That communication will no doubt take place. My amendments puts it in the Bill.

As my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire said, clause 44 provides for the Secretary of State to instigate an inquiry into any matter connected with policing and to appoint a person to conduct that inquiry. It also provides for the Secretary of State to make known a summary of findings and conclusions where a full report is not published. Amendment No. 92 requires the Secretary of State, before causing an inquiry to be held, to consult the Police Authority, unless to do so would be inconsistent with the interests of national security or might jeopardise the safety of any person.

Clause 45 allows the Secretary of State to establish bodies to undertake research and provide advice on matters

    ``affecting the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland Police Service.''

Amendment No. 93 requires the Secretary of State to consult the Police Authority before establishing such bodies.

The arguments for the amendments have been well rehearsed, not only just now by my hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire but because we have used essentially the same ones on previous amendments. Again we are trying to include in the Bill a statutory requirement for the Secretary of State to consult, because we believe that without it the Police Authority is in danger of becoming the poor relation in the tripartite structure.

Amendment No. 92 therefore requires the Secretary of State to consult the Police Authority if she has it in mind to cause an inquiry to be held into any matter connected with policing. That is not a meaningless requirement; it is highly likely that the Police Authority would have an authoritative opinion to offer. The Secretary of State should be aware of that opinion and, most likely, would find it helpful. She would probably find consultation with the Police Authority beneficial in deciding whether to proceed with an inquiry. This role for the Police Authority would also, as my hon. Friend said, help to maintain its credibility in the eyes of the public.

Amendment No. 92 does not impose an absolute requirement on the Secretary of State. There are two qualifications. The Secretary of State will not be obliged to consult the Police Authority about holding an inquiry if consultation might be

    ``against the interests of national security or might jeopardise the safety of any person.''

The Secretary of State alone would determine whether the subject of the inquiry fell into one or both of those categories. As it is an Executive matter, it is right that the Secretary of State should make the decision, no doubt with the advice of her security advisers, in practice.

The same line of reasoning that lies behind amendment No. 92 lies behind amendment No. 93. Whereas clause 44 deals with inquiries into

    ``any matter connected with policing'',

clause 45 and amendment No. 93 relate to what may appear to the Secretary of State to be

    ``necessary or expedient for the purpose of undertaking research''

with the aim promoting the efficiency and effectiveness of the police service. With regard to this matter also, it is logical that the Secretary of State should act only after consulting the Police Authority.

The Opposition's supporting argument for amendment No. 93 is essentially the same as that which we used for amendments Nos. 90 and 91. There is no need to repeat it in detail. It is sufficient to say that securing the efficiency and effectiveness of the Northern Ireland police service is the responsibility of the Police Authority. It is self-evidently appropriate that the Police Authority should be consulted about research and advice that has those objectives.

We go back to the sort of argument that I deployed in relation to a previous clause about Her Majesty's inspectorate of constabulary. It would be wrong if the subject of an inquiry were its object, and if the body concerned was involved in a formal consultation before a decision was made.
