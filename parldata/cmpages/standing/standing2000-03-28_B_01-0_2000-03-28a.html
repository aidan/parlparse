We all know that walking—it seems sensible to deal with part I first—is Britain's most popular leisure activity. The Countryside Agency found that 1.3 billion visits were made to the English countryside in 1996. For many decades, there has been discussion about whether a voluntary or statutory approach is the most appropriate way of improving access to the countryside. The Government have decided that the statutory approach is best. We can argue with that decision, but the time for such argument is past. We accept that the Bill has received its Second Reading and that the provisions must now be made as appropriate as possible for the country's needs, for people who walk in the countryside and enjoy it and for people who want to preserve and enhance our wildlife heritage.

As ever, it is instructive to read the excellent Library background notes to the Bill and to consider the international comparisons to which they refer. It stands out starkly that access to the countryside is preserved in different ways in each of the European countries. Each country has a different approach as each has its own traditions, but more basic factors also apply, as each country has its own land mass and open space. Therefore, an approach that is appropriate for Germany will not be appropriate for the Netherlands. We must learn a lesson from that and use the Committee to develop a system that is completely appropriate for Britain.

The Minister will know that many issues will arise during discussion of the first couple of clauses and schedules, which are at the heart of the access provisions and which deserve detailed analysis. For instance, power to extend the right of access to coastal land by order is slipped into the provisions. As the Minister knows, many people are worried about putting such power into ministerial hands. The provisions also give the Countryside Agency the job of mapping all registered common land and open country. That is a major task, whose practicalities also deserve detailed exploration.

Other important issues will be considered in relation to enforcement and protection on access land. The Bill allows district councils to appoint wardens, but contains no detailed or specific provisions about the cost and practicalities involved. It allows landowners to exclude or restrict access for any reason for up to 28 days a year without permission. The Minister will be aware that that period is already the subject of energetic and lively debate. He said that landowners or tenants can seek further exclusion for land management reasons, but the Bill goes no further than that and the Committee should explore what is meant by the land management reasons to which it refers. The Countryside Agency or the Countryside Council for Wales will be able to approve the exclusions and restrictions; similarly, it is worth exploring how permanent such measures can or should be. The Minister will know about the debate on whether provisions for restrictions on dogs on access land will be adequate for the protection of farm livestock and of nesting birds and other wildlife.

The Countryside Council for Wales makes an important point, which we will need to explore in Committee, about whether the Bill's aspirations run ahead of its practical proposals. Its particular concern is the amount of money made available to implement the Bill's provisions. The Council welcomes the Bill in principle, but it has been allocated only £100,000 to take forward its proposals having bid for £2 million. It says that it will be able to take forward the work in only one or two demonstration areas. I arm sure that the Minister will agree that, if that applies in England and the resources are not made available either to the nationwide countryside agencies or to local authorities to implement the Bill properly, it will be a disappointment, particularly to its strongest supporters.

There are other areas of concern in part 1, not least the problem of level playing fields; some who offend against the Bill will be criminalised, while others will not. It will be interesting to explore the thinking behind that part of the Bill. There is also deep concern over liability for injury caused by natural or non-natural features of the landscape. Only natural features are covered. All will be aware that features ranging from iron age forts to simple barbed wire can cause injury or accident. Landowners reasonably feel that such features should be included in the same provisions as natural features. Drystone walls are perhaps the starkest example. The Countryside Agency estimated that in 1994 25 per cent. of drytone walls were derelict and, given the nature of drystone walls, they could be dangerous. If they are not included because they are not natural features, there will be some unfairness in the allocation of liability for any injury caused by them.

Mr. Paice: We now come to the meat of the Bill. All the amendments we have tabled are designed to improve the Bill or to probe the Government's intentions. Amendments Nos 2 and 1 go together because amendment No. 2 removes a significant part of the start of clause 1, amendment No. 2 replaces it and amendment No. 140 is an alternative form of words. The Bill needs to be clear. Clarity for everybody affected by this legislation is essential if they are to understand their relative rights and responsibilities. Each of the parties will then be able to plan their activities accordingly. The purpose of the amendments is to ensure that the mapping exercise is comprehensive. At present, the Bill does not provide for maps showing all the access land in detail. Clause 4(4) says that the map

    need not distinguish between different categories of open country.

That shows that the Bill does not provide for the comprehensive mapping that is necessary if everybody is to understand the situation in any one area. The mapping exercise covers registered common land and open country, it does not cover excepted land as defined in schedule 1, part 1. That may be surrounded by open country or common land, but is not as we understand to be included in the mapping exercise.

The Bill should provide for a full mapping of the access land, which would give walkers, farmers, landowners and public bodies certainty as to the situation in their location. Walkers would know precisely where they can and cannot go. The curtilage of buildings—an issue to which we will probably return later—would be clearly laid out on a map. Farmers and landowners would know what land was subject to access, reducing the chance of mistakes being made and of disputes about notices, restrictions, obstructions and other issues that are covered later in the Bill. That would also reduce the prospect of appeals relating to the mapping of open country. At present, the maps will not exclude excepted land, so farmers or landowners who are concerned about the proposals can only dispute the open country designation, whereas if the mapping exercise were more comprehensive, it might be possible to assuage their concerns, or at least to address in more detail the specific points that worry them.
