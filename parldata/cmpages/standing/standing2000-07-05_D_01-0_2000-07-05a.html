Mr. Jonathan Sayeed (Mid-Bedfordshire): I beg to move

    That, during proceedings on the Census (Amendment) Bill [Lords], the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock.

I am delighted to serve under your chairmanship, Mr. Benton. I know that you will take it in exactly the right way if I say that I hope that your duties as Chairman of the Committee are not too time-consuming.

There are four tests that any piece of legislation must pass. First, it must be necessary or useful. Secondly, it must be proportionate-in other words, it does not use a sledgehammer to crack a nut, and new laws are required for the task in hand. Thirdly, it must be drafted to achieve the purpose for which it is intended. Fourthly, it must be understandable not only by lawyers, but by ordinary mortals, such as most of us.

The purpose of the Bill is simple and clear. It seeks to change the Census Act 1920 so that a voluntary question on religion may be included in a census of population in England and Wales and, subject to the necessary secondary legislation, so that such a question may be included in the next census in April 2001. In so doing, the Bill will bring census legislation in England and Wales in line with that of Scotland-where similar legislation was passed by the Scottish Parliament in March this year-and Northern Ireland.

Does the Bill pass the four tests? First, is it necessary and useful? These days, many groups of people prefer to identify themselves primarily in terms of their culture or religion. If we are to deal with discrimination in the provision of health, welfare and community care services, we require better information, including better baseline figures against which racial disadvantage and social exclusion within particular minority groups can be monitored. The 2001 census offers a once-in-a-decade opportunity to collect information from such groups. One need not only accept my view that the Bill is necessary or useful. It was passed without division in the other place, and it was overwhelmingly supported in our own Chamber at Second Reading.

Secondly, is it proportionate? It cannot be a draconian measure because compliance is voluntary. New legislation is necessary, partly because there are no reliable figures for ethnic sub-groups or religions. Thirdly, is it drafted in such a way that it will achieve its purpose? That is undoubtedly so-it is clearly and simply laid out, and even I, as a non-lawyer, can understand it. Fourthly, is it understandable by normal people? The census rehearsal last year clearly proved that the proposed question is understandable. The Bill thus passes all four tests.

My hon. Friends the Members for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway) and for Chichester (Mr. Tyrie) have tabled amendments and a new clause. Although I believe that the amendments are unnecessary-I will seek to explain why at a later stage-I am grateful that my hon. Friends framed them so as to demonstrate that they accept that the introduction of a question on religion is of value in the census. The only point at issue is how best to reflect the voluntary nature of the question.

I would like to thank the Minister and the Government for finding the parliamentary time to consider this modest measure. Without that help, the Bill would have hit the buffers of time and been derailed. I commend the sittings motion.

Clause 1

Particulars in respect of religion may be required

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss the following: new clause 1-Particulars in respect of religion may be requested-

    `.-After section 1 of the Census Act 1920 (power to direct taking of census) there shall be inserted the following new section-

    ``Power to include question on religion in census1A.-(1) The Secretary of State may by order made by statutory instrument make provision for the inclusion in a census held in England and Wales of a question on religion.

    (2) No person shall be required to state particulars requested in a question on religion provided for in an order under this section and a person who refuses or neglects to state such particulars shall not by virtue of that act or omission be guilty of an offence.

    (3) An order under this section shall include provision requiring a statement of the voluntary nature of responses to a question on religion to be included in a prominent position on the form which contains the question.

    (4) An order under this section shall be laid before Parliament in draft and shall not be made unless it has been approved by resolution of both Houses.''.'

Amendment No. 3, in Title, line 1, leave out ``required'' and insert ``requested''.

Mr. Sayeed: Clause 1 would add religion to the specific matters in respect of which particulars may be required to be provided in a census. Subsection (1) provides for religion to be added as a specific matter at paragraph 5A of the schedule to the Census Act 1920.

It is necessary to amend the Census Act 1920 because the schedule, which specifies the matters in respect of which particulars may be required to be stated in a census return, does not provide for particulars on religion. Most of the traditional census questions are covered by paragraphs 1 to 5 of the schedule. Other questions, which may be asked from time to time, are covered by paragraph 6, which identifies

    any other matters with respect to which it is desirable to obtain statistical information with a view to ascertaining the social or civil condition of the population.

Legal advice over the years has been that religion is not a matter of civil or social condition, so a change to the Act is necessary.

Subsection (2) removes the liability to a penalty under section 8(1) of the Census Act 1920 of anyone who refuses or neglects to state in the census return particulars in respect of religion. That would make answering such a question on religion voluntary. Although the statutory requirement under the Census Act 1920 to answer all other census questions remains, the voluntary status of the proposed question on religion reflects the particular sensitivity that surrounds the topic.

The value of including a question on religion is that when the information is cross-indexed with the information received in response to the ethnic group question, we will be better able to identify areas of racial disadvantage and social exclusion, monitor discrimination, support resource allocation and facilitate the provision of services, particularly those relating to health, education and welfare.

There are tensions in all multicultural, multi-faith societies. Disadvantage and discrimination fuel such tensions. Nowadays, many more people seek to identify themselves in terms of their religion or culture. That is the case especially among non-Christian minority groups. It is such groups that are vulnerable to disadvantage and discrimination. It is in our enlightened self-interest to lessen the disadvantage and discrimination and diffuse those tensions. Knowing the facts will allow us to do that better.

The purpose of the amendment is to establish a statutory basis for the Economic Secretary's description of her own legislation. During a debate in the early hours of the morning, she said:

    The Bill emphasises that answering the question is voluntary. The form will also make it clear that it is a voluntary question.-[Official Report, 20 June 2000; Vol. 352, c. 305.]

Although it is true that, in the light of the Lords amendment, the Bill will make it clear that the penalty for non-compliance has been waived, as it stands there is nothing in the Bill to suggest that answering the question is voluntary. The Economic Secretary has assured us that the public will not be required to answer the question, but there is no statutory protection to ensure that they will be made aware of the fact.

 

Mr. John Bercow (Buckingham): I note the point that my hon. Friend has made, but can we also be assured that the names of those who decline to answer the question will not be centrally stored and available for potential use for other purposes?

Mr. Tyrie: That is a question for the Economic Secretary, and I have no idea what the answer is. It may well be important, so perhaps she will answer it in due course.

If anyone doubts the Bill's position on this matter, they should read the long title, which makes it clear that this is a Bill to

    Amend the Census Act 1920 to enable particulars to be required in respect of religion.

Amendment No. 3, which would replace the word ``required'' with the word ``requested'', would sort out that problem. It cannot be said that there has been a simple oversight. In respect of clause 1(1), the explanatory notes, which are equally clear, state that religion is added

    to those topics that are specified in the Schedule to the Census Act as being matters in respect of which particulars may be required to be given in the Census.

The explanatory notes also state that subsection (2) will remove the penalty for failing to answer the question, but not the requirement to answer it.

That leaves us in a curious position. The best construction that one can put on the Lords amendment, which is something of a pig's ear, is that the introduction of a statutory provision and the simultaneous removal of a penalty is a legal oddity. As I have said in the House, I know of no other similar legislation. The worst construction of it is that the concession is meaningless. Unless those who will be required to fill in the form are absolutely clear on the matter, they will treat this question like all the others, and it will not be voluntary at all.

Subsection (2) of new clause 1, which would deal with the problem by making it clear that the question is voluntary, states:

    No person shall be required to state particulars requested in a question on religion and a person who refuses or neglects to state such particulars shall not by virtue of that act or omission be guilty of an offence.

That seems to provide exactly the protection that, according to the Economic Secretary, the Bill was intended to provide.

The second problem to which I referred-that people should know about the voluntary nature of a response to the question-is covered by proposed new subsection (3). That would make it clear that the Government had a duty to make a reasonable effort to let people know that responding to the question was voluntary. Proposed new subsection (4) would merely put the new clause into line with the treatment of censuses laid down in the 1920 Act, which states that censuses should be subject to affirmative resolutions of both Houses. Proposed new subsections (2) and (3) are the crucial parts of the new clause.

I worked carefully with the Public Bill Office to ensure that the new clause conformed exactly to the Minister's-and the Government's-stated intentions that she set out at Second Reading. If any hon. Members have any doubts about that, they should look closely at the words that the Minister used. She said:

    The Bill emphasises that answering the question is voluntary. The form will also make it clear that it is a voluntary question.-[Official Report, 20 June 2000; Vol. 352, c. 305.]

Those are clear statements, but we have only her assurance. All that I ask is that those statements be given statutory backing.

What possible objection could the Government have to incorporating the new clause in the Bill? Why did not they include such a provision from the beginning? I have no idea why they did not amend the Bill in this way in the House of Lords. However, they did not, and I have been wracking my brains to understand why the Government have not already said that they will accept new clause 1, so that we can all get out of the Committee before lunch.

I have a few tentative suggestions as to the reason why. One is that the Bill would have to go back to the House of Lords. That would be a bit of a hassle, but there is still plenty of time to prepare a census for next spring. If that is not true, why on earth did not the Government ensure that the Bill was introduced earlier? Why on earth do we suddenly find ourselves on some critical path timetable for the preparation of the census? If the Bill had been introduced a month earlier, the Government would have had the chance to table a more intelligent amendment. We should not be prepared to put such blatantly deficient legislation-which does not even achieve the Minister's stated objective-on to the statute book, just because the Government have failed to find a few hours of parliamentary time for the Bill over the past three years.

A second possible objection that the Government might have to incorporating my proposal-which would enact exactly what the Minister said was their intention-is that it would damage the Government's original purpose in deciding to include the religious question in the Bill. Lord McIntosh, speaking in the House of Lords, said that a purely voluntary question would not be worth having. He said that making a question voluntary would affect the response that would be obtained from the question. In other words, some religious people might answer it, but others who were not religious-or who were feeling less religious-might not bother. Therefore, if a question was voluntary, the answers received would be virtually valueless.

Lord McIntosh could be right-I do not know. If that is the case, perhaps the issue would be better addressed through the use of surveys. However, he went much further than that in stating his concern about the voluntary question. He said that a voluntary question could damage the rest of the information collected in the census. That could prejudice the census's value. He said:

    It has been shown that making a question voluntary seriously affects the response not only to the question itself, where response bias could devalue the information obtained-

that is the point that I have just made-

    but also as regards other questions, because people are confused about some questions being voluntary and others being compulsory-[Official Report, House of Lords, 27 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1717.]

In other words, if the question were truly voluntary and the public knew that it was, it would be devalued to such an extent that it would not be worth asking. Lord McIntosh may be right about that-I do not know.

My concern is that that thinking may lie behind the Government's apparent reluctance to make clear in the Bill the question's voluntary nature. The Bill should clearly state that people will not have to pay a penalty for non-compliance and that they will not commit an offence by failing to answer the question. The Minister could sort out those problems quickly simply by saying, ``I shall ensure that on Report the assurances that I gave in the House on 20 June will be incorporated in the Bill. I shall either agree to the new clause or introduce a new amendment that would have a similar effect.''

My main point is that the Bill does not offer appropriate protection-it will remain an offence to fail to answer the question-or make it clear to people who fill in the census the question's quasi-voluntary nature. We have to rely entirely on an assertion to that effect by a Minister; its relevance may come or go. The Minister gave that commitment in Committee and we, as parliamentarians, are aware of it, but the public may be completely ignorant of it.

I hope that the Minister will respond to the concerns that I raised and that she will agree to the new clause. If she cannot, I hope that she will produce an amendment on Report that will have a similar effect-we should be happy to support it.

Mr. Sayeed: Subsection (1) sets out the short title of the Bill. Subsection (2) limits the extent of the Bill to England and Wales.

The amendment to the Census Act would align the position in England and Wales with that in Scotland, where the Parliament recently passed similar legislation, and Northern Ireland, where legislation already provides for the collection of particulars on religion on a voluntary basis, similar to that proposed.
