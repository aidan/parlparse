Mr. David Rendel (Newbury): I beg to move amendment No. 127, in page 6, leave out lines 4 to 6 and insert--

    `(6)(a) For the avoidance of doubt, the fact that a claimant has appealed a decision under section 9 above or section 11 below shall not prevent the Secretary of State revising the decision under subsection (1) above before the appeal is determined.

    (b) Where the decision is revised under paragraph (a) and the decision as revised is the same as the decision that would have been made on the appeal had it been fully successful, the appeal shall lapse.

    (c) Where a decision is revised under paragraph (a) but paragraph (b) does not apply, the appeal shall proceed as though it were an appeal against the decision as revised, unless the appellant requests that the appeal lapse.'.

The amendment seeks to discover what the Government mean by prescribed circumstances in clause 10(6). There may be unfortunate circumstances in which an appeal should not lapse, and I want to ensure that they are covered by the Government intentions. A decision could be revised under the clause, but not necessarily in the way that the appellant had hoped. A partial revision of a decision would be possible. For example, claimants might appeal for a sum much larger than was originally granted, only to find that the revised sum was half the amount for which they had appealed. It is important, therefore, that in those circumstances the appeal does not necessarily lapse--and it will not if there is a clear definition of the prescribed circumstances.

I suggest that once an appeal has been lodged and clearly argued, at least on paper, it makes sense--if for no other reason than the sheer amount of bureaucracy involved--to allow that appeal to stand unless the revision meets the appeal requirements. I hope that that would speed up the process and ensure that an appeal did not lapse when the appellant wanted to proceed simply because the appeal had not been fully met by the revision.

It is probably fair to say that a number of revisions will meet the full requirements of the appeal. An appeal might be lodged because there is an obvious fault in the decision, which is corrected by the revision. Nevertheless, there will be other cases in which that does not happen, and it would be sensible for the appeal to go ahead.

Suggested new paragraph (b) in the amendment has been worded so that the appeal would lapse if the revision fully met its requirements. Under new paragraph (c) the appeal would lapse if the appellant felt that the revision was sufficient to make it unnecessary to go forward to the full appeal.

The amendment would give claimants a more sensible option for retaining their appeal when they wanted a greater revision than had been granted and, at the same time, would cut down on the bureaucracy of having to start a new appeal.

Mr. Webb: I beg to move amendment No. 136, in page 12, leave out lines 10 to 12 and insert--

The clause relates to the finality of decisions. Clearly, in some cases it makes sense for a decision to be treated as final for the purposes of considering other benefits. I recently visited the Stockport welfare rights unit, where people told me of the bizarre possibility of a claimant's signing on sick and being told that he is not sick enough and should claim jobseeker's allowance, yet when he turns up at the jobseeker's allowance department he is told that he is too unwell to work and should claim sickness benefit. He is entitled to neither benefit, because one office is using one set of rules and principles and the other is using another. The idea that a decision should be made once and then applied in all cases seems perfectly sensible.

For that reason, proposed new subsection (3) uses incapacity for work as an example of a case in which we approve of the principle of finality. However, there may be other instances in which a decision, once made, should be revisited without necessarily being regarded as final. For example, someone who is denied income support because he or she is deemed not to be habitually resident, and who makes a subsequent claim, may receive the immediate answer, ``No, because we have already said that you are not habitually resident; our decision was final then and remains so.'' The amendment seeks to probe whether finality would benefit claimants in some but not all cases while allowing for other issues to be reconsidered if they are central to a claim. That is why we want to amend the clause.
