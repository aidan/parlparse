Mr. Brian Cotter (Weston-super-Mare): I beg to move amendment No. 90, in page 4, line 30, at end insert--

The Chairman: With this we may discuss new schedule 1--The Consumer Panel--

    'Functions

    1. The functions of the Consumer Panel are to include--

    (a) advising the Authority on the appropriate degree of protection for consumers,

    (b) considering how to make the financial system and markets more accessible, fair and efficient in the interests of consumers,

    (c) advising the Authority on the development of the financial system and markets,

    (d) referring proposals for changes in the financial system and markets to the Authority, and

    (e) carrying out research.

    2. The Consumer Panel may publish a copy of any research carried out by it under paragraph 1 in such a manner as it thinks fit.

    Annual Reports

    3. (1) The Consumer Panel shall make an annual report to the Authority on the discharge of its functions during the year to which the report relates.

    (2) The Consumer Panel shall provide the Authority with such information relating to the discharge of its functions as the Authority sees fit.

    (3) The Authority must publish a copy of any annual report made to it under sub-paragraph (1) in such a manner as it thinks fit.

    Budget

    4. The Authority may, to such extent as may be approved by the Treasury, defray or contribute towards the expenses of the Consumer Panel.'.

Mr. Cotter: We welcome the fact that the consumer panel will be put on a statutory basis under the Bill. The amendment would add a new schedule to place a duty on the consumer panel on a clearer statutory basis than is already allowed for, and would ensure that if fulfils certain minimum functions.

The amendment is intended not to be exhaustive, but to establish a reasonable remit for the consumer panel to follow. Ultimately, one of the Financial Services Authority's most important functions is the protection of consumers. I strongly consider myself to be a consumer, and perhaps it is good that I am present today to make that point. Unlike my hon. Friends the Members for Twickenham (Dr. Cable) and for South-East Cornwall (Mr. Breed), who are very experienced in the field--I see some blushes--I struggle considerably with matters such as pensions.

Under the Bill as drafted, there is a real danger that the consumer panel could easily be sidelined, which we want to avoid. It could become a mere appendage rather than an important element of the authority. Clause 9 and its related schedule would allow for mechanisms to ensure that the panel has the credibility that it needs to operate effectively.

The consumer panel should have the statutory right to carry out its own research. New schedule 1 would provide that. Otherwise, the consumer panel may be dependent on the FSA for the flow of information on which it must deliberate. There has been much debate throughout this parliamentary Session about the importance of clear access to information.

I do not propose to speak at great length to the new schedule, because our intentions are clear. I hope that the Financial Secretary will agree that the consumer panel's inability to commission its own research would seriously compromise its effectiveness, and I look to her to assure the Committee that that will be within its powers.

The need for openness is paramount. If the FSA is able to keep the consumer panel's advice to itself, how will we know the extent to which the FSA acts on that advice? If we cannot be satisfied that the consumer panel will wield some clout in respect of the authority's thinking, it may as well not exist. Accordingly, the new schedule would enable the panel to publish an annual report, which would describe exactly what it had been doing throughout the year, and which could be viewed in conjunction with the authority's own action. Such a procedure would reassure us that the FSA was taking proper note of the consumer panel's advice, and in turn we would know that the consumer's interests were being properly represented.

The amendment is clearly a probing one which is intended to flag up important points arising from the sparse details in clause 9. Therefore, I should be grateful if the Financial Secretary would expand on some of the matters that I have raised.

Mr. Flight: I beg to move amendment No. 91, in page 5, line 19, at end insert--

The amendment is designed to provide straightforward and established protection to those whom the person conducting the review calls to give evidence. Sir Nicholas Lyell: I support the amendment. It will assist the reviewer. It is obviously desirable that the reviewer should have available to him all the documents provided to the authority. Some of those documents may have been provided with an element of restriction. For example, legal professional privilege could have been claimed for the documents, but their owner was happy for the authority to see them, provided that it was for that purpose only and that it was made clear that they would not be shown to others.

The amendment would make it possible to ensure that the rules that apply to ordinary civil proceedings could apply to the sort of review that is contemplated by the Government. The adding of those words will not prevent someone who has privilege for their documents from waiving that privilege in a limited form, or from waiving it completely. However, in the context of that sort of review, which is not wholly unlike some legal proceedings, the protection of the citizen will be kept parallel to the protection that would be enjoyed if the matter were being heard in court.

The reasons for legal professional privilege are perfectly simple. Public policy entitles the citizen to a substantial degree of privilege for documents--the privilege not to reveal documents, even in a court case, or privilege for documents that have been revealed in a court case. Public policy states quite simply that the citizen should be able to consult a lawyer in confidence; otherwise it would be difficult for the lawyer to give advice that was not damaging to the client. The client's explanation to his lawyer, which might be incomplete, could involve giving evidence, which if it were in the public domain, might be taken as proof of guilt or of wrongdoing.

Likewise, the lawyer's advice might be that his client had transgressed, or could be thought to have transgressed. If a document giving such advice were put in the public domain, it would make it much more difficult for the lawyer to argue his case, even though his client was not guilty. However, even if the client was responsible and had somehow erred, such an admission could damage him when trying to beat off unfair or exaggerated criticisms from another direction.

Placing the procedures for that sort of review in parallel with court procedures will give the citizen confidence. It is also more likely that documents will be made available, both at an early stage and in relation to a particular review, which seems to me to be almost entirely beneficial. I should be interested to hear what the Minister has to say, and what those advising her think about that suggestion.
