Mr. Allan: I am glad to begin the detailed consideration of the Bill with these amendments, which relate to the provision to allow entry clearance officers overseas to deal more substantively with applications from people to come to this country and to give entry clearance at that point instead of relying on the immigration officer at the port of entry. We support the additional clarity that that would give to travellers who are granted leave to enter the country at the point of application for their visa, but we are worried about the possible implications of a refusal at that point and wish to explore that matter with the Government. This is in keeping with our stated aim to try to assist the Government to pass legislation that accords with the international conventions to which this country is a signatory.

Our particular concern is that we risk breaching article 31(2) of the 1951 refugee convention, which states:

    ``The Contracting States shall not apply to the movement of such refugees restrictions other than those which are necessary and such restrictions shall only be applied until their status in the country is regularised or they obtain admission to another country''.

We may impose an unnecessary restriction that prevents those suffering genuine oppression or fear of oppression abroad from coming to this country. We could not support such a policy.

To clarify the matter we wish to delete the phrases that refer to refusal. This is a substantive issue, because it will frequently be difficult precisely for those who fear oppression to gain the necessary clearance abroad. They will often be frightened to approach foreign embassies or to be seen trying to obtain a visa, and even when they manage to approach an entry clearance officer and make an application, they will often have difficulty in producing the necessary documentation to obtain a valid travel document.

We wish to see the procedure streamlined in such a way that entry clearance and leave to enter can be processed appropriately for not only ordinary travellers, as I think that the Government intend, but exceptional travellers—and in this case refugees—so that, even if they cannot obtain the formal documentation because of their oppressed status they can reach this country and present themselves to an immigration officer here who will decide on their status. Our concern is that the entry clearance officer's refusal of leave to enter would be taken as a complete block on a person's travel.

We are still at an early stage, but we shall return again and again to this theme. We are creating a framework within which Ministers can make more detailed orders, so we are not dealing with the substance of what they will tell entry clearance officers. However, I hope that the Minister will be able to tell the Committee that the Government do not intend to break the spirit of article 31(2) of the 1951 convention and that, through whatever orders are made, those who genuinely fear oppression in their home country will still be able to make the journey to the United Kingdom, if they believe that appropriate, to claim their right under that convention. I hope that the Minister will be able to reassure the Committee. I regret that the Bill is again imprecise and that the decision will be made later, when the Minister or his successor presents the relevant orders to effect the clause.

Clause 1 allows us to operate immigration control more flexibly by ensuring that we are not tied to the current cumbersome methods of granting and refusing leave to enter.

Amendments Nos. 1 to 4, which were tabled by the hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam, would have strange practical effects. Amendment No. 1 would allow the Secretary of State to present an order to set out new and various ways of granting leave to enter. However, it would not allow him to provide for new, additional ways of refusing leave to enter.

Amendment No. 2 would allow the Secretary of State to grant but not to refuse leave to enter before the applicant arrived in the United Kingdom. Therefore, people would have to come to the United Kingdom to be refused entry.

Amendment No. 3 would limit the Secretary of State's powers. He would be allowed to specify the form and manner of granting and varying leave, but not of refusing it. Thus, leave would have to be refused through a written notice, as happens at present. Such a provision will not work, and is nonsensical. If we provide for new methods of granting leave to enter, we need a parallel provision for refusing leave to enter. Otherwise, we shall be tied to an outdated immigration system that cannot take full advantage of new developments in technology and new ways of working.

Amendment No. 4 is different. As I have already explained, under section 4(1) of the Immigration Act 1971, the power to grant or refuse leave to enter the United Kingdom rests solely with an immigration officer, but the power to grant or refuse leave to remain rests with the Secretary of State. Subsection (7) of the Bill changes that, so that the Secretary of State can also grant or refuse leave to enter. The change is intended to streamline current procedures and lead to more efficient decision making. The provision will not apply to those who are already in the United Kingdom. It is intended that those powers will be used principally in cases in which the asylum decision must be taken by a caseworker in Croydon.

At the moment, bureaucratic work is being duplicated as a result of inefficient provisions in the legislation. It is far easier and more straightforward for the official who makes the decision to grant asylum to issue the documents that permit that to happen, rather than oblige another official, who happens to be an immigration officer, to issue such a stamp.

Amendment No. 4 would give the Secretary of State power to grant leave to enter the United Kingdom, but not to refuse such leave. That would mean that a caseworker in Croydon could undertake the consideration, decision and the granting of leave to enter, but that the power to refuse leave to enter would rest solely with an immigration officer at the port of entry. In practice, that would mean that the file would have to be sent to the port so that an immigration officer could refuse leave to enter. Such a process would create a bureaucratic burden and would tie up immigration officers in bureaucratic, nugatory work. Such work could be undertaken efficiently from a central point.

The hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam spoke about restricting refugees' access. He implied that any controls would restrict refugees' access because controlling access through immigration controls means controlling immigration and those who may come in.

There will always be some controls on refugees. I assume that the hon. Gentleman is not arguing that we should remove all controls, but is probing whether the measure will apply new restrictions on refugees' ability to enter that were not there before. The answer is no; we are not proposing new restrictions. He rightly says that ordinary travellers should travel more freely and effectively. We seek to remove unnecessary bureaucratic delays, including the need for people to be interviewed at the entry clearance point and the point of entry to the United Kingdom. Having two interviews when one would be adequate does not seem to be worthwhile.

    ``to exploit the opportunities of up to date information technology so that the processing of entry clearance applications, and dealing with the applicant at the ports and after entry, can be done as swiftly as possible''.

As a starting point, it is important to examine our current system of on-entry controls. Presently, on arrival at a United Kingdom port of entry, persons who are subject to immigration control must be examined by an immigration officer to establish whether they satisfy the requirements of the immigration rules for granting leave to enter in a particular category—including the possibility that none of the general grounds for refusal apply to them. That is the general nature of our on-entry immigration controls.

Under clause 1, the Secretary of State is taking a power to enable the process of granting leave to enter to be carried out before arrival at the United Kingdom and by others apart from immigration officers. We must clarify both the role of on-entry control in this new dispensation and the powers to be exercised by immigration officers within the framework. That is the purpose of the amendment.

Paragraph 38 of schedule 12 is relevant. It will insert a new paragraph 2A after schedule 2 of the Immigration Act 1971 that states:

    (1) This paragraph applies to a person who has arrived in the United Kingdom with leave to enter which is in force but which was given to him before his arrival.

    (a) whether there has been such a change in the circumstances of his case, since that leave was given, that it should be cancelled; or

    (b) whether that leave was obtained as a result of false information given by him or his failure to disclose material facts.''

That suggests that, in future, examination by an immigration officer will take place for much more limited purposes than the examination that currently takes place to examine whether someone comes within the ambit of immigration rules. Paragraph 38 will confine an immigration officer to examining only whether there has been a change in circumstances, or whether the person involved has obtained leave to enter as a result of false information or non-disclosure of material facts.
