Mr. Hoon: I had thought that I understood clause 13 until I listened to the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway). However, I shall do my best to answer his questions and explain how the clause works.

Clause 13 is a provision that is new to UK data protection law. It is modelled on article 15 of the directive, and I accept that neither is entirely straightforward. The clause is concerned with decisions made by automated means, which significantly affect individuals and which evaluate matters about them, such as their work performance, credit worthiness, reliability of conduct.

Subsection (1) gives individuals the right to write to data controllers requiring them not to take such decision by automated means. People with principled objections to machines deciding such questions can object, up front and for the specified reasons.

Subsection (2) deals with the situation where controllers have received no such notices and have taken decisions of the kind covered by the clause that is, by automated means. It requires controllers to write to the individuals concerned giving them the opportunity to write seeking a review of the decision. In effect, people objecting to a decision can challenge it.

Subsection (3) requires controllers to respond in writing to written notices from individuals made under subsections (1) or (2).

I now come to the provisions that the amendment would affect.

Subsections (4) to (7) provide exemptions from the entitlements provided in subsections (1) and (2). The exemptions are of two broad kinds. the first relates to decisions taken, and I use the term loosely in the context of a contract between the data controller and the data subject. The second is where the decision is authorised or required by statute. Both conditions are found in subsection (6).

However, those conditions are not sufficient in themselves to justify the exemption. they must be accompanied by one or other of the conditions mentioned in subsection (7). The first of those is, in essence, that the decision in question goes in favour of the data subject that for example, people seeking credit get the loan that they have asked for. The alternative condition is that, if the decision does not go in the data subject's favour, steps have been taken to provide safeguards for his or her interests.

I hope that that answers the general problem raised by the hon. Member for Ryedale, but I shall try to be more specific about the second condition, which seems to be the essence of the matter.

The wording used in the directive specifies that the data subject's request "has been satisfied". That is intended to apply when the data subject's request has been granted in other words, that he or she has been given the loan or whatever it was that was wanted.

However, if the loan was not granted as a result of automatic processing, the data subject has the opportunity to make representations: for example, he or she may seek a review of the decision by non-automatic means. The general principle running through the directive was that, although automatic processing should be allowed, a decision to turn down a data subject's request for a loan, for example, should be reviewable by non-automatic means. It could properly, be argued that the directive's underlying principle was to allow and encourage automatic processing, so long as it was in favour of the data subject; where it turned out to be against or potentially against the data subject's interests, the means should be available to him or her to have that decision reviewed by human agency.

I do not know how much detail Committee members want me to go into on the specific amendments. I am sure that the hon. Member for Ryedale recognises that if we accepted them, they would wholly undermine the scheme that I have just set out. I can deal with them in detail if he wants, but I took them to be probing amendments that would allow him to ask various questions.

Mr. Hoon: Clause 21 provides for a mechanism that is new to UK data protection law. It has been introduced as a result of a requirement in the directive. The clause describes it as a preliminary assessment mechanism. In the language of the directive, it consists of prior checking by the commissioner before processing starts.

Under usual arrangements, a data controller subject to the prohibition on processing without notification must properly notify but may then go ahead and process. It is for the commissioner to intervene if he or she becomes aware of an actual or potential problem.

The directive and clause 21(1) impose an obligation to wait between notification and starting to process for select categories of data processing. It effectively provides that the commissioner must first be notified of the intention to process. Under current UK data protection law, and under that of most other countries with equivalent legislation, direct legal obligations are placed on organisations and others who process personal data to comply with specific legal requirements. The data user, as the relevant organisation or person, is defined in current UK law, and is a key figure. The obligations ensure that compliance falls on him and that he is answerable for compliance with the full body of the law, however he manages to deliver that compliance.
