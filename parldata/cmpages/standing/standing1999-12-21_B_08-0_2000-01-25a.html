Clause 26

Relations within the United Kingdom

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Question again proposed.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. David Lock): The hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway)—

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I am grateful to the Parliamentary Secretary for reminding me that I was about to intervene to ask whether he might give some examples.

Mr. Lock: To set the example in context, I remind the Committee that clause 26 exempts information the disclosure of which would or would be likely to prejudice relations between any two Administrations in the United Kingdom. The clause has a prejudice test: it is not a blanket ban. The commissioner may order disclosure if she finds that the information would not prejudice relations between Administrations. Clause 13 will apply, thereby ensuring that even if information would prejudice relations between Administrations in the UK, it would none the less be released if the public interest test favours release, which would be a recommendation from the Minister.

Let us suppose that an E. coli outbreak takes place in one of the Scottish regions and that a civil servant from the Department of Health in London writes a report to his Minister in this Administration, perhaps relying on information provided by the Scottish Executive, which may be highly critical of the handling of the outbreak and of how the Scottish Executive is handling health issues generally. If that information were to be disclosed before the Scottish Executive had had an opportunity to consider it, respond to it and make appropriate preparations for its being made public, that may jeopardise our good relations with the Scottish Executive.

Hon. Members asked whether the test is designed purely to avoid embarrassment. In such circumstances, disclosure would not be justified if it would purely embarrass Scottish Ministers If it would, but would cause no further mischief, the information should be disclosed. Nothing in the Bill allows information to be withheld on grounds of embarrassment. However, if disclosure would cause a breakdown in relations between the UK Government and the Scottish Executive by disclosing at that point, such that future information of that nature would not be freely exchanged between Administrations, it is right that an exemption founded on the prejudice test and subject to clause 13 should be available.

Such information would be covered under clause 26 because disclosure before the Scottish Executive has had a chance to consider and respond to the report would prejudice relations between any two Administrations in the UK. It would not be right for the Scottish Executive to learn about such a document's existence from a national newspaper, having found that it had to be disclosed as a result of an application made in London.

That is not the end of the story by a long way. If the exemption is properly claimed—if it were not, the Information Commissioner could order disclosure—the public authority, in this case, the Department of Health, must under clause 13 consider whether the public interest in disclosing the information outweighs the public interest in maintaining the exemption. If it does, the information will be disclosed under clause 13. Again, we are dealing with hypothesis, but I should like to illustrate a point.

If the information shows that the mishandling of the outbreak led to its spreading further, it may be considered that the public interest in disclosing the information outweighed the fact that disclosure would prejudice relations with the Scottish Executive. The information would then be released under clause 13. However, we need to retain the exemption. Material may be held by the Government or by other public authorities that is critical of one or other of the Administrations and prejudices our relations with it.

It is not unreasonable that such information should be protected. What about any views that the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions may have on the Scottish Executive's proposed reorganisation of local authorities? We may wish to have an input, or to comment publicly on the matter, but we would not want to release a report that made a stinging criticism of the proposals as it might prejudice our good relations with the Scottish Executive. We may wish instead to comment constructively.

That is not big brother tactics; we do not seek to subvert the Scottish Administration, the Executive Committee of the Northern Ireland Assembly or the National Assembly for Wales. That is standard practice in other freedom of information legislation in countries with a similar need to preserve good interstate relations. It is right to retain the Bill's protection of that category of information. The clause should therefore stand part of the Bill,

I hope that my explanation provides the reassurance that the hon. Gentleman needs to be sure that the clause is not concerned with embarrassment but with genuine issues of prejudice.

Clause 27

The economy

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 132, in page 14, line 22, leave out from first `would' to end of line 26 and insert—

    `(a) harm the ability of the Government to manage the economy, prejudice the conduct of official market operations, or lead to improper gain or advantage, or

    (b) prejudice the assessment or collection of tax, duties or National Insurance contributions, or assist tax avoidance or evasion'.

The exemption in clause 27—another contents exemption— is too widely drawn. The amendment seeks to substitute the words in clause 27 with words used in the code of practice to provide a general exemption for matters relating to the economy. The clause is drafted in such a way that it will catch all information relating to the economy. That is much too wide an exemption. It goes beyond what is necessary or reasonable and should be much more tightly drawn.

I do not wish to detain the Committee, but I shall give a couple of examples. The clause would exempt any information

    ``if its disclosure . . . would, or would be likely to, prejudice—

    (a) the economic interests of the United Kingdom''.

That is a vague term and capable of wide application. Similarly, in subsection (1)(b), the exemption applies to

    ``the financial interests of any administration in the United Kingdom''.

The administrations are listed in clause 26—the United Kingdom Government, the two Assemblies and the Scottish Parliament. Subsection (1)(b) is equally wide ranging and we are uncertain about its application.

There is a case for an exemption in relation to the economy, but we need to be much more specific about which aspects of the economy or financial interests are to be the subject of that exemption. We believe that the code had it right. We should be concerned about the release of information that, in the words of the amendment, would

    ``harm the ability of the Government to manage the economy''.

That is a perfectly straightforward and obvious position to take, and similarly, if the release of such information would

    ``prejudice the conduct of official market operations''.

In schedule 1, part VI, which lists the organisations that are not public authorities in terms of being Government organisations, but which are public authorities none the less, such as the Bank of England, there is a clear exemption for the conduct of official market operations in respect of the Bank. It is right to extend that exemption to ensure that no information is released that could lead to improper gain or advantage. Beyond those restrictions, we cannot see how such a vague description as

    ``the economic interests of the United Kingdom''

can be justified. Why have the Government gone way beyond what was in the White Paper when such an exemption could be much more tightly focused and defined?

We could agree with the Government if disclosure of information would

    ``prejudice the assessment or collection of tax, duties or National Insurance contributions or assist tax avoidance or evasion''.

That is a standard principle in tax legislation. We cannot see why the exemption in the Government's version in clause 27 needs to be anything like as extensive as it appears.

Our amendment is much more tightly worded and adds a sense of purpose to why exemptions are required. The definition of potential damage or harm is clearly much more tightly drawn and would mean that the exemption would not be used more liberally. It would restrict the exemption only to those specific matters that should be exempted in the public interest. It shows much more clearly what the clause would exempt, so I commend the amendment to the Committee.

Mr. Maclennan: I support amendment No. 57, tabled in the name of the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central (Mr. Fisher), but I shall not trespass, at least initially, on the hon. Gentleman's turf.

The effect of amendments Nos. 68 and 69, tabled in my name and that of my hon. Friend the Member for Somerton and Frome, is best described as removing the indefinite element of the exemption for information about investigations and proceedings. It has the backing of not only the Campaign for Freedom of Information, but the Law Society.

As the Bill stands, information reclaimed in connection with an investigation into a possible offence would be permanently exempt, even after any proceedings had been completed. There will be no harm test and no time limit for the exemption, which is unattractive and unacceptable. It means that information gathered in connection with, say, the Paddington rail crash could never be revealed under the Bill. Following an investigation into a major disaster, many bodies, including the police and the Health and Safety Executive, would hold information.

Once the police have decided whether a criminal investigation will take place, that information should be made available, or at any rate be available, to the families of the bereaved and other interested parties, subject only to a harm test. Only with access to such information could families decide whether to bring an action against those who may have some responsibility for the disaster. Alas, there have been too many disasters in recent years, such as the Marchioness, Zeebrugge and Hillsborough, when access to information held by the police that would have been vital for the families of the dead could have been withheld under the Government's proposals.

It is clear that access to information may also be vital in seeking to establish whether there is a continuing systemic problem within the authority holding the information or about which information is held by another body. An example might be a series of deaths in custody. Again, there have been far too many such cases recently. Once a decision on whether to prosecute has been taken, information which could establish evidence of a systemic problem should be available. Access to information might show that the training of police or prison officers in restraint techniques should be reconsidered. There might therefore be a benefit not only to individuals, but to the general public interest.

The blanket exemption in clause 28 is also at odds with the Woolf reforms of the civil justice system. One of their principle themes is to avoid unnecessary and costly litigation by encouraging parties to resolve disputes at the pre-action stage. Pre-action discovery lies at the centre of the Woolf initiative. Above all, we need to avoid litigating for the primary purpose of obtaining information and amendments Nos. 68 and 69 are designed with that in mind. The Bill clearly works against the Woolf initiative. The effective resolution of disputes at an early stage requires those concerned about the conduct of public authorities to be speedily provided with wide disclosure of material held by the public authorities. Much litigation against authorities arises out of secrecy and inadequate information in the early stages and members of the public who are aggrieved by a public authority's conduct frequently resort to legal proceedings to pursue their grievances, in some cases unnecessarily. The amendment would thus allow class exemptions to stand, but only while the decision to prosecute is still under consideration or any prosecution has not been completed. Thereafter, such information would be disclosable, subject to the prejudice test set out in clause 29.

I have probably said enough to enable the Minister to deal with the principal issues that I want him to consider.
