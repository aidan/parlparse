Extension of functions

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Keith Hill): I want to discuss the detail of the amendment and the new clause, which were tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Scarborough and Whitby (Mr. Quinn), before I turn to the broader issues that are associated with rail freight.

Amendment No. 855 emphasises the importance of the needs of customers of rail freight. The new clause would establish a rail freight users' committee—we shall discuss that proposal in due course.

The amendment would impose on the Rail Passengers Council a duty to co-operate with the customers of rail freight users. We agree that rail freight is important and that the need to develop rail freight is one of the key reasons for setting up the Strategic Rail Authority. As my hon. Friend knows, the franchising director has no rail freight responsibilities, although the SRA will be able to co-ordinate strategy towards, and to balance the capacity demands of, passengers and goods.

Having said that, I do not believe that that is the best or most appropriate way in which to help rail freight or passengers, for reasons that I shall explain. We have been keen to strengthen the powers of the Rail Passengers Council and to ensure that it can be an effective champion of the passenger. That is why we have extended its powers to co-operate with other bodies representing the interests of users of other public passenger transport services. We recognise that a passenger is a consumer of more than just the railway and we are aiming for integrated journeys.

However, the main role of the Rail Passengers Council is to speak up for passengers. Co-ordinating with freight users as well as passenger groups would distract it from that core function, and, spread its expertise rather too thinly. I accept that passengers will have an interest in the possible conflicts between freight and passenger traffic but we believe that that will be better handled, and more effective, at the level of the SRA. The Rail Passengers Council can best use its expertise, in the range of passenger concerns, by being the eyes and ears of the passenger and feeding passengers' views to the SRA as part of the SRA's wider remit.

I congratulate my hon. Friend on tabling new clause 33. The nature of freight services, and the relationship between the customer and the service provider, is, however, very different from that of passenger services.

Unlike franchised passenger service operators, the private freight operating companies are not required to maintain any particular level of services and they rely entirely on attracting and retaining their customers to maintain their businesses. Clearly, freight operators already have a strong incentive to listen to their customers and to resolve any problems that may arise. The main role of RPCs will be to speak up for passengers and to represent their interests when things go wrong and to ensure that train operators take their complaints seriously. Freight services by their very nature tend to be used by private companies, which are better placed to represent themselves and to take up complaints directly with the freight operator concerned.

The new clause would place a statutory duty on the Strategic Rail Authority to consult the proposed rail freight users consultative committee on all matters relating to rail freight and in respect of closure proposals and other matters. I fully accept that we want to protect rail freight interests and infrastructure. However, making such a committee, as a proposed RFUCC, a statutory consultee on all matters relating to rail freight as well as being a statutory consultee in respect of all major and minor closures, network changes and the undefined ``reasonable requirements of all operators'', would place a massive burden on the administrative system at a considerable extra cost. Those consultation requirements would be far greater than those that exist for the passenger committees.

I reassure my hon. Friend that safeguards of freight interests will be put in place. The SRA will be able to take freight interests into account when considering passenger-related closure proposals, and the authority will be able to define its own reasonable requirements of Railtrack in respect of the strategic capability of the network. I assure my hon. Friend that the Government are committed to supporting rail freight as part of an integrated and sustainable freight distribution system. I am sure that he agrees with me that, in that regard, the record speaks for itself

I am happy to say that rail freight continues to attract new business, although there is clearly much more to attract. We want to encourage further growth, and we are prepared to pay for it where it makes economic sense to do so. Establishment of the SRA means that developing fail freight will finally be part of the strategic management of the whole rail sector. The shadow SRA is already working on the freight element of its strategy, which involves vital issues of capacity, demand and investment. The overall objective remains to put more f eight on rail.

We drew on the advice of the shadow SRA in preparing the 10-year plan for transport and we made it clear that there has to be more investment to expand the network's capacity to carry freight. The 10-year plan involves a long-term strategy and it will tackle the legacy of under-investment and fragmentation. I hope that my hon. Friend will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Lawrie Quinn (Scarborough and Whitby): I have listened carefully to the remarks of my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary. In view of the safeguards that he placed on record, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Syms: I beg to move amendment No. 971, in page 124, line 14, after `necessary', insert

    `and is unlikely to become necessary'.

This is an important clause, because it relates to the closure of unnecessary track. There is always concern when unnecessary track is closed, because many groups have aspirations about reopening sections of track and, once the track has gone, they do not have the opportunity to do that. Mr. Hill: Let me reassure the hon. Gentleman that there is a prospect of goodies to come, but not yet, I fear.

The clause widens the definition of minor closure to include the track in stations and light maintenance depots that they do not need or where closure of part of the station or depot is proposed. In the past, such track closures have had to go through the full closure process, which has caused unnecessary delay and bureaucracy.

The amendment requires the authority to judge whether the circumstances under which it is considering this type of closure proposal might change and whether the section of track or associated installations might once more become necessary for providing passenger services.

I can understand the hon. Gentleman's concern that we should not give the authority carte blanche to close down parts of the railway that we later discover we need. We are not allowing that to happen. The clause will not cover track outside stations or depots, that will still be covered by the full closure process.

The making of a determination by the authority under the clause is discretionary, but I assure hon. Members that we do not expect it to make a minor closure decision lightly or without considering its wider purposes. The authority has wide-ranging duties to develop the railway, not to dismember it, and to protect passengers' interests. If it decides that an operator's proposal is not a minor closure, the operator will have to go through the full closure process. It would then be a matter for the Secretary of State to decide.

We want to streamline the closure process without making it easy to close parts of the railway. Under the Railways Act 1993, the closure process was unwieldy and bureaucratic; we want to make it workable. I hope that, with those assurances, the hon. Gentleman will seek to withdraw his amendment.

Clause 215

Powers of Executives

Mr. Hill: I beg to move amendment No. 1019, in page 126, line 12, leave out from beginning to `subsection' in line 13 and insert—

    `(1) Section 34 of the Railways Act 1993 (Passenger Transport Authorities and Executives: franchising) is amended as follows.

    (2) After'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 1020 to 1028.

Mr. Hill: The clause clarifies the relationship between the passenger transport executives and the authority.

PTEs have wide powers under section 34 of the 1993 Act. For each passenger rail service in their area they can give notice to the franchising director—in future, the SRA—specifying the level and quality of services needed to meet public transport requirements in their area, including the basket of fares to be charged for those services. Whatever the PTE specifies must then be included in the relevant franchise, and the PTE meets the cost.

It is right that PTEs should champion local transport needs. I have no doubt that they will continue to do so vigorously, but we are establishing the authority centrally to develop the railway network to a nationwide strategy. Therefore, we do not intend to reduce the PTE's role or to modify its powers to specify services, but we must ensure that a balance can be struck between local and more strategic considerations, and that is the purpose of the clause.

The clause provides that the SRA should not, without a direction from the Secretary of State, implement a notice from a PTE that will prevent it from complying or seriously hinder it in complying with directions given to it by the Secretary of State or Scottish Ministers. Amendments Nos. 976 and 977, which are next on the amendment paper, affect that provision.

The clause also provides that the SRA should not act on a statement from a PTE if, or to the extent that, doing so would have an adverse effect overall on rail passenger or freight services, whether inside or outside the PTE's area. Finally, it provides that the SRA need not act on a statement if so doing would increase the payments that it must make under the franchises.

The Government amendments relate, first, to the proposed procedure for resolving disputes between PTEs and the SRA. Section 34(17) of the 1993 Act sets out a general procedure for resolving disputes over franchising matters between a PTE and the franchising director—in future, the SRA. It provides for either party to refer the matter in dispute to the Secretary of State, who may then give the PTE or the authority such directions as he thinks fit. That might include a requirement under section 34(1) for the authority or PTE to enter into a franchise agreement on terms decided by the Secretary of State.

We intend these dispute provisions to apply also to the new provisions in the clause. If there is a conflict between local and strategic considerations that the parties cannot resolve, it can be referred to the Secretary of State, who is ultimately accountable to Parliament for local and national railway services. He will determine where the balance lies. In so doing, he will not act in a quasi-judicial capacity—that is, confined to adjudicating between conflicting claims from the PTE and the SRA. He will act as he considers appropriate, balancing all the facts and circumstances of the case and having regard to representations by the SRA and PTEs. Under section 34(19) of the 1933 Act, he can even disapply parts of section 34 to arrive at the correct result.

However, the PTEs have made representations to us that the Bill may not quite achieve that, so we have tabled amendments Nos. 1020 and 1025, which we hope will provide useful clarification. They remove the words ``in its opinion'' from the clause, to make it clear that, if there is a dispute, it is over the facts of the matter and not over whether the SRA holds a certain opinion. Amendment No. 1028 then makes it clearer that, when a dispute is referred to the Secretary of State under section 34(17), he may resolve it as he thinks fit. I hope that members of the Committee, not least those who have the PTEs' interests at heart, will welcome the amendments.

Amendments Nos. 1019 and 1021 to 1023 contain technical drafting changes, so that the adverse effect test by which the SRA will judge whether it should disregard a PTE statement is on all fours with the separate test of compliance with directions or guidance.

With regard to PTE statements affecting SRA payments under franchises, the PTEs have also expressed concern to us that, under the clause, the SRA need not act on a statement if doing so would increase the payments that it must make under franchises. The provision relates not to payments of grant by the SRA to the PTEs, but to payments by the SRA direct to franchise operators. Our intention is to ensure that the local requirements specified by PTEs in their statements do not impose additional costs on other franchise services, for which the SRA then has to pay.

The Bill prohibits the SRA from acting in such a case on a PTE's statement under the 1993 Act unless there are special circumstances. That amounts to a general presumption against acting on the statement. On reflection, we believe that that is too strong, as there may be cases where the SRA is willing to carry the extra cost, which may be quite small. For instance, a PTE may wish to enhance a local service which, to gain the greatest benefit, should be extended for a short distance beyond the PTE boundary and therefore needs to be supported directly by the SRA for that part of the journey. That barely counts as special circumstances, but we would not want to rule it out. We have therefore tabled a simple set of amendments, amendments Nos. 1024, 1026 and 1027, which provide that the SRA need not act in a certain case. That enables us to remove the reference to ``special circumstances''.

I hope that the Committee will welcome the amendments, which have been made in response to positive representations from the PTEs and members of the Committee.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause 217

Competition functions of Regulator

Question, proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Syms: I should be grateful if the Under-Secretary could set out a little more clearly how the Competition Act 1998 will function. Under the clause, responsibility will be concurrently between the Rail Regulator and the Director General of Fair Trading. Will the Under-Secretary set out how that would operate and give members of the Committee a little more detail about this important subject, in which I am sure many of them take an interest?

Mr. Hill: I am pleased to respond to the hon. Gentleman's request for clarification of the clause.

Since 1 April, the Rail Regulator has enjoyed powers under the Competition Act 1998 concurrently with the Director General of Fair Trading, in respect of anti-competitive agreements, practices and so on that relate to the supply of railway services. The clause amends section 67(3) of the Railways Act 1993 to make it clear that the regulator's Competition Act jurisdiction would not be confined to railway services in the narrow Railways Act sense of the phrase. It will also cover certain other specified services relating to railways over which he has no general jurisdiction under the 1993 Act. Those services are the provision or maintenance of rolling stock, the development, maintenance or renewal of a network, station or light maintenance depot and the development, provision or maintenance of systems designed wholly or mainly to facilitate the provision of railway services. The Secretary of State has the power to add to or subtract from the list of services relating to railways, and that requires the affirmative procedure.

The hon. Gentleman asked a number of other questions, in particular in relation to concurrent jurisdiction and double jeopardy. The Government have already minimised the risk of double jeopardy through the Competition Act 1998 (Concurrency) Regulations 2000, which came into force on 1 March. They are fully effective in preventing double jeopardy—they prevent process double jeopardy and remedies double jeopardy. The Director General of Fair Trading and regulators have to agree which of them is to exercise functions before any one of them can exercise formal investigatory powers.

The Competition Act 1998 also provides that a regulator should make a final enforcement order in respect of a licence only if he is satisfied that the order is the most appropriate way to proceed under the 1998 Act.

Question accordingly agreed to.

Clause 218

Pension guarantees

5.15 pm
 

Mr. Syms: I beg to move amendment No. 981, in page 127, line 36, leave out `may' and insert

    `shall'.

This is an important clause because it deals with pension guarantees. The SRA will take over the relevant responsibilities. The clause states:

    The Authority may give guarantees to the trustees of an occupational pension scheme if the Authority considers that it is desirable to do so.

The amendment would replace the word ``may'' with the word ``shall'' because, as a responsible employer, the SRA should give firmer guarantees than those contained in the Bill.

Mr. Hill: I assume that the intention that underlies the amendment is to ensure that British Rail's approach to the provision of guarantees to pension schemes in order to ensure a proper level of benefits to pensioners will be continued. The proposed amendment would not have the desired effect, but I hope that the hon. Gentleman will contain himself until I have explained the matter further.

We have been giving further thought to this whole issue. The pensioners of the older schemes, who currently rely on the provision of guarantees by the British Railways Board to maintain full funding of their schemes and indexation of payments are, generally, BR's oldest and poorest pensioners. BR has ensured in the past that those pensions have always been secured and indexed in the same way as pensions from BR's own newer schemes. However, in some cases there may be no legal obligation on BR to continue that practice in the future and thus no obligation that we could transfer to the SRA by a transfer scheme.

I am accordingly happy to say that we agree with the concerns that seem to underpin the amendment. We believe that the pensioners should be given assurances about the future security of their pensions, including indexation. In relation to the privatisation of BR, Labour Members fought long and hard to get the previous Government to ensure the future security of railway pensions. I know that that is the case because I was there. Pensioners should know that current pensions will always be paid and that the real value of those pensions will be maintained through indexation during the whole of their retirement.

We are therefore considering the position carefully and we will discuss the matter with BR and the trustees of the funds, with a view to bringing forward at a later stage an amendment that will guarantee the position of the pensioners and ensure that additional funding is provided in such cases whenever necessary.

In view of that approach, I hope that the hon. Gentleman, who has scored a minor victory in a good cause, will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Syms: I thank the Under-Secretary for his answer. Many people will be very happy with his reassurances. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
