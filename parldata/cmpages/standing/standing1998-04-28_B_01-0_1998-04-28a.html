Mr. Michael: It may be helpful if I say at this stage that we do not support the motion that the clause stand part of the Bill and will vote against it in order to delete the clause from the Bill.

The clause was inserted in the Bill at Third Reading in the House of Lords, when an amendment by Lord Ackner was accepted by a majority of nine votes on a Division. The Home Secretary has informed colleagues of his intention to seek removal of the clause from the Bill during the Committee stage.

The clause seeks to reconstitute the old Advisory Council on the Penal System, which operated in the 1960s and 1970s. The council provided valuable advice to the Government of the day, but the advice was not always accepted, nor was it always provided quickly--some reports took three to four years. The pace of life is faster and the demands of the public are much greater now.

We agree with the basic thrust of Lord Ackner's remarks that the Government should have well-informed advice to underpin their policies. However, we do not think that clause 1 provides a body that would achieve that end. Instead, well-targeted advice from people with specific expertise in particular areas is needed. That is already available from a wide range of sources, some of which did not exist in the 1960s and the 1970s. There has been a considerable change since then, which I shall outline.

The Criminal Justice Consultative Council contains leading representatives from across the criminal justice system. It is underpinned by local groups, which comprise senior practitioners within the area. That ensures that it has a well-informed understanding of the practicalities, as well as being able to take a broader and more strategic view when appropriate. It is the first body that is available to provide advice to Government.

The second is the trial issues group, also supported by sub-groups, which has a wealth of experience in practical and procedural issues. Thirdly, Her Majesty's chief inspector of prisons and the probation service produce the thematic reports on penal issues of concern. The Law Commission also advises on the adequacy and effectiveness of the criminal law. We have input from the National Audit Office and the Audit Commission, both of which have a role to play in advising on efficiency and effectiveness in these matters--for example, the work on youth justice, which we shall consider later in the Bill.

The Audit Commission's report on youth justice was a seminal work, and has informed the provisions in the Bill to reform practice and legislation. In preparation for the reforms to the youth justice system, we have gone further and established a representative youth justice task force; there are formal and informal ways in which Government can seek and obtain advice.

Two other bodies will be in place when the Bill has completed its passage: the youth justice board, which will provide advice on the operation of the youth justice system and bring an important degree of coherence to the way in which young offenders are dealt with, and the sentencing advisory panel, which will have regard to the range of available disposals, their effectiveness and their appropriateness for different types of offence and offender when drawing up proposals for the Court of Appeal. It will have the benefit of advice from a wide range of organisations and will publish its advice.

Within Parliament, there is the Select Committee on Home Affairs; its terms of reference enable it to cover the whole area of criminal justice and penal policy. It consults widely and produces authoritative and well-researched reports on a range of issues. Taken together, a formidable array of bodies covers criminal justice and penal policy very effectively.

We have taken the debate in the House of Lords very seriously, and listened especially to the views of Lord Ackner, while considering whether there is still a case for advice from a new source with a wide-ranging remit. We have concluded that it would be difficult for the new body proposed in the clause, with wide terms of reference, in practice to contain representatives of all the sources of expertise that would be needed.

It would be wrong to engineer an arrangement that would have the effect of delaying the development and implementation of policies to which the elected Government are committed. We therefore concluded that such a body is not needed and, indeed, that it would be positively undesirable, without considering issues of cost to which the hon. Member for Woking referred in discussing amendments Nos. 81 and 82. Obviously, cost is a consideration, but the question is whether such a body would introduce delay and confusion rather than help the process of good government.

We are ready to consider ways of ensuring that we get the best advice from existing bodies. We are always open to suggestions for improvements. For example, the Criminal Justice Consultative Council's terms of reference do not give it a specific responsibility to advise the Government on issues within its remit. It has an obligation to consider them, but not a remit to advise.

The chairman of the council recently wrote to my right hon. Friend the Home Secretary about the matter, and in response my right hon. Friend made it clear that he sees a good case for an amendment to the terms of reference to make the provision of advice explicit. That shows our willingness to respond constructively to points raised, including those that were raised when the clause was debated in the other place. However, we do not believe that a wide-ranging body of this sort would provide added value, and therefore we recommend its deletion. My recommendation to the Committee, therefore, is to vote against clause stand part.
