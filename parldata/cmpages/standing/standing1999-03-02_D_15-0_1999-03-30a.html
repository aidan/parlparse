Mr. Quentin Davies (Grantham and Stamford): It is no good the Government trying to pull the wool over the eyes of the Committee or the general public. Two inescapable facts have emerged from this discussion: the Government are taking money away from widows and are destroying the national insurance contributory principle. The Conservative party has been discussing this for some time in different contexts. We have attempted to draw it to the attention of the House and the public, and the Minister has now confirmed it today.

The agenda here is not to create greater justice, to bring the welfare system up to date and to provide equality for women or any of those grand high-sounding phrases to which we have been treated. That is poppycock. The intention is take money from widows. Why widows? Because they are a particularly soft target and because, by definition, people do not know in advance that they might be victims of this measure. The Labour party therefore think that the political impact of getting £500 million out of this category of people will be less than getting it out of other categories. They are in a true sense a soft target.

It would have been open to the Government to have decided, in the light of the need, either for its own sake or to meet what may be the judgment of the European Court of Human Rights, to treat men and women equally for the purpose of bereavement and widows benefit. The Government could have come forward with a revenue-neutral proposal taking from the one and giving to the other, and leaving the net contribution of the national insurance fund for those who are bereaved the same as it is today. That approach would have been perfectly possible.

I am quite certain that the Minister is intellectually capable of devising an even-handed approach that would be cost and revenue neutral, but he did not bother to do that. What he has done, doubtless on instructions from on high and from the Treasury, is to find a lot of increasingly shopworn excuses for abstracting £500 million from widows. That is quite different.

Had he gone down the route that it would have been useful to have explored, of a revenue-neutral proposal ensuring that the total sum made available for bereavement benefit was the same as it is today, he could legitimately have talked about a reform. That would have been a reallocation between men and women. It might, by definition, have reduced the generosity of the existing benefits for women and there could have been some time limits.

Several possible approaches along those lines are expressed in the amendments. There could have been corresponding advantages for men who are bereaved. That would certainly have been a reallocation. It would have been an even-handed reallocation and it could have been dignified with the term ``reform'', but under no circumstances can the Government's proposal be so dignified. Today's debate thoroughly confirms what I have said before. This is not a welfare reform Bill, it is a welfare reduction Bill.

The arithmetic has been well set out in the excellent research paper produced by the Library. I refer Committee Members to page 40, which clearly states that the increase in the lump sum payment from £1,000 to £2,000 will cost £50 million a year. The measures to introduce a widowed parents allowance, which will allow men to benefit for six months and will replace the widowed mothers allowance, will cost a further £50 million a year.

The abolition of the widowed mothers benefit, and the means-testing of payments to widows after they have claimed benefit for six months, will yield the Treasury £575 million. A child of five with the most elementary knowledge of arithmetic could work out the figure that I gave the Committee earlier. The net take by the Treasury at the expense of the bereaved—it is nothing more or less than that—will be £475 million. This is not welfare reform; this is welfare reduction. Let us call a spade a spade. If the Government are embarked on a course that is less than edifying, let them at least have the courage and honesty to recognise that.

The Government are the first in at least 60 years not to be committed to the principle of national insurance. That was made abundantly clear earlier by the Minister's remarks. He clearly said that the Government wanted to change the incentives and to push people back to work. He treated a national insurance benefit for which people have contributed as if it were a means-tested welfare benefit.

We shall come to a Government amendment that will make what I thought was a minor technical change when I first read it. I had not seen its deep significance. It will amend the clause so that the reference to widows pension becomes widows allowance. ``Allowance'' is presumably new Labour-speak for handout. I realise now the amendment's significance: Ministers do not like talking about the widows pension. They consider it to be an allowance or handout. On that they differ fundamentally from us.

There is no doubt that the concept of widows pensions was introduced in 1925 as a piece of social insurance—a way of protecting people against actuarially predictable hazards of life. Those hazards might not be predictable on an individual basis, as we have already discussed and all know, but they are actuarially predictable. It is possible to provide such insurance, and it is much cheaper and easier to provide it on a universal and compulsory, rather than on an individualised, basis because of the risk to insurers of self-protection, by which people take out their own insurance.

There is a strong economic case for social insurance, which was accepted by the Conservative Government when they introduced the Widows', Orphans' and Old Age Contributory Pensions Act 1925. That has been the principle behind our system for the 74 years since then, but it is being changed fundamentally now. The Government well know about that change, and they find themselves slipping, as the Minister did earlier, into a vocabulary that implicitly recognises it. The Minister talks about widows pensions as a benefit, an allowance and a handout. That is how the Government look at the subject. If they want to make money quickly, the Minister thinks that it is perfectly legitimate for them simply to withdraw pensions to which people have contributed, and insurance payments to which they are entitled on account of the contributions that women or their husbands have made.

There is no doubt about it: the insurance principle has gone both in reality and from the vocabulary used by new Labour. The clause represents two vital and inescapable facts. The first is that it will involve welfare reduction by the quantifiable sum of £475 million a year. That will be at the expense of people who find it difficult to defend their own interests. The Government have cynically and ruthlessly recognised that and muscled in on an opportunity.

The second fact that the Government cannot get away from is that they are moving away from the insurance principle. Indeed, they have abandoned it and left it way behind. That is thoroughly regrettable, but it will not be long before the country as a whole wakes up to the essential facts that the Government are doing so much to disguise.
