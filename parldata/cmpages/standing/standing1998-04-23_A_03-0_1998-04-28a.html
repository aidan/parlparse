National Lottery Bill [Lords]

Clause 6

The New Opportunities Fund

4.30 pm

Mr Richard Spring (West Suffolk): I beg to move amendment No. 23, in page 9, line 13, at end insert--

    `(5) The New Opportunities Fund shall solely and only make grants to, or make or enter into arrangements with causes where it can be demonstrated that they would not otherwise be funded out of taxation or other local or national government revenues.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take new clause 1--Restrictions on distribution--

    `In section 25 of the 1993 Act (appliation of money by distributing bodies) after subsection (4) there shall be inserted--

    ``(5) A body shall distribute any money paid to it under section 24 solely and only to causes where it can be demonstrated that they would not otherwise be funded out of taxation or other national or local government revenues.''.'.

Mr. Spring: We oppose the Government's proposed New Opportunities Fund because it violates the additionality principle. Lottery proceeds should fund only those initiatives that are additional to core Government spending programmes, which should be funded by taxation. That principle is at the very heart of our objection to the Bill. Conservative Members therefore want the matter to be given a proper airing.

The White Paper ``The People's Lottery'' maintains that the Government are committed to the additionality principle. In it, the Prime Minister stated:

    ``We don't believe it would be right to use Lottery money to pay for things which are the Government's responsibilities''.

Given the proposals that underpin the New Opportunities Fund, it is hard to believe that the additionality principle will be observed. The Government's credibility is in this respect open to question.

On 6 April, Peter Riddell wrote an article in The Times about the lottery's future, entitled ``A winner whatever the odds''. The article's subtitle claimed that the Treasury's aim was

    ``to milk the lottery cash cow for its own ends.''

Riddell specifically noted:

    ``The new fund also violates the basic principle. . .That alone appears to rule out the new uses. More precisely, there is the concept of additionality, that lottery money should not be used to replace existing spending.''

That is the core of the problem. Political commentators and members of the distribution bodies are worried by what is a clear breach of the additionality principle.

The Conservative manifesto for the 1992 general election stated our intention to restore our heritage and to promote projects that would become a source of national pride. That is precisely what happened.

On Second Reading, my right hon. Friend the Member for Huntingdon (Mr. Major) said that those areas on which the lottery concentrates--and the umbrella which the former Department of National Heritage had provided--constituted a recognition that certain areas of national life would remain under-resourced, regardless of which Government were in office. Arts, sport and heritage are inevitably pushed to the back of the Treasury spending queue, because other areas are seen to be more important in political terms.

When the national lottery was devised, a clear divide was made between core Government responsibilities that should be paid for by the taxpayer, and those that the lottery should pay for according to the principle of additionality.

The Bill proposes schemes that will be under the aegis of the New Opportunities Fund. Those schemes include information technology training for teachers and librarians, and the provision of healthy living centres. However, such schemes should be paid for by general taxation, because they relate to education, health and the extension of after-hours school activities.

The Bill threatens the principle of additionality that was encapsulated in the original legislation and which lies at the very heart of the national lottery. What might happen in future years if the Government establish this precedent? It could prove to be the thin end of the wedge. Increasing amounts of money may be diverted into health and education--far more than is implied in the current proposals and in the draft regulations that we received last night.

The Bill gives additional powers of direction to the Secretary of State. Once again, that is a departure from the founding principles of the national lottery. The Secretary of State would have the power to divert money into pet projects and fill breaches in core Government spending in order to move further towards what the Government would regard as a politically desirable agenda.

I alluded earlier to the Green Paper on national childcare strategy which is soon to be published. As I understand it, out-of-school activities are to be a key element in that strategy. If that is so, it underlines my point that the relationship between core expenditure--driven by the Treasury and carried out by the Department for Education and Employment--is being linked unacceptably to the national lottery.

I turn now to this country's system of teacher training for information technology. We should be proud of one or two aspects of that. An extraordinary expansion of information technology provision has occurred in our primary schools. More computer and information technology equipment is available in the United Kingdom's primary schools than in those of any other European Union country. That is wholly desirable, because computer literacy is crucial if we are to have a successfully functioning economy. There is a rising generation of young people who are comfortable with information technology, and teachers should be trained to deal with that. As the father of relatively young children, I know that there is generation gap between my ability to grapple with the new technology and their ability--they are entirely comfortable with it. The Government should take responsibility for reacting to these developments.

Mr. Spring: That is exactly the point. My hon. Friend is entirely correct to point out that that precedent has already been established.

The huge expansion in information technology can proceed only if teachers are adequately trained to deal with it. The issue at stake is where the money for that is to come from.
