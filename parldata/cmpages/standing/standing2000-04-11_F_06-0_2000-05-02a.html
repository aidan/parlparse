The provision is precisely what it seems. It is a straightforward, positive, statutory duty for the Learning and Skills Council to encourage individuals to participate in learning. I had hoped that Opposition Members would welcome such a simple, straightforward and clear clause, and would recognise that such encouragement is vital to meeting the challenges that lie ahead. Mr. Wills: We are conscious of the needs of businesses. I assure the hon. Gentleman that the interests of small and medium-sized companies are at the forefront of our mind in any proposed legislation. I hope that I can shed further light on why the amendment is not necessary.

It almost goes without saying, given that it has been the policy of successive Governments for a considerable period, that employers should be required to make an appropriate contribution-in some cases, 100 per cent.-to the costs of training from which they benefit directly. That already happens in relation, for example, to further education colleges that provide dedicated training courses for employers. The Further Education Funding Council has assumed, depending on the circumstances, that employers will meet a high proportion of the costs, and, in many instances, the full cost. Training and enterprise councils have combined the resources of the public purse and those of private employers in running work-based learning programmes to great public benefit and the benefit of individual employers.

There is nothing novel in the proposition that employers should meet the cost of training when they benefit directly; indeed, doing so undoubtedly helps to ensure that training meets their commercial requirements. Similarly, there is nothing novel in the idea that the state should be prepared to contribute to the costs of training in transferable skills and portable qualifications, which help labour markets to function efficiently. Again, that is common ground between the parties.

In a debate about employer contributions, it is important to distinguish between the arrangements for work-based training for young people and those for other job-related training. When young people are training on advanced or foundation modern apprenticeship schemes, it is right that the state should pay for the costs of obtaining those qualifications, as it would if they were studying for A-levels or vocational A-levels. The requirement on the employer in relation to such schemes is to meet costs such as employees' salaries and the opportunity costs of trainees taking time off to train. That is different from job-related training for other workers. Further proposals for the new funding system will be set out in an additional consultation document, which we intend to issue shortly. I hope that that will reassure the hon. Gentleman.

We have already listened and will continue to listen to views on the extent to which local LSCs should have scope to adjust contributions to the cost of provision in accordance with individual and local circumstances. Five detailed proposals involved in the next consultation are of particular relevance to the issue, and I hope that it will reassure the hon. Gentleman if I briefly detail them.

The first proposal is that the state should carry all costs relating to the achievement of qualifications for work-based training for young people, with the exception of salaries and employers' opportunity costs. The second is that employers should be responsible for a bigger investment in other job-related training for their employees. The third is that employers should pay the full costs of dedicated training for their employees, with the exception of work-based training for young people. The fourth is that, if the LSC contributes, providers should charge employee contributions at rates that the LSC sets. The final proposal is that the state should be prepared to fund the assessment costs of employee training that would increase the number of people with transferable qualifications. Those are the proposals on which we are consulting.

If an employer and a training provider make an agreement between themselves about employee training for which the employer will pay in full, there is no reason to involve the LSC. However, if the LSC contributes to the cost of training that raises the question whether it should stipulate the amount to be collected by the provider, or whether that should be left as a matter between the provider and the employer. We shall invite views on the issue in the consultation paper, which we shall publish shortly.

I hope that hon. Members will agree that those are balanced consultation proposals.
