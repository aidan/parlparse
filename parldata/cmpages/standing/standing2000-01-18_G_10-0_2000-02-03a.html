Mr. Stunell: I aplogise to the Committee both for my shortness of breath and for the delay to our proceedings.

The amendments would ensure that those who have to make returns must surmount a realistic set of hurdles before they are deemed to have committed an offence. They should be taken in the context not only of subsections (1) and (2), but of subsection (3), which says that a person commits an offence if he fails to fulfil the requirements set out in subsections (1) and (2). The first requirement relates to the length of time before an item of expenditure is notified to the party treasurer and the second to the period of time before a claim must be settled. The Government have tabled an amendment that deals with one of those points, and which to some extent makes my case for me.

The amendments seek to differentiate between the different circumstances in which returns are required. The first is where campaign expenditure has been incurred at a national level as part of the run-up to an election and the second is national campaign expenditure that has been incurred during an election period. I have sought to differentiate between the timetable of the first and the second, so that during an election period national reporting phases in with the reporting of local expenditure.

In any case, the 12-day notification followed by 30 days seems exceptionally short, bearing in mind that failure to submit an expense in that period leads to an automatic offence. That offence can be relieved, but only by going to the High Court or a county court. Therefore, if somebody is on holiday, a cheque goes astray in the post or an invoice or receipt is mislaid, as the hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) has said, it is not simply a question of phoning somebody up and saying, "Send another one", the clock is ticking away on an extremely short timescale. I accept, the Minister's point that one would expect there to be a difference in scale and efficiency between the treasurer of a major national party and poor old Mrs. Brown doing her best in a local election campaign. Even so--and again I draw on the words of the hon. Member for Beaconsfield--not all national parties have huge substance and professionalism. I think that it was the hon. Member for Battersea (Mr. Linton) who suggested that the Liberal Democrats might be running the average primary school. I said that it might be nearer to a secondary school.

I do not make this plea on behalf of the national treasurer of the Liberal Democrat party in regard to dealing with matters once they have come to his or her attention, but the information supplied may sometimes not be of the highest quality, nor may the time in which it is submitted be sufficient. Even the most centrally directed, authoritarian and efficient party must sometimes pass messages down the line to tell people to buck up and make an effort. In the middle of an election campaign that could easily absorb 21 days, or at least the time left when one realises that 21 days have nearly expired.

As the penalty for misreporting is as severe as the penalty for not reporting at all, there is no way out of the problem. A national treasurer who received a report that a certain part of the campaign had cost £1,000 might ask for the invoice and receive the reply, "It's all right. I've got the invoice in front of me. It is only for £920." He would then have to say, "That's not good enough. Send it in. "He could not say, "We could put £1,000 in the return and we would know we were covered", as submitting an inaccurate return is just as much an offence as putting in no return.

Therefore, we need some breathing space for these things to be worked through properly in the ordinary course of events in a national campaign outside of an election period. Even if the Parliamentary Secretary is not inclined to accept that argument, which I believe is strong, I hope that he will bear in mind that, during an election campaign, the timetable would not be in synchronisation with the timetable of local or general election constituency campaign returns.

During an election there is not only a national campaign, when the expenditure requirements set out in the clause are operational, but also a general election campaign with many local constituency parties taking part in elections. Again it depends on how the party incurs expenditure, but I draw the Committee's attention again to the wise words of the hon. Member for Battersea. He said that in North Dorset there could be posters saying, "Vote Walker" in the local election campaign and posters that say "Vote Conservative" in the national campaign. Depending on the parties' procedures, they may be paid for separately, or by settling a bill that has to be apportioned. If there are two timetables for reporting that expenditure, the national treasurer's task will become almost impossible. One might think, other things being equal, that the national party would have longer to put its act together than the local party, but, as the timetable is set out in the clause, the national party has less time than the local party agent, and he or she is even more remote from the action than those at the local level, so there seems to be something wrong there. The Government's amendment may go some way towards acknowledging that there is a problem, but the problem must be dealt with specifically.

Subsection (1) refers to

    "the period of 21 days beginning with the date on which the expenditure was incurred"

and subsection (2) states that the 30 days begins with the date mentioned in subsection (1). It seems that the 30 days runs from the date on which the expenditure was incurred. It is not an additional 30 days from the time when the information reached the treasurer. I do not think that I am misreading that. My amendments make some effort to deal with that problem.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Privy Council Office (Mr. Paddy Tipping): The hon. Gentleman is correct. The 30 days runs from the date on which the expenditure was incurred and his amendment No. 58 would extend the period to 42 days. He has recognised the Govemment's amendment and is pushing an open door.

Clause 70 places a series of fetters and restrictions on the ability of parties involved in campaign expenditure to make, conclude and then sue under or obtain benefit on a contract. It is a serious infringement of what would normally be described as private rights. My concern is directed not so much at the political parties, although I am concerned about them, but at the poor commercial enterprises that supply the services.

Let us consider the clause in detail. It may seem innocuous, but it states that a claim for payment will

    "not be payable if the claim is not sent, together with the relevant invoice,"

within 21 days. We live in the real world. Some businesses send their invoices within 21 days, but we all know from our experience of being invoiced for services that it is often more than 21 days before an invoice is received. Perhaps such people should not be involved in commercial activities; perhaps they are not acting in their own best interests; they may have trouble with their paperwork if they do not have someone to do it and they are too busy to settle down and deal with it. Those people will be under an obligation to submit their invoices within 21 days if they wish to be paid. The clause is unreasonable in principle and, as the hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell) said, the time allowed is much too short.

I am mindful that the Minister will explain the background principles and I understand that we do not want the payment of invoices and claims that may arise on them to take so long that a party cannot make a full return on its campaign expenditure.

I seek clarification on the clause because, if political parties may dispute claims in such a way as to avoid the clause's provisions, what is to prevent an unscrupulous party that wishes to muddy the waters of its election campaign expenditure from choosing to query many of the bills that it receives? The Parliamentary Secretary said that collusion was one of the mischiefs at which the proposals were aimed. No collusion is easier than for two willing parties to spin out legal proceedings. I oppose the clause because of the abuses that it is likely to cause, but I look forward to the Parliamentary Secretary's response.
