Social Security Bill

Clause 18

Finality of decisions

Amendment proposed [30 October]: No. 136, in page 12, leave out lines 10 to 12 and insert

    `(2) Except as provided in subsection (3) below, subsection (1) above shall not make any finding of fact or other determination embodied in or necessary to a decision, or on which is based, conclusive for the purpose of any further decision.

(3) Regulations shall provide a determination that a person is capable or incapable of work for the purpose of one relevant benefit shall be conclusive for the purpose of his entitlement to another relevant benefit.

(4) For the purpose of subsection (3) above, ``relevant benefit'' shall have the same meaning as for section 9 above.'.--[Mr. Webb.]

10.30 am

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Keith Bradley): Thank you, Mr. Gale, and welcome to the Committee. I am sure that, under your expert guidance, we shall make the progress that I know everyone wants to make in our deliberations on the Bill.

On amendment No. 136, clause 18 re-enacts section 60(1) of the Social Security Administration Act 1992. It ensures that, as now, decisions that are made by the Secretary of State will be final unless they are specifically revised or superseded under the provisions of clauses 10 or 11 or appealed under clause 13. It also allows regulations to be made to specify cases in which findings of fact or other determinations which are the component parts of a decision are to be made final for other purposes. We do not intend to regulate as yet for the majority of such findings and determinations to be final--only the incapacity for work test will be final for all benefits.

The amendment would prevent findings of fact, other than findings of incapacity for work, from being conclusive for the purposes of other decisions. I should explain that we intend to retain provisions in regulations that findings or determinations on incapacity for work--such as the all work test--should be conclusive for the purposes of subsequent decisions.

The hon. Member for Northavon (Mr. Webb) raised concerns about different rules applying to claims for jobseeker's benefit and incapacity benefits, but I want to make it clear that the problems that he mentioned should not arise. If he becomes aware of specific cases in which they do, however, I would of course be happy for him to write to me with any information. I would then ensure that the matters were properly investigated.

Similarly, the hon. Gentleman raised the issue of the habitual residence test. Clearly, if someone fails the test, he will not be entitled to those provisions that relate to it at the point of the claim. That does not, however, apply to all benefits. Obviously, the person can make a fresh claim for benefits once he believes that he has established the right to abode in this country. Such a claim would be treated as a fresh claim and the decision on it would flow to other benefits. There should not be the confusion that the hon. Gentleman suggested.

There are, of course, other examples of findings or determinations that are made in one context, which it would be desirable to make final for other purposes. The information technology is, however, not in place to enable the necessary read-across between the different benefits and business areas with which the Department deals. At the moment, such a read-across could be achieved only with disproportionate effort on the part of the Department or the public. As technological developments occur, however, we expect to be able to read across far more often, which will enable us to collect information from customers only once.

A further effect of the amendment would be to limit the scope of finality to relevant benefits only. The Government would, in due course, prefer findings to be made final for the purpose of all relevant decisions. The Government want to build in flexibility and do not want such requirements to be included in the Bill. The amendment would prevent us from responding to technological advances and making further improvements for customers. I therefore hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Steve Webb (Northavon): I am reassured by the Minister's saying that the absurdity which we discussed on Thursday--people being transferred from one benefit to another and finding that they fall between all stools--is being dealt with and that the proposed regulations will explicitly provide that incapacity for work should be treated as final. I still have concerns about the implications of some other decisions being made final, but they are insufficient to make me force a vote. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 4

Unified appeal tribunals

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

 

Mr. Keith Bradley: The clause introduces the first of the Bill's provisions relating to the new arrangements for appeal, and brings together the functions of all the current tribunal jurisdictions under a new tribunal system. More specifically, the clause provides for the transfer of the functions of social security, medical, disability, child support and vaccine damage appeal tribunals to appeal tribunals constituted under clauses 6 to 8.

My right hon. Friend the Secretary of State made it clear on Second Reading that she will assume personal responsibility for the administration of appeals. She has made a commitment to set and publish demanding targets for administration and to report on the results. We have decided in principle to establish a new DSS executive agency to administer appeals. That will be the best way to meet out objectives of improving service and accountability for performance. Agency status would enable the service to operate at arm's length from Ministers. It would provide scope for greater accountability in terms of expenditure, performance and a better service for customers.

The Child Poverty Action Group and the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux have told me that they consider that public confidence would be enhanced by the establishment of a new executive agency to administer appeals. I share their view.

Agency status would enable the Secretary of State to set objectives for the service and demanding targets for administration. The chief executive could be required to report regularly to Ministers to ensure the efficient running of the service. Importantly, the chief executive could be appointed accounting officer, directly responsible to Parliament for the expenditure and efficiency of the service.

Setting up a new agency would enable appeals administration to be properly focused, with no competing demands. It would provide the potential for a close working relationship with the judiciary which the Council on Tribunals considers essential to an effective and efficient service.

This approach is consistent with the trend in other appeal systems. For example, pensions appeal tribunals are administered by the Court Service Agency, and industrial tribunals and employment appeal tribunals are administered by the Employment Tribunals Service, which is an agency of the Department of Trade and Industry. For those reasons, we have it in mind to set up a new DSS executive agency for the sole purpose of administering appeals.

The clause is a key element in taking a new and more sensible approach to the constitution of appeal tribunals--to enable a new, unified appeals service to deal with the wide range of issues that arise under social security, child support and vaccine damage legislation. That new approach will not only ensure that the highest level of decision making is continued but will introduce a more streamlined system for constituting appeal tribunals under the ambit of one single, uniform piece of legislation.

I therefore commend the clause to the Committee.
