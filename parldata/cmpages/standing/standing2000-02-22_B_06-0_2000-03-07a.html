Postal Services Bill

Clause 2

The Consumer Council for Postal Services

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson): Clause 2 creates a new consumer body, to be known as the Consumer Council for Postal Services, which will represent the interests of consumers of the universal postal service. It replaces the Post Office Users National Council and the three national councils for Northern Ireland, Scotland and Wales that were established under section 14 of the Post Office Act 1969, and which the Bill abolishes. The clause gives effect to schedule 2, which contains detailed provisions relating to the membership and consultation of the council.

Mr. Richard Page (South-West Hertfordshire): As the Minister says, the clause creates the son or daughter of POUNC. I must confess that I feel a little sorrow that we shall witness the end of a clear acronym. Many a business, having spent tens of thousands of pounds establishing the brand, would be most reluctant to see it go and be replaced by the CCPS, which sounds like a subset of the old Soviet Union.

Much of the detail is specified in schedule 2. We shall want to probe the status and independence of the new organisation. The Minister will recall that in discussing clause 1 and schedule 1 he went out of his way to explain how independent the regulator will be, but when we examined clauses 32 and 33, we found phrases such as, "the Secretary of State may give directions", so we shall be most interested to examine related provisions later in the Bill.

It took a little time to drag out of the Minister the estimated cost of the new commission and the regulator. I hope that he will be able to say a little sooner how much it will cost to run the new consumer council. Schedule 2 will give us the opportunity to probe the details.

Mr. Johnson: We appreciate all the work that that splendid body POUNC has done over the years. The name had to change, because we are no longer dealing only with the Post Office but with the regulator, the new consumer body and universal service obligation providers, which may not always mean the Post Office, although initially it probably will. The estimated cost did not have to be dragged out of me; clearer questions would have been clearly answered in the previous debate. The estimated annual cost of the CCPS is between £2 million and £2.5 million.

Question put and agreed to.

Mr. Breed: Again, these are modest amendments, the purpose of which is to ensure as much transparency and information as possible, so that the provision is not caught between two tests. Under the amendments, there would be only one test of whether information should be made available namely, if it did not "seriously prejudice" the interests of the affected person. That would provide greater transparency. In dealing with regulators, a fair bit of information has sometimes been caught by the problem of commercial confidence. We need transparency because information has not always been easy to obtain. It would be wrong for people to be seriously prejudiced and we want to ensure that they are not, but where there is no danger of that, we want it to be clear that information should be available, especially to the consumer groups that will no doubt make representations to the council. Our modest amendments are designed to reduce the two tests to one, and to make it clear that such information ought to be available unless that test is seriously prejudicial to anyone's interests.

Mr. Alan Johnson: The important word is "might". The amendment would unnecessarily and disadvantageously widen the council's powers to disclose information. The council may not disclose information if it

    considers that its disclosure would or might seriously and prejudicially affect the interests of the person to whom it relates.

    The hon. Gentleman clearly thinks that that test of the council's power to disclose information is too restrictive, so he wants to remove the word "might", but the wording is necessary for two reasons. First, it sets a proper balance between the individual's rights as opposed to the council's rights to disclose information. Secondly, it allows the council to do its job properly. Greater powers to disclose information, as the amendment proposes, could lead to mistrust between the council and some people whom it attempts to represent. It could mean that the council has greater difficulties in acquiring information.

    We need to consider what the effect of the amendment would be. It could do a lot of harm if the council could disclose information that might seriously and prejudicially affect a person's interests, as the amendment would allow. It could lead to the disclosure of information that is commercially sensitive or relates to someone's business affairs. For example, it could seriously disadvantage the person to whom the information relates if it were disclosed to other USO providers or licence holders.

    We have to strike a careful balance between the needs of the council to disclose information as opposed to the needs of the person to whom the information relates. Too great a power to disclose information may lead to the council being mistrusted by some of the people with whom it has to work, such as postal operators. The council may find it more difficult to acquire information in the first place if people feel that they cannot trust the council not to disclose it.

    We must also remember that the council has the power in the clause to decide for itself--the wording is "if the Council considers"--whether the disclosure of information would or might seriously and prejudicially affect the person's interest. I believe that that gives the council enough discretion over what to disclose. Any more power could be damaging to people to whose affairs the information relates and to the council.

    We also think that the wording currently used is easier to understand. It is easier to assess whether someone's interests are "seriously and prejudicially affected" than whether they are seriously prejudiced.

    For those reasons, I ask the hon. Member for South-East Cornwall to withdraw the amendment. If he does not, I ask the Committee to reject it.

Mr. Breed: Where the balance of interests lies is a matter of judgment. Between regulators, the competition commission and the Office of Fair Trading, there is inevitably experience of dealing with people who are asked to provide information. Before information is provided, it is often agreed that it will not be disclosed. I accept that the use of the little word "might" is an attempt to predict something that might be a long way ahead, so the tendency is to say that information might prejudice someone's interests, and if it might, it will not be disclosed. Almost any information might at some stage be prejudicial to someone's interests, or may be seriously prejudicial.

The amendment was an attempt to tip the balance slightly in favour of those organisations genuinely seeking to obtain information. If commercially confidential information is requested by the council, agreement is often reached before its receipt so that people know straight away that it will not be disclosed. Therefore, mistrust would not often be relevant. Information could be genuinely available to consumer organisations that would help them present cases in the most effective way, and we wanted to prevent the council, the regulator or anyone else from forming the habit of hiding behind wording that protects them by saying that information might at some stage be seriously prejudicial. They would not know whether it might be, but they would not make it available for that reason.

I think that we have made our point and that the Minister will be glad to hear that I shall not press the amendment. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
