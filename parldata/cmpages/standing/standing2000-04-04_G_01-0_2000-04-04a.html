It outlines the service's raison d'être. Mr. Lidington: I beg to move amendment No. 29, in page 2, line 10, after `proper', insert

    `and effective'.

The amendment would alter clause 2(2)(c). The Bill defines one of the aims of the National Probation Service as

    the proper punishment of offenders.

Amendment No. 29 would amend that to

    the proper and effective punishment of offenders.

I tabled the amendment, to draw out a little more of the Government's thinking on how they intend to ensure that community penalties, as administered by the new National Probation Service, will command greater public confidence in future—something that would be good in its own right—and will be more effective in dissuading those people who have been placed on probation from resuming a criminal career.

The Minister and most hon. Members know that a feature of public belief in the criminal justice system is that almost all community penalties are weak and inadequate—they let evildoers get away with crime and fail to give proper punishment or to protect the public against future crimes. That has been a feature of public opinion for as long as I have been following such matters, which was before my election to the House of Commons in 1992.

If the Bill and subsequent policy initiatives allow the Government to build on previous measures, such as the creation of national standards for probation, and produce greater public confidence, that would be welcome, but the lack of public confidence in community penalties cannot simply be attributed to misinformation or our constituents' naivety, nor can it be written off, as many politicians are tempted to do, as the fault of too many people reading too many tabloid newspapers. We know that the unjust or inadequate sentence makes the best story and is headline grabbing. The case of the offender who breaches the terms of his probation or other non-custodial order and who is allowed to run riot by the authorities without adequate redress, is the incident in the criminal justice system that probably arouses the greatest ire among our constituents.

It is not simply a matter of the public failing to understand what is going on or the press sensationalising untypical examples. There is prima facie evidence that all too often the system of non-custodial penalties has failed to do its job of protecting the public or punishing adequately those who have committed crimes. I draw the Minister's attention to last year's research by the Criminal Justice Association. Its chairman is Mr. Peter Coad, a former senior probation officer, and it numbers among its patrons those champions of effective criminal justice policy, my right hon. Friend the Member for Penrith and The Border (Mr. Maclean) and the noble Lord Mackenzie of Framwellgate—two distinguished names with a proven track record of concern for the victims of crime and for effective criminal justice policies.

The Criminal Justice Association analysed Home Office figures for 1994 on the conviction of offenders and the application of community penalties, and examined the 1997 prison statistics for England and Wales to get an idea of the likelihood of people who had been subject to community penalties reoffending. It concluded that in 1994 roughly one third of people under 21 who were placed on probation already had between three and six previous convictions. When they examined the 1997 statistics, the current statistics for England and Wales, they found that no fewer than 80 per cent. of people who had been placed on probation after three to six previous convictions were then re-convicted within two years of completing their probation order. These two sets of figures relate to different years and contain information that is between three and six years out of date. They do not represent the whole story, and are only a snapshot of the problem, not a thorough analysis.

I ask the Minister to consider with his Home Office officials the collection of the appropriate statistics on the new non-custodial measures and some rigorous research on them so that we can assess their effectiveness in protecting the public and providing adequate punishment. The concern that I and many others have is that those who are given non-custodial penalties are almost by definition those most likely to be amenable to rehabilitation; who have not become so habituated to a criminal life, that good supervision and appropriate counselling and mentoring will not help them to lead a law-abiding life. A layman would assume that somebody who is sentenced to a period of imprisonment by a court will already have had previous dealings with the police and have received many cautions and non-custodial penalties before the criminal justice system finally lost its patience with them and decided that prison was the only alternative.

The key to rehabilitation, whether one is talking about non-custodial penalties or prison regimes, is the readiness of the criminal to co-operate with those who are offering him rehabilitation and a way forward. I vividly remember a conversation that I had with a serious offender in Grendon prison, who could serve as an example of the difficulty of rehabilitation both in terms of custodial and non-custodial sentencing. The man said that he had benefited hugely from the therapeutic regime at Grendon and that he was determined when he was released on life licence that he would try to lead a law-abiding life. He also said to me that if that regime had been offered to him five years previously, let alone 10 years previously, it would have made no difference to him, because he had not realised until recently that he was making a complete mess of his life and was wrecking any prospect that he had of making a reasonable future for himself by his persistent criminal behaviour. For probation to work, the offender has to recognise that what he has done is wrong and must be determined to co-operate with those who are offering a way forward. That is when he stands a chance of making progress.
