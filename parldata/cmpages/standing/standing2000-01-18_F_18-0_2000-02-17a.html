Clause 33

Member-nominated trustees

Mr. Rooker: I beg to move amendment No. 269, in page 31, line 12, at end insert—

    `( ) In subsection (8) (persons ceasing to be member-nominated trustees on ceasing to be qualifying members of the scheme)—

    (a) for ``The arrangements must'' there shall be substituted ``The arrangements—

    (a) must''; and

    (b) at the end there shall be inserted ``; and

    (b) may provide for a member-nominated trustee who—

    (i) is a qualifying member of one of the following descriptions, that is to say, an active, deferred or pensioner member, and

    (ii) ceases (without ceasing to be a qualifying member) to be a qualifying member of that description,

    to cease, by virtue of that fact, to be a trustee.''.'.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss Government amendment No. 271.

Mr. Rooker: Clauses 33 to 36 make changes to the Pensions Act 1995 which govern the appointment of member-nominated trustees and directors to the boards of occupational pension schemes. Amendments Nos. 269 and 270 amend clauses 33 and 34. The amendments make the same change; one of the clauses operates in respect of member-nominated trustees, the other in respect of corporate trustees. The amendments correct an accidental omission For convenience, I shall refer only to member-nominated trustees, for everything will apply equally to member-nominated directors, who are the subject of clause 34.

The amendment will allow nomination and selection arrangements to include a provision for member-nominated trustees to stand down automatically if they change the category of membership. If an active member leaves to take up employment elsewhere and becomes a deferred member, the individual will be able to cease to be a trustee.

The power is permissive. Such a rule will not be adopted without a conscious decision by the trustee or the employer. If such a rule is adopted, a trustee required to stand down will, of course, have the opportunity to stand for reselection, and the members may decide whether they want the person to serve as a member-nominated trustee. Such people will still be members of a scheme, although their category has changed. It is reasonable to give the membership the opportunity to decide whether they want a deferred member who was originally nominated when an active member to continue, or whether they want to nominate someone else.

Without the amendments, we will not give full effect to the proposals in the consultation paper issued on 1 October. They were developed in consultation with key organisations in the pensions industry, which fully support them. As I said, they correct an accidental omission.

Amendment agreed to.

Mr. Rooker: I beg to move amendment No. 270, in page 31, line 18, after `which' insert—

    `, and the time within which,'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider Government amendment No. 272.

Mr. Rooker: The amendments make the same changes as the previous two amendments considered and also apply to clauses 33 and 34. One deals with member-nominated trustees, the other with member-nominated directors. For convenience I shall refer only to member-nominated trustees.

The amendments correct an oversight in the drafting of the clauses. An equivalent regulation-making power is contained in section 21(4)(b) of the Pensions Act 1995, under which regulations are in force. We always intended to carry that power forward to the new provisions. As it stands, the Bill removes section 21(4)(b) from the 1995 Act without making similar provision elsewhere. The amendments restore that provision.

The amendments enable us to continue to provide a time limit within which member-nominated trustees must be nominated and selected in accordance with the arrangements. The regulations will prescribe a time limit of six months for the nomination and selection of member-nominated trustees. That is the current limit, so there is nothing new in the amendments. Not only will members have to make the arrangements, but they will have to put them into operation. Without the amendments, there would be a small loophole in the Bill. We do not want to allow such loopholes to remain.

Amendment agreed to.

The amendment deals with the provision of training for trustees who require it. I remember listening to long discussions about the training of trustees during consideration of the Bill that became the Pensions Act 1995. Anybody who has had experience of occupational pension funds will know how heavy, difficult and often bewildering the rules, regulations and legal responsibilities are for the trustee of a pension fund. Although many trustees are well equipped, those who come from the members will often need more help. The issue has been circulating for a long time, and some responsible employers and many trade unions have provided training. I am happy to learn from the experts on the Labour Benches, but I understand that there has been a noticeable diminution in the amount of training that trade unions can provide, largely because of their declining membership. Not only will that deter willing volunteers who are unaware of the relevant issues from becoming trustees; it will discourage people from volunteering at all. A large number of trusts will have to be created under the stakeholder pension scheme envisaged by the Government, so there will be a huge demand for people who are willing to serve as volunteers.

The question of who should pay for training has always been problematic. As I have said, companies will sometimes pay for it, especially well funded, responsible companies. Trade unions will sometimes pay for it and some members may take the matter sufficiently seriously to fund it themselves. It occurred to us that the best way to fund training would be to pay for it out of the expenses of the pension fund.

I am well aware that the primary duty of trustees, and indeed anyone involved with the management of pensioners' and potential pensioners' money, is to produce the best possible return on the money invested and to ensure that money is put into a wide range of investments, including gilts, equities and property portfolios, in a sensible, well run fund. The costs of fund managers obviously fall on the pension fund, and it occurred to me that, in ensuring that pension funds are as well run as possible, where necessary, the training of trustees should be paid for out of the pension fund, but without diluting the return on the investment.

Mr. Rooker: I want to say a few words on clause 33. One of the problems of considering a Bill, line by line, is that there is no provision for a Minister to make introductory remarks on a group of clauses before the Committee discusses amendments. What I am about to say in this clause stand part debate applies to a group of four clauses. Government amendments Nos. 273, 274, 275 and 288 are minor amendments. I am concerned that we should not agree to clauses 34, 35 and 36 without saying something about their contents, other than my two sentence response to the hon. Member for Gainsborough (Mr. Leigh).

Clauses 33, 34, 35 and 36 form a group of clauses that ensure that every occupational pension scheme has at least one third member-nominated trustees, or member-nominated directors if the trustee is a company. The Government believe that member-nominated trustees add value to any trustee board. Member trustees contribute their different perspectives and experiences, and help to give members confidence in their scheme and a sense of ownership. The value of member trustees is also recognised by most pension industry organisations, and many well run schemes have had ordinary members on their trustee boards for donkey's years.

The existing provisions in sections 16 to 21 of the Pensions Act 1995 have worked well, but not as well as we would like. The current provisions enable the employer to propose his own trustee arrangements, although they cannot be implemented without the approval of the members. We have been told of cases in which an employer has used that freedom to avoid including member trustees on the board. People argue that the current legislation is too complex and difficult to operate. Another regular complaint is that it is too easy to exclude pensioner members from the process. Members of the pension fund will fall into three broad categories: active members who are paying in, deferred members who have left the company and pensioner members who are receiving their pensions.

We have listened and responded to those concerns, and have conducted two rounds of consultation. Under the new provisions, every scheme will have member-nominated trustees or directors. Some schemes, including statutory ones, are exempt, but that is another matter for another debate; we are talking about private-sector occupational schemes. Employers will still have the right to propose bespoke nominations and selection arrangements for their schemes, but their proposals must provide for a minimum of one third member-nominated trustees and be approved by the members. There will be no opportunity to opt out.

The clause deals with cases in which the employer does not make a proposal or fails to gain the approval of members. For convenience, I shall refer to that as the trustee route.

 

Mr. Leigh: I hope that the Minister will forgive my lack of knowledge on the subject, but I must say that I am still concerned about member-nominated trustees. Will he briefly explain how such trustees will be nominated and elected?

Mr. Rooker: Yes, I shall come to that. Under current provisions, when the employer has not made alternative arrangements, trustees can either propose their own nomination and selection arrangements or use rules set out in regulations. If the trustees choose to make their own rules, they must put them to the scheme members—who will fall into the three categories to which I have just referred—for approval. Under the amended section 16 of the Pensions Act 1995, trustees will implement nomination and selection procedures using the framework set out in regulations. There will be no need for member approval, so the process will be cheaper and simpler.

Regulations made under amended section 16(9) of the 1995 Act will provide a framework within which trustees will have flexibility over certain aspects of the nomination and selection. For example, they will be given discretion to divide the membership into constituencies if they so choose. There will also be limitations. For example, all the active and pensioner members must be given the opportunity to make nominations. The members who stand could be divided into constituencies according to their category: whether active, deferred or pensioner. Members of all those categories must be free to stand for election.

If a selection panel is used, it should have a majority of scheme members. The Government's proposals further strengthen the legislative framework. This may be the appropriate moment to deal with the point made by the hon. Member for Gainsborough. One of the other clauses that we have amended would allow bodies such as trade unions or pension organisations to nominate trustees. That widens the process, although the trustees would obviously be members of the scheme.

There was a campaign for designated separate seats on the board for pensioner trustees. We have not adopted that proposal, although it is seductive and recalls to mind the case of the Lucas pensioners in my constituency, who fought their case over a surplus. We do not want such problems. Trustees are not representatives, and it does not matter how they arrive as trustees. Once people are trustees they are bound by the duties of trustees, which is crucial. All categories of pensioner will none the less be able to stand for election. We have worked with the pension industry to produce changes to give members confidence in their schemes. By and large, those changes have received widespread support. They will make it easier to make a reality of the stakeholder schemes when the legislation flows.

My remarks on clause 33 apply equally to clause 34, although that deals with member-nominated directors rather than with trustees. The member directors will be elected by the same process.

Mr. Leigh: Will there be a postal ballot for the different groups?

Mr. Rooker: There will have to be ballot arrangements. I do not know the detail, but I am aware of postal ballots in constituencies, although I have not been involved in any. Postal ballots can be expensive—one must keep track of deferred members, especially—although it will usually be known that active members are paying and where pensioner members are. Deferred members might have moved companies three times; their pensions will be frozen or deferred, but they will be spread to the four corners of the country—or the planet—and arrangements for them will be more difficult.

There must be proper ballot arrangements. All members must know that they have the opportunity to vote and to be nominated. There must be genuine communication from the trustees when nominations and elections occur. Extra work for the Post Office will be a natural consequence. Everything that I have said will apply during clause 34 stand part—members will be involved in the same way in choosing directors, albeit of a different category. That is why I do not propose to say anything during that debate.
