This important amendment limits the scope for pension splitting and for pension sharing to the cases in which it is necessary to use that route. There are several reasons why we should be reluctant to use that route. I mentioned the first in another context this morning--namely, the fact that pension sharing and pension splitting involve additional costs. In other words, the assets to be split will be reduced by the simple fact of splitting them. That problem is specific to pension sharing and pension splitting. If one gives a person a house or one assigns them £1,000 a week, that does not reduce the value of the house or the sum of money, but if one splits a pension, costs are involved and the amount to be split will fall. It follows that that approach should be adopted only when it is absolutely necessary.

The second consideration--I feel no embarrassment in raising this, although I am not sure whether the Government share my view--is the fact that there is something special about pension assets. They often represent the fruits of a working lifetime, in which a person has saved and provided for his pension. We recognise that fact in our legal system, which gives tax advantages to pensions. The Government and the Opposition were at one in providing additional protections for pension assets against bankruptcy. In a sense, our legal system already recognises the fact that pension assets are rather special and we should be reluctant to deprive a person who has earned them of their benefits.

Mr. Pointer suggested that Parliament should make the decision on pension splitting or sharing. We should decide whether it should be an automatic response of a court charged with the reallocation of assets on divorce; a reserve weapon to be used when it is not possible to make a fair allocation; or a tool to be used in special circumstances or under the general criteria in section 25 of the Matrimonial Causes Act 1973, which require it to be considered as a means of fulfilling courts, obligations.

It would be remiss of Parliament not to respond to Mr. Pointer's invitation and that is the purpose of our amendment. It states that pension sharing should not occur unless the court

    "is satisfied that it cannot achieve a just redistribution of the parties' resources by using its other powers under section 21".

Section 21 sets out the powers of divorce courts to make orders for periodical payments, lump sums or the split of property assets.

The amendment goes to the heart of a chief--if not the essential--difference between the Government and the Opposition on pension sharing. The triumphalist, militant feminist approach of the previous Secretary of State, which was displayed in the introductory document to the draft legislation last summer, made it clear that pension sharing should be regarded as a positive gain for humanity. However, we are reluctant to deprive the courts of any flexibility in the sad matter of matrimonial breakdown. We recognise that there may be arguments for adding this pension-sharing weapon to the courts' armoury, but we also recognise that there are sources of unfairness, including perverse incentives and incremental and gratuitous costs, if pension sharing is used to fulfil the obligations of courts to make a fair distribution of assets.
