We have before us a Government redrafting of this most important clause. Our amendments attempt to correct it; we know that the Government share some of our misgivings.

The question must be asked what point was served by publishing clause 19 in its original form. It is grotesquely wide, as we discovered in a previous debate on these matters. Clause 19 is not the only example in the Bill of the Government starting with the intention of trying to catch pretty well everything and slowly, under the impact of external criticism and debate, restricting it. I propose constructively that if the Government can identify in advance other examples of clearly defective clauses, it would save a great deal of the Committee's time.

I repeat that we do not accept that primary legislation is just overall guidance and that the real meat of the Bill lies in secondary legislation; that devalues the Bill. The purpose of a Bill is to set out people's rights and obligations and the possible sanctions that they may face if they breach the law. The Bill will be the source document for many years to come; people want to know, by looking at the Bill, and not at a mass of regulations, exactly what they are expected to do. It is important to define matters closely and correctly in primary legislation.

There is a disappointing development which has partly been corrected by the positive way in which the Government have conceded it and introduced an amendment. However, on first reading, the revised clause still does not appear to do the trick. One subsection is drafted widely and attempts to outlaw communications received on the internet that are

    ``capable of having an effect in the United Kingdom''.

I am sure that we shall have an opportunity to debate the internet more fully later in the Bill, but that phrase represents no change from the clause's original wording.

    ``capable of having an effect in the United Kingdom''

I welcome the fact that the clause now excludes private individuals and will catch only those communications made in the course of business. I will not be an authorised person under the provisions in the Bill, and I have no intention of becoming one. However, the Bill's definition will catch—whether deliberately or otherwise—many communications of a business-like nature that are conducted between people who are not authorised or registered.

It would be absurd if I were not able to offer, as I have in the past, my cottage to hon. Members for a holiday because I might fall foul of clause 19. It might be deemed that I am inviting them to take a right or an interest in something that I own.
