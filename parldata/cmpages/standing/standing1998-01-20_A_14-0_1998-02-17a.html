The key effect of the amendments would be to place local education authorities under a duty to inform the chair of governors, rather than the whole governing body, when there was serious concern about the performance of the head teacher, and to furnish that head teacher with a copy of its report. Under the Bill, the provision is not compatible with the capability procedures for head teachers that are being drafted by many LEAs as a result of the decisions of the Advisory, Conciliation and Arbitration Service working group, which was set up to examine teacher competence. Furthermore, the procedures would not work closely with the competency procedures being developed by the diocesan authorities.

The first stage of the capability procedure is for the chair of the governing body and, usually, the chief education officer to discuss with the head their concerns about his or her performance, to identify areas for improvement, to set targets, and to set a time scale for improvements to be made. Only at the next stage does the governing body become involved typically a personnel sub-committee of the governing body, because it would leave other governors free to hear an appeal without prejudice if dismissal was proposed. The amendments would introduce such a procedure into the Bill in place of the existing one.

I draw the Committee's attention to amendment No. 203. Judging by the Government's proposed amendments, they are unlikely to accept it. Under the Bill, an appeal against a head teacher's dismissal will be heard by a panel consisting of members of the governing body not involved in proposing the dismissal. That is the position under existing legislation for all schools except grant-maintained schools; for the latter, an appeals panel must include an independent member. The requirement for an independent member was introduced some time after GM schools came into being. Presumably, therefore, the introduction of an independent member was based on experience and considered to be sensible.

The appeals panel will effectively be considering a decision made by fellow governors. Members of governing bodies support and respect the corporate nature of their role. They delgate functions to sub-committees and accept their decisions, and work as a team. It could therefore be argued that it would be almost impossible for an appeals panel to overturn the decision of a group of fellow governors, particularly on such a vital matter as the dismissal of a head teacher.

Amendments Nos. 203 and 204 propose the inclusion of an independent member of the appeal panel in all cases, not only in former GM schools, which are to become foundation schools.
