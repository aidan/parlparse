PPP agreements

New clause 39 defines the conditions that must be met for a contract to be defined as a PPP agreement. I will not talk the Committee through all of the subsections of new clause 39, as most of them are self-explanatory, but it may help the Committee if I briefly mention subsections (5) and (6), and new clause 40.

Subsection (5) of new clause 39 stipulates that the contractor undertaking or securing the carrying out of works under a PPP agreement must not be a public sector operator. The PPP is a partnership between the public and private sectors. If the drafting appears odd, I gather that that is because it is simpler to define a public sector operator, and say that it cannot be the other party to a PPP agreement, than to define a private sector operator, and say that it must be the other party to such an agreement. New clause 40 defines what is meant by a public sector operator.

Subsection (6) of new clause 39 will enable London Underground, for example, to give assistance to a PPP contractor where that does not constitute a substantial proportion of the work. We would not want London Underground, for example, to be prohibited from providing various services to a PPP contractor where that clearly made practical and commercial sense for both parties. New clause 42 will define the term "key system assets". Those assets could comprise property, rights or liabilities and could be designated in one of two ways. First, a PPP agreement as originally concluded could list the relevant assets, or describe the categories within which those assets would be designated. Secondly, the contract may provide for assets to be designated by London Transport or TfL after contract award, in accordance with the terms of the PPP agreement or by amendment to it.

We envisage that a large range of assets will be designated as key system assets so that they enjoy the protection afforded by new clause 45. That must be done through the PPP agreement or amendments to it, so that assets cannot be designated without the agreement of the PPP contractor.

New clause 43 will ensure that interested individuals and bodies can find out whether an asset has been designated as a key system asset. I am thinking especially of those that might make financial advances, such as a bank that proposes to lend to a PPP contractor on the security of the assets, or someone wishing to buy equipment from a PPP contractor. If an asset has been so designated, there are significant restrictions on what a PPP contractor may do with it. Those restrictions are set out in new clause 45. It is important that third parties can find out if an asset is so designated so that they do not unknowingly enter into a transaction with a PPP contractor that will be void.

New clause 43, therefore, will require London Transport, and subsequently TfL, to maintain a register of all key system assets, including dates of designation and sufficient information for assets to be identified. The register must be available for inspection by members of the public, free of charge, at all reasonable hours. Anyone inspecting the register may make copies from it.

New clause 44 will empower London Transport, and again, subsequently TfL, to enter into agreements with third parties involving the provision of property or rights for use for, or in connection with, a PPP agreement. Such third-party agreements may designate assets, or provide for their designation, as key system assets as if the agreement were between London Transport or TfL and the PPP contractor.

The purpose of the amendment is to give the same protection to assets that are owned by third parties and used in connection with the Underground PPP as would be given to assets owned directly by the PPP contractor. It is likely that, for example, a PPP contractor might want to lease new trains or other equipment from the manufacturer or a leasing company. In such cases, London Underground would almost certainly want to enter agreements directly with sub-contractors of a PPP contractor, so that it could step in and take over from the PPP contractor at the end of the contract, or if the contract were terminated before its due end date.

New clause 44 permits such agreements, and enables property and rights provided by such key sub-contractors to be designated so that they receive the same protection under new clause 45 as other key system assets.

New clause 45 is intended to ensure that all the assets needed to keep the underground operating are given statutory protection. Ordinarily, a PFI contract would restrict a contractor's ability to sell important assets, or would enable a third party to take security over those assets. Such restrictions could be enforced through the courts. But a PPP is not an ordinary PFI contract, and ensuring the continuity of underground services is of paramount importance. Ms Jackson: 169 is a "peg" clause inserted before the Bill's introduction because detailed consideration of the necessary provisions for those with disabilities had not concluded. We are committed to comprehensive enforceable civil rights for disabled people. We are bringing forward regulations under the Disability Discrimination Act 1995 which will require new trains, buses and newly licensed taxis to be accessible to disabled people.
