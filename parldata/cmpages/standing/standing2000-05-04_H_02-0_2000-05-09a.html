Clause 2

Rates of duty on cider

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): Thank you, and good morning, Mr. Cook.

Clause 2 deals with the rates of duty on cider. We debated beer duty on the Floor of the House and drew attention to the severe problem of smuggling. We also alluded to the strange fact that the indexation rate used by the Government for all such duties was more than 3.4 per cent. We want to concentrate on that figure this morning and find out the purpose behind it. The Chancellor said in his Budget speech that all such duties—not only cider duty but hydrocarbon duty—would increase by the rate of inflation. However, the Government have not claimed that inflation is generally running at a rate of 3.41 per cent., the percentage used to uprate the duties. A stealth tax is in operation, which caused The Daily Telegraph to run the headline ``Brown's `big fiddle' costs £750 million.''

It had calculated immediately after the Budget that the difference between the assumed inflation rate and that used by the Chancellor would net the Treasury an extra £750 million in the coming year. Large sums of money are involved and the matter certainly needs explaining.

I shall explain some of the history of excise duty on alcohol. The previous Government faced the problem of the smuggling of alcoholic products and, although we were running a budget deficit at the time and were short of money, we responded to that problem by freezing the duty on alcohol products in our last two Budgets and cutting it on spirits. When the Labour Government came to power, they ignored the warning signs. Although they talked a lot about harmonisation of taxes in the European Union and although they indisputably have discretion over excise duties, they increased the alcohol duties and had an extra Budget. The Conservative Government had autumn Budgets, but this Government slipped in an extra one, as a result of which alcohol duties have been increased again. The duty differential has continued to increase, which has made the smuggling situation worse. We shall be debating that problem in future sittings, but I shall concentrate now on how the Government have calculated the uprating figure.

The conventional inflation rate is not 3.4 per cent. Page 138 of the Red Book shows that personal allowances have been uprated not by the expected figure of 3.4 per cent., but by only 1.1 per cent. The Treasury uses a much lower actual inflation rate when that suits it. The working families tax credit has gone up by only 1.6 per cent.; I am not clear why it has gone up by slightly more than the personal allowance. Increases of 1.6 per cent. for that credit, the basic credit, the single persons credit and the lone parents credit look meagre aside the much bigger figure that the Treasury chooses when it suits it for uprating its own taxes. The treatment is clearly different, in accordance with the evolving formula whereby the Government pick an inflation rate simply to suit their revenue requirements.

The mystery deepens when we consider that inflation and its calculation are extensively dealt with in the Red Book. There are two pages of detail and forecast on the inflation rate, and three pages starting with page 173 are about performance inflation, its prospects and the link between inflation and the economic cycle. That section includes some independent forecasts as well. There is further information about inflation on page 186. Page 195 shows that the National Audit Office has audited many of the assumptions in the Budget, but the page does not include inflation. It is odd that the Government discuss inflation in great detail, but draw back from including it as an independently audited figure. Our suspicions are well and truly aroused.

It is odder still that the figure of 3.4 per cent does not appear once, despite the pages of analysis. Given the importance of the uprating figure, which includes hydrocarbons, alcohol duties and tobacoo—tobacco has gone up by more than inflation, but the Government have used the figure of 3.4 per cent. as their base indexation again— and given the significant revenue netted as a result, one might expect the figure of 3.4 per cent to appear in the inflation discussion.

There is clearly something to hide, and the Government now have the opportunity to explain fully how they reached that figure and why they changed their method of calculation. The previous Government used the actual inflation figure—a historic, precise figure. When the Labour Government came to power, not only did they have the extra Budget that I mentioned, which netted them extra revenue, but they changed the basis of calculation to a projected figure. That is why they expect a substantially higher inflation figure for the year ahead. An explanation is needed for that, given that the Government have put so much emphasis on achieving and sustaining low inflation.

In this important stand part debate, we want a full and open explanation of why the Government changed the basis of calculation, why they are so secretive about the figure used, and how it is constituted.

That happened to be 2.1 per cent. The new Government came to office and, in the July 1997 Budget, switched from a backward-looking measure of the RPI all-prices index to a forward-looking forecast. That has created an oddity, which is that, unlike under the Rooker-Wise amendments, under which forecasts needed to be corrected, there is no statutory obligation on the Government to correct, ex post, the effects of an uprating based on the forecast in relation to excise duties on cider. A systemic bias one way or the other could be introduced by switching from a backward-looking use of the index to a forward-looking use. It is not for me to suggest that any Government would engage in such an activity, but the Chancellor could systematically year after year consciously overestimate inflation for the year ahead, as measured by the RPI all-prices index, and never catch up with himself and so introduce a hidden form of taxation.

When the forward-looking figure was used, for example for pensions uprating, a correction was built in ex post—but asymmetrically, so that recipients benefited only when there had been an insufficient uprating.

In the July 1997 Budget, the Government uprated cider tariffs by 3 per cent., which was then the forecast RPI all-prices index for September 1997. Then came the March 1998 budget—which is where I need to correct what I think was an inadvertent error by the Financial Secretary in response to my hon. The effect of the November 1996 Budget, the July 1997 Budget and the March 1998 Budget taken together was that over a 15-month period there was roughly a 8.35 per cent. increase—the Financial Secretary, as a mathematician, will allow me to add percentages of small numbers, rather than multiplying—whereas the RPI all-prices index forecast over the same period was only 5.7 per cent. I am talking not about the difference between forward-looking and backward-looking figures but simply about the forecast.

By having two Budgets in a compressed period, the Government managed to introduce a very intricate form of stealth taxation, whereby they raised not only the rate but the base by more than the cumulative inflation rate over the relevant period, which affects all subsequent years. The fiscal effect of the measures was therefore significant. That may have been entirely happenstance, or someone very clever in the Treasury may have worked out that that was a jolly good way of increasing revenues at a time, unlike the present, when the Chancellor needed cash. Be that as it may, we now face the position that my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells described. We are being asked to approve a rate of increase in the Bill that is based on the Government's present forecast—this is the first part of the answer to the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton—of the RPI all-prices index of September 2000, which is 3.4 per cent. As my right hon. Friend pointed out, that is odd because the figure does not appear anywhere else—I think that we have combed every relevant phrase of the ``Budget 2000'' Red Book.

The comparable figure did appear, interestingly, in the 1999 Red Book on page 150. It did not get star billing, but table B4 on that page gave a September 2000 forecast for the RPI all-prices index of 3 per cent; I shall return in a moment to the question of the gap between 3 and 3.4 per cent., which is highly material. That forecast of 3 per cent. proves that the Treasury at that time was transparent at least to the extent of telling the public its forecast of the index used to uprate, among other things, cider duties. The disappearance of that figure from the analogous table in the current Red Book, table B2 on page 176, is therefore notable. There must have been a reason why a forecast that was given in the previous Red Book, and indeed a string of Red Books before it—RPI all-prices indices were the standard form of index before RPIX was used, and persisted through the journey of tax and prices indices and many other measures that my right hon. Friends the Members for Charnwood and for Fylde (Mr. Jack) will remember having introduced and discussed—this year, for the first time, disappears.

Why does the Treasury not want to discuss the forecast of 3.4 per cent. for the RPI all-prices index for the year to September? The answer is slightly more devastating than the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton realises. To understand the answer, we need to ask how, from an RPIX central forecast that to, within a smidgen, tallies with the Monetary Policy Committee's forecast of 2.25 per cent. for the same period, it is possible to arrive at an RPI all-prices index forecast of 3.4 per cent.

Having investigated the matter, I am surprised that we did not do so earlier and that commentators have not done so in more detail. It reflects what my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells and the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton said—when the Red Book reaches a certain level of opacity, the experts give up looking for measures that will affect people by only about £1 billion a year. They look for hundreds of billions, and perhaps even tens of billions, but the odd billion disappears from view. That seems to have happened in this case. However, there is an explanation. That is the good news. The bad news is what that explanation is.

The first part of the explanation is that the RPI all-prices index, unlike RPIX, measures the net effect of interest costs, principally for homeowners, or people with mortgages. I use the term ``net effect'' because it registers, for reasons that are obscure to me, the post-tax effect. It is a unique example of a direct tax being taken into account by an index other than the TPI.

As many millions of homeowners are aware, the Government have abolished mortgage interest relief at source. In doing so, they have increased their own estimate of the RPI all-prices index, because MIRAS provided a shield for homeowners against the effects of interest rate rises. People experience the disappearance of MIRAS as an increase in their net interest payments to banks and building societies. In estimating the RPI all-prices index for the period to September, the Government have, rightly, allowed for the abolition of MIRAS. We may complain about the abolition of MIRAS, but we cannot complain about the principle that that should be included if that is how the RPI all-prices index is calculated, as it is.

The second development, although big news for many people, is no longer new news. In the past few months, a significant rise in mortgages has taken place compared with the relevant period in the previous year. That, too, is registered in the Government's estimate of the RPI all-prices index.

So far, so good. We have accounted for about 0.75 percentage points of the change from the 2.25 per cent. RPIX forecast to the roughly 3 per cent. RPI all-prices index forecast. However, the difference between 3 per cent.—the figure estimated, perhaps by chance, on page 150 of the 1999 Red Book, as the RPI all-prices index rise for the relevant period under discussion—and the 3.4 per cent. now being used is a mystery. However, the mystery dissolves in the face of an important fact that we hope to bring home to the public today and which it would be good if the Treasury would admit, because it must be understood.

The Chancellor has implicitly estimated that mortgage interest rates will increase sharply in the near future. To arrive at 3.4 per cent. from 3 per cent., he needs to have estimated that mortgage interest costs will rise by about 0.7 percentage points, and will need to do so pretty smartly. To be included in the index and have a sufficient effect by September, it must happen soon.
