That gives those schools the opportunity of studying how the new status would affect them and of viewing how other categories are working within the framework. Clause 40

Responsibility for fixing dates of terms and holidays and times of sessions

9 pm

Ms Estelle Morris: I beg to move amendment No. 152, in page 31, line 25, at end insert

    "(d) that for all or any prescribed purposes of the regulations references to parents are to be read as excluding those who are not indiviudals.".

The amendment will bring the clause into line with other provisions in the Bill. Hon. Members will note that the definition of parents can include the local authority when it is acting as a parent. The amendment will give the option in this set of regulations not to include the local authority as a parent.

Amendment agreed to.

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Dorrell: I rise briefly to ask why the Government are drawing a distinction between the power to determine the school term times of foundation and voluntary aided schools, which rests with the governing body, and community schools, which rests with the LEA. The Under-Secretary has made many speeches about the need to delegate decision making, yet the Government are drawing a distinction between those different types of school. For the life of me, I cannot understand why a community school is not deemed capable of deciding its own term dates.

 

Ms Morris: I am seeking guidance. I do not know whether the position has changed from the previous system for local authority-maintained schools. I am not sure of the answer to that question and shall be happy to clarify it, but I suspect that it has to do with the nature of the different relationship between community schools and local authorities, as opposed to other groups of schools and local authorities.

Mr. Dorrell: I am sure that that is right, but the Government are creating new types of school. They say at every opportunity that they want to encourage delegation. We are considering community schools, voluntary-aided schools and foundation schools and I do not understand why the Government are making a conscious policy choice to distinguish between foundation and voluntary-aided schools, and community schools. Why should term times not be decided locally in all cases?

Ms Morris: It re-enacts existing legislation in the light of the new framework, but I am happy to reflect on the right hon. Gentleman's interesting point and I shall write to him.

Clause 42

Annual parents' meetings

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Don Foster: I rise briefly to ask Ministers to give some thought on Report as to whether or not it might be appropriate on some occasions, for the governing body of a maintained school to decide that it is impractical to hold a meeting in a particular year, without there being a further requirement in subsection (3)(b) that

    "at least 50 per cent. of the registered pupils at the school are boarders".

The vast majority of members of the Committee are concerned that the governing body is seen to be accountable. That is extremely important. However, many of us know from both personal and constituency experience, that the best form of accountability is not the annual governors meeting for parents, which is often poorly attended. It is not unusual to find only six or seven parents on a wet Thursday night sitting on undersized chairs in a school hall. It is not an effective form of accountability. While I do not want to propose a detailed amendment, it is an issue that the Government must consider carefully. The Government may want to consider including a requirement that such a meeting be held if a certain proportion of parents request it if they do not want one, they do not have to have one and that the annual report be published and circulated to parents.

Mrs. Browning: My concern also relates to subsection (3)(b) and involves the fact that the governing body may decide that holding the annual parents meeting is impractical if at least 50 per cent. of the registered pupils are boarders. That would particularly affect schools which provide for children with special educational needs, and I suggest to the Minister that it would not be impractical in the interests of openness and involving parents for such an annual meeting to be held on the last day of term. That is common practice in the private sector.

The last day of a term or a year is often the only time when parents come to the school to collect their children, as they often do from boarding schools and therefore presents a good opportunity. I would not want a governing body to use the clause as a cop-out for not holding an annual meeting when that would provide an ideal opportunity to do so.

Mr. Byers: On the final point raised by the hon. Member for Tiverton and Honiton, the provision reflects a measure enacted by a Conservative Government. However, there is merit in what she said; I take her point and will reflect on it. As is the way with such things, this measure consolidates previous provisions. The hon. Lady has made a good point, and we will reflect on it and seek ways to return to the matter on Report.

On the point made by the hon. Member for Bath, we all know that some annual parents meetings are a bit ofa disaster; but some are highly successful, and we need to learn from the best. It may well be that in the new climate and under the new procedures introduced in the Bill with targets being set for individual schools and with schools being included in the local education authority's education development plan there will be greater interest among parents about the targets for individual schools and whether they are being met. We must wait to see whether that is the case.

We need to encourage good practice and to show schools what works in neighbouring schools. I have been struck by schools in some of the most deprived parts of my inner city Tyneside constituency that get a good attendance at their annual parents meetings because they make a real effort and link them to a social event something that is a bit more fun. As the hon. Member for Bath says, once one has sat on an infant-sized seat for five minutes and one's knees have cracked, it becomes difficult to concentrate on what is going on. Occasionally, there is a deliberate attempt to make it unattractive for parents to attend the meeting.

I would be reluctant to agree to removing the requirement for an annual parents meeting. That does not mean that the Government believe that they are all working well at present. They are working well in some cases, but not all. If we can promote good practice, that will be constructive.

With those comments, I hope that the Committee will agree that clause 42 should stand part of the Bill.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 42 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
