Dr. Howells: I should like to speak to Government amendments Nos. 88, 89, 91 and 92 together. The Bill establishes new consumer councils for gas and electricity, for telecoms and for water. Those councils will have to be funded. Their activities will be directed towards furthering and safeguarding the interests of utility customers. The requirement for councils stems directly from the activities of the utility companies. It is, therefore, appropriate for the costs, including set-up costs, to be met from licence fees whenever legally possible.

There must be certainty that the councils can be funded from licence fee income. Under utilities Acts, a licence modification normally requires the agreement of each licensee, or the agreement of a significant proportion of them. If there is a failure to agree, and the regulator still wishes to proceed with the modification, the matter has to be referred to the Competition Commission for a view on whether the modification would be in the public interest. That would be a lengthy process and the outcome would be uncertain. More than 300 licences have been issued in the energy sector, and even more have been issued in the telecoms sector. To attempt to amend licences through normal procedures would prove unnecessarily complex. There is broad support for the principle of consumer councils--I understand that the Opposition support the principle--which need a firm and clear funding basis from the outset.

Clauses 7 and 88 need amending to ensure that the authorities may modify the licence conditions, so that the councils' expenses and costs arising from the establishment of the councils or new authorities may be recovered without the need for reference to the Competition Commission. The amendments are designed to achieve that certainty.

As amended, the clauses would be similar to clause 56, which will empower the Secretary of State to make licence modifications relating to the new electricity trading arrangements. It will provide a suitable mechanism where there is need for certainty about the outcome of the licence modification procedure, and where there is broad support--as there is for the principle of independent councils--for the policy that underpins licence modification. None the less, the amendments will require the authorities to consult licensees before any modifications are made to the payment conditions of licences.

The consultation provision is more complicated in amendment No. 92, which applies to the telecoms sector because of the need to accommodate class licences as well as individual licences. In respect of class licences, it is not always possible to contact holders directly, so the amendment provides an alternative procedure for dealing with this category of licensee, based on the publication of a notice.

The clauses will limit to two years the period within which the authorities are able to exercise power to amend payment conditions in licences. In other words, the period will not be open-ended.

The original clauses enabled the Secretary of State to direct the authority on exercising the power to insert new conditions in licences, and we recognise that that power was drawn too widely. Therefore, amendment No. 89 and its equivalent in respect of the telecoms sector--amendment No. 92--define the scope of those powers of direction more narrowly. They make it clear that the power can be used only to secure payment by 173 174 licence holders of sums relating to the council's expenses, and the Secretary of State's expenses in respect of establishing the council and the authority.

A power of direction is needed to safeguard the independent position of the consumer council. The Secretary of State must be able to intervene, if necessary, through direction to ensure that modifications to licences to recover approved costs are satisfactory from the council's and the Government's perspectives. Without a power of direction, councils would be entirely reliant on the authorities' discretion over implementing satisfactory payment conditions.

Once a licence condition is in place, the regulator may make changes to it with the agreement of the licensee. The regulator also has powers to issue new licences to new entrants. Although a clause may be satisfactory at the outset, that may change over time, or a new licence may not contain the relevant provision. Subsequent amendments could be detrimental to the councils, so, to safeguard their independence from the authorities, the Secretary of State's ability to give directions on payment conditions relating to councils' expenses must be on-going.

However, it is important to recognise that, after the initial time-limited, two-year period within which an authority is empowered to make licence modifications, the scope of the Secretary of State's powers of direction will diminish considerably. Within the first two years, an authority will have the power to make changes to licence conditions in response to the Secretary of State's direction. However, when that period expires, the Secretary of State's powers of direction on licence conditions will effectively be limited to a power of veto over changes to the payment conditions.

I urge the Committee to accept amendments Nos. 88, 89, 91 and 92.

Astonishingly, the phrase "wherever possible" has not appeared in the final draft of the Bill and we believe that that is an error. It makes an enormous difference to the interpretation of the clause.

The word "appropriate" refers to whether a particular course of action is suitable or proper and, therefore, seems to be within the discretion of the relevant authority. The term imposes no obligation to act wherever it is possible to do so. The word "possible" means capable of existing or being achieved, and in the context of the amendment becomes an imperative imposing a requirement to secure competition wherever possible, except where such a course of action would be inappropriate. Will the Minister explain why the phrase, which remained intact throughout the Government's series of consultation documents, was suddenly removed from the Bill?

I turn to amendments Nos. 212, 213 and 214. The teeth and meat of the regulatory regime are the licence conditions. The amendments seek to put some common sense back into those duties, and to redress the balance by requiring the regulatory authorities to do three things: to ensure that the regulatory burden imposed by licences is the minimum necessary to attain the authority's objectives; secondly, to keep such conditions under review; and, thirdly, to modify or revoke any conditions that impose a regulatory burden that is not necessary to attain the authority's objectives.

The authority must, therefore, have clear aims when it sets new licence conditions for the specific objective of a particular regulation. Without setting that specific objective, no matter whether the purpose of the regulation is social, environmental or consumerist, the regulator will be unable to assess whether aims have been met. When such an objective has been achieved, it will be time for those conditions or regulations to be revoked or repealed. Without the amendments, the Bill will simply provide for perpetual regulation, and the regulators will keep finding jobs for themselves, even in a fully competitive market.
