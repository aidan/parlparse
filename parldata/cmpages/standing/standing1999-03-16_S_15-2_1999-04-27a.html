Clause 35

Use of live television links at bail hearings

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Clappison: It is important to reflect on some of the evidence that we have received about television links. The clause will enable television links to be used in bail hearings. Some of those who gave evidence, especially those representing the ILPA, the Joint Council for the Welfare of Immigrants and Amnesty International, expressed anxieties about the clause. It is important to consider the clause in the context of immigration detainees' possible language problems and their being able to communicate with their representatives.

The Crime and Disorder Act 1998 provides for magistrates' hearings to be conducted through television links, and some pilots are taking place. We did not oppose that provision, although we expressed some views and anxieties about it. The same anxieties arise with this clause, and I hope that the Minister will tell the Committee that the Government will reflect on the results of those pilot studies, paying particular attention to the concerns of the organisations that have given evidence to the Committee.

Mr. Gerrard: I should like to make two brief points about the clause. One of its provisions deals with the fact that the detained person should be able to see and hear the court and be seen and heard by the court. However, some of the evidence that we heard questioned the ability of a detained person appearing at a bail hearing under such circumstances to communicate privately with his representative if he so wishes. In a normal hearing, we would expect the person involved to be able to do so. If we proceed with experiments under the clause—which I assume will initially be treated with caution—I hope that we shall ensure that facilities to enable a person involved in a bail hearing taking place through a video link to communicate privately with his representative are clearly specified in the procedures.

We must approach the provision with caution. One of the issues in a bail hearing may be the credibility of the person involved, especially in asylum cases, in which adjudicators must often make decisions about credibility. Those of us who are relatively familiar with sitting in front of cameras will recognise that it is not always easy, especially when one is facing a camera and the other person to whom one is speaking is at the other end of a video link rather than in the same studio. It is easy to understand how someone who is unfamiliar with such circumstances might sometimes not react naturally to questions.

I hope that we shall approach the provision with caution. The ability of the detained person to consult his representative in private is important. I do not suggest that we necessarily need to amend the Bill to specify that, but even if it is not specified, that provision should be made when a bail hearing is conducted in such circumstances.

Mr. Allan: The hon. Member for Walthamstow (Mr. Gerrard) has made some useful points. I trust that the Minister will explain what is meant by having suitable facilities available at the institution. There may be a wide range of what is deemed to be suitable. In a debate on an earlier clause, I mentioned people's wish to be surrounded by those who are supporting them in their efforts to be released from detention. There may be a difficulty if a suitable facility is taken to mean an individual stuck in front of a camera in an empty room in the detention centre or prison in which that person is being held.

Will the Minister consider individuals' ability to consult others, by which I mean their ability to make contact with their legal representative and those who are supporting them in their application for bail during what may be a traumatic process in which their liberty is at stake?

Mr. Mike O'Brien: The clause allows any court that is conducting a routine bail hearing to direct that live television links may be used for the purpose of the hearing. Such a direction may be made only once representation has been made by the parties at the hearing. Representatives will be allowed to make representations about whether it is appropriate that that should happen. There may be circumstances in which it is difficult for the applicant to be present. For example, a television link may avoid delaying a hearing if it has been decided that that hearing should take place on time according to routine procedures and the applicant is in another part of the country. The television link would enable the applicant to see what is going on. It is therefore appropriate for a decision to be made after representation has been made by the parties concerned.

I have made it clear that we do not intend to introduce that measure until the appropriate facilities are in place in courts, detention centres and prisons. Furthermore, the measure will not be used until we are completely satisfied that the detainee has a full opportunity to consult his or her representative in private before and during any hearing conducted by television link. The hon. Member for Hallam will note that I said ``before and during''.

There are various ways of facilitating such a consultation. The advocate does not necessarily have to be in continuous private contact with the person whose application is under consideration. Communication may be conducted via a private link by a representative who is not acting as advocate, but who passes notes to the advocate. Matters are often conducted in that way in courts in which defendants are present. We intend to ensure that, as the person most directly affected by the measure, the applicant is in a position in which he or she can properly carry out his responsibilities and wishes.

We have considered carefully our obligations under article 6 of the ECHR. We are not satisfied that article 6 applies under current Strasbourg jurisdiction. Nevertheless, we are confident that all the safeguards that article 6 offers will be provided at routine bail hearings, whether or not they are conducted by live television link.

The hon. Member for Hertsmere asked us to reflect on the pilot studies. Let me reassure him that we will certainly do that. Obviously, the measure is modelled on some of the work done for the Crime and Disorder Bill. We intend to take that into account in determining the speed at which we proceed to deal with such matters. Such a provision is unlikely to be introduced in the immediate future, except on a pilot basis. We will proceed with a degree of caution.

My hon. Friend the Member for Walthamstow rightly said that it is important to get things right and proceed with caution. I can reassure him that we will do so. I have already confirmed that we envisage that private communication with a representative will take place before and during the hearing.

I am aware that some people may not perform well on television. However, that may not be relevant, as the applicant may be able to give evidence during a bail application, although that will not normally happen. We would wish to make a careful response to the hon. Gentleman's urging for caution before creating circumstances in which the applicant may be asked to give evidence via television link.

It is right to be aware of such circumstances. Our commitment that the representatives will be heard before the procedure is permitted will allow people's anxiety about how they will perform on television to be taken into account before the courts decide whether to use that system.

I therefore hope that Committee members will support the clause.

Clause 38

The Immigration Appeal Tribunal

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hoon: Clause 38(1) establishes and allows for the continuance of the present Immigration Appeal Tribunal, although with a significantly enhanced membership. Such a change does not actually need legislative provision, but we are aiming to increase the status and the importance of the tribunal and clarify its role as a source of a comprehensive and coherent jurisprudence in an increasing complex and sensitive area.

To mark its importance, the Lord Chancellor has decided to appoint a High Court judge to be president of the tribunal in the future. That will be a crucial position under the new system and the new appointee will have unique importance as the source of expertise, judicial guidance and training in immigration law. There will be a strong leadership team and the Lord Chancellor will also appoint a deputy president, who will be a senior circuit judge, and who will assist the president as requested and stand in for him or her in times of absence.

Clause 38(2) refers to schedule 2 which, at paragraph 1 deals directly with the process for appointing full-time and part-time legal chairmen to the tribunal. It provides that, as before, those tribunal appointments are to be made by the Lord Chancellor. We aim to concentrate the role of the tribunal as being a source of a coherent and developing field of law and the Bill allows only for the appointment of legally qualified members. Several people who gave evidence, however, argued strongly for a more flexible model as the issues involved are complex and need careful consideration. We found the evidence helpful and we will return to the matter.

Question put and agreed to.

Mr. Clappison: Clause 45 deals with appeals to adjudicators against decisions by the Secretary of State to make deportation orders or to refuse to revoke deportation orders that have been made.

I preface my remarks by saying that we share the Government's objective of wanting to streamline the appeal system, wherever possible and always consistent with justice. If we think that a right of appeal should be questioned, we shall do so. I look forward to the Minister's response. He may be able to reassure me that there is no need to ask about the right of appeal in question, but I wish the Minister to consider another point.

The Committee may recall our debates on clause 6, under which the enforcement mechanism for overstayers was changed from deportation to removal. We explored at some length the fact that that change removed the right of appeal for those who were subject to removal rather than deportation. It curtailed their rights of appeal. We make no observation on that, except that those who lose that right of appeal, although overstayers, may otherwise have led blameless lives; they may wish to argue compassionate circumstances arising from the length of their stay in this country.

Those people will have had their rights to appeal curtailed by the change from deportation to removal. Others besides overstayers have been subject to the deportation regime, but they will remain subject to deportation. Overstayers are no longer subject to it but others, including those who have been recommended for deportation by the courts, will still be dealt with under the deportation mechanism.

Subsection (1)(a) provides a right of appeal against a decision of the Secretary of State to make a deportation order. Amendment No. 270 takes that right away from someone who has been recommended for deportation by the courts. Will the Minister confirm that a person recommended for deportation will have the right to appeal against the decision of the Secretary of State to make a deportation order under subsection (1)(a)? If so the Committee should examine what that means in practice.

The person concerned will have been convicted of an offence before the courts. His legal representative will have addressed the court on the recommendation for deportation. The official handbook on immigration and refugee law for magistrates says:

    ``The person or representative should be asked to address the court specifically on the question of a recommendation and the court should also consider the effects of a recommendation on others, such as the person's family''.

This is the first opportunity for the person concerned to have all his circumstances, including compassionate and family circumstances, looked at.

If a recommendation is made by the court in the first instance—the magistrates court or, more commonly, the Crown court—that person has a right of appeal. In the case of the magistrates court, the appeal goes from the magistrates court to the Crown court. If the recommendation is made in the Crown court, it goes to the Court of Appeal because the recommendation for deportation is part of the sentence and there is a right of appeal against that sentence.

On appeal in the Crown court or the Court of Appeal, all the circumstances can be looked at, including compassionate circumstances. That is therefore the second occasion on which all circumstances can be looked at. If the appeal is not allowed the recommendation goes to the Home Secretary. He then must consider whether to follow the court's recommendation. He has to consider any representations that he receives, including special or compassionate circumstances. That is the third time the circumstances of someone with a criminal conviction will be considered.

If the Home Secretary decides to make an order, clause 35 gives the person concerned a further right of appeal to an adjudicator against the decision of the Home Secretary to follow the recommendation of the court. That is the fourth time that his circumstances will be looked at. Will the Minister confirm that in such a case the appeal to an adjudicator against the Home Secretary's upholding of the recommendation and the decision to make a deportation order is a full appeal in which all the merits of the case are looked at, including compassionate circumstances? Will the Minister also confirm that the appellant will remain in the United Kingdom until the appeal is heard?

That is our understanding of the position. It may be wrong and we look to the Minister for confirmation. It it is not, that is the fourth time that the person's circumstances are looked at. In contrast, the Home Office—not a court—looks at the compassionate circumstances of the overstayer only once, although he may be of good character and have no criminal convictions even for non-immigration offences. The overstayer has a limited right of appeal, not on compassionate grounds, but he can exercise it only after he has left the country.

Perhaps I am wrong, but the person whom the courts have recommended for deportation gets a good deal, but the overstayer who is dealt with by removal gets a bad deal. The overstayer has one-and-a-half rights of appeal—the Home Office considers his case fully only once, and he has half a right of appeal because the grounds are limited and the appeal takes place while he is not in this country.

Looking at such matters in that light, we wonder whether or not there should be a right of appeal against the Home Secretary's decision to deport following a court recommendation, because that is the fourth time that a person has had a right to have his case considered. Perhaps the right place for the appeal to be heard is in the appellate court, following the original conviction. Is there not a case for saying, ``Enough is enough'' at that stage?

It is right that the Home Secretary looks at the person's circumstances before making a deportation order, but is it necessary to have a further appeal after that, as there has already been one appeal and the case has been considered three times? Of course, the appellate court has criminal jurisdiction and can judge the seriousness of the offence while taking into account compassionate circumstances.

It would appear that courts of first instance get it right, because we understand that last year fewer than 10 of the 360 appeals against recommendations for deportation made by the courts were upheld.

Adjudicators inevitably have less experience of dealing with criminal cases, yet by the time that a case gets to an adjudicator it will have been through the hands of one—perhaps two—criminal courts and of the Home Secretary. After all those people, including magistrates and specialised criminal judges, Crown courts or even the Court of Appeal and the Home Secretary, have considered the case, how can an adjudicator say that they have all been incorrect and that the decision to deport is wrong? How often does an adjudicator say that they are wrong and allow an appeal against the making of a deportation order that has followed a court recommendation?

We wonder whether the procedure is appropriate for those who have committed serious criminal offences, as the majority of those who are recommended for deportation have. In a parliamentary answer—which appears in column 668 of the Official Report of 22 April—the Minister showed the seriousness of the offences of which those people have generally been convicted. He told me that, in 1997, of those recommended for deportation, not one was given a community sentence; 17 were given a sentence of imprisonment for less than four years, but 297—the overwhelming majority—were given a sentence of imprisonment for four years or more. Clearly, those are serious offences. That colours how we look at those people, and they seem to be treated generously in the appeals system, compared to overstayers. I hope that I have made my point. Is there not a case for removing one of those layers of appeal—the appeal to the adjudicator against the making of a deportation order? We look forward to the Minister's response.
