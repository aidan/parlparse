The cut-off for amendments that can be discussed on Tuesday 6 June is 4.30 pm on Friday 2 June. Amendments tabled before then will be unstarred. By implication, therefore, those tabled afterwards will be starred and not called.

Indeed, because of last-minute changes, one or two hon. Members may not be aware that they have been appointed. For that reason, the usual channels have agreed that this morning we shall deal only with the sittings motion. I anticipate a brief debate on the order of consideration, if that is necessary, at the start of business proper on Tuesday when we return from the recess. I hope that that assists Committee members.

Finally, for the comfort of those hon. Members who have not presumed on my generosity, it will be in order to remove jackets. It will, of course, be up to Mr. Illsley to decide whether to exercise the same extraordinary generosity when he is in the Chair.

None the less, we look forward to some constructive debates over the next four or five weeks. The principles behind the Bill enjoy a consensus among hon. Members of all parties. The Government are building on a long-term trend in legislation, which started in 1990, and which seeks to tighten and strengthen the regulation of social care. However, several concerns remain, which are relevant to the issue of the time that the Committee needs to consider the Bill.

Many of those concerns were identified in the other place. I am glad to say that, at that stage, the Government were able to make some concessions, but our noble Friends were unable to persuade the Government of the wisdom of their views on many other parts of the Bill, and imposed their views against the Government's wishes. I anticipate that the Government will seek to reverse some, if not all, of those changes. I am pleased that some further Government amendments have been tabled, which suggests that they have considered the points raised in the other place.

Our job is to consider the Bill as it now stands, drawing on the reasoned debates in the other place. Our journey through the Bill might be slightly fitful—

Mr. Julian Brazier (Canterbury): Hear, hear.

Mr. Hammond: Perhaps my hon. Friend would allow me to finish. There are few areas in which the meaning of the Bill and its implications are clear and concise. Where the full purpose and import has yet to be made clear by secondary legislation, we shall want to pause and seek clarification and reassurances from the Minister.

I want to identify three principal causes of the need for greater scrutiny. The first is the Bill's architecture, which is too dependent on regulation and, worse still, too dependent on standards that are to be used by Ministers without any opportunity for parliaments scrutiny. So, we shall need to probe the Minister in some detail about the Government's intentions. In many areas, no draft regulations are yet available and there is little indication of their scope. It is unclear why the Government have been unable to produce either draft regulations or, at least, some outline document to indicate the scope and impact of those regulations.

The Bill started its passage some six months ago in the other place, and I would have expected that, by now, the Government might have been able to let the Committee have sight of something to flesh out the framework. In many cases, I am minded to give the Government the benefit of the doubt and to believe that many of our fears will disappear when we see that the regulations are reasonable and sensible. However, since our job is to scrutinise legislation, we can look only at the Bill. To the extent that it provides open-ended scope for Ministers to lay regulations and set standards that will not be subject to parliamentary scrutiny, it is the Committee's duty to assume the worst. If the Government do not constrain themselves, either by publishing regulations or by making clear statements, we must explore the Bill's impact in the worst cases. I hope that Ministers can clarify the scope and extent of the Government's intended use of the regulation-making powers, so that the debate will resolve itself satisfactorily.

Another issue needs to be raised. I mentioned it last year during our debates on the Health Bill, but it remains a concern. Although Ministers can make commitments on the use of the Secretary of State's regulation-making powers in respect of England, no one in the Committee can make binding statements on those that will be vested in the National Assembly for Wales. I realise that we can do nothing about that, but it is a serious gap in Parliament's ability to scrutinise legislation. In practice, although the Minister may be able to reassure us on matters relating to England, he will be unable to do so on those relating to Wales.

Gentleman, we work in partnership with our colleagues in the National Assembly and we shall have severe discussions about how such matters affect Wales. Mr. Hammond: I do not know where the hon. Gentleman gets the impression that we do not work in conjunction with our colleagues in the National Assembly for Wales. The Minister's comments will doubtless be helpful, but it remains a constitutional fact that, although he can explicitly say that the Government do not intend to use their regulation-making powers in a certain way or that they will constrain their actions, no one is able to make a similar statement to the Committee in respect of the National Assembly for Wales. It remains to be seen whether that will prove to be a problem.

We shall want to probe the Government a little further on the financial implications of the higher quality agenda. The Bill is almost silent on the subject. We all support higher quality in the delivery of all forms of social care, but we must recognise that higher quality means higher cost. We need to know whether those costs will be met by central Government, and how, so that local authorities can continue purchasing the same volumes of care at the higher quality—and thus, by implication, at a higher price. If local authorities are not fully reimbursed for the cost of the quality-improvement programmes, the result will be higher quality care but less of it, because local authorities will not have the wherewithal to meet the cost and maintain the volume of care.

The reason why I will probe the Government particularly on the matter is that, if they acknowledge that they will have to foot the bill for the higher quality that they are imposing on the sector, they had no reason to wait this far into the Parliament before introducing the legislation, which may not become effective for another two and a half years. If the Government really want to achieve higher quality in delivery of social care and are prepared to pay for it, they could do so through contracting and the power of their role as the principal financer of social care purchasing. The hon. Member for Blackpool, North and Fleetwood (Mrs. Humble) told us on Second Reading of an interesting example in her constituency of a system of differentiated payments to ensure that higher quality care is recognised in higher payments.

I have a lingering suspicion that, because the Government did not choose in advance of introducing the Bill to procure higher quality simply by paying for it and using the mechanisms of the marketplace—I am not suggesting that that is an alternative to the need for legislation—thereby speeding up the process, they will somehow seek to avoid the full cost of implications of the quality programme that they are imposing. That would have serious consequences for care users, care providers and local authorities. We will table amendments to ensure that the Bill's good intentions on quality translate into the production of a stable supply of services by ensuring appropriate systems for recognising quality and meeting its higher costs—using carrots as well as sticks to deliver the higher quality regime that we all seek.

We will also raise for consideration the areas where the Government insist on ignoring deliberations in the other place. We now have a reformed House of Lords. The present Prime Minister has filled it with more peers than any previous Prime Minister. The House of Lords has been shaped to be compliant to the Government's bidding. We were told two years ago, even 18 months ago, that reform was needed because the body was anachronistic and out of tune with the thinking of modern Britain. The Government have done their dirty work in the other place.

Mr. Hammond: That is precisely my point. The reformed House of Lords is the image of the interim body that the Government chose. We might reasonably expect that the Government would listen to the body that they created in its preferred form. I may be surprised; the Government may have a late change of heart and accept some of the careful deliberations in the other place and do not seek to reverse all the changes imposed against the Government's will.

On issues of principle raised and debated in the House of Lords in a serious and well considered way, we will seek to engage the Government in a constructive debate. We will not stand on the ramparts defending the Lords' amendments, but we shall consider the principles that the Lords sought to introduce and the Government's specific objections to them. We shall also explore other ways in which we might obtain a compromise that addresses the points made by the House of Lords but which acknowledges the concerns expressed about them by the Government.

The Bill's passage will occur at a variable pace, but I hope that the Minister will acknowledge that the Opposition are approaching it in a constructive spirit. There is no political division of principle about the Bill's objectives, but there are practical concerns about the excessive use of regulation and the absence of acknowledgement that quality must be paid for and of any explanation of how such payment will be made. The intentions behind the Bill are good and in keeping with a tradition of legislation that seeks continuously to improve and tighten the quality of regulation of social care. It also takes some significant steps forward. For example, it acknowledges the need for uniform regulations, registration and inspection procedures for residential care homes. We welcome the Government's modern thinking on that issue and several others, as we shall point out during the Committee's deliberations.

We will seek to improve the Bill by building on its best features and by taking the Government's logic and their arguments—as we shall, for example, on residential care homes—and drawing them out to their full consequences. We shall table amendments that will, I hope, lead to a better and perhaps more far-reaching Bill that is built none the less on the same principles, arguments and processes of logic that the Government have already applied in taking some significant steps forward in important areas.

I look forward to an enjoyable, constructive and—I hope—not too acrimonious few weeks, and to working with hon. Members from all parties. I look forward also to probing the Bill's weaknesses and to trying to strengthen it so that its principal purposes—on which I think all hon. Members are agreed—are effectively enacted.
