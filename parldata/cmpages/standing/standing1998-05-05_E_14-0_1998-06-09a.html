I welcome you, Mrs. Dunwoody, to another week of the Finance Bill. I am sorry that the right hon. Member for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory) has not managed to get any satisfaction this morning.

The amendment is technical. It will put beyond doubt the ability of the Occupational Pensions Regulatory Authority to impose sanctions on Inland Revenue appointed independent trustees, commonly called pensioneer trustees. Indeed, pensioneer trustees who fail to carry out the requirements of the Pensions Acts 1995 will be the subject of a number of our debates this morning.

An important feature of our measures to prevent trust busting is that the managing trustees of a small self-administered pension scheme should not be able to force out the scheme's pensioneer trustee. Paragraph 7 of schedule 15 overrides the rules of existing approved schemes so that, with limited exceptions, a pensioneer trustee can be dismissed only if immediately replaced by another such trustee.

The existing exemptions are when a pensioneer trustee dies, acts in a fraudulent way or is released by a court order. To make it clear that OPRA's powers are not affected by the scheme rule override, the amendment adds a further exception to enable a pensioneer trustee to be removed when OPRA uses its powers to prohibit, suspend or disqualify a pensioneer trustee from acting as a trustee.

Mrs. Liddell: It may help the Committee if I explain what a pensioneer trustee is and why they are needed. Pensioneer trustees are actuaries, solicitors or other pension professionals who are approved by the Inland Revenue to be an independent trustee of small self-administered pension schemes. Under an SSAP scheme, the beneficiaries are normally the controlling directors of the company that sponsored the scheme. The directors also act as scheme trustees and they have control over the scheme's assets and investment policies.

Obvious conflicts of interests can occur because the same people are, in effect, wearing different hats. Substantial amounts of tax relief are usually involved. It has usually been a tax-approval condition that an SSAP scheme must have a pensioneer trustee who is familiar with the tax-approval conditions. The role is therefore taken be a specialist. The aim is simply to ensure that the SSAP scheme is managed properly. That protects taxpayers' interests in the scheme by ensuring that they are used for genuine retirement provision, which means that they are in keeping with the conditions under which tax approval has been given.

The Occupational Pensions Regulatory Authority may appoint trustees only in specified circumstances, namely when they are meant to replace a disqualified trustee or a person who is prohibited from being a trustee by OPRA; ensure that the trustees as a whole have the necessary specialist knowledge and skill; ensure that the number of trustees is sufficient for the scheme's proper administration; or ensure that the scheme's assets are properly used.

OPRA's responsibility for the supervision of pension schemes is laid out in the Pensions Act 1995. A pensioneer trustee is appointed by the scheme's other trustees as a condition of that tax approval, as I explained earlier. Unless that trustee has committed a breach of trust, a suspected breach of the Pensions Act or has acted in a manner that is not in keeping with the Pensions Act, it would not be appropriate for the authority to become involved, either in the appointment or replacement of the pensioneer trustee.
