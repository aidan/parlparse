    `shall'.

The clause deals with the publication of information for users and the importance of that justifies the firming up that we seek. Indeed, on several occasions, we have sought to clarify and firm up the Bill.

The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson): The argument is the same as before. The amendment is prescriptive and nothing is to be gained from it. The council has a duty to provide users of postal services with specified information and it is only sensible for it to decide the best way of carrying out that duty for itself. We have empowered it do so in two ways, but do not want to rule out other ways. More generally, we do not want to impose an arbitrary requirement on the council.

One of the council's vital duties is giving information to users. However, there would be no advantage in specifying the means by which it should provide such information As with the argument about the Postal Services Commission, replacing ``may'' with ``shall'' is too prescriptive. I urge the Committee to reject the amendment for the reasons I have given before.

Mr. Richard Page (South-West Hertfordshire): I am unable to attend the Committee on Thursday as I will be in Sri Lanka. I believe that the Committee will make a lot of progress while I am away. I hope that that encourages everyone.

Clause 42 deals with the publication of information for users. It requires the consumer council to make available such information on relevant postal services and its own activities as ``it considers expedient'', which is almost like giving it a blank cheque. That duty is qualified by subsection (3), which states that publication should not

    seriously and prejudicially affect the interests of the person to whom it relates.

That is rather like saying ``only slightly dead'', so I hope that the Minister will explain what it means. Does it mean that it could damage a company, but not seriously? It is a question of the degree of discretion that the council will have in publicising information to users about its activities or about the relevant postal services.

The terms ``expedient'' and ``appropriate'' are very legalistic. They are all right for lawyers, but how will they work in the real world, in which a postal service has to be operated and run? What criteria will the council use in deciding whether it is expedient to give users information? Will it check with the users affected before it releases information—showing them what it proposes to send out and asking if they have any objection to it? If it does not do that, companies could be seriously affected. There should be some form of consultation first. There is also nothing in the clause about what redress is available if a company is commercially affected—``seriously and prejudicially''—by the release of information. What redress against the council would a company have in such a case? Will the Minister reassure us that the council will discuss the release of information with the companies involved before it is released into the public domain?

As the Minister knows, we could have peppered the amendment paper with amendments to tease out such information but I thought it right to raise the issues on the clause stand part debate so that the Minister could respond to them all together, as it were. Some companies have legitimate concerns that information will be released that could seriously affect their business.

Mr. Alan Johnson: We debated earlier the issue of whether disclosures

    would or might seriously and prejudicially affect the interests of the person to whom it relates.

We should be clear what we are talking about. We are talking about the council making available information to users of relevant postal services. Our earlier debate was on people who were not relevant postal users. The clause relates only to relevant postal users, defined in clause 40 as users of universal service obligation providers and licence holders. No blank cheques are involved. The use of ``expedient''—the clause states that the council will make available to users

    such information as it considers expedient to give—

simply gives a non-governmental public body the discretion to decide the issues for itself It is important that we do that. The opposite of giving a blank cheque would be for us to lay down every dot and comma of what that important body, which protects consumer services, should do. The council can be trusted to judge what it considers relevant. The provision does not deal with the information itself, but with the publication of information to users, most of which would be well received by the people to whom it is sent. There is no provision in the clause for information to be discussed with users before publication, but clause 50 deals with forward work programmes. Both the council and the commission will have to set out their forward work programme for the coming year. If users felt that that involved sending out information that would go straight into the wastepaper bin, they could make that clear.

I am enormously pleased that Opposition Members did not table a series of amendments to the clause. We have got the balance right. I am not talking about the standards of performance of licence holders. That is dealt with under clause 34. This clause deals with other information that the relevant postal services will be happy to receive. There will be no danger in the Committee accepting that clause 42 stand part of the Bill and I hope that at least some of what I have said has eased the fears of Opposition Members.

Mr. Alan Duncan (Rutland and Melton): This is a sensible amendment if only because it renders the clause consistent with earlier parts of the Bill. One of the patterns that has emerged in our deliberations over the past few days is repetition. We are repeating ourselves because certain aspects of the duties of the commission are the same as those of the council. In other words, they are parallel organisations performing a slightly different function with identical responsibilities. Whether we call it mirror imaging or perfect dovetailing, the fact is that the responsibilities of the council match and shadow the responsibilities of the commission. This amendment simply puts similar responsibilities for the council on to the same template as the responsibilities of the commission. If the council is required to provide information and offer advice in order to assist the commission with its duties, it makes sense that the nature of that advice should match and reflect the duties of the council. If the commission has responsibility in certain areas, the nature of the advice that emanates from the council should serve that duty as perfectly as possible. That is what the amendment requires. If the commission has to do this in A, B and C, the council has to provide the sort of information that it needs in areas A, B and C. The amendment is asking that the duties of the council match the responsibilities of the commission and would ensure that it properly provides information and advice to help the commission do its work. It is sensible. There are similar repetitions in the schedules.

Mr. Johnson: The amendments will enable the council to exercise its functions more effectively than as drafted in the Bill. Amendment No. 65 makes it clear that the council must, to facilitate the exercise of its functions—so far as it considers practicable—collect and keep under review information about the provision of postal services in the United Kingdom and elsewhere. That could, for example, include information about best practice on how other postal operators deal with complaints both here and overseas. As the UK is a member of the European Union and is bound by the same postal services directive as other European operators, it is important that the council should be aware of practice overseas.     in the United Kingdom and elsewhere

Amendment No. 66 gives the council a duty to collect and keep under review information about not only ``relevant postal services'', but any postal services as is practicable in facilitating the exercise of its functions. It is right that best practice from the unregulated area influences the council to improve the practices of the relevant postal services users within the regulated area.

Amendment No. 67 gives the council a duty to collect and keep under review information about the interests and users of all postal services in the United Kingdom, rather than restricting the duty to relevant postal issues.
