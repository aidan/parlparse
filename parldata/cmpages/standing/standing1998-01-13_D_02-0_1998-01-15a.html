We feel sure that you will apply similar qualities to today's proceedings, Mr. Atkinson. On that happy note, I am keen to rejoin debate on the matter that we were discussing on Tuesday.

The Government have sought to justify the inclusion of the words ``by his employer'' in clause 1, not least on the grounds that it is necessary to ensure a level playing field. They believe specifically that there is a prospect of bad employers driving out good ones unless those words are contained in the Bill. That is what motivates them. The Government are mistaken in their insistence on the retention of those words and I want briefly to focus on some of the reasons why.

First, in so far as an employer who pays low wages is operating inefficiently, it logically follows that he or she gains no commercial advantage from paying low wages. It has been part of the Government's argument all along that it is not only socially just to pay good wages--by which they presumably mean at least the minimum wage--but that it is economically efficient to do so. The President of the Board of Trade has consistently regaled us with that argument, although with deplorably little evidence to support it. If the Government believe that, they should not be bothered about an employer who pays low wages; he is behaving inefficiently and gains no advantage over efficient employers who pay higher wages. So the argument about the level playing field manifestly does not apply. If it applies at all, it goes against the bad employer who behaves inefficiently and pays excessively low wages. Who should mind if a bad employer, by his own behaviour, suffers an uneven playing field which is against his interests? That is the first argument which I invite Labour Members to consider. [Interruption.] The hon. Member for Falmouth and Camborne (Ms Atherton) is becoming a little excited. Let us suppose that an employer paying relatively low wages is behaving as a rational, economic actor--sensibly, in terms of his commercial interest. In so far as he is doing so, it follows that the President of the Board of Trade cannot argue that that person is an inefficient employer. The existing playing field is the result of the natural state of the market. In such circumstances, there is no justification for the interference of public authority.

The truth is that the only respect in which the argument about a level playing field applies is the one to which my hon. Friend the Member for Ashford (Mr. Green) alluded towards the end of Tuesday's proceedings. Market entry, or prevention of entry to the market, is the real point at stake. If wages are raised artificially by Government fiat rather than by market necessity, the consequence is that some people are kept out of the market--not because they do not have a contribution to make, not because they could not run a business and not because they could not successfully employ and retain people, but because the Government have decided that they shall not be allowed access to the market. If the Minister believes that he is a better judge of the way in which the market should operate than a would-be business man who cannot afford to pay minimum wage which the Minister intends to require to be paid, let him argue his corner. Member for Stockton, South (Ms Taylor), is a better judge of market wage rates and appropriate pay than is the Minister.

At the heart of the argument on the clause and the amendment is a debate between those of us who believe that what matters is remuneration and those who contend that what matters is a contract between an employer and an employee. The Government believe, wrongly in our judgment, in the latter. We say this to them: ``Are you in the business of raising the remuneration of employees or of settling scores with employers? Do you have an interest in punishing those who take on workers or in bolstering the living standards of the work force?''

The remuneration of employees is what matters to Conservative Members. My hon. Friend the Member for Runnymede and Weybridge (Mr. Hammond) made that point succinctly and forcefully on Tuesday in the context of tipping. He knows that in the catering industry tips account for a substantial part of the remuneration of workers. The Minister believes that the minimum wage should have to be paid by the employer and that what matters is the level of income that the employee derives from the employer. What matters to my hon. Friends and me is what remuneration the employee is able to secure. The tips, of course, do not come from the employer, but from a satisfied customer. However, in many circumstances, waiters or waitresses are able to secure a greater share of their income from tips than from their wage.

We want to establish why the Minister has a hang-up about the retention of the words ``by his employer'', why he is obsessed with the burden that the employer has to bear and why it is so important to him that the contractual relationship between the employer and the employee should be the criterion on which the level of the minimum wage is assessed. Why not instead consider the overall earnings of the employee--the remuneration that he is able to secure? That is the basis of our position. The argument is not between those who want people to be well paid or well rewarded and those who do not; it is about how fair pay is to be achieved.
