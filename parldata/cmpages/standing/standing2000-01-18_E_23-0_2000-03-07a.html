Mr. Raynsford: As you rightly say, Mr. Gale, we have reached part III of the Bill, which deals with schemes for congestion and workplace charges. If you are happy, I shall start with a brief overview to set the context of the policy behind this part of the Bill.

Clauses 139 to 146 deal with charging schemes and the procedures for making them. Before I deal with their purpose, let me set them in context. They are designed to give local traffic authorities the power to implement road user charging schemes to combat local congestion. They allow flexibility to operate joint schemes between different charging authorities, where that is the most effective way to deal with local traffic problems. The clauses also enable the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales to charge on trunk roads in limited circumstances to support local schemes or to help fund expensive infrastructure.

They are new and important powers, and the Government will work closely with the first charging authorities that plan to use the powers, to assist them, to learn lessons and to pass on best practice. Schemes will be made by order and local authorities will be expected to open them to widespread consultation and, if appropriate, to a public inquiry. Every scheme will be submitted for confirmation by the appropriate national authority. The Secretary of State will ensure that there has been full consultation and that the scheme is in the interests of the local community. That is likely to mean that public transport improvements have been made in advance of introducing the scheme and that there are sensible and robust plans for spending all the scheme revenue on a range of transport improvements.

Clauses 139 to 143 identify the authorities that can make charging schemes. As the Greater London Authority Act 1999 already provides powers for London authorities to make such schemes, the clauses provide such powers for all other local traffic authorities. Joint schemes may be made between local traffic authorities or between local, non-London traffic authorities and London traffic authorities, to give the flexibility required to combat congestion on local roads.

Clause 140 makes a direct link between charging schemes and the objectives in authorities' local transport plans. Any charging scheme must be an integral and coherent part of an authority's overall integrated local transport strategy. Charging schemes must help meet the policies in an authority's local transport plan, directly or indirectly. That gives an authority the flexibility, for example, to fund complementary measures outside its immediate area and promotes the Government's integrated transport policies.

Clause 143 sets out the two cases in which charges may be introduced on roads where the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales is the traffic authority. Charges may be introduced on a trunk road carried by a bridge or one that passes through a tunnel of at least 607 metres in length. This will allow charges to be introduced to help fund expensive new infrastructure or to promote integrated transport objectives such as reducing congestion. The Secretary of State has no plans to introduce charging on existing structures that are not already tolled.

Charges may also be introduced on roads for which the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales is responsible, if they are requested by a charging authority to complement its charging scheme. This limited power will ensure the effectiveness of local charging schemes. It might be used where a trunk road passes through a local charging area or where the effects of a local charging scheme would otherwise cause traffic management problems on a nearby trunk road.

Charging schemes will be made, varied or revoked by means of an order. There may be joint orders between authorities. The Secretary for State or the National Assembly for Wales will confirm—and could modify—the orders. Where a London charging authority is party to a joint scheme with a non-London authority, the order will need to be confirmed by the Greater London Authority and the Secretary of State and will not be confirmed until both are content.

It is envisaged that the Secretary of State will normally wish to ensure that there has been full consultation; that public transport improvements have been made in advance of introducing the scheme; that there are sensible and robust plans for spending all the scheme revenues on a range of transport improvements and, in some cases, that the right technology is in place. The Government will work closely with the first charging authorities that plan to use the new powers.

Charging authorities will consult interested parties when they make an order that establishes or varies a charging scheme. They will also be able to hold public inquiries when they feel that those are necessary. Clause 146 provides an additional safeguard by allowing the national authority or the GLA to require further consultation or a public inquiry and thus to safeguard the interests of the public and of business, if either feels that local consultation has not been adequate. Those powers can be used where an authority is making or varying an order, but if a charging authority wishes to revoke a charging scheme in the interests of the local community, there will be no barriers to it doing so immediately.

Amendments Nos. 606 and 626, for road user charging, and amendments Nos. 637 and 656, for workplace parking charges, serve two purposes. First, they improve the drafting of the Bill by separating liability for paying charges from liability to pay penalty charges that might result from the non-payment of a road user charge or failure to obtain an appropriate workplace parking licence. As drafted, liability for paying both charges and penalty charges is specified in clauses 149 for road user charging and in clause 165 for the place parking levy. Both clauses deal principally with the enforcement of charging and licensing schemes.

Secondly, the amendments make it clear that the registered keeper of a vehicle will be responsible for paying road user charges and penalty charges. The amendments provide that responsibility for paying workplace parking charge and penalty charges will rest with the occupier of the premises. As the Bill is drafted, the Secretary of State would need to make regulations to provide for those charges. The amendments remove that requirement and make the principle clear in the Bill. I hope that the Committee will agree that that is sensible, as they are important points of principle.

In addition, amendments Nos. 626 and 656 provide that regulations dealing with the notification, adjudication and enforcement of penalty charges are to be made by the Lord Chancellor. This will ensure that the Lord Chancellor's Department is responsible for regulations that deal with legal issues and will guarantee consistency in the legal process across England and Wales. Amendments Nos. 625 and 655 provide that regulations dealing with policy matters will be the responsibility of the appropriate national authority.

Amendments Nos. 680 and 694 to schedule 12 also provide that the registered keeper of a vehicle or the occupier of a premises will be responsible for paying charges and any penalty charges for road user charging and workplace parking levy schemes that may be introduced in London. Again there is a provision to make regulations to make persons other than the registered keeper of a vehicle and the occupier of premises liable for these charges. As before, the amendments also provide that regulations dealing with the notification, adjudication and enforcement of penalty charges in London are to be made by the Lord Chancellor. These are all tidying up provisions to the 1999 Act to ensure that it is brought into line with the provisions of the Bill.

That is an overview of the purposes and effect of the clauses that we are considering and the reasons why the amendments should form part of the Bill. I hope that the Committee will concur.
