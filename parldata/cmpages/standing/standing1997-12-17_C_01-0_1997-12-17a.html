The Chairman: Before I call the hon. Member for Worcester (Mr. Foster) I do not have to remind the Committee that we shall be discussing a matter that excites great passion and interest for the House and the public. It is important that we conduct our affairs in the highest traditions of debate in the House. In particular, I ask the Committee to co-operate with the Chair in ensuring that sedentary exchanges across the Floor do not take place. The proper way to intervene, as hon. Members know, is to stand and ask the hon. Member who has the Floor to give way. Should that hon. Member not give way, the hon. Member wishing to intervene must sit down. Otherwise, it will be difficult for the Committee's words to be recorded properly by our stenographers and the Committee will quickly descend into disorder. I am sure that none of us wants that to happen.

Mr. Michael J. Foster (Worcester): I beg to move,

    That, if proceedings on the Wild Mammals (Hunting with Dogs) Bill are not completed at this day's sitting, the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock.

I want to emphasise your words, Mr. Wells, concerning the conduct of the Committee because the eyes of the country and of other hon. Members will be on us when the Committee is sitting.

I am pleased to welcome the Members of the Committee. We can contribute much of value to the debate because of our breadth of experience. The Committee includes former members of the League Against Cruel Sports and people who hunt, so it encompasses a wide range of experience. Some members of the Committee have held ministerial office and others are newly elected, which will add valuable insight to the arguments that will be borne out during the next few months.

I am pleased to say that almost all members of the Committee attended the Second Reading debate and voted on 28 November. It is fair to say that every Member of the House welcomed that debate and many comments have been made about the enjoyment of having a debate with a full House, a free vote and such a wide exchange of views. Whatever the stance taken by hon. Members, all accepted that it was a valuable debate.

I particularly enjoyed the result of the debate because of the overwhelming endorsement that was given to the Bill. The massive show of support was almost unprecedented in private Member's business--411 hon. Members voted in favour of the Bill. The percentage of hon. Members who voted--73 per cent.--more or less mirrors the feeling of British public opinion on the issue, so the House has done a service to the people of this country. We showed that we are in touch and in tune with British public opinion. The Committee and the House cannot ignore that vote and the size of it, nor the issues that were raised in the debate.

Today, we are not here to restate the arguments made on Second Reading because that is not the purpose of the Committee. The Second Reading was valuable and raised issues that had been raised before. The Committee need not concern itself with the arguments about civil liberties, jobs or traditional country practices. Our debate and the Bill, are based on the premises of animal welfare and the need to prevent cruelty to wild mammals and the Committee should concentrate on those matters.

I have also considered the role that the Committee will play in the coming few weeks--it should examine the detail of the legislation. On Second Reading, my right hon. Friend the Member for Maidstone and The Weald (Miss Widdecombe) made an eloquent speech, which was well received by all, in which she pointed out that the Committee's job is to revise and improve the Bill. If Committee members think that the Bill is badly drafted--that has been said of it--this is the time to put that right. When it reaches the statute book--as I sincerely believe it will--we should rightly be proud of the fact that we ensured that it was workable and decent legislation.

I have heard the argument about private Member's Bills being badly drafted. It was raised some 30 years ago when David Steel--Lord Steel as he now is--was involved with the passage of the Abortion Act 1967, which was opposed not because of its policy but because it was badly drafted. Now is the time for all Committee members to improve the Bill.

I can forgive some of the comments that were made on Second Reading. The right hon. and learned Member for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir N. Lyell) completely misinterpreted the Bill. I accept that that was an accident--I do not believe for one moment that he misinterpreted it deliberately. Emotions were running high and perhaps they got the better of the right hon. and learned Gentleman. The Committee's role is to make the Bill's implications clear to members of the public. For example, it has been alleged that the Bill will stop people from walking their dogs in the park because of the fear that they will chase squirrels. People who read the Bill carefully will see that that is nonsense. The Bill is not designed to have that effect and is not drafted with that end in mind. As I said, the Committee's role is to clarify such matters.

As you pointed out, Mr. Wells, the Committee's role is also to use reasoned argument to come up with suggestions for improving the Bill. The Committee should not indulge in deliberate delaying tactics--doing so would be wholly wrong. The British public and many hon. Members do not necessarily understand some of the House's more arcane practices. At the end of the day, we sit in the British public's Parliament--it is their House of Commons. As Members, we are involved in passing legislation through the House. We must be seen to be upholding democratic values as strongly and as strictly as we can. I urge all Committee members not to use delaying tactics or deliberately to filibuster. It is therefore right to condemn the comments that the hon. Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) made on Second Reading. He said that he would use every possible device to delay the Bill. That is not what we should do in Committee; we should be seeking to improve the Bill.

I prefer the attitude of the hon. Member for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway), who opposes the Bill. In a letter to one of his constituents, he wrote:

    ``However, I accept the will of the House and I write to give you an undertaking . . . my undertaking to you is that I recognise the desire of the House to pass this legislation and I will not engage in any Parliamentary tactics which will delay the Bill.''

Before any Committee member rushes to write an early-day motion to establish the date that that letter was written, I hasten to say that it was written on 8 December 1997. One does not need to go too far back in history to study that piece of information. Although the hon. Gentleman and I may not agree on every issue that we shall debate in the coming months, I find this attitude very worthy. I am sure that we all agree that the eyes of the British people are on us. We must ensure that we uphold the valuable traditions that we cherish and protect.

I hope that democracy will be seen to be working in the Committee and that delaying tactics will be put to one side. I hope that the Committee will attempt to improve the legislation to make it workable, so that when it is presented to the House, we can say that we have done a valuable job in our contribution to the debate.
