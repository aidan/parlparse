Mr. John Whittingdale (Maldon and East Chelmsford): When we adjourned, I was arguing that bingo differs greatly from other forms of gambling although it is taxed in the same way. It is almost impossible to lose a large quantity of money by playing bingo. However, other forms of gambling, such as the national lottery, set no limit on the number of tickets that punters can purchase. They may spend their entire life savings on national lottery tickets if they so wish. Similarly, those who bet on horses or on greyhounds may also stake sums that greatly exceed what they can afford. In casinos, the potential to lose very large sums of money is of course considerable. That is not the case with bingo.

Committee members who have attended a bingo session will know that there is a physical limit on the number of bingo cards that one can play in a single game. Regular players of bingo are extremely professional, and can play as many as six cards simultaneously. Personal experience has shown me that it is very difficult to keep up with just one card. I was indebted to the lady who sat next to me during that game, who managed to study my card as well as her own four, and to point out when I had made a mistake.

It is therefore virtually impossible to run more than six cards in one game. Given that each card costs no more than 50p, the maximum amount of money that can be staked in a single game is extremely low. For that reason it is impossible to chase one's losses. The financial risk attached to playing bingo is therefore wholly unlike that associated with other forms of gambling. Nevertheless, bingo has traditionally been treated as a form of gambling, and is subject to many of the same controls to which casinos and betting, for example, are subject.

Since the introduction of the national lottery, the bingo industry has been under considerable pressure. A monthly survey conducted during a two-year period showed that bingo players' propensity to play the national lottery is twice the national average. Attendances at bingo halls have therefore fallen quite substantially since the national lottery's inception. Attendances during 1997 were 8 per cent. lower than those for 1994. As a result, a number of bingo halls have gone out of business altogether. Since November 1994, 158 bingo clubs have closed down. In 1997 alone, 66 clubs closed. Attendant on that figure was the loss of 3,500 jobs.

Mr. Philip Hammond (Runnymede and Weybridge): Is it not also correct that restrictions on the advertising of bingo halls mean that those that are not well positioned have tended to close down and be replaced by halls in prominent locations, where they can overcome the tight restrictions on advertising that have prevailed until recently?

Mr. Whittingdale: That is correct. The controls governing the advertising of bingo halls and the restrictions on membership and on playing the game are another subject, about which I feel equally strongly. Perhaps now is not the time to show that, but my hon. Friend is right.

New halls have been set up and the larger halls are still making a profit. Nevertheless, the fall in attendances has put great pressure on the industry. A number of the halls that have gone out of business have undoubtedly done so as a result of no longer being economically viable.

The effect of bingo duty on the industry is determined by its structure: as it is not linked directly to the number of people attending, the amount of duty raised has not decreased at the same time. To maintain the attraction of playing bingo, proprietors of bingo halls have been adding their own money to the prize pot to make up for the fact that fewer people are paying for tickets. Bingo duty is levied not only on the stake the amount of money put up by those playing the game but on the additional money put in by the proprietor. Therefore, while takings have fallen, the amount of tax that the industry has had to pay has not fallen proportionately. The burden of the tax has been greater and proprietors have been subjected to an ever increasing squeeze.

It is worth comparing the impact of tax on bingo with the impact of tax on other forms of gambling. Those other forms are much more dangerous to the punter, who has a much greater chance of losing a lot of money, but bingo bears the heaviest tax burden. The best way to show that is to take the combined amount of duty and VAT as a proportion of net consumer spend the aggregate of the amount of money spent by those gambling in whichever form minus the amount of money returned to them as prizes. On that basis, the duty rate on the national lottery is 24 per cent., the duty rate on betting is 30.3 per cent. and the duty rate on bingo is 37.2 per cent. The figure for bingo increases to more than 40 per cent. if the effects of the additional tax on added prize money are included. Those figures show that bingo is being taxed disproportionately in comparison to other forms of gambling.

As the hon. Member for Kirkcaldy (Dr. Moonie) said, I spoke this morning about the importance of halls in local communities. Given that 66 clubs closed down in 1997 alone, it is important to be fully aware of the impact that each of those closures has on the local community. I draw the Committee's attention to a recent study, carried out by Faith Freestone of the Worcester college of higher education, called "Four Towns Study". She picked four locations where clubs closed last year: Keynsham, Fareham, Hartlepool and Paisley. I am glad to see that the hon. Member for Paisley, South (Mr. Alexander) is in his place. I am sure he would agree that the closure of a bingo club is damaging to a local community.

The study consulted 200 people who were either regular players at the clubs or employed by them. The overwhelming majority 96.6 per cent. of players said that the prime reason they played bingo was social. The study found that almost two thirds of the total sample stated that the closure of the clubs had made a difference to their existence. There are many quotes to illustrate that. A 72-year-old lady said:

    "I don't have anywhere else to go now. I miss my bingo so much."

A 58-year-old lady said:

    "It has made a lot of difference to my social life. I have lost touch with all my friends. I am just a small fish in a big pond now."

Some 70 per cent. of employees found that they faced the prospect of unemployment after the club closed.

It was readily apparent from the players' comments that there was a sense of loss of community, companionship and friendship coupled with growing feelings of social isolation. It is important to bear in mind that we are not just talking about a commercial enterprise; the loss of a bingo club will have a damaging effect on the local community.

The Henley Centre's recent major study of the bingo industry shows that profitability varies according to the type of club, but that profitability in the industry as a whole has fallen by 33 per cent. since 1994. The Henley Centre concentrated on 600 clubs which it divided into six categories. Two categories of clubs those in new buildings with medium attendance levels and those in traditional buildings with smaller attendance levels are loss making. Those two categories comprise more than 300 of the 600 clubs. It is fair to say that more than half the clubs are now operating at a loss. Indeed, since that study was carried out a few months ago, a further eight clubs have closed.

If all the clubs in those two categories were to close they are operating at a loss and some are in the process of closing the cost to the Exchequer would be £37 million in lost duty and VAT. We must also take account of additional losses the loss of VAT on the various activities that take place in a big club, the loss of corporation tax, and the loss of up to 5,000 jobs.

To amendment would address the loss of profitability in the industry by implementing a 5 per cent. cut in bingo duty. The Henley Centre used its industry model to simulate the effect of that 5 per cent. cut. In carrying out the analysis, it assumed that half the benefit from the cut would go towards the participation fee and half would be added to the prize money. The effect of the cut would be to make both the categories of clubs that are currently loss making profitable. The effective tax rate on the industry, which I mentioned earlier, would decrease from 40.8 per cent. to about 28.9 per cent., which is roughly on a par with other forms of gambling.

The industry put up a powerful case to justify why it needs help, but of course the Chancellor in his Budget ignored its pleas. Not only did he pay no attention to the industry's requests for relief through reduced bingo duty, but he made the position far worse by increasing duty on amusement with prizes machines by 20 per cent., which we debated earlier. Many of those clubs also rely on those machines.

Dr. Cable: I want to discuss the impact of the Government's tax reforms especially the taxation of savings on friendly societies.

I am sure that all Committee members know that friendly societies are mutually owned institutions, and there is a good grounding in the Committee about what mutuality means. I believe that you, Mr. Butterfill, are one of the leading lights in the all-party group on building societies, and the chairman of that group earlier made an effective contribution to the debate on demutualisation.

The principle of mutuality is well understood. Friendly societies occupy a particular niche in society: they provide a vehicle for long-term savings, usually in the form of 10-year insurance policies, which are savings rather than life policies. Traditionally, their purpose has been to attract low-income savings in traditional working-class communities. They have relied over the years on the spirit of mutual self-help and they are especially attractive in that context. They have very low overheads and relatively low management costs, and they embrace the traditional values of the society in which they grow.

It has been recognised for some years that there should be some tax exemption for such long-term savings. The previous Government granted, perhaps rather grudgingly, a limited amount of tax relief initially up to £100 a year in 1984, and the figure gradually increased to, I believe, £275 in 1994. That was a useful but modest tax concession to that method of saving.

I have moved the new clause because the situation has changed under the Labour Government in two significant ways. The first change involves the introduction of individual savings accounts. The philosophy behind ISAs is to attract low-income savers, and the scheme may be a considerable success when it is launched. However, one of its main features is that it will encourage instant access it will encourage liquidity rather than long-term savings.

I should remind Labour Members of their election manifesto commitments on the subject they are highly germane to our discussion. The election manifesto said:

    "We will introduce a new individual savings account and extend the principle of TESSAs and Peps to promote long-term saving."

Whatever the scheme does it will promote not long-term savings but instant access and liquidity a very different principle is in operation.

Mr. Andrew Love (Edmonton): Will the hon. Gentleman accept that instant access is necessary for the scheme to attract the low-income savers for whom it is designed?

Mr. Cable: Yes, I accept that. I do not intend to criticise the principle of instant access to ISAs. It is desirable to attract a particular class of saver but, in attracting that class of saver, one needs to be mindful of those people who have committed themselves to long-term savings and the effect that ISAs will have on them. For example, if someone invests a £1,000 a year premium in a friendly society, about three quarters of the profits on that will be taxed, but an investment of the equivalent sum in an ISA will be tax exempt. It is almost certain that the friendly society product will become virtually unsaleable. Our proposal would correct the imbalance that the Government have inadvertently created.

The second reason that this is an important and topical issue on which the Government should reflect seriously is that friendly societies appear to play a key role in the Government's thinking on stakeholder pensions. The Minister for Welfare Reform, the right hon. Member for Birkenhead (Mr. Field), who is the driving force behind many reforms, has said several times that he envisages mutual institutions performing the key role in mobilising pensions. That role may be exclusive; at the very least, they will have a special status. I am not sure that the Treasury has thought through the implications of creating serious difficulties for the friendly societies in attracting their normal savers, especially when other parts of the Government regard the institutions as the principal vehicle for welfare reform.

The Association of Friendly Societies worked hard on the new clause and new schedule. It tried to ensure that they are technically compatible with the requirements of tax legislation. For example, they are designed to accommodate the requirement that there should be regular premiums throughout the year.

I am specifically asking the Paymaster General to give the matter careful thought. I am sure that the Government will argue that friendly societies could simply float their own ISAs. However, many smaller friendly societies are not in a position to meet the regulatory requirements, in particular the stipulation that they must give best advice. Many of the larger friendly societies will be disadvantaged because they will no longer offer a distinct product. The purpose of the friendly societies is to offer one major distinct product a long-term savings account which is linked to insurance. By competing with a large number of other financial institutions that offer ISAs, their distinctiveness as mutual institutions will be lost.

I hope that the Paymaster General will recognise that the situation has changed since the previous Government were in office. It requires a fresh look at tax exemption. I also hope that he will raise the limit from £270 to £1,000 because that would make friendly society tax treatment compatible and competitive with ISA provision.

Mr. Geoffrey Robinson: Let me start by saying that the Government strongly support the ideals of the friendly society movement. The encouragement of thrift and providing protection of savings for those on modest incomes is worth while. To reflect that, the Government have done much in the Bill to help friendly societies. The hon. Gentleman will be aware that clause 88, for example, allows payment of tax credits on exempted business for a further five years. I shall outline the nub of the problem. The Insurance Companies Act 1982 and European regulations surrounding the insurance market make it clear that insurance companies a definition that includes the insurance operations of Lloyd's of London need to be independently audited. The 1982 Act requires that a certificate be furnished by the auditor to the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry, stating that the requirements of the audit have been met and, in particular, that the auditor has made an independent assessment of the assets and liabilities of the syndicate and that he is satisfied that the assets are adequate to cover the liabilities. That is important, as it is the Secretary of State who must be satisfied that those requirements have been met. However, certificates in accordance with the 1982 Act have not been issued.

It became apparent early on that auditors were unable or unwilling to fulfil the obligations placed on them by the 1982 Act. A famous letter I believe it is known in legal circles as the Neville Russell letter was written to Lloyd's making it clear that the auditors were concerned about the vast scale of potential liabilities overhanging Lloyd's and about their unquantifiable nature. It drew attention to the fact that on a strict interpretation of the obligations that the 1982 Act placed on them, they would have to qualify the accounts of, in effect, the entire market.

As a result of discussions in response to that letter, Lloyd's auditors were required, by guidance that the society issued, to rely on underwriters' assessment of the liabilities that the syndicates faced. The certificates that were furnished to the Secretary of State under the guise of the certificates that were required by the 1982 Act were not based on an independent audit of the assets and liabilities of the syndicates. By virtue of the guidance that Lloyd's issued, the auditors simply accepted the estimates of liability that were provided by the underwriters.

The problem is that members who joined the society during the later 1980s saw sets of accounts that suggested that the syndicates were profitable. It does not take a genius to work out that the profitability or otherwise of a syndicate depends on an assessment of the provision for losses that will occur in what I understand is called long-tailed business. That involves business that, by its very nature, may give rise to claims many years after the premiums have been paid. That class of business involves, in particular, asbestosis, which has greatly affected Lloyd's of London.

From the early 1980s, underwriting profits were consistently overstated and tax was paid to the Inland Revenue by underwriters on the basis of profits that were overstated. The accounts were supplied to outsiders who were thinking of joining the market. In its prospectus recruiting new members to the market, Lloyd's emphasised the extremely high quality and standards of its independent audit requirements. Many people joined the market during that period expecting that the accounts that they had seen and which had been certified by the Secretary of State were true and fair representations of the position of the syndicates that they were joining.

An underwriter who joined the market in 1991 told me, "As events subsequently unfolded, I was bankrupt the day that I joined, but I thought that I was joining a profitable and successful syndicate."

Members who joined under those circumstances feel that the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry failed in respectively over the years his or her duty to protect investors and to ensure that the Insurance Companies Act 1982 was enforced in relation to certificates that were submitted to the Secretary of State by auditors.

I am not convinced that a further cut would have a positive impact on pools turnover: I doubt whether it would help.

New clause 13 is unworkable, as I am sure the right hon. Member for Wells realises. It is technically flawed, and would not achieve its aim. More importantly, it would create an onerous and bureaucratic system of data collection for both the trade and officials. It would be impossible for the Treasury to satisfy itself through this form of monitoring that reductions in turnover related solely to competition from the lottery. How could we be sure of that? Any number of factors including the weather or the world cup could have their influence. The Opposition have not produced a very cleverly crafted new clause. They might like to reflect on the rather unusual course they propose of introducing important taxation changes through secondary legislation, rather than through the full parliamentary scrutiny achieved under primary legislation a process that the Opposition have urged at each of our sittings. If the turnover in those industries increased, would the Opposition be content for the duty rate to be increased by that method?

I assure the Committee that as part of the Budget considerations, we shall continue to consider carefully those duties and the ability of industries, particularly the pools industry, to bear them. We shall also keep in mind the significant impact of the lottery on that industry, which is an important part of our sporting and gambling life. I hope that that assurance will enable the right hon. Gentleman to withdraw his unworkable new clause.

 

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I am grateful to the Paymaster General for acknowledging that the industries are affected in the way that we have detailed, although I have to say that when he takes refuge in the technical deficiencies, real or supposed, of our amendments or new clauses, it is obvious that he is trying to hide the fact that he is on weak ground.

We have sought to highlight the issue and to ask the Government to make their own assessment which can never be scientifically precise of the effect of the national lottery on the turnover of other industries. Such matters are never entirely mechanical, but involve a degree of speculation and assessment, as is made clear in the Coopers and Lybrand report, of which I am sure that the Government have a copy.

In the light of the Paymaster General's assurance that the matters will be kept under review, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.
