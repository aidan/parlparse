Clause 41

Mr. Clappison: The amendments relate to the point at which time limits should begin to run. The present framework of time limits under the Prosecution of Offences Act 1985 sets the time at which the defendant is charged as the point at which time begins to run for the purpose of the time limits. The Government wish to take the time of arrest as that point in the case of young offenders.

Conservative Members believe that it is a worthy objective that police officers should make swift decisions about whether to charge a person. However, we have some anxieties that we wish to put before the Committee.

Arrest is a step in the process of an investigation; it is not the completion of that investigation. That is an important point to remember. When a police officer has a reasonable suspicion that a person has committed an offence, he arrests the person so that an investigation can proceed.

To put the matter simply, we need to cater for cases in which a little time might be needed, after arrest, to gather enough evidence for a charge to be brought: for example, if a person is arrested and it becomes clear after arrest that scientific evidence is needed before the police can decide whether to bring a charge. Such circumstances must be accommodated. DNA tests have been useful in important cases. Such tests can take a long time. If an officer makes an arrest and a DNA test is required, will the time that is spent waiting for the results count as part of the time limits on the proceedings? Will all the time tick away in that period?

Will a defendant's release after arrest affect the time limits? Does the time limit begin at the point of first arrest and run until the next stage, when the defendant is charged? If a defendant is arrested and released on bail without charge, pending further inquiries, and is rearrested, does the time between the first and second arrest count?

The provision applies only to younger offenders--those of 17 years or younger. Do the Government intend to extend the use of time limits to older offenders? Perhaps our understanding of the way in which the time limits will operate is incorrect. If so, I am sure that we will be corrected.

We ask the Government to take account of the pressure that time limits may put on police officers. Swift decisions by police officers about whether to charge are a worthy objective, but the police should not be under too much pressure to complete a time-limit stage. They should make as full an investigation as possible without feeling pressurised to bring a charge. The Times today contains a report about the senior officer in charge of the Stephen Lawrence case. The article states:

    ``The senior officer in charge of the Stephen Lawrence murder inquiry admitted yesterday that he made a critical mistake in not arresting up to five suspects at the earliest opportunity.''

We do not want police officers to delay arrest in future simply because they are worried that that will set the time limits ticking. Ministers must consider that carefully.

The police should not be under pressure to take short cuts or to ignore some lines of inquiry in case they turn out to be too lengthy. Police officers should be able to complete their investigations thoroughly. Investigations should be timely, but thorough.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): The hon. Member for Hertsmere has made legitimate points, which I hope that I can answer satisfactorily. Clause 41 provides for the introduction of a statutory time limit for the period between arrest and first listing, and conviction and sentence. The amendment would alter the definition of the time limit in paragraph (a) so that it would run from charge to first listing instead of from arrest to first listing. The decision to use the arrest as the starting point was made with reference to our key pledge to halve the time between arrest and sentencing for persistent young offenders. The amendment would undermine our wish to administer justice expeditiously, particularly for young offenders; it is very important in their case.

Our plans for statutory time limits tackle the need for a speedy response to offending by young people. Delays can impede justice and frustrate the victims of crime. It is important that a clear link should be established in the mind of offenders between their behaviour and the sentence. All parts of the youth justice system must therefore work as quickly as possible. Swift police decisions about whether to charge are an important part of that process.

In some parts of the country the interval between arrest and charge is several weeks and there have been complaints about delays and the approach that the police sometimes take to investigating cases. I appreciate that it is not desirable to put improper pressure on the police to progress quickly. However, it is proper to put pressure on them to recognise the imperative to forge a link in a young person's mind between offending and its outcome. Setting the time limits to begin with charge rather than arrest would undermine our attempts to speed up the youth justice system.

The Government accept that several factors can be outside the control of the police in determining the length of an investigation prior to charging a suspect. However, the police can take some measures to manage the process and resolve it more speedily. Currently the main cause of delay between charge and arrest is the practice, which in some instances has become almost routine, of referring a case to a multi-agency panel before deciding whether a charge or a caution is appropriate. The Government have issued guidance that such referrals should be avoided. The new final warnings scheme should end the practice. The guidance on that scheme will be issued in due course and will allow the police to take decisions in those matters swiftly, without needing to consult others.

The hon. Member for Hertsmere rightly raised such issues as the obtaining of scientific, and particularly DNA, information. The time taken to collect DNA information and get test results would count in the time limits, but it would be possible to extend the limit by making an application, so that the courts were aware of what was happening. As to the question of re-arrest, the time limit would not be affected by release following arrest.

Under the provision the police would have the scope to minimise the time between arrest and charge, by taking decisions swiftly without consulting too many other agencies.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: I shall deal first with the points raised by the hon. Member for Hertsmere, with which I have a degree of sympathy. The aim of the provision is severely to circumscribe the circumstances in which such hearings will take place. Lord Denning said, ``If we have to trust anyone, trust the judges.'' We put them into positions of authority to ensure that the interests of justice are served.
