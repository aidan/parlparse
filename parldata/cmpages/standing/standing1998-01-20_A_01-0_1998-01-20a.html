That first point essentially concerned the convenience of honourable Members. To those outside the Committee, the nature of the Bill is of much greater concern. The most important clauses enable the Government to introduce major issues of education policy by regulation. To any who seriously argued that that was not true, I would simply point out the first line of clause 1, which provides that

    “The Secretary of State may by regulations . . . impose limits on class sizes”

and so forth. The very first line of clause 1 reveals that the bill works by giving Ministers the power to make policy by regulation. We will not be able to have sensible discussion of the nature of the powers that are proposed under the Bill unless the Government give a clear statement of how they intend to use those powers.

I hope that the Minister will give an undertaking that before the Committee is asked to discuss clauses that give Ministers regulation-making powers—some of my hon. Friends may want to apply this point to the parts of the Bill that particularly concern them—he will publish, ideally, a draft of the regulations or, at the very least, a clear statement of the nature and purpose of the proposed regulations.

Let me illustrate the importance of that point by referring to a few clauses that rely on regulation-making powers. I have mentioned clause 1, which will give effect to the Government’s election pledge to limit infant class sizes to 30. That is not specified in the Bill, however; the clause gives the Minister power to introduce regulations to impose limits on class sizes and to fix the dates by which those limits should apply.

When do the Government intend to deliver their election pledge? Moreover, what is the nature of that election pledge? The concept of a class size of 30 does not find expression in the Bill, so what regulations will the Government introduce under the clause 1? The Minister’s hon. Friends may also want to ask such questions because, at the end of this Parliament, they will have to face the electorate against the background of the Labour party’s election pledges.

Clause 2 gives the Government further powers to make regulations to determine the precise nature of the plans that local education authorities must prepare to show how they will deliver the class size limits under clause 1. Clause 2(2) states:

    “The statement shall . . . take such form, as may be prescribed”.

The clause also provides the Government with the power to prescribe who will be consulted over preparation of the plans. We need to know about the regulations before we consider the statutory basis of the regulation-making powers.

The Committee will remember that the Government said that they would pay for smaller infant class sizes by using money saved from the assisted places scheme. Clause 3 provides for the financial implications of clauses 1 and 2. Again, the provision of cash to enable LEAs to pay for the infant class size policy will be determined by regulations. If the Minister’s hon. Friends are interested in the deliverability of the pledge, they should ask, as LEAs will, about the regulations to be introduced under clause 3: how much will they provide for and how will that money be distributed between individual LEAs?

I mention the first three clauses, but I could pick on almost any passage of the Bill to make the same point: the detail of the Bill rests in regulation. It is hard for us to have a serious discussion of the measure without knowing more about the regulations that the Government intend to introduce.

Let me briefly reflect on the other key passages of the Bill where the same principle applies. Under clause 21, the basis of foundation schools will be defined by regulation. Member for Bath might be interested to know about foundation schools, especially as his track record on foundation and grant-maintained schools is one of an interesting evolution of policy.

The importance of understanding what will be in the regulations also applies to codes of practice. I am especially interested in two codes of practice. The first is that on admissions policy, which is paved by clause 75. The question of who is responsible for schools admissions and on what terms has been the centre of much political debate about schools policy for many years. Clause 75 begins:

    “The Secretary of State shall issue, and may from time to time revise, a code of practice containing such practical guidance as he thinks appropriate”.

It is impossible to have a sensible discussion about admissions policy on that basis. I hope that the Minister will be able to give the Committee a firm undertaking that a draft code of practice will be published and available to the Committee before we reach clause 75. That is a key part of the Bill; we cannot have a sensible discussion of admissions policy while the code of practice is held up the Government’s sleeve. I am not making an overtly partisan point—any fair-minded observer would consider that unreasonable.

The other code of practice in which I am interested is one about which Ministers are fond of talking, but which I believe—the Minister may correct me if I am wrong—is not paved in the Bill: the promised code of practice containing the Government’s view of the proper role of a local education authority. I have left that until last because it is in some senses the most unsatisfactory aspect of the Committee’s position.

One of the key questions raised by the Bill is where power should rest in the school system between the school, the governing body and the head teacher—the school as a local institution—the local education authority and the Secretary of State. The Bill has been subject to a wide variety of interpretations. Some LEAs have made no secret of the fact that they consider the Bill to be an opportunity to re-establish a direct managerial responsibility for schools that have established a degree of independence from them; Ministers have been reported as saying, at least in private, if not in semi- public, that the Bill provides an opportunity for them to monitor and control the function of LEAs. They believe that it will take power away from LEAs and give it to the Secretary of State or, more precisely, in the routine business of government, to officials in the Department for Education and Employment.

If the Committee is to understand the Government’s intentions about who will be responsible for taking key decisions in the management of the schools service—the management responsibilities set out in the Bill—it needs access to the code of practice on the proper role of the LEA, which was promised by Ministers. It is also unsatisfactory for a Bill to be presented to the House of Commons seeking to redefine the relationship between schools, LEAs and the DFEE by a Government who have promised to set out in a code of practice their view of the future of LEAs, when the code of practice has no rooting in the Bill.

I seek from the Minister the assurance, not merely that the promised code of practice will be available to the Committee when we discuss the relevant parts of the Bill, but that that code of practice, once promulgated, will be statutory. if it is not, the obvious observation will be: it is fine to have a code of practice setting out what the Government think ought to happen, but how will it be enforced? The Government’s opinion is very interesting, but the statute is the statute. The Government should have a statutory basis for offering guidance to which the LEAs or schools—and, ultimately, the courts—must pay attention, rather than having the Secretary of State circulating a private letter. What are the Government’s intentions and when will they publish the code of practice? What are their intentions in terms of rooting it in the Bill, so that the provisions of the code of practice, when they are seen and understood, can be seen to have some practical influence on the evolution of schools’ policy?

I hope that Ministers will recognise those as serious questions that need to be addressed before the Committee gets into the meat of the Bill.
