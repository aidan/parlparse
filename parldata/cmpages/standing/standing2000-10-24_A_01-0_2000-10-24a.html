Clause 30

Remuneration of trustees of charitable trusts

Mr. Stephen O'Brien (Eddisbury): I beg to move amendment No. 3, in page 11, line 23, at end insert—

    `(2A) Regulations under this section shall not make provision for any payment which the Charity Commission has no power to authorise.'.

I do not want to take up too much of the Committee's time but I should like to start by setting the scene and putting the amendments in context. The purpose of the Bill is to update the powers of trustees and make them more appropriate to the complexity of modern trusts. The Bill deals with the current limitations on trustees in relation to delegation and investment, which is welcomed by Opposition Members. However, the Bill raises certain concerns in the not-for-profit sector, some of which were discussed in the House of Lords at Second Reading and on Report. In particular, the powers to remunerate trustees have caused what little debate there has been on the Bill and I wish to raise a couple of issues on that matter.

Amendments made in the Lords Grand Committee on 7 June have made the Bill's provisions more acceptable to the not-for-profit sector, but a serious concern remains. It is appropriate to raise this in Committee because we are all seeking to implement the best possible legislation, albeit that it is somewhat inchoate in scope, as the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett) said. I hope that we will be able to avoid revising it too much in the short term.

Although we are discussing an amendment to clause 30, the matter partly relates to clauses 28 and 29, which we passed over. The powers included in clauses 28, 29 and 30 relate to remuneration. Indeed, clause 28 provides for the trust instrument to entitle corporate trustees and trustees acting in a professional capacity to payment from trust funds for services to the trust. Trustees acting in a professional capacity are those whose profession or business involves the management or administration of trusts. The clause provides for professional trustees to be paid for such services to the trust, even if these could have been provided by a lay trustee.

Clause 29 deals with exclusions for charitable trusts and requires that payment to professional trustees must have the support of the whole board. Payments may then be made for any services provided.

The position for charities is set out in clause 30. Before I concluded that it was appropriate to table an amendment, I read with some care the contribution of Lord Phillips of Sudbury at Second Reading in the other place, where he discussed the matter at some length. Clause 30, as amended, provides for the Secretary of State to make regulations for the remuneration of professional trustees and trust corporations who are charity trustees. The clause allows for different provision to be made for different cases and types of charity, which is crucial given the enormous range of charitable purposes and charitable trusts that now exist. It requires the power to be created by statutory instrument, which must be laid before Parliament and approved by each House.

The Bill as originally drafted caused concern in the not-for-profit sector and contradicted the responses of the recent Charity Commission consultation exercise on the payment of charity trustees. Charities are governed by voluntary trustees, who have an obligation to act in the charity's best interests and to put those interests first. They are not to benefit from the trust, and the principle of voluntary trusteeship has helped to reduce the risk of trustees using charity funds to their own advantage. Trustees are often faced with conflicts of interest and the voluntary principle provides the context in which difficult decisions must be made.

Changes in the charity environment have caused some to question the voluntary principle. Charities have become significant providers of public services on behalf of the Government and operate under complex funding arrangements. That has encouraged many charities to seek trustees who have professional skills. In addition, the beneficiaries or users of charities are becoming increasingly involved in charity governance. Many of those trustees will be on low incomes and may suffer a loss of earnings due to their commitments as trustees. To a high degree, that becomes the nub of the issue.

It has also been argued that financial inducements would address difficulties in recruiting high-quality trustees. We cannot divorce ourselves from the effects of the Bill on the quality of future charitable trustees. The Charity Commission already has the authority to allow the payment of trustees and has dealt with questions of payment on a case-by-case basis. There has been an increase in the number of requests for permission to pay trustees, which is what led to the consultation exercise. Most applications related to the payment of trustees for professional work undertaken separately from the trusteeship.

The classic example is the solicitor who charges for legal services such as conveyancing or contract negotiation. At this point, I must declare an interest in that I remain on the roll of solicitors. However, I have not practised in 12 years and I would not ask anyone to trust my advice at a stage in my career that is so distant from my professional practice. The commission—this important point goes to heart of the amendment—also includes blue-collar professions in that category.

For example, a builder who sits on the board of a charitable community centre may receive payment for building an extension to it. The commission recommends that the potential conflicts of interest presented by such cases be managed properly by an open and fair process of awarding contracts for professional work.

It is worth looking at the commission's submission to the consultation document. Views are sought on the payment of trustees for professional services, the payment of trustees as members of staff—traditionally, members of a charity's staff cannot serve on its board—and reimbursement for loss of earnings. Respondents to that document supported the continuation of the voluntary principle and favoured the status quo. No doubt the commission will continue to consider applications case by case, and I understand that it is due to issue new guidance shortly.

The Bill is causing potential conflicts—some would argue that they are interesting, but I would describe them as challenging—to emerge. There was a strong presumption in the responses to the commission's consultation against the payment of charity trustees, yet clause 30 proposes to empower the Secretary of State to create a statutory instrument to that effect. The wording of clause 30 has been amended from the original Law Commission draft, which applied to all charity trustees, to restrict its application to corporate trustees and trustees working in a professional capacity. It also requires that each House approve any statutory instruments, whereas the original draft said that either House could annul them.

Those amendments are welcome, but they also pose the question; what would a statutory instrument add to the commission's existing power, and indeed its willingness, to authorise the payment of professional trustees on an individual basis? It is difficult to foresee how wider powers to pay charity trustees might be developed without altering the nature of charity trusteeship.

With my right hon. and hon. Friends, I have tabled an amendment to limit the role of the Secretary of State to the degree of discretion currently enjoyed by the Charity Commission. To go beyond that would take away the proper and objective role, and indeed the experience, of the commission, which most, if not all, would admit has conducted itself in a thoroughly objective and even-handed manner.

I do not want to take up the Committee's time by reading the relevant parts of the Second Reading debate in the other place, but I draw the attention of the Minister and the Committee to the views expressed by Lord Phillips of Sudbury, who was supported, to some degree, by Lord Dahrendorf. We need to bear in mind the words of the Lord Chancellor when he opened that debate. He said that

    trust law governing the powers and duties of trustees has not kept pace with the evolving social and economic role trusts now fulfil.—[Official Report, House of Lords, 14 April 2000; Vol. 612, c. 373.]

I pray that in aid in seeking the Minister's acceptance of the amendment.

The amendment is designed to limit the power, so that

I am grateful to the hon. Member for Eddisbury for raising his concerns, but the aim of the amendment is not clear. It might be helpful if I explain. Those powers cannot be used to authorise trustees to do anything expressly forbidden by, or for which other explicit arrangements are made in, any constitutional provision in the trust documents.

However, the commission also has the power by scheme under section 16 to remove or modify any such constitutional provision and can therefore, as a matter of vires, confer the relevant remuneration authority, even in the face of existing constitutional incompatibility. To speak of

    payments which the Charity Commission has no power to authorise

is meaningless, because the commission has, by its constitution under the Act, the power to authorise any payment. In practice, the commission's power is not limited, so the category of occasions that the hon. Gentleman wants to exclude is a category with no occasions.

Clause 30 enables the Secretary of State to make different provision for different cases. Whatever else he might do, I find it hard to credit that, if the clause were ever considered for implementation, a Secretary of State would be tempted to give charitable trustees default powers that went far beyond those provided for non-charitable trustees. The hon. Member for Eddisbury mentioned the Charity Commissioners' power in individual cases. Clearly, the charity would incur an administrative expense.
