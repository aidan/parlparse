Mr. Page: I welcome you to the Committee, Mrs. Michie. I assure you that the little disagreement that you have just heard take place is unusual for the Committee. Our first day passed, if not in sweetness and light, at least in general agreement about the purpose of the Bill. We all want it to succeed and to work well in practice. However, several shortcomings will be exposed when the Bill is implemented.

I was approaching the end of my remarks about a clutch of amendments when the time beat us at 1 o'clock on Tuesday. The amendments fall into two sets. The first set was tabled to highlight the possible downside if the moratoriums do not work; I have been through the problems that could occur. I response to the remarks made by hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett), I pointed out that my aim in my observations was to suggest what might happen if the moratoriums failed and creditors did not receive the moneys that they would have received if a receiver had been brought in a lot earlier. The Minister said that what I had described was unlikely to happen because shareholders, owners and managers would want the moratoriums to succeed. However, I am not sure that that will always be the case.

Companies may reach that rocky point in their existence by several routes. Companies that were once profitable may be unable to continue trading for structural reasons—because something has occurred to change the ground rules. For example, a small grocery that has been running profitably for years, enabling its owners to pay the bills and keep their family, may suffer from the impact of a new supermarket being built in the town. The owners may wonder whether the problem is temporary and whether they can trade through it until their customers come back to their shop, in which case they would be strongly tempted to ask for a moratorium to allow things to be reassessed after trading patterns settled down. They would want the moratorium to succeed because their business has a successful history, and they would want that to continue after they have overcome the commercial pressures put on them by the supermarket.

On the other hand, there are companies that, for years, bounce along their overdraft limit until, eventually, the owners write a cheque too far, whereupon the bank manager says ``Enough's enough'' and takes the appropriate action. The owners may apply for a moratorium on the grounds that their business has been going for years and that the difficulty is only temporary. What would happen, however, if the owners realised during the company's moratorium period that they had no chance of trading out of their problems? Would they not be strongly inclined to acquire some of the assets at preferential rates—possibly a rate of zero? It is difficult to estimate value inside a company because some stock and assets may be written down to well below their value. The problem of walking assets has been a nightmare for receivers and liquidators over the years. To put the matter bluntly, some unscrupulous directors or managers of companies may try to use the moratorium to get what they can out of the company, while they have the time and opportunity.

Every small business man or woman should be able to judge whether the company started or supported by the loan will fail; if they are unable to make that judgment, they are the wrong sort of people to be running a business. It might be argued that the change unnecessarily affords protection to a company even after it has traded out of its difficulties and exited the moratorium successfully, to which we would all say amen. It would be rather bizarre if, many years later, a successful and valuable company were entitled to avoid a security that was—arguably—freely and justifiably given. To allow that might create an unjustifiable and unnecessary statutory intervention in the marketplace.

What will happen if moratoriums fail to any great extent, resulting in lower returns to debenture holders and those famous walking assets that we discussed on Tuesday? Perhaps your eye, Mrs. Michie, will be caught in a moment for another expansion on that aspect—I leave it to you to decide. If debenture holders know that security can be rendered unenforceable for up to three months, and if moratoriums fail in significant numbers, they will immediately adjust their lending criteria downward. They will not lend the same proportion of the lending value of a company's assets as they would have without that three-month risk. That will seriously affect funding for new businesses.

One of the tasks of the nominee, the person who decides whether a moratorium should be granted, will be to decide the amount of funding that will be available for the company to trade in that period. All of us should consider that, because there is no point in granting moratoriums if no funding is available to run the business during the moratorium period. As we have all said, the moratorium is the main plank of the rescue culture. If a company is to continue trading during a moratorium, it must have funding, but no bank debenture holder will continue an overdraft facility if the right to appoint an administrative receiver and crystallise that floating charge is removed. The bank will cancel the overdraft immediately, and the moratorium will fail. I want the Committee to be certain how people on the other side of the coin will view the decisions that it makes and the legislation that results.

I did not speak in great detail to amendment No. 15, because its provisions appeared an amendment tabled in another place by my colleague, Baroness Buscombe, on 4 April, and because Government amendment No. 40 addresses the problem in question. I would not have tabled amendment No. 15 had I known that Government amendment No. 40 would be tabled. Nevertheless, it should be noted that it took the Government seven months to produce their own provision. It is right and sensible that the amendments will prevent a landlord from re-entering premises when a moratorium is in force. The provisions as originally drafted prevented all creditors, even secure ones, from enforcing their rights, except landlords, who had a right peacefully to enter their premises in the event of a failure to pay rent or a breach of a condition of a tenancy. I am delighted that the Government have proposed such an important improvement.

I have said several times that the act of the moratorium may fail or perform badly. However, I was disappointed that the Minister did not respond to amendment No. 19, which we believe to be most important. If a moratorium is to succeed, as we would all want, it is essential that the company in difficulties is assisted by the Bill's other provisions. I hope that the hon. Member for Great Grimsby realises that I am trying to make a balanced argument, so that I cannot be accused of trying to destroy the Bill's essential aim. Paragraph 14 of new schedule A1 undermines the prospect of a company being rescued by a moratorium while offering creditors an unnecessary and excessive degree of protection.

The problem of getting funding while a moratorium is in force will, more than any other factor, kill the chance of more moratoriums being started. Almost inevitably, a company under a moratorium will need to locate a source of finance if it is to survive; that is vital and I see no point in having a moratorium without it. Under the Companies Act 1985, the courts learned to scrutinise the source of a company's credit and whether it had sufficient funding to continue trading under the statutory protection afforded by the administration order. The principle is exactly the same under moratorium arrangements. It has been recognised in the Bill that on-going funding will be essential.

Against that background, paragraph 14 would deprive the company of the ability to grant good security; that will make life difficult. From my business experience I can tell the Committee that I have seen exactly how that can happen, and I declare all sorts of knowledge and interests. I know of a business that wanted to carry out a profitable development that would put it into loss for the period of the development. It told the bank what was going to happen and the bank said, ``Fine.'' The change was to take about five months and included a lot of rebuilding. Two weeks before the end of the project, the bank manager lost his nerve and said, ``I think we'd better have someone run a rule over the company.'' Coopers & Lybrand did so and, bearing in mind that the company was profitable, concluded that the changes that it had introduced should enable it to break even, but that if Coopers & Lybrand was asked to give advice and management guidance, the company would make a profit of one unit. Fortunately, the company had considerable assets and it was able to go to another lender. Within a year, it was not only breaking even, but making three to four times the profit that Coopers & Lybrand had calculated it would make if had been asked to give its valuable managerial advice. If the company had not had substantial assets, it would have been squeezed and landed in serious trouble.

Companies must be given every opportunity to obtain funding while a moratorium takes place. One part of the Bill should not act in detriment to another.
