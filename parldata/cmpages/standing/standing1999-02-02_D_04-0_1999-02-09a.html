Mr. Gibb: I beg to move amendment No. 42, in page 3, line 12, at end add—

    ``(8) The accounting conventions published by the Office for National Statistics in `UK National Accounts: Concept Sources and Materials', in respect of the accounting treatment of tax credits shall apply to the tax credits.''.

The amendment seeks to enshrine in the Bill the way in which the tax credits will be accounted for and presented in the Government's Green, Red and Blue Books.

We have seen on many occasions how the Government have manipulated the presentation not only of the tax credit but of many other items in the Red and Green Books. For that reason, Opposition Members now feel that it is necessary to begin to enshrine in legislation how such measures should be presented in the accounts, so that the numbers are presented fairly and transparently.

In July last year, the tax credit was excluded from social security expenditure in the comprehensive spending review. It was hidden away on page 16 of the review, Cm 4011, under an obscure line in table 2, ``Annually Managed Expenditure''. The line ``Accounting and other adjustments'' will increase from £13.4 billion in 1998-99 to £20 billion by 2001-02. Probing through parly Questions revealed that the tax credit expenditure was included in that line rather than in the line on social security benefits. It included the element paid in the pay packet over and above the reduction in tax. It also includes direct payments to the recipient. The amendment would avoid that.

The amendment mentions the publication, ``UK National Accounts: Concept Resources and Methods'' produced by the Office for National Statistics. Paragraphs 21.69-71 refer to the distinction between tax credits and how we should account for them. The gist of the principle is that where a tax credit is merely a benefit paid through the tax system and not part and parcel of that tax system, it should not be taken out of negative taxation. The ONS insists that if an allowance or genuine tax credit results in a taxpayer paying less tax to the Exchequer, there is a case for regarding that expenditure as negative income tax. Instead of recording it as income tax in and expenditure out, it is recorded as less income tax received.

Personal tax allowances are treated in the same way. They are not regarded as public expenditure, but they do reduce the amount of tax that we pay. They are accounted for by the Treasury in how much tax is raised. Because of personal tax allowances, the Treasury raises that must less income tax. But a tax credit is ambiguous. For example, mortgage tax relief allows us to pay less money to a building society. That could be construed in two ways: either as a subsidy paid to building societies by the Government, which makes it part of public expenditure, or as a mortgage holder paying less income tax to the Government by virtue of having a mortgage.

The ONS decided to set down clear guidelines. It said that, if a tax allowance is to be treated as negative taxation, it must meet three criteria: first, that the benefits to the individual taxpayer do not exceed the amount of tax paid by that individual; secondly, that the tax allowance is made as a matter of economic policy; and thirdly, that the allowance is an integral part of the tax system. To determine whether a tax credit is an integral part of the tax system, we must see whether the refund or allowance can be calculated independently of the procedure for calculating an individual's tax payable, for which we should use different criteria. The ONS went on to say that the refund or allowance should not

    ``depend on the actual amount of tax to be paid, or the marginal rate of tax''

and that it should not be construed as part of the tax system.

Tax relief on mortgages was available at all rates of tax, but that has been reduced over the years to just 15 per cent. It could be argued, however, that because the level of mortgage tax relief would depend on a taxpayer's marginal tax rates, that would be a genuine tax credit. Therefore, any loss of revenue caused by that measure could be account for as lost revenue rather than public expenditure.

Where the same benefit is given to taxpayers by way of tax allowances and to non-taxpayers by way of cash benefits—as with WFTC—the ONS is clear that the availability of the rebate to non-taxpayers is usually a strong indicator that it is not negative taxation. The ONS believes that WFTC should not be regarded as negative taxation.

Between publishing the comprehensive spending review in July and the ``Pre-Budget Report'' in November, the Government changed the accounting procedure. They accepted that the tax credit should be construed as public expenditure. The Library briefing says that the effect of the change in accounting is to add £2.7 billion to total managed expenditure in 1997-98.

That is largely the result of private health insurance premiums, residual life assurance premium relief and, on a smaller scale, mortgage tax relief. But by 2000-2001, when WFTC will feed through, the effect of the change will be about £2.5 billion to £2.75 billion.

The Government have accepted that the tax credit should be regarded as part of public expenditure and not hidden away in ``Accounting and other adjustments'' because it has become regarded as tax forgone.
