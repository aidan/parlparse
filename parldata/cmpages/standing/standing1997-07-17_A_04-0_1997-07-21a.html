Friends who are new to the House and who come from constituencies where Labour had not previously enjoyed overwhelming support, now find themselves with massive majorities because of the wide recognition of the new Labour Government's concern for industry. This can be seen clearly in clause 18, which reduces the main rate and the small companies rate of corporation tax from 1 April 1997. Both rates are reduced by 2 per cent.: the main rate from 33 per cent. to 31 per cent.; and the small companies rate from 23 per cent. to 21 per cent.

When the Paymaster General spoke recently about the pressures faced by small companies, I was conscious of the delight that many such companies experience when they find that they have reached a position where they can pay corporation tax. I say this with some feeling as before being elected I was chief executive of a company that had the responsibility of establishing small to medium-sized enterprises. Our aim was to ensure that they quickly reached a position where they could pay corporation tax.

The Government have reduced corporation tax rates at the first opportunity. Uniquely, we are the first Government to have backdated a tax cut to before we took office.

From discussions with my constituents and others, I am aware that the cut is greatly welcomed. It is the first cut in the main rate of corporation tax since 1991. Those of us from less-developed parts of the UK welcome this reduction as it helps us to attract inward investment.

In my constituency, one of the poorest in Scotland, we are anxious to attract such investment. While the hon. Member for Daventry can pray in aid his mother's interest in the Bill, my husband--a senior executive of a multinational company that is involved in an inward investment project--reminds me daily of the importance of corporation tax rates in encouraging inward investment.

I visited Ireland last year to look at its regional development structure. I was much impressed by the arguments that were made about corporation tax levels. The Government's action on corporation tax, which seems to be attracting no interest among Opposition Members, enhances the UK's position as a major industrialised nation with the lowest main corporation tax rate--the lowest we have ever had.

Mrs. Liddell: The previous Government's policies, which were tied to short-term boom and bust, did not create the climate for economic growth that Britain is crying out for. If the business community had been convinced by the performance of Opposition Members, they--not us--would now be on the Government Benches. Members who are new to the House and who believe that our responsibilities, as Ministers or otherwise, mean that we should be full-time Members, are conscious of the emphasis on the structure and possibilities of growth of small companies as significant generators of employment. The clause aims to introduce rate reductions which are widely welcomed throughout the UK and are perceived as a threat by other countries that would seek to organise their economies in the same way as the new Labour Government.

I therefore commend the clause to the Committee. It will be viewed as a major move towards ensuring the growth and long-term stability of the UK well into the next century.
