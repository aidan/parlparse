In response to the hon. Gentleman's question as to how many times the powers have been used, the answer is that there is no recorded instance of that ever having happened. Whenever the Post Office has sought to ensure that universal service provision is afforded to the British public, a sensible deal has been struck that does not require the powers to be used. The Post Office has a universal service obligation to deliver to the door six times a week, which means that delivery offices must be located locally. I am not talking about the network of 18,500 sub-post offices, but the 4,500 delivery offices that are essential in carrying out the universal service obligation. Every Member of Parliament knows that if their local post office closes it causes a problem. If their local sorting office closes and is relocated miles away, they feel, usually rightly, that service will deteriorate as a result.

The powers are necessary to ensure that there can be local delivery offices. Sometimes, planning development has meant that existing offices, which are usually located in the centre of town and close to the railway station, have had to move. The Royal Mail has itself been subject to compulsory purchase orders. It has to have the safeguard of being able to secure other premises, if necessary by compulsory purchase. I understand the concerns raised and we considered the matter long and hard. Having retained the power until now, we felt that abandoning it would present a real risk. It might send the perverse signal that the commitment to a universal service will be made not by 4,000 local delivery offices but a centralised fleet, which might deliver less frequently and later in the day. For those reasons, we decided to retain the power.

Amendments Nos. 119 and 120 are allied. The Post Office bureaucracy may regard the sentiment behind them as a hoary old chestnut, but I see them as an attempt to address a constant irritant in the way that the Post Office has power over a small part of an individual's identity—that is, one's address. It is hard to exaggerate the extent to which some people get steamed up about that. The current regime is one of control; it is inflexible, insensitive and annoying. This does not need to be a party political matter. If the Government can accept the amendment, they may make a little difference to many people's lives.

Amendment No. 119 concerns the postcode address file, which is a national database that is carried on fast-acting computers. For the purposes of mail order and many kinds of telephone inquiry, the postcode can provide the exact details of the address that should appear on an envelope, and ultimately the name of the person who lives there. When one phones up to take out a subscription to The Spectator, or some such sensible magazine—it could even be the New Statesman—the first question that one is often asked is, ``What is your postcode?'' The person dealing with the inquiry can then say, ``Oh yes, you live in such and such a street''. They can immediately match one's name and postcode to an exact address. Some people object to the amount of personal detail that can be gleaned from the holding of such information, but that is a different argument for another day.

It is often impossible to get details on the database changed and, given that, I am concerned about gremlins creeping into it. Many new houses are being built. The numbering of those that are built on greenfield sites is likely to be clear. In the case of houses that are built on a land infill area—such as a former farmyard—each building plot is allocated numbers at the beginning of the planning process. The plot number then becomes the house number, which is added to the postcode address file. After that, there is absolutely nothing that can be done to change the wretched thing if it is wrong. For the rest of the inhabitant's life in that house, whenever he phones up to take out a subscription, the irritating person on the other end of the phone will refuse to accept his proper address details. If he tries saying to the lovely lady on the phone—with whom it is difficult not to get annoyed—that he would like to have an input into changing the details, she will say, ``It's not me, love, it's that Post Office lot''. When he writes to that Post Office lot, they will, by and large, ignore his entreaties. That is an unacceptable state of affairs.

In urban streets where the numbering is absolutely clear, the Post Office does not find it acceptable for those house numbers to be replaced with lovely-sounding house names. They claim that that makes it difficult for the postman to make his deliveries. In many rural areas, with their higgledy-piggledy villages and little lanes, a proper address with a house name is far more helpful to the postman. The belief that a number is more helpful is complete stuff and nonsense. The postman knows his round; the name of the house may help him locate it, and the lane and village names are equally helpful. The amendment would help to get the gremlin out of the works, so that the citizen, the homeowner, would be able to say, ``Cannot we tweak the address a little, because it will help me and the postman.''

I refuse to accept the Post Office's blind notion that every house must have a number and that the description must be entered in the national postcode address file. There are too many cases of rubbish in, rubbish out. The uniformity demanded by the Post Office does not suit the efficient delivery of post. If someone living in a terraced house wanted to call their house Westminster Castle, that would obviously be unreasonable.

The amendment states that the Post Office must

    make provision for any person to seek and secure any reasonable change in their details.

The key word is ``reasonable''. Indeed, during our debates, the Minister has often defended the use of that word. We even have cross-party agreement that the use of the word ``reasonable'' is itself reasonable. I urge the Minister to accept the amendment. It would make life that little bit better for a lot of people who get mightily hacked off by having an address forced on them that is a poor illustration of where they live and that is not helpful to the postman.
