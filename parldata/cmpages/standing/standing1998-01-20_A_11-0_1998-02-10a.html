Clause 23

School organisation committees

Mr. Don Foster (Bath): I beg to move amendment No. 20, in page 21, line 17, after "area", insert

    "to act in an advisory capacity for the local education authority".

Thank you for your ruling, Mr. Gale.

I thank the Minister for sending members of the Committee the helpful note on school organisation. I welcome the Government's decision to reconsider school organisation arrangements and to allow greater determination at local level. We support the broad thrust of the Government's proposals, but we do not believe that they have got them absolutely right.

It is vital that the current arrangements for school organisation are changed significantly. I am sure that all members of the Committee are aware that the current procedure is extremely lengthy, bureaucratic and unsatisfactory. It requires a local education authority to hold a review on an issue and to produce a consultation paper containing several proposals, not just one. The LEA must then hold public meetings involving staff, parents, governors and other interested parties. It must produce a report on the consultation process and consider the proposals in the light of the representations that it has received, and then decide on the appropriate action to take.

The LEA must then, in effect, go through the whole procedure again. It has to issue public notices and wait two months in case objections are made. If there are no objections, the LEA can go ahead but if 10 or more objections are received, and if the LEA still wants to go ahead, it must send its proposals and the objections and comments on them to the Secretary of State. Finally, the Secretary of State will make a decision, but, under the current arrangements, there is no pressure on him to do so quickly. The process will be long-winded and the procedure will have to be gone through twice.

We must ask ourselves whether we are in danger of replacing one monster with another monster and whether the Government's proposals are unnecessarily cumbersome and bureaucratic. Will the procedures be subject to sufficient democratic accountability? Would it not be better, as our amendment proposes, to accept the need for reform of the current system and improve the consultation arrangements, but to do so without the loss of democratic accountability?

I am sure that all members of the Committee have a number of concerns about the proposals. I wish to touch on one or two to give the Under-Secretary an opportunity to respond, because the Committee will be interested to know the Government's thinking and more about the details that are covered by the Minister of State's helpful note. It is suggested in the note it is not what appears in the Bill that five different groups of people will be represented on the school organisation committee. For example, the guidance note makes it clear that the schools will be represented by serving school governors, which is eminently sensible. However, the note states that that group will include

    "governors from a primary, a secondary, and a special school (and a middle school where appropriate). It should also cover community, foundation and voluntary schools (other than those maintained by the Church of England or Roman Catholic dioceses)."

The note also refers to the need to cover special educational needs provision in both mainstream and special schools.

By any way of counting, that group will already have at least seven members, yet the note also says that the Government

    "expect community, foundation, and non-diocesan voluntary schools to be represented in the group in proportion to the number of pupils in the LEA that attend their schools."

It is difficult to see how all those legitimate interests can be represented by the seven people comprising that group on the school organisation committee.

Mr. Stephen Dorrell (Charnwood): I agree with much of what the hon. Gentleman says about the unsatisfactory characteristics of the process. He said that the guidance note sets out the Government's expectation that there will be five groups, but that is not how I read it. The note states:

    "Each committee will include groups representing"

those five categories. The Government must make it clear whether that list of five headings is intended to be exhaustive, or whether those five headings are illustrative and that those who establish the school organisation committees will determine how many groups there will be.

Mr. Foster: I am grateful to the right hon. Gentleman, who anticipated my next comment. The Bill lists only three possible groups, but the guidance note lists five possible groups. I have already said that it will be difficult for one group to include all the legitimate interests described in the note. Problems will arise if it is intended that other groups of people should be represented. The schools group will undoubtedly need all seven members, so if all five groups listed in the Bill want a matching number of people, school organisation committees will have 35 members. If other groups are to be included the body will become unwieldy.

Mr. Foster: I think that the hon. Gentleman misheard me. I said that only three are referred to in the Bill. I acknowledge that the Bill makes it clear that there could be other groups. My point is that the number of potential groups is increased to five in the guidance note, but that no ultimate limit has been put on the number of groups. Indeed, the note implies that there could be more than five groups.

My next question is about the status of the school organisation committee. Will it be a committee of local government? If so, will it be covered by local government law and all that follows from that? If not, what will be its legal status? Will it be democratically and legally accountable, and will individuals and groups be able to challenge its decisions?

I am also concerned about the position of the adjudicator to whom the statutory body the SOC will be accountable. From my reading of the Bill and the note, the adjudicator for decisions on school organisation appears to be the same person as the adjudicator on school admission appeals. There will be a conflict of interest if one individual is responsible for both the organisation of school places and admissions appeals. Clarification about that matter would be helpful.

Another matter that is unclear in the Bill and the guidance note concerns the groups involved. The note says that the LEA group will comprise "elected members". However, when we debated clause 9 a couple of weeks ago, the Committee, against my advice, voted for elected school governors with voting rights to sit on local authority education committees. Will those elected parent governors be considered suitable to be members of the LEA group?

I said earlier that my concern about the proposal is whether, in abolishing one monster, it will create a new one. Having a statutory committee rather than, as my amendment proposes, an advisory body may build in inflexibility and delay. The guidance note sensibly says that the school organisation plan will be a five-year rolling plan, reviewed annually. However, that raises the question of what will happen when new circumstances intervene after the annual agreement of the school organisation plan by the school organisation committee.

During the past few days, I have been talking to various people around the country about such circumstances, and they have raised a large number of questions. For example, what will happen when a school organisation plan has been agreed, but before the next review is due the head teacher of an infant school retires and the local education authority wants to use that as an opportunity at least to consider the benefits of an amalgamation of an infant and a junior school? That will not have been covered by the school organisation plan.

What will happen when, as has recently happened in several places, there is a sudden decision by the American air force to change the arrangements for its air bases? Those bases have a huge impact on the provision of education in an area. It might be decided to close an air base or, as happened recently at St. Mawgan's, to change the base into a family air base, which gives rise to a sudden large influx of children into the area. It is unclear how such events will be handled.

What will happen when, as happened in my area, an independent special school closes at short notice and the children have to be catered for?

Another issue of concern is the whole bureaucracy of the SOC as described in the note. That bureaucracy will incur a cost. What will be the cost of operating the SOCs?

Mr. David Jamieson (Plymouth, Devonport): Socks?

Mr. Foster: That is the name given in the Minister's note.

Where will the money come from? Will additional funds be given to LEAs to pay for the SOCs? What would happen in the interesting case which may arise in which a SOC proposes a budget to the LEA and the LEA refuses it? Who will make the final decision about the budget?

Under the Bill, LEAs will still have to undertake their own detailed consultation on the development of the school organisation plan. They will then hand those plans to school organisation committees, which will also have to go through their own consultative machinery. It appears that the current two-tier system will be replaced by another two-tier system, but, in fact, it is rather worse than that there will be a three-tier system because LEAs will have to consult. Ultimately, if there is a dispute, the LEA will consult, the SOC will consult and finally the adjudicator will take a decision. The Government are not replacing a two-tier system with another two-tier system; they are replacing a two-tier system with a three-tier system.

The matter is not even as simple as that there might be a four-tier system. In announcing the proposal in general on Second Reading, the Government said that decisions on school organisation would be returned to the local level. However, under the Bill and the guidance note, the Secretary of State will still have a major role to play. The note makes it clear that he can still make proposals on shortages of school places or excess school places. If he makes such proposals over the heads of the LEA and the SOC, the adjudicator will be required, according to the note, to hold public inquiries so a two-tier system will be replaced with a four-tier system.

The proposed four-tier system raises a question of democratic accountability. SOCs will be given considerable power, but no responsibility. For example, if a SOC decides not to close a small, uneconomic school, it will not have to live with the financial consequences, whereas the LEA, if it is unhappy with that decision, will have to continue funding that school.

What will happen if a SOC decides not to take what might be difficult, controversial decisions? Sadly, that has often been true of LEAs, which have sometimes not been prepared to bite the bullet. If an LEA does not take a difficult decision, it is democratically accountable; but if an SOC does not, it is not accountable. That presents a problem.

By all means let us find ways of slimming down the existing procedure, let us introduce a much more effective consultation system on reorganisation proposals we might call it a citizens' jury or an SOC but it is crucial that LEAs are responsible for taking decisions on the proposals that SOCs put forward under the consultation arrangements. The SOC could investigate and report to the LEA, but the democratically elected body should take the decision.

The Government's proposals contain problems of accountability, reduced democracy and excessive bureaucracy. If the SOCs are advisory bodies, we can ensure that they genuinely do what we all want establish informed public debates on long-term needs. Such advisory bodies could produce some significant, desperately needed improvements to the system without losing democratic accountability or creating unnecessary bureaucracy.
