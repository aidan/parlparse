Does that apply only to permanent health insurance schemes that are arranged by employers, or to all permanent health insurance schemes, including those arranged by individuals? If employer-financed schemes only will be affected, a damaging perversity will be introduced into the system. Anybody who knows the first thing about insurance—I am becoming slightly worried about aspects of Ministers' knowledge in that regard—knows that premiums are a negative function of the range of people who are covered by a given policy. The wider the scope of the policy, the lower the premium can be. The difficulties of moral hazard and self-selection are therefore removed. It would be possible to cover the whole House of Commons at a considerably lower premium than 650 times the premium that you or I, Mr. McWilliam, would be quoted. The whole of London could be covered by a lower premium, and the whole of the United Kingdom by an even lower one. That is a fundamental principle of insurance.

Are the Government saying that health insurance schemes paying out on a permanent basis for permanent illness or disability will be targeted for means-testing, but that schemes taken out by individuals will not be so targeted? If so, they are denying to the British public the benefits of the logic of insurance—that it is better to arrange such policies collectively than individually. The aggregate cost of health insurance will consequently rise, as people will not receive the benefit of such collective schemes.
