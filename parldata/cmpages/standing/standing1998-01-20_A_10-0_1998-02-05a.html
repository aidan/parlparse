Schedule 2

Allocation of existing schools to new categories

Amendment proposed [this day], No. 166 in page 95, line 32, leave out "Secretary of State" and insert "Local Education Authority." [Mr. St. Aubyn.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are discussing the following amendments: No. 169, in page 95, line 32, leave out from "determine" to end.

No. 170, in page 95, line 37, leave out paragraph 8.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Ms Estelle Morris): When we stopped our proceedings this morning, I was beginning my response to the group of amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Guildford (Mr. St. Aubyn), which concerned who should make the decision when there was a dispute between the governing body and the parents about the choice of categories. The amendment would remove that power from the Secretary of State and give it to local education authorities.

It is clear that a person must have the power to determine the status of the school, but it is not acceptable to give it to local education authority. The decision must be made by a neutral and impartial person who is regarded as such by all concerned. The local authority may be thought to have an interest in such decisions for example, in whether a school becomes a community school, in which case it would have more of a stake than if it became a foundation school. Therefore, it is important that the final decision is made by an arbiter a postholder without a vested interest in the outcome. No matter how fair a local authority was and how determined it was to judge the facts, some would regard it as having a vested interest.

Amendments Nos. 169 and 170 would remove the constraints on the Secretary of State when making such a decision. It is important that he is guided by several constraints. For those reasons, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw the amendment. If he does not, I shall ask the Committee to vote against it.

Mr. Nick St. Aubyn (Guildford): The principle behind the amendment has been clearly stated. I am grateful for the support of Liberal Democrat Members and for their agreeing that decisions should be made locally. Having made my point, I am happy to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 21

Kinds of foundation and voluntary schools and types of foundations

Mrs. Angela Browning (Tiverton and Honiton): I beg to move amendment No. 148, in page 19, line 22, leave out subsection (6)(c).

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 149 page 19, line 38, leave out subsection (7).

Mrs. Browning: The purpose of the amendments is to effect two deletions from the Bill, relating to the

    "model instrument of government for adoption by a foundation body"

and the powers of the Secretary of State to vary the instrument. That applies to both subsections 6(c) and (7).

We tabled the amendments because it seemed strange that the Secretary of State should have statutory powers to intervene on a foundation body. Surely such bodies are capable of making their own decisions without the Secretary of State intervening to such an extent. Despite all his other powers, we are not sure why this provision needs to be so precise or why the Secretary of State should interfere at such a low level in a body that will be well established and able to take day-to-day decisions about how a school is structured.

Mr. Byers: It is worth stressing that the clause refers not to powers that will affect foundation schools, but to a concept that we want to extend group foundations. Such groups already exist, especially with regard to religious bodies, many of which have a group foundation to support voluntary-aided schools. There is a common interest through religion which leads to a group foundation being formed and to foundation governors being nominated.

However, that is quite distinct from foundation schools. There is no intention in the clause to interfere in how foundation schools operate. We shall address the issues of governance when we come to those later in the Bill. Group foundations will allow schools to come together if they feel that they have a common interest. The model instrument is a facility and a device to enable that to happen. All the Secretary of State wants to do in a simple way we intend that the instrument should be simple is to allow a group foundation to be established and for schools to join if they so wish.

With regard to voluntary-aided schools, it is quite common for them to be part of a wider foundation. That brings benefits and there is no reason why such benefits should be denied to, for example, community schools or others if they feel that they want to share their common interests. It is not the intention of a group foundation to take over the powers of individual school governing bodies; it is a facility that will allow schools to come together. Time will tell whether schools avail themselves of the option, but the Government believe that the provision should be in the Bill because it may help and we would not want to deny it to particular schools.

Mrs. Browning: On that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

The Church authorities at that time took the view which they still hold strongly that to maintain their involvement in the work of voluntary-aided schools they should make a contribution of 15 per cent. towards capital costs. I do not know whether the Opposition really want that to happen, but it would not be particularly good news for those grant-maintained schools to which foundation status appears attractive.

It is true that voluntary-aided schools receive different treatment as to capital. Churches treasure the fact that they make their contribution. They regard it as a contribution from their community to their schools. We discussed with the Churches whether they thought that the arrangement should be altered, such as by changing the split to 90 or 95 per cent. from central Government and a smaller percentage from the Church authorities.
