Clause 1

Offences relating to fur farming

Mr. Hunter: I beg to move amendment No. 10, in page 1, line 5, leave out "or primarily".

In the words of the explanatory notes, subsection (1), which the amendment would amend, will create

    "a primary offence of keeping animals solely or primarily for slaughter for the value of their fur".

Paragraph (a) specifies that the offence can be committed by the person who keeps the animals or by another individual. It is immaterial whether the slaughterer is also the keeper of the animals. Paragraph (b) widens the offence to include breeding progeny for such slaughter.

The explanatory notes are helpful, but not in the way in which the hon. Member for Garston intends. Indeed, they focus attention on a possible ambiguity without which the Bill would be better.

That ambiguity lies in the explanatory notes' clarification of and distinction between the words "solely" and "primarily". Someone who keeps animals partly for slaughter and partly for another purpose, will be guilty of an offence only if the primary purpose is slaughter.

I understand that the intention is to ensure that people such as rabbit farmers, who produce and market, or enable others to market, both meat and fur would be guilty of the offence if the value of the fur that they produced was greater than the value of the rabbits' meat. To use the vernacular, that is crazy.

I shall elaborate my argument shortly, but subsection (1) is disturbingly defective. The provision as drafted is bound to lead to legal uncertainty. It oversimplifies, in that it does not do justice to the variations in and complexities of rabbit farming--to continue with that example. It will present lawyers with a wonderful opportunity to practise their skills.

I do not know whether the hon. Member for Garston has any experience of running her own business. If so, she will understand my point. Rabbit farming is free from price support, market controls and mechanisms and is exposed to the vagaries of the market. The value of rabbit meat and fur varies in both absolute and relative terms. At given times, the value of meat and fur produce will rise and fall through the combined operation of supply and demand, both of which can be, and often are, affected by the way in which the same market forces are operating for overseas--mainly European--producers. It follows that it will sometimes be extremely difficult to determine whether the value of the fur that a rabbit farmer produces exceeds or is less than the value of the rabbit meat that he produces.

My argument is not abstract or academic but considers the reality of the business of rabbit farming. How will a court reach a conclusion on whether an offence has been committed, when the market for fur and meat is so volatile, especially in cases in which the value of meat and fur that a company produces is broadly approximate for much of the time? At different points in the year, or different moments in the economic cycle, one may be earning more than the other for the company, but the situation may change and change again. How is a court to decide what is the predominant interest of the business and on what date to make that decision? Those are fundamental defects in the subsection.

The problems do not stop there. To base a definition of primary purpose on the values of the finished products--I emphasise that term--of fur and meat is to take a highly selective view of the value of a business and how it is run. To do so is to concentrate on only the bottom line of the current account, the profitability, and to ignore the dimension of capital investment. Such investment can be out of kilter with the returns on it. The intrinsic value of a business cannot be measured solely by sales values. The explanatory notes, which I acknowledge are not intended to be a comprehensive description of the Bill, make no mention of that dimension. I look forward to the hon. Member for Garston correcting me, if appropriate, but I suspect that she may not have fully considered how to assess the value of a business.

With the inclusion in subsection (1) of the words "or primarily", we open up an enormous grey area, without which the Bill would he better.
