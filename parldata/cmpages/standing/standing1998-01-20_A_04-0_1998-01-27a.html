School Standards and Framework Bill

Clause 2

Plans by LEAs for reducing infant class sizes

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 64, in page 2, line 15, at end insert

    "(3A) In the course of preparing the statement the authority shall have regard to recent experience of parental preferences expressed in the arrangements provided under section 77 of this Act.". [Mr. Dorrell.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Ms Estelle Morris): Before we broke for lunch, I was assuring Opposition Members, particularly the hon. Member for Maidenhead (Mrs. May), about our desire to accede to parental preference. I was also assuring the hon. Member for Guildford (Mr. St. Aubyn) that we, too, were concerned about standards and did not consider smaller class sizes and raising the standards of teachers to be incompatible. I wanted to reassure the right hon. Member for Charnwood (Mr. Dorrell) that we sympathised with his argument and that our unwillingness to accept the amendment did not demonstrate an unwillingness to see parental preference reflected in the plans.

My hon. Friend the Member for Blackpool, South (Mr. Marsden) used a technical argument against the amendment. I assure members of the Committee that such a suggestion was not in the official briefing. My hon. Friend was right in that the word "recent" could mean several years; it could mean the previous year or the previous decade. Although such cases may be exceptional, I am sure that the right hon. Member for Charnwood can imagine instances when circumstances in an area have changed recently and current parental preference may not reflect recent parental preference.

Current parental views must influence the plans and I assure the right hon. Gentleman that we are making efforts to ensure that local parents are consulted on the plans. Our guidance will ensure that that happens. When the Secretary of State examines the plans, he will want to see that that exercise has been carried out.

Had we merely wanted to fulfil the pledge without any regard to the quality of education or parental preference, we need not have spent another penny on the provisions other than on transport costs. We could have merely instructed local authorities to move children from over-subscribed schools to those with classes of fewer than 30. I cannot give an exact statement that that would have been exercised at no extra cost, because such a proposal never entered the Government's mind. I ask the right hon. Gentleman to accept that the fact that we have put extra resources into the class-size pledge is an acknowledgement that we do not see the pledge being met merely by a transfer of children from popular schools to under-populated schools.
