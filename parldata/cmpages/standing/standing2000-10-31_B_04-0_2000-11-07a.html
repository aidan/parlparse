Dr. Howells: Further to your comments about our making rapid progress, Mr. Gale, all I can say is, ``If only!''

Three amendments need to be made to take account of the effects of the Financial Services and Markets Act 2000. Part XXIV of the Act sets out the rights of the Financial Services Authority—which I shall refer to as ``the authority''—to participate in insolvency procedures involving regulated persons and companies doing financial services business.

The basic idea is that the authorities should be able to act in circumstances wherein financial services consumers may not have the resources or the expertise to take any requisite action themselves. The Act gives the authority the same rights as creditors to petition, for example, for the winding up of an insolvent financial services company. It also has the right to attend and speak at creditors' meetings, to apply to the court and to be heard on applications made by another person during insolvency proceedings. It would be inconsistent and leave a gap in the regulatory regime if the authority did not have similar rights in respect of the new company voluntary arrangement provision as it has under other insolvency procedures. The amendments are designed to prevent inconsistencies and gaps.
