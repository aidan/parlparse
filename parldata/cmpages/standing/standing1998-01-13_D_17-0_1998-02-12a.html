Mr. McCartney: The hon. Gentleman is right--nothing in the clause is extraordinary or unprecedented. These methods are all tried and tested in statute. However, to clarify the meaning of the clause, especially for discussions that may occur in another place, I will put on record the reasoning behind it.

The clause aims to reflect the ever increasing complexity in working arrangements. Many are unconventional but that should not equate to being unequal, in this case in having the right to the national minimum wage. The purpose of the proposals is to ensure that however complex or unconventional their working arrangements, people should still be paid the minimum wage.

The clause defines ``employee'', ``contract of employment'', ``worker'', ``employer'', ``employment'' and ``employed''. With only two exceptions, which I will come to in a moment, those definitions follow previous employment legislation such as the Employment Rights Act 1996, as the hon. Member for Daventry asked me to confirm, and which, as he said, was a consolidating Act. It makes a clear legal distinction between an employee and a worker.

The term ``employee'' is relatively narrow. In legal terms, it covers people who work under a contract of employment. Such a contract is, in more legal language, a contract of service or of apprenticeship, whether express or implied, and whether oral or written. In practice, most people in work are employed under a contract of employment.

The term ``worker'' is wider. As well as covering employees who work under a contract of employment, it covers people who work under any other contract. I return to the legal language. The Bill states that the term applies if

    ``the individual undertakes to do or perform personally any work or services for another party to the contract whose status is not by virtue of the contract that of a client or customer of any profession or business undertaking carried on by the individual''.

Although that is a legal mouthful and neither myself nor the hon. Member for Daventry are lawyers, we should be aware that a simple premise underlies that paragraph. It is intended to provide a wider definition and to cover people who work under contracts that are not contracts of employment, and under which they agree to work or to perform services personally. It does not apply when such people are genuinely in business on their own account--that is to say, when they are genuinely self-employed.

A plumber who is normally self-employed, for example, could agree to work for one person on a long-term job, without having a contract of employment. If the terms of the contract were in practice decided largely by that person, and the plumber was working only for that person and was not free to work for anyone else during the time involved, the plumber would be likely to have a worker's contract.

I offer that as an example to help the Committee. Obviously, if a case came to court, it would be treated on its merits, because each case is different. I shall not be tempted to speculate about fictitious cases. The general principle, which I outlined, is that a direct relationship that determines the outcome of work carried out comes under the definition of worker. A totally self-employed person, who has control over the situation, would not come under that definition.

On Tuesday, the Committee debated the construction industry, and how employees in it will secure entitlement to the national minimum wage. The Committee debated the same principle when discussing home workers and agency workers. The Government's position is clear. Workers will be deemed as workers for the purposes of the minimum wage unless it is proved otherwise, and it can be proved that a person is genuinely self-employed. It means that if an employer wishes to use self-employment to get round the minimum wage, he or she will have to prove that it is genuine.
