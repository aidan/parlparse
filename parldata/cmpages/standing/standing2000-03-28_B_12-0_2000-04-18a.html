Review of maps

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I beg to move amendment No. 188, in page 6, line 26, leave out ``ten'' and insert ``five''.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss amendment No. 189, in page 6, line 29, at end insert

Mr. Heath: The amendments are small but important. Amendment No. 188 deals with the first review of the conclusive maps to be issued under clause. 10. The Bill provides that that review should take place not more than 10 years after the issuing of those maps, and it then provides for a 10-year cycle of reviews. Given the degree of latitude allowed under the Bill, which we have already explored and which will inevitably give rise to uncertainties and ambiguities, and given the opportunity for errors to be made or for the Bill not to work as we had hoped, it would be of great benefit to landowners, those seeking access, the countryside agencies and the Government if the first review were to be rather earlier than 10 years. I suggest that it should be within five years.

The Bill does not stipulate that the first review should take place after 10 years, but that it should take place after not more than 10 years. When does the Minister expect the first review to take place? If the amendment were to be accepted, it would be after no more than five years. Not only would that give time for the Bill's provisions to be implemented, it would provide an earlier opportunity to check that it was working effectively.

Amendment No. 189 may be defective in that it refers only to English Nature. I confess that I was stil in devolutionist mode when I drated it; I seem to have disapplied that provision in Wales. Howver, considerations in England are different from those in Wales in that consultation between the various parts of the countryside and conservation interests in Wales is automatic, which is not necessarily the case in England. It should be considered good practice for the Countryside Agency to consult with the conservancy council for England and English Nature, and it would be appropriate to provide for consultation in the Bill. That would make it plain that it is a co-operative venture, and that not only access, recreational and countryside issues need to be considered but that environmental issues, too, must be weighed in the balance. Other legislation is littered with requirements to consult, so there is nothing extraordinary about such provisions. For some reason, however, the Bill fails to identify the bodies that it is appropriate to consult before mapes are drawn up, reviewed and so on. The amendment seeks to fill that lacuna.

Mr. Damian Green (Ashford): I should like to speak briefly in favour of amendment No. 188. During a previous sitting, the Committee discussed the difference between the new statutory maps and Ordnance Survey maps, which walkers are most likely to use. It is clearly important that the maps are reviewed and altered regularly to keep pace with changes in land use. The statutory maps will be used by the Ordnance Survey and other bodies in reviewing their published maps, so the more up to date they are, the more practical help they can provide to walkers. It is unlikely that Ordnance Survey maps will be revised on the same time scale as maps of access land, so more rapid review of official maps of access land will increase the opportunity for Ordnance Survey maps to take account of the most up-to-date information on the extent of access land. I am sure that Ministers will agree that such arrangements would have a useful purpose for those who want to take advantage of the access land, which is why the Opposition support the amendment.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Chris Mullin): The amendments, which were tabled by the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath), relate to the provision requiring countryside bodies to review maps at least every 10 years. Amendment No. 188 suggests that the first review should occur within five years. I accept that there are some arguments in favour of an initial review of the statutory maps after five years and then 10 years, as proposed in the Bill, and we have given the matter careful consideration. On balance, however, we consider that 10 years is appropriate as we want to promote a period of stability after the access right has taken effect. We do not envisage enough changes in land use to warrant the undertaking of a review within five years.

We are confident that the careful work that will be put into the initial set of maps will ensure that they provide a solid foundation to the new right for a number of years. If the countryside bodies were required to review the maps after five years, they would be at risk of having to start on the next lot of maps soon after they had finished the first batch. That would produce undesirable uncertainty for users and landowners.

Clause 10(2) imposes an obligation on the countryside bodies to undertake a review at least every 10 years. However, subsection (1) requires them to conduct a review ``from time to time'', so there is nothing to stop them undertaking a review before 10 years have elapsed if they think that there are good reasons for doing so. Subsection (3) allows the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales to initiate an earlier review by making regulations to amend the periods specified in subsection (2). Some of the hon. Gentleman's aims are therefore already catered for.

The hon. Gentleman also proposes that the Countryside Agency should consult English Nature when it carries out the reviews. For clarity, and in response to a point on which the hon. Gentleman touched briefly. I should explain that the Countryside Council for Wales fulfils the functions both of the Countryside Agency and for English Nature in Wales, so the issue does not arise in respect of Wales. With regard to England, the purpose of consultation with English Nature will be to establish whether land should be subject to long-term closures or restrictions. On that basis, I have no doubt that the Countryside Agency will not only consult, but work closely with English Nature in conducting reviews. Clauses 24 and 25 make clear the requirement to have regard to English Nature's view. There is no need for the requirement to be included in clause 10.

Mr. Geoffrey Clifton-Brown (Cotswold): What, if any, procedure will there be if, after the definitive map has been issued by the Countryside Agency, errors are found before the 10-year period is up? Will there be a method of notifying those who are interested of modifications to the plan?

Mr. Mullin: Maps can be objected to when published if there are errors in them. However, as I have made clear, if errors are found after publication, there is nothing to stop a review starting within 10 years. Significant errors in a map would be a good reason for suggesting an earlier review. We should not compel everybody to have a review. We are the party of deregulation; I increasingly think that, in this case at least, the Opposition are the party of over-regulation.

Mr. Clifton-Brown: I am making a serious point. Although an alteration might be minor in terms of the national map, it could be significant to an individual landowner. Will there be a mechanism whereby a modification regarding, for example, the diversion of footpaths, can be lodged with the Countryside Agency so that anyone who is interested can know that the plan is incorrect?

Mr. Mullin: I shall take advice about that. I repeat again that there is nothing to prevent a review from taking place before 10 years. I understand that there are provisions for the correction of minor errors in the regulations under clause 11. I hope that that meets the hon. Gentleman's point.

Clauses 24 and 25 make clear the requirement to have regard to English Nature's views. There is no need for such a provision to be included in clause 10. Although the conservation merits of land are not directly relevant to the duties of the countryside bodies to map open country and registered common land under clause 4, English Nature may be able to supply information about the qualities of the land, such as vegetation cover, that will assist the Countryside Agency in classifying land as mountain, moor, heath or down. We would certainly expect the agency to liaise closely with English Nature to ensure that such data are considered in the drawing up of draft maps.

The amendments are unnecessary and I ask the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome to withdraw them.

Mr. Heath: I am grateful to the Under-Secretary for at least giving me credit for the arguments under amendment No. 188 which proposes a shorter period. He has rejected those arguments in favour of the flexibility made by other provisions. A review might occur within 10 years if the countryside body so chooses, or if the Government change their mind and introduce different regulations under clause 11. However, that does not add up to a definitive view and I maintain that there is an argument for an earlier review.

The Under-Secretary talks about stability; however, I find it perverse to consider stability in error. I would not applaud stability in error-there is a thin line between that and obstinacy. Although there are arguments for an earlier review, I do not intend to press the matter today. I hope that the Under-Secretary will reflect on the debate.

With regard to amendment No. 189 which deals with consultation, I accept that it is very likely that there will be close colaboration between the countryside bodies, the Countryside Agency and English Nature. It is not a matter of regulation but of stating a clear intent. The Bill is deficient in that its wording does not make transparent the consultaiton process. Although I accept that, in practice, there is probably little difference between the Minister's position and my own, I would prefer the wording of the Bill to be more transparent so that everyone may be clear about what is intended and how the process will work. having said that, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
