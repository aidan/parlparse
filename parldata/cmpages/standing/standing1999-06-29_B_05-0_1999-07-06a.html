Acquisition and review of information

Mr. Andrew George (St. Ives): I beg to move amendment No. 80, in page 3, line 44, at end insert--

    `including consumer-focused research to ascertain consumer attitudes to matters connected with food.'.

Clause 8 concerns the acquisition and review of information. The amendment seeks to ensure that the agency can consider consumer attitudes as well as scientific research. We should recognise that consumer attitudes are not always based on rational considerations or scientific evidence. Consumer attitudes are a material factor. The Bill should allow the agency to consider the consumer viewpoint and not only cold scientific facts.

On his reply to me on an earlier amendment, the Minister said that references in the Bill should not be repeated unnecessarily, and I accepted that and other factors when withdrawing the amendment. However, consumer attitudes may not necessarily reflect scientific understanding. Consumers may worry about food technology. The amendment would allow the agency to make positive and constructive judgments on such matters as modified foods. We have seen recently how the public perception of risk can be different from the scientific interpretation: the public weigh the perceived risks against the supposed benefits.

The agency's decisions should be based not only on science but on the broader issues, and consumers' ethical and environmental concerns and perceptions should be taken into account. They are not a priority, but the agency should include them in its overall assessment.

The Minister of State, Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Jeff Rooker): I must resist the amendment. At the risk of being repetitious, and although the intentions of the hon. Member for St. Ives (Mr. George) are the same as ours, its wording would increase the definitional problems to which I referred this morning. Consumer research is covered already under subsection (1) and the amendment would narrow its scope, not broaden it. The agency will conduct such research, having taken over research programmes from the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food. It might be better for me to stick to the narrow effects of the amendment at present, because I shall give a breakdown of the agency's work in the clause stand part debate.

The joint food safety and standards group is the embryo of the agency. It will have responsibility for planning surveys in connection with the general public and stakeholders later in the year. It will create a benchmark for future research into the effectiveness of the Food Standards Agency and its crucial communication policies. The group will ensure that the agency's audience--the general public--receive the information that they want in the most appropriate form. Such work will not await the start of the agency. The joint food safety and standards group will commission a couple of projects later this year, so research will already be under way on vesting day. As I said, I shall give details of the work that will be undertaken in the clause stand part debate. I recommend the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment, because its entire thrust is covered under subsection (1).

    ``other interests of consumers in relation to food''.

It has been helpful to have the Minister clarify that research undertaken by the agency will include consumer-focused research and consumer attitudes to food and, on that basis, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Rooker: I take the point that the right hon. Member for Penrith and The Border (Mr. Maclean) makes. He might think that the clause does not need to be included in the Bill, but it contains one of the agency's guiding principles that we set out in Chapter 2 of the White Paper ``A Force for Change'' in January 1998 because we wanted to make it abundantly clear that the agency's functions would have parameters, constraints and limits, and that it would not be a tiger set loose on the food industry but would work within certain rules.

We pledged to include those nine guiding principles in the Bill, and the clause fulfils one of those promises. It sets out the guiding principle that the agency should use the best scientific evidence to inform its decisions, thereby specifying in the Bill the rationale for policy making and decision making, which we thought important. Other clauses set out other guiding principles, such as to ensure that risk is assessed and that action is proportionate to risk.

The agency must keep itself informed of recent scientific research that others have carried out both at home and internationally. Within the group, we already keep up to date with WTO and Codex Alimentarius, and Britain services and plays a key role in some international committees. Strategic blue skies research will be the function of the research councils and others. We are careful to try to avoid overlap, although I would not say that it does not happen.

The present portfolio falls into five general areas--chemical food safety; microbiological food safety; radiological food safety; food quality and authenticity; and nutrition. To give a broad idea of the breakdown, chemical food safety constitutes about 37 per cent.; microbiological food safety constitutes 20 per cent.; radiological food safety constitutes 5 per cent.; food quality and authenticity constitutes 8 per cent.; and nutrition, including food choice and acceptability, constitutes 30 per cent. Those five general areas comprise 28 individual programmes, and 400 projects are currently under way. Some last a year; some last two years. The average is about three years. Very few go beyond four years.

Some projects were set in train one or two years ago, and are still in the system. Others are now subject to bidding. All joint food standards and safety group research is tendered for; none is placed in house. Some projects are currently subject to tendering for the 2000{**nrl**}01 programme, and assessments and decisions will be made later this year.

Responsibility for the projects will leave MAFF and be rolled over to the agency. The approximate figure given in the White Paper is £25 million. There have been a few adjustments to our research budget, which we discussed at Question Time last week, but it will probably be just under £25 million--a significant sum.

The agency will have a duty to keep itself abreast of developments and properly informed, so that it can carry out its functions, and to base its policy and advice on sound science, not on a hunch. The clause represents a sensible way to ensure that that is done and to include guiding principle number four--no, the order has changed.

Mr. Clifton-Brown: May I give the Minister some breathing space? The clause might seem insignificant but it is important because, on the vesting day of the Bill, the Minister's function in negotiating the British Government's position in the European Union and other international forums will become redundant. Mr. Rooker: That is a perfectly legitimate question.

I was perhaps a little confused, but the clause deals with guiding principle number two:

    ``The Agency's assessments of food standards and safety will be unbiased and based on the best available scientific advice, provided by experts invited in their own right to give independent advice.''

Because of the parliamentary draftsman's expertise, we can fulfil that guiding principle under no less than three different clauses--one of which is clause 8.

On the hon. Gentleman's perfectly legitimate question, the agency will be independent of the day-to-day management of Ministers. It will be a non-ministerial department, but its staff will be civil servants--it is a Crown organisation. It will produce policy advice, draft legislation and represent the British Government in international negotiations, including the European Union and the World Trade Organisation.

The joint food safety and standards group currently advises Ministers on the position that our negotiators should take, for example, in Brussels. Sometimes the group does so hourly as discussions take place. I have not been kept hanging on the phone, but I have been within an hour--that close--of negotiations before being advised what line to take in discussions in order to build a coalition for a certain way forward. However, if the Government do not accept the agency's advice, it will negotiate according to the Government's instructions. That is exactly what happens now.

In other words--to put it crudely--our civil servants and advisers can advise Ministers to take a particular line in negotiations--perhaps voting for or against a proposal, or abstaining, so as not to upset this or that camp while trying to build a coalition. However, because of other issues and constraints, Ministers may say that we must send the right signal and be positive, either by voting yes or no. Ministers' instructions are then followed. Exactly the same will happen with the agency; it will be free to negotiate on

behalf of the British Government within the decisions taken by Ministers, because Ministers are ultimately accountable. That sounds like a contradiction or a paradox, but it is not, given what happens now.

Removing the joint food safety and standards group to the agency and placing it under different day-to-day control will not alter the policy lines given to Ministers in international negotiations. The expertise for the nuances of negotiation will, as now, be that of the agency staff; it is certainly not that of Ministers, who take a strategic view of all factors. The agency will negotiate to the Government's instructions.

Clause 9

General functions in relation to animal feedingstuffs

Mr. James Paice (South-East Cambridgeshire): I beg to move amendment No. 24, in page 4, line 10, at end insert

    `, but has the additional duty of ensuring that all animal feedingstuffs are accurately labelled with a complete list of ingredients, whether they are genetically modified and the country of origin of each ingredient'.

I first became involved in agricultural politics almost 30 years ago, and the issue of what is contained in animal feed has been around all that time. There has always been a significant lobby of farmers who want clear labelling as to what their animal feedstuffs contain. The trade resisted that for years, which is why such labelling still does not exist. That is why we tabled this amendment to the clause, which extends the agency's functions to animal feed.

It is important that producers of livestock, whether of meat or livestock products, should know what they feed their animals. Most people would think it rather odd that they should not now have that right. That is why we want the agency to be given labelling obligations for animal feed.

We can move from the farmers themselves to the ultimate consumers of livestock products, who have a right to know what has been fed to animals. The issue of genetically modified ingredients in food is topical. Some supermarkets, such as Marks and Spencer, are declaring themselves GM free, but they can make such declarations with validity only if they can ensure that all the ingredients of animal feedstuffs are GM free. No farmer is in a position to know that himself as he buys soya or maize either as part of a compound feed or by the lorryload. We want to see the clause amended to state that the agency is obliged to ensure

    ``that all animal feedingstuffs are accurately labelled with a complete list of ingredients, whether they are genetically modified and the country of origin''.

The country of origin is of huge significance these days, partly because of the obvious competitive pressures, but also because consumers are increasingly aware that there are variations in different countries' legislation concerning animal feed treatment and contents. For example, we know that many countries in Europe, such as Belgium, still use meat and bone meal, and that some countries use antibiotic growth promoters now banned in Europe. Those are key issues that must be made transparent by labelling animal feed.

It is perhaps odd that the Opposition should raise an issue that goes back to our experiences of BSE. There is no doubt that there was a vast range of novel animal feed ingredients during the 1970s and 1980s, when I was still involved in practical agriculture. I can remember a dozen and one different ingredients from African countries and elsewhere in the third world, including ground-up peanuts and rape stalks. There was also the recycling of individual species to produce meat and bone meal. If many farmers had known what went into their animal feed, they might not have chosen to use it. However, we do not know for certain; we are using hindsight, which is an attribute that we all have, but which perhaps we use too often. Many farmers have said that they would not have used some of those feeding stuffs had they known what they contained. That again illustrates the necessity of ensuring that the animal feed that is provided is properly labelled with all the detail that we propose in the amendment, to reassure the farmer, his customer, which may be a supermarket, and ultimately the consumer.

I remind the Committee of the vast range of possible ingredients. We need only look at a page from Farmers Weekly to see the huge list of items that can be included, such as brewers grains, citrus pulp, distillers dried grains, fishmeal, maize gluten, molasses, palm kernels, rapeseed, soya bean, supergrains, sugar beet pulp, wheatfeed, and many others. They can all be used in animal feed. That is why the manufacture of compound feeds should be open, clear and transparent, so that the farmer, his customer--the supermarket--and the consumer can be entirely clear about what they are buying and what went into it. They will then know whether GM feed, meat and bone meal, or anything else has been used in the production process.

The amendment has been tabled in a genuine attempt to ensure that the agency can do its job properly. The Minister may tell us that the matter is already covered by clause 8. I do not think that it is; it should be properly included. The agency should have the power to enforce the labelling of animal feeding stuffs, and the measure should be as inclusive as described in our amendment.
