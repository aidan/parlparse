Mr. Gibb: Amendment No. 219, which relates to energy centres, would amend clause 5, and amendment No. 225, which relates to telecommunications centres, would amend clause 87. They would require the regulatory authority to include in its annual report to the Secretary of State an assessment of the operation of the memorandum of understanding between an authority and the relevant consumer council.

We have already discussed the memorandum of understanding. It is difficult to overemphasise the industry's concern about the fact that competing regulatory authorities, consumer councils and the Secretary of State will clamour for information from utility companies. There is a real danger of duplication and of the expensive absorption of management time.

The Prime Minister and the Secretary of State are quick to condemn British management, which they see as the source of all Britain's economic ills. This country will never have super-efficient, consumer-focused and innovative management if the Government continue to pile more demands on industry, which is required to fulfil requests for information and to complete a host of Government forms. Managers have to spend hours reading new, complex and often ambiguous regulations with which they have to comply. Those problems are compounded by the requirement to meet duplicate requests for information from Government quangos. Those requests are received at different times, when the relevant files may have been returned to their shelves. The Government quangos and authorities make no effort to co-ordinate their requests for information.

Increasing demands will also result from the terms of the Competition Act 1998 and from the Director General of Fair Trading. No one questions the Government's motives for seeking to introduce new legislation or for making regulatory demands. We need more competition and a better deal for consumers. However, the method by which the Government are seeking to deliver those objectives—greater regulation—has proved, time and again, and in many countries, to be counterproductive.

The amendments would minimise those problems by ensuring that the regulatory authority carefully considers how well the memorandum of understanding, which we have seen in draft, is functioning. We should clarify how many complaints have been received from beleaguered utility companies that are seeking to cope with a plethora of competing demands for information.

I am sure that the Minister could accept the amendment if he were minded to do so. It would demonstrate his understanding of the difficulties that industry fears and would show a sensible and pluralistic approach to the Bill's progress through Committee.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): I share the concern of the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) to see an effective working relationship between the authority and the council, based on the memorandum of understanding. Where we differ is on the extent to which it is necessary to surround what is intended to be a flexible working relationship between two public sector bodies with a lot of due process. We have confidence that the parties will put in place a satisfactory framework for co-operation between them. They are both public sector bodies, answerable ultimately to Parliament. There will be strong operational and other pressures to make sure that the relationship works.

The Bill sets out a core minimum requirement on the parties that they must agree a memorandum between them and, once agreement is reached, it must be put on the public record. We do not believe that further statutory provisions are necessary, as they would compound the iniquities of over-prescriptive regulations that the hon. Gentleman sees in everything that the Government do.

The amendment would amount to an unnecessary over-elaboration of the authority's annual reporting requirement. The aspects that the authority should be required to report on should be restricted to its outputs. In other words, it should report on what it has achieved in the year under review. The operation of the memorandum of understanding does not fall into that category, but is a means to an end, a means by which the authority and the council can more easily fulful their respective functions. It is an important piece of bureaucratic procedure, but it is not a goal in itself.

If the Government had serious concerns that the memorandum of understanding was not working as effectively as it should, a number of levers could be brought to bear. Principal among those would be the budget-setting process; another would be subsection (2)(d), which gives the Secretary of State a power to ask for

    ``a report on such other matters as the Secretary of State may from time to time require.''

If there were concerns about the operation of the memorandum of understanding, the Secretary of State could require a report on it to be included in the authority's annual report.

I ask the hon. Gentleman to consider whether that reserve power is sufficient to allay his concerns and to accept that the annual report should be seen primarily as an opportunity to focus on outputs rather than on the procedures that help the parties to achieve those outputs. I urge him to withdraw the amendment.

Amendments Nos. 123 and 130 would require the regulator, in his annual report to the Secretary of State, to include an estimated timetable as to when, if at all, he expects competition in each sector to be sufficient to enable the authority to withdraw from regulating that sector. That goes to the root of the question why the regulator is necessary, which we have already touched on this afternoon. Why have a regulator in these four utility sectors, given that there is no such regulator in most areas of private commerce, other than a general competition regulator for all sectors?

The proper purpose of a regulator, from a Conservative philosophical perspective, is to provide a proxy for a competitive market in a sector where no competitive market exists. That may be because sufficient relaxation of the legal protected monopoly has not taken place; because, despite the relaxation of the rules, no competition, or too little competition, has emerged; or because a service is a natural monopoly—for example, the utility distribution networks.

In performing the duty of providing a proxy for competition, the regulator must ensure that consumers are receiving a fair deal and are not being exploited by the monopolist. He must also ensure that the elderly, the disabled, the chronically sick and those in rural areas are protected from such exploitation. When competition is allowed, and subsequently materialises—as in electricity and gas supply and telecommunications—it is appropriate, after a convenient interval, to withdraw the regulator from the sector and let competitive forces deliver the benefits to all consumers.

That is why we support the new separate licensing arrangements for electricity supply and distribution. There is massive competition in electricity supply, but none in distribution. It therefore makes sense to try to narrow the focus on the monopoly that exists, so that the regulator can withdraw from the segment of electricity supply and confine himself to the distribution sector.
