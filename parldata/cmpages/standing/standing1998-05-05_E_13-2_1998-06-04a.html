Personal portfolio bonds

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 171, in page 72, line 10, after "546(4))", insert

    "in cases where a holder acquired a personal portfolio bond with the sole or main object of avoiding tax".

My observations on the clause will not stray outside the terms of the amendment because they are on the theme of legitimate and illegitimate measures to counter tax avoidance. The Treasury and, I regret to say, the Inland Revenue have been shown in a most unfavourable light. They have gone a long way beyond the legitimate and understandable actions provided by successive Finance Bills to counter tax avoidance where the wishes of Parliament are clearly being circumvented and the loss of revenue is on an unacceptable scale.

The measures before us to counter tax avoidance are disproportionate to the problem identified or described to the Committee, unless the Paymaster General can give us better particulars. What has especially annoyed ordinary taxpayers who hold personal portfolio bonds is the terms of the Inland Revenue tax notice which says:

    "This type of bond is designed primarily for tax avoidance purposes".

The bonds in question are a particular type of product. They are marketed to those people living and working overseas who wish to build a significant fund for their retirement in lieu of the normal pension arrangements that might have been open to them had they been UK residents throughout their working lives. Under the present tax statutes, they cannot avail themselves of the normal tax-exempt pension plans and savings products available to those who work in this country. They find that particularly galling.

6.15 pm

It seems to those people that the Government have a deep suspicion of anyone who is, or who has been, an expatriate. That became apparent during our debate on the foreign earnings deduction clause earlier this week, when the Government made clear their view that most expatriates work abroad primarily to avoid paying tax. However, in the experience of Opposition Members, such people work overseas not only in the legitimate pursuit of a career objective but often to advance this country's interests.

On publication the clause achieved a certain notoriety in the press. Since then I have received a number of letters from people who took out personal portfolio bonds not to avoid tax, but in good faith as a normal savings plan that they assumed to be in line with the Government's wishes. The Government frequently speak of the need to build up personal savings as a long-term insurance against state dependency. Such people are therefore acting responsibly and, moreover, entirely legally.

However, the Inland Revenue fought a long campaign against personal portfolio bonds, which it finally lost in the House of Lords. The route to the House of Lords was peppered with a number of unpleasant observations by officers of the Revenue, which suggested that anyone who persisted in buying and holding such bonds would be taken to endless appeals. It is not surprising that many taxpayers of modest means capitulate when they are thus confronted by the Revenue.

The special investigation section of the Revenue said that anyone who successfully appealed to the special commissioners would in turn be appealed to the High Court and eventually to the House of Lords. That did in fact happen, but the Revenue comprehensively lost. That outcome owes much to the courage and perseverance of Professor Willoughby, a man of unimpeachable integrity who was not to be browbeaten. On receiving his OBE from Christopher Patten in Hong Kong, the citation made reference to

    "the immense contribution you have made in the fields of taxation and the law."

Here is a man who, in addition to working legally overseas, contributed to the field of public policy and taxation. That may be why he was able to take on the Revenue and win comprehensively. In delivering the Law Lords' judgment, Lord Nolan described the fallacy that was at the heart of the commissioners' case. This was not a mere technicality. The judgment, which is extremely long and which I have no intention of quoting from at length, outlines the Inland Revenue case and subsequently demolishes it comprehensively. Lord Nolan describes what he calls the attack that the Revenue launched on Professor and Mrs. Willoughby and other Royal Life bond holders. Lord Nolan observed:

    "I am unable to follow the reasoning of the commissioners."

He concludes that Parliament's intentions were not circumvented by Professor Willoughby or the other bond holders. But the Inland Revenue now asserts that it lost only on a narrow point, which is a grotesque description of what Lord Nolan and other Law Lords said.

Despite losing a case in the highest court in the land, this clause is being introduced to overturn that judgment and to continue what can only be called the persecution of many hundreds I do not know the exact number of people who took out such bonds in good faith.

The attraction of onshore bonds is that they allow investors to invest their lump sum in a taxed investment, a fund managed by an independent fund manager of their choice on which they will pay higher rate tax on the profits on encashment. There is no way of avoiding tax, which is what the amendment seeks to define. It gives choice to the investor. The Paymaster General is one of the biggest advocates of choice for savers and investors, as we have seen in the various financial products that he has launched.

The other attraction of onshore bonds is that the investor may be paying a lower rate of tax on retirement, which is when he or she will want to take advantage of that long-term savings scheme. That is a perfectly legitimate form of investment. A 15 per cent. deemed gain is absolutely penal. More than that, it is a 15 per cent. deemed gain based on the total premiums paid up to the end of each policy year. As my right hon. Friend the Member for Wells said, it is cumulative, so that £100,000 would have a deemed gain after the first year of £15,000 and after three years of £19,837, regardless of how the bond has performed.

That deemed gain is taxed, despite any surrenders that may have been made to the investor during any of those years, and despite any losses that may have been made on investments within the personal portfolio bond, and in addition to any normal tax charge arising on a chargeable event; false surrender, death or maturity are the three main options. Those deemed gains are[Mr. Tim Loughton] subject to tax at the higher rate, yet no money has been taken out by the taxpayer; the taxpayer will pay out of his or her own pocket for deemed gains that he or she may not even have on paper and which he or she probably has not enjoyed in practice.

It is absurd that no relief is given if those deemed gains exceed the gross termination gain. The strong possibility is that in poorly performing markets, the investor will end up paying excessive tax, based on a loss-making investment vehicle. Neither you, Mr. Butterfill, nor I would ever dream of investing in such a vehicle if it were shown to us in that light, yet it remains a perfectly legitimate way for people to save for their approaching retirement.

The terms are absurd, and 15 per cent. is a particularly harsh rate. In clause 87, we see one law for the Revenue and another for the legitimate investment community. It is bizarre. It smacks of expropriation more than of tax.

That would trigger a chargeable event, but as many of those PPBs are long-standing, many of them may have made considerable gains. Many people will therefore face a large tax bill at the marginal rate long before they expected. That is not a particularly attractive option.

Clause 89

Provisional repayments in connection with pension business

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hammond: I am curious about this clause. As I understand it, it will have retrospective effect. Payments of tax that would normally fall to expire in 1998 the Inland Revenue would not be able to claw them back after that are now to be subjected to a further year of vulnerability, during which time the Inland Revenue might clause them back.

The clause extends the Inland Revenue's ability to claw back tax in respect of accounting periods ending before 1 July 1992 for one more year until 1 July 1999. It is extraordinary to put such a narrow and limited provision into the Bill that I wonder whether there is a special reason for it. Is the Inland Revenue targeting a particular victim or company from whom it wants to claw tax back? Does it want the provision extended for another year because it cannot claw that tax back by July 1998? If there is a special reason for the measure, will the Paymaster General tell us what it is?

I understand that as a result of the clause, after 1 July 1999, a general provision will allow the Inland Revenue to do what it is now trying to do during the year July 1998 to July 1999. The clause is therefore special and targeted, and I should like to understand why.

Mr. Geoffrey Robinson: It may help the Committee if I briefly outline what the clause will do and then reply to the hon. Gentleman, who clearly has an interest in it.

The clause extends the time limit for recovering income tax and tax credits that have been over-repaid to insurance companies carrying on pension business. It will apply only when the amount over-repaid cannot be ascertained until more than six years after the end of the relevant accounting period. I shall explain its more narrow purpose in a moment. What is being said may not please the hon. Gentleman, but it is clear why it is being said. The clause may also affect friendly societies carrying on pension business or tax-exempt life business.

The repayments in question are those that the company or society can claim during an accounting period in respect of pension and exempt business income. The claims are, necessarily, estimated, and it can take many year to ascertain the correct amount to which the company is entitled. At present, an assessment to recover excessive repayments can be made only within six years after the end of the accounting period. At the same time, it cannot be made before the correct tax liability for the period has been established. A delay in settling the liability could therefore have the absurd result that the excessive repayment could never be recovered. The new rule extends the time limit to the end of the accounting period following that in which the amount of over-repayment is finally determined. A similar rule already exists in relation to periods that will be affected by self-assessment for companies. The clause therefore provides a welcome measure of compatibility between the two systems.

The inability to recover an over-repayment in such circumstances would represent an entirely unjust enrichment of the companies affected. I am not suggesting that companies have deliberately dragged their feet over settling their tax liabilities, but leaving the law as it is would give them a strong incentive to do so. Removing that incentive and making it clear that we need the extra time that the clause specifies will encourage companies to behave well. The clause should be welcomed by the Committee.
