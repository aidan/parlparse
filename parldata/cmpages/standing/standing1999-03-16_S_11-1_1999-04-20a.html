I draw the Committee's attention to three contributions, the first from the Immigration Law Practitioners Association. When section 8 was originally debated, ILPA and others warned of its potential for discriminatory practices. The Labour party in Opposition appeared to agree with such concerns and to support its repeal. That remains, in ILPA's view, the right course to take:

    "There are other offences created by the 1971 Act which are ample to deal with racketeering."

The expert lawyers who have looked into the problem see no reason whatever for the Government to have changed their original position before the election.

We have already mentioned the CRE, which is at the front line of issues of discrimination in employment practice. The CRE is clear that clause 13 will not prevent discriminatory practices and that section 8 of the 1996 Act should be repealed instead. The CRE told me that there is much evidence of employers misunderstanding section 8, which goes to the heart of the matter. The previous Government--and now the present Government--could defend section 8 on the grounds that it would catch only unlawful employers and that no discrimination would be caused to people with proper immigration status.

Governments can talk in those terms until the cows come home but the reality remains that the CRE and other organisations receive phone calls every week from employers who clearly misunderstand the law and who make decisions about jobs often on the basis of the colour of people's skin rather than their nationality. Employers are denying jobs to people who can legitimately work in this country because of their misunderstanding of section 8. That is in accordance with reams of guidance sent out by the previous Government. The core of our debate is whether the code of practice will, on the basis of available evidence, make any significant change to previous circumstances in which guidance was sent to employers but they misunderstood it.

The third Organisation with much to contribute to our debate is the National Association of Citizens Advice Bureaux, also at the front line every week. Up and down the country our constituents go to citizens advice bureaux to ask about employment law. Its advice is straightforward:

    "The CAB Service believes that Section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996 is racially discriminatory in effect, and that it is not possible to address this effect through the current Bill's proposal for a code of practice. This view is based on the discrimination experienced by CAB clients since the introduction of employer immigration checks; and more generally on CAB evidence that people with little or no knowledge of immigration regulations cannot be expected to make accurate assesssments of immigration status. The CAB Service therefore believes that section 8 should be repealed."

NACAB goes on to provide specific examples of people coming through CAB doors who have experienced problems. In Hertfordshire, the CAB reported

    "a black client who was well educated and articulate, and had been trying to sign on for work with employment agencies. He was entitled to work, but the agencies informed him that they were required to check with the Home Office."

The briefing continues:

    "A CAB in north London reported a Somali asylum seeker who had been granted exceptional leave to remain in the UK, with permission to work. She had obtained work in a hotel, but when her exceptional leave came up for renewal, her papers were misplaced or delayed by the Home Office"--

an exceptional case, of course, of which there could be very few other examples!

    "Her manager suspended her without pay, stating that he was afraid to employ an illegal immigrant. The client went to the Home Office, who in turn telephoned her manager to say that her papers would be processed as soon as possible"--

that means only a few months.

    "Her manager nevertheless remained wary of being taken to court himself--on the basis of having read the Home Office booklet, "Employed Illegally" . The client therefore remained suspended.

Finally:

    "A CAB in Yorkshire reported a Chinese client who had been legally resident in the UK for 28 years. He worked as chef, and his employer had recently demanded to see his passport. The client was shocked and anxious about his security of employment".

It seems clear that his employer was demanding his passport because of fears raised by section 8. A code of practice may in fact generate a new set of problems when employers see a booklet landing on their doorsteps describing it and the fact that section 8 is still there. Employers simply chase employees about whom they have any suspicions on immigration status.

I believe that the law is unnecessary. The Immigration Act 1971 provides for the imposition of criminal charges against those employing people illegally. We all want to target those who deliberately racketeer and exploit others. It is as bad for the immigrant as it is for national well-being that such an exploitation racket takes place.

Section 25(1) of the 1971 Act lists the offence of assisting or facilitating illegal entry, which can carry a sentence of imprisonment on conviction. Section 25(2) makes it an offence to harbour an illegal immigrant, an over-stayer or people in breach of their conditions of leave. We can prosecute under the 1971 Act if the Government are willing to do so. If they believe that the Act is not up to it, they should revise it in a sensible way so that there can be criminal prosecutions against those involved in illegal racketeering, rather than leaving in place the blanket check of section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996.

I remind the Committee that we have recently finished debating clauses 11 and 12, which place in the Secretary of State's hands the tools for an intelligence-led approach. There was some querying. Section 8 seems an easy way in which to do the job. Rather than trying to find the racketeers, it places a blanket prohibition against all employers and one is reliant on that mechanism, rather than getting intelligence, targeting people and building the pattern of evidence that may be required under the 1971 Act. However, clauses 11 and 12 give the Secretary of State exactly the powers that he will need to gain and exchange the necessary information to successfully prosecute under the 1971 Act.

I hope that we are going to have an interesting debate. I notice some familiar faces from the Committee stage that approved section 8 of the 1996 Act. I am sure that they will have plenty to say following their experience three years on from the enactment of what was an "obnoxious and dangerous" clause.

Finally, I would like to comment on a question asked to the chief executive of the Immigration Advisory Service in the evidence-taking sessions of the Committee. We were talking about whether intent should be shown in the case of employment of immigrants. It was made clear in that gentleman's response that intent is looked for in many other areas of the law. Intent is a critical element. One of the problems with section 8 is that it removed the issue of intent and placed the onus on employers to check immigration status as anyone could be prosecuted if they had made a mistake, however unwitting.

I would like to see the authorities prosecuting those employing people illegally, but I would like to see them doing so having got the intelligence, built their case and done so as they would have done in any other area of criminal law. I do not want to see a general prohibition that requires people to sweep up the problems for the Home Office and their agents, as we have seen outlined in clauses 11 and 12. It would be appropriate for organisations such as the National Crime Squad and the National Criminal Intelligence Service to do that job. They can gain the information and make proper prosecutions, and we can combat the problem of the employment of illegal immigrants without all the downsides of unfair, unnecessary and outrageous discrimination brought about by section 8. It strikes me that the Government, if they are to be consistent with their previous claims, have no alternative but to accept the amendments and move towards repealing section 8 as quickly as possible, rather than the odd compromise of placing a new code of practice on top of section 8. That strikes me as a cowardly way to approach a situation on which they seemed to be clear before winning the election in 1997.

Too often it is the case that well-meaning, but perhaps not sensible, voluntary organisations, or well-meaning countries which accept groups of people coming out of Kosovo to facilitate their entry into other countries do not keep proper documentation. The UNHCR has asked us to co-operate so that refugees are properly documented when they arrive from Kosovo.
