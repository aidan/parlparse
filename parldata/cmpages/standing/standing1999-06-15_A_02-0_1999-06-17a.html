The other issue that the clause covers, perhaps more covertly, and with which this group of amendments deals, is the debate about best available technology not entailing excessive costs—BATNEEC—versus best available techniques, or BAT. I know that many Labour Members are concerned about that. Behind the jargon lie a real difficulty and genuine fears on the part of many businesses. Since the Environmental Protection Act 1990, they have observed the BATNEEC principle, which has served well, partly because it is balanced. Businesses are encouraged to fight pollution with the best available technology, but the essential second part is that that should not entail excessive cost. The application of the directive via the Bill allows the continental version—best available technology—to be introduced. That is a profound change in the law.

I was fascinated by a discussion yesterday in a Room on this Corridor with the Minister's colleague, the Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions, about how the Government regard the difference between the two concepts. He said that there was no practical difference between them. However, after researching this group of amendments, I am rather disturbed by that response.

While it is true that the definition of best available techniques in the directive contains a reference to cost, it is nothing like as clear cut as the British principle of not entailing excessive cost, which has served us over the past decade. Moreover, that reference is included only in an annexe, so it is clearly not a central part of the thinking behind the measure. The annexe merely states:

    ``Considerations to be taken into account generally or in specific cases when determining best available techniques, as defined in Article 2(11), bearing in mind the likely costs and benefits of a measure and the principles of precaution and prevention''.

Clearly, that is a much weaker formulation than the simple ``not entailing excessive costs''. Businesses genuinely fear that by slipping from one to the other, as the Government are with this clause, and by not including such a stipulation in the Bill, we may be introducing a new concept into our pollution control law that will be damaging to business. Replacing the word ``any'' with ``significant'' would deal with that problem simply and practically.

I am sure that the Minister will discuss what he understands to be the difference between BATNEEC and BAT. I hope that my worries about it are a result of me being new to the subject and that there is indeed no difference. From my reading of the provisions, however, there appears to be a genuine difference. I should be grateful if the Minister would set my mind at rest on that.
