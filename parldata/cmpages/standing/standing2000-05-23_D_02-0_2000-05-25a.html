Clause 1

Determination of mode of trial

Sir Nicholas Lyell (North-East Bedfordshire): I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 13, leave out ``but not'' and insert ``or''.

I, too, welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Sayeed. We hope that the Committee will not take unduly long, but there are serious points that are bound to take a few sittings to debate.

I attempted to obtain an answer to the same question as that asked by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes). I think that the Home Office cannot work out how many offences the Bill covers. My own research suggests that it covers about 700 offences in 390 different categories, but I have received approximately the same answer as the hon. Gentleman.

Either-way offences constitute the bulk of offences that come before the criminal courts and include some very serious offences: serious offences against the person, for example, grievous bodily harm, all the major drugs offences and most dishonesty offences. That is one reason why the Bill gives rise to such deep concern.

This seems to be a tiny amendment, but it is fundamental or at least close to the point that caused the Government to change the Bill between the withdrawal of the Criminal Justice (Mode of Trial) Bill-on which the Lord Chief Justice spoke in Committee in the House of Lords-and the introduction of the Criminal Justice (Mode of Trial) No. 2 Bill. As the Committee knows, I am fundamentally and passionately opposed to the Bill on principle, but I hope that that will not affect my ability to argue the case. The amendment goes to the heart of the Bill, which says that the court, when considering whether the offence should be tried summarily or on indictment, shall consider

    (a) the nature of the case;

    (b) any of the circumstances of the offence (but not of the accused) which appears to the court to be relevant; and

    (c) whether, having regard to the matters to be considered under paragraph (b)-

that is important and I will return to that-

    the punishment which a magistrates' court would have power to impose for the offence would be adequate.

The clause is deeply unjust and dreadfully confused.

Let us consider a small hypothetical case that exemplifies the situation that the Government consider to have been abused. Ministers tend to quote the Lord Chief Justice, for whom I have the highest respect, as I have for many people who take a different view on the Bill. He supported his view by describing a multiple offender whom he said was of weak intellect and who had about 60 previous convictions. The person did not plead guilty before the Lord Chief Justice, but he was duly convicted. Would the Minister be kind enough to find out what sentence he was given? That is material to the argument.

If the Bill becomes law, magistrates will be able to consider the fact that that was a shoplifting offence. We do not know how serious the offence was, but let us suppose that it was similar to that which the Attorney-General described when he opened the debate on the original Bill. I thought that his example was trivial, and I agree with my right hon. and learned Friend Lord Mayhew of Twysden, a former Attorney- General, who said that he hoped that the authorities would rarely prosecute for a single banana. However, let us assume that the Attorney-General was merely illustrating his point, and that the item was a sweater from Marks and Spencer or some other frequently stolen item that is valuable, but not immensely so.

Magistrates could consider the nature of such a straightforward shoplifting case and the circumstances of the offence, such as the fact that the item was stolen from Marks and Spencer and that shoplifting occurs frequently and must be stamped out. However, they would not be able to consider the circumstances of the accused. That is where the injustice leaps to mind. At least in terms of broad justice, the Committee will instantly recognise that there can be huge differences between someone with a string of previous convictions and no character, and a housewife charged for the first time or a man who has a blameless reputation, whose job is on the line and who could lose his position in society because he has been charged for the first time.

For reasons on which I shall briefly expand, the original Bill's provision that allowed consideration of such circumstances was removed, and now magistrates will not be allowed to take into account the fact that an individual has 60 previous convictions, a fact that was important to the Lord Chief Justice. It is even more absurd that proposed new section 19(2)(c) of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980 will not allow the court to have regard to previous convictions when considering the adequacy of the punishment that a magistrates court could impose.

Let us focus on that provision. It would be astonishing if a first offence of stealing a jersey, a bottle of milk or a tin of beans from Marks and Spencer gave rise to a custodial sentence. However, a multiple or serial offender with a string of previous convictions for dishonesty is likely to receive a substantial sentence, and, as many members of the Committee will agree, deserves and requires a substantial sentence well beyond the six months that magistrates are entitled to give. Under the Bill, the court is expressly forbidden to consider the circumstances of the accused, including their good character and position in society, the nature of their job, and the effect on that job of a conviction, or their bad character and frequent reoffending and the fact that they are a menace to society.

It has been said that the Government have one influential supporter for the principle of the Bill, and that is my respected right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Folkestone and Hythe (Mr. Howard). I disagree with him on the fundamentals of the Bill-one can disagree passionately with right hon. and hon. Friends, and it is part of our duty to speak up and say so.

My right hon. and learned Friend put his finger firmly on that flaw in the Bill when he spoke on Second Reading and eloquently set out exactly what I have put to the Committee. Clause 1 is fundamentally flawed and does deep injustice. That does not require overlong repetition, because the depth of the injustice stands out. One may be trying a difficult case of someone whose whole life and reputation are on the line, or a straightforward case of shoplifting, which is common. My right hon. and learned Friend Lord Mayhew, with her permission, gave the example of his wife, who walked out of a supermarket, pushing a trolley, without paying. She crossed the road towards the car park and, realising what she had done, rushed back. She might well have been acquitted in the magistrates court, but in such a case anyone who has experience of the courts would advise someone to go for trial by jury.

One difficulty in asking for trial by jury is that it is considered to be a slight upon stipendiary and lay magistrates and suggests that one does not respect them. I hasten to say that that is not the case.

I come from a legal family: my father is a barrister and High Court judge, my stepmother for 40 years-my mother died young-was a magistrate for 25 years. She was chairman of the Dacorum Bench at Hemel Hempstead. In my early days, as the Parliamentary Secretary will have done, I appeared many times before magistrates. I have a high regard for them and for Crown court judges, but I genuinely believe that it is difficult for anyone who tries cases day in, day out, to consider matters with equal objectivity and not to become case hardened. One has to deal regularly with the police. A jury tries the matter for only a fortnight and tries only a handful of cases. As I said at our previous sitting, a jury is a little parliament. It can stand back from these matters and give a different and higher standard of justice. It is a standard of justice which, whether one regards it as higher or lower in the absolutes, is the justice of the norm of the ordinary people of this country. It is the great strength of trial by jury.

Although my practice developed into employment law, industrial law, commercial law and, more recently, public and administrative law, I did a significant amount of work before magistrates in my first few years and a certain amount of work before juries. This is not to criticise the individuals but simply to state a fact of human nature: those who work as professionals in the system are part of the establishment. That applies from the very highest levels of the judiciary, down to the lay magistrates, who are in a sense the most junior judiciary although the most numerous. I am part of the system. I am part of the establishment. I sit as a recorder and I try cases as a recorder with the jury.

I shall give one or two examples of the kind of case where I would have done one thing and the jury did another and where, on reflection, I believe that justice was done and that our society is more comfortable with itself as a result. It is unlikely that the magistrates would have sent my first example for trial by jury. It was one of the first cases that I had to try as a junior assistant recorder at Southwark Crown court and it came from east London. It concerned a young man who worked in a hotel and was accused of stealing a telephone call to the value of £1.50. That was 10 years ago and it would be worth about £3 today.

One might ask why the case was brought. [ Interruption.] I am not sure whether the hon. Member for Salford (Ms Blears) considers it surprising or understandable. It was brought because the making of unauthorised telephone calls, and thereby stealing a value, had become rife in the hotel. The hotel said that it had made it absolutely clear that anyone who was caught doing it would be sacked and prosecuted. The issue was not that the young man had made the telephone call, but that he thought it was permitted. He did not consider that he was stealing and did not intend to steal. That was the proper issue in the case. We heard evidence that the hotel had made the position clear, and were shown written notices. He then gave his evidence. I summed up absolutely fairly, as I thought, and straight down the middle. Although it was a small case and he was likely to be given a conditional discharge or a modest fine, if he was found guilty, I though that the jury would probably convict. I suppose that if I had been sitting as a lay justice, I would probably have convicted, as my colleagues probably would. However, the jury acquitted. Why did the jury acquit? That is something that we do not know. I believe, as did the late Lord Taylor of Gosport, that we are wise not to allow research into juries. I could expand on that but there are one or two other aspects to be dealt with. We will have to deal with Hedderman and Moxon and the research from Professor Lee Bridge. I have very real worries about research. I shall deal with that and in doing so be strictly within the rules of order.

Sir Nicholas Lyell: The hon. Lady is on a slippery slope. The hon. Lady has said that she believes that that young man was wrongly acquitted by a jury. [ Interruption.] That is the implication of what she said although I have not thought it through completely yet. I have thought a lot about that. It is possible that the jury felt-we shall never know-that this young man had been sacked anyway, and that enough was enough. This is a fundamental strength of the jury system. It is why a jury is a little parliament. A jury sets the standards for our society, and is why we are a freer society that is more comfortable with the standards of justice that are meted out than we would be if cases were always decided by people who are part of the Establishment.

I return to what the former Lord Chief Justice said about the clause to which the amendment refers when the original Bill was debated in the House of Lords; the proposal is, of course, significantly different now. He said:

    I am not greatly enamoured of the criteria set out in new Section 19(3)(d) and (e). I respectfully think that it is better to give magistrates and circuit judges a wide and almost undirected discretion.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 20 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1254.]

The Government use Lord Bingham's support at every opportunity in debates on the Bill, but far from following what he advises, they have gone flatly and completely against it. They have not given magistrates and circuit judges an almost undirected discretion, but a discretion that is so tightly fettered that it amounts to a grave injustice and makes nonsense of the question whether they have sufficient powers of sentence and whether, under substituted section 19(2)(c) in clause 1,

    having regard to the matters to be considered under paragraph (b), the punishment which a magistrates' court would have power to impose for the offence would be adequate.

The clause as drafted is fundamentally flawed, and will attract direct and devastating criticism from the Lord Chief Justice if he has to speak about it in another place. The amendment would remove the defects, although not my fundamental objection to the Bill. I suggest that under new section 19(2)(b) the court should consider any of the circumstances of the offence and of the accused-that is, all the circumstances of the case and, as Lord Bingham said, that the magistrates and circuit judges should be given a wide and almost undirected discretion.

Helen Jones: I have listened carefully to the hon. Gentleman. The point that struck me most forcefully was that he said that if he were innocent, he would wish to opt for trial in front of a jury. Mr. Hughes: The hon. Lady raises all sorts of questions, some of which I shall leave until later. All courts have one obligation-to decide whether the accused is guilty on the basis of the evidence before them. I have no doubt that periodically the innocent are convicted by magistrates courts. Equally, I have no doubt that the innocent have also been convicted from time to time in the Crown court, sometimes because the evidence has been fabricated or corrupted, or because a mistake has been made. It is not a fact of magistrates or sheriffs courts that an innocent person is more likely to be convicted against a not guilty plea. A relevant issue for later debate is the number of appeals against conviction by those who pleaded not guilty. We need to compare the success of appeals against conviction in a magistrates court with appeals against Crown court convictions, but the outcome cannot be pre-determined.

It is because I am absolutely certain that magistrates have wrongly convicted that I would rather the decision about conviction was in the hands of 12 lay people than one or three magistrates. Another reason why the amendment should be accepted can best be illustrated by an example. As drafted, the Bill precludes consideration of the physical or mental ability of the accused.
