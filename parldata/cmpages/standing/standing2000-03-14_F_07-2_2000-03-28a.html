Mr. Harry Cohen (Leyton and Wanstead): I have had my cup of tea and am ready to go-[Interruption.] We shall have to wait a bit longer before going, though.

Mine is amendment No. 72, in the group of amendments moved by the hon. Member for Hallam. He described them as typical Liberal amendments; the Liberal ones were woolly, mine has more depth.

I differed from the Minister when I listened to him discuss communications data. Communications data are worthy of a higher safeguard than that proposed. It is disappointing that, according to paragraph 154 of the explanatory memorandum, the key safeguard is that content of telecommunications will not be disclosed because it is excluded from the definition of communications data.

That is all well and good, but the fact that the content of a communication is not involved is not a full safeguard. That overlooks the fact that, when a communication is made, its contents might often be deduced. When I use a telephone banking service and press particular keys to get a bank statement, I am not ordering a pizza-I am declaring a precise relationship with a financial organisation. The content of the communication could be deduced. As the Minister knows, I am identifying where I am to an accuracy of about 100 yards and who I am contacting if I use a mobile phone. There should be positive measures to afford statutory protection because confidential information can be deduced.

The data protection commissioner, in her ``Briefing for Parliamentarians'', agreed with that assessment. She said in paragraph 8:

    The Commissioner questions the distinction made in the Bill between the requirements for gaining access to data contained within an intercepted communication and those for gaining access to other communications data such as traffic and billing information. Both sets of data provide insights into the private lives of individuals and should be subject to equivalent controls and safeguards.

Consequently, my amendment would retain all the main national security and policing elements of clause 21(2), but would introduce a test of prejudice to mirror that found in section 29 of the Data Protection Act 1998. The data protection commissioner agrees that such a test would be useful. In relation to other parts of the Bill that provide access to communications data without a warrant, she said in paragraph 24 of her briefing:

    Whether or not access can be required should be subject to a prejudice test similar to that set out in S. 29 of the Data Protection Act 1998.

I do not say that communications data relating to policing and to national security may not be disclosed for that purpose. I would rather argue that such data could be disclosed if failure to disclose would prejudice a purpose. The person authorising the disclosure might be called to demonstrate that, perhaps to the relevant commissioner, in a dispute.

The clause allows a person authorising the collection of communications data to say, ``Here is a crime. I'm authorising access to the communications database.'' I want that person to say ``Here is a crime: failure to access the data will mean that it is likely to prejudice our efforts to catch the criminal.'' My amendment would introduce a minor but much-needed and important semantic change in emphasis.

I have a worry about the public health and safety provisions in clause 21(2). My reading of the clause is that on public safety grounds the police and MI5 can obtain communications data that relate to members of Greenpeace or any other non-violent political group. Will the Minister assure the Committee that public safety will not become a euphemism that can, in the hands of some authorising officials, permit the authorities to hinder or even stifle legitimate, peaceful and lawful public protest?

There is a danger that the clause can be used to interfere with lawful protest. I recollect, after all, that Robespierre had a committee for public safety. The amendment deals with public safety and public health issues differently from the clause, by using a test of

    serious harm to the health of any individual

Public safety can therefore be invoked on the grounds of serious harm to health, as individuals get hurt in violent demonstrations. Public health would come into play in respect of communications data relating to an individual suffering from Lassa fever. Such data could be used under the amendment, because failure to provide it would

    cause serious harm to the physical or mental health of any individual.

My proposal would, therefore, still cover the contacts of that particular sufferer.

I admit that I depart from the provisions on assessment and collection of any tax or duty contained in subsection (2). The effect of the amendments is common in that to a greater or lesser degree they all restrict the purposes for which communications data can be sought under the Bill, but in slightly different ways. The Liberal Democrats want to restrict the purposes to serious crime. The Conservative want to restrict them principally to the IOCA purposes, serious crime, national security and the economic well-being of Britain. This suggests that there is no difference as far as the Conservatives are concerned between the kind of intrusion involved in the collection of communications data and the kind of intrusion involved in high-level interception. I do not accept their argument. My hon. Friend the Member for Leyton and Wanstead wants to restrict them so that they are closer to the provisions of the Data Protection Act. The amendments come from different positions in their detail, but from the same perception that there should be a more restricted approach than that set out in the Bill.

I accept, as I have said before, that any intrusion into privacy should be tightly controlled. That is the intention of the Bill. It is the Regulation of Investigatory Powers Bill. We should also look closely at whether we want to over-regulate and therefore restrict the ability of the police and others to investigate crime. This is a serious issue, as became apparent from our discussion on clause 20 about the nature of communications data. Raising the threshold from prevention or detection of crime to prevention or detection of serious crime would restrict the ability of the police and others.

I will give some examples. There are many crimes that would not fit the definition in this Bill of serious crime that are nevertheless serious for the victim. Without the ability to use communications data, the ability of the police to investigate would be considerably reduced. I will give some examples of the crimes that we are talking about-crimes that might not result in a three-year term of imprisonment for a first conviction for a person over 18, burglary, malicious telephone calls, race hate crimes and some paedophile crimes. These crimes would not fit the general description of serious crime but, as the hon. Member for Hallam would accept, they are serious for the individuals concerned. If the police were able to collect communications data that might enable them to find the perpetrators of these crimes, it would be an important tool-I assess it no higher than that-in the armoury of weapons at their disposal. This is not an exhaustive list, but these crimes are significant because any investigation of them necessarily focuses on communications data. That is why I have listed them.

It is also necessary to consider the practicalities of investigation. Information might come in to the police-perhaps to a freephone number-that a person is involved in a particular crime. How does the investigation proceed? The police would check their records for any information, but before putting expensive resources into what might prove to be a fruitless investigation they could check that the information passed to them was accurate. If the information includes a telephone number-as it often does-they will need to check who is registered to that telephone. At that stage, there is no way that the police can be sure that the crime they are investigating fits the serious criteria. That is why the level must be kept at prevention or detection of crime. That addresses the points raised by the Liberal Democrats in this discussion. I appreciate the fact that the hon. Gentleman stated in his speech that he was looking again at the question of tiers following our conversation.

It will be worth while going through the other purposes for which communications data may be required under the Bill since the remaining amendments all restrict to some degree the ability of the intercepting agencies to access the data.

It is self-explanatory why communications data may be needed

    in the interests of national security.

The agencies will try to identify people with whom a suspected terrorist is communicating.

Accessing communications data on the basis of an ``in the interests of'' criterion allows an intelligence picture to be built up and operational decisions to be made on the deployment of more intrusive resources.

Under amendment No. 75, the telecommunications or postal operator would be reassured that the proportionality tests had been applied.
