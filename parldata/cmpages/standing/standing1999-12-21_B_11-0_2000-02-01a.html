Freedom of Information Bill

Clause 41

Commercial interests

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 140, in page 22, line 30, at end insert-

    "(4) In this section "trade secret" means any confidential trade information which is disclosed to a competitor would cause harm to its owner".-[Mr. Greenway.]

4.30 pm

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I had just moved amendment No. 140, and was about to give way to the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath).

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I wish only to say that the hon. Gentleman"s earlier remarks might have been construed as suggesting that the Liberal Democrats opposed all commercial confidentiality. That is, of course, not the case. We accept that there are certain circumstances in which that might be appropriate. We object to the breadth of the exemption in the clause. The hon. Gentleman addresses that in his amendment, and we shall support it.

Mr. Greenway: I am sure that the Committee is grateful for that clarification because, when the hon. Gentleman spoke earlier, I almost thought that he did not support the amendment. I was sure that that could not be right.

We believe that there is a need for commercial interest exemption generally, but have some concerns about the drafting of this clause, especially subsection (1), which provides an exemption for information if it constitutes a trade secret. Some commentaries on the clause appear to suggest that subsections (1), (2) and (3) somehow work together. That is not how I read them. Subsection (1) provides a separate exemption from that in subsection (2). Subsection (1) provides a class exemption:

    "Information is exempt information if it constitutes a trade secret."

End of story. The provisions in subsection (2) about information that would

    "prejudice the commercial interests of any person"

have nothing to do with subsection (1). They are two separate exemptions.

In that respect, the clause is unique because I cannot recall any of the other provisions for class or contents exemptions having two separate exemptions in the same clause-one a class-based exemption, the other a contents-based exemption. I am sure that the Minister will advise me if I am wrong. That may have added to the confusion. The majority of such measures are not in that category.

We must consider the question whether the sentence-

    "Information is exempt information if it constitutes a trade secret."-

is adequate. The Committee needs to consider whether the term "trade secret" should be defined in the Bill. The amendment would add the following subsection:

    "(4) In this section "trade secret" means any confidential trade information which is disclosed to a competitor would cause harm to its owner."

What is a trade secret? I am sure that we would agree that the formula for making Coca-Cola would be a trade secret. That is a secret manufacturing process or formula which, if obtained by a competitor, could, and probably would, remove a significant commercial advantage enjoyed by its owner. I am always impressed when I visit the Nestlé factory in my constituency to find that there are secret ingredients in a number of the items of confectionery made there. I would be happy to name them, but I do not want to be out of order.

Some things are obviously trade secrets, but others are less obvious. Is a negotiating position, marketing information, competitor assessment, personnel information or information about customers a trade secret? More important for the purpose of this exemption, what element of contracts let by public authorities to private sector organisations constitutes a trade secret and should therefore benefit from this exemption?

When, in the last Parliament, the Home Affairs Committee looked in detail at the management of the private prison service, it constantly came up against the question of which parts of the private prison contract were commercially confidential and which were open to scrutiny. Commercial confidentiality verged on being absolutely necessary as an exemption in the minds of the directors of the company, because in their view it was a trade secret, something that they had to keep from their competitors.

We do not believe that the trade secret goes far enough, because it is not obvious that there needs to be a harm test. As I have said-and I think that there is general acceptance-this is a blanket class exemption, but should there not be a harm test? The wording contains no requirement for confidentiality, nor does it say whether it belongs to the person whose commercial interests might be at risk if it was disclosed; nor is it clear whether it relates only to the manufacture or sale of goods or to all the contracts, some of which are of the kind that I have described. Therefore, the Bill offers enormously wide protection.

The protection given in the code of practice, which already exists, is much narrower. It contains just one exemption:

    "Information including commercial confidences, trade secrets or intellectual property whose unwarranted disclosure would harm the competitive position of a third party."

That is a crystal clear definition, which commends itself. We do not suggest that the Government"s exemption should be limited to that, but to ensure that as much information as would be released under the code would be released under the Bill, the Bill could benefit from a definition of trade secret.

Circulating among members of the Committee is a suggestion from the Confederation of British Industry that it would not welcome a definition in the Bill. Far be it from me not to sit up and take notice when the CBI gives us its view, for which we are grateful, but its argument that there should not be a definition stands on two premises. The first is the approach that the Law Commission has adopted to the misuse of trade secrets in its consultation paper-I shall come to that in a moment-and the second is that it would like the matter to be covered by guidance.

As regards the Law Commission"s approach to a definition of trade secrets, its 1997 report observed:

    "We are unaware of any authority on the construction of the phrase in these provisions of trade secrets"-

that is to say in "Legislating the Criminal Code: Misuse of Trade Secrets", the Law Commission"s own consultation paper of 1997. The commission went on to propose a new statutory offence of misusing trade secrets. It proposed a definition, whose essential elements were that it applies to information that its owner wishes to keep secret, is not generally known, and has some economic value which derives from the fact that it is not generally known.

The Bill therefore protects three different types of information held by a public authority about business. The first is information supplied to and accepted by the authority in confidence, which is covered by clause 39 and does not need to be covered by this clause. The second is information, whether confidential or not, which could prejudice anyone"s commercial interests, which is the second exemption in clause 41(2). The third is information that is not generally known but not necessarily confidential, which has economic value as a result. The disclosure of such information, however, would not necessarily prejudice anyone"s commercial interests because there is no prejudice test in the subsection (1) provision of a blanket class exemption of trade secrets. The case for such a blanket class protection is far from clear. On the contrary, it could lead to a general presumption that non-confidential information held by a business would be exempt. We think that that danger is another argument for having a clear definition of a trade secret, which adds important qualifications. There are three such qualifications: first, the information would be confidential; secondly, it would relate to trade; and, thirdly, its disclosure would be likely to cause harm to its owner. The Government"s overriding motivation for including such a provision-which we do not oppose-is to prevent the harm that would arise, because of the use that a competitor would make of it, were the secret to be divulged. Although we understand the CBI"s concern, it is important that the Committee should address that matter.

It is far from clear how guidance could be framed, and under what provision. What status or force would such guidance carry? Because there is no definition of trade secrets, no requirement in the exemption even for the most substantial damage test-let alone a harm or prejudice test-and no confidentiality requirement, subsection (1) needs attention. We hope that the Government will consider those points carefully, and will respond to our concerns today and at a later stage.

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor"s Department (Mr. David Lock): It is a pleasure, Dr. Clark, to serve in a Committee under your chairmanship. The amendment would mean that "trade secret", under clause 41(1), would attract a definition, and therefore be limited to the definition set out in the amendment. There are a number of UK statutes in which the expression "trade secrets" is used, but where it is not defined further. For example, a number of Acts contain provisions prohibiting the disclosure of trade secrets obtained in connection with the exercise of a power of entry or inspection given by those Acts. Section 325(3) of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 and section 8(5) of the Data Protection Act 1998 also contain a reference to trade secrets.

Part of the reason why the term is not defined is that it covers such a wide range of materials in different types of industry. The courts have considered that. Without wanting to sound like a barrister making submissions-my previous occupation-I shall refer the Committee to the case of Lansing Linde Ltd v. Kerr in the Court of Appeal, which I cited on many occasions. It described a trade secret as

    "information, which, if disclosed to a competitor, would be liable to cause real (or significant) harm to the owner of the secret".

That statement was qualified by the court in two ways. First,

    "it must be information used in trade or business".

Secondly,

    "the owner must limit the dissemination of it or at least not encourage or permit widespread publication".

Save for that, the courts have not precisely defined a trade secret in every circumstance.

4.45 pm

I draw the Committee"s attention to the first important feature of the definition adopted by the courts. In Lansing Linde Ltd v. Kerr, a trade secret was described as

    "information, which if disclosed to a competitor, would be liable to cause real (or significant) harm to the owner of the secret".

The amendment, however, would define a trade secret as

    "information which if disclosed to a competitor would cause harm".

Clearly, that definition is potentially much wider than the courts", which specifies

    "real (or significant) harm to the owner of the secret".

The hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) asked why the clause does not establish a harm test. The addition of a further harm test would be otiose where a trade secret is defined as information that, if disclosed to a competitor, would be liable to cause real or significant harm. Therefore, the Government are content to adopt the generally understood definition set out in English law.

In countries such as Canada and the United States, a statutory definition has been adopted in freedom of information legislation, but in other countries, notably Australia, it has not. In Searle Australia Property Ltd. v. Public Interest Advocacy Centre, the federal court of Australia considered the meaning of the term "trade secret" in section 43(1)(a) of the Freedom of Information Act 1982, which is the corresponding Australian legislation. The court held that the term should bear its ordinary meaning, and cited the "Oxford English Dictionary" definition of a trade secret as

    "a device or technique used in a particular trade or occupation and giving an advantage not generally known".

The court then cited Lansing Linde Ltd v. Kerr, and maintained that a trade secret need not be technical, but that it must be used or useable in the trade in question. Again, a harm test was adopted and the question whether the relevant information could truly be said to be a trade secret was taken seriously.

That demonstrated that the term "trade secret" has been described in, but not defined in, case law. It is sufficiently wide to cover information of a non-technical nature, but sufficiently narrow to exclude information not used in trade or business. On that basis, and given that the term has been generally used in an undefined form in other legislation passed by this House, it is neither necessary nor sensible to define in the Bill the precise meaning of "trade secret".

The hon. Member for Rydale asked whether this is the only clause in the Bill to combine what are, in effect, class and contents-based exemptions. Clause 38 strikes a similar balance between two related but not identical parts that deal with personal information.

 

Mr. Greenway: I do not think that that counts.

Mr. Lock: The hon. Gentleman says from a sedentary position that that does not count.

Mr. Greenway: The Parliamentary Secretary understands the point that I am making. I am happy graciously to accept that, like clause 41, clause 38, is a hybrid clause, but it does not quite fall into the same category as other, narrow definitions in the exemptions.

Mr. Lock: I, too, understand the point that the hon. Gentleman is making, but it is not central to our debate. He rightly referred to the definition of "trade secret" that is proposed in the Law Commission"s paper on the new criminal offence for the misuse of trade secrets. However, it is one thing to propose a definition in the context of creating a new criminal offence, where the boundaries of that offence must be circumscribed with clarity to ensure that people know where they stand with regard to criminal law, and quite another to propose a definition in civil rights legislation, which is what we are doing. Our definition will encourage openness, and will balance the right to access information available to official bodies with the need to protect emerging and evolving business practices.

The Government are concerned that, if the amendment were accepted, the categories of information that might be regarded as a trade secret could be considerably wider than those that appear to be defined by the courts. Member for Ryedale will agree with the way in which the Government want to proceed.

Mr. Lock: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for providing the opportunity for me to say something on that point. Guidance is ultimately a matter for the commissioner, but in determining what is appropriate in such a clause, the commissioner will be guided by courts" interpretation of the phrase "trade secret". Like the rest of us, she will be constrained by the meaning of that phrase. The courts have made it clear that a trade secret is

    "information, which, if disclosed to a competitor, would be liable to cause real (or significant) harm".

However, within those limits, the exact phrasing of guidance will be a matter for the commissioner.

Mr. Greenway: That was an extremely useful exchange and I am grateful to the Parliamentary Secretary for his response. Given the court definition of "real (or significant) harm" to which he referred, and given the Government"s refusal to accept significant harm or substantial prejudice in respect of most other exemptions in the Bill relating to public authorities, it is tempting to suggest that there is one law for the private sector and another for the public sector. In years to come, this exchange may well lead us into Pepper v. Hart territory, which will cheer up the hon. Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central (Mr. Fisher). The Parliamentary Secretary has made it clear that, in introducing this proposed class exemption, the Government intend that the definition of a "trade secret" should meet the "real (or significant)" harm test determined by the courts. It is important to business, and to the public interest in disclosure, that we get the balance right.

While the Parliamentary Secretary was speaking, I was wondering how a public authority might come by such secret information. The Food Standards Agency may, in investigating various food processes, become privy to the recipe for a food product or drink that would be a trade secret. I understand the point made about real or significant damage, which would result if such information were released.

Mr. Lock: I assure the hon. Gentleman that, in determining whether there is a class exemption for trade secrets, the public authority is none the less constrained by the public interest balance test contained in clause 13. Does he, therefore, accept that the public interest will be protected by clause 13 in determining the right balance?

Mr. Greenway: That is a helpful intervention. I was about to use an analogy that I have used before, in which an organisation might seek to use this exemption to persuade a public authority not to release information that genuinely should be released in the public interest, but which might be deemed a trade secret using a broader definition. I also take the Parliamentary Secretary"s point about guidance being a matter for the commissioner. I take a degree of reassurance from the Government"s intentions, and from their explanation as to why they would not include a formal definition of a trade secret in the Bill. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
