The second safeguard is even more relevant to the amendment. There are long-standing rules on the conduct of non-departmental public bodies, which deal with ethical considerations, responsibilities, responses to sponsor departments and to Parliament, accounting and audit, reporting and so forth. They also cover the financial interests of members.

The Cabinet Office publishes a draft code, which each NDPB adapts to its own circumstances. The draft code requires the chairman and other members to declare any personal and business interests that may conflict with their responsibilities as members. NDPBs are required to draw up rules of conduct in consultation with their sponsor departments to identify and deal at an early stage with any conflicts of interests.

The draft code refers to

    ``the keeping of a register of interests appropriate to the body's activities. The register should, as a minimum, list direct or indirect pecuniary interests which members of the public might reasonably think could influence judgment.''

The code goes on to say that NDPBs should consider whether registers should include non-pecuniary interests. It says that the register should be published once a year, or annual reports should give details of how access to registers may be obtained. The fact that there is access to the register should be made widely known. NDPB members should be required to update the registers as changes occur.
