Clause 38

Power to apply Act to individuals who are not otherwise ``workers''

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I beg to move amendment No. 247, in page 24, line 10, at beginning insert

    ``After a process of consultation with persons representative of industry''.

The Chairman: With this, we may take the following amendments: No. 200, in page 24, line 10, after `38', insert `(1)'.

No. 241, in page 24, line 10, after `may', insert

    ``where he reasonably believes that such provision is required to avoid the unreasonable exclusion from the provisions of this Act of any person whose position is analogous to that of a worker as defined in this Act but whose contractural arrangements do not fall within the definition of a worker in this Act,''.

No. 201, in page 24, line 18, at end insert--

    ``(2) Nothing in regulations made under subsection (1) above shall cause to be treated as a worker for the purposes of this Act any person counted as self-employed for the purposes of such an individual's tax.''.

Mr. Boswell: We will indeed be alert. The clause is important and these are modestly important amendments to it. I endorse the sentiment behind the two longer amendments tabled by my hon. Friends--the first merely paves the way.

I shall confine mose of my remarks to the amendment in my name, which would make the clause begin:

    ``After a process of consultation with persons representative of industry''.

I can hardly believe that that sentiment would create difficulty across the Committee, but I suspect --I judge the Under-Secretary's smile to mean that it is possible to anticipate what her brief will say--that I will be told that the amendment is unnecessary because the words are prescribed by what is reasonable.

I am sure that the Government would want to consult in normal circumstances. The purpose of the amendment is to hammer home the fact that consultation is necessary to get the fairest outcome.

In government, the Under-Secretary has sounded and has often been positive to the interests of smaller businesses. The amendment is in no sense tied to such businesses, but the people who run smaller businesses think that consultation is important.

As the Minister of State would no doubt say if he were here, both sides of industry should be represented in the consultation process. I do not like that phrase. The Minister sometimes slips into the continental style and talks of social partners. I always understood that phrase to be the European translation of ``both sides of industry''. We will not dilate on that. I once used those words in Europe to rather electric effect--people were not expecting me to say them.

The clause is meant to deal with problems. If difficulties arise, it would be common sense for the Secretary of State and Ministers to talk to industry. That does not always happen in the way that it should. I do not wish to refight the battles over earlier clauses, but there was concern about whether there had been adequate consultation with the Chamber of Shipping over clause 37.

We want the Bill to provide for consultation. To borrow and modify a phrase used by the Prime Minister, the best conduct of government arises from a process of consultation, consultation, consultation. That is not a cop-out from Ministers' responsibility for decisions, but a commonsense observation that if they talk around a problem, they may find a solution that requires no legislation or regulation.

Mr. John Bercow: (Buckingham): Does my hon. Friend agree that the merit of the amendments is that they provide protection from the arbitrary use of ministerial power? They would ensure that future provision for new categories of people to be treated as workers for the purpose of the Bill would be based on objectively definable criteria.

Mr. Boswell: Exactly so. My hon. Friend is perceptive. He is a staunch supporter of the Freedom Association. Every member of the Committee has a favourite libertarian organisation, such as the National Council for Civil Liberties.

The Government want sufficient government and a sensible outcome for their problems. Who knows what those problems are? I will not tease the Under-Secretary to tell me what problems might require regulation. If she has a list, she ought to come out with it, but I hope that she has not. I hope that she feels that she has produced a comprehensive Bill. If she strikes a set of problems, she should consult both sides of industry and come up with a fair and workable solution because it will then be more likely to work and be accepted.

I return to the importance of consultation with people in what might loosely be called the small business or small employers' sector. In my experience, which is not only of taking decisions in government but of suffering decisions as an ordinary regulatee, Governments occasionally fall into the trap of talking to the great and the good but not talking to representatives of smaller operations. The Under-Secretary is nodding.

Some years ago, I had a farm petrol tank. I received a set of regulations telling me that I would have to be there when petrol was delivered or nominate a responsible person to be there to check that the petrol did not spill over the top of the tank. The regulations stated that the person who would perform the task would have to receive appropriate training. I remember being unsure whether I should approach the Chief Whip and ask if I could be let off the three-line Whip so that I could go home to check that my farm petrol tank had not overspilled or whether I should get my chap off the combine when he was busy--he was the only person conducting farming operations for me. I did not know what kind of training was needed. I nearly asked ``What set of clots has passed this regulation?'', but I thought that as it was my colleagues and, perhaps, myself, I should not do so. I ended by saying, ``It's a fair cop, guv. I'm going diesel.''

I make that point because it was obvious from the inquiries I made about that issue that the big operators of specialist sites dealing in large quantities of fuel daily--they probably use more in a day than I would require by offtake in a year--were perfectly happy with the provision to have professional, trained persons performing the task, and rightly so. However, the regulations were not tailored to the circumstances of smaller operations.

As the Governments new evidence to the Low Pay Commission reveals, small businesses are a productive part of the economy. There are 1 million new employee jobs as well as additional self-employed jobs in that sector. Although I have not highlighted the point in the amendment, consultation should be not only with the great and the good, but with the smaller and less vocal. The legislation would then be more likely to work.

I want to make two other points, one of which my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham (Mr. Bercow) has perceptively flagged up. I do not wish to accuse Ministers of seeking or planning to oppress business people or employers by suddenly imposing new and arbitary regulations. There would be legal constraints on their doing so, as the Under-Secretary will know. Even if there were no such constraints, however, or if they were not effective because the Under-Secretary kept her schemes to herself, it would still be better to have the filter of a consultation process.

There is only one conceivable argument against consultation, and I would be interested to test the Under-Secretary on it. There could be a clear case of abuse if somebody found a loophole and was frustrating the intentions of the national minimum wage regulations. I said earlier that Conservative Members had no sympathy for such an approach. In such a case, it might just be necessary to run the new regulations from a starting date to avoid evasive action. Anyone who is familiar with tax law will know exactly what I mean. That is a possible qualification, and I would be interested in the Minister's response.

Broadly speaking, however, the process should move from consutlation with representatives from across the scale of employers to find the best practical solution to the open presentation of regulations to meet the case. Ideally, the regulations would be properly presented to and considered by the House, as they would be under clause 38.

I want the Under-Secretary to consider the issue of retrospections, which follows on from the thought of my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham. Even if the Under-Secretary were faced with a loophole, it is unlikely that she would wish to go back in time to penalise an employer who had found a way of doing things, but then discovered that the regulations had struck it down and that he was confronted with a retrospective assessment for the minimum wage. I hope that you will forgive me for my shorthand on the matter, Mr. Atkinson. It would be much better to run new regulations from a starting point. They would presumably have to have effect from the pay reference period of the individual employer, which would be initiated after the regulations came into force; otherwise, there could be problems about whether the regulations were in place in the periods they straddled.

As a general principle and unless there is a good reason to do otherwise, we should avoid retrospection. The purpose of the amendments is to show that Conservative Members want there to be consultation and an open decision-making process to tackle what employers, too, will often see as abusive ways of getting round the regulations. We should not, however, use regulations to oppress the individual employer or to confront him with a retrospective bill for the national minimum wage.

I promised to say two words about the amendment in the name of my hon. Friend the Member for Runnymede and Weybridge (Mr. Hammond). He has now returned to the Committee and will make the case himself. I think, however, that he is essentially making the same point as me, which is that Ministers need to operate reasonably and have good reasons for their decisions, rather than acting arbitrarily.

My other hon. Friends from the Back-Bench team are looking for a coincidence between the regulations on self-employment for the purpose of the Inland Revenue and the provisions before us. Leaving aside the Government's little local difficulties with the armed forces, the big exception to the adult national minimum wage are the self-employed. It would be sensible to take the same approach to the definition as the Inland Revenue. The Under-Secretary will know that that subject is sensitive because many problems with 714 certificates and with the redefinition of people as employed have arisen in the building industry. I shall not go into that matter now. It does seem silly, however, to create circumstances in which a person who is treated as employed for the purposes of the national minimum wage can be treated as self-employed with regard to income tax. If that can be avoided, it should be.

I take comfort from the fact that the Government argue in their explanatory notes on clause 40 that share fishermen should be excluded because they are de facto and ``de taxo''--if I may shorten the phrase--treated as self-employed. That is a useful analogy.

To summarise, I do not take exception to the clause in principle. The Bill should contain an exceptions clause if it is properly filtered through regulations that will enact any changes. The clause requires proper consultation and a due process, rather than the arbitrary action of Ministers. It is in that spirit that I move the amendment.

Mr. Philip Hammond (Runnymede and Weybridge): I should like briefly to add to what my hon. Friend said in speaking to amendment No. 241, which was tabled in my name and that of my hon. Friend the Member for South Cambridgeshire (Mr. Lansley).

Clause 38 allows the Secretary of State wide and sweeping powers to extend the provisions of the Bill to any other group. Such wide and sweeping powers are undesirable in any legislation. The Bill contains very comprehensive and carefully phrased definitions of employee and worker to ensure that all the people whom the Government intend to entrap or endow with the benefit of the legislation--depending upon point of view--are entrapped or endowed. The Government may have missed something when drafting the legislation. Later events may demonstrate that additional provision is necessary to give effect to the original intended scope of the Bill.

The purpose of amendment No. 241 is simply to impose an explicit test on the legislation. In acting to extend the scope of the Bill, the Secretary of State must do so because he--at present, she--reasonably believes that such extension is required to avoid an unfair outcome, which would result in the exclusion, via a technicality or loophole, of someone who was clearly intended to be included in the scope of the Bill.

I can see no objection to such an amendment. The Bill devotes great attention to defining precisely and comprehensively the people who are to be included. It contains not only the routine definition of an employee, but a carefully crafted definition of a worker. It therefore appears that to some extent such a catch-all extension clause will be redundant. However, a potentially dangerous amount of power will pass into the hands of the Secretary of State unless there is a specific restriction on her freedom of action.

Mr. Bercow: Does my hon. Friend agree that it should logically follow that if the Bill is fundamentally sound, it should be possible at least to predict confidently that such extensions will be few and far between?
