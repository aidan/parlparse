Clause 1

Prohibition on use of controlled title

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider the following: new clause 1--Approval to be required for company names or business names including the expression ``chamber of commerce''--

    ` . It is the duty of the Secretary of State to secure that the expression ``chamber of commerce'' and its Welsh equivalent (``siambr fasnach'') is specified--

    (a) in regulations under section 29(1)(a) of the Companies Act 1985, and

    (b) in regulations under section 3(1)(a) of the Business Names Act 1985,

    as an expression for the registration of which as or as part of a company's name, or for the use of which as or as part of a business name, the approval of the Secretary of State is required.'.

New clause 2--approval of certain company names--

    ` .--(1) Before determining under section 26(2) of the Companies Act 1985 whether to approve the registration of a company under a name which includes--

    (a) the expression ``chamber of commerce'' or ``siambr fasnach'', or

    (b) any other expression for the time being specified in regulations under section 29(1)(a) of the Companies Act 1985 which begins with the words ``chamber of'' of ``chambers of'' (or the Welsh equivalents),

    the Secretary of State must consult at least one relevant representative body.

(2) The Secretary of State may publish guidance with respect to factors which may be taken into account in determining whether to approve the registration of a name to which this section applies.'.

New Clause 3

Approval of certain business names

    `.--(1) Before determining under section 2(1) of the Business Names Act 1985 whether to approve the carrying on of a business under a name which includes--

    (a) the expression ``chamber of commerce'' or ``siambr fasnach'', or

    (b) any other expression for the time being specified in regulations under section 3(1)(a) of the Business Names Act 1985 which begins with the words ``chamber of'' (or the Welsh equivalents),

    the Secretary of State must consult at least one relevant representative body.

    (2) The Secretary of State may publish guidance with respect to factors which may be taken into account in determining whether to approve the use of a business name to which this section applies.'.

New Clause 4

Relevant representative bodies

    `.--(1) The relevant representative bodies for the purposes of this Act are--

    (a) British Chambers of Commerce;

    (b) the body known as Scottish Chambers of Commerce.

    (2) The Secretary of State may by order amend subsection (1) by adding to or deleting from it the name of any body (whether corporate or unincorporated).

    (3) The power to make an order under this section is exercisable by statutory instrument which shall be liable to annulment in pursuance of a resolution of either House of Parliament.'.

Mr. Andrew Lansley (South Cambridgeshire): May I say what a pleasure it is to serve on a Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Gale? It is not a pleasure that I have had, and I look forward to doing so in future. I hope that the chambers of commerce in Kent, as in other parts of the country, will monitor our proceedings and reflect positively on them. I should also like to thank the Minister for Small Firms, Trade and Industry for her time and trouble in preparing the Bill. I look forward to her comments on this first substantive debate on clause 1 and the new clauses.

I am grateful to you, Mr. Gale, for allowing the discussion of the clauses in this way. It will be more helpful to the Committee if I explain the purposes of the Bill as a whole at this stage than if I explain the purpose of the clauses one by one. I hope that the Committee will agree that the original clauses should be negatived and replaced with the new clauses.

I shall speak in two parts: on the purposes of the Bill and on the procedure of the proposed legislation and how it might work.

For many years, companies legislation has recognised the title of chamber of commerce as sensitive and requiring special approval. That sensitivity arises from several factors. First, the title is global--in Europe alone, there are 1,200 chambers of commerce and industry, representing 14 million enterprises. Chambers of commerce have an international role, and businesses, whether they trade in this country or abroad, must have confidence that chambers of commerce--or bodies so titled--will act objectively in encouraging trade between their members.

Chambers of commerce are therefore in a position of trust. Although they are membership organisations and--in this and some other countries--private sector bodies, they represent an element of public service. For example, chambers of commerce have played their part in establishing training and enterprise councils, supporting Business Links and working in local economic development partnerships. In a real sense, they emulate the philosophy of private enterprise allied to public service. That philosophy plays an important part in current policy, played an important part in past policy and, I hope, will play an important part in future policy.

The title denotes an organisation that should be accountable to the business community and the overall area that it serves. For those reasons, it is a sensitive title. It has always therefore been argued--with justification, I hope--that the title should not be allowed to be used by inappropriate persons, or in a way that does not provide the accountability that is the essential characteristic of a chamber of commerce.

I emphasise that the existing legislation has not been applied badly. Robust procedures within the present system have been followed by Companies House and the British Chambers of Commerce. I do not regard the Bill as a criticism of Companies House in relation to its activities under existing legislation; the Bill will help Companies House to do its job more effectively.

The Bill is intended to address weaknesses in existing legislation. When Companies House receives an application for the incorporation of a chamber of commerce, it must consider whether the application will result in a genuine chamber of commerce, which will enjoy the respect, and attract the membership, of the business community. No benchmark exists for making such a judgment; in British law there is no definition of what constitutes a chamber of commerce, and there are no criteria for approval as a chamber of commerce, or for related titles or the use of those titles. The Bill will provide for guidance to be published by the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry, to enable objective criteria to be used in determining which bodies should be able to use the title of chamber of commerce.

Chambers of commerce will vary from area to area, as the demands of industry in one area will differ from those in another. That is partly why there have been distinct problems in making judgments under the current legislation. Many of those problems have related to applications to establish bilateral chambers, which are involved in trade between this country and another country or are British chambers of commerce established in country X or country Y. Trade with eastern Europe since 1989 has been not the least important factor in that development. The British Chambers of Commerce has feared that some of the chambers resulting from such applications would be effectively a front for a relatively small group of businesses, and that the chamber would be more a trading house than a representative body. Too often, such proposals have come from public relations companies rather than from the business community as such.

It is not only bilateral chambers that have caused such problems. How can a judgment be made on applications to establish a Northern Ireland Chinese chamber of commerce or an international Christian chamber of commerce? There is currently an application before Companies House for the incorporation in Great Britain of the Democratic Republic of Congo chamber of commerce, the draft articles of association of which could result in a board consisting of one person. Companies House must be able to make such judgments with the benefit of objective, published benchmarks and criteria.

All over the world, the title of chamber of commerce is clearly identified with a specific geographic area. That is an important part of the judgments that Ministers, and Companies House on their behalf, make on the suitability of a title. Disputes about the geographic descriptor are often resolved at a local level and are not a matter for Companies House. In the past, however, a chamber of commerce, which was established only in one part of a region, has applied to amend its title to embrace the whole region. For instance, one city in west Yorkshire sought to become the west Yorkshire chamber of commerce, despite the fact that there were strong, powerful and traditional chambers of commerce in Leeds, Bradford and mid-Yorkshire. An application was also made for a black country chamber of commerce, even though there are strong chambers of commerce throughout that area. An application for a Wessex chamber of commerce related only to a small part of west Wiltshire.

The use of geographic descriptors therefore creates problems. In the past, there have been arguments about whether east London is the same as east of London. The east London chamber had already been established, but another group of people sought to set up another chamber of commerce with the geographic descriptor, east of London. The imprecise nature of the law, and Ministers' lack of type of powers that will be made available by the Bill, led Ministers to decide that they could not reject the east of London chamber title. That is not to say that such a body should not necessarily be established as a chamber of commerce, but the geographic descriptor applied should already be understood and justified by its membership and intentions.

When an application for incorporation is received, the current defences are good but not good enough. In effect, anyone can set up a chamber of commerce, canvass for members and sell services under a chamber trade name. In practice, virtually nothing can be done to stop that process. No evidence exists of wide-scale abuse. However, as many chambers of commerce are unincorporated associations, we may see further examples of businesses attempting to sell services to local small businesses in particular. They may also try to use the title ``chamber of commerce'' even though their constitution and membership does not justify the use of that title.

The structure of the Bill that was passed on Second Reading set out the objectives. That is one way of proceeding. However, the securing of Government support opened up new opportunities, for which I am grateful to Ministers.

We must be alive to the fact that chambers of commerce constitute an organic movement that has been established for more than 200 years. We wanted to assure chambers of commerce that the legislation would not limit the opportunity to reflect change. For example, during the past two years chambers of commerce in some parts of the country have merged with training and enterprise councils, which have duly been granted distinct functions such as training. In so far as Ministers take decisions on controlled titles, they are able to update and vary the factors and the characteristics of the chamber of commerce.

Happily, Ministers have contributed to and co-operated fully with the proposed structure, which is more consistent with existing legislation. For example, the listing of controlled titles will be carried out through the same regulations as the listing of companies and business names for all other purposes. Business will therefore find all titles relating to the chamber of commerce where it expects to find a list of controlled titles.

The measure will be fleshed out in guidance to be issued by the Secretary of State, which will provide a degree of flexibility over time. The British Chambers of Commerce and the Scottish Chambers of Commerce have discussed the matter with Ministers. They are happy that the legislation will reflect those factors that should determine which bodies enjoy the title ``chamber of commerce''.

I shall turn to the way in which we might proceed under your guidance, Mr. Gale. I hoped that we might use the first debate to discuss the Bill's substance, as you rightly said. I should inform the Committee that I am happy that we move through and negative the clauses that currently stand part of the Bill. Having then debated the new clauses, we might bring them in, along with the amendments.

 

The Chairman: Order. It must be understood by the Committee that no further opportunity will arise to discuss new clauses 1 to 4--they will simply be moved formally later and then voted on. The hon. Gentleman is therefore right to say that the clause should be moved and discussed now.

Mr. Lansley: Mr. Gale, I am grateful for that helpful clarification.

The Minister will want to explain precisely the structure of the new clauses. However, in simple terms they seek to lay a duty on the Secretary of State to control the title ``chamber of commerce''. They will also require the Secretary of State to publish guidance on the factors to be taken into account in determining whether to approve an application. New clause 2 and new clause 3 set that out. Such factors would embrace the characteristics of a chamber of commerce, its representative functions, its membership character, and its accountability to the local business community and the community at large. Moreover, the new clauses will ensure that the proposed geographic descriptor is justified in respect of that body's constitution.

The new clauses will also lay upon the Secretary of State a requirement to consult a relevant representative body. In that context, the new clauses will also specify that the British Chambers of Commerce and the Scottish Chambers of Commerce constitute such bodies, although not exclusively so. The guidance should take into account bodies such as the training and enterprise councils' national council, a legitimate and relevant representative body that the Secretary of State might consult when establishing a chamber of commerce. Should a chamber of commerce and a training and enterprise council merge, the resulting body might enjoy a chamber of commerce training and enterprise title.

If the Committee is minded to negative the clauses, the schedules will also fall. Amendments Nos. 1 to 7 need not then be considered, because they would be overtaken by the clause stand part votes. We would then have just two sets of amendments with which to deal, one of which tackles the change to the short and the long title. That amendment would bring the title into line with existing title usage governed by Companies House under the companies and business names legislation.

The commencement and extent provisions will enable Ministers to enforce the legislation once they are happy that guidance can be published. With regard to extent, there will be a change. We had previously thought it sensible to extend the legislation to Northern Ireland. However, companies legislation has in the past been extended to Northern Ireland by way of subsequent statutory instrument, and I am happy for us to proceed in the same way in this case.

As you say, Mr. Gale, having debated the new clauses we would be able formally to move them in order to bring them into effect at a subsequent stage. I hope that the Committee now understands the Bill's purpose and the way in which we plan to proceed this morning. I invite the Committee to support the Bill by negativing clause 1 and proceeding subsequently to approve the new clauses.

The Government warmly support the work of the British Chambers of Commerce and the Scottish Chambers of Commerce in promoting recognition of the title of chamber as one that is synonomous with quality. We all know, as constituency Members, how important the services of chambers are to local business, which makes this a good opportunity to record our thanks for their work.

The Government are therefore very pleased to support the core objective of the Bill, which is to establish a statutory right for the BCC and the SCC to be consulted when the Secretary of State is considering whether to approve new business or company names that include the expression, ``chamber of commerce'', or specifiy related expressions. The new clauses and amendments seek to secure the core objective of statutory consultation in the clearest and most workable way, by providing a clear link with existing legislation on the protection of business and company names and by ensuring that there is no duplication in the Bill.

As has been said, the new clauses will require the Secretary of State to consult the BCC or the SCC when determining whether expressions such as ``chamber of commerce'', ``chamber of trade'' or ``chamber of commerce, training and enterprise'' may be used as part of a registered company or business name. The names would be specified by statutory instrument under existing names legislation and not specified in the Bill. That is a more effective way of ensuring that businesses wishing to use such names know that the Secretary of State's approval will be required.

The new clauses will also ensure that the expression ``chambers of commerce'' and its Welsh equivalent could not be removed from the relevant statutory instrument without primary legislation. That is important. The new clauses provide for the Secretary of State to issue guidance on the factors that she would take into account before approving the expression ``chamber of commerce'' or similar specified expressions. My Department would agree this guidance with the BCC and SCC, and once agreed it would be placed in the Libraries of both Houses.

Finally, the amendments would provide for the Bill to apply in Great Britain, rather than in the United Kingdom as a whole. In line with the normal form for companies legislation, comparable provisions will be introduced in Northern Ireland by order, and not through this Bill.

There were many helpful and productive discussions between the Government, the hon. Member for South Cambridgeshire and the BCC prior to the Committee. That illustrates the way in which Labour and Opposition Members can work together on legislation to help the business community as a whole. I am delighted to commend the new clauses and amendments to the Committee.
