Clause 78

Secretary of State's scheme

Question proposed [this day], That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed.

Mr. Richard Allan (Sheffield, Hallam): In our clause stand part debate, I have tried to refer to general concerns raised by the Minister's response to certain amendments. Concerns remain in two critical areas. The first is the level of assets with which an individual will be left before the provisions kick in, and the second is the level of support that the Government can reasonably expect from outside organisations, such as churches and support groups.

The Minister's responses did not satisfy our queries, and only added to the concerns of Opposition Members. As the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) pointed out, we may expect further debate on such matters. That is especially true of the other place, where many people are involved in faith groups, churches and support organisations. However, at this stage, that is all that I need say on clause 78.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): It is plain nonsense to suggest that we are going to raid the coffers of churches, voluntary organisations and charities—no doubt, even the coffers of charities for widows and orphans—to avoid paying for support. I condemn the hon. Gentleman's attempt to stir up alarm for blatantly political and short-term ends.

We are setting up a national system of support to replace the haphazard local authority, voluntary and charitable schemes that are currently in place. Charities such as the Refugee Council and others receive state funding to provide some services for asylum seekers. We will continue to support such services. As charities provide support as agents acting on the Government's behalf, it is quite right that they should be funded by the state. Clause 78 deals with that.

However, we do not expect charities to step into the state's shoes. I am not aware, for example, of any charity providing the long-term full support proposed by the Government for the 14-day period needed to take people outside the public support system that we are implementing. Therefore, charities, church groups and others should be reassured and should not listen to the rather alarmist nonsense that has been trotted out.

The Secretary of State will establish the scheme set out in clause 74, and clause 78 provides for the detail of how it will be put in place. Subsection (3) provoked a lively, if ill-focused debate this morning, but it does not set out matters in the way that some have suggested.

Let me explain what subsection (3) is intended to do. In setting out the new support scheme in regulations, we want to be able to take into account support and assets that are otherwise available to a person or might reasonably be expected to be available. The provision is directed, first and foremost, at a person's own resources, but may extend to support from friends and relatives already in the country and, in principle, to support from certain organisations, should those organisations be willing and able to provide such support.

Contrary to the impression that may have been given this morning, the Department of Social Security generally disregards £3,000 of assets in assessing eligibility for income support. However, when assessing asylum seekers' eligibility, the Department takes into account a person's total assets. That reflects the fact that asylum seekers are assumed to be here in the short term and are being helped in an emergency, which is reflected in the fact that they receive 90 per cent. of normal income support. We want to establish our own rules and protocols for how to treat the assets of someone on income support. They will cover how much cash to disregard and consider items of jewellery and other personal possessions.

All manner of interesting quesitons will be considered. For example, we shall have to consider how we deal with someone who owns a car. Will we regard an old banger in the same way that we regard a Rolls-Royce? What will happen if someone has a mobile phone, and what will be our position towards credit cards? Such issues will produce much discussion in the consultation process. However, they are not a matter for the Committee. They will be covered in regulations and interesting discussions will form part of the draft.

I am not writing the regulations now; they will be set out at the appropriate time. However, I wish to deal with the issue of jewellery. Personal jewellery, watches of a reasonable value and wedding rings are not relevant. Let me reassure everyone that they will not be considered. We intend to consider people who deliberately secrete away large sums of money in the form of gold and jewellery. Sometimes the assets are worth thousands of pounds. As I said earlier, a woman told me quite openly why she had such assets.

If someone makes a deliberate attempt to hoodwink the taxpayer, the regulations should be able to deal with that. The hon. Member for Hertsmere said that there was no distance between us on that. If there is a good reason for not taking account of an asset—the person involved might not own it, or it might be a family heirloom—we shall be able to consider those factors reasonably through the regulations.

We also need to take a view on the resources available to someone. If an asylum seeker has relatives who are already established in the country and who are willing and able to offer support that partly or wholly alleviates destitution, we shall want him to consider drawing on that support. We might need to top it up if required. However, if relatives decline to offer assistance, we shall not be able to force them to do so and we shall not generally reject an asylum seeker's claim for assistance on the ground that support might be expected to be available. That is an important reassurance.

We are familiar with the circumstances that might occur at the limits. For example, a sponsorship arrangement might be in place that imposes a legal obligation on a relative from which he cannot walk away. In such cases, we would steer the asylum seeker towards that support, although we would be bound to support him if the relative refused to do so. We would then reclaim the costs from the relative in the way described in clause 89.

We need to include the concept of support that might be reasonably expected to be available to the asylum seeker in order to cover several situations. First, support may be avialable but an offer is not made. For exmaple, a rich relative is known to exist, but he may not yet have been approached.

Secondly, someone might deliberately deprive himself of assets to qualify for support. Let us consider the case of someone who arrives at Heathrow with £5,000 in his pockets and who, three days later, claims to be destitute and cannot explain where the money went. However, he might suggest where it went by pointing to new gold jewellery or to a car that he has just bought. We would want to take account of such assets and the money that would arise if he disposed of them. The Department of Social Security arguably adopts a more robust test when someone deliberately deprives himself of cash in order to qualify for benefits.

Thirdly, there might be other organisations to which the asylum seeker might look for support in certain and particularly limited circumstances. That brings me to the role of charitable and other organisations. I want to reiterate the reassurance that I gave about them. They have a clear role to play in supporting asylum seekers. Among other things, we are in active discussion with several organisations, including the Refugee Council and the Refugee Arrivals Project, about the role that they might play in the initial reception and screening of asylum seekers for which we shall fund them through grant aid.

State funds will be available if such organisations offer support that is comparable to what we might otherwise offer. If they are able to use state money to support asylum seekers, we will expect them to take advantage of state funding, which may be directed through a charitable or voluntary organisation. That might reasonably be expected to be available, at least for the duration of the period for which the support was on offer. For example, support is on offer for a day or two while people are initially brought into the country and their circumstances are sorted out. There are other circumstances in which support from the voluntary sector might be an alternative to or complement the support that the directorate can offer. As I said earlier, we would not want the double counting of benefits in which state funding were provided through a voluntary organisation and directly through the asylum support directorate. Double counting would not be fair to the taxpayer or to the charitable donor.

The offer or provision of temporary or limited support from a voluntary body would not place an ongoing liability on that body or disqualify the asylum seeker from entitlement to support under part VI. In formulating the regulations covering the issue, we aim to recognise the limited role of such assistance and to provide a route from that assistance to support under part VI.

Let us be clear: we would not use the provision or regulations made under it to define a role for voluntary organisations that they were unable or unwilling to take on. It would be neither practical nor defensible to expect support to be available from a voluntary body if that body declined to operate. There is no question of our saying, ``Go away, agency X ought to help people like you and we will not provide any assistance.'' There is no intention whatever to impose an obligation on the voluntary sector that it is unwilling to take on. The subsection will be used to form a partnership with the voluntary sector. I want hon. Members who earlier sought to construe otherwise to be clear that their attempt to do so was misconceived; I hope that they will accept that.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: This is the sort of issue that can largely be dealt with in regulations, but the hon. Gentleman is right to raise it at this stage and probe the way in which we will deal with it.

The amendments would require the Secretary of State to provide continuing support pending the determination of an appeal. The intention is that the asylum seeker should make an application for support if he would otherwise be destitute. The Asylum Support Directorate, using eligibility procedures which are currently being devised and which will in due course be the subject of regulations, will determine whether an asylum seeker is capable of supporting himself. We expect that the overwhelming majority of asylum seekers will not be able to do so, and the number of people who will fall outside the provisions will in practice not be large. It may include those who have large amounts of gold or cash with them or access to large bank accounts.

We believe that the provision is the best way to keep control of where people are—making sure that they keep IND informed of their address if they are merely on support or remain in the accommodation if they have access to both support and accommodation under the Asylum Support Directorate's mechanism. Therefore, there will be no incentive to declare a person destitute; in many ways the incentive will be to ensure that, wherever possible, we keep in contact, because we would want to be able to process the case quickly.

Equally, if the directorate concludes that an asylum seeker can provide support for himself, he will be given reasons and will be able to undertake an appeal. If we cease to support someone, it will typically be because it is clear that he has other means of support. It would be unreasonable if the Secretary of State were to conclude that someone who did not have access to the system had support when he did not. We would have to make an assessment based on the eligibility criteria and decide whether the person was otherwise likely to be destitute. We intend that any application to an adjudicator should take place speedily. If the Asylum Support Directorate has concluded that the asylum seeker is not destitute, that will be the basis on which the means to support him are considered.

How the person supports himself in the intervening period will depend on two things—how much money he has and the detail of the regulations that emerge in due course. We may look at ways in which we can provide some interim support if it is clear that it is a marginal case where there is some doubt. I suspect that most people in that category will not qualify and, therefore, the marginal case to which the hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam (Mr. Allan) referred will not arise.

The regulations will be the place to deal with marginal cases, the detail of how we assess them and whether and the extent to which we provide interim support pending an appeal. I accept that it is a straightforward and proper issue for the hon. Gentleman to raise now, in order to probe the way in which we seek to do it. At this stage, because we will look at the detail in the regulations, I can only say that we will look at marginal cases and see whether there is some means of support. For example, we could give charitable or voluntary organisations funding to enable them to provide interim support pending an appeal, in hostel or other accommodation. Various options are open to us. None of us wants people to be without provision. Indeed, our commitment is that they should not be.

Having given those indications to the hon. Gentleman, I hope that he will seek to withdraw the amendment.
