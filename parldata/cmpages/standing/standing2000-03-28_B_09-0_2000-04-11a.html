The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): I am delighted to see you, Mr. McWilliam, and I hope that you are delighted to see us. We shall judge that at the end of the sitting.

Paragraph 1 of schedule 2 sets out a number of activities in which people should not engage when exercising the right of access on open country and registered land. They are meant to be common-sense restrictions. The hon. Member for Somerton and Frome asked why we have only those restrictions and no more. They are derived from the second schedule to the National Parks and Access to the Countryside Act 1949, but they have been updated to reflect the development of new forms of recreation—for example, hang gliding, paragliding—and new legislation. Rather than seek a completely comprehensive list, I remind the Committee that, as I have said before, where a particular difficulty arises it will be possible to apply for a restriction under chapter II, and if the problem is serious enough a byelaw may be justified. We also have the power by regulations to update the list of restrictions in schedule 2 in the light of experience.

Hon. Members have tabled a large number of amendments, and I shall try to deal with each of the points that they cover. They divide into four categories, the last of which is composed of further amendments. The first category comprises those activities that are already criminal offences, and many of the activities listed in schedule 2 are also criminal offences. They have been included because we believe they might cause problems in open country. I am sure that hon. Members will agree that it is important to keep the number of restrictions manageable and to avoid replicating existing offences where unnecessary, otherwise the list would be endless.

We expect the restrictions in paragraph 1 to be widely published—certainly that is our wish—particularly in the vicinity of access points, which we debated last week. An excessively long list would serve only to discourage the interest of passers by and would be counterproductive. We must be realistic about how much information people are prepared to assimilate. However, I do have some sympathy with the argument that certain other criminal activities should be added to the list of restrictions. For instance, it is undesirable that somebody who has been abusing alcohol or drugs cannot be asked to leave the land unless he offends against one of the restrictions in paragraph 1. I do accept that point.

Elements of amendment No. 100 on the removal of minerals, amendment No. 103 on the misuse of drugs, and amendment No. 134, sub-paragraph (aa) on polluting water, and sub-paragraph (bb) on breaking into a shooting cabin are worthwhile. I shall consider tabling an amendment on Report which would provide that the commission of any offence while on access land constitutes a breach of the restrictions in schedule 2, which would, of course, render that person a trespasser and therefore liable to be removed from the land. None of the amendments tabled seek such a general provision, but it is right that landowners should be able to ask someone to leave their land if they commit an offence of any sort. In some cases, the offence may not be related to access, which was the reason for my initial doubts on this, and may have little or no impact on others or on anything in or on the land, so such a measure might be considered rather sweeping. If the offence is serious, we would expect action by the authorities and criminal proceedings to be brought. I have made that statement before. The second category of restrictions relates to harmless activities. That has less merit. The debate had an Alice in Wonderland atmosphere, which I shall try not to encourage. Amendment No. 102 would place a restriction on anyone who leaves any item, whether or not they intend to return to it. I presume that the intention is to prohibit the leaving of litter, which we have discussed. Paragraph l(p) places a restriction on depositing rubbish or leaving litter. The criminal offence of dropping litter in a public place is punishable with a fine of up to £2,500, although that is rarely exercised. The provisions are sufficient to cover problems that might arise, but the amendment goes further. It would—no doubt unintentionally—prevent people from leaving a rucksack under a hedge when they climb to a nearby viewpoint.
