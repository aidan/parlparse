Warm Homes and Energy Conservation Bill

10.30 am

Mr. David Amess (Southend, West): I beg to move,

    That, during proceedings on the Warm Homes and Energy Conservation Bill, the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten and at half-past Four o'clock.

You and I entered Parliament 17 years ago, Mr. Gale. This is the fourth Parliament that we have enjoyed together. I speak for many others when I say that we have come to admire you as a man of great integrity and as a very hard-working Member of Parliament, fair-minded and determined but in every sense a champion of the underdog. I welcome your chairmanship of the Committee. The previous Committee of which I was a member that you chaired concerned a health matter. I am not suggesting that you were the champion of the underdog then, but I recall that I bored on at great length on several issues but always remained in order. I thank you for your indulgence during the hours that we spent in that Committee.

The good news is that I do not expect to have to make such lengthy speeches in this Committee. I moved the sittings motion to allow us to sit next week, for several reasons that concern me. I hope that we will all then have an opportunity to reflect on where we are with the Bill.

It is not immodest to claim that the Bill is noble because I do not regard myself as its chief architect. The plaudit for that should go to other hon. Members. One would have thought that the matter could have been dealt with just this one sitting; it goes without saying that we die unless we exist in an environment of a certain temperature, just as one dies if one does not eat or does not breathe air. Everyone should welcome the Bill. We had a splendid Second Reading with, I think, the largest turnout of any Friday. That was due not to me but to the excellent organisation behind the Bill. In all, 143 Members of Parliament turned up to support it, many others could not be there for genuine reasons, but we hope that they will be able to support it when it returns on Report. We had 22 speeches, covering a range of issues.

I have read carefully the Hansard report of the debate. Although 143 hon. Members supported the measure, it would be disingenuous of me to claim that everyone wholeheartedly agreed with it. I spoke for longer than I had expected on Second Reading, but I wanted to take all the interventions. I said to my right hon. Friends the Members for Bromley and Chislehurst (Mr. Forth) and for Penrith and the Border (Mr. Maclean) that I accepted that they were being constructive and that I very much wanted them to serve on the Committee, and I reinforced the point when we discussed the money resolution. I wanted them to be here, as they would probably have improved the Bill. Hon. Members would have been given the opportunity to make their detailed points concerning financial repercussions and duties on landlords.

My hon. Friend the Member for West Chelmsford (Mr. Burns) talked about the affirmative resolution on Second Reading, and the hon. Member for East Antrim (Mr. Beggs) mentioned Northern Ireland. I fully expected all hon. Members with concerns to volunteer to be on the Committee. As we know, one need not serve on a Committee to table amendments. I tried to encourage others with concerns to serve but they were busy with other matters. I do not know what more I can do than suggest that such hon. Members serve on the Committee.

The main reason why we must sit next Wednesday is the Government's position. I welcome the Minister, the hon. Member for Sunderland, South (Mr. Mullin). I say gently that I fully accept that he has difficulties with the Bill because Lord Whitty, the Minister with the lead responsibility on the subject, sits in another place. The hon. Member for Plymouth, Sutton (Mrs Gilroy) was at the meeting with the Government before Second Reading on 10 March; we had worthwhile conversations with Lord Whitty. There were difficulties concerning the number of definitions, but I felt that the meeting was constructive and that we had arrived at a compromise with which we could all live.

Before hon. Members groan, I should say that I regard myself as a straightforward person. Politics can be something of a game and there might be a sub-plot. If there is one, I hope that it will emerge this morning or next week. There seems to be a difference between what I thought we had agreed during the private meeting and the amendments that have been tabled.

I say to the Minister that I have no problem with taking three clauses from the Bill and inserting the new clause, but I have problems with the detail of the new clause. In essence, it seems to change the original thrust of the Bill. If I laid down and accepted it completely, the Bill's supporters would be annoyed with me for meekly giving way to the Government's new direction. I pay tribute to Miss Jenny Saunders and National Energy Action, which has done an enormous amount to promote the Bill. As I said on 10 March, although I find it awkward to be in a consensual role, I hope that we might persuade and encourage the Minister to reflect on the subject.

The new clause would first remove the requirement to set the eradication of fuel poverty as an objective. On 10 March, one or two of my colleagues grimaced at the expression ``fuel poverty'' and I remarked on it myself. Let us forget about the words: we are talking about people who cannot afford enough heating to ensure they are reasonably comfortable. I accept that the Government have a problem with definitions, but given the huge talents in the civil service it cannot be impossible to arrive at an acceptable definition. In fact, I felt following the meeting with Lord Whitty that we had arrived at an acceptable definition.

New clause 1 appears to restrict those who would be helped to lower-income households. If that restriction is accepted, there is a danger that only those on benefit will be included in the measures and other categories of people, who are also in need of assistance, will be excluded.

Mr. Simon Burns (West Chelmsford): I support the sittings motion, as we need time to discuss the issues. My hon. Friend has said that he does not want to exclude people who are not on benefit and that he wants the Bill to be inclusive. When then does the Bill not apply to Scotland?

Mr. Amess: My hon. Friend is tempting me into a debate on Scotland, but knowing the mind of our Chairman I will resist him. Members who have raised questions were not available to serve on the Commitee, and because I am concerned about the intent of the Government's new clauses, I believe that we should sit next Wednesday in the morning and the afternoon. Mr. Tom Brake (Carshalton and Wallington): It is a pleasure to serve under your chairmanship, Mr. Gale. I support the suggestion of the hon. Member for Southend, West (Mr. Amess) that we should sit next week. I, too, have some fundamental concerns about the Government's amendments. We shall need time to ensure that the pledges that have been made by a series of Ministers will be honoured and that the expectations of the hundreds of Members of Parliament who signed early-day motions in support of a 15-year programme to insulate millions of homes will be fulfilled. That will take some time. It is regrettable that when hon. Members become Ministers, they shed all their previous commitments and withdraw their names from all early-day motions. However, the record shows that the Under-Secretary put his name to several early-day motions supporting the principles outlined in the Bill.

The Prime Minister has pledged to eradicate fuel poverty, saying that it is a serious problem that affects millions of people. One of the key factors that we shall need to establish-if not today, next Wednesday and, if necessary, the following Wednesdays-is how many millions of people the Government believe are affected by the problem. Is it 4.3 million? Is it 5 million? Perhaps it is 8 million?

We need to know what definition of fuel poverty the Government intend to use. If amended, the Bill would cover only lower income groups. That sounds good in principle, because it would ensure that someone who owned a manor house with 25 bedrooms did not qualify. However, that definition could exclude a substantial number of people in fuel poverty who are just on the edge. Under those criteria, they will not receive benefits. They will miss out on the measures proposed in the Bill, and will remain in fuel poverty.

The definition of fuel poverty is important. Changing the criteria from the recognised definition of 10 per cent. of disposable income to 10 per cent. or 20 per cent. of gross income does not magically make things better for those who used to be covered by the 10 per cent. disposable income criteria. They will no longer be covered; they will continue to suffer fuel poverty. They will not be miraculously swept out of fuel poverty and be sitting in warm homes simply because the definition has been changed slightly.

We need to know also how long the programme will take. The hon. Members, who signed those early-day motions, many of whom are here today, signed up to a 15-year programme. Where is that programme? What is the final target date? It seems to have disappeared from the Bill under the Government's amendments. That is a source of enormous concern. We shall have to return to the matter, if not today then in the days and weeks to come.

Finally, we need reassurance that what the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst (Mr. Forth) said last night does not apply and that the Government are not seeking to make the provisions into a wishy-washy Bill. The Bill that became the Road Traffic Reduction Act 1997 springs to mind as an example of such legislation.

We should be concerned about the hard, concrete delivery-I shall not use that phrase again-of the Bill's aims. I am worried that the Bill will not ensure that fuel poverty is eradicated if new clause 1 is agreed to. I am also worried that within a fixed 15-year period, the millions of people whom the Prime Minister acknowledged to be seriously affected will not be sitting in homes that are properly insulated and sufficiently warm.

Mr. Alan Simpson (Nottingham, South): I, too, consider it a privilege to serve on the Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. Gale. In paying tribute to the people who have played a part in tackling fuel poverty, we should acknowledge the all-party genesis of the Bill, which stretches back a number of years. My first involvement in such a Bill was when Sir John Hannam tried to push a Bill on home energy conservation through the House. He put in many years of work before he managed to introduce a private Member's Bill.

An unsung hero in that process was the Minister, Robert Jones, who gave a phenomenal amount of open-handed help to identify the grounds on which it was possible to make progress. If anyone brought home to me the value of extra sittings of a Committee and the need to clarify a Minister's objectives, it was Robert Jones. He said that the objectives in a private Member's Bill are not to seek perfection, but to try and get right what we can. If we identify what can be done and do it well, it will prepare the ground for all that is to follow. Those who followed the Bill's precursors also benefited from that wisdom-Baroness Maddox, who took the matter on to the next stage, myself, the hon. Member for Plymouth, Sutton, and now the hon. Member for Southend, West. We are part of a process. It will not end here; it will move the policy on incrementally, which will eventually meet the objective of eliminating fuel poverty in the United Kingdom.

The most important reason for wanting additional sittings is to clarify which limited but important objectives are to be included in the Bill. The Government's amendments include a number of worthy terms that are dangerously unspecific. Unless we give ourselves time to reflect on those terms, we could do a disservice to the process, getting bogged down in a lack of clarity rather than addressing the key objectives. The Committee needs to consider several terms, on which I hope the Minister will reflect. For instance, the phrase ``reasonably practicable'' is difficult to define, and the term ``lower incomes'' is open to an enormous range of interpretations. It would be extremely helpful if the Bill gave some clarity to the terms used and to the objectives that the Bill attempts to meet. It would help also to re-impose a time frame on the Bill's objectives.

I was quite amused during the debate on the money resolution that took place in the House last night. The right hon. Members for Penrith and The Border (Mr. Maclean) and for Bromley and Chislehurst (Mr. Forth) rightly raised important questions about money issues. They asked how much money would be involved, how it would be raised and whether it would come from the public domain. It would be extremely helpful if we were able to discover the component parts of those costs. It is by no means certain that those costs would have to be paid entirely out of the public purse. In fact, it could probably be done at no cost to the public purse. It would have a cost to society, but those costs could be met in different ways. If we can legitimately talk about the various cost choices rather than how much it would cost, the Committee will have done Parliament a considerable service.

The challenge of addressing fuel poverty has two distinct aspects-those who live in fuel-poor households, and those who live in fuel-poor homes. We should recognise that it may not be possible to devise a strategy that will address both. One of the tragedies of life is that those who live in fuel-poor households are often moved around-or they move themselves around-from one fuel-poor home to another. The core of the problem is how we deal with housing conditions. A sobering reflection after the presentation of the Green Paper yesterday, is that this country is starting the century with a housing stock that does not meet the minimum standards that Scandinavia set itself in the middle of the previous century. I mention that to define-without blame-the scale of the problems that Parliament must tackle.

Member for Carshalton and Wallington said, there are other definitions.

 

Mr. Brake: The Minister says that there are differences of opinion about the numbers of such households. Why have the Government decided to use the figure of 4.3 million?

Mr. Mullin: The Government have taken no such decision. We recognise the importnce of the issues and that those who have taken a long-term interest in them, such as many members of the Committee, disagree about the figures. That is why we have set up the interministerial group on fuel poverty, which is chaired by my noble Friend Lord Whitty. To include such a definition in the Bill would bypass that group's work, without allowing the views expressed as part of the wide-ranging consultation to be considered properly. Therefore, I propose that the amendments should be resisted. They are not necessarily wrong or misplaced, but we simply do not want to prejudge the outcome of the wide-ranging consultations that the interministerial group is undertaking.

During the debate on the sittings motion, my hon. Friend the Member for Plymouth, Sutton asked me when that group would reach its conclusions. I understand that it hopes to publish its strategy this autumn.

Mr. Alan Simpson: I understand the difficulty that my hon. Friend the Minister is trying to address, but will be concede that the phrase ``at reasonable cost'' is not helpful? However, the test of reasonableness will not provide a benchmark. Will my hon. Friend consider how to redraft the Bill so that it will allow the Government to make improvements while providing a sensible starting point so that all authorities, which, in effect, will be given a mandate under the Bill, know what to do and how to quantify whether they have hit their targets?

Mr. Mullin: I acknowledge the point that my hon. Friend makes about the need for clarity and to provide a definition. I do not necessarily reject the definition that he suggests, but the interministerial group is working on that complex problem. The group will publish its strategy, or at least a draft of it, in the autumn, which will be the moment to reach some conclusions.
