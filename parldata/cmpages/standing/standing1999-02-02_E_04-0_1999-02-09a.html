Clause 3

Meaning of "position of trust"

10.30 am

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I beg to move amendment No. 15, in page 2, line 38, after `section 2', insert

    `and (offences aggravated by breach of trust)'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take new clause 6 Offences aggravated by breach of trust

    `. (1) An offence is aggravated by a breach of trust for the purposes of this section if

    (a) the person committing the offence is aged 18 or over and in a position of trust in relation to the victim of the offence; and

    (b) the victim of the offence is aged under 16.

    (2) A person who is guilty of an offence under section 6 of the Sexual Offences Act 1956 (unlawful sexual intercourse with a girl under the age of 16) which is aggravated by a breach of trust shall be liable

    (a) on summary conviction, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or to a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum or to both;

    (b) on conviction on indictment, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding fourteen years or to a fine or both.

    (3) A person who is guilty of an offence under section 14 (indecent assault on a man) or section 15 (indecent assault on a woman) of the Sexual Offences Act 1956 which is aggravated by a breach of trust shall be liable

    (a) on summary conviction, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or to a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum or to both;

    (b) on conviction on indictment, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding fourteen years or to a fine or to both.

    (4) A person who is guilty of an offence under section 1 of the Indecency with Children Act 1960 (indecent conduct towards young child) which is aggravated by a breach of trust shall be liable

    (a) on summary conviction, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding six months or to a fine not exceeding the statutory maximum or to both;

    (b) on conviction on indictment, to imprisonment for a term not exceeding fourteen years or to a fine or to both.'.

Mr. Clappison: We come now to measures, in addition to those already in the Bill, that would strengthen the protection afforded to young children. At present, the Bill is concerned with protecting 16 and 17-year-olds from those who breach a position of trust. We want that protection to be as strong as possible, and we have tabled constructive amendments with that in mind. The Bill, however, does not add to the legal protection that is already available for youngsters who are aged under 16. It does not give them specific protection from breaches of trust by older people. As we all know, young people in that age group can be all too vulnerable to such breaches of trust, often when they are away from home and looked after by older people in a variety of settings.

New clause 6 takes the concept of a position of trust, as it is established in the Bill, and applies it to the existing law to give greater protection to young people under 16. Under the new clause, when an older person in a position of trust commits an offence against a person under 16, that offence would be aggravated by the breach of trust provision and that older person would face a higher maximum sentence than would otherwise be the case.

I shall cite some examples. It is already an offence for an older person to have sexual intercourse with a girl aged under 16 years. As we know from last week's discussions, unlawful sexual intercourse by that I mean consensual sexual intercourse, not rape carries a maximum sentence of two years. Under the new clause, when the older person who had unlawful sexual intercourse was in a position of trust, that offence would carry a higher maximum sentence of seven years' imprisonment.

Furthermore, it is already an offence for an older person to carry out an indecent assault on a youngster under the age of 16 as it is for an older person to carry out an indecent assault on anyone else. That carries a maximum sentence of 10 years, and that offence is often committed by those with paedophiliac tendencies.

Under the new clause, in cases when the person who carried out an indecent assault is an older person and in a position of trust, and the victim is a youngster under the age of 16, there would be a maximum sentence of 14 years, which is higher than the existing maximum sentence of 10 years. The new clause sends a clear message that those who are in a position of trust and who carry out offences against youngsters under 16 would face a stiff sentence. Such a message needs to go out to people and be part of the legislative framework.

Members of the Committee will recall the concern that was expressed in June 1998 about vulnerable young people. Such a debate was engendered by the original amendment to lower the age of consent, and was taken up by the Government's working party. The background to that concern was a series of scandals that involved the abuse of children, which had taken place in various settings throughout the country, often in residential care of one form or another. Such incidents were well documented in the Utting report, which was commissioned by the previous Government and reported to the present Government. Hon. Members will recall that that report was referred to during our debates in June last year and subsequently.

The Utting report was the result of the continuing and widespread revelations of the sexual, physical and emotional abuse of children in a variety of settings during the preceding 20 years. The report said that a repetition of abuse on that scale, whether in children's homes or in a variety of residential settings, was less likely today because far fewer children now live in such places than did so in the 1960s and 1970s. The report also said, however, that here were no grounds for complacency. not least because of the appalling consequences for the victims of such abuse. The report said:

    "A single perpetrator is likely in a lifetime's career to abuse hundreds of children, who suffer pain, humiliation and torment, and incur permanent emotional damage" .

One of the most poignant and striking features of child abuse is that it does not come to light until many years later, no doubt because of the heartache and hurt that it causes over the years; but it is often said that those who are responsible for such sexual abuse were themselves victims of abuse. That process continues: we hear revelations from all over the country. The Minister will have seen that I have a pile of documents with me that set out and chronicle cases of abuse in a variety of settings, all of which involve people who are in charge of children. I do not propose to speak about them all, but it is important to note that, during the past month or so, since the debate was launched on how best to protect children, it has been evident that cases still occur that contain all the features that we have known of over the years.

I shall cite only one case, but it is typical of many. In December 1998 only a few weeks ago a man of 52 was sentenced at York Crown court for taking advantage of vulnerable youngsters for his own sexual gratification between 1967 and 1974. He was arrested in June 1998 the very month when the House was debating the subject after a former resident of the Spring Hill Barnardo's home in Ripon in North Yorkshire, which cared for children with behavioural problems and learning difficulties, made a complaint to the police. The man eventually admitted to 14 charges of indecent assault, three of serious sexual assault and two of cruelty to children relating to 12 boys and one girl aged between six and 15.

The prosecutor told the court that the abuse began almost immediately after the man joined the home in 1967. He sought to establish control of the children by violence or by emotional grooming. Prosecuting counsel said that the accused

    "sought to be able to engage these children is sexuality activity when he liked and without any repercussions for himself."

In mitigation, defence counsel said that his client had been the victim of sexual abuse as a child.

That recent case encapsulates all the features of the terrible events that can too frequently happen to children. Such events led to our debate in June a debate that continues. In that case, as in so many other cases, the victims were youngsters aged between six and 15. That is why we must take the opportunity to protect children under 16 years of age as well as those over 16.

As I said at the outset, the Bill does not offer any additional protection to children under 16 who are likely to be much more vulnerable, to be far more susceptible to the attentions of older people in positions of trust and to have far less ability to look after themselves than those over 16. Children under 16 need the most protection; if the concept of a position of trust can be imported into the law to protect over-16s, it should be possible to do so, through the new clause, to protect the under-16s.

I make no claims for the drafting of the new clause, but its aim of giving such protection to the under-16s is consistent and legitimate. Bearing in mind that we need to do all that we can to protect the under-16s, who have so often been the victims of sexual abuse., I look forward to hearing the Minister's reply.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Paul Boateng): The amendment is undoubtedly well intentioned. It is, however, misdirected and flawed. The Government want primarily to protect 16 and 17-year-olds who will be over the age of consent under this measure, but who require special protection because of the four principles that I have previously outlined: that the young person is especially vulnerable because of personal circumstances; that the location makes him or her especially vulnerable; that the special influence of the adult is such that the relationship is similar to in loco parentis and is brought to bear on the young person; or that there is a lack of access by that young person to other adults and therefore the absence of a countervailing influence. In such circumstances, 16 and 17-year-olds require special protection.

At the heart of the Bill is the fact that the sexual activity with which we are primarily dealing is that which takes place between the young person and a person in a position of trust on a consensual, albeit unequal and improper, basis. The provisions in the Bill are designed to counter that particular mischief. However, on any interpretation of the clause, the offence of abuse of trust is not limited to those of 16 or 17 years of age, so the point made by the hon. Member for Hertsmere is misdirected. It could be used against someone who abused a position of trust in respect of, for example, a 13-year-old. However, as the amendment implicitly recognises, the criminal law can already deal with such offences as unlawful sexual intercourse with a girl under 16, indecent assault and indecent conduct towards those under 14. Therefore, in such circumstances, the amendment, although obviously well intentioned, is simply not necessary. Even if the Crown Prosecution Service did not bring a prosecution under any of those offences, it could still bring a charge of abuse of trust against the person involved. However, I expect that that would not be the case, and that the Crown Prosecution Service would choose to bring a prosecution for the more serious offences that I outlined.

The amendment is unnecessary because, as the hon. Member for Hertsmere should be aware, the Court of Appeal has laid down on numerous occasions that it will take into account abuse of trust as an aggravating factor in sentencing. In the case of R v. Baker in 1989, Mr Justice Hutchison identified a breach of trust as an aggravating factor in a range of sexual offences, including indecency with a child and unlawful sexual intercourse with a girl under 16. In the case of R v. Roberts and Roberts in 1982, the then Lord Chief Justice, Lord Lane, identified abuse of trust as an aggravating factor in rape, as some hon. Members will be aware. As recently as 1997, in the case of R v. B, Mr. Justice Poole identified a gross breach of trust as an aggravating factor in a case of rape and indecent assault. In all such cases, an addition will be made to the sentence to reflect the extent of added injury that the court considers has occurred as a result of the abuse of trust.
