Clause 1

Withdrawing or withholding

of medical treatment

Mr. Joe Ashton (Bassetlaw): I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 5, after `person' insert

    `directly'.

This is a probing amendment that would insert ``directly'' after ``person'' in the clause, which reads

    ``It shall be unlawful for any person responsible for the care of a patient''.

The net is spread wide—everyone in a hospital could be said to have that responsibility. It would be nonsense to introduce a law that could prosecute 15 or 20 people involved in a patient's death in the way in which the Bill specifies.

The Tony Bland case has been referred to many times, and I would have liked to have talked at length about it on the Floor of the House. When 95 football fans died at the Hillsborough stadium on 15 April 1989, I was an eye-witness standing not much further from the incident than the distance between me and the end of the Room. I saw those unfortunate fans crushed to death, and saw the police standing still with rigid iron discipline. The person who should have instructed them to open the gates to let the youngsters run on to the pitch had frozen in shock or fear and did not give the order. The crushing took place for 20 or 25 minutes, and many of the fans were literally crushed to death.

Three or four fans survived and came round after a few weeks, but Tony Bland, who was 18, was starved of oxygen when he was crushed against the barriers for a considerable time. He was later diagnosed as being in a persistent vegetative state and cared for at the Airedale general hospital in Keighley, where he lived. After eight months, the neurologist, Mr. Jim Howe, was informed by the medical defence unit of the district coroner that disconnecting the feeding tube that kept Tony Bland alive could leave him open to a charge of murder or manslaughter. That test case is what the Bill is all about.

A plea was made to the Home Office by the Airedale national health service trust and the then Conservative Member of Parliament for Keighley, Gary Waller. I think that he was a supporter of the hon. Member for Congleton (Mrs. Winterton), but a lot of people were involved and it is difficult to remember. That plea did not result in a change in the law. Mr. Waller was a member of the organisation concerned with such matters, ALERT—Against Legalised Euthanasia, Research and Teaching. John Patten, then a Conservative Home Office Minister, said that

    ``there were no plans for a change in the law to allow artificial treatment to be withdrawn from Tony Bland''.

He referred to the Criminal Law Revision Committee, which covered the subject in 1980 and recommended that no changes be made.

Tony Bland's parents were in deep distress. His doctor decided to apply to the High Court. The case was backed on British Medical Association advice that artificial feeding was medical treatment and therefore could be withdrawn. Lawyers acting not for the parents but for the NHS trust submitted an affidavit to the family division of the High Court, which held a two-day hearing. Tony Bland's father gave evidence, and the Official Solicitor's counsel argued that it would constitute murder to cease treatment. The health authority was represented. The president of the family division, Sir Stephen Brown, later ruled that doctors may lawfully discontinue all life-sustaining treatment and medical support measures. The Official Solicitor announced that there would be an appeal in the House of Lords.

The appeal dragged on until 30 November 1992, when the judges were asked to give a definitive ruling. The Master of the Rolls and Lord Justice Butler-Sloss said that they were likely to dismiss the appeal, but they gave the Official Solicitor leave to appeal to the House of Lords. The position of Tony Bland was analysed by the most eminent legal brains in the country. The full Appeal Court judgment was made that the withdrawal of medical care, including the removal of artificial feeding procedures, was not unlawful when the patient was in a persistent vegetative state from which he would never recover and when it was known that, after such withdrawal, the patient would die.

However, in such cases application can be made to the court for the proposal to be sanctioned. In December 1992, the Law Lords reserved judgment until the new year. On 4 February 1993, the Law Lords ruled unanimously that artificial feeding should be withdrawn from Tony Bland and called on Parliament to legislate on when the doctors could stop such futile medical treatment. I presume that the Bill is a result of that terrible accident. The Law Lords expressed unease at having to rule in such a difficult area, without legislative backup for their decision. Lord Keith of Kinkel, Lord Goff of Chieveley, Lord Lowry, Lord Browne-Wilkinson and Lord Mustill stressed that there was a crucial difference between withdrawing treatment and actively tending life. The ruling was backed by the BMA.

The Bill will overturn the rulings of those Law Lords, the evidence that was submitted and the legal arguments that went on for three or four years. It is trying to say that a handful of Members of Parliament know better.

Mrs. Ann Winterton (Congleton): Is the hon. Gentleman aware that the BMA's advice was limited to a paper that was drawn up by a mere handful of doctors, without consultation with its membership? It had not been adopted as official BMA policy at the time.

Mr. Ashton: Nevertheless, the Law Lords took each of those points into consideration. I wish that we could have had this sort of debate on the Floor of the Houses, but many of us were not called.

Father Morrow was refused access to the Airedale hospital. Three weeks later, treatment was withdrawn from Tony Bland. On 3 March 1993 at 9.15 pm, he died aged 22, nine days after the treatment was withdrawn.

I shall refer later to evidence given by experts on comas, who say that no one has ever come out of a coma after 12 months. Yet that poor lad had been in a coma since April 1989. Some people in comas have stayed alive for as long as 30 years. The magistrates refused to allow him to do so. An inquest six months later ruled that Tony Bland died as a result of the crush at the Hillsborough football stadium, not as a result of the doctors' decisions. There was a further inquest. The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Yvette Cooper): Thank you, Mr. Gale, for that clarification. I had intended to speak in the stand part debate, but because of the wide-ranging discussion on the amendment, I will speak now.

Clause 1 goes to the heart of the Government's concerns about the Bill. I made it clear on Second Reading that our greatest concern was with the use of the words

    ``purpose or one of his purposes''

in relation to the doctor or the carer. For a start, the phrase switches the focus of the law from the patient's best interests to the purpose or one of the purposes of the doctor. Secondly, the hon. Member for Congleton said that she wanted to use the Bill to make a link with the law on murder, but the criminal laws on murder and manslaughter use the concept of primary intention, not the concept of purpose. As a result, it is unclear how the word ``purpose'' would be interpreted in practice. Interpretation would have to come from the courts, and there is much scope for wide interpretation. Someone's intention can include everything he or she knows about the likely consequences. I understand that the hon. Member for Congleton used the word ``purpose'' to avoid the problems with intention; however, the Bill does not get round those problems, because it talks about the purpose or one of the purposes.

Mr. Michael Trend (Windsor): We have had a broad debate and the explanatory memorandum is clear, so why does the Minister have a problem with the attempt to define ``purpose'' clearly? What my hon. Friend the Member for Congleton seeks to do and not to do seems quite straightforward to me. Why is the intention inadequately clear?

Yvette Cooper: The problem is that the Bill talks about

    ``his purpose or one of his purposes''.

There are practical difficulties in working out quite what someone's purpose might be. For example, a doctor's main purpose might be to ease a patient's pain, but it is open to argument that one of his purposes might be held to be to hasten death. That is why the Government fear that the clause will make it harder for doctors to respect patients' wishes to refuse treatment. Respecting patients' decisions might be the main purpose, but another purpose could be held to be to hasten death. Equally, the wording may make it difficult for doctors to respect the legally binding status of valid advance refusals of treatment. Patients might have to be given burdensome treatment that was not in their best interests if the withdrawal of treatment might also hasten death.

The issue that may raise most concern in the House is that the clause could damage effective palliative care, by affecting the provision of proper pain relief. Although the main purpose could be to provide effective palliative care, another purpose, assuming that the doctor knew the consequences of providing that form of palliative care, could be interpreted to be the hastening of death.

In common sense, I intend what I choose either as an end or a means, but not what is neither an end or a means, even if it is certain. In common sense, the terms related to ``intention'' are the equivalents of words and phrases such as ``purpose'' and ``with the purpose of'', ``aim'' and ``with the aim of'', ``for the sake of'', ``in order to'', ``with a view to'' or just plain ``to''—as in the phrase ``to impress my friends''.
