Clause 6

The New Opportunities Fund

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Richard Spring (West Suffolk): First, may I welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Gale? I understand your injunction, but we have had representations and further concerns have been expressed about the issues. The twin elements of the clause--the additionality and the arm's-length principle--are at the heart of our objections.

Under clause 6 and schedule 2, the New Opportunities Fund will be established as a body to distribute lottery funds for initiatives specified by the Secretary of State in connection with the new good cause. The Secretary of State will be given an order-making power to specify such initiatives and to give directions to the New Opportunities Fund. The notes on clause 6, helpfully provided by the Government, state that

    ``rather than providing general support to its sectors as the other distributors do, it will support a rolling programme of specific initiatives. These will be determined by the Secretary of State by order following consultation and directions on the approximate expenditure on each initiative, its duration and targets will be made through directions.''

The new good cause is therefore qualitatively and substantively different from the existing good causes.

I remind the Committee that proposed new section 43B refers to grants

    ``which are designed to give effect to such initiatives concerned or connected with health, education or the environment as may from time to time be specified in an order made by the Secretary of State.''

That is confirmed in proposed new section 43C, which states:

    ``In exercising their functions under section 43B the New Opportunities Fund shall comply with any directions given to them by the Secretary of State.''

That is the theme at the heart of the debate.

The additionality principle was accepted by Lord McIntosh of Haringey during the Bill's Committee stage in another place, when he said:

    ``I think the Committee would agree from the force with which the arguments have been made that to many noble Lords, this is the most important single issue in the Bill.--[Official Report, House of Lords, 19 January 1998; Vol. 584, c. CWH41.]

We fully accept that and I am grateful to you, Mr. Gale, for allowing us to air our concerns again.

I thank, in his absence, the hon. Member for Southport (Mr. Fearn), because he supported our amendments. I see from the speeches made by Liberal Democrat peers in another place that Lords Falkland and Redesdale, in particular, supported us comprehensively. On Second Reading, Lord Redesdale said:

    ``The problem is that the Bill goes a long way towards destroying the principle of additionality.''--[Official Report, House of Lords, 18 December 1997; Vol. 584, c. 761.]

That is at the heart of the issue.

The Prime Minister defined additionality in the White Paper, ``The People's Lottery'' when he said:

    ``We don't believe it would be right to use Lottery money to pay for things which are the Government's responsibilities''.

However, the Bill contradicts that assertion.

The public's continuing confidence in the national lottery and in the Government's good faith is at the heart of the issue. The Bill will enable the Government to make the lottery a different animal from that which it was originally intended to be.

In a BBC interview, the hon. Member for Hove (Mr. Caplin) rather gave the game away. He said:

    ``I think what we said in Opposition was clear, and it was right at the time that the lottery was introduced. But what we have now is a situation where the Budget spending level is set, and we have said that we are going to stick to that level for two years. I think it's right that a Government can say: We have a different view of how the lottery should operate.''

That is precisely the point. The Bill will violate the very principles on which the lottery was first established. The public will regard as wholly unacceptable the Labour party's admission that election manifesto commitments can be broken when a party comes to power.

Mr. Spring: The principles that underlie our objections to the Bill and to this particular clause are very clear. Public services should not depend on contributions that are variable and voluntary. The national lottery does not exist to finance that which is merely desirable and it should not be regarded as a hidden, cost-free, painless form of tax.

The Secretary of State has said that core funding by central Government will continue, citing the examples of school buildings and national health service beds. Once he has secured his additional powers, will he go down the route advocated by The Sun--that great supporter of the Labour Government--and use the lottery to pay for NHS beds or school textbooks? The Bill will empower the Secretary of State completely to replace core funding with money from the national lottery.

The Prime Minister, speaking about information technology, said that every educational institution should be involved in the information super-highway. I understand that the national lottery is to contribute some £220 million to child care facilities and yet the Chancellor of the Exchequer has already announced the allocation of £80 million for that purpose. Friend the Member for North Norfolk (Mr. Prior). Unless people understand the distinction upon which additionality is based, they will lose confidence in the national lottery and the good causes may begin to suffer. Those good causes were originally chosen for the benefit of traditionally under-resourced areas such as the arts, heritage and sport. Once the Secretary of State has acquired his additional powers, the temptation may be to extend the principle of additionality into the grey areas that I have described and thus to endanger the very foundations of the national lottery. That is why clause 6 is critically important.

As my right hon. Friend the former Prime Minister has said, Chancellors avoid giving financial support to some areas of national life for political reasons. The national lottery was designed to alleviate that kind of underfunding. It was intended to build up the country's sporting activities, artistic endeavour and heritage for future generations.
