The point is a contradiction--or at least a tension--within Government thinking. Clause 1 itself does not contain that contradiction. Rather, the whole Bill is an expression of it, and most clearly so in clause 1. The clause extends the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 by two years and is the first clause of the sixth successive Bill in a series of laws applicable to Northern Ireland. Along with successive UK-wide prevention of terrorism Acts they have supplemented the ordinary criminal law to give the police and armed forces additional powers where necessary to deal with terrorism.

The Government, like their Conservative and Labour predecessors, repeadly state that these powers remain only for so long as is necessary. However, whereas Minsiters of previous Governments have stressed that genuine peace would result in suspension and eventual repeal of the emergency provisions, that is not entirely the case with the present Government. The Home Secretary announced on 30 October that the Government will publish a consultation document in the new year to set out options for the replacement of the PTA and the EPA with a single piece of counter-terrorism legislation. The Government have not yet said when that legislation will be introduced, but the consultation exercise has been presented at the first, important, step.

The pertinent point is that neither on Second Reading nor in any press release or statement have the Government adequately addressed the tension or contradiction between the temporary extension of the 1996 Act and the Government's declared intention of reviewing and replacing the PTA and EPA.

It may be argued that the Committee should be considering not the temporary extension of the 1996 Act but the new legislation that the Government have promised. I can see three reasons why that is not the case and why the Bill is before us, but I do not think that those reasons stand up to investigation. First, if the Government were to argue that they have not had time to consult on or prepare a replacement for emergency provisions, and that they therefore have no alternative but to extend the 1996 Act, they would face a credibility problem. It is not as if the Government have decided to move into new and uncharted waters. In a democratic state with an independent judiciary, the potential scope for anti-terrorism laws is limited and well defined. The Government did not have to undergo a vast learning experience on coming to power; they were very familiar indeed with the range of powers from which they could select, or that they could adopt.

Moreover, well before 1 May, Labour Front Benchers--then in opposition--had already committed themselves to some principles and positions. With the extension of the life of the 1996 Act in clause 1 the Government have failed to deliver the goods that they had led us to expect, and they have failed adequately to explain why.

If the Government had been so minded, it would have been possible to launch the consultation process in May or June. There was no valid reason why it was postponed until January. The second reason that the Government have attempted to present for their extension of the life of the 1996 Act is that if they did not do so, there would or could be a vacuum in June 1998, when the temporary provisions lapse, and the problem would be compounded in August of next year when the Act itself expires.

Clause 1 is not imperative, as the Government would like us to think. Not only did the timetable allow the Government to introduce and enact definitive legislation before August 1998, but the temporary provisions can continue in force for the duration of the Act through a further continuance order, effective from June next year. On the Government's own premise of the desirability of that definitive legislation yet to come, it is risible that the Government did not pursue that option with due speed.

Thirdly, there is an inherent fault line in the Government's wish in clause 1 to extend an amended 1996 Act. That alone provides good reason to object to clause 1. Each supplementary or emergency power that the Government--any Government--want to maintain or introduce must be valid and justified, and defensible in its own right. Each one of those supplementary or emergency powers, however, forms part of a total package of such powers. The total package itself must also be valid and justified; it must provide the police and the armed forces with a combination or range of powers that together equip them to deal with the threat of terrorism.

The difficulty that the Committee now faces is that we do not know of what the final package that the Government have in mind will consist. In extending the life of an amended 1996 Act, the Government are seeking approval of three key changes. Ultimately, however, we can pass only qualified judgment because we do not know the whole picture of what the Government intend to introduce.
