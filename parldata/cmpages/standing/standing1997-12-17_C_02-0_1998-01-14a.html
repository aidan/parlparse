I have been highly critical of the aim behind the amendment. The Bill is about banning hunting with dogs and that is what the House voted for on 28 November. Licensing or regulation would enshrine in law something that runs counter to the will of the House and the British people. I am sceptical about any regulation or licensing. The Masters' of Foxhounds Association has a code of conduct which, in its opinion, is a form of self-regulation. I have put that to the test. On Second Reading, on 28 November, I mentioned an incident that took place at Wharncliffe primary school in Sheffield. A pack of hounds who were following a fox jumped over a 4-ft fence and went through the playground. Mr. David Rogers, the headmaster of the school, said:

    ``had the hounds jumped the fence minutes earlier a group of up to 33 children under five would have been in their path.''

I wrote to the Masters' of Foxhounds Association to see what it would do about the incident. It kindly replied that a committee of inquiry had considered the issue and had found that the masters were not in breach of rule 1. Rule 1 says that one must make sufficient effort to prevent hounds from hunting a fox into a built-up area, so the masters' conduct was fine. However, they were found to be in breach of another rule for not reporting the incident without delay. In the minds of those who practise self-regulation, it is far more important to report an incident promptly than to stop the incident occurring in the first place. That confines self-regulation to the dustbin.

At our previous sitting, the hon. Member for Montgomeryshire (Mr. Öpik) spoke of the need to license terrier work because of its important role in fox control, especially in his constituency. Most people with some knowledge of fox hunting would accept that terrier work is one of the cruellest aspects of the sport. Sending a dog into a confined space underground, without having any control over what occurs because of the nature of the underground terrain, is abhorrent. If one reads articles in magazines, such as Earth Dog Running Dog, written by people who enjoy terrier work, it is clear that they get pleasure from setting dogs underground to bait foxes. In some cases, dogs have been set underground to bolt out foxes in order to pursue a chase further. We cannot licence that or allow that to be enshrined in law.

The suggestion that only soft terriers, as opposed to hard terriers, should be allowed underground or that a limit should be imposed on the amount of time that dogs may spend underground is totally impractical, yet those are the sorts of issues that the Secretary of State would have to decide if he had to licence and regulate the activity. I do not believe that can be done. To licence such activity would be an obscene act by the Committee and the House.

Animal welfare is a central plank of the legislation. The amendment is concerned with defining what species of wild mammal it is acceptable to hunt, but the amendment begs the question of who will make that decision. The hon. Member for Denton and Reddish (Mr. Bennett), who tabled the amendment, said that stag hunting was unacceptable, yet a Secretary of State could allow stag hunting with dogs to continue. Hare coursing is similarly cruel. Mink hunting is inefficient and totally disrupts the countryside. That only leaves the regulation of fox hunting. I believe that the amendment panders to the fox hunting fraternity, who claim that they can conduct their activities legally, lawfully and humanely.

I do not believe that a long chase, in which a fox is chased to the point of exhaustion because of its shorter sprinting ability compared with the staying power of a hound, should be regulated because it is inhumane. That is one of the reasons why I urge the Committee to reject the amendment.

Then there is the kill itself, which I described on Second Reading. Evidence shows that the kill is quite horrific, and I cannot believe that the Committee could contemplate accepting the amendment and allowing that practice to continue as we approach the 21st century. The amendment would regulate where hunting could take place. If the Committee and the House decide that hunting should be stopped as a matter of principle because it is cruel and inhumane, how on earth could we argue that it is okay to hunt in some parts of the country but not in others? As for the earlier point about allowing fell packs in the Lake district to hunt, if it is cruel for hunting to take place in Worcestershire, surely it is cruel for it to take place in the Lake district.

My hon. Friend the Member for Denton and Reddish mentioned the effectiveness of fell packs in the Lake district. Fell packs are not a particularly effective way in which to control fox numbers. There are far more humane and efficient ways in which to control them. Some evidence suggests that artificial earths have been created in those locations, thus encouraging foxes to breed for a day's sport and pleasure. I do not think that the Committee could encourage such activities.

As for the times when it is okay to hunt and not okay to hunt, the Wildlife Network's submission to the debate proposes that cub hunting--autumn hunting as it is so quaintly known--should be dispensed with in favour of a shorter hunting season. If we were considering fox hunting simply as a way in which to control the fox population, which I am told is the purpose of fox hunting, I would suggest that killing a vixen when it is pregant is an effective way in which to kill foxes and control their numbers. Killing cubs when they are young and have not gained the skills to evade capture is also an effective way in which to reduce fox numbers. I do not believe for one moment that fox hunting is about controlling fox numbers. I think that it is about sport--pleasure, pure and simple.

The cynic in me suggests that a shorter season has been proposed because the thought of chasing a pregnant vixen is not especially nice. Perhaps pregnant vixens do not run as fast or for as long as other foxes and the hunters do not get the same day's entertainment and pleasure. Why have a close season to control fox numbers? Shooting would not have a close season. If a fox is a pest or a nuisance, someone could be called out to get it shot quickly, humanely and without fear of breaching a close season agreement. That is why I am against the amendment and urge the Committee to vote against it.
