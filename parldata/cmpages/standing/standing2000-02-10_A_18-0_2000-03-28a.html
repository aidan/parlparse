Mr. Gibb: Clause 47, for electricity, and clause 75, for the gas industry, relate to the financial penalties that the authority can impose on licence holders for failing to deliver in accordance with licence conditions or the standards of service under the clauses that we discussed at our previous sitting. They are the most controversial clauses in the Bill. The Electricity Association, the Confederation of British Industry and others are greatly concerned about the absence of a cap on the fines that can be imposed by the regulatory authority. Concern is felt also about the circumstances in which a fine can be imposed and the appeals procedure.

The first set of amendments relates to the limitless nature of the fine and the circumstances in which it can be imposed. Amendments Nos. 318 and 319 cover the electricity industry, while amendments Nos. 324 and 325 relate to the gas industry. The four amendments would remove an unfairness from clauses 47 and 75. At present, when or if a licence holder contravenes a licence condition, the regulator informs the licence holder of the breach through a provisional order, which states what is wrong and how to put it right. Such communication takes place solely between the regulator and the licence holder. The objective is for the utility to rectify the error. If the licence holder fails to do so, the regulator issues a further order requiring the utility to remedy the situation. Such an approach is sensible because many of the issues are subjective and often depend on the interpretation of a licence condition by the regulator. It makes sense to give the company a chance to put the matter right before coming down on it like a tonne of bricks with a range of punishments.

Under the arrangements in clauses 47 and 75, a fine can be imposed if the regulator is satisfied that a licence holder

    has contravened or is contravening

a licence condition or

    has failed or is failing

to reach a certain standard. The key points are ``has contravened'' and ``has failed'', meaning that something has happened previously. Fines can be imposed for past transgressions without giving the utilities the chance to remedy matters. Given the subjective nature of the standards of service requirements and some licence conditions, not only is the provision unfair, it will have the damaging consequence of making the utility companies extremely cautious when introducing innovations to their services or operations in case, by mistake, they are not to the regulator's liking and the companies therefore run the risk of attracting a limitless fine.

As a result, British electricity and gas suppliers and distributors will become less innovative and responsive to consumer demands. The provision will, in effect, make them seek advance clearance from the regulator about new management procedures, new efficiency measures, new products or services. The industry will not be able to risk a fine that could wipe out a company. It will not be able to turn forward, sideways or backwards without keeping an eye on the regulator and clearing its reforms in advance.

As well as slowing down innovation, the provision will add to costs, which will be passed to the consumer. The regulator will be forced into the invidious position of second-guessing management decisions—a role for which the regulator is totally unsuitable. Such an impediment to swift change will harm the British industry as gas and electricity companies worldwide begin to consolidate. The Government's actions are again damaging British industry to the benefit of overseas conglomerates.

The amendments would remedy the problem. The provision would read:

    Where Authority is satisfied that a licence holder—

    (a) has contravened or is contravening and is likely again to contravene any relevant condition or requirement.

If the regulator has informed the licence holder of the problem and the licence holder is clearly taking appropriate action to deal with it, that should be the end of the matter. The amendment would clarify the position; if appropriate action has clearly been taken, it is unlikely that such requirements will be contravened again. That makes perfect sense.

The rules in the clause ought to ensure compliance with licence conditions and the standards of service provisions. However, if the provisions result in the fining of a company for a breach that will not or cannot be repeated, their purpose must be to punish. That will discourage innovation.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): Is the hon. Gentleman saying—I assume that he is not—that a company that has operated a scam for a year or two should not be punished for making disproportionate profits from it, against the interests of ordinary consumers?

Mr. Gibb: The Minister makes the point himself; those directors were banned under current legislation. The Bill introduces a regime that assumes that business is up to no good, when the industry is doing its best to perform well. That will allow them to remedy the problem. To bash them first and ask questions later is draconian, and not how a responsible Government should behave.

Companies must be told that they are offending against standards of service contracts or breaching a tightly drafted condition of licence agreements, and they must be given a chance to remedy that. Often, a breach is caused not by the deliberate and malicious behaviour of companies out for a quick profit, but by misinterpretation of subjectively phrased requirements. Companies simply need to be told to change their behaviour, and they will do so. If their behaviour does not change after that, heavy fines could be imposed. The amendments would not remove the fining provisions; they would merely make it clear that there should be no need to fine companies if the activities are not repeated.

 

Mrs. Gilroy: My right hon. Friend the Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe mentioned doorstep mis-selling. Gentleman says, why are problems still reported every day to the gas consumers council?

Mr. Gibb: I am sure that there are problems, because we are dealing with human nature. How should we respond to bad behaviour? Should everybody be fined, regardless of whether they breached a licence condition deliberately? Should people who deliberately breach a licence condition be treated the same as those who do so inadvertently because they misinterpret the subjective wording of the requirement? The Bill does not distinguish between the two. The amendments, including some that we will discuss later, would allow us to distinguish between deliberate or negligent behaviour and inadvertent behaviour that was due to a wrong or new interpretation of a condition imposed by the regulator. It is important to make that distinction.

The Opposition are not saying that companies that commit on-going breaches of conditions should not be fined when there is clear evidence that they should be. However, the amendments would ensure that we did not fine companies that inadvertently breached a condition, put the matter right as soon as it was brought to their attention and were unlikely to repeat the breach.

The other serious consequence relates to third parties. Under the current regime, the provisional order is purely a matter for the regulator and the utility company. Provided that the breach is put right, the company will not incur third party liabilities. The fact that a fine may be imposed will be sufficient to result in third party action—a further significant disincentive to innovation. That covers the reasoning behind amendments Nos. 318, 319, 322 and 324.

Amendments Nos. 320 and 326 deal with what amounts to strict liability. Under the clause, it will not matter whether a company intended to breach the conditions, as I said in response to the hon. Member for Plymouth, Sutton (Mrs. Gilroy). The fact that a breach has occurred—intentionally or not—will be sufficient to trigger financial penalties. That notion of no-fault liability is regarded as a strict liability. The CBI says in its briefing to the Committee:

    This appears to impose de facto strict liability, as there is no need for the authority to prove fault, just that a breach occurred.

The Electricity Association shares that concern about strict liability. It says:

    The electricity industry would wish to see a specific requirement for the Authority to take account of the intent of the licensee in cases where contraventions or failures have been made. It is the view of the industry that penalties should only be imposed in cases where the contravention or failure is found to be intentional or the company is found to be acting negligently.

The amendments would remedy the problem by requiring, before a fine could be imposed, that the contravention or failure had been, or was being, committed intentionally or negligently.

The amendments would make the Bill consistent with other legislation, in particular the Competition Act 1998. Section 36(3) of that Act, which was passed by this Government, says that the Director General of Fair Trading must be

    satisfied that an infringement has been committed intentionally or negligently.

I cannot understand why that Act states that there must be an intention to breach competition rules, whereas, under the Bill, it is simply a matter of whether the rules are breached. It does not matter whether the breach is permanent, intentional, negligent or otherwise—the company will be fined. Perhaps the Minister will respond to that point. It seems inconsistent, as regards strict liability, to have an unfair, draconian regime under the Bill, but a much fairer regime under the Competition Act.

The Electricity Association is also concerned about the issue of triple jeopardy.

    Section 39 and the proposed section 39A already allow for the payment of compensation to affected parties in cases where the company has failed to meet the prescribed standard. Double jeopardy arises by virtue of sections 39 and 39A stating that compensation payment is without prejudice to any other available remedy in respect of the failure in question. The power of the Authority to take further enforcement action through the imposition of a financial penalty for the same event amounts to triple jeopardy.

The concern is that the Bill will result in not double but triple jeopardy for one offence. The Minister must respond to the fact that companies will be liable for three layers of fines and compensation.

Amendments Nos. 321 and 327 would deal with the absence of a cap on the fine that the regulator can impose, which is probably the industry's biggest concern about the Bill. A theoretically limitless fine could wipe out a company. It would increase the electricity and gas sectors' regulatory risks, which would increase the return demanded by investors when lending to or investing in utilities companies. Given the capital-intensive nature of those industries, that higher interest rate would translate into significantly higher interest charges and costs, which would have to be passed to the consumer in some way. The CBI stated:

    over stringent methods may increase regulatory risk, costing industry more in borrowing, and ultimately increasing the burden on the end consumer.

Throughout industry, the provision is seen as the most damaging aspect of the Bill.

The Electricity Association expressed its concern in a briefing to the Committee. It said that

    The Bill does not set an upper limit for the financial penalty. It is the policy intention that any penalty imposed will be reasonable and proportionate to the contravention or failure. However, in the absence of firm legal provision the Authority has considerable discretion, the very element that the Bill aims to eradicate. The electricity industry finds the Bill to be lacking in this respect and would wish to see a cap on the penalty the Authority is able to impose included in the Bill.

PowerGen too is concerned about the lack of a limit. It has said that

    Companies are already subject to a variety of financial sanctions under the Competition Act 2000, under price controls imposed by the regulator, and the compensation requirements to customers where services standards are not met . . . companies should not be exposed to the multiple risk of incurring financial penalties from a number of different sources, each determined in isolation. The level of any fine should take into account other financial sanctions on companies and the Bill should impose an upper limit on the level of fine that can be imposed.

Utilities companies are especially alarmed by the precedent in the Competition Act. The maximum fine that it set was 10 per cent. of the annual turnover—significant in itself—but that has become 30 per cent. due to a statutory instrument to define the meaning of turnover. That was not mentioned during the passage of the Competition Act, and has enabled the Director General of Fair Trading effectively to fine three years' worth of annual turnover. Ten per cent. has been turned into 30 per cent., so the precedent has been set for large fines.

The Bill does not even set the same limits as the Competition Act, which raises the spectre of even higher fines. I recall the Secretary of State for Trade and Industry intimating six-figure fines, but I suspect that they could go beyond that. If the Government do not accept the amendments, the Bill will change the character of the industry. The fining provisions and the burdensome requirements elsewhere in it will foster an atmosphere of state control and intervention. The ever-present threat of unlimited and possibly catastrophic fines would give the regulator unprecedented power and leave the private sector in a new and dangerous quasi-state-controlled environment. That would damage the industry. I see that the Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs does not agree. It is clear that there is a total lack of understanding of the effect that the measure will have on the industry. One cannot set down in legislation a theoretically unlimited fine because of the effect that that would have on risk.

The Minister knows that all other financial penalties set out in criminal or civil law are limited. The fine that we are dealing with is unlimited; but according to the precedents, 10 per cent., or 30 per cent., of turnover could be set as a minimum fine for the sector. I hope that the Ministers will take those points seriously and will respond to them thoroughly.

Amendments Nos. 322 and 328 ensure that the regulator has regard to his own policy statement on penalties before proceeding and that his intention in imposing a fine is to deter future, rather than punish past, breaches. If it is clear to the regulator that the breach was unintentional and that no future breaches are likely because the matter has been put right, that fact should be taken into account in assessing the level of a fine. The points that I have made need a full response from the Minister because the clause elicits the most serious concerns from industry.

Mrs. Liddell: I start by apologising for the quality of my voice, particularly to the Hansard staff, who might find it even more difficult than usual to make me out.

Like the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb), I shall respond to the amendments in groups because they cover a wide range of areas. To make it easier, when looking at the circumstances in which a financial penalty can be imposed—for contraventions of licence conditions and other specified statutory requirements and standards of performance—I shall use the shorthand of ``obligations on the company''.

The people who need to worry about the clauses are those who are not honouring their obligations; there are many who do honour their obligations, and they have nothing to worry about. In fact, they have a great deal to gain because companies that fail to honour their obligations are undercutting those that operate fairly and appropriately in the marketplace. The amendments to which I shall come shortly deal more with the level of the penalty.

Before addressing the first group of amendments, we need to look at the overall context for the proposed financial penalties. We seek to ensure that companies that meet all their obligations punctiliously do not operate in an environment in which others that are more lackadaisical about their obligations can steal a march on them. In our debate in Committee and on Second Reading, we have often heard about the danger of increasing regulatory risk. The clause seeks to ensure that companies that honour their obligations enjoy a much more transparent system of regulation. Indeed, those who seek to justify companies that do not honour their obligations thereby deprive good and honourable companies of the credit that they deserve.

 

Mr. Gibb: Does the right hon. Lady accept that, although obligations might subsequently be narrowed down, the drafting of the description of some of them is subjective? For example, under the social action plan, utility companies have an obligation to improve people's health. How far does it go? Later clauses in that plan may have narrowed it down, but the phrasing is subjective. However sincerely and earnestly companies wish to fulfil their obligations, they may not do so—inadvertently—because they fail to interpret the widely drafted phrases in the same way as the regulator. They are not at fault; it is just a different interpretation. Because of that, such fines for an innocent misinterpretation of a widely drafted obligation seem draconian.

Mrs. Liddell: Any fine must be reasonable and should meet the criteria of reasonableness. If a company believes that the regulatory authority has not been reasonable, it can seek redress. That is its protection. Indeed, the regulator has to make a policy statement about the nature of any penalty that he seeks to enforce, and that is a protection.

I turn first to amendments Nos. 318, 319, 324 and 325. The Government propose that the authority should have a power to impose a financial penalty in relation to a current or a past—that is, a completed—contravention. The amendments would reduce significantly the impact of the Bill. They would restrict the imposition of financial penalties to current and likely future breaches. A penalty could be imposed in relation to a future breach only if such a breach had occurred once and was likely to be repeated. The amendments go to the heart of why the powers are necessary.

It might help the Committee—the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton wanted a full response—if I outline how the existing enforcement system operates. At present, the regulator has a duty to ``make orders'' to secure compliance with licence conditions and specified statutory obligations. The regulator must issue an order if it appears that a supplier is contravening, or is likely to contravene, a licence condition or a specified statutory requirement. In certain circumstances, the regulator is not permitted to take action, such as when his general duties preclude him from doing so, and he is satisfied that the Competition Act 1998 is the most appropriate way of proceeding. In the case of gas, the regulator can impose a financial penalty as part of an enforcement order. Again, that relates only to current and future breaches, not to past breaches.

The crucial weakness of the enforcement regime is that it applies only to current and future breaches of obligations, so a company that breaches its obligations, but ceases to do so when challenged by the regulator, cannot face further action regardless of the duration of the contravention, of the harm that may have been done to the interests of consumers, or indeed of the damage that that may have done to its competitors. If that company contravenes the same licence condition a fortnight later, the regulator will be compelled to start enforcement action again. Each time a company is challenged, it has only to desist from the act or omission to avoid the consequences of its non-compliance.

Mr. Gibb: Has not the right hon. Lady just given an example of a company that is likely to continue to breach the conditions? She gave the example of its intending to do so in two weeks' time. That would be precisely covered by the amendments because there is a likelihood of the breach continuing.

Mrs. Liddell: The hon. Gentleman is seeking to enshrine in the Bill an existing weakness, which is that the regulator is always one step behind the company. There is little to prevent a company from repeatedly breaching its obligations. As long as it ceases to contravene those obligations when challenged, the regulator cannot take further action, and is left chasing shadows. That is a serious flaw in the current enforcement system. The inadequacy of the current arrangements in the energy sector was highlighted during the roll-out of gas competition, when, as my hon. Friend the Member for Plymouth, Sutton so ably pointed out, the gas regulator could not deal with companies that had been guilty of unacceptable doorstep selling tactics. The defects in the current system illustrate why we need to introduce new powers to impose penalties for past as well as current breaches of obligation. The new powers will ensure that the authority can ensure compliance and deter future breaches rather than constantly being left running one step behind.

Of course, the powers must be properly circumscribed and couched in due process, and, to answer the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton, I shall explain what those limitations and due process requirements will be. The power will apply only to contraventions committed after it comes into effect. The Bill is not retrospective. Past contraventions are breaches of obligations that begin and end after the powers take effect and that are subsequently brought to the authority's attention, not contraventions that are taking place now.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton expressed anxiety about the lack of a specific monetary figure. However, the Gas Act 1986, which the Administration whom he supported were responsible for introducing, also provided for unlimited penalties. Although the provisions do not specify a figure, we must damp down some of the wilder speculation about likely financial penalties. The matter hinges on the use of the word ``reasonable''. The penalty must be reasonable in the circumstances of the case. The penalty might be very large, but only if the breach in question was extremely serious and had done enough harm to warrant it. When the breach is less serious, the amount involved will be smaller. The regulatory authority must answer for its decision about the level of the penalty.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton mentioned the read-across to the Competition Act 1998. I emphasise the reasonableness tied into the Bill. We expect the regulatory authority to take into account a penalty imposed under the Competition Act for an act that contravenes a licence condition and infringes the Competition Act prohibitions. However, there are not many circumstances in which a contravention would have an impact on competition.

Transparency is critical. The provision is a prescribing element on the authority. The authority is required to publish its policies for calculating penalties and to consider those policies when imposing penalties. The authority will determine the precise content of the policy statement, but we envisage that the policy might include considering the need to secure compliance, the consequences of the breach on individual consumers, or a group of consumers, and the need to deter future breaches so that we do not return to circumstances in which companies start the action again.

The authority will have to consult on its policy statement, which will always be in the public domain, and revisions of the policy statement will require further consultation. The policy statement itself and the consultation that will surround it will provide an important element of certainty for companies and a means whereby the authority's activities are made transparent and are constrained by the reasonableness provided for under the clause.

Mr. Gibb: The right hon. Lady says that the authority will decide the maximum fine. Does she not believe that Parliament should decide a matter of such importance as the level of fining, and especially the maximum? I can understand why the Government would want the decision about the details of the precise fine in a particular set of circumstances to be left to the regulatory authority, but the maximum fine should be decided by Parliament. What does she believe should be the level of fine? Should it be the same as that under the Competition Act or should it be more or less than that?

Mrs. Liddell: The hon. Gentleman is a little confused. Not 10 minutes ago he was talking about state control of energy companies, yet he is now referring to Parliament intervening on a regulatory authority that we are establishing deliberately to be at arm's length from the parliamentary process. I take him back to reasonableness: the level of the fine must be reasonable. If a significant and repeated breach has taken place, a significant fine should be imposed—as the Conservative Government recognised when they introduced the Gas Acts. The authority will be under a responsibility to be transparent, but also to take into account the damage that has taken place.

Such detailed procedural requirements mean that the authority must publicise its intentions, give notice with prescribed information and receive and consider comments from interested parties. There will be procedures for modifying the penalty and for notifying the company concerned and interested parties of the final decision on the imposition of a penalty. If the company wants to do so, it can pay its penalty in instalments—as some of us pay for our television sets. Everything is being done to ensure transparency and a sense of responsibility on the part of the regulator.

The company will continue to have a right to challenge in the court the imposition and the amount of the penalty as well as the paying schedule imposed by the authority. During the consultation process, the requirement to pay a penalty will be suspended until the case is determined. The court may then quash or reduce the penalty, or extend the time scale for payment.

I have given a fairly lengthy explanation of why the powers are necessary and how they will operate. The amendments go to the heart of the matter, but not in a helpful way. I hope that the Committee recognises the proper constraints and limitations on the operation of the powers. We must not let the complaints of a few blind us to the fairness of such a measure. Such powers are genuinely needed to protect the interests of consumers and those companies that strive to fulfil their obligations and do not take short cuts for their own advantage.

 

Mr. Norman Baker (Lewes): If a penalty was levied and subsequently appealed in court and then suspended pending the court's decision, that process could take a considerable time, given the slowness of our judiciary system. If the penalty were upheld by the court, would interest be payable?

Mrs. Liddell: I had not reflected on that interesting point, but I understand that interest would be payable. The hon. Gentleman made an important point, because we all know that it is possible, not for malicious reasons, for such actions to take a considerable time.

Regulated companies that abide by their licence conditions and fulfil the obligations that they have accepted have nothing to fear, but it is not good enough for the authority to be left permanently running behind companies that have much to gain from such contraventions and repeatedly undercut not only consumers but competitors.

Amendments Nos. 320 and 326 would restrict the imposition of financial penalties for breaches of licence conditions, specified obligations and standards of performance in cases of negligence and intent. The Government do not accept that the amendments are the right way forward, although they may hold a superficial attraction for those who want to limit the powers available to the authority. I have already described the checks and balances on those powers. The amendments ignore the context in which the powers will be exercised as well as the nature of the powers themselves.

The current regulatory system makes no concessions in that respect. Companies operating in energy markets supply important services that are required for the maintenance of human life. Any contravention of licence conditions or specified statutory obligations to which they are subject may have serious consequences. Consumers have a right to expect that companies will make their best efforts to achieve standards of performance, particularly when they have little choice over who provides the service, as with electricity distribution companies. Whether failures were intended is of little interest to those affected by them. We all know that from our constituency work.

Companies that enter the energy sector are aware that they must operate within a clearly defined framework of obligations and that they must strive to fulfil those obligations. The onus should be on the company to do so. The authority needs appropriate powers to ensure that companies that do not fulfil their obligations, for whatever reason, can be brought back into line.

If the authority decides that it is appropriate to impose a financial penalty in respect of a contravention—it has a power, not a duty, to do so—that penalty must be reasonable in all the circumstances of the case. I mentioned earlier the requirement to consult on, publish and have regard to a statement of policy. Without wanting to pre-empt the contents of that policy statement, it seems likely that negligence and intent would be factors that the authority might take into account in determining the level of any penalty. If an infringement was genuinely accidental, the authority could take that into account and conclude that it should not impose a penalty. Any company that felt aggrieved by the imposition of a penalty could challenge it in court.

Amendments Nos. 322 and 328 would impose a requirement that the authority should have regard to its statement of policy in deciding whether to impose a penalty and that, in those circumstances, it should also have regard to the principle that the purpose of penalties is to deter similar contraventions or failures in future.

I am puzzled by the first part of the amendments. I have described the provision relating to the authority's statement of policy and mentioned the fact that it also requires the authority to have regard to that statement in deciding whether to impose a penalty and how severe the penalty should be. That part of the amendments is therefore already present in the Bill. As it would simply introduce repetition, I shall talk about it no further.

On the second part of the amendments, the Government are clear that a key purpose of the penalties is to ensure compliance both when they are imposed and into the future. The statement of policy will allow the authority to provide further detail on the relevant factors that it weighs up in determining whether, and at what level, to impose a penalty. The key factor is that the financial penalty must be reasonable in all the circumstances of the case. That will enable a regulator to take into account matters such as the need to secure compliance and deter future breaches; the amount of loss or damage caused by the breach; its frequency and duration; and any other matter or circumstance. There is no need to specify purposes as proposed in the amendments—unless they are a way of limiting penalties to something less than is reasonable in all the circumstances.

Amendments Nos. 321 and 327 are designed to ensure that certain conditions and qualifications apply to the imposition of financial penalties, and to impose a cap on the amount of any penalty. To put a bit of flesh on the bones, they are designed to ensure that penalties are

    proportionate having regard to the nature and specificity of the condition or prescribed standard in question.

They would require that the level of any penalty should take into account the seriousness and duration of a contravention, and would impose a limit of 10 per cent. of annual turnover on the amount of any penalty.

The amendments are well intentioned and our discussion on them has been useful, but I shall spell out why I believe that they are unnecessary. The provisions will give the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority powers to impose penalties for current and past breaches of licence conditions. All penalties must be reasonable. That specification is vital. ``Reasonable'', unlike ``proportionate'', is a definition recognised in English law. ``Reasonable'' is also less limiting than ``proportionate``. The provisions of the amendments are already covered in the legislation, which builds factors into the requirement for a penalty to be

    reasonable in all the circumstances of the case.

That means that proportionality comes within the definition of reasonableness. I remind the Committee of the provision requiring the authority to publish a statement of its policies and of why it has come to its decisions. That would assist any subsequent court action that a company might take. The statement may also include explicit reference to the seriousness and duration of, and the harm caused by, the contravention or failure.

The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton referred to the role of Parliament. The authority has the expertise to impose a penalty that is reasonable in all the circumstances and to set out for public inspection the factors that it will take into account in determining the appropriate level of penalty. The Government do not want to prejudice or skew that process by giving undue prominence to particular factors in the Bill. However, the appeal procedure is extremely limited. In proposed new section 27E(4) of the Electricity Act 1989—and in clause 75, which would insert the equivalent proposed new section 30E(4) into the Gas Act 1986—three grounds for appeal are set out.

The first, in proposed new paragraph (a) is

    that the imposition of the penalty was not within the power of the Authority under section 27A.

All that that means is that the regulatory authority must be satisfied that a licence holder has contravened or breached a condition or standard and that that, in the regulator's opinion, is reasonable. So, provided that the regulator has taken all the factors into account, that judgment is up to him. In other words, the first ground of appeal is similar to the provision for judicial review, with which it is difficult to succeed, and does not enable any appeal on the basis of the actual fine or actual facts. An appeal can be made only on the basis of whether the authority acted in a quasi-judicial and fair manner, regardless of the decision made. The fine could therefore be unreasonable, under any objective assessment, but provided that the authority regarded it as reasonable—and the regulator had taken all steps to enable him to take that view—there could be no appeal. The Government may take a different view, but having consulted with lawyers outside the House, I believe that that is the view that the courts will take. They will not wish to second-guess any decision made by the regulatory authority.

The second ground for appeal is where the procedures in subsections (2) to (4) and (6) of proposed new section 27A have not been complied with. Those include purely procedural matters, such as the terms of the notice to be issued to the licence holders by the authority and the specification of a period in which they can object—within 21 days. Clause 48

Licence enforcement

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Gibb: Clauses 48 and 76 introduce a new subsection (5A) to the Electricity Act 1989 and a new subsection (5A) into the Gas Act 1986. These clauses were hinted at by the Minister when she was talking about the grounds for resisting one or two of the Opposition amendments to clauses 47 and 75.

The Bill states that the authority is not required to make a final order if it is satisfied that the licence holder has agreed or is taking steps to remedy the breach, or if the breach is trivial. The clauses use the phrase ``not required to'', not the words ``shall not'', which means that the regulatory authority will still have the power to fine. If the clause achieved what the Minister hinted at earlier, it would state that the authority shall not impose the fine if

    the licence holder has agreed to take and is taking all such steps

to remedy the breach. As it stands, the clause provides no assistance or comfort to the concerns of industry. Nor does it reduce the regulatory risk that will be of concern to consumers.
 

Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): Broadly speaking, the provisions are right, so perhaps the arguments advanced by the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton were a little misplaced. We are talking about a quasi-judicial process and the need for large commercial players to be regulated adequately by the regulatory system. We need a strong and firm regulatory system, not one that can be bluffed and kidded. If a loophole of the sort implied by the hon. Gentleman were left in the regulations, it could be so treated—and I would be most unhappy if that happened.

Mrs. Liddell: In urging that clause 48 stand part of the Bill, I would take the criticisms of the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton more seriously if he had tabled an amendment. The clause introduces minor amendments to the licence enforcement provisions in the Electricity Act 1989. Its purpose, and that of clause 76, is to streamline enforcement procedures and to increase consistency between the utility sectors in respect of licence enforcement. It is a minor change and would give the authority greater flexibility at the margins without fundamentally altering the basis of enforcement action. The authority will not be compelled to impose an order in the circumstances described, but it should not be precluded from doing so if the circumstances are appropriate.

The clause reduces the minimum time for representation to be made in the notices on the issuing, modification and revocation of enforcement orders from 28 to 21 days. The proposal streamlines the enforcement procedures and aligns the time period for representations in respect of enforcement orders in the electricity sector with that for representations on notices in respect of financial penalties in that sector. I commend the clause to the Committee.

Clause 49

Links between directors' remuneration

and electricity service standards

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Gibb: We have dealt with clauses 47 and 75, which are the most controversial clauses, and we have now reached the most extraordinary clauses, although they are harmless. Each runs to 91 or 92 lines over several pages and relates to the remuneration of directors of utility companies. In the run-up to the Bill's publication, we read press stories, spun by the Government, about how the Bill would directly link directors' pay to the charges payable by customers, and about how any pay rise that Ministers decided was excessive would result in the regulator punishing the company through the price cap under the RPI-X formula. Had the Government introduced such a measure, it would have been the most interventionist since those of the 1970s and would have amounted to a national pay policy for directors. Therefore, the Government wisely backed off, which is slightly surprising, given their rhetoric in opposition.

Instead, we have this damp squib of a couple of clauses that say merely that companies that link an element of a director's pay to the standard of service that they provide should publish details of the arrangement. If no such arrangement exists, that fact should be published too. I am sure that the left of the Labour party is deeply disappointed by the clause. I see that the Government Whip, the hon. Member for Hyndburn (Mr. Pope)—[Interruption.] Well, I do not see him, but I can well believe that he has performed his task with draconian aplomb, in that no amendments have been tabled to the clauses. Who knows what may happen on Report, when the Government have less control over who tables amendments?

The clauses achieve little, if anything. They take up four pages of the Bill and impose a burden on companies that establish a link between pay and the level of service that they provide, in that whole hosts of publications and dealings with the regulator are required. They constitute an unnecessary burden, which has been put in the Bill to deal solely with the politics of directors' pay.

Dr. Howells: I thought that the left had been purged a long time ago; I did not know that any of its members were left.

Mrs. Eleanor Laing (Epping Forest): They are all behind you.

Dr. Howells: First, I will clarify the purpose of clause 49. As the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton said, it is not about curbing boardroom pay levels—that is not the job of Government. Setting boardroom pay is a corporate matter for companies and their shareholders. What is a matter for Government is ensuring that the overarching regulatory framework in sub-competitive markets provides adequate protection for consumers.

Where competition is fully developed, consumers' purchasing power will provide the impetus for companies to improve their customer service standards. Companies know that if they do not, consumers will vote with their feet and change supplier.

Where companies and customer choice are insufficiently developed, and prices are regulated as a result, it is important that companies should be given every incentive to attain higher standards of customer service. That is why the clause is important. It will oblige companies to make public any links between directors' pay and the company's performance in terms of the customer service standards achieved in price-regulated activities. In turn, that will encourage companies to strengthen those links and focus on performance in respect of service standards.

The clause will achieve those aims by obliging price-regulated gas and electricity companies to inform the authority as soon as is practicable after the end of each financial year whether their directors' remuneration levels are linked to the attainment of service standards in price-regulated activities.

If there are no links between directors' pay and customer service standards, the company is simply required to say so, and to state whether there are any plans to establish such links in the next financial year. If a company plans to introduce links, it is required to describe what they might entail. If, by contrast, directors' pay is linked to the company's performance on service standards, companies will be required to disclose certain information such as when the arrangements were made, to which service standards directors' pay is linked, how performance on service standards is assessed and what effect it has on the calculation of directors' remuneration.

The clause also gives the authority the power to publish that information, and acts as a safeguard to ensure that the information is placed in the public domain and adequately publicised. The publication of links between price-regulated gas and electricity companies' directors' pay and companies' performance on service standards provided for under the clause will ensure that consumer service standards are firmly at the forefront of boardroom agendas. It will put pressure on companies to strengthen links between directors' pay and service standards, thereby improving overall service standards as a result. I commend the clause to the Committee.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 49 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
