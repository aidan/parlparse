Mr. Raynsford: This is a large group of amendments that have slightly disparate purposes. Their main purpose, insofar as there is a common theme, is to establish, as the hon. Member for Croydon, South emphasised in moving the amendment, the creation of an environmental strategy group. The amendments misunderstand the purpose of the authority. One specific part of the GLA's general purpose is to promote environmental improvement in London. In addition, the authority will have specific environmental responsibilities in respect of the preparation of strategies on waste, air quality, noise and biodiversity.

There is therefore no need to lay down in primary legislation how the mayor will obtain advice on, and organise the running of, his or her environmental responsibilities any more than in the part of the Bill that we discussed relating to the need for a separate planning advisory group to advise the mayor on how he or she should discharge his or her planning responsibilities.

It was clear that we wanted a structure that avoided unnecessary bureaucracy and an unnecessary proliferation of committees. We wanted a structure that clearty defined the responsibility. The responsibility rests with the mayor and, as is clear in the Bill, the mayor is subject to extensive duties to consult. We have already given undertakings on how we will come back to the duties to consult to ensure that they are truly and accurately focused in the most appropriate way.

In order to fulfil the authority's general purpose, the mayor will have to put arrangements in place to obtain advice from environmental experts on all of his or her policies and proposals. This will have to be built into the management structures of both the authority and the functional bodies.

The amendment makes it clear how far we differ from the Opposition's approach. Their amendment would compartmentalise the treatment of environmental matters within the GLA by making them the responsibility of a separate environmental strategy group. There would be no read-across to the GLA's other functions.

We believe that the Mayor should retain the flexibility to make arrangements to ensure that environmental considerations are taken into account in all the authority's actions. The mayor's flexibility in this area should not be restricted by primary legislation.

What is the point of devolving power, if one seeks to predetermine the minutiae of how that power can be used or if one carefully draws up working methods to which those to whom power is devolved must adhere?

The mayor may decide to work in a number of different ways to ensure that proper account is taken of environmental issues across the entire authority. That might be done through the setting up of a strategy group. It might be done by using a round table model, made up of senior officers from the GLA, its functional bodies and other environmental experts. It might be done by appointing a series of environmental experts to work throughout the authority on the entire range of its responsibilities. More probably, it will be done by a combination of all these options.

As the hon. Gentleman will be aware, the mayor will have the benefit of expert and professional staff who will come into the new authority as a result of the structure that we are creating. In particular the expert body, the London Ecology Unit, which has done first-rate work in recent years, will provide expertise for the mayor from the establishment of the new authority.

I hope that I have made the Government's position clear. We do not support the establishment of an outside body of experts to advise the mayor or to write strategies on his or her behalf. Consequently, we do not support the amendments that propose this; nor can we support any of the amendments that are consequential upon them.

There is, however, one consequential amendment within this group that I would like to speak to in a little detail. Amendment No. 1212 would require the mayor to consult the environment strategy group and the London boroughs on the London ambient noise strategy.

We cannot support the element of the amendment that implies the need to establish an environment strategy group. Nor is the part requiring the mayor to consult the London boroughs necessary, as earlier clauses already require the mayor to consult all London local authorities on all his or her strategies, of which noise is one.

However, I am prepared to consider the case for including the Environment Agency as a statutory consultee for the noise strategy. The Environment Agency is a statutory consultee for the three other environment strategies: waste, air quality and biodiversity. I accept that there is a case to be made for the agency to be consulted by the mayor on the noise strategy. That case is strengthened by the fact that I understand the agency is about to take on some responsibility for noise as a result of the Pollution Prevention and Control Bill, which is currently making its passage through Parliament. I undertake to consider the point on this part of the amendment further and, if appropriate, to come back with a further amendment at a later stage. I hope that in the light of my comments the hon. Gentleman will agree to withdraw his amendment.
