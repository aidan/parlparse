In recognition of the complexity of the Bill, we propose to give the Committee an opportunity to work on its structure. For the next week, we will sit only in the morning. We shall sit on Tuesday 15 February from 10.30 am until 1 pm and on Thursday 17 February from 9.55 am until 11.25 am. The Government want to make progress on the detail of the Bill from then on. We propose four sittings a week, on Tuesdays from 10.30 am until 1 pm and from 4.30 pm until 7 pm, and on Thursdays from 9.55 am until 11.25 am and from 2.30 pm until 5.30 pm. Members are aware that a short recess has been announced and that it will run from 23 to 25 February, assuming that the business of the House has been completed by that time.

I hope that hon. Members will find the proposed arrangements satisfactory and that they will contribute to an orderly, successful and detailed scrutiny of the Bill. I am pleased to see in the Committee hon. Members who spoke on Second Reading and I am happy that the Committee of Selection has included them. My officials have been prepared to brief members of all parties to help them to deal with the technical complexity of the Bill. Members' knowledge--not least mine--is not of the required level.

It will improve the transparency, consistency and predictability of regulation, and it will provide a stable framework. I am sure that hon. Members would agree that we have an excellent opportunity further to improve the Bill. It will allow the people of this country to benefit from utilities regulation reform in a changing marketplace that will provide many opportunities for better service. I look forward to a lively and constructive debate.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): I welcome you, Mr. Hood, to the Chair. I have not had the pleasure of serving on a Committee that is chaired by you, and I look forward to working with you over the coming weeks.

The Opposition regard the Bill as extremely important. We want to ensure that it is debated fully; aspects of it concern us. Our approach is to improve it by taking into account the views of people outside the House, so that we have a satisfactory regulatory system, but I am worried that the process is riddled with incompetence. I know that there is a shortage of parliamentary draftsmen and that it is difficult to get Bills drafted, but this Bill has not been well handled. There is uncertainty about next week's sitting and some clauses were missing, which meant that a revised version had to be issued.

The rumour is--I must base my comments on rumour because the Minister has given me no information--that there will be several hundred Government amendments. We need to know when they will be tabled. The Government allegedly pride themselves on their consultation with industry, but 12 amendments were tabled yesterday and might be debated next week. What time does that give the Opposition and people outside the House to promote their views? It is inadequate and unsatisfactory to table amendments so late. That might be necessary for some clauses, but there has been time since Second Reading to hold discussions on amendments with outside parties. I want two assurances: first, that we will have sufficient time to consult people outside the House who are worried about the Bill and the amendments and, secondly, that we will have time to table amendments to the amendments.

The explanatory notes give a long list of the categories that are to be amended. They state:

    "the Government intends to bring forward amendments later in the passage of the Bill...to give effect to them."

How many amendments will the Minister table to each category? It is not unreasonable to expect that information. I would be alarmed if the Department did not have it. If it is not provided today, the Committee should be delayed until it is available. When will those amendments be tabled? We must ensure that we do not sit too soon after they have been tabled. The Government Whip is sighing, but the point is serious. As Opposition spokesman, I intend to take the Bill seriously. However, no Opposition Member can do that unless he has sufficient time to consider amendments and to consult outside bodies.

There will be Government amendments under several categories, including some concerning

    "a requirement for each regulator to take into account the implications of its decisions for other regulated sectors within a multi-utility".

When shall we have the amendments? There will be

    "a requirement for regulators to give collective consideration to matters of common interests, and to publish the results".

When shall we have those amendments? There will be

    "a requirement for the regulators to establish a code of practice on their consultation and decision-making procedures".

There will be amendments on

    "the regulators' existing powers to publish information to align them with the equivalent powers of the new consumer councils so that the regulator can also publish information where this is in the interests of consumers".

There will also be amendments relating to

    "measures consequential on the separation of electricity distribution and supply--including provision for transfer schemes for public electricity suppliers"

and

    "measures to increase competition on gas transportation".

Finally, there will be

    "amendments to the procedures for referring licence modifications to the Competition Commission consequential on the new procedures for licence modifications".

We need to know today the number of amendments under each of the categories, when they will be published and when they will be debated.

In moving the sittings motion, the Minister said that the structure of the Bill would be debated next week. Will she clarify whether that means that we shall debate from clause 1, clause 84 and schedule 1 according to the motion, or does she have in mind a quasi-Second Reading debate on the organisation of the Bill?

The Bill lacks necessary details. It refers to many new regulatory powers--key clauses will give important new powers to the regulator and the Secretary of State. When will the draft statutory instruments referred to be published? I strongly believe that they should be published in good time for the debate on the power-making clauses. We do not know how many people the new authorities will employ or at what cost; nor the way in which the new consumer council people will be appointed.

We shall need considerable time to debate the Government's method. How do they reconcile their regulatory and interventionist approach with other views? David Edmonds--the telecoms regulator who will continue under the new regime--has said that regulation should be rolled back before competition is introduced. How do they reconcile the approach with the statement of Ian Byatt, the Director General of Water Services, that the Bill was over-complicated because it was over-prescriptive, and that the regulator would be festooned with detailed requirements likely to raise the cost, but not the effectiveness, of the regulations?

These are important philosophical points. Information on the statutory instruments is needed. I am pleased that guidance has already been published and I would like to see more published on social and environmental issues. Before we can debate the points, we need to know about powers, cost and who will be appointed.

How do the Government reconcile the approach with the views of PowerGen, which says that the regulations have

    "the potential to increase significantly the costs and risks of business ... increasing costs to companies and working against the long-term interests of"

the consumer?

The approach is different from that of the Gas Act 1995, which was about potential future conduct. Under the Bill, unlimited fines may be applied to past acts--the scope is unlimited. The consequences for industry are dramatic. In most cases, fines will ultimately be borne by the consumer, for whom we are concerned.

We must spend considerable time on the issue and on understanding the way in which the system of fines will work. How will they ensure that the fines are proportionate and fair, and what will be the mechanism for implementing them? We need to debate those issues.

The appeals mechanism is a matter for concern. The Bill provides for a limited appeals mechanism, which is effectively limited to judicial review. There is no provision for the court to consider the facts. I am concerned about subsection (8) of new section 27E on financial penalties, which says:

    "Except as provided by this section, the validity of a penalty shall not be questioned by any legal proceedings whatever."

That is a far-reaching provision, and we must know what the Government mean by it. It contrasts sharply with the provisions of the Competition Act 1998, under which fines are imposed only if there is negligence or intent. It will be possible to impose fines under the Bill even if the activity was unintended. The approach taken in the Bill seems to be adversarial and I want to ensure that we have sufficient facts to enable us to debate it properly.

All those uncertainties will make companies more cautious and less innovative than they should be, with the result that the industry will be less competitive. That is bad for business. I am worried about the information requirements, a question that was raised on Second Reading, and about the regulatory gaining that could take place. That happened in the United States, where rivals who were also customers of the big utilities, used the information requirements in similar legislation to find out information that would enable them to compete more effectively, or even unfairly, with existing players. How do the Government propose to tackle that risk, of which the Minister said that she was aware?

On directors' pay, the published provisions relate to price-regulated activities, but what if the directors are in a public limited company, with only one or two utility- based subsidiaries, and its main activities are elsewhere? How do the Government intend to resolve that problem, in which the main players in that public limited company are not even on the board of the utilities companies? We must resolve such questions before we can debate the issue properly in Committee.

A key aspect of the Bill is the issue of duties and downgrading the promotion of competition. That concerns me because competition has produced lower prices for consumers, but lower prices are not achieved through legislation, We need to be certain about the Government's intention, which is vague. Why are the Government changing the order of objectives and duties? Is it because of purely presentational issues or do they have concrete concerns about the existing arrangements? What is the definition of consumer in the new objective? Does it include other electricity, water, telephone and gas companies, or does it relate only to the individual domestic consumer? That needs to be defined more precisely so that we know whom the Government intend to target.

What if there is a conflict between present and future consumers? How do the Government intend to resolve that conflict? It might be that today's consumers will benefit from a certain prescription but at the expense of future consumers. How can the regulator act as a custodian of both the consumer interest and the competitive process? Do the Government intend the regulator to deal with the industry and the consumer council's to deal with consumer bodies? That is not clear in the Bill or the explanatory notes, but we must fully understand it before we debate other clauses. Why are the Government downgrading the security of supply, which was always the utility companies' primary objective under the previous Act?

What will be the consequence for consumers' utility bills of giving the Secretary of State powers to impose social and environmental obligations on the regulators? Will those social and environmental costs simply be added to consumers' bills? As there will be no price capping of electricity bills, how will we ensure that costs are not merely passed on to consumers in the form of higher electricity bills? We already pay £1.20 a year for such objectives, which seems reasonable; will that figure be increased to £5 or £10? It is beholden on the Minister to explain precisely the effect that the proposal will have on consumers' electricity bills. The fear is that it is another taxation through stealth, imposed by the Government to implement their programme.

I am concerned about how the Committee will scrutinise the Bill. I am anxious, too, about the number of Government amendments that have been tabled. I want to know today the timetable, the number of amendments and how they fit in to the new matters on which the Government intend to legislate, which are detailed in the explanatory notes. The Bill has not been well handled so far and it is for every member of the Committee, not just Opposition Members, to ensure that the Bill is properly scrutinised. At the end of the day, we should have a Bill that will serve--[Interrtiption.]
