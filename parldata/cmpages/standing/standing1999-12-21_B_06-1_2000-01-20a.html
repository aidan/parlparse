Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross explained, the amendment would prevent a public authority from charging an applicant for telling him that information is held, if the fact that information is held is exempt from clause 1. A public authority is required to disclose whether it holds information that is sought unless the requirement does not arise by virtue of part II, or the cost of determining whether it holds the information would exceed the appropriate limit set out in regulations.

If an authority is required to disclose whether it holds information, any charge will be made according to regulations made under clause 11. The provision that the amendment seeks to change will come into play only when an authority can neither confirm nor deny whether it holds the information and provisions in part II apply, or when the cost of determining whether it holds the information exceeds the limit, which is expected to be set at £500.

The regulations will specify that the marginal costs of locating and finding information can be charged to the applicant. That deals with the point made by the hon. Member for Ryedale and to some extent with that made by my hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central. Any member of the public who makes a request for information must recognise that he or she is inviting an authority to conduct a certain amount of work to provide an answer. Whatever answer is made, the charge will be made not so much for the answer but for the work that the authority has undertaken to provide it.

In most cases, the cost of determining whether information is held will be minimal and no charge—or at least only a minimal charge—will be made. If, exceptionally, the cost of determining whether an authority holds information is significant, it is right that the applicant should bear some part of it. That cost would not otherwise disappear into the ether but would fall on the remaining services of the Government body in question and thereby restrict its activity to carry out its other statutory functions. As the hon. Member for Ryedale rightly said, a balance must be struck between cost to applicants and to taxpayers in general.

Mr. Lock: The amendments would require a notice on a decision on discretionary disclosures given under clause 13 to be given within 20 working days of receipt. The amendment is related, as the hon. Gentleman rightly noted, to amendment No. 80 to clause 13. The arguments against the amendment are much the same as the arguments against amendment No. 80, and I am unable to accept it.

The Bill currently allows an authority up to 20 working days to reach a decision under clause 1(1 ) in order to decide whether the information requested is exempt. If the authority decides that the information is exempt, clause 13 requires it to make a further decision on whether to exercise its discretion to disclose, taking into account all the circumstances of the case and the desirability to communicate exempt information to the applicant, where the public interest in disclosure outweighs the public interest in maintaining the exemption. Clause 13(6) requires that this decision be made within such time as is reasonable in the circumstances.

There are good reasons for not specifying a time limit for decisions under clause 13. First, it is important to note that two distinct steps are involved. In making a decision under clause 13, an authority must have already determined that the information is exempt. While this decision must be made promptly, it is conceivable that it could take up much of the 20 days allowed by the Bill. For example, the authority may wish to consult those to whom the information relates or who would be likely to be affected by the disclosure, and the Secretary of State's code of practice under clause 44 will contain guidance for authorities in such consultations. In this case the amendment would leave no time for decisions under clause 13 to be taken.

Clause 13 also requires the authority to take all the circumstances of the case into account in balancing the public interest. Such a decision may be far more complex than the original decision as to whether the information is exempt. It is therefore impossible to specify a time limit for taking such decisions, because much will depend on the circumstances of each case. However, the Bill provides that the decision must be taken within a reasonable period in the circumstances.

The amendment would therefore not only disadvantage authorities by imposing an impractical time limit on them, but would disadvantage applicants and others affected by disclosure by causing decisions on the public interest to be taken hastily. If time is short, authorities will be more likely to withhold information than disclose it, on the basis that where a matter has not been properly considered it is better to be safe than sorry. Alternatively, authorities might decide not to consult those affected by disclosure on the basis that it would take too long, which would be equally inappropriate. That is why clause 13(6) provides for a decision to be taken within a reasonable time, in order to ensure that decisions are taken soundly.

I recognise the concern behind the amendment. To prevent authorities from stalling, however, the Bill gives the Information Commissioner power to issue an enforcement notice under clause 52(2), which requires the authority to make a decision in accordance with clause 13 within the time set out in the notice. Therefore, safeguards already exist in the Bill. I hope that the right hon. Gentleman will accept that that is a far more practical and realistic approach. On that basis, I invite him to withdraw the amendment.

The amendment would require authorities to publish the internal guidelines that are used by officials in their dealings with the public. It reflects an existing requirement of the open government code, which is already in place but which the Bill, rather surprisingly, has dropped, contrary to the commitment in the White Paper. As the Under-Secretary may be aware, such a provision appears in every English language freedom of information law throughout the world.

Paragraph 3.ii of part I of the open government code requires the authorities

    ``to publish or otherwise make available, as soon as practicable after the Code becomes operational, explanatory material on departments' dealings with the public (including such rules, procedures, internal guidance to officials and similar administrative manuals as will assist better understanding of departmental action in dealing with the public) except where publication could prejudice any matter which should properly be kept confidential under Part II [the Exemptions] of the Code.''

The guidance on the code states:

    ``Departments should plan for the progressive release of all the guidelines and other material used in their dealings with the public. This need not mean publication where departments consider that the level of interest would not justify it, or where the guidelines in question are voluminous, but eventually the aim should be to make all guidelines available for purchase or inspection on request.''

Annual reports under the code have described examples of the kinds of guidance that have been released under this provision. The 1997 second edition of the code of practice on access to government information, guidance on interpretation, states:

    ``In 1994 the Inland Revenue set a target to publish their 45 main guidance manuals by the end of December 1995. Only 5 volumes failed to meet this deadline and these were published by the beginning of February 1996.''

It goes on:

    ``The Health and Safety Executive's rolling programme for preparing existing internal guidance for release was completed on 31 March 1996.''

It also notes that the

    ``Home Office's Immigration Service Enforcement Directorate received 95 requests for internal enforcement policy guidelines in 1996, all of which were met'',

that the

    ``Office for Standards in Education provided all nursery education providers with a copy of the Inspection Notebook used by its Inspectors in 1996'',

and that

    ``In March the FCO''—

that is, the Foreign and Commonwealth Office—

    ``published Guidance to Desk Officers on Export Licence and Arms Working Party Applications. This was superseded by the Criteria to be used in considering licence applications for the export of conventional arms which was issued in July''.

The 1997 code further states:

    ``All main Child Support Agency guides and manuals are now publicly available''.

The Freedom of Information White Paper made it clear that it was intended to continue those duties but, lo and behold, the Bill omits them. That is yet another retrograde step in the progress of the Government's thinking about the duty of disclosure. It is extremely disturbing that they are moving in that direction. It will be thought that they have no intention to live up to their purported ambition to improve what we already have.
