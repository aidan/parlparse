Mr. Clappison: This interesting and important clause proposes a change to the law of bail. It amends section 25 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 regarding the law of bail for people convicted of certain offences. As a general proposition, defendants are entitled to bail unless there are reasons why it should not be granted. For example, the court might think, that defendants would be likely to commit further offences, or would fail to surrender to custody, or would interfere with witnesses. Defendants have a right to bail, unless the court thinks that such matters will occur.

There is a bar to bail, however, for defendants charged with, or convicted of, an offence of homicide or rape. Section 25 of the 1994 Act provided that such defendants could not be granted bail if they had a previous conviction for homicide or rape. Clause 53 removes that bar to bail from those defendants. It provides that a court or a police officer considering their case may grant bail, if the circumstances justify it.

When I first read the clause, I wondered why the Government wanted to remove the bar to bail for defendants with previous convictions for such serious offences. I am sure that that was also the reaction of many other people. Why was the removal of the bar such a priority that it was included in this flagship Bill on law and order? How will this assist in the fight against crime, and how will it fulfil the Government’s proud boast that they would be tough on crime and tough on the causes of crime? I suspect that many of my constituents, and those of other hon. Members, would not immediately see that the clause was tough on crime and tough on the causes of crime.

Conservative Members believe that these matters are potentially of the utmost seriousness. We seek a full debate on the implications of the change, the risk that it creates and the reasons for it. We look forward to the Minister’s comments.

The amendments would introduce safeguards to protect the public from the Government’s course of action. Amendment No. 189 would require decisions about bail in cases of these very serious offences to be taken only by a High Court judge. Amendment No. 190 would prevent a police officer from making such a decision on his or her own. Amendment No. 191 would require any court, which granted bail for a serious case, to state in open court what the exceptional circumstances were that allowed bail to be granted.

As I have said, Conservative Members are puzzled about the Government’s reasons for including the clause. Will the Minister tell the Committee what consultations the Government have carried out about this proposed change in the law? Have they consulted any victims’ organisations? We believe that a vast majority of the public would say that the best possible safeguards should be included in the clause if the Government are determined to have their way with clause 53. We agree.

Mr. Mike O’Brien: Opposition Members have spoken as if the clause would create an entirely new situation. However, during 14 years of the previous Conservative Administration, custody sergeants and magistrates were able to grant bail in the cases outlined in the clause. During that period, there were never any cases in which it was believed that the powers were wrongly used by the police and magistrates.

The previous Government changed the law; they took a view on the law and there was considerable parliamentary debate when they proposed the changes. One of the key arguments against removing the powers from the police and magistrates was that there had never been any cases in which the powers had been abused.

The Government intend that the powers should be used only in the most exceptional circumstances. Let me offer an example from the top of my head—not a case that has actually occurred—about a case in which a doctor had been convicted of a homicide arising from euthanasia. The conviction would probably have been for manslaughter and he would have received a sentence, although not a substantial one.

The doctor then falls under suspicion for a similar offence. He is not regarded as a threat to the general community and it would be easy to impose a bail condition that he should not practice as a doctor. There would, therefore, be no chance that he would reoffend. He had not absconded during the previous case, and has good links with the community, so there is no prospect of him absconding during the second case. In such circumstances, there might be a debate about bail. It would be a matter for the courts to decide whether bail was appropriate.

Hon. Members might argue that it would be better to consider such cases at a higher level than I have suggested. However, the Government are of the view that the laws worked properly in the past. We have built safeguards into the clause, so that the courts are able to consider all the circumstances. However, in very exceptional circumstances—I must stress that they would be exceptional, as I cannot think of any real cases similar to the one that I have just described—it might be appropriate to grant bail.

The Government do not think that it is necessary to remove the right to grant bail in the type of case that I have outlined. It cannot be right to fetter discretion in that way. It is always possible, although most likely, that in exceptional circumstances it would be appropriate for a person to be released on bail.
