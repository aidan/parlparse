The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for International Development (Mr. George Foulkes): At the end of this morning's sitting there was some confusion about the 25 per cent. shareholding. I must accept my share of the responsibility for that misunderstanding. For the benefit of the Committee and for the record, I shall make the position clear. As I mentioned earlier, the golden share contains rights over several important matters, such as the investment policy, the right to appoint the two directors and the ability to stop alterations to various provisions of the company's memorandum of understanding and articles of association. Therefore, it was considered that the market would respond more positively to the Government's retention of such rights through the golden share if the Government also maintained a substantial economic interest in the Commonwealth Development Corporation through their ordinary shareholding.

During this morning's sitting, I described the importance of that substantial economic interest. A shareholding of 25 per cent. was chosen because it was thought to be the appropriate minimum figure to constitute a substantial interest. It is, for example, the level around which an ordinary shareholder could block a special resolution under the Companies Act 1985. Therefore, it constitutes what might be considered to be a significant minority shareholding.

As the hon. Member for South-West Devon (Mr. Streeter) rightly said, it is not an exact figure--sometimes a 75 per cent. vote is needed to pass a resolution. However, there is no direct or formal link; we are aware that a 25 per cent. shareholding will not block a special resolution--more than 25 per cent. would be required for that. The percentage has been chosen intentionally. The Secretary of State has the powers that she requires as part of the rights that attach to the special share, which are clearly specified in the articles of association, so the Government do not intend to vote using the ordinary shareholding other than in exceptional circumstances. I hope that I have now made the position clear.

Last week, the hon. Member for Chesham and Amersham (Mrs. Gillan) asked about the retirement age of directors. Following her comments I gave a commitment to consider that matter, and we have done so. The article to which she referred simply reflects a requirement on all public companies under section 293 of the Companies Act 1985. However, I am glad to say that that Act is being reviewed. If she wants to pursue such matters, she could do so in that more general context.

Mr. Foulkes: It is not within the power of this Committee to amend the Companies Act 1985 in relation to all companies. If the hon. Lady and her colleagues want to pursue that matter, it would be better to do so in a wider context, given that the Government are reviewing that Act.

Mr. Gary Streeter (South-West Devon): I am grateful to the Minister for clarifying the position of the Government's shareholding. We do not intend to press these amendments to a Division and, therefore, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Clause 21

Treasury consent

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Sir David Madel (South-West Bedfordshire): I should like to say a few words on what I regard as the Treasury control clause. The list of what cannot be done without Treasury consent is pretty long.

I have always thought that the country was overzealously controlled by the Treasury. Receipts from council house sales cannot be spent, arguably because one could not do so without somehow upsetting the public sector borrowing requirement. However, I have never understood why such money cannot be spent straight away on the housing market. I know that that is outside the scope of the Bill, but it is an example of Treasury control.

I want to deal with two points on the clause. Subsection (1)(d) states that the Treasury must give consent before the Secretary of State can make

    ``a determination under section 9(3), 10(6) or 15(3)''.

Clause 9 appears to be a condition whereby the Secretary of State might want to make an emergency loan to the corporation or an associated company. However, clause 9(2) states that

    ``The Treasury may issue to the Secretary of State out of the National Loans Fund sums required for loans under this section.''

That is a bit vague. Even if the Treasury said, ``All right, you can have a certain sum of money for the loan,'' would the Secretary of State have to go back to the Treasury to get final clearance that the money provided from the national loans fund was acceptable as an emergency loan?

There is no doubt in clause 9(3) as to who is in charge of repayments. It states that they shall be made

    ``(a) at such times,

    (b) by such methods, and

    (c) with interest paid at such rates and at such times,

    as the Secretary of State shall determine from time to time.''

There is no question in that subsection of the Secretary of State having to get permission from the Treasury to determine what he or she wants to do.

Clause 10(6) states:

    ``A guarantee under subsection (1) or (3) may be given on such terms and in such manner as the Treasury or the Secretary of State may determine.''

That is interesting. Has the Treasury suddenly relaxed its control and said to the Secretary of State, ``You decide that,'' or will it insist that it has a veto? What happens if there is no agreement? There is some vagueness that should be cleared up.

Clause 15(3) states:

    ``For the purposes of this section equivalents in sterling shall be calculated as determined by the Secretary of State.''

There is no argument about that: it is entirely for the Secretary of State to determine, without reference to the Treasury. However, as we think that we are escaping from the clutches of the Treasury, clause 21(2) states:

    ``The Treasury's consent--

    (a) may be general or specific, and

    (b) may be conditional or unconditional.''

Talk about a catch-all situation! It is almost like Mao Tse-Tung's ``100 flowers'' speech, when suddenly China felt that there would be a great degree of liberalism, but all the power was taken back to the centre.

There is some mystery and vagueness in clause 21. I can see what the argument is about and where it comes from, but the points that I have raised are worthy of the Minister's comments before we proceed with the Bill.

Mr. Bowen Wells (Hertford and Stortford): One point mystifies me. Under clause 21(1)(c), the Treasury and the Department for International Development will continue to have the capacity to lend to the new public corporation. Will the repayment--both the capital and interest--of such a loan go to the Department for International Development to add to the amount of money that it already has available, or will capital be repaid to the Treasury and the interest to the Department for International Development, as happened at one time? I should be grateful if the Minister would clarify the position?

Grouping them together, rather than dealing with them separately clause by clause, which is a question of drafting style, appears to emphasise the number and the control. In each case, we have considered whether consent should be required before including them.

Let us take in turn the points that have been made. Loan funds properly require Treasury consent. It is a national loan fund, and in any well-run Administration, we would all agree that if different Departments are to lend money to outside organisations, there must be some control over the amounts involved. That is appropriate and quite normal practice in all Governments. I understand that that will arise only in the interim transition period, and that there will be no question of loans from the loan fund after the company has been restructured. There will then be a possibility for it to borrow, but not from the Treasury loan fund. At present, the CDC borrows money from the loan fund with Treasury approval, and that arrangement will stay in place until we have a balance sheet and a totally restructured company.

The hon. Gentleman referred to the interest paid, the rate and the time, as determined by the Secretary of State. We are replicating the current arrangements for the transition period.

 

Mr. Wells: As I understand it, there will be three stages. The first is when the company is formed with £50,000 capital, and totally owned by the Government. The second stage is when there is a balance sheet and a profit and loss account, and the company is still wholly owned by the Government. Is the Secretary of State saying that at that stage, the corporation will cease to have the capacity to borrow from the Treasury? If so, it will have no other way of borrowing. It cannot borrow in the private market because it is still owned by the Government. I think that the Secretary of State is saying that it is only when the Government have divested themselves of 75 per cent. of the shares, that the corporation can borrow from the private market. Is that right?

Clare Short: The hon. Gentleman is right about the three phases. In the period when the balance sheet is put in place, we will make decisions on how soon to sell, depending on the strength of the balance sheet and the emerging markets. As the hon. Gentleman knows, the market will be judged at the time. There will be a possibility of borrowing in that intervening period, if it is thought desirable. There might be a time when either would be possible. Logically, that must be right; by the time we get to stage three, the borrowing would be from the private sector. Of course, the Department and the Government could put more resources into the company, but they would do that in the normal way.

I hope that that is clear. I am grateful to the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford for trying to protect the Department from too much Treasury intervention, but I have to say that our working relationship with the Treasury is good. Our settlement in the comprehensive spending review was satisfactory. We kept it rather quiet, but we got the biggest increase of any Department. Clare Short: At last, we come to the provisions on tax. I hope that hon. Members found useful the briefing notes provided by the Department and the notes provided today that record the Department's verbal briefing.

The clause serves only to give effect to the new schedule. New schedule 1 sets out all the details of the proposed tax treatment for the CDC. The overall aim of the proposal is to provide the CDC with a level playing field in relation to competitors located outside the United Kingdom, so that it can achieve its goal of investing in developing countries, using private capital raised in the context of a competitive private investment market. It does not seek to give the CDC anything more than that.

The key element of the new schedule is that, unless the partnership were to end, the CDC's income and chargeable gains--and the CDC's only--would not be chargeable to UK corporation tax, capital gains tax or income tax. The CDC would remain liable to applicable local taxes, as at present, and investors in the CDC would be liable to tax on their income and gains according to their own circumstances. It is also intended that where the CDC undertakes activities other than investment, such as investment management, that would be undertaken through a separate subsidiary, and be subject to tax in the normal way. I might return later to the comments of the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath) on that matter.

The schedule makes the detailed provisions. I hope that the Committee will agree that it is a clear, strong way of enabling the CDC to meet the Government's objective of increasing private-sector investment in the poorest countries, which have difficulty in attracting private-sector investment without going offshore. It is a major instrument of British development policy, and it should not go offshore. I note that the Hansard report of the first sitting suggested that I said that the Cayman islands would have been acceptable but that it was not available. I did not say that. I said that it was suggested to us that the CDC could have gone to the Cayman islands, just as it could have gone to Ireland, but that I thought that it was completely inappropriate.

We want a simple, level playing field. The CDC should be able to raise funds from the private sector and not be disadvantaged by the fact that it is onshore and thus liable to tax. That would make it unable to compete with private-sector investment organisations, which are offshore because of the better tax arrangements. That is the purpose of the new schedule. I hope that we have achieved it and I hope that the Committee will agree that it is a desirable objective.
