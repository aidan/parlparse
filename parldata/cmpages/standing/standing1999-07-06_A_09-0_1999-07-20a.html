Cases in which the Treasury may arrange independent inquiries

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 75, in page 6, line 1, leaveout `serious'.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Barbara Roche): It was my belief that the hon. Member for Chichester (Mr. Tyrie) was about to get to his feet but, alas, it seems that the Committee will not have the joy, privilege or opportunity of hearing him speak to the amendments. It is a great sadness for everyone. I shall not tempt fate, but for me it was his one bite at the cherry.

The power for the Treasury to commission independent public interest inquiries into regulatory matters is another of the Bill's innovations. It is an important element in the Financial Services Authority's accountability framework. I said that the power is an innovation. I accept that public interest inquiries have been held before on an ad hoc basis, but this is the first time that statutory provision has been made for them in a specific sector. I remind the Committee of the inquiry into the collapse of the Bank of Credit and Commerce International by the then Lord Justice Bingham and, more recently, the inquiry by the Board of Banking Supervision into the Barings failure.

Members of the Committee will agree that those inquiries were both concerned with serious regulatory failures. We want to ensure that there is a proper statutory basis on which to set up an independent inquiry, were there a serious regulatory failure. That is an important step. The Bingham inquiry was important and produced a very authoritative report. However, it was conducted in the absence of any formal powers under which, for example, witnesses could be called. It is inherently unsatisfactory that such a vital inquiry should have had to rely on witnesses coming forward voluntarily.

The Bill provides for an inquiry to be conducted with the full range of powers to gather evidence, as in the High Court. The fact that we have aligned the powers of the inquiry with those of the High Court is itself an eloquent statement of the type of inquiry we intend should be held. Such power will enable us to set up inquiries into serious regulatory failures, which is not what the Opposition envisage under their amendments.

The amendments may look slight, but they would have a significant effect on the character of the inquiries provided for by the group of clauses. The inquiries will be formal; they will be funded by the taxpayer and have powers akin to those of the High Court in respect of the attendance and examination of witnesses. Setting up an independent inquiry is certainly not something that we would want to do lightly. Such procedure is a serious step to take.

It is only right that the Bill should state clearly that the purpose of such inquiries is to investigate serious regulatory failures. Nothing would be gained by removing the adjective ``serious''. The effect would be to create an unhealthy expectation that such inquiries would be common-or-garden events. There would inevitably be pressure to have a full, independent inquiry into all sorts of regulatory decisions.

By their nature, regulatory decisions will often not suit everyone and may be viewed as failure on the part of the regulator, but the proper recourse should be to the tribunal or the complaints investigator. The response should not be a full inquiry along the lines of Bingham. That is why the triggers in the Bill have been deliberately set high. We do not want such inquiries to become commonplace.

I hope that I have explained why the word ``serious'' has been used. I am minded to believe that Opposition Members have tabled this as a probing amendment, and I encourage them to withdraw it.

Ms Hewitt: As well as speaking to the Government amendments, I shall say a little about the clause. The regulation of financial promotion is an important part of the Bill. We shall no doubt return to the larger issue of the effect of clause 19, but the amendments, both Government and Opposition, deal with issues of territoriality, technology, the nature of the process of approval of communications by authorised persons and the restriction of the regime to clearly promotional communications made in the course of the business.

Together, amendments Nos. 80, 83, 84 and 85 would define the scope of the financial promotion prohibition and the interaction of new technologies such as the internet. As right hon. and hon. Members will be aware, earlier this year we published a consultation paper on financial promotion, which discussed the issue in considerable depth. In the light of responses to that paper, we intend to publish draft regulations for further consultation while the Bill is still passing through its parliamentary stages. The structure of clause 19 is to set out a broad prohibition in primary legislation and then to narrow the scope of that prohibition in secondary legislation.

The clause 19 prohibition starts by applying to all communications originating inside the United Kingdom, and also to communications originating outside the United Kingdom that are capable of having an effect in the United Kingdom, but the territorial scope will be cut back in the exemptions order. We proposed in consultation that the prohibition should apply only to promotions directed at the United Kingdom. Promotions that are merely available in the UK --for example, over the internet --but are not directed at UK consumers should not be caught by the UK's financial promotion regime. That approach has been widely welcomed in the consultation process.

The question might then be asked --I am sure that it will be --why we do not wish to put a scope test directed at UK consumers in the Bill. The answer, which is equivalent to the answer that I have given in previous debates on what should be included in the Bill and what should be introduced under secondary legislation, is that clause 19 must not provided for a rigid system of financial promotion that cannot keep up with a time of extraordinary change. Technological changes, in particular, might soon challenge the rationale of any scope that is set out in primary legislation.

Even beyond the issue of technological change, the Bill must be capable of implementing a wide range of possible international agreements on the treatment of financial services offered across borders. For instance, the draft e-commerce directive that is under negotiation in Brussels would provide a home state regulation of some financial services offered over the internet --that is, UK regulation, and only UK regulation, of financial services offered over the internet from the UK into, let us say, France.

The financial promotion regime needs to be sufficiently flexible to allow us to adopt this home state approach if necessary, as well as providing for home state regulation. Currently, advertisements and other promotions into the UK are subject to UK regulation. All that could change over time and we do not wish to be constantly trying to make time to rewrite the primary legislation when the situation regarding technology and international agreements could change quite radically.
