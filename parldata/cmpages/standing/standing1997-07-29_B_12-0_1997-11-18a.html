Mr. Simon Burns (West Chelmsford): When we broke for lunch I was reminding the Minister of several remarks made by his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State before the last general election. She adamantly expressed an opinion that was completely at variance with the proposals in the clause. I should like to wind up this part of my speech by reminding the Minister of a final quote. The right hon. Lady said in the House:

    ``We cannot sort out the £10 billion benefit bill for lone parents by shaving their benefits bit by bit.''

Earlier in the same debate, she said:

    ``The way to get lone mothers out of poverty and cut spending on benefits for them is not by cutting the amount on which they have to live year by year and plunging them further into poverty.''--[Official Report, 28 November 1996; Vol. 286, c. 500-501.]

My colleagues and I look forward to the Minister's explanation of why the Secretary of State changed her mind in such a short time.

I do not want to anticipate the Minister's speech, but I fear that we already know what he will say to justify the change. His answer will refer to the Government's commitment to keep to the previous Government's tight public spending limits. If that is the reason that is cited--I believe that it probably will be--will the policy be reversed when the two-year adherence to the previous Government's public spending limits ends? Surely the logic of the argument is that the measure will be overturned if and when that commitment ends and the Government can resume a policy to which the Secretary of State adhered so vehemently before the general election.

Clearly, the Secretary of State fundamentally disagreed with the policy and is implementing it only because she has lost a battle with the Treasury, if indeed she ever had such a battle between the general election and the July Budget. If the decision is not reversed when the spending ceiling is reviewed in two years, will not the arguments which the right hon. Lady now repeatedly uses have been shown to be bogus?

The Secretary of State's rhetoric against cutting child benefit and lone-parent premiums was unequivocal--she argued that such action was wrong. According to her, the previous Secretary of State's proposed cut would

    ``make hundreds of thousands of the poorest children worse off''.

Yet the measure before us is designed to implement the very cuts that she said would increase poverty.

This sorry tale takes another twist that is not just embarrassing for the Government, but a damning indictment of the fact that before the general election the right hon. Lady not only attacked the measure when it was introduced as I have shown, but promised to reverse it if the Labour party were elected to government. However, she is now, for obvious reasons, doing all she can to play down the promises that she made in the early months of this year.

On the BBC's ``Question Time'' on 2 October this year, the right hon. Lady protested that she had not pledged not to introduce a cut in lone-parent benefits. In short, she was being somewhat economical with the truth. On 22 January this year in an interview with Polly Toynbee of The Independent, the right hon. Lady said that she would not implement the cuts proposed by my right hon. Friend the Member for Hitchin and Harpenden (Mr. Lilley). Ms Toynbee asked:

    ``So will she or won't she introduce Tory legislation to cut single parent's benefits?''

It would be wrong of me, however, to single out the Secretary of State for Social Security and not to mention her right hon. Friend the Prime Minister. I can assure the Committee that his hands are not altogether clean in this matter. As Leader of the Opposition, the Prime Minister was just as guilty of raising the electorate's expectations. He made a commitment to lone parents that he would not force through the changes. When asked on ``The World at One'' on BBC Radio 4 whether he would stick to plans to equalise benefit payments, which included the proposal in clause 68, he replied:

    ``No . . . we believe we can avoid that situation within the existing budgets.''

In saying that, he went even further than his right hon. Friend the Secretary of State, because he explained that the Labour party would not need to implement the previous Government's proposals despite its commitment to keep to their spending ceilings for the first two years in government. So the Prime Minister and the Secretary of State both made personal pledges that the cuts would not be introduced. Both are guilty of breaking those pledges and both are condemned by that breach of faith.
