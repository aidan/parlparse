Amendment Nos. 49 and 65 are about fish farming. I suspect that some people may wonder why fish farming involves the use of land if it always takes place in water. That is not always the case, because fish farms are often found in above ground tanks or hatcheries, and some rearing, particularly of trout, takes place in such tanks, which may be located in areas that would otherwise be open country.

Fish farms are extremely vulnerable to disturbance. Fish are obviously valuable, and some people might wish to steal them if they had free access. There is a serious risk of infection, and fish are rather susceptible to disease. The farming operation could be disturbed. Fish farms need no buildings, and we would argue that the exclusion of a building would not, of itself, exclude fish farming. We argue that such land should be excepted.

We therefore argue that we should carefully consider the access provisions and that a similar approach should apply—that it should not be classified as open country. Clearly, it would be daft to stop up a footpath along a defined route in school grounds if the legislation defines the whole area as open country.

Amendment No. 53 raises an obvious and minimal, but none the less important, point. Land covered by satellite, television or radio transmitters should be excepted.

Amendment No. 54 relates to land comprising a scheduled ancient monument, and perhaps has wider implications than some of the other amendments. Scheduled monuments are of national importance, and I am sure that we would all wish them to be maintained for future generations. The Ancient Monuments and Archaeological Areas Act 1979 provides for public access to monuments held under the Act. Many monuments are in private hands, but English Heritage and local authorities are able to extend access by entering into guardianship agreements. If the Bill were to extend access, that could result in ancient monuments being damaged, which the Government have recognised by providing for exclusions under clause 24 to preserve ancient monuments. However, I argue that it would be more sensible to exclude scheduled monuments from the regime entirely, so that voluntary agreements could be developed, site by site. Otherwise, without rehearsing previous debates about remaining on or walking over, it is possible that some fringe organisations will want to use ancient monuments for ritual purposes.

Amendment No. 141 would insert into the schedule

    Land designated as a Site of Special Scientific Interest.

Such land is a matter of principle that will be discussed later during our consideration of the Bill. Perhaps more than any other matter, it highlights the potential—I stress the word ``potential''—conflict in the Bill between access and conservation. Inevitably, a balance must be found. My view is that conservation should always take precedent, although others may hold different views. There is little point in extending protection for sites of special scientific interest elsewhere in the Bill if such areas can be wandered over by people using their right of access.

A large proportion of heather moorland is under some form of protection, although not necessarily under. SSSI measures; such land could be a special protection area, for instance. Heather moorland sites contain some of our most delicate habitats and are home to many protected or endangered species, including the Dartford warbler, the Glanville fritillary and early gentian on the Isle of Wight. Six of the birds on Europe's red list of the most threatened birds are moorland breeding birds, while a further six feature on the amber list. Such birds have a long breeding season, and we shall discuss them further later.

Article 3 of the 1985 EEC directive on endangered birds states:

    Member States shall take the requisite measures to preserve, maintain or re-establish areas of habitats for all of these birds.

Are the Government are satisfied that those European obligations will not be harmed by the Bill if SSSIs are not excluded? Clause 24 allows for exclusion of access to SSSIs, but I think that it would be much better to rule them out altogether.

Amendment No. 59 relates to the issue of curtilage. The Bill defines land as having a curtilage, but land does not have a curtilage—a building has a curtilage— which is why we have suggested removing ``such land'' and inserting ``a building''. The Court of Appeal recently confirmed in the Skerritts case that no piece of land could be in the curtilage of more than one building. Paragraph 2 risks an error by referring to

    Land covered by buildings or the curtilage of such land.

It would be safer to refer to the curtilage of a building, which is the purpose of amendment No. 59.
