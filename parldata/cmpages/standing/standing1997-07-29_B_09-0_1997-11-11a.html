Amendment No. 248 seeks to remove clause 27 (4), which puts beyond doubt the fact that restrictions on arrears will also apply where the commissioner or court determines that regulations are invalid, or what is known as ultra vires.

The Secretary of State makes regulations on the basis of her understanding of the powers laid down by Parliament. It is rare for that power to be misunderstood, but where it happens, there are well established procedures for handling those cases. In 1989, in the Cotton case, a tribunal of commissioners established that restriction on arrears may be applied in such cases.

Amendments Nos. 249 and 271 seek to exclude decisions of the European Court of Justice from the restrictions that I described earlier. The Bill makes it clear that decisions of the European Court of Justice are determinations by a court, so similar restrictions on arrears apply. That is not a new provision. The words are in the Bill to clarify existing legislation. The amendment seeks to exempt those judgments and would result in potential unlimited backdating of arrears for some people.

Clause 27 may not be popular, but it is fair. It treats all relevant determinations in the same way. It would be unfair to exempt the decisions of the European Court of Justice, and it could also result in the court's becoming clogged up with cases as people try to circumvent the restrictions.

In the light of what I have said, I urge the hon. Member for Newbury to withdraw the amendment, but I will elaborate later on Government amendment No. 71, which covers the points raised by amendments Nos. 245 and 246.

Mr. Steve Webb (Northavon): I beg to move amendment No. 273, in page 18, leave out lines 18 to 26 and insert--

    `(7) For the avoidance of doubt, a decision of a Commissioner or the court in a claimant's favour on a point of law is not a relevant determination unless it is the first occasion on which a Commissioner or the court has determined the point of law in favour of any claimant.'.

    Subsections (7) and (8) of the clause are designed to allow regulations to prescribe how the date of a relevant determination is to be determined. A decision could have been made by commissioners, a court or the House of Lords, and any date could be the relevant date. Subsection (8)(b), however, allows a discretion to permit the last date on which a decision is made to be the relevant date.

To return to the case of Halliday, which we discussed this morning, at each stage the decision was in favour of the appellant. Commissioners, those involved at the next stage and finally, the House of Lords, all agreed that money was due to the appellant. It is therefore rather odd for the Government to say to the appellant that, although the first decision is proved right, that date will not be counted as the relevant one, and that they will wait for the final decision. People could be deprived of one or two years' money, even though the first decision was right and proven to be so. That relates to the point made a few moments ago by my hon. Friend the Member for Newbury.

The purpose of the amendment is to remove subsections (7) and (8), which allow the earlier or the later date to be chosen at whim, and to require that, if at every stage the verdict goes in favour of the appellant, the first occasion on which that happens should be deemed to be the relevant date.

 

Mr. Keith Bradley: As members of the Committee will know, clause 27 is not a new provision. It serves the same purpose as sections 68 and 69 of the Social Security Administration Act 1992 but it will make that provision clearer and, we hope, less prone to argument about the intention behind the legislation.

Clause 27 restricts arrears of benefit to similar cases following a decision on a lead case by the commissioner or the courts. I make no apology for that. The extent of the arrears in those similar cases depends on the date of the relevant determination, and which decision is the relevant determination is obviously important. Subsections (7) and (8) provide for regulation-making powers to determine that date.

The regulation-making powers are important, as different cases can be identified in different ways. For example, a commissioner may decide a case one way but on appeal, the Court of Appeal may take the opposite view, while the House of Lords may decide that the commissioner's interpretation is correct. In such cases, regulations will specify the date of the commissioner's decision as the date of the relevant determination. Amendment No. 273 would, however, apply that simple rule to all cases, even though some decisions are not sufficiently clear cut.

Sometimes the points of law that the courts consider will be different from those addressed by the commissioner--perhaps something in the commissioner's reasoning will raise a point of law for the court which is not a point of law before the commissioner, or maybe the court will identify a different point of law altogether. The regulation-making powers are important if we are to accommodate the different points at which the commissioner or court interprets a statute with the effect of awarding or increasing benefit. Regulations are necessary to build in such flexibility for complex cases, and I hope that my explanation will allow the hon. Gentleman to withdraw his amendment.

Clause 31

Incapacity for Work

Mr. Keith Bradley: I beg to move amendment No. 74, in page 20, line 29, at end insert--

    `( ) Provision may be made by regulations for matters of such descriptions as may be prescribed to be determined by the Secretary of State, notwithstanding that other matters fall to be determined by another authority.'.

Clause 31 essentially re-enacts subsection (2) of section 61A of the Social Security Administration Act 1992. The amendment ensures that the clause also re-enacts subsection (3) of section 61A, which was omitted when the Bill was first published, as I am sure that all members of the Committee noticed.

The amendment inserts a new subsection (2) into the clause. It provides the power to make regulations that enable the Secretary of State to decide whether someone is incapable of work, when the question arises in the context of a claim for a disability premium in housing benefit or council tax benefit. All other aspects of claims for these benefits are determined by local authorities. The regulations under the existing power will continue in force under the new power.

The amendment ensures that the issue of whether a person is capable of work continues to be determined by officers with the right expertise. It ensures consistency in all the relevant benefits. I commend it to the Committee.

Amendment agreed to.

Clause 31, as amended, ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Clause 32

Industrial Diseases

Mr. Keith Bradley: I beg to move amendment No. 75, in page 20, line 36, leave out subsection (2).

The amendment removes a provision that is no longer needed. Clause 32, which relates to industrial diseases, re-enacts section 62 of the Social Security Administration Act 1992, with minor changes to reflect the decision-making provisions in the Bill. Subsection (2) is a re-enactment of section 62(2)(b) of that Act, which provides for the making of regulations related to the payment of fees in connection with medical examinations.

The medical examinations in question were examinations by Department of Social Security doctors of persons in occupations in which there was a risk of contracting pneumoconiosis. They were conducted when the person took up the occupation, and at regular intervals while they remained in it. The regulations providing for these examinations, and the fees payable by the employer for such examinations, were revoked in 1994 because they duplicated regulations made under the Health and Safety at Work, etc. Act 1974. It follows that the provision in clause 32(2) is no longer required. I invite the Committee to agree to its removal.

Amendment agreed to.

Mr. Webb: The clause relates to the Child Support Agency and the concept of staying a decision or appeal, which we discussed on clauses 25 and 26 in relation to social security benefits. There might be a case for staying a decision on a social security benefit pending a test case on the grounds that it would save the Government money. The already slow and cumbersome appeals and decision-making process of the Child Support Agency needs stays like it needs a hole in the head. A stay will further add to the uncertainty surrounding the maintenance assessment in respect of the child. The Government are keen to encourage lone parents to move from welfare into work. One facet that would help a lone parent to make that move is certainty about her maintenance entitlement. If she is to take, for example, the sort of low-paid job that would be boosted by family credit and maintenance, and each of those elements is part of her in-work income package, she needs certainty about the maintenance element. Clause 43 proposes that she might have to hang on for six or 12 months while a lead case is decided. Even then, we do not know whether it will produce more maintenance or less. Mr. Keith Bradley: The clause allows decisions relating to child support to be held back pending resolution of the lead case. It mirrors the social security provisions of clauses 25 and 26.

Currently, cases and appeals must be decided even where we know that the decision rests on a point of law being challenged in another case. The clause enables us to delay making decisions, or to make a partial decision, until the other, lead case has been decided. Where a case depends on a lead case, clause 43 will give the CSA two options for dealing with it.

The agency may delay the look-alike case entirely, so that it does not have to make a decision about it until the lead case has been decided. That may be an appropriate course of action if the lead case will be decided in a short time, and it will be advantageous both to the agency and to the client. That has two implications. First, obviating the need to make a decision on a case and then revise that decision once the lead case has been decided will minimise unnecessary work. The agency will then be able to make more effective use of its resources.

Secondly, and more importantly, it means that clients will be spared the inconvenience of having a decision made on their cases that will then have to be revisited, perhaps resulting in maintenance debt and the need to recover money from the parent with care. However, when it is likely to take some time to decide a lead case, it would not normally be appropriate for a decision to be delayed, as that would mean that the parent with care would have to wait months or even years--as the hon. Member for Northavon (Mr. Webb) rightly pointed out--before receiving any maintenance for her child. In such circumstances, the agency would seek to make use of the provisions in the clause to make an interim assessment, which will result in a safe rate liability maintenance.

That will ensure that the parent with care receives some maintenance while the lead case is pending, and that the absent parent does not pay more than is right. The clause will ensure that the agency can make decisions on cases that rest on lead cases in a manner that is appropriate to the individual case. That will enable it to make best use of its resources and to provide the best possible service to the client.

I hope that that explanation will enable the hon. Member for Northavon to support the clause.

Mr. Webb: I am grateful to the Minister for dealing with our anxiety about the clause, and I am reassured by the Minister's confirmation that, in practice if not explicitly in the Bill, only lead cases that are decided in short order will result in a stay.     Clause 45

    Vaccine Damage Payments: Decisions Superseding Earlier Decisions

Mr. Keith Bradley: I beg to move amendment No. 182, in page 30, line 32, leave out `superseded' and insert `reversed'.

The amendment clarifies the procedure for changing vaccine damage payment decisions made both by the Secretary of State and by an appeal tribunal. A vaccine damage payment is not a social security benefit. Using the word ``reversed'' as opposed to ``superseded'' clearly distinguishes vaccine damage payments from social security benefits. More importantly, the term ``reversed'' better describes what happens when a vaccine damage decision is changed.

A vaccine damage payment is a one-off payment--it is either awarded or it is not. If it is not, and the payment is made at a later date, it is clear that the original decision has been reversed. The amendment is a straightforward clarification of the Bill's drafting, and I commend it to the Comittee.
