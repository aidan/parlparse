Mr. Moss: One of the amendments would remove the specified fine from the Bill and replace it with a level on the standard scale. The other would necessarily lower the maximum level of fine from £20,000 to £5,000, which is level 5 on the standard scale and provide the court with the option of imposing a custodial sentence as well as, or instead of, a fine.

It is unusual for a specific fine to be built into a Bill. More commonly, fines are prescribed with regard to the level on the standard scale, which avoids the need for changes to primary legislation when the scale is revised. We submit that £20,000 is a hefty fine. That amount is typically imposed on corporate bodies-not on individuals-for perpetrating offences. Corporate manslaughter, video piracy, environmental offences such as pollution-particularly river pollution-and so forth are examples. It is for such offences that a fine-only punishment, rather than a combination of fine and/or imprisonment, is usually available.

Most fur farmers are, of course, individuals, rather than corporate entities. The punishment in the Bill is therefore unsuitable. By comparison, the maximum punishment provided in the Breeding of Sale of Dogs (Welfare) Act 1999 is three months imprisonment and/or a fine of up to £2,500.

The offences in the Bill are summary offences, which are triable only in a magistrates court. The maximum penalty is again severe when the defendant has no right to a trial by jury. In some circumstances, a term of imprisonment would be a more suitable than a fine. The court should have the discretion to impose a sentence that it deems appropriate in the light of the individual circumstances of the offence. The amendments would therefore change the scale and nature of the fine and would allow the courts the flexibility to impose a prison sentence, if appropriate.
