The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson): I am confused as to whether the Opposition welcome or condemn the clause, which is essential in the transfer of the Post Office's property to the Post Office plc. It repeats various provisions from previous legislation, and is at the heart of the provisions to convert the Post Office from a statutory corporation to a company governed by the Companies Acts. The conversion is one of the essential elements of the reform package. As has been said, it underlines the commercialisation of the Post Office.

The clause permits the Secretary of State to make an order setting the day for the transfer under clause 62, so that the property, rights and liabilities of the Post Office are transferred to a company formed and registered under the Companies Acts. The clause also requires that the company to which the transfer is made is wholly owned by the Crown at the date of vesting. All being well, that date is expected to be 26 March 2001, which is the last day of the Post Office's financial year.

The transfer will effectively change the status of the Post Office from a statutory corporation to a public limited company. The hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire (Mr. Page) is right: it will underline the new commercial freedoms of the Post Office and its new arm's length relationship with Government. The transfer will give the Post Office the added certainty of operating as a plc in a recognised business environment in which, like its private sector competitors, it will be governed by the full range of company law. The transfer will also underline the accountability of the directors and the new commercial status of the Post Office, and help clarify the separate roles of Government and the board. There is no question of our giving the Post Office privileged status. The hon. Gentleman mentioned debates that we shall have in future, but in this debate we are talking about the transfer of Post Office property and other paraphernalia into a new company. I reassure the Committee that the clause can safely stand part of the Bill.
