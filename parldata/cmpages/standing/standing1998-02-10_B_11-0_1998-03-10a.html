Police (Northern Ireland) Bill

Clause 45

Research and Advice

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): Welcome back to the Chair, Miss Widdecombe. I shall speak briefly, as we had an important debate on clause 45 at the end of our last sitting when we discussed amendment No. 93. The Minister rejected my amendment on the basis that the research to which clause 45 refers was such that the Secretary of State would wish to contribute to it on a United Kingdom-wide basis rather than having research specific to Northern Ireland. Although research would apply to the Northern Ireland police service and the Royal Ulster Constabulary, it would be used to contribute to wider research projects.

I see the merit in that, as important lessons can be learned from the operational activities of the RUC and of the whole police service in Northern Ireland. They face one of the world's best organised terrorist groups and have had considerable experience during the troubles of dealing with civil unrest and terrorist activity. There is great merit in using the lessons learnt and the expertise and skill of the RUC and the rest of the police service for wider research projects.

However, the Bill ought to give the Police Authority--or the Secretary of State in consultation with the authority--the ability to become involved in Province-wide research. Clause 45 does not make it clear that that is to be the case. The Committee on the Administration of Justice, in its submission on the Bill, said:

    ``The value of policing research bodies was so obvious in all of the jurisdictions CAJ visited, we would propose making this a duty. Furthermore, we propose that this be a duty imposed on the Police Authority rather than the Secretary of State.''

The committee put forward some ideas on amendments which went much further than those the Opposition have suggested. We thought that the Secretary of State could, after consultation with the Police Authority, set up bodies to undertake research. The CAJ believes that that should be the responsibility of the Police Authority, not the Secretary of State.

Clause 2(3) places a heavy responsibility on the Police Authority. It states:

    ``The Police Authority shall secure that the Northern Ireland Police Service is efficient and effective.''

In the context of that overarching duty, the Police Authority will need to establish research bodies to ascertain whether it fulfils the duty that that provision outlines. We believe that clause 45 should refer to the Police Authority's involvement in and influence on research. The CAJ goes even further and claims that undertaking research should be the prerogative of the Police Authority.

We agree with the Minister that it is important for Northern Ireland to be part of the wider research sphere, but I do not understand why that cannot go hand in hand with allowing the Police Authority to establish research bodies. The measure should contain in further reference to research. I leave it to the Minister to decide whether the addition can be made in later clauses or in the schedules.

Research is important, and is deemed important by the Police Authority, which wishes to be involved in it. That is right and proper. Although we did not push our amendment to a Division, the Minister should reconsider the matter and give the Police Authority powers to instigate research. It needs to do that to fulfil its duties under clause (2)(3).

I did not attend our previous sitting because I was engaged on ministerial duties at the intergovernmental conference in Dublin, and the transcripts of the Committee's proceedings do not always give the full flavour of the exchanges.

However, given that I was not privy to the full flow of the discussion, I undertake to consider the matter further. The Police Authority has the right and responsibility to carry out research. In the case of a major investigation, instigated by research sponsored by the Secretary of State, consultation between the Secretary of State and the Police Authority would occur. There would be communication between the Secretary of State and the Police Authority about the necessity of research and the purpose of an inquiry. Such matters would be in the public domain irrespective of consultation.

I shall take advice and consider the matter further on Report, although I do not believe that the addition that the hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire (Mr. Moss) proposes is necessary.

Clause 46

Expenditure by Secretary of State for police purposes

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.45 am

Mr. Moss: The wording of the clause, which is very short, mirrors that used in section 57 of the Police Act 1996, although there is a slight rewording: the legislation for England and Wales uses the word ``considers'' but the Bill uses the word ``thinks''. The Bill states:

    ``The Secretary of State . . . may make such contribution . . . as he thinks necessary''.

I do not know why the word ``considers'' was changed; in this context, pretty well all the other words in the 1996 Act were used in the Bill. Perhaps there is a subtle difference between ``considers'' and ``thinks''. If so, I hope that the Minister will enlighten us about it. I could not justify tabling an amendment on the matter.

Section 57(3) and (4) of the 1996 Act give the Secretary of State powers to make regulations. Subsection (3) states:

    ``The Secretary of State may by regulations make provision for requiring all police forces in England and Wales to use specified facilities or services, or facilities or services of a specified description''.

Subsection (4) continues:

    ``Before making such regulations under this section, the Secretary of State shall consult . . . persons whom he considers to represent the interests of police authorities, and . . . persons whom he considers to represent the interests of chief officers of police.''

He will consult the organisation representing police authorities and the Association of Chief Police Officers of England, Wales and Northern Ireland before making regulations, which refer to services.

Clause 46 states:

    ``The Secretary of State may . . . make such contribution to the provision or maintenance of such organisations, facilities and services; and . . . make such other payments . . . as he thinks necessary''.

When is it deemed unnecessary to include in the Bill the provisions in subsections (3) and (4) of section 57 of the 1996 Act, which give the Secretary of State the power to make regulations? He or she would be able to ensure that the Northern Ireland police service was involved with the organisations, facilities and services that he or she established. Much of the wording of the 1996 Act has been transferred to the Bill, and I cannot think why there should be a difference in this case.

An important power seems to have been set on one side--although I am not necessarily asking for the Secretary of State to have more powers. Most of the Bill's provisions give the Secretary of State powers that the Home Secretary in England and Wales does not enjoy. That important power has been omitted either because it is deemed to be unnecessary or because the matter has been overlooked. I look forward to the Minister's response.

 

Mr. Ingram: I do not have a quick answer for the hon. Gentleman, so I shall write to him and to other Committee members to explain the subtleties involved. The clause is intended to make the situation clearer. When Bills are drafted, people ask whether a particular phrase is clear, and whether it could be made clearer. The change from ``consider'' to ``thinks'' falls into the latter category. I had understood previously that it was being argued that too much power was accruing to the Secretary of State. It is interesting that the hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire is now implying that more power should be transferred to the Secretary of State.

Mr. Moss: No, I was asking why there was a difference.

Mr. Ingram: I refer the hon. Gentleman to clause 40, which concerns the power to require use by police of specified facilities, equipment or services. That is relevant to what the hon. Gentleman was saying about section 57 of the 1996 Act. If that proves inadequate to deal with the matter, and there proves to have been an omission that weakens the Bill, I shall, having heard the arguments that have been advanced in Committee, take any necessary action on Report. However, I do not think that any omission has occurred, as clause 40 replicates the function of section 57 of the Police Act 1996.

Clause 47

Annual and other reports by Police Authority to Secretary of State

Mr. Moss: I beg to move amendment No. 94, in page 22, line 37, leave out `4' and insert `5'.

This is a probing amendment. Clauses 47, 48 and 49 deal with reports of various kinds. There is a parallel provision, section 9, in the Police Act 1996. Clause 47 is a hybrid; it incorporates that provision, and also section 43, in subsections (4), (5) and (6). I do not argue against the fact that it brings together borrowings from earlier Bills. That probably makes sense.

Section 15 of the Police Act (Northern Ireland) 1970 also relates to annual and other reports. Subsection (1) states:

    ``The Inspector General shall, as soon as possible after the end of each calender year, submit to the Police Authority a general report in writing on the carrying out by the police force of their functions during that year and the Authority shall transmit that report or a copy thereof to the Minister who shall lay a copy before each House of [the Parliament of the United Kingdom].''

The provision now in force requires the Chief Constable to submit the report to the Police Authority, which in turn submits it to the Secretary of State. There is no current requirement for the Police Authority to prepare its own report. Clause 47 is new in that respect. It mirrors the 1996 Act and is very important. It concerns the way in which the Police Authority will be seen to be accountable for its duties under clause 2. It is to be hoped that the public will be able to scrutinise the report and reach their own conclusions. Subsection (2)(a) alludes specifically to an assessment of how the annual policing plan, which we discussed at great length under clause 17, has been carried out. Under subsection 2(a), the annual report after the end of each financial year performs the vital role of showing the extent to which the aims and targets of the annual policing plan have been met.

The 1996 and 1970 Acts do not specify how many months after the end of each financial year the report shall be issued. The Bill specifies that it shall be issued

    ``not later than four months''

after the end of each financial year. The 1996 Act simply says that the Police Authority shall issue that report ``as soon as possible'' after the end of each financial year, as does the 1970 Act in relation to each calendar year.

Someone has decided that four months is the appropriate time scale. Why should it not be three or five months? Why not retain the wording of the 1970 Act or the England and Wales legislation, which would be reasonable and would mirror the present Northern Ireland and England and Wales legislation. The reason why Northern Ireland should be any different escapes me. No doubt the Minister will enlighten us as to why clause 47(1) specifies four moths.

The 1970 Act specifies that the Chief Constable's report should go the Secretary of State via the Police Authority. Section 15(1) of that Act specifically states that the Minister

    ``shall lay a copy before each House of [the Parliament of the United Kingdom].''

There is no such requirement for the Police Authority's report to be similarly treated under clause 47. Clause 47(1) requires that the Police Authority shall

    ``issue a report relating to the policing of Northern Ireland for the year.''

Under subsection (3)(b), the Police Authority shall

    ``send a copy of the report to the Secretary of State.''

The Secretary of State is not required--there is certainly no such requirement under clause 47--to lay that report before either House of Parliament, to send it to the Library, or, it would seem, to make it available to the public. That is an incredible oversight.

If I may move ahead to clauses 48 and 49, which happen to deal with the Chief Constable's report, I shall point out that it is strange that clause 49(5) requires the Secretary of State to

    ``lay before both Houses of Parliament every report submitted to him under subsection (4).''

Subsection (4) refers to the Chief Constable's report as defined in clause 48(1). What is the reason for that inconsistency? Clause 49 states that the Chief Constable's report, which goes to the Police Authority and the Secretary of State, shall be laid before Parliament by the Secretary of State. Yet the Police Authority's report, which is just as important--if not more important--is not required to be laid before either House of Parliament. Under the 1970 Act that report is supposed to be laid before Parliament. There is an inconsistency there.

I have not been able to find other provisions in the Bill under which reports of the Police Authority should be laid before Parliament.

Section 15(1) of the Police Act (Northern Ireland) 1970 says that the Chief Constable should submit a general report in writing to the Police Authority, which

    ``shall transmit that report or a copy thereof to the''

Secretary of State. There is a clear intention in existing legislation that the report should go first to the Police Authority, which should then hand it on to the Secretary of State. The report does not go simultaneously to each. The Bill is writing a change into Northern Ireland legislation. Does not the current system work? Why is the existing reporting system deemed unsatisfactory? Why change it?

Mr. Hunter: Clause 49 allows the Secretary of State to require the Chief Constable to produce reports on

    ``matters connected with the policing of Northern Ireland''.

It replaces, with some amendments, the provisions of section 15 of the 1970 Act. In principle, I do not quarrel with what clause 49 is intended to achieve. I see nothing wrong with it.

The Opposition's quarrel is not with the theme of the Clause or with subsection (1), which requires the Chief Constable to produce reports on demand, so to speak, from the Secretary of State; it is not with subsection (2), which deals with the report's format and the time scale within which it must be produced; it is not even with subsection (3), which deals with the report's publication; it is with subsection (4), which amendments Nos. 96, 98 and 97 would change.

We believe that a principle, which would be enacted by the amendments, is at stake, and that amendment No. 96 strikes at its heart. It returns us to the familiar ground of the relationship between the three parts of the tripartite structure--the Secretary of State, the Chief Constable and the Police Authority--which we have discussed many times. We argue that the relationship between those three parts of the structure must be fair and balanced, and that failure to achieve fairness and balance would undermine the Bill. It is essential that no one part of the tripartite structure has undue influence over the other two.

The Minister will be aware from previous debates that we are concerned that the Secretary of State's functions are too great at certain key points in the Bill, and that the authority's and the Chief Constable's functions are correspondingly weakened. My hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire argued that the Chief Constable's annual report to the Police Authority should be precisely that--it should be his report, which he should give to the authority, and nothing should alter that situation. It is right and proper for the authority to be the first body to receive and to consider that report. If it were submitted at the same time to the Police Authority and to the Secretary of State, it would immediately cease to be the Chief Constable's report to the Police Authority.

I must give more emphasis than my hon. Friend did to the fact that if the report went at the same time to the Secretary of State and to the Police Authority it would be theoretically possible for the Secretary of State--advertently or inadvertently--to influence the authority's reaction, which would be wrong. In fulfilling its statutory obligations to secure an efficient and effective police service in Northern Ireland, the authority should, among other things, reach its own conclusions on the Chief Constable's annual report. It should not be influenced by the Secretary of State's perceived or known reactions. That is why I said that it would be wrong if the Secretary of State--advertently or inadvertently--made known her response to the Chief Constable's report, and thereby influenced the Police Authority's reaction. That would undermine the value of the Chief Constable's report and of the Police Authority's reaction to it.

Amendment No. 96 would redress the excessive powers and functions of the Secretary of State in one important respect. It would preserve the integrity of the Chief Constable's annual report and the integrity of the Police Authority's response to it.

Amendment No. 98 is consequential to amendment No. 96, and I do not believe that it requires any further explanation. Amendment No. 97, however, is not unimportant. It underlines the argument that the Chief Constable's report to the Police Authority is precisely that, by differentiating his annual report from other reports which may be required of him by the Secretary of State. According to subsection (3), the Secretary of State may dictate the manner in which those reports are to be published. That should not be so in the case of the Chief Constable's annual report: the Chief Constable should determine the manner in which that is to be published.

The amendments are important; they would help to ensure a fair and reasonable balance within the tripartite structure. I hope that the Government will acknowledge that and respond positively.
