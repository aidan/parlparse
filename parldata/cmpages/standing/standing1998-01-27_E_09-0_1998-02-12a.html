Clause 16

Provision of information etc. to the secretary of state

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Yeo: This is a fairly short and, I hope, uncontroversial clause. However, it is potentially far-reaching in that the Secretary of State may require RDAs to provide him

    "with such information, advice and assistance as he may"

see fit. Can the Minister give us some examples of the information, advice or assistance that the Secretary of State is likely to require? We should like some clue to the Government's thinking on that.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Angela Eagle): As the hon. Gentleman has rightlypointed out, the aim of the clause is uncontroversial in simply allowing the Secretary of State to be provided

    "with such information, advice and assistance as he may require"

to do the jobs laid down, to issue directions and to ensure that RDAs keep within guidelines. It is potentially about everything that an RDA does in its region and about whatever the Secretary of State requires in order to give directions and guidance. It is as all-encompassing as that.

    "A regional development agency shall provide the Secretary of State with such information, advice and assistance as he may require."

The northern region complained that investment has been attracted to Wales and that the north would suffer. Investment may become more difficult to attract because of the interest rate in the United Kingdom, which is affected by the exchange rate. Angela Eagle: The right hon. Gentleman raises an extremely important issue. He will know of the legacy that the previous Government left us. The work of attracting inward investors to the regions is currently done by regional development organisations, which work out of the Department of Trade and Industry rather than the Department of the Environment,[Angela Eagle] Transport and the Regions. The cost to regions of inward investment may be bidded up the regions might have secured such investment at a lower price if bidding had not taken place and I am sure that all Committee members agree that that has to stop. For the enlightenment of the hon. Member for South Suffolk (Mr. Yeo), who asked for examples of the kinds of information that the Secretary of State might require, the Committee should be mindful that the Secretary of State is accountable to Parliament for the RDA's actions. He or she will need to gather information to fulfil that duty and to inform himself or herself in order to make decisions on the future direction of policy. The Secretary of State might want information about the amount of land available for regeneration in a particular region. Alternatively, he might want a profile of an RDA's employees for inclusion in an annual departmental report, or to compile any information that the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions might want to make available to Parliament and the public. The clause attempts to ensure that no RDA would try to conceal such matters from the Secretary of State.

Clause 17

Annual report

Mr. Yeo: I beg to move amendment No. 118, in page 7, line 28, leave out

    "As soon as reasonably practicable after"

and insert

    "Within three months of".

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 119, in page 7, line 37, leave out "Following" and insert

    "Not less than 14 days after".

Mr. Yeo: Clause 17 deals with the important matter of annual reports, which we touched on during a debate on an earlier amendment. The public will pick up the costs of regional development agencies in one form or another and it is their interests, in theory at least, that the regional development agencies exist to serve. The annual reports are likely to be the public's main source of information about the RDA's activities in their region. The value of that information will lie largely, but not totally, in its quality. Amendment No. 124 sought to ensure that the quality of the information in the annual reports was high and the Minister gave some assurances on that.

Part of the value of the annual reports and the information which the public will receive from those reports will lie in their time limits. I have had to draw attention to some of the gaps and limited information in the White Paper. I shall not labour the point, but the figures quoted for gross domestic product per capita were five years out of date. Since the Committee started its deliberations, I have obtained some up-to-date figures for the English regions. The Committee may be interested to know that during the three years following the year that was chosen for the figures in the White Paper, the gross domestic product per capita in some English regions rose by 16 per cent. in three years. That is a very fast rate of growth indeed. It seems at least possible to put it no more strongly that if the English regions were achieving such a growth rate during the years 1994, 1995 and 1996, they may have a slightly better showing compared with the European Union average at the end of 1996 than they had in 1993, the year chosen for the information in the White Paper.

I have used that point to illustrate the importance of time limits when evaluating the quality of information in a document. It is interesting to note the regions which performed well. GDP per capita rose by 16 per cent. in the west midlands and 15 per cent. in Yorkshire and the Humber. In my own area the eastern region where there is very limited demand for a regional development agency, the growth in GDP per capita was in excess of 16 per cent. during those three years. In the present context, I wish to emphasise that annual reports should be up to date if they are to be of any value.

Mr. David Taylor (North-West Leicestershire): The hon. Gentleman's point is interesting, but does he not agree that the years to which he is referring 1993-96 followed the depths of what some might call a Conservative-inspired recession. Is not that expected in a recovery phase? If an area such as the west midlands I live within five minutes of its borders can do as well as that without the RDA structure, how much better it will be when there is a concerted planning effort.

Mr. Yeo: Who knows what will happen when an RDA is established? In my judgment, the impact of RDAs on GDP per capita in regions such as the west midlands, the eastern region and the south-east will be minimal or even negative. It remains to be seen.

The crucial point is that the Government chose to present to the public in support of their case for RDAs information which was five years out of date. To make any claim about relative economic performance in different regions of England on the basis of figures that are five years out of date is extremely unsatisfactory. I have managed to pull away some of the lawyers of secrecy that appear to cover that kind of information and to establish that some English regions performed extremely well in growth-rate terms in 1994, 1995 and 1996. I hope that the Government, with all the resources at their command, will, before the end of the Committee or Report stages of the Bill, produce figures for elsewhere in the European Union. Even figures for 1994 or 1995 would be a start.

I return to the need for annual reports to be timely if they are to serve much purpose. Amendment No. 118 seeks to specify a maximum time after the end of the year during which the RDAs shall prepare a report. The Bill says:

    "As soon as reasonably practicable".

That phrase is better than those used elsewhere which have left too much to the discretion of the Secretary of State. Even so, it would be better to have a clear timetable that left nothing to chance.

I dare say the Government will claim that they have done everything practicable to obtain up-to-date figures for the economic performance of European Union regions. Even if they did, the information provided is almost completely worthless. I do not want the same situation to arise when RDA reports are made available to the public.

Putting a maximum time into the Bill would be useful. I have suggested three months, which is adequate time in which to complete thoroughly the necessary work. That may even eliminate the need for the Secretary of State or his officials to chase progress. If the Government feel that three months is too short, we could accept four or six months as better than nothing. Anything is better than the Government thinking that information five years out of date can meet needs.

Amendment No. 119 deals with a separate question. At present, the clause provides for the RDA to send a report to the Secretary of State. The report does not go to the stakeholders of whom we heard so much a couple of days ago, or to the regional chamber that is supposed to have a special relationship with the RDA and almost to oversee RDA work. It does not go to democratically elected bodies in the RDA's region namely the local authorities. Perhaps not surprisingly in the light of the rest of the Bill, the report goes to the Secretary of State.

Clause 17(3) provides for the Secretary of State to lay the report before Parliament. Unfortunately, no time limit is specified. Under the terms of the clause, the Secretary of State could hold on to a report for weeks, months, even years before sending it to Parliament. I do not understand why that should be so. I would be less concerned had I received a more interested and co-operative response to my request for timely information on what the Government said was central to their justification for the existence of RDAs.

On Second Reading and when we debated the sittings motion two and a half weeks ago, the Minister said that one of the main reasons for setting up RDAs was to address the problem of English regions underperforming the European Union average by such a margin. I said that his information was out of date and suggested that greater efforts should be made to produce up-to-date information to allow us to examine[Mr. Yeo] whether the case, which might have existed in 1993, existed today. The Minister showed no interest, sympathy or concern. His dismissive and contemptuous attitude and response that Parliament of all places should want up-to-date information to test a theory that he had rehearsed on the Floor of the House and in Committee has driven me to press the matter.

We should not pass a Bill that allows the Secretary of State to sit on an annual report for an indefinite period. He has the power to appoint, remove and compensate members of the board. If he found a report unsatisfactory for example, it might embarrass the Government he could sit on it for six months, in which time some changes might have been made to the RDA board. He could then send the report back and have a few changes made. The scope for preventing the public from receiving timely information, which is made possible by the subsection, is a serious matter which is open to significant abuse.

Why do the Government believe it necessary to allow the Secretary of State to have that indefinite period? Is it in danger of being used to tinker with the report's contents? What changes might the Government make to reports in the indefinite period? If they do not intend to make changes, they cannot object to my amendment. It would ensure that the annual report was quickly passed on to the House after it had been received by the Minister. Fourteen days is adequate time for the Minister's private office to scrutinise the report's contents and to brief him on anything about which he may be questioned.

The Minister for the Regions, Regeneration and Planning (Mr. Richard Caborn): I shall deal briefly with the statistics in the White Paper. We have gone back to Eurostat. It has not produced more up-to-date figures. We have also made a number of calculations. Even with the growth suggested by the hon. Member for South Suffolk, the position of English regions relative to European regions is the same. He was right to say that we need accurate and reliable dates.

I am not going to speculate on the relative position of the English regions. We will publish new figures as soon as they are available from Eurostat. The difficulty is in getting statistics from member states. Eurostat has to factor them into the European Union, which takes a little longer. It is not for me to judge whether that is good or bad. If the hon. Gentleman thinks that we are basing the decision to establish RDAs on a single table in the White Paper, he is as far from the truth as some of his contributions, and that is a long way.

Most non-departmental public bodies are required to produce annual reports, and RDAs will be no different. Clause 17 will provide that their annual reports should be prepared as soon as is reasonably practical after the end of the accounting period in a form and content set by the Secretary of State. The report will be laid before both Houses of Parliament and published. That is in accordance with the procedure for all NDPBs.

I broadly agree with the intention behind the amendments. Although I cannot accept them, I hope that I can give the hon. Gentleman some assurances on the annual reports.

Amendment No. 118 would set a deadline of three months after the end of the accounting period within which an RDA's report must be produced. Of course, we shall want to see the annual report as quickly as possible. Normally, my Department likes to see the reports of its non-departmental public bodies by the end of July, but there may be circumstances in which that is not possible. For instance, I expect that we shall ask all RDAs to include in their reports the statement of accounts audited by the National Audit Office. I am sure that the hon. Member for South Suffolk acknowledges that that is a respected body and that it is therefore important that that information be included in annual reports. The amendment would leave a period of only three months within which the statement of accounts would have to be prepared by RDAs and audited by the National Audit Office. So although I hope that RDAs' annual reports will be produced within three months, I do not think that we should accept amendment No. 118.

Amendment No. 119 would require the Secretary of State to publish the reports and to lay them before both Houses of Parliament within 14 days of receiving them. There is no question of the Government's delaying the publication of information contained in annual reports, but it is not a sensible amendment. We may decide, for instance, to publish all the RDAs' reports on the same day, in which case we might have to wait more than 14 days for all of them to be received by the Department. If we accepted the amendment, the Secretary of State could then be subject to a judicial review which, in these circumstances, would be clearly inappropriate. With that explanation, I ask the hon. Member for South Suffolk to withdraw the amendment.

 

Mr. Yeo: The Minister referred to fresh calculations about economic performance and GDP per head for regions elsewhere in the European Union. Will he make those calculations available to members of the Committee? If he cannot do so today, will he write to us, and tell us today when we can expect to receive that information? For which years do they apply?

Mr. Caborn: On a point of order, Mr. Benton. I think that you received a copy of a two-page letter dated 31 January 1998, addressed to the hon. I shall ask my officials whether any further statistics are available that would guide the Committee, but I understand that the figures corresponding to the table to which the hon. I shall try to furnish the Committee with as much up-to-date information as possible.

Mr. Yeo: I am grateful for that assurance. I did indeed receive a letter, but wondered whether some additional information had become available in the past 12 days, in the light of the Minister's first response to the amendments.

I am encouraged to discover that the Government maintain an end-of-July target for the receipt of reports from non-departmental public bodies. In the light of that assurance, I am satisfied that it is not necessary to press amendment No. 118.

I am, however, still worried about the scope that the Bill allows for Ministers to sit on reports that they receive from RDAs. The Minister said that he might want to publish all reports on the same day. I am not sure what advantage that would give to people in the regions: if someone is anxious about the performance of the Eastern region, it is not satisfactory to be told that the report is not available because there is a delay in receiving the report from the Yorkshire and the Humber region.

An RDA may experience a problem, such as a dispute, or litigation arising from a transaction, that delays its report. It would not be satisfactory for several months to go by, and for people in other regions to be denied the opportunity to scrutinise what their RDA has done, just because it is administratively convenient to insist that all reports be available on the same day. In the light of that, although I am willing to seek leave to withdraw amendment No. 118, I shall press amendment No. 119 to a Division.

This system is more streamlined than the compulsory purchase order legislation and gives regeneration a chance to go ahead while preserving everyone's rights, as well as the transparency of the process.

Clause 20

Acquisition of land

Mr. Yeo: I beg to move amendment No. 144, in page 9, line 36, at end insert

    "provided that such land shall not include any part of an area which is designated as a national park or an area of outstanding natural beauty under the National Park and Access to the Countryside Act of 1949.".

The amendment has a modest and limited aim, to which few people could take exception. I certainly hope that the Committee will not do so, because the Opposition attach great importance to it.

I regret the fact that the Liberal Democrats are absent from this afternoon's sitting the clause is one of the most important in the Bill. It deals with the power of RDAs to acquire land, which they can do voluntarily, through negotiation with the owner, or compulsorily, using the established procedure. I agree with the Minister who said in our previous sitting that that procedure was extremely complicated. I agree it is certainly not without its flaws. The Government and the Opposition can find common ground and agree that the matter should have a fresh look. I shall certainly consider it in that light over the next few months, and I hope to make suggestions about how to improve it. I am sure that that was not the desired effect. The exchange of land procedure allows for the replacement of common land acquired for development. I can see no better way to ensuring that people are adequately compensated for any loss of amenity in those rare cases in which the RDA finds it necessary to acquire an area of common land.

Amendments Nos. 111 and 112 are aimed at an RDA that compulsorily acquires new rights over common land. The intention seems to be to prevent an RDA from using the deed poll procedure in the Compulsory Purchase Act 1965 to vest those rights in itself when it cannot obtain a deed of grant from the landowner.

The effect of the amendments seems to be somewhat limited, and I cannot see that they confer any significant benefit on common land. Nor can I see why the process for the compulsory acquisition of new rights over land should be made more difficult for RDAs. Such acquisitions are subject to the normal procedures laid down by the Acquisition of Land Act 1981, which provide for the notification of objections and holding of public inquiries. In the rare cases in which a compulsory purchase might involve common land, existing rights over the land will be an important consideration in determining whether the purchase should be allowed.

I do not envisage that RDAs will make wide use of these particular powers, but that is not a reason for removing them altogether from the Bill. Sufficient safeguards are provided in the Bill to ensure that people are not unduly disadvantaged by the RDAs' exercise of those powers.

Paragraph 4 of schedule 6 allows an RDA to disregard Acts relating to common land in general or Acts specifically regulating the area of common land which it has acquired. Without that provision, an RDA might need private legislation to amend or repeal Acts affecting the land which it has purchased, but it does not mean that the RDA can do exactly as it likes. Any use which it, or any person, makes of the land must be in line with planning permission. Schedule 6 does not affect any rights that people have under common law.

In providing statutory powers for bodies such as the RDAs, we must allow for rare occasions such as the purchase of common land. This does not mean that we expect wide use to be made of such powers it may be that they will never be used. But we have a duty to ensure that the tools are there to deal with such cases. Otherwise, when cases do arise we will be blamed for our lack of foresight. The important thing is that sufficient safeguards should be in place to ensure that the powers cannot be used unreasonably, and to provide that those affected can be properly compensated through the exchange of land.

I thank the hon. Gentleman for giving me the opportunity to put that on the record. I am sure that it will be interesting for those who read the report of our proceedings.
