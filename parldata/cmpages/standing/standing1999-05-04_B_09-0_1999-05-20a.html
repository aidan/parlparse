Clause 33

Maintenance payments

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 96, in page 16, line 40, leave out subsection (7).—[Mr. Heathcoat-Amory.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are considering amendment No. 97, in schedule 20, page 157, column 3, leave out lines 23 to 28.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): At the end of this morning's sitting we were debating the proposed withdrawal of tax relief on maintenance payments. I was complaining about the inadequacy of the Paymaster General's response to our debate and, in particular, about the excuse that she offered that the withdrawal of married couples allowance left an unfairness, because the existing tax relief on maintenance payments would reward marriages that had broken down. I was pointing out that that is a bizarre justification. It amounts to saying that, because the Government have penalised one set of taxpayers, it is unfair if they do not penalise another. We do not agree that it was right to withdraw married couples allowances, but that does not justify the withdrawal of tax relief on maintenance payments.

The Government seem obsessed with creating a quality of misery for all taxpayers, believing that an increase in one area justifies an increase in another. That is faulty logic, particularly as there is no necessary or close connection between the two allowances. The fact that married couples have until now received an allowance called married couples allowance does not mean that its demise necessitates the withdrawal of a separate tax relief on maintenance payments.

Another excuse that was offered by several Labour Members was that it was simply another tax change and that those suffering could alter their affairs and change their savings or tax liabilities to suit the new situation. Such arguments have been advanced in a number of other debates, notoriously when the Government abolished the repayment of dividend tax credits to non-taxpayers. The Government said that those affected could switch their investments out of shares and into other forms of savings. That may technically be possible, but they were referring to possibly the poorest and least sophisticated group of savers in Britain—often elderly people who have a few shares. It is inappropriate to expect them to rush off and consult an adviser to take out individual savings accounts or whatever it may be.

If that was a somewhat cynical response, it is entirely inappropriate in this case, because the clause deals with people who may be paying and receiving money under court orders. Therefore, it is impossible for them to adjust their payments without returning to a court for a variation order, and we all know that that is a lengthy, uncertain and expensive procedure. The Government are deliberately, unnecessarily and retrospectively interfering with arrangements made many years ago under court orders. We maintain that that is unfair and unnecessary.

In the light of the completely inadequate reply that we have received, we intend to divide the Committee on the main amendment.

Question put, That the amendment be made:—

The Committee divided: Ayes 10, Noes 22.

Clause 35

Withdrawal of relief for interest on loans to buy land etc

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: The clause abolishes MIRAS. We object in principle to measures that increase the burden of taxation further on the British people, because it is being done in breach of a clear promise before the election that the Government would do no such thing. Tax increases already announced and commitments already made will increase the burden of taxes on the British people by some £40 billion over the lifetime of this Parliament. We look for all opportunities to stop this tide and, where possible, to reverse it.

We therefore take a very critical view of a clause that withdraws tax relief on mortgage interest. It has been restricted in recent years and currently stands at 10 per cent. on qualifying loans up to £30,000. It is one thing to restrict or limit tax relief if that is done in the context of a commitment and a mission to cut the burden of taxes overall. It is quite another to try to end a tax relief simply to raise money and, in the context of an overall project, to raise the burden of taxes on the public, particularly as many other taxes that have been raised hit the property sector and home owners in particular. I refer specifically to the three increases in stamp duty that we have seen.

We look for opportunities to halt this tax-raising project and we oppose the clause on those grounds. I have a more specific question for the Minister.

Mr. Andrew Love (Edmonton): My question to Opposition Members is about the interference of MIRAS in the free market in housing. Do they agree that that system deformed housing tenure choice, and do they continue to support it?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: Yes, of course. Gentleman suggests. It can happen, certainly in theory, and in practice it has sometimes happened, but given the present level of relief, that is neither an objection nor a reason for abolishing it.

The right hon. Members for Wells and for Fylde (Mr. Jack) asked about loans that employers can offer to employees for a range of benefits such as season tickets. Loans up to a maximum of £500 are outside benefit-in-kind taxation. Where an employee has both a mortgage loan and a season ticket loan on favourable terms from his employer, the benefit of the cheaper loan is generally taxable, but there is an exemption if the total loan is £5,000 or less.

The situation will not change for a person who has a season ticket loan for less than £5,000 and no other loans. The benefit in kind legislation will apply to a person who has a loan for a mortgage from his employer at a beneficial rate and a preferential loan for a season ticket—or for anything else—and who receives MIRAS. Whether someone will be affected depends on how many preferential loans he has received from his employer, including, in this context, a loan for a mortgage. If he stays under the £5,000 limit, the position will not change, but if he goes over that limit the benefit in kind legislation will apply.

Our proposal is entirely reasonable. Provisions elsewhere in the tax system deal with benefits in kind that are provided by employers to employees. The position of season ticket holders will not change, unless the loan exceeds £5,000.

The provisions in last year's Finance Act ensured that employees who receive shares, subject to forfeiture, are taxed on a fair and sensible basis. Section 149B of the 1992 Act ensures that when shares that are subject to forfeiture are sold, the acquisition cost for capital gains tax purposes is the amount paid for those shares.
