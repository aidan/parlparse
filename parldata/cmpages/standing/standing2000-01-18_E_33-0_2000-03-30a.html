Clause 187

Securing of services by franchising

Mr. Owen Paterson (North Shropshire): I beg to move amendment No. 904, in page 102, line 46, at end insert—

    `(2C) The Authority shall not exercise its powers under subsections (2A) and (2B) unless necessary for the purpose of exercising its powers under section 30.'.

I do not want to have the same discussion as we had this morning on the iniquities of nationalisation. There are, however, two ways of skinning a cat: the Government can either buy the shares, or regulate the industry back into nationalisation, as is happening in utilities and other areas.

I am worried that the clause gives the state authority much stronger powers to run the trains than the Railways Act 1993 did. The Bill replaces the words ``as eligible'' in section 23 of that Act with the word ``ought''. The amendment would ensure that those powers are used only as a buffer stop, or as the last resort, The language in section 30 of the 1993 Act, was again weaker than what we have in the Bill. It gave the franchising director the duty to

    secure the provision of those services until such time as they again begin to be provided under a franchise agreement.

A little further on, it stated that the franchising director may have

    the power to secure the operation of any additional railway assets.

The new section 30 is considerably more aggressive in specifying that the authority

    shall provide, or secure the provision of,

services. It is another hidden form of state interference in the running of the railways. The amendment would ensure that that option is used only as a last resort.

Mr. Hill: Under section 23 of the 1993 Act, the franchising director designates services as eligible to be franchised. Clause 187(1) amends that provision, so that the SRA designates services that ought to be franchised. Subsection (2) provides for such designations to be varied or revoked, but not in relation to existing franchise agreements. The variations or revocations would take place only on expiry of those agreements.

Under amendment No. 904, the designations could be varied or revoked only to allow the SRA to exercise its powers under section 30 of the 1993 Act, when necessary, as operator of the last resort. However, the SRA does not need to vary or revoke designations when it becomes the operator of the last resort. In such cases, the franchise services remain as originally designated. The SRA effectively becomes a substitute franchisee. The amendment would allow designations to be varied or revoked when they do not need to be, and would prevent them from being varied or revoked when it might be useful to do so. That would obstruct franchise replacement when part of the replacement agreement involves changes in the pattern of designated services.

I am sure that the purpose of the amendment is to probe once again the Government's intentions on back-door renationalisation. The hon. Member for North Shropshire Mr. Paterson raised that issue when he claimed that, without the amendment, it would be easier for the SRA to renationalise services by de-designating them and then providing them itself under clause 188(1). That would require the consent of the Secretary of State.

If I understand the hon. Gentleman's thesis correctly, he believes that there will be a grand conspiracy between a future Secretary of State and the SRA to renationalise part or all of the railways. However, clause 187 makes it clear—and it is apparent in the thrust of the 1993 Act—that exceptional procedures must be followed before a franchised service need not be secured through a franchise agreement. Quite apart from the convoluted nature of the supposed conspiracy, acting in the way that the hon. Gentleman fears would be unlawful, as it would be an expedient designed to circumvent Parliament's intention.

The amendment would achieve nothing. It would remove the flexibility that is necessary for future franchises to reflect changing circumstances. The protection that it supposedly provides against renationalisation by the back door is unnecessary. I urge the hon. Gentleman, in the light of my explanations and reassurances, to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Paterson: I should like to probe a little further. The Under-Secretary's reply was helpful, but suggests that I was right in thinking that the Bill confers stronger powers on the SRA as opposed to the franchise director to run trains. Will the Under-Secretary confirm that?

Mr. Hill: It gives greater powers to the SRA to run trains. The SRA negotiates with operators to run trains. It is true that operators will normally deliver train services through franchises, which the SRA will secure. Some non-franchised rail services are in operation, such as the Heathrow-Paddington express and English Welsh ÿ Scottish Railways' freight services. We are making provision in the Bill to enable the SRA to operate services as an operator of last resort, but it is perfectly clear that a number of hoops must be leapt through to reach that point. It is certainly not the intention of the Bill to facilitate that kind of direct operation. The last resort provision is obviously there in case, for example, a franchisee went bust and it was essential that the services were operated. There is no intention that the SRA should be a primary operator.

Mr. Hill: I am happy to respond to the points made by the hon. Member for Hexham. It is important that we are as clear as possible about the Government's purposes in the clause.

It is with a certain irony that I recall our proceedings on the previous Railways Bill when the Labour Opposition at that time were only too keen to facilitate the possibility of the franchise director of British Rail becoming an operator of last resort. However, times change, and we are happy to offer the reassurances that the hon. Gentleman is seeking.

The clause is concerned with what happens if the SRA's invitation to tender for a franchise produces an unsatisfactory response. I understand the difficulty that concerns the hon. Gentleman. He is worried that the SRA will issue invitations to tender that are calculated to be so demanding and unreasonable that no one responds to them. Alternatively, he fears that the approach that the SRA takes to assessing the economy and efficiency of the responses will be calculated to justify it in seeking a direction from the Secretary of State to dispense with franchising. I take it that those are the fears that he was groping towards. Let me further explore his fear, which is that that will then create an opportunity for the Secretary of State to set aside franchising, direct the SRA to provide the services instead and—hey presto!—renationalisation by the back door.

We have a difficulty here. I am sure that the Committee will accept that a failure, of whatever kind, in the franchising process must not stop the trains running. I hope that the Committee will also accept that the SRA must not be placed in a position where it has to accept a tender that is manifestly bad value for public money, just because the tendering process has failed to deliver anything better. We need to provide for cases of that kind.

That is exactly what subsections (3) and (4) are designed to do. Subsection (3) inserts new sections 26A, 26B and 26C in the 1993 Act. New section 26A deals with the case where there are no responses to an invitation to tender. New section 26B deals with the case where the only responses do not, in the SRA's view, represent good value for money. New section 26C is procedural. Subsection (4) then amends section 30 of the 1993 Act, which places duties on the franchising director—in future, the SRA—to act as operator of last resort.

New section 26A is relatively straightforward. If there is no response at all to an invitation to tender, the Secretary of State may then direct the SRA to try again; or else he may direct the SRA not to do so, in which case it must provide the services itself under new section 30.

Amendments Nos. 905 and 906 would remove that discretion from the Secretary of State. The SRA would be obliged to try again, with changes calculated to encourage tenders: I presume that that means on ever more generous terms, making ever less demanding requirements, and repeatedly, until it has succeeded in receiving at least one tender.

We have already heard from the hon. Member for Hexham about having a dog and barking. The proposal contained in the amendments really is a case of the tail wagging the dog. It makes the process of franchising more important than the outcome for which we are aiming, which is that the designated services should be economically and efficiently provided. If it was necessary to provide a bulwark against back-door renationalisation—although I have said repeatedly that that is not the Government's intention—the amendments would not achieve that. If there was a conspiracy, the SRA need only wait to receive a tender, deem it uneconomic or inefficient under new section 26B and invite the Secretary of State to give it a direction to provide the services itself. The Secretary of State can also do that under new section 26A.

I do not accept that there is a genuine risk of that happening, but the amendment would create as much of a risk as the situation that it is designed to circumvent. If the hon. Gentleman peers into his soul he will see that problem.

Amendment No. 907 would sweep away new sections 26A, 26B and 26C and replace them with a simple requirement to keep retendering until a valid tender is received and accepted. Under the amendment, the value-for-money safeguard would be abolished: that is like using a sledgehammer to crack a nut. The Opposition's paranoia—there is no other word for it—about renationalisation by the back door means that they are prepared to set aside everything that they have ever said about the need for efficiency and economy in spending public money.

Amendment No. 908 would strike out proposed new section 26C(3) of the Railways Act 1993. New section 26C(3) provides that the Secretary of State's directions to the SRA on selecting a tenderer, inviting fresh tenders, or providing the service itself may be challenged in court only under section 26C(2), which specifically allows for legal challenge, within 42 days, from disappointed tenderers whose tender has been rejected by the SRA on economy or efficiency grounds.

We aim to strike a balance between the need to protect the interests of tenderers and the need to complete the tendering process in a well-ordered fashion. Tenderers obviously have a legitimate interest: if their tenders are not accepted, they need the opportunity to test in court the basis on which that decision has been reached. The Bill protects their interest. However, to expedite the process, tenderers alone should be able to challenge the Secretary of State's decision. We do not want protracted litigation, creating uncertainty, from those who do not have a direct commercial interest at stake.

Behind the amendments is the fear that a future Secretary of State may exploit the Bill's provisions to frustrate the purpose of the 1993 Act as amended by the Bill. However, the various discretions given to the Secretary of State do not extend to an ability to subvert Parliament's entire purpose in the 1993 Act. Indeed, as I have explained, the amendments would not achieve their purpose.

In the light of my explanations and, I hope, reassurances, I trust that the hon. Gentleman will agree to withdraw the amendment.

Clause 188

Limited additional powers to provide railway services

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Syms: The explanatory notes state:

    Clause 188 gives the Authority limited additional powers to run goods or undesignated passenger services where it acquires an obligation to provide that service in consequence of a former obligation of the British Railways Board or they are no longer being provided by another person. The clause states:

    The Authority may provide services for the carriage of passengers or goods by railway if the Secretary of State consents to the provision of the services by the Authority.

and it goes on to list several cases in which that would happen. Does that mean that the Secretary of State must consent to every case in the list? ``Limited additional powers'' means limited powers because the Secretary of State will be the person who determines what shall be done. Are all the provisions necessary? Clause 188(2)(a) states that the authority may

    provide or operate network services—

that is quite a big item—

    station services or light maintenance services.

Will the Secretary of State have to give permission for the SRA to undertake light maintenance agreements if it is a matter under schedule 18, or if it is decided to take them on for operational reasons? Will the Secretary of State be concerned with the nooks and crannies of everything that the authority does, or will there be a cut-off point to ensure that minor matters are not referred to him? The Bill sets out categories in which the SRA will be able to undertake services, all of them subject to the Secretary of State.

In the good old days of the 1970s, every time British Rail wanted to invest more than £250,000, which even then would pay only for a major refurbishment of a station, the proposal had to go to the Secretary of State for Transport. That was not the most productive use of the Secretary of State's time, but it was done because the funds counted against the public sector borrowing requirement. The Bill seems to be heavy-handed about getting the Secretary of State's consent. That may be appropriate for major passenger and goods services, but when minor matters are involved, I wonder whether the Bill is correctly drafted.

Mr. Hill: On this occasion, the Opposition are expressing their anxieties about the SRA as a creature of the Secretary of State, rather than a wildly unaccountable quango.

I shall deal, first, with the question put by the hon. Member for Poole whether the Secretary of State has to issue a consent in every case. The answer is yes, but hon. Members must remember we are talking about operators of last resort. Consents will be very rare, because the events will be very rare.

The broad purport of clause 188 is to establish a limited power to provide non-franchised passenger and goods services. The clause gives the authority the power to run freight or non-franchised passenger services, either where it has inherited a legal obligation to run services from the British Railways Board, or where such a service has ceased. The clause also gives the authority the necessary associated powers to run those services, such as the power to store goods. It provides a necessary and limited power to carry out legal obligations or to step in to run services that have ceased. The authority will need the consent of the Secretary of State, who can decide that he does not want a specific service to be reinstated—for example, where a commercial service has failed and it is not in the public interest to reinstate it.

The hon. Member for Poole may feel that the powers in clauses 187 and 188 are too broadly drawn. The clauses make it clear that the Authority's powers to run a railway service are not general, but limited to a franchised service that has failed and cannot be re-let, or to a non-franchised or freight service that has ceased or is an inherited legal liability, and then only with the consent of the Secretary of State. There is no power to be a general provider of railway services in any other circumstances. We revised the provisions formerly in the Railways Bill to put it beyond doubt that the SRA is an operator of last resort only, as recommended by the Select Committee on the Environment, Transport and the Regions.

We consider that the powers are necessary so that there is public sector provision of railway services, should the private sector fail. We do not intend to replace private sector provision of railway services, but it would be unacceptable for the public sector not to be able to step in in extremis.

Mr. Peter Atkinson: Can the Under-Secretary give the Committee an example of an inherited freight or passenger service that the British Railways Board has been required to run?

Mr. Hill: I shall be delighted to give an example. The British Railways Board would be liable through its obligations in the channel tunnel usage contract to secure the provision of alternative freight and passenger services through the channel tunnel if the existing service failed. The capacity to perform such obligations has to be transferred to the Authority.

Without such a provision, the authority may say, ``We can't do this. We have to get the Secretary of State's approval, but he is on a canoe up the Amazon. What can we do?''

Mr. Phil Woolas (Oldham, East and Saddleworth): Phone him.

Mr. Hill: Let me refute the suggestion of my hon. Friend the Member for Oldham, East and Saddleworth (Mr. Woolas) that the SRA should phone me—that would be tantamount to lese-majesty. I can speak from experience: my right hon. Friend the Deputy Prime Minister is always available. Some assert that he is too available, although that is not my belief. We are talking about a proper process by which the response of the SRA and the consent of the Secretary of State have to be secured. They complement consumer protection provision, which is included in franchise agreements—foremost, the provisions on fares and passenger charter compensation matters.

However, subparagraph (c) does not prevail in this case because the list is exhaustive. If a condition is not specified in it, it is not a consumer protection condition. The intention is to transfer responsibility for these conditions to the Strategic Rail Authority. The regulator will remain the person who grants the licence, but in future the following arrangements will apply. The regulator will need the authority's approval to include the licence conditions relating to consumer protection and the authority's approval for the consensual modification of a licence condition relating to consumer protection. The authority will be responsible for approvals relating to a consumer protection matter. This includes the approval of insurance or policing arrangements, The authority will be responsible for the enforcement of consumer protection conditions of a licence, including new penalty arrangements or enforcement under section 55 of the 1993 Act. The authority will also need to join the regulator in consenting to the assignment of a licence.

We also propose that the authority should have the conduct of any reference to the Competition Commission on a matter relating to consumer protection on the ground that it operates against the public interest. This could lead to a licence modification being imposed on an operator. We propose that the authority should be able to make such reference, although it will require the regulator's approval to do this.

It is also worth noting how far we have gone in this part of the Bill to ensure that licence holders cannot argue that the burden of regulation will be increased. In exercising its functions under this part, the authority's usual purposes and functions are switched off and will not be subject to instructions and guidance from the Secretary of State under clause 182. Instead, the authority will be under the same duties and responsibilities as the regulator under section 4 of the 1993 Act although, of course, we are aligning the regulator's and the authority's respective duties as closely as possible, given their differing functions. This provision is in new section 7A(7) in line 33 on page 35 of the Act. I was surprised to discover that amendment No. 927 would delete this important provision, but we shall come to that soon.

The provisions make no change in the licence holders' substantive obligations. I think that that was a worthwhile overview.
