I do not want to get involved in complex legal arguments, and I am sure that no one else does, but, as a question has been raised about the compatibility of the clause with the convention, I will go, briefly, into further detail about that.

There is no requirement in clause 5-unlike clauses 4 and 8-that a warrant be issued before access to premises is sought. A requirement to obtain a warrant is considered to be an appropriate control on the exercise of a general power to enter premises, particularly a power to search for items or information or to collect evidence for possible use in a prosecution. Clauses 4 and 8 contain general search powers of that sort, which is why the powers can be exercised only on the granting of a warrant. Clause 5 does not provide a power to enter premises and conduct a search, nor does it provide a power to enter premises and carry out activities defined in general terms. The agency's rights as covered by clause 5 relate only to the entering of premises to conduct certain tightly defined activities. That is the key point. Under clause 5, it is not considered that a warrant is necessary or appropriate.

We have considered whether that interpretation is consistent with the convention. The key article-I cannot remember whether it was quoted by the hon. Member for North Shropshire-is article 8. It States:

    Everyone has the right to respect for his private and family life, his home and his correspondence.

The hon. Gentleman will tell me whether that is the article that he quoted. It also states:

    There shall be no interference by a public authority with the exercise of this right except such as is in accordance with the law and is necessary in a democratic society in the interests of national security, public safety or the economic well-being of the country, for the prevention of disorder or crime, for the protection of health or morals, or for the protection of the rights and freedoms of others.

That point has already been made.

The article is relevant because, although it is very unlikely, it is possible, at least in theory, that the powers in clause 5 may have to be exercised on domestic premises. Committee members will recall that some of the most sensitive plants in Iraq were in Saddam Hussein's palaces, which were defined as domestic premises. Case law suggests that the term ``home'', which appears in the additional protocol, might encompass a professional person's office. The hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire pointed out on Second Reading that increasing numbers of professionals are working at home and that the key tool is the computer. To deny access to computer records, which may be crucial in relation to, for example, the sale of part of a nuclear trigger mechanism, would be folly indeed. We are satisfied that it is necessary in a democratic society to allow entry in the circumstances and for the purposes that are describe in the clause.

The hon. Member for Hereford asked about remedies for the misuse of powers. If an authorised officer of the Secretary of State exceeded his or her powers, he or she would be acting unlawfully and open to legal challenge in the usual way. The appropriate form of legal proceedings would depend on what had happened. Judicial review or a claim for compensation might be involved. If an IAEA inspector acted beyond his or her powers, that too, would be unlawful, and the United Kingdom would complain strongly to the agency. Agency inspectors are covered by a form of diplomatic immunity, and neither they nor the agency would normally be subject to legal proceedings. If an inspector exceeded his or her powers in a serious manner or in a way that had serious consequences-such cases were discussed by the hon. Member for Hereford-we would need to establish whether the agency should waive immunity in that case.

Mr. Keetch: Let us be specific. If an inspector came into a premises in my constituency and caused damage or prevented a business from trading for a couple of hours, the agency would be covered by diplomatic immunity. How would my constituent get money to pay for the repair of a lock or computer equipment, or for the loss of the morning's activities?

Dr. Howells: He or she could challenge along the lines that I described earlier-namely, because the inspector's activities were unwarranted. The inspector may have acted outside his or her remit and, therefore, acted illegally. There is no remedy for damage that is caused by an inspector who properly exercises the powers. In view of the type of activities that the inspectors are permitted to carry out, such damage is most unlikely to occur. In other words, the hon. Gentleman was discussing exceptional circumstances. I cannot think of similar instances in other legislation, except when company investigations are carried out because there have been allegations of distortions of markets.

Mr. Paterson: Let us take the practical example of an inspector who goes into a business in Hereford or Shropshire. The Minister said that most of the relevant information would be on computer. Dr. Howells: Ultimately, the courts will do so, as per normal.
