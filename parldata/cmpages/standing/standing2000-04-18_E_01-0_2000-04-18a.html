Clause 1

Disclosure of information

Mr. Greenway: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 6, leave out, ``at the request of the BBC''.

I am grateful to you for that ruling, Mr. Illsley. It will be convenient for the Committee to consider amendment No. 1 separately, as the amendment gives us the opportunity to consider the principle of the Government's chosen method for ensuring that all pensioners who are aged 75 and over, and are entitled to a free licence, receive it.

The Government have chosen to place the onus on the BBC to request information about whether a pensioner is entitled to a free licence-that is, whether they have reached the appropriate age-and to obtain other information to cover the security aspect, especially in relation to the fact that pensioners receive a free licence for only one address. We understand and concur with the Government's approach in that respect. However, it begs the question-which many have already asked-why the Government thought it inappropriate to compile a list of the 3 million or so eligible pensioners in order to produce a database of the information that could be made available to the BBC. The question was raised on Second Reading, but we have not yet received a satisfactory explanation of the more obvious method was not chosen.

I add the caveat that I, the Minister and other members of Committee are at a disadvantage in that we are not social security experts. The workings of the Department of Social Security, in terms of the way in which records are kept and computers are organised, might mean that this would be a difficult operation. Perhaps the argument has already been lost. However, it is right to ask, at the commencement of the Committee's proceedings, why the Government have chosen to adopt a method that requires the BBC or its contractors to verify, on every occasion, that an old age pensioner aged over 75 who has applied for a free licence is entitled to it, when it might be preferable to provide the BBC with a complete database of eligible individuals.

Another argument centres on cost. What will it cost to process each individual request for information? Common sense suggests that it will be more expensive to deal with the matter on the basis of individual applicants. If the BBC were to be given a complete list of the 3 million or so eligible pensioners-I assume that the DSS knows who they are-people would be less likely to fall through the net, either by not having a licence or by buying one when they did not need to. That issue, too, was discussed in detail on Second Reading.

To put the onus on the DSS or the Government to supply information, instead of on the BBC to request it, would also be a more effective way of dealing with the question of people who die, because one assumes that the DSS is among the first to know when an elderly person dies. It could then supply that information to the contractors dealing with the licence records; it should be a straightforward procedure. As the Government will pay for it anyway, we should ask at the start of our Committee proceedings why they have decided on the individual route, as opposed to setting up a complete database.

However, it will not be the BBC that makes the request or receives the information, but the contractors or companies that administer the licensing system, currently known as TV Licensing. That body already has a mass of information about us-who we are and where we live-so we should not get too excited about a specific age-related list; however, the Bill gives wide powers to provide social security information. There is understandable concern about that apparently unprecedented departure from normal practice, and we should not allow the desirability of the end-free television licences for the over-75s-to justify a lack of care in implementing the concession.

If the government believe that there should be a request and supply mechanism, a key question is where the request should originate. That is why we have tabled amendments Nos. 2 and 3, which are clearly related to amendment No. 1. Should the request come from the BBC? If not, we see no prospect of the BBC being able to supervise the arrangements properly.

The BBC is defined in the clause in terms of its contractors; therefore, the contractors will make the request and receive the information, with no supervision by anybody in the BBC. Therefore, we suggest that the definition of BBC in subsection (2) should be amended to make it clear that the contractor definition relates only to the receipt of the information and not to the making of the request. If the Government accept our suggestion that a database should be provided to the BBC and, from it, to the contractors, the supply of information would be complete.

I do not want to detain the Committee any longer on the arguments, but two clear issues emerge that require consideration. The first is why the Government have decided to deal with the matter as they have, instead of asking the DSS to draw up a proper database of everyone who qualifies. Secondly, notwithstanding the clear and severe penalties set out later in the Bill for wrongful use of the information, some thought must be given to the fact that the contractors, rather than the BBC, will be the main players. What supervision will the BBC exercise over its current contractors and, more important, future contractors that it appoints. If we agree to the Bill we, in effect, give the BBC a free hand in relation to appointments.

Mr. Norman Baker (Lewes): I have received several complaints about TV Licensing, which has written to some of my constituents stating that it refuses to believe that they have no television, or only a black-and-white set. Janet Anderson: I will try to respond to the points that have been raised. We will come to many of them later in our consideration. Amendment No. 1 deals with the Government's method of ensuring that the BBC has access to the relevant information. I shall set out some of the alternative methods that we considered.

Without the social security information that the Bill will make available to the BBC, every elderly applicant would have to produce documentary proof of their age, which would impose a considerable additional burden. Many applicants do not have such documentation and would therefore have to obtain it before they could apply, which would cause delay, inconvenience and expense. A system involving manual handling of more than 3.5 million documents would also be far more expensive to administer. An alternative proposal, which would require the BBC to seek verification of claims from the DSS and the Northern Ireland Department, would still entail much of the information that members of the Committee are concerned about being disclosed through the verification process. Such an approach would also involve the BBC and Government Departments in a duplication of effort and cost.

The points raised in this morning's debate are important, and I welcome the opportunity to reassure hon. Members about the control that Parliament will have over the information to be provided to the BBC. Hon. Members referred specifically to the BBC's supervision of the contractors, which is an issue to which we shall return later in our proceedings. That will be set out in a memorandum of understanding between the DSS and the BBC: the DSS will ensure that appropriate safeguards are in place. The contractors will also be subject to the Data Protection Act 1998, and there must be specific written contractual guarantees, including security measures. The data protection commissioners will investigate breaches and other concerns. I hope that that reassures hon. Members.

Members of the Committee are understandably concerned to ensure that personal information released by the Government to third parties is properly controlled and supervised. Amendment No. 1 is-perhaps, with the best of intentions-designed to prevent the BBC from indulging in a fishing expedition be requesting information that is not strictly necessary for the administration of free licences for the over-75s. However, the Bill does not give the BBC any right to require information to be supplied. It provides only that the Secretary of State or the Northern Ireland Department ``may'' supply social security information at the BBC's request; they will be under no obligation to do so. The reference to a request from the BBC in subsection (1) relates to the timing and procedure for such transfers, not to the information that can be provided. Any supervision of what is released will be exercised by the Secretary of State.

Mr. Greenway: The Minister made an interesting comment earlier, when she said that it would be expensive if the BBC or its contractors had to seek verification on an individual basis. I agree with that, but that was my understanding of the way in which the concession would work. Is the Minister saying that the information that she expects to be provided will be provided more in bulk, along the lines of the database that I have proposed? That is the issue that I wanted to get across in our brief debate on the amendment. I entirely understand the point: we, too, are not thinking about fishing expeditions.

Janet Anderson: I thank the hon. Gentleman for that timely intervention. We expect the information to be passed on in bulk: that is one reason for choosing the mechanism that we have chosen. The alternative route, whereby the DSS would check claims only at the BBC's request, would not be cost effective. The total cost of the concession in the current financial year is estimated to be £368 million, so the arrangements for administering it must be, not only user-friendly, but secure against fraud. The alternative route would not be cost-effective because, instead of using a relatively simple data-matching exercise, the DSS would need to establish its own unit. That has not been fully costed, but we estimate that it would multiply the cost by a factor of 10. It would also cause considerable delay in processing, and we are anxious to ensure that all pensioners entitled to the concession should be able to avail themselves of it from 1 November, which is the date of implementation.

I hope that that reassures hon. Members. I ask the hon. Member for Ryedale to consider withdrawing the amendment, in view of the assurances that I have given.
