The Minister for Transport in London (Ms Glenda Jackson): I was previously responding to the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey(Mr. Hughes), which would allow assembly members to appoint political advisers. As I pointed out, the mayor and not the assembly will be the political pivot round which the authority turns. The assembly will not be an alternative political administration and will not be there to put forward an alternative political agenda to replace that of the mayor. It will be there to test and challenge the mayor's proposals and strategies, where it finds them to be at fault, to scrutinise their implementation and to examine the other actions of the mayor and his or her agents. With the exception of the 12 appointments that we were discussing earlier, the assembly will appoint all the authority's staff, including the chief administrative officer.

Clause 61 applies the provisions of section 4 of the Local Government and Housing Act 1989 to the chief adminstrative officer as head of paid services. That Act requires the head of paid service-that is, the GLA's chief administrative officer-to prepare a report setting out his or her proposals for the manner in which the Authority's different functions are to be co-ordinated, the number and different grades of staff required by the authority for the discharge of its functions, the organisation of those staff, and their appointment and proper management.

It is in our view inevitable that in fulfilling that duty, the chief administrative officer must provide the assembly with adequate administration and staff resources. That provisions will most probably take the form of a secretariat dedicated to the service of assembly members and drawn from GLA staff, not unlike the private officers that service Ministers of the Clerks of the House who serve its Committees. Of course, members of the Assembly will also be able to call on the services of all GLA staff, other than those in the mayor's office.
