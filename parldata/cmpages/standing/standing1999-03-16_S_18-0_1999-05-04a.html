Mr. Allan: We have reached what is, in the eyes of many people, the most contentious part of the Bill. It deals with support for asylum seekers. The amendments start the ball rolling in a gentle way, and I hope that they will help the Government. They would extend the Secretary of State's powers to provide support to asylum seekers during the appeals process.

The Bill attemps to simplify the appeals procedure into a one-stop appeals procedure and to incorporate into that the United Kingdom's obligations under the European convention on human rights. We have had those obligations for some time, but they have been given added force by the enactment of the Human Rights Act 1998.

When the Secretary of State considers whether to provide support to someone entering the appeals process, the amendments would ensure that he could provide it not only on the basis of article 3 of the European convention on human rights. That article enables people to appeal on the ground that they would be sent back to a country where they might be subject to torture or to inhuman and degrading treatment. We wish to extend the provision to include all the grounds that are covered in the convention.

However, amendment No. 825 makes it clear that we are not referring to the general grounds that might be raised in a deportation case or those that deal with family circumstances or the querying of the decision of an entry clearance officer. The amendment refers specifically to ``protection'', and it is right that protection issues should be incorporated in the clause.

Article 4 of the convention prohibits slavery and forced labour; article 5 is on the right to liberty and security; article 6 is on the right to a fair trial; and article 7 states that there should be no punishment without the law. All those articles provide legitimate grounds for an asylum claim.

The Government's intention is that someone should be supported in those circumstances; they have already accepted that someone who claims that he might be tortured should have an appeal heard and that he should be supported for the duration of the appeal. Therefore, it would make perfect sense for the Secretary of State to have at least the discretion to award support to someone claiming under the other articles of the convention.

If the Government do not accept the amendments, we fear that the Secretary of State might not have the discretion to provide support to someone who has a valid claim to appeal. The articles to which I have referred might be raised during the appeals process. At that point, the Secretary of State might need to consider a special adjudicator's recommendation or might wish to hear further representations. For example, it would be valid to raise the article on the prohibition of slavery and forced labour, and we all know that domestic workers in this country have faced many problems. The Secretary of State might rightly want to consider the issues further, but he will find himself without the discretion to offer support to someone appealing on those grounds.

Although we shall have arguments later about the nature of the support being offered, we believe that the Government wish to provide some support while asylum and ECHR claims are being considered. The amendments would help the Government to achieve that objective and we hope that the Minister will accept them.

I turn now to amendments Nos. 319 and 320. Asylum seekers who have been receiving support under the Secretary of State's scheme will no longer be eligible to receive it, first, if a positive decision is made on their asylum claims, secondly, if their application is refused and they do not lodge an appeal or, thirdly, if their appeal against refusal of asylum is decided and the original refusal is upheld. The Immigration and Nationality Directorate will wish to terminate support as quickly as is reasonably possible in such circumstances.

The Bill as drafted does not lay down the length of such a period of grace, but clause 73(3) allows the Secretary of State to make regulations specifying how long it should be. The draft process manual—a copy of which is in the Library—sets out how it is envisaged that the claims for support will be handled. It suggests that the period should be 14 days, which would allow time for the successful asylum seeker to move to the social security system or to make arrangements to work, and for the unsuccessful applicant to make arrangements to leave the country. It is important to note that the latter does not apply to families with children who will receive support for as long as they remain in the United Kingdom. It is envisaged that the only people who are not likely to be supported beyond the suggested 14-day period are single persons without children. The aim is for flexibility. Different circumstances may apply; some people may more easily be able to transfer to another system of support, to go to live with relatives or to change their circumstances quickly, others may take more time to make arrangements.

Flexibility is needed and that is more likely to be achieved by regulation rather than by the Bill. We want a broad-based consultation on the proposal of 14 days to decide matters such as if so, for whom and in what circumstances, and to discuss other periods. We shall take careful note of submissions already made by various organisations and to those made in the coming months. We must consider the background to the proposals. Changes have been made since the Asylum and Immigration Act 1966 was introduced as a result of court cases that considered the definition of destitution and what was and was not acceptable. The Joint Council for the Welfare Immigrants brought one such case and it was decided that people could not be left utterly destitute, but had to be supported under the National Assistance Act 1948 and the Children Act 1989. That led to the current position in which some asylum seekers are provided for by the benefits system and others by the fairly ad hoc mechanisms of the NAA and the Children Act.

The Government's proposals in the Bill are an attempt at reform, but they have not been clear and open and put a definition of ``destitution'' in the Bill. They have left it to the Secretary of State's discretion. However, the issue is too significant for it not to be defined in the Bill.

I bear in mind the Chair's comments about reading quotations, so I shall quote briefly from Justice's evidence to the Committee. It used its expertise to consider whether aspects of the Bill complied with our international and, under the Human Rights Act 1988, domestic obligations. It said:

    ``The provisions for removing, or refusing, support and accommodation to asylum-seekers who are destitute and whose protection claims are still not finally determined raise serious questions of compliance with the ECHR and the 1951 Convention. They create the risk of undermining the protection of Article 3 ECHR and Article 33 1951 Convention by forcing asylum-seekers to leave. They also create the risk of directly breaching Article 8, Article 3 and (in extreme cases) Article 2 of the ECHR.''—[Official Report, Special Standing Committee, 16 March 1999; c. 80.]

It recommended amendments appropriate to that problem.

It also defines the end of the asylum process and provides that support will end on a prescribed day after that period, although there will be a safety net for families with children. We are presently minded to opt for a period of 14 days, but we have agreed to reflect further after consultation.
