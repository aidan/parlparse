Mr. Moss: Welcome, Mrs. Michie. We do not intend to keep you up too late, although others have different intentions.

In his closing remarks the Minister alluded to our not having mentioned the Royal Ulster Constabulary in dispatches, and I did not want the Committee's proceedings to begin without referring to that. The Minister knows from his dealings with the Chief Constable and the RUC in Northern Ireland that the previous Government gave that institution every support. It goes without saying that we continue to admire the incredibly courageous work that it does. As a result of the Bill, and other legislation, its task may become harder, or easier--we cannot predict these things.

Over many years the RUC has been at the forefront, at the interface, in tackling terrorism. It is one of 52 police forces throughout the United Kingdom, and is more or less the same as any other force in terms of its structure, ranks, management and style. But the RUC differs in one very important respect--in the past 25 years, 300 of its officers have been murdered, both on and off duty, and sometimes in the bosom of their families, and with their friends. About 7,000 have been injured in some way--some physically, some psychologically, and most of that injury has impaired their lives for ever. I pay tribute to the RUC, and want it on record that the official Opposition admire and respect the fine work that its officers do.

Times are indeed changing. I understand the latest statistic is that about 22 per cent. of new applicants come from the nationalist community, compared with about 8 per cent. a few years ago.

From my knowledge of discussions about the security situation, I know that the previous Government adhered to the letter scrupulously-- such was the nature of the then Secretary of State--in following all the operational guidance and recommendations of the Chief Constable. Returning to my earlier point, can the present Government hold up their hands and say, ``We are following the same path''? I wonder whether the present Chief Constable's memoirs will suggest differently?

The Minister said that the Government would ensure that the RUC had all the resources it needed to do its job. Much of its job relates, of course, to finding evidence with which to charge people in the Diplock courts, with which we are dealing in clause 2.

In the light of the present situation--the pressure must have eased somewhat because of the ceasefire--can the Minister assure the Committee that sufficient resources are being made available to the RUC to enable it to fulfil all its duties and requirements? Has the Chief Constable approached him in the past few months for additional resources either because of the changing situation or because of the implications of the changes in clause 2?

If the Minister did a straw poll of average RUC officers does he think that a majority would be in favour of the changes that he proposes to the schedule of offences in clause 2? That is an interesting question and I wonder what the average RUC officer's attitude would be. Clause 2 deals with legislation relating to Diplock courts and changes provisions for some previously scheduled categories, which the Attorney-General had no means of certifying out.

Let us remind ourselves of why the Diplock courts were introduced. In the late 1960s and early 1970s, it proved impossible to convict people whom the authorities knew had carried out terrorist acts because of the high levels of IRA intimidation of witnesses and jurors. It became well nigh impossible to convict anyone of a terrorist offence because of that. The problem did not stop at threats because in some circumstances, the IRA murdered witnesses and jurors.

When our party was in government, Conservative Members always believed that the Diplock process was right in principle and its implementation has produced some success. Having paid tribute to the RUC, I now pay tribute to the Northern Ireland judiciary. Its members, too, have been at the sharp end of considerable intimidation and threats to their lives and property. We have a distinguished counsel in our presence tonight and, if he has the opportunity, he will no doubt go into more detail about such intimidation and threats. However, it is certainly the case that the quality and dedication of people involved in the legal process are perceived, not just in Northern Ireland but throughout Britain and abroad, as second to none. Society in Northern Ireland is privileged to have such gifted and dedicated servants.

As was said earlier, in opposition the Labour party held a view that it repeated ad nauseam for many years. It wanted to remove the Diplock process as soon as it was able. The Minister confirmed that by saying that the reason for producing the legislation was to honour manifesto commitments that were given in opposition. The Labour party opposed removing Diplock for some time. I shall not accuse Labour Members of attempting or even of suggesting that they could do that at a stroke. They stated that they wished to make fundamental change, and that they were opposed in principle to the notion of Diplock courts.

One way in which the Labour party wished to make that change was to remove all the scheduled offences under schedule 1 and allow the Attorney-General to certify in those offences that should be considered in the Diplock courts. Currently, the Attorney-General looks carefully at each case and decides whether it would be in the interests of justice and of the legal process to take it out of the list of scheduled offences and try it in the normal criminal justice system. It would then be heard before a jury instead of being tried in a Diplock court without a jury.

In Committee on the legislation that led to the 1996 Act, the Labour party proposed an amendment to that effect. Labour Members spoke at length to the amendment, and divided the Committee on it. That was about a year and a half ago. The buzz word at the time was that the Attorney-General should be more ``proactive''.

Why has the Labour party, now in government, had that fundamental change of heart? The Minister ducked the question earlier, as the record will show. He said that the Committee would reach it later. Later has arrived, and the Committee has reached the same question. the Minister must answer it. He cannot, on the one hand, say that the legislation is intended to honour commitments, and on the other just tinker at the edges of one of its most essential planks. Diplock courts will still be in place. The changes are cosmetic, and tinker with the overall notion. Will the Minister explain the Labour party's change of heart? Is it just another U-turn, or a special U-turn? Is it a U-turn based on principle, or on facing the facts when in government? In opposition, there is a propensity to indulge in free thinking without facing key issues.

In Committee on the 1996 Bill, as it then was, and in debates on the Floor of the House since then, Labour Members have made a number of proposals. Some of them were made by the present Under-Secretary of State, the Member for Clydebank and Milngavie (Mr. Worthington).

Those proposals were designed to effect change in this area of legislation, in other words to phase out the Diplock proceedings. Although no details were given, one proposal by the Labour party in opposition was for a mechanism that would allow sections of the legislation to lapse. I am not sure whether the proposals that we are debating constitute a lapse or an amendment. Perhaps the Minister will enlighten us. Another proposed mechanism was a collegiate court, which meant that instead of having one judge and no jury, the trial could be presided over by three judges as an alternative to the present constitution of the Diplock court.

The Labour party proposed a different method for tackling intimidation. Labour Members thought, when in opposition--obviously, they no longer think so--that measures could be introduced directly to tackle the intimidation of juries. They spoke of a review by Mr. Gearty and Mr. Kimbell, whose report contained proposals that, at the time, were espoused by Labour Members. I shall quote some of their report. It said:

    ``The names and addresses of jury panels should not be publicly accessible information in scheduled cases . . . Balloting of jurors should take place by number only in scheduled cases . . . Jurors should not have to give their name and address orally at any stage . . . Prospective jurors should not know whether they will serve in what are now scheduled or non scheduled cases . . . What are now scheduled cases should be heard in court rooms where the jury can sit out of sight of the public gallery . . . And jurors should not have to wait and eat in areas in which ordinary members of the public have access . . . As a matter of administrative practice especially in the early years following the restoration of juries, court officials should look favourably upon applications to be excused service on scheduled cases from people living in areas where loyalist or republican paramilitaries have a particularly strong influence.''

Those proposals are from one section of a fairly extensive tome, which contained hosts of similar recommendations. On first reading they seem extremely plausible. Why have the Government rejected them? Why have they chosen a very different course of action? Why did we not have a debate on Second Reading about why those plausible proposals, made by the Labour party in opposition, have now been rejected?

The Labour party in opposition also had extremely strong views on the previous Government's support for the certifying-out principle. The matter is complicated, but as I understand it, the certifying-out principle meant that the Attorney-General was able to relieve a defendant of the special case as defined in schedule 1, rather than impose such restrictions, which is of course a different matter.

Let us remind ourselves of the role of the Attorney-General. He or she is responsible for all prosecutions. As such, certification would involve the Attorney-General--the prosecution--deciding the mode of trial. In other words, almost perversely, the defendant in that particular case would have been denied the right to trial by jury. I suspect that no Attorney-General worth his or her salt would countenance taking on such a role and that the realities of the situation were driven home hard to the incoming Government when they took up the reins seven months ago.

We always believed and continue to believe that Parliament, through legislation, should specify scheduled offences and that the role of the Attorney-General should be that of a second tier, not a backstop, whose responsibility is to review individual circumstances in the interests of justice. We made our case in the teeth of opposition from Labour Members, on not just one occasion but time and again. It is puzzling: the Government have now turned turtle and are now arguing from the stance that we took in government.

I turn to the detail of the offences with which the clause deals. Schedule 1 of the 1996 Act is referred to. There are 22 paragraphs--major headings--and 60 sub-paragraphs. It is detailed and runs to four pages of close type. The clause makes changes to five major headings and only two sub-paragraphs. I said in my opening remarks that that was tinkering at the edges. The Government were scraping the barrel when deciding what to pick out to curry favour with whoever--I think that we know who. What is the point? What is the logic? If the Government oppose the Diplock courts and whan fundamental change, they should get on with it and act positively to end that system--as the Labour party wanted in opposition. They should not tinker in this cosmetic way--it means virtually nothing.

The first change is at clause 2(1)(a), which refers to ``paragraph 3 (riot)''. Riot is not defined. Does the Minister consider the events of Drumcree at their height to have been a riot? Were they related to terrorist offences? After Drumcree, there was a major incident at Larne habour and incidents throughout Northern Ireland. The RUC was stretched to breaking point. That will be confirmed by Northern Ireland Members, who, more than I did, lived through it.

Why take ``riot'' out of the legislation as a scheduled offence? What purpose is served? What advantage is to be gained, or is there a political agenda? However, that makes little sense in terms of keeping the peace or ensuring that when one wishes to convict for rioting, witnesses and jurors will not be intimidated, because that will obviously happen in a Drumcree situation. Intimidation would prevent one from convicting anybody.

Paragraph (b) refers to schedule 1(12) of the 1996 Act. Paragraphs 12 and13 of that schedule come under the sub-heading of hijacking; the former refers to offences under section 1 of the Aviation Security Act 1982, which would involve aircraft, while the latter refers to offences in Northern Ireland, under section 2 of the Criminal Jurisdiction Act 1975, which would involve vehicles or ships. I do not want to go into massive detail, and no doubt the Minister will put me right if I am sailing down the wrong tack, but we seem to be talking about hijacking aircraft, ships or trains--the word is ``vehicles'', but I would not have thought that that meant cars. The provisions relate to serious hijacking of the larger modes of transport.

Why have the Government concluded that there is no longer a perceived threat of such hijacking? Just because such events have not happened does not mean that they could not happen in future. I am puzzled as to the logic of selecting those two offences when a range of others could be reconsidered which are no more or less important. Will the Minister confirm that those offences have been chosen for specific, logical reasons and that they are linked?

The Government should not approach the issue from a need to do more confidence building or to make concessions on Diplock. They now realise that they cannot do in government what they promised to do or what they wanted to do because, first, the current provisions do an excellent job and, secondly, all the advice from the security people and the lawyers in Northern Ireland suggests that although this is not a perfect world and the measures do not necessarily bring about significant increases in convictions, they are better than nothing and work reasonably well.

I met very few people in Northern Ireland who want to see current provisions amended or removed simply as a concession or as a confidence-building measure. I do not suppose that they wanted them removed from the point of view of security, because most people in Northern Ireland would say that the situation is inconclusive--they are in the middle of another so-called ceasefire, but there is no settlement, no promises, no move towards decommissioning and no move towards a total cessation of violence. The trickle of information from the Francie Molloys of this world paints a very different picture--a serious and sinister picture--which suggests to most sensible people that making concessions in such a climate will not necessarily yield the hoped-for results.

Paragraph (d) relates to schedule 1(17) of the 1996 Act--offences under the Taking of Hostages Act 1982. Again, why is it deemed necessary or important to remove that? Is taking political hostages something that the IRA would never do? Of course, we are talking not only about the IRA but loyalist and other terrorists. Just because history has not shown it to be one of the modus operandi of the IRA or of loyalist terrorists does not mean that it could not be so in future.

Political hostages could be taken, as well as what I would term economic or financial hostages. Extortion is a much more likely option, and it has been tried in Northern Ireland and on mainland Britain.

They said that, currently, several scheduled offences automatically had to be tried in the Diplock courts, but argued that it was conceivable that certain offences could be committed in circumstances not connected with terrorism. To state the most obvious, emergency powers exist to enable the police and the armed forces to deal with the real threat that terrorism poses to the rule of law and order, but they also send out signals of the Government's awareness of the nature and extent of that threat and the Government's seriousness in countering that threat. Therefore, the existence and use of emergency powers is partly an expression of the Government's attitude to, and assessment of, the activities of terrorists. So emergency powers can make a political statement, as well as serving their primary purpose of providing a security response.

It is worth reflecting on three incidents that happened last week because they illustrate my point. The first took place on the road from Crossmaglen to Cullaville. Four Provisionals, wearing balaclavas, combat jackets and armed with Armalites, chased in their saloon car a car driven by two postal workers. They forced the postal workers to stop. The four members of the IRA emerged and started to threaten and assault the postal workers. Miraculously, through skilled driving, the postal workers escaped unharmed. That was the first occasion since the July ceasefire on which the Provisionals had authorised the use of firearms in south Armagh, and it happened only last week.

In the second incident, also last week, armed and masked men hijacked and burned vehicles in Lurgan and Armagh. In the third incident, an IRA gunman, in a balaclava, held up a post office in Ballinderry, County Tyrone. He beat up the postmaster and stole several thousands of pounds. I emphasise that all those incidents happened last week.

The re-emergence of IRA guns is a reality, and it is happening at a time when there are growing signs of serious divisions within the republican movement and the public nature of that dissent has placed the ceasefire under real strain. It would be folly to deny that or to pretend otherwise. Is this really the right time for the Government to dilute the certifying-out regime? Does the situation on the ground justify it? I would argue that it does not.

If the Government are arguing that those offences are now less likely to be committed by terrorists in the course of terrorist activity than previously, they have not made their case effectively.
