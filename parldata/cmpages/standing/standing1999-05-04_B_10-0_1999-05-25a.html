Clause 43

PRP and agricultural pay

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Whittingdale (Maldon and East Chelmsford): I will not delay the Committee unduly, although I believe that we said that in relation to the previous clause, I just want to raise a couple of queries.

The clause relates to those who receive agricultural wages and who receive also an element of profit-related pay. Under the clause, the profit-related pay will be included when assessing whether a person has received a minimum wage, whereas that element was previously excluded. That diminishes the value of profit-related pay, because although in a profitable year a proportion of the wage may be generated through profit-related pay, in an unprofitable year an employer might have to make up the basic wage package to reach the minimum wage. Therefore, the employee might notice little difference in the amount that he or she finally receives.

I should be interested to hear the Economic Secretary's comments on the clause, and to know whether she has received representations from the National Farmers Union, the Country Landowners Association or any of the other bodies that represent those affected by the clause, and if so, what their views are.

Ms Hewitt: In the light of the previous debate, I wonder how long it will be before the House agrees that interactive electronic voting should be available both in Committees and in the Chamber.

As the hon. Member for Maldon and East Chelmsford (Mr Whittingdale) rightly said, the clause removes the present rule that profit-related pay should not count towards the existing agricultural minimum wage. My predecessor, my right hon. Friend the Member for Airdrie and Shotts (Mrs. Liddell), who is now Minister for Transport, explained the background to the clause in a written reply to a parliamentary question on 28 July 1998.

The reason for the change is that we accept the Low Pay Commission's recommendation that incentive payments covering payments of tax-relieved, profit-related pay should be included in the calculation of earnings for the national minimum wage. Therefore the clause ensures that employers and employees in the agricultural sector are subject to the same profit-related pay rules as employers and employees in other business sectors. It removes any unfairness that might be caused by treating a fairly small number of low-paid workers in profit-related pay schemes in the agricultural sector differently from workers in PRP schemes in other sectors.

Some businesses, including those in the agricultural sector, have expressed concern that if profit-related pay was not allowed to count towards the national minimum wage, that might lead them to abandon their profit-related pay schemes. I think that the hon. Gentleman will agree that that would be a pity, and the clause will prevent it from happening. That is in keeping with our desire that the national minimum wage should be simple and fair, as well as transparent to workers and easy for employers to comply with. I am not aware of any representations that have been made against the clause. I therefore commend it to the Committee.

Clause 44

Cars available for private use

Mr. Whittingdale: I beg to move amendment No. 89, in page 23, line 43, leave out '15' and insert '13'.

The clause is the appetiser for next year when, we are told, a new system of company car taxation is to be introduced. Instead of being differentiated according to business mileage and other factors, it will be calculated on the basis of emissions, in order to contribute to the Government's environmental objectives. In principle we do not oppose the change, but it has led to some concern, especially as few details have so far been given about the new system.

When the Economic Secretary replies to the debate, as well as addressing the changes to be made this year, which I am about to talk about, will she say a little about the Government's overall strategy in the light of the change to be introduced next year? I draw her attention in particular to the concerns of the Confederation of British Industry, which said:

    'The details of the future tax treatment of company cars has yet to be determined'.

It was not yet clear, the CBI said, whether the charge would be based simply on the amount of carbon dioxide emitted or whether other toxic emissions would be taken into account as well.

The CBI went on to say:

    'The lack of detail and of worked-through proposals to which business can respond is currently causing considerable concern about the likely impacts of reform.'

I am sure that the Government want a proper consultation process before introducing changes in next year's Budget, so they have an opportunity now to say more about their intentions for future tax treatment, which would allow business to respond in greater detail during the consultation period.

One of the reasons why criticism has been levied at the existing system of company car taxation is that it creates perverse incentives to drive more miles. That is specifically mentioned in the explanatory notes on the clause:

    'The existing business mileage discounts have been widely criticised as sending perverse environmental signals, as they give an incentive to drive additional, unnecessary business miles in order to reach the discount thresholds. There is evidence that the extra miles driven in company cars could be as much as 0.3 billion miles a year.'

That is an extraordinarily large figure. I would not dispute the perverse incentives in the existing system, but I would be interested to know the origin of the figure.

If that is the main criticism of the existing system, it is worth pointing out that the clause will make no difference to it, because it preserves the existing banding system and will still encourage drivers to drive more, clock up additional business miles and reach a higher band in order to enjoy a lower rate of tax.

The Government's proposed system, whereby the level of tax is calculated purely on the basis of the environmental effect of the car, in terms of emissions, may help the Government to meet their environmental objectives, but it will do away entirely with the distinction between those who have company cars for their business--and clock up many business miles for genuine business purposes--and those who have company cars as a perk. Scrapping that distinction entirely is curious.

The Daily Telegraph cited the example of two drivers, Alan and Bill, who drive the same model of company car and paid the same money for it--£16,000:

    'Both drive around 20,000 miles a year but, whereas Alan's mileage is almost all business travel, Bill only does a handful of business miles. Alan's car is four years old, Bill's car is new',

and they are both in the same tax bracket. At the moment,

    'both have a basic taxable benefit of £16,000 multiplied by 35 per cent. to give a total bill of £5,600. Alan gets a reduction to 15 per cent. rather than 35 per cent. because of his high business mileage, reduced by a quarter because of the age of his car, resulting in a tax bill of £720. Bill has...a tax bill of £2,240.'

It is sensible for Alan, who has a company car as a perk, to pay more than Bill, who has a company car and needs it for his business miles. However, as the article continues:

    'Under the new scheme, the basic benefit, will be within the 15 per cent. to 35 per cent. range but it will be based on the carbon dioxide emissions of the car. There will be no mileage discounts nor discounts for older cars. If we assume that the car concerned carries a 25 per cent. rating, both men end up with a tax bill of £1,600.'

It seems a little odd that while the Government are proposing to move to a system which may have some merit on environmental grounds, they are throwing away the quite important distinction between genuine business use and a company car that is included as a perk in an overall salary package. I hope that the Minister will address that point when she says a little more about the Government's proposals for the overall reform of business car taxation.

The change proposed in the clause will be effective only this year. As it does not address the main criticism of the effect of the banding in creating perverse incentives, it is rather difficult to see why the Government suggest making that change, particularly as it will last for only a year before the whole system is scrapped and the new one introduced. The suspicion must be that the Government see it as another means of raising additional tax revenue from the hard pressed motorist. We are now familiar with the Government's view that the motorist is a cash cow--that they can levy more and more tax on motorists who have no choice but to stump up. The clause is another example of that attitude, and company car drivers will pay more.

The way in which the Government are increasing the charge is also curious. The general tax charge on a company car, calculated on basis of 35 per cent. of the list price, is not being changed. The increase affects the discount which applies to business use. Consequently, drivers who use their company cars for 2,500-18,000 miles each year, and who before the Budget paid 231/3 per cent. of the list price of the car, will now pay 25 per cent. of the list price. That represents an increase of 7.1 per cent. in their tax charge. But drivers who use their company cars for more than 18,000 miles and who before the Budget paid 11.67 per cent. of the list price, will now pay 15 per cent. of the list price. That represents an increase in the tax charge of 28 per cent.

Those who use their cars for more than 18,000 miles a year--the heavy business users--are being required to pay a far bigger increase than those who use their cars less. Clive Tulloch, a tax partner at PricewaterhouseCoopers, has calculated the effect of these changes as follows:

    'The company car driver who does between 2,500 and 17,999 business miles a year, is a basic-rate taxpayer, and owns a new £15,000 car, will be about £57.50 worse off as a result of these changes. The same driver in the same tax bracket with the same kind of car who does more than 18,000 miles each year will be £115 worse off.'

That is considerably more. Mr. Tulloch continues:

    'I doubt if the changes will kill off the company car, but it will become more expensive.'

The disproportionate increase being levied on those who use their cars for more than 18,000 miles each tax year has led to considerable criticism from the professional bodies. The Chartered Institute of Taxation said:

    'Whilst acknowledging the policy of the reduction in the discounts given for business miles driven, we would suggest that the increase for those driving more than 18,000 miles in a tax year is unfair, given that those employees who use their cars for insubstantial business purposes will not incur an increase in assessable benefit.'

Its response continued:

    'Those employees who drive more than 18,000 business miles are required to use their cars for the proper performance of their duties of employment, and the increase in their assessable benefit is likely to have an adverse impact on businesses.'

The Institute of Chartered Accountants made a similar point. It stated:

    'We are puzzled by this provision as it seems to penalise those who are obliged to travel extensively in the course of their work.'

It cited the example of a farm or forestry manager in Scotland who might have to cover long distances regularly, and would have no alternative public transport option. Such a person would bear a disproportionate increase in tax.
