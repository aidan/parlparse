Mr. Tom Brake (Carshalton and Wallington): I beg to move amendment No. 31, in page 5, line 3, leave out from "time" to end of line 5.

The purpose of the amendment is to remove the 12-month limit. The 12-months during which people have either to accept or to decline a water meter is an arbitrary period. It is certainly not sufficiently long for people to gain a clear understanding of what their water bills are likely to be with a meter, because the use of water from one year to the next may vary significantly, depending on how hot or otherwise the weather is. If the limit were removed, people would be able over a longer period to establish clearly whether having a water meter was to their advantage.

I hope that the Minister will be willing to accept the amendment on those grounds alone, but it would also help to reduce the possibility of creeping metering. Under the Bill as drafted, once people have accepted their meter, that will be it, they will be stuck with it. If the limit were withdrawn, consumers would have a much longer period in which to assess the meter, and so some of those who initially thought that they wanted to stay with a meter might revert to non-metered charges.

The amendment is straightforward, and I hope that the Government will be willing to support it. Removing the 12-month limit will allow consumers a longer period in which to establish whether there are advantages to water metering, and I hope that the Minister will respond positively to the amendment.

As regards the 12-month period, we could have chosen a different period. It is a matter if judgment as to what is right.

The amendment raises an important point about the right that we are giving consumers under the Bill to have a meter fitted. I want briefly to explain why we have included the right to revert to an unmeasured charge in the Bill before I explain why we have deliberately time limited it.

Paying for water on a measured basis might be attractive to some people, such as those who live on their own and who are likely to use only limited amounts of water. There is evidence that people who pay on a measured basis are more economical with water and use perhaps up to 10 per cent. less.

The right that we are introducing to revert to an unmeasured charge is in line with the focus of the Bill another piece of consumer protection. One way of looking at it is to compare it with the protection that is given under consumer credit legislation. Under such legislation, consumers can change their minds about a product or service for which they have paid within a specified period.

The hon. Member for Carshalton and Wallington asked why we want to time limit the right to revert. Our answer is that it is right to strike a balance between providing greater choice for consumers and ensuring stability in water charging. We are keen to ensure that there is a proper balance between customer choice and certainty about future bills. Our view I cannot prove that this is exactly right is that a period of about a year provides the right balance between adequate consumer protection and providing water companies with certainty about future revenues. As a result, it provides consumers with certainty about the level of their bills.

For that reason, the right to revert is important. If there is to be stability and an assurance for water companies, however, that right has to be time limited. A year is a reasonable period. The hon. Gentleman said that it was not sufficiently long for consumers to make a measured judgment about whether metering was appropriate for them. I disagree a year is long enough. For those reasons, I hope that he understands the rationale behind the Bill and will not press his amendment.
