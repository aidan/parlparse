Mr. Chidgey: The amendment gives us the opportunity to take the arguments a little further, bearing in mind the comments made in the debate on the previous group of Government amendments.

As the Bill stands, clause 27 gives the Director General of Fair Trading the power to require the production of documents that he considers to be relevant to the investigation. Subsection (6)(a)(ii) gives him the power to require persons, including former employees, to provide an explanation of such documents.

We agree that the director general needs strong investigatory powers to ensure that competition policy works effectively; it is often pointed out that these powers give the Competition Act the necessary teeth. We are not arguing about that, but the amendments would ensure that the director general could make a judgment with the benefit of the full facts. We intend not to influence the director general but to ensure that the company has the opportunity to comment on the role of the employee before he reaches his decision. If such information is not made available before the director general's decision, a company may find itself wrongly accused of breaching a prohibition and seek to appeal.

The rules for compensating a company are not spelt out in the clause, but I believe that under schedule 8, which we shall discuss later, the rules on costs and expenses from the appeals tribunal will be laid down after the Bill has been passed. Would they be helped indirectly by the removal of a competitor from the market or directly by the lack of after-sales service? There would be no continuity, which would create a gap.

It is worth asking the Minister to clarify whether "bringing to an end" means immediately, or whether the director general will have regard to other considerations as a sensible digression of the infringement to bring matters under control in a reasonable period. The points are not nit-picking. It is important that the director general addresses them. If we are interested in competition policy, we need to ensure that the consequences of enforcement are not unnecessarily onerous on business or likely to give rise, because of literal interpretation, to consequences that we would not wish to see.

Mr. Griffiths: Hon. Members will be aware that similar amendments to the clause were debated in another place. I see no reason to change our view. Before any action is taken under clause 33, the director general will have considered whether there has been an infringement of the chapter I prohibition and reached a decision that there has been. He will then judge what directions are appropriate to bring the infringement to an end. We have no reason to suppose that the director general will give directions that go beyond what is necessary to end an infringement, and there is the safeguard that the giving of a direction under clause 33 can, of course, be the subject of an appeal under clause 47.

Amendment No. 170 would restrict the persons to whom the director general may give directions, for the purpose of bringing the infringement to an end, just to parties to the agreement. Clause 33 is widely drawn to include "such person or persons" as the director general

    "considers appropriate to bring the infringement to an end" .

That includes individuals and corporate bodies.

As discussed in another place, it is quite possible that the undertakings involved in infringements will be part of, or the whole of, a complex corporate group structure. The immediate party to an infringement agreement may not be the actual instigator of that infringement. A subsidiary may be the entity directly involved, but the infringement of the chapter I prohibition may have been instigated by the parent company. As was said in another place, the director general must have the ability to get at the real culprit and his power to give directions should not be circumscribed in this way.
