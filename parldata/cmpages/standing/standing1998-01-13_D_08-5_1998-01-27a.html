Mr. Ian McCartney: On behalf of the Committee, I wish you good morning, Mrs. Dunwoody.

In the short debate that we had, several questions were asked--some about principle, some about technical detail and some relating to both. I hope to deal with them, although perhaps not to the satisfaction of all hon. Members.

I was asked about the roles of the Prime Minister and the Secretary of State. The Bill's arrangement follows the precedent that was set by the teacher pay review board, which was set up by the previous Administration. The Prime Minister appoints the members of that board and he was similarly involved in appointing the members of the Low Pay Commission. Although I do not always agree with Opposition Members, at least there was some agreement between us on the principle of appointments. I hope that that satisfies hon. Members.

The Bill requires the Low Pay Commission to report to the Prime Minister and the Secretary of State. As is usual when taking decisions on matters of importance, there will obviously be collective discussion within the Government before any decision is taken on the report. All Ministers with an input into the issue, including my right hon. Friend the Chancellor of the Exchequer will participate in the Government's collective decision making.

The regulations that will flow from the Government's decisions on the Low Pay Commission's recommendations will be made by the Secretary of State alone, as provided for in clauses 1(3), 3(2) and 4(1). The Government's view is that the commission should have a continuing role and that it could reconvene as required to consider any issues relating to the minimum wage. Clause 8, which I hope we shall debate later this morning, gives Ministers that power. It enables us to consult the commission before using the main regulation-making powers in clauses 1 to 4--for example, before changing the rate itself. The clause is crucial in giving us the flexibility to consult the commission, but it does not require us to do so .

We have never before had a single statutory minimum rate in the United Kingdom and we may need to do some fine tuning once the Bill has been implemented. When drafting the Bill, we cannot possibly know how matters will develop in the future--hence the need for flexibility and the wide range of matters that can be referred to the commission at the appropriate time.

The clause gives us the flexibility to consult the commission on any matters relating to the minimum wage, as well as any changes to the rate, the calculation or the modifications under clauses 3 and 4. That could, for example, include studies on the impact of the minimum wage and advice on enforcement. The Commission could also be asked to consider more specific matters such as the effect of the minimum wage on any of the special classes of people described in clauses 31 to 37, or the need to extend the minimum wage to others using the powers in clause 38. For the convenience of the Committee, those clauses relate to agency workers, home workers, the Crown armed forces, mariners, and House of Commons and House of Lords staff, while clause 38 contains powers to prevent avoidance by applying the provisions to those who may not be defined as workers.

There is no requirement for the Secretary of State to hold further consultations--she is required only to consult before setting an initial rate under clause 5. It is not necessary to compel future consultation with the Commission; the Secretary of State is free, if she chooses, to proceed directly with any regulations once the initial rate has been set. If, however, she thinks that it is sensible for the Commission to consider an issue relating to the national minimum wage, the clause enables her to consult.

Opposition Members asked why we have included a non-consultation option in clause 6 and wanted to know under what circumstances it might be used. The provision gives us an alternative route to make changes to the national minimum wage when reference to the Low Pay Commission may not be considered appropriate. For example--[Interruption.] I will give a clear example. If hon. Members wait, they will see what I mean.

The regulations might require amendment because they do not cover particular wage circumstances, whether through an oversight or otherwise. For example, a component of pay might not have been considered fully or appropriately, but there may be an opportunity for the Secretary of State to examine such a case. Problems of technical drafting might also arise.

Such problems in regulation arise under every Administration, especially in relation to new legislation, when regulations are introduced. I remember that such problems arose from the Jobseekers Act 1995. The previous Government returned to the House on many occasions, over several months. The then Secretary of State did not ask the commission that examined social security issues, the relevant Committee, or the House to draft amendments to the regulations.

I hope that the Government will avoid having to table such amendments, but procedures must exist in case they cannot.

Mrs. Roche: I recognise that the hon. Member for Eastleigh wants to ensure, like me, that the Low Pay Commission follows sound procedures when making reports. Clause 7(3) provides flexibility. When bodies such as the commission are tasked with reporting to Ministers, it is usual to provide a power to extend the reporting deadline, which is sensible; indeed, it would be unrealistic not to do so. We of course recognise that unforseen circumstances may mean that a deadline must be extended. Without such a power, a report could be invalidated if the commission missed a deadline of five minutes or so, which would be absurd. However, the amendment is unnecessary because the Bill does not prevent the commission from requesting an extension. There is therefore no need to enable the commission to make such a request.

It is a general legal principle in this country that things can be done unless the law prevents them. That is the case here. If the commission wanted to extend the time allowed for making a report, it could simply ask for an extension. There is no need to make express provision in the Bill. It is up to the Secretary of State to decide whether to extend the deadline. I hope that the Committee agrees that that is right, as the Secretary of State requested the report and set the deadline in the first place.

The Government amendments are technical changes to the Bill's fine detail, designed to simplify the clause and clarify procedures. They do not change the policy behind the clause.

The general effect of clause 8 is to give the existing, non-statutory Low Pay Commission automatic statutory status unless the Secretary of State decides otherwise. The Government amendments essentially are transitional provisions to cover the way in which the existing Low Pay Commission fits into the legislative framework.

Subsections (3) and (4) give the Secretary of State power to decide that references to the existing, non-statutory Low Pay Commission are not to be treated as though they were references to a statutory Low Pay Commission. Subsection (5) also allows the Secretary of State not to recognise reports by the non-statutory Low Pay Commission.

I shall explain the purposes of the provisions. The powers of derecognition act as a safety net. They give the Secretary of State a second option: for example, to make it clear that the existing commission was not functioning properly or that the report was seriously defective. The Secretary of State already has the option of overriding a defective report. The derecognition power provides a further option for her to make a new referral instead to a new statutory commission.
