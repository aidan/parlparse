On resuming—

Mr. Mike O'Brien: Clause 95 is a necessary step in the creation of the new scheme for asylum seekers. It excludes persons subject to immigration control from income-based jobseeker's allowance and from other specified social security benefits.

The current position is something of a mess. Asylum seekers who make their asylum claims at ports of entry are able to obtain social security benefits, at least until their initial application has been determined. After that, they cease to be able to claim ordinary social security benefits and they are supported by local authorities. Those who apply in country are not able to obtain social security benefits. Instead, they must look to social services legislation for emergency support, using it in a way that was never intended. That has also meant that, where the benefits are local authority based, certain areas have suffered an excessive burden.

Some authorities have sea ports or airports within their compass; others, either through tradition or through natural accretion, have become home to concentrations of particular groups. Asylum seekers have tended to gravitate towards those areas, or finding themselves there, have remained there by inertia. That has resulted in some areas shouldering more of the social and financial burdens of providing for asylum seekers than others. That is inherently unfair.

Direct cash benefits provided under the social security system have proved to be a magnet, drawing economic migrants to the United Kingdom and undermining the integrity of the asylum system. Our objective is to restore its integrity. Economic migrants have placed intolerable demands on the system, making it more difficult to give swift consideration to the cases of genuine asylum seekers. It is essential that we deal with the problems in the system, enable genuine refugees to be recognised and helped quickly and deal with the backlog.

Removing access to benefit, which is a factor for economic migrants, will, in the long term, help to restore integrity to the system and enable genuine refugees to be recognised more quickly. Less of a burden will be provided by those seeking mere economic migration rather than having a genuine claim under the 1951 convention. That group is important for the purpose of excluding people from a range of benefits. Therefore, I hope that the Committee will support the clause.

I shall deal with the withdrawal of disabled living allowance, which was mentioned by my hon. Friend the Member for Hackney, North and Stoke Newington. The provisions of clause 75(1) will allow for higher levels of payment for disabled people and others if their essential support needs require that. The Government's policy in respect of other people who are subject to immigration control is that they should not have direct access to state-funded Department of Social Security benefits.

The Bill will enable us to support the disabled and those who have special needs. In due course, we shall introduce the requisite regulations that will enable us to do that. Given those reassurances, I hope that the Committee will approve the clause.

Clause 96

Amendment of section 21 of the National Assistance Act 1948

Amendment made: No. 785, in page 56, line 7, at end insert—

    ``but for the references in subsections (5) and (7) to the Secretary of State substitute references to a local authority''.—[Mr. Mike O'Brien.]

Question proposed, That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Allan: Again, I am uncomfortable about the withdrawal of safety nets, especially given the context and history of the National Assistance Act 1948. It was clearly pioneering social welfare legislation that was introduced by a Labour Government in the spirit of optimism that followed the second world war—a time of so much pioneering social legislation in Britain.

We debate the clause in that context because the courts deemed it sufficiently encompassing to cover those who would otherwise have been left utterly destitute by the 1996 Act, which was introduced by the previous Government. Lord Justice Simon Brown made an important and significant judgment when he deemed that no one in a civilised country should be left utterly destitute. The Government now propose to remove that safety net from this category of people in a way similar to the removal of benefits, their defence obviously being that the new support arrangements provide an alternative method of saving a person from destitution.

In earlier debates, we heard about people being exempt from the support arrangements—perhaps because of an act that they had carried out, or a rule that they had broken. So our disappointment remains that a safety net in the 1948 legislation that was introduced in a pioneering social welfare sense, and designed to cover all people resident in the country, is being removed.

I question the Government's motive. I understand the wish to remove the burden from local authorities, but I wonder whether, if the 1948 Act's provisions remained in force, the Government would be able to answer Lord Justice Simon Brown's point by saying that we now have a new support system for asylum seekers and that therefore there is no harm in leaving it in place. After all, the Government have admitted that they will provide for the vast majority of asylum seekers through their support arrangements, and have said that people will no longer have recourse to the 1948 Act under the terms by which they were given access by the courts, because access was conditional on there being no support arrangements whatever as the 1996 Act envisaged them. It is regrettable that the Government have withdrawn the ultimate safety net. The only justification that they can claim is that there are strong grounds for thinking that it would be brought into play and would cause an on-going problem for local authorities. I know of no evidence to support that. If an asylum seeker went to the courts in the same way as people took test cases to court under the 1996 Act, on the basis of the 1948 Act a court could properly say, ``No. You have support from the Asylum Support Directorate. The National Assistance Act does not apply.'', so the earlier local authority problem would no longer exist. Taking away the safety net seems an unnecessary belt-and-braces approach. In symbolic terms, it is a retrograde step that will create a class of people whom the Minister says he does not wish to be socially excluded but who will be excluded from social welfare provision. I fail to see the difference between social exclusion and a category of people who are excluded from all the normal benefits and support arrangements that are available to others but depend on the Secretary of State's largesse when he interprets the regulations to be made in his support arrangements.

I shall be interested to hear if the Minister can offer reasons for why this is necessary since these utterly destitute people will not exist because he will have provided for them in other ways. Does he have advice of which I am not aware to suggest that asylum seekers would still be able to present themselves to local authorities under the National Assistance Act, if they had an acceptable and reasonable offer from the Asylum Support Directorate that the courts considered made the person not so destitute that, in Lord Justice Simon Brown's terms, he would have to bring the Bill into play?

Mr. O'Brien: The 1948 Act has an important role in delivering care to the community. Asylum seekers with specific needs will continue to be eligible for such support, However, the Act was never intended to provide financial support and accommodation for asylum seekers solely because of their destitution. When the clause takes effect, the Act will cease to be necessary for that purpose.

The clause also removes the entitlement from others who were never intended to benefit from it. Asylum seekers will no longer be able to say, ``We refuse to accept the provisions of the Asylum Support Directorate and instead will rely on the alternative provisions under the National Assistance Act.'' If we did not remove the entitlement, an alternative system would be available to asylum seekers, who could say ``I don't choose to accept that provision. Instead, I shall exercise my rights under the National Assistance Act.'' Although we are setting up an alternative scheme, the other provisions still have effect in law. Therefore, we must remove those provisions to avoid arbitrage, whereby asylum seekers choose the support system under which they wish to operate.

Question put and agreed to.
