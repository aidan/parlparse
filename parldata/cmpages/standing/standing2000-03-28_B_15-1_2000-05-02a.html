Mr. Green: We turn to clause 20 in chapter II, which deals with exclusion or restriction of access. The first two amendments in the group relate to clause 20, and the third and fourth relate to clause 21. Their purpose is to enable exclusion or restriction of access to part of the land in question, as well as its totality. All members of the Committee will agree that we must make the Bill as workable as possible. At the same time, we must maximise the benefit to those who own and occupy the land, and those who seek access for recreational purposes. The amendments go to the heart of that aim.

We contend that closure of part of an area of access land ought not to count as closure of the entire area. The Bill should be so structured that it is in the interests of the owner or manager of the land to keep open as much land as possible for as long as possible. As such, the definitions of closure should encourage landowners to change the nature of access, but in a way that will not necessarily forfeit one of the precious 28 days of closure time that the Bill will grant.

Members of the Committee will be aware that we shall discuss the 28-day limitation and other limitations in due course, so I shall not go into that now. However, even if we assume that those limitations will endure, the Bill could be changed in a way that would increase the quantum of access land available, and the period for which it is available. In that respect, we are at one with the Government. The Minister said several times that he wants the Bill to be flexible. The amendments would introduce a new element of flexibility that would encourage landowners to allow greater access for longer periods.

As a practical example, where access to a moor is restricted because burning or shooting is taking place on a small part of it, it might be possible to grant access to the rest. Clearly, it would be unfortunate and unnecessary to close hundreds of acres of access land where only a relatively small area was needed for burning or shooting. The amendment recognises that land mapped as access land should be closable in part as well as in whole. As such, the amendment facilitates an interpretation of the Bill that will encourage opening and co-operation.

We seek greater clarity by eliciting the Under-Secretary's views on this suggestion, and we also seek greater flexibility. Landowners should be encouraged to allocate their 28 days—or whatever limitation is decided on—in a way that that will not seem onerous. They should be allowed the flexibility to manage land in a sensible way, while allowing the maximum degree of access. I trust that the Under-Secretary will accept that the amendment would increase the quantum of access, and I commend it to the Committee.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Chris Mullin): We share the concern expressed by the hon. It is both sensible and reasonable to provide this flexibility in the Bill and we believe that the provision will help to ensure that land management needs are met. The Bill already achieves the objective that the hon. Gentleman seeks, and I therefore invite him to withdraw the amendment.

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): As I have learned to expect, the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath) has proposed an interesting amendment. It has prompted us to think again about whether those who will have absolute discretion to restrict or exclude access should be expected or, indeed, required to inform more people than just the relevant authority—that is, the Countryside Agency or the Countryside Council for Wales or, in the case of a national park, the national park authority. I should explain that the purpose of the requirement for notice in clause 21(1) is to ensure that any discretionary closures or restrictions are properly notified to the relevant authority in order for them to have effect.

I understand and share the hon. Gentleman's wish to ensure that those with interests in the land are informed of actions that may affect them. That is a perfectly reasonable principle. The proposal that those entitled to use discretionary closures should be obliged to notify any other entitled person of their intention to exclude or restrict access is, therefore—at first sight—attractive. However, we need to be clear about the circumstances in which such a requirement would be justified. For example, the issue is likely to arise only if the 28 days had been apportioned between different people. That could happen, of course, if there were separate shooting rights, for example in a case in which the 28 days has been apportioned between a tenant farmer and someone holding separate shooting rights. I am not persuaded that there will be a need for the farmer to tell those with shooting rights that the land will be closed to the public. To those with shooting rights, it would not matter when the land, or part thereof, was closed by the farmer. Similarly, it is difficult to see why a tenant farmer would need to know that people were not allowed onto the land for a period because shooting was taking place. Of course, I am not against communication. There is nothing to prevent the different parties from informing each other, and we would expect that to happen often, in practice—for example, one party might agree to look out for people who breach the closure.

On balance, I am not persuaded that we need a provision along the lines suggested by the hon. Gentleman. As I have said, we wish to apply a light touch, in terms of the burden on those who will administer the system of closures, but equally, we wish to avoid adding what might be a significant burden for landowners and others who wish to close the land or otherwise restrict access under their discretionary entitlement. We certainly, and here I agree with the hon. Gentleman, wish to encourage good practice, but we believe that that should be achieved through the agreement of the parties concerned rather than by compulsion. Again, we sympathise with the objectives of the amendment. We strongly support good practice, but believe that it is over-bureaucratic to make it compulsory. On those grounds and having listened to the argument, I ask the hon. Gentleman to seek to withdraw the amendment.
