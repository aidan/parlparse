Dr. Brand: I hope that the Bill will have swift passage through Committee, and that we shall discuss matters of substance. Committee members may be surprised that I should say that at the outset of deliberations on this group of amendments, as it might appear, on first glance, that adding the phrase, ``young persons'' after ``child'' is simply a matter of presentational gloss. However, I am concerned about the fact that the Bill will be a working instrument that will affect many young people, so it is important to strike the right tone in the relationship between care leavers, the eligible child or young person and the surrogate parent or local authority. Young persons who are over 16 will object if they are affected by legislation that is called the Children (Leaving Care) Act and if they are described as children in the regulations on which they have to depend.

I am grateful to the Minister of State for his letter, in which he tried to deal with some of the matters that I raised on Second Reading. I am not convinced that we cannot improve the Bill's phraseology so that it acknowledges that young persons should be active participants in the process of their being looked after. I appreciate that parliamentary draftsmen are not too concerned about what people think of legislative descriptions that are supposed to apply to them, but people have to live with the consequences of legislation, and when one is trying to persuade young persons to work with a local authority, it is important to accord them the dignity and respect that they deserve. When one is 15 or 16, and certainly when one is 17 or 18, there is nothing worse than being described as a child, which is paternalistic and, to be frank, extraordinarily bad manners and so likely to set the relationship off on a poor footing. I urge the Minister to think again. I realise that the situation is complicated because the Bill amends another Act—the Children Act 1989—but I am sure that the ministerial advisory team contains sufficient ingenuity to deal with the problem.

Let me now deal with amendment No. 24. Although the Bill is a worthy one, it is likely to produce a fairly rigid straitjacket unless adequate flexibility is introduced. A young person in care who wishes to continue in care may strike up a stable relationship, although he may no longer feel that he should continue to be part of an arrangement that involves being parented by a local authority. We shall discuss that is more detail when we reach amendment No. 34. I have frequently come across cases in which children cope with a family—perhaps that is the best way to put it—and families cope with their children up to the age of about 16. They have not gone into care, but are extremely vulnerable and need the support and the parenting role of the local authority. One of the worthy aims of the Bill is to provide young persons with access to the direct support of a local authority acting in loco parentis, but such young people would be denied such access.

Will the Minister look at the qualifying criteria for being an eligible child? I realise that the Bill was drafted before the amendment was accepted in the House of Lords, so we might have a problem discussing a different category of older children and young persons. If we accept the provision without amendment, we could create difficulties for vulnerable children and young people whose family relationships have broken down after the age of 16—15 years and nine months, if we adopt a three-month qualifying care period, and 15 years and six months if a six-month period is chosen—because they might be unable to benefit from this extremely positive measure.

On those grounds, I move the amendment and look forward, as usual, to the Minister's reply.
