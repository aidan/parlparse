Data Protection Bill [Lords]

Clause 31

Journalism literature and art

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 289, in page 20, line 5, leave out `either' and insert `one'.--[Mr. Greenway.]

4.30 pm

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 281, in page 20, line 11, at end insert

    (c) that in any proceedings the court or the tribunal has, on the application of any party, made a determination to the like effect to a determination which the Commissioner could have made under section 43, with respect to the data in question.'.

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I was saying by way of a preamble to amendments Nos. 280 and 281 that subsection (4) provides for proceedings against a data controller to be stayed. Will the Minister consider first and foremost what the reason for action by a data subject might be? I want us to consider the mechanism by which the provisions in subsections (4) and (5) would work.

Essentially, an individual data subject would seek to make use of the data subject access provisions in clauses 7, 9, 12 or 13--the order has, I think, been reversed by an earlier decision of the Committee. What would the reason for data subject's action be in such circumstances? Would it be to stop the processing of material that was likely to cause damage or distress? That is the provision in clause 9. Would it be to correct inaccurate data that might, as we discussed earlier, be based on gossip and third-hand information, or would it simply be to find out what information the data controller had in his files and records? Those are all valid interests for the data subject to pursue.

At that point, he would not know for certain the data processor's intentions.

Subsection (4) refers to

    ``any time . . . in any proceedings against a data controller under section 7(9), 9(4), 12 or 13(8) or by virtue of section 11''.

It is at that point that the data controller would say, ``I am processing the data because publication would be in the public interest in freedom of expression and I intend to publish.'' He would say that he intended to rely on the defence in clause 31(1)(b) and (c).

The Bill would then provide for a stay on the proceedings that the data subject had instituted. His efforts to find out the information, to correct inaccuracy or to block the processing would be stayed until such time as a determination had been made under the provisions of subsection (5) on whether the data controller's public interest defence was valid.

The Government's clear intention is that any proceedings to enforce data subject access rights in those circumstances must be stayed pending a determination on the public interest test.

I want both Ministers to be clear that we entirely agree with the principle that underlies the decision to stay. The phrase that is often used in that context is the ``Saturday afternoon injunction''. We are entirely at one with the Government if they intend that the data subject access provisions should not be used as a means for somebody to try to block a publication.

However, the more one investigates the issue, the more one asks further questions. For example, does any stay by the commissioner or by a court freeze the right to publish until the commissioner or the court has ruled? Would it not offend natural justice if publication were allowed to take place before the public interest test that is underpinned by the special purposes exemption is determined? Material could be published prior to the determination in favour of the data subject that the public interest test was not valid. In other words, the data controller's argument would not be upheld. Where does the balance of justice lie in such circumstances--in allowing publication, or in blocking it?

If a court rules that publication must be frozen pending the outcome of the determination, how could the provisions be used by a data subject to circumvent the alternative actions that might be available to him? Those actions would inevitably be affected by the general principle of the Bonnard v. Perryman ruling, which states that if someone seeks to block a publication using the Saturday afternoon injunction, the courts must exercise discretion in favour of the publisher. I was told by an eminent Queen's Counsel who practises in this area that, over the years, there have been many attempts to get around the Bonnard v. Perryman ruling--that is, to find a means of blocking a publication.

Is it the Government's view that the Bonnard v. Perryman test would apply to any proceedings arising under these provisions? For example, a data subject may say to a court, ``I have an inkling that this particular data controller has information about me which I believe to be inaccurate. He intends to publish it and I would like to exercise my rights under the provisions of this Bill to find out what he has.'' The data controller may then go to the court and say, ``No--we are going to rely on the public interest test and the proceedings are stayed.'' It is not clear whether the legislation requires that the proceedings should be stayed in those circumstances. It was certainly not clear to the person from whom I sought legal advice whether there could be a freezing of the right to publish in such a case. That is crucial, and it goes to the heart of amendments Nos. 280 and 281.

The Data Protection Registrar and her officials have told the Minister and his officials that they do not like the provisions of subsection (5). Hon. Members who have studied the responses sent to the Committee by the Data Protection Registrar and others cannot fail to have noticed the deep anxiety that they reveal about the conditions set out in subsection (5)--that the commissioner shall determine whether the public interest test is valid, and that the matter may go to a tribunal following that determination.

I have a briefing note from the Data Protection Registrar. I should be happy to copy it for the Committee, although I suspect that the Minister and his officials know its contents. It proposes a timetable in which the data subject applies to the court for compensation under section 11 of the Data Protection Act 1984, the data controller claims that he is processing for the special purposes, and then the application is stayed pending determination by the Data Protection Commissioner. It is suggested that that process would take six months.

A different route, taking four months, would involve the data controller applying to the Data Protection Commissioner for assessment under section 41 of the 1984 Act. If the commissioner considers the request of the data subject and needs further information, he would serve a special information notice on the data controller. It would take another 28 days to allow an appeal to be lodged. If the data controller appealed against the notice that he had not met the public interest test, the matter would go to a tribunal, taking the period involved up to eight months. The process is extremely long and drawn out.

I discussed the matter with my right hon. Friend the shadow Home Secretary. Our feeling was that circumstances may exist in which that would be the appropriate method for dealing with the determination. So we do not suggest that the provisions in subsection (5) should be altered and an alternative procedure substituted. Amendments Nos. 280 and--especially--281 would provide that a court could make the determination as well as the commissioner or a tribunal.

The Data Protection Registrar's understanding is that the Bill does not intend the public interest test to be determined by a court, only by the commissioner, subject to appeal to a tribunal. The amendment proposes the additional choice of using a court to make the determination.

Subsection (5) sets out the conditions as follows:

    ``(a) that a determination of the Commissioner under section 44 with respect to the data in question takes effect, or

    (b) in a case where the proceedings were stayed on the making of a claim, that the claim is withdrawn.''

This is an obvious choice.

Amendment No. 281 offers the third choice

    ``( ) that in any proceedings the court or the tribunal has, on the application of any party, made a determination to the like effect to a determination which the Commissioner could have made under section 43, with respect to the data in question.'.''

The application therefore could be made by either party, and the amendment provides an alternative way to deal with the matter.

Neither the Bill nor the notes on clauses make it clear why the court cannot decide either that the processing is not for the special purposes or that it is not done with a view to publication. The Bill is so procedurally complex that an ordinary person could be given an extremely hard time, whether by media organisations or by unmeritorious data controllers abusing the provisions of clause 31(4). The restriction of the Data Protection Commissioner and the specification of the time taken together confine the value of the provisions to those with deep pockets. The amendment would mean that a court or tribunal could clear that statutory blockage on the application of a party if it were satisfied about the matters set out in clause 43. The appeal would lie in the usual way, and the court would determine the matter.

4.45 pm

The Minister must tell the Committee why the courts cannot be trusted with the power proposed by the amendment, which was not tabled with the intention of weakening the ability to stay the proceedings. My initial questions are important because they concern such weighty and complex arrangements, which is why hon. Members will have to return to these matters on Report. If the staying of proceedings were to block publication, it would be highly likely that the data controller seeking to publish would prefer an alternative resolution that is speedier than the several months' procedure--I understand that purely on the word of the registrar--laid out in the Bill. The amendment would provide nothing but flexibility. As I said, the preference of the Data Protection Commissioner--the current registrar--is a determination by the court. We are not suggesting that; we believe that it should be an alternative. If publication were blocked on the staying of proceedings, a data controller or data subject might prefer the more speedy process of determination by the court.

I have outlined as best I can the questions in Opposition Members' minds. I again emphasise our belief that the stay on proceedings pending a determination is the right approach, as my right hon. Friend the Member for North-West Cambridgeshire (Sir B. Mawhinney) made clear on Second Reading. Given the uncertainties regarding implementation in relation to the courts and to the legal profession, which are far more expert than the Minister or I, the alternative of allowing a court to determine the public interest test would benefit the legislation.

The hon. Gentleman also asked whether any stay froze the right to stay publication pending a court decision, where the balance of justice lay, and whether it could be used to circumvent action.

The commissioner's determination, which lifts the stay on proceedings, is not related to the public interest as the hon. Gentleman seemed to suggest. It is--as clause 4 provides--about whether personal data are being processed solely for the special purposes or whether they are being processed with a view to publication of any previously unpublished material. The stay on proceedings to which the hon. Gentleman referred would not block publication. It allows processing to continue until such time as the determination is made. It is not for special interests and, for that matter, not with a view to fresh publication. I hope that that is helpful. It is the court action and not the proceedings that is stayed. The processing itself is unaffected in the meantime.

The hon. Member for Ryedale also asked about timing. The tribunal's rules of procedure can, in appropriate cases, provide for a fast-track approach to appeals against a determination by the commissioner. The hon. Gentleman made the reasonable point that all this would often be far too complex for individuals to understand. I accept that. However, clause 52 provides that the commissioner can assist individuals in bringing proceedings. That would involve making the provisions understandable, so that those individuals could make their way through the complex language.

The hon. Gentleman explained that his amendment would add a further condition justifying lifting the stay on proceedings that involved special purposes material under clause 31(4). Subsection (4) deals with circumstances in which proceedings are being taken against a controller involving processing for special purposes. It provides for the courts to stay those proceedings in certain circumstances. The stay on proceedings may be lifted only where one of the conditions in subsection (5) is met. Those conditions are

    ``that a determination of the Commissioner under section 44 with respect to the data in question takes effect'',

or that, if the proceedings were based on a claim by the controller under subsection (4), that claim is withdrawn.

The hon. Gentleman's amendment would add a third condition. The stay on proceedings could be lifted where the court or tribunal--I assume that that means the Data Protection Tribunal--had, in respect of the data in question, made a determination similar to that which the commissioner could have made under clause 44. The amendment refers to clause 43, but I assume that it should refer to clause 44. However, I shall not quibble about that.

I shall come straight to the point on this amendment. The Government cannot accept it because it would fundamentally undermine the purpose of the exemption provided in clause 31.

That clause, taken with other related provisions in the Bill, in particular clauses 43, 44 and 45, establishes a carefully worked out mechanism for ensuring that the Bill properly meets the requirement in article 9 of the directive for processing for the special purposes to reconcile individuals' right to privacy with freedom of expression. That is a key element from which we should not depart too far.

The directive provides for ccertain exemptions for that purpose. The mechanism built around clause 31 delivers them, as I noted in detail this morning. The Committee knows very well, in the light of our debate, what the Government's position is, and the reasons why we have followed the approach that we have.

We think it right that there should be no possibility of challenge to processing for the special purposes, prior to publication. We wish to avoid what has been called the chilling effect of pre-publication restrictions. That is, in our view, wholly inconsistent with freedom of expression. A central thrust of the arrangements made in clause 31 and the associated provisions is to achieve that principle.

Subsections (4) and (5) of clause 31, together with later provisions, are directed at ensuring that no injunctions or other forms of relief depending solely on data protection considerations may be used to stop processing pre-publication in special purposes cases. The amendment would seriously weaken this carefully constructed mechanism by allowing applications to be made by third parties for injunctive and other proceedings relating to the special purposes to go ahead prior to publication.

The major flaw in the idea of just allowing the criteria in clause 31(4) to be litigated in the way suggested by this amendment is that it would indeed reopen the door to injunctions relief and the often unwelcome effect of pre-publication court action on journalism in progress. We want to avoid issues that should be considered by the courts being dealt with in another way.

If we make the criteria for the stay justiciable, we enable those with something to hide and an interest in spiking investigative journalism to raise enough of a query about the application of the criteria to engage the court's inherent jurisdiction to injunct the journalism, pending further consideration. That would be self-defeating.

I know that there is a view that the current arrangements are too wide in that they allow any controller to claim that he was processing for the special purposes and to secure a stay on proceedings, whether or not that was the case. That is an important point, but the Bill does not neglect it. It deals with it through the power that it gives the Data Protection Commissioner to issue special information notices under clause 43.

That power enables her to make the necessary inquiries to determine whether processing is as a matter of fact being carried out for the special purposes with a view to publication of any previously unpublished material. The powers apply where she has received a request for an assessment under clause 41, or where she has reasonable grounds for suspecting that the processing in question does not satisfy the relevant issues.

If she finds that the processing is not solely for the special purposes, or that publication of previously unpublished material is not intended, she may make a determination to that effect. Once the determination has effect, the enforcement arrangements apply. This mechanism has been included in the arrangements in the Bill expressly to ensure that there is a safeguard against the abuse that the critics of the provision fear.

In cases of very flagrant abuse of the staying provisions, the commissioner might not even need to use her special information notice power. She may be satisfied on the basis of existing evidence that it is right to make a determination. There is no reason why that could not be done very quickly in appropriate cases.

The amendment would make it a requirement for a subsection (3) enforcement notice to do the first of those, but leave the second discretionary. The hon. Had he said that it would normally be appropriate for one of those steps to be taken, the Government could have agreed. However, we cannot accept that that would invariably be necessary. Perhaps I can give some examples.

Circumstances might well arise in which it would not be right for any of those steps to be taken. One such set of circumstances is identified in subsection (3)(b), where inaccurate data accurately record wrong information given to the controller. For instance, an illness might be misdiagnosed. The patient's record will contain that inaccurate information and he or she might well have been given treatment on the strength of it. Imagine the consequences if that information had to be rectified, blocked, erased or destroyed. Supposing further treatment were needed to remedy the consequences of the wrongly given initial treatment. The information on which to base that remedial treatment would no longer be available or usable. That cannot be right.

In our view, it is better for the commissioner to determine in the light of all the circumstances of a particular contravention what obligations should be placed on the data controller. Clause 39 leaves the commissioner with that discretion. The approach is modelled closely on that of the 1984 Act.

Mr. Hoon: I am grateful to the hon. Member for Ryedale for raising the question, which he has probably already answered. I will, however, try to explain the function of clause 39(4). It deals with the situation in which rectification, blocking, erasure or destruction of inaccurate data is required by an enforcement notice, or has already taken place. It recognises that the rectification, blocking and so on, may take place after the inaccurate data have been disclosed to third parties. Accordingly, it provides that an enforcement notice may require the data controller to notify those third parties of the action taken. However, the notice may do that only if the action is ``reasonably practicable'', and has regard to the number of persons who would have to be notified.

The amendments would remove the possibility for the practicability of notifying third parties to be taken into account by the commissioner in deciding whether to include such a requirement in the enforcement notice. If the words in the amendment were taken out, that would have the curious effect that the commissioner could include such a requirement in an enforcement notice, even if it was impracticable for the data controller to comply with it.

He or she wants to comply, not only because he or she wants to do what is right, but because non-compliance is an offence. But it is simply not practicable to comply. He or she cannot do what the enforcement notice requires. He or she is, therefore, put in a position of being in breach of the notice and of facing the possibility of criminal proceedings.

That is not fair. Although the matters could be the subject of an appeal to the tribunal we would not want to put the data controller in that position in the first place.

In including the provisions we are following the line established by the directive. Article 12 of the directive is the origin of both the requirement to tell third parties of the rectification and of the proviso disapplying that requirement where it would not be reasonably practicable to comply.

The provision will be new to UK law. It is right in principle that controllers should make reasonable efforts to ensure that third parties to whom inaccurate data are disclosed are alerted to the inaccuracy and that action is taken. That is what the clause achieves. It gets the balance right. The amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Ryedale go beyond what the directive requires and the Government cannot accept them.
