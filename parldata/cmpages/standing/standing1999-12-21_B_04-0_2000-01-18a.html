The Chairman: Before I call the first speaker, I should like to make a short statement. At the beginning of our sitting last Tuesday morning, the Minister made some remarks about the Bill's applicability to Northern Ireland and the Government's intention to await the outcome of debate in the Northern Ireland Executive Committee on the subject of legislating for freedom of information in respect of Northern Ireland, which is a devolved matter. He said that it would be inappropriate for the Committee to amend the Bill in respect of Northern Ireland and that any amendments that might be needed to reflect decisions taken by the Northern Ireland Assembly about the coverage of Northern Ireland bodies would be debated on Report.

The Procedure Committee has reported on the procedural consequences of devolution. Its report supported the Government's expectation that in respect of public Bills—

    ``a convention would be adopted that Westminster would not normally legislate with regard to devolved matters without the consent of the devolved body.''

The House has debated that report, but it has not reached any decision that affects the constitutional fact that the House and its Committees retain power to legislate for all parts of the United Kingdom, even on devolved matters, until the House makes any such decision.

I have no power to rule that amendments relating to devolved matters should not be debated if they are otherwise in order. That applies to amendments dealing with devolved matters relating to Scotland and Wales as well as to Northern Ireland. It is not for me to encourage or discourage hon. Members from tabling such amendments, but I felt that I should explain that they can do so if they wish. I hope that my remarks have clarified the constitutional situation.

Mr. Robert Maclennan (Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross): I beg to move amendment No. 30, in page 5, line 11, leave out `subsection (5)' and insert `subsections (5) or (6)'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider amendment No. 31, in page 5, line 22, at end insert—

    `(6) No fee for complying with section 1 may be charged for disclosure of information—

    (a) which prior to the commencement of this Act was provided to applicants without charge; or

    (b) the disclosure of which is in the public interest.'.

Mr. Maclennan: The amendments deal with related but distinct matters. Amendment No. 30 is designed to ensure an arrangement whereby fees for information previously available would be waived to make it free of charge. Amendment No. 31 deals with disclosure in the public interest. In respect of information that is currently available, it would be anomalous if a Bill designed to open up information were to impose a charge which meant that less information was available. What does the Under-Secretary have to say about that? In parenthesis, I acknowledge that it may be regarded as unusual to deal with such matters other than in regulation. The amendment is, however, particularly significant, as it relates to ensuring that the principle of the Bill is not abrogated in practice. That principle is greater exposure, and the practice of charging could abrogate it.

There is a certain lack of clarity about the Government's intentions, so I welcome the opportunity to hear what the Under-Secretary intends. Some helpful details were contained in the Bill's explanatory notes, which state:

    ``It is proposed that the regulations governing fees will specify that up to 10 per cent. of the reasonable marginal costs of complying with the request may be charged. This will be a maximum figure and there will be no requirement on an authority to use this formula, or indeed to charge anything. The regulations will provide that the additional costs involved in providing the information in the manner or form requested . . . may be charged in addition to any fee.''

The second of the amendments raises more complex issues. It is common in freedom of information laws elsewhere to allow fees to be waived in the public interest. In paragraph 5.12 of the Government White Paper ``Your Right to Know'', a similar approach is envisaged. It states that the commissioner would have the power to waive the charge

    ``if disclosure is considered to be in the wider public interest. For example, the commissioner might consider that there is a compelling public interest in disclosure which could go by default if the applicant could not afford to meet the charge being levied''.

As I understand the matter, the proposed fee regulations could stipulate that fees should not be charged in some circumstances, but it is not clear to me that that is intended to happen when disclosure is in the public interest. Indeed, there have been some suggestions that it might be thought appropriate to charge higher fees for matters in the public interest than for ordinary disclosures. My understanding is that information that is not exempt must be disclosed, and that such mandatory disclosures will be subject to a charge of no more than 10 per cent. of the actual costs, provided those do not exceed the appropriate limit under clause 11.

The second type of disclosure that is envisaged in the Bill is of exempt information that may be disclosed on a discretionary basis, where the public interest justifies it. In that instance it is clear that a higher fee may be charged. Paragraph 58 of the Home Office consultation paper states that

    ``where an authority uses its discretion, under clause 14, to disclose information, it would also have discretion under that clause to charge a fee. It is Government policy that this should be set at a level reasonable in the circumstances and not subject to the 10 per cent. ceiling that would apply to fees under clause 9.''

That could lead to an unacceptable and, in some ways, perverse situation in which someone from a business background, seeking commercial information, would obtain non-exempt information at the 10 per cent. rate, whereas a member of the public seeking information with the object of exposing serious misconduct by a public authority would have to pay a 100 per cent. rate. That would seem anomalous and, as a matter of policy, undesirable. If such a result is not intended, it is a matter important enough to be on the face of the Bill.

It is not entirely clear, and it seems unlikely that we shall be able to elicit the relevant information in a debate on regulation according to the normal scrutiny procedures of the House, how the 10 per cent. ceiling figure would be calculated. The consultation paper gives the costs in question as

    ``the marginal cost of locating and disclosing the information.''

It appeared when that account was published that costs were not intended to include staff time spent in assessing whether information was exempt. However, in response to question 26, in annexe 6 of the Public Administration Committee report, House of Commons paper 570-I, a Home Office note stated:

    ``The costs would relate to locating the information and preparing it for disclosure, for example, any necessary editing to remove exempt material''.

If the cost of reading a document line by line against the exemptions is included when calculating fees, the cost of dealing with a request is more likely to reach the appropriate limit which, according to the Government, may be set at £500. Once the appropriate limit is reached, authorities can charge the full cost. That could mean a leap from a capped figure of 10 per cent. of the appropriate limit—£50—to one that is well in excess of £500. More seriously, once the limit is reached, the authorities could refuse to disclose the information altogether under clause 11(1), which provides that there is no duty to disclose information if costs exceed the appropriate limit.

Mr. O'Brien: These matters are best dealt with in regulations and will appear, to some extent, in the detail. I accept, however, that the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross (Mr. Maclennan) raises legitimate issues.

Let me identify what we are considering. Clause 13 ensures that there will be a charge which, in some circumstances, will be 10 per cent. of the £500 limit. It remains the case that if no fee is chargeable in legislation, no fee will be charged. The Bill does not change that. However, if an application under the Bill goes beyond existing legislation, a charge might be levied. Some public authorities do not charge for information as a matter of practice, although it might be permissible under legislation, because, depending on the number of requests that a public authority receives, it may not be worth while to set up the administrative structure to charge a fee. That may be the case with freedom of information if a public authority seldom receives requests for information. To some extent, that will be a matter for the public authority.

However, we must remember that other public authorities that previously charged no fee for applications for information, although they could have done so under legislation, may decide to establish structures and to introduce a fee because of the volume of requests produced by the Bill. They may find that worth while in certain circumstances, although I cannot imagine an example of when they might. The fee may be small, but if the £500 limit causes problems when requests are made for information under clause 11, the public authority will have to balance the interests of the taxpayer with those of the applicant. When legislating, we must remember that our duty is not only to individual applicants. Applicants may be journalists, big corporations or people who are well off—we all know that journalists are well paid. We also have a duty to individuals who are not so well off. We must consider those concerns, as well as our tax-paying constituents.

The Government have made provision under clause 8 for the Secretary of State to make regulations in relation to the issues raised. Subsection (4)(a) provides for him to prescribe by regulation those cases where no fee will be payable. Our intention in making that provision is to avoid the need to include in the Act unnecessary and potentially confusing detail that might be difficult to amend except by primary legislation. We are better able to deal with the matter by regulation. We are currently considering the detail of the regulations and hope to produce some while we are still in Committee. We shall be looking especially at circumstances in which no fee may currently be charged by the authority.

However, simply because a fee is not charged now is not sufficient reason for the information to be supplied free under the Bill. The Bill will place an additional burden on authorities and it may be appropriate, when calculating the cost of providing the information under the regulations, for the cost to be borne by the applicant, possibly with the taxpayer. It could be said that a release of information was in the public interest simply because an application for its release had been made, so no fee could be charged for any disclosure of information. I cannot believe that the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross wishes to go that far. The regulations will be the subject of secondary legislation. Once they have been tabled, hon. Members will have the opportunity to debate them if there is sufficient support. That would be the best time for us to consider the details.

We have decided to apply a charge equivalent to 10 per cent. of marginal costs. We shall deal with that matter later in regulations, but the right hon. Gentleman has raised points with a direct application to the amendments so I should say why we took the view that we did. We have chosen to provide by regulation that an authority may charge 10 per cent. of the marginal costs of supplying information because that is the average percentage of marginal costs under other common law freedom of information legislation. Unlike other jurisdictions, we are limiting the application to the marginal cost of locating and finding the information. The proposal is modest by international comparison and will provide a valuable subsidy from the taxpayer to the applicant—freedom of information will involve the taxpayer considerably subsidising the applicant. The question is whether we should go as far as allowing an applicant to have information for free whenever it is wanted; the right hon. Gentleman would not suggest that we went that far. A fee should be payable, and I believe that our approach and the sum proposed are reasonable.

Clause 11(1) allows an authority to refuse to comply with a request if the cost of compliance exceeds the appropriate limit. The authority must then consider whether to make a discretionary disclosure of the information while having regard to the circumstances of the case and the public interest. However, it cannot be compelled to disclose information. The commissioner can only recommend disclosure; she cannot require it. The amendment would make it possible to require an authority to disclose information when the appropriate limit is exceeded if the applicant is willing to pay ``a reasonable additional fee'', which would not exceed the cost to the authority of providing the information. However, we recognise that that would not be appropriate if compliance with a voluminous request interfered substantially and unreasonably with the authority's work, even if the applicant was willing to pay the additional fee.

The amendment would establish two tests: first, that interference is substantial and, secondly, that it is unreasonable. Compliance with a request could interfere substantially with an authority's work while being entirely reasonable and not inhibited by the cost. For example, if a problem with compliance arose because of the incompetence of an authority in managing its filing system, or where the issue at stake was sufficiently grave and of great public importance—perhaps involving serious misconduct by an authority—it would be reasonable to require it to locate the information and make it available. Similar arrangements have been introduced into freedom of information legislation in other countries, such as Ireland and Australia, and have not caused any difficulty. I hope that this practical and prudential amendment will commend itself to the Under-Secretary.
