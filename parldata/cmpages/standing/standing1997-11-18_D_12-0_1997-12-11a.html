Mr. Boswell: I am pleased to see you in the Chair, Mr. Butterfill, on another bright and sunny morning. It may be possible for us to conclude the Committee stage of the Bill today. It is good to see the Paymaster General in Committee and for him to have found the time to respond to our important debate when, clearly, he has several other matters on his mind.

The clause should not go undebated. I accept that my amendments are fairly narrow in scope, although my right hon. and hon. Friends may wish to go wider in the clause stand part debate. The purpose of the amendments is to probe some matters in connection with the transfer of securities gilts on the National Savings stock register to the main gilt register. We do not object in principle to such a transfer, but I should be grateful to receive the Paymaster General's comments on the important change of practice that facilitated it. We must consider the importance of national savings and ensure that those involved in the transfer receive satisfactory assurances.

It has not escaped our notice that the Government are fond of using the appellation ``the people's'' when describing some activities. It was suggested in a scurrilous journal, which I read from cover to cover, that the Bank of England Bill should rename the Bank, ``The People's Bank of Britain''. The origin of national savings, as with the Trustee Savings bank to which I referred briefly in earlier proceedings when the Economic Secretary was in Committee, was concerned very much with the people's savings. As the Paymaster General knows, we take a critical view of the Government's consultation proposals on TESSAs, PEPs and ISAs. Nevertheless, there is a common interest in the Committee of ensuring that people of relatively modest means can save economically and conveniently and one such facility in the past has been through the National Savings stock register.

Under the Finance Act 1997, as the Bill now is, all savers may receive interest gross in normal circumstances. At the time we welcomed that, and I do not dissent from it now. A particular operational distinction for keeping a separate register has now fallen, and I have no problem with that. However, the Paymaster General must give us assurances about the likely consequences of that proposal. Will the Paymaster General comment on the harmonisation of those charges? I flag up the inevitable concern that there may be some degree of creep, the net effect being higher charges for small savers than currently apply.

The Paymaster General (Mr. Geoffrey Robinson): Conservative members of the Committee have asked a number of questions, which amount to requests for reassurance on the clause. Without a merger, there would be pressure on National Savings to increase charges, and we should consider the clause against that background.
