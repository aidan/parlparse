    `(d) a way leading to any access land'.

If people cannot legally gain access to access land, they cannot enjoy it, and if they cannot legally gain access to access land, they will become frustrated and angry. On means of access, the Bill does not go far enough. The definition of means of access is restricted to situations in which the access land is adjacent to a highway or another place to which the public have a right of access. The clause refers to a way through

If a means of access is provided, it is useless unless people can legally reach that point.

    a way leading to any access land.

Mr. James Gray (North Wiltshire): This is a particularly unhelpful amendment, from the point of view of the Bill and those of us who want to encourage as much access as possible to the countryside. In that context, I had a useful series of meetings with the Ramblers Association in my constituency on Saturday. It and the landowners are beginning to operate a worthwhile modus vivendi in Wiltshire, which I hope will prove an exemplar to other areas. As I have said on previous occasions, the Bill should seek to avoid confrontation between landowners and those who seek access to the countryside.

The clause seeks to clarify how people will gain access to access land, which is welcome. It says that if there are stairs, a gate, a stile, or any of the other things specified in paragraphs (a) to (c) that provide access to land, that is reasonable, and we have not tried to change that. That seems a sensible provision to include in a Bill that we have said is unacceptable for other reasons.

I have two objections to the amendment. First, it is meaningless. The word ``way'' has no technical definition. The hon. Member for Calder Valley (Ms McCafferty) says that it is used elsewhere in the Bill, but, where it is-the Minister may want to clarify this point-it is specifically used as a general term for the means by which one gains access to access land. By contrast, the hon. Lady is not using a general term but is being specific about what she wants to make available.

Secondly, as I understand it, the hon. Lady means that a track or a path may lead across private land-non-access land-into access land, although, interestingly, she did not give any precise examples. There are three possibilities for such a track. First, it may be a public right of way anyhow. Most people who know the countryside would say that most tracks into moor, downland and heathland would have a right of way on them, in which case there is no need for it to be specified in the Bill. Secondly, it may be a private track. There is no reason why there should be access along a private track. Someone's front drive may fall into that category. Access land may be behind a house, or behind a council house, and there may be a track down the side of the house towards it. Is the hon. Lady really suggesting that the public should be given a right of access down the drive of someone's council house to achieve the access land behind it? I can think of an example in Corsham in my constituency, where there is access land behind some small private houses. The amendment would allow access as of right down people's front drives to achieve access to land.

The third possibility would be equally unhelpful. As the amendment is drafted, people could gain access to access land down a way-it is an ill-defined term-whether or not there was another means of getting to that access land. The hon. Lady is not saying, ``If this access land is totally inaccessible, but there is one track into it, that track will become an access track,'' but ``If there is any way on to this access land, it must become an access track''. I can think of a number of examples around the country where a large piece of what will now become access land has a road on to it, where people can park and gain access perfectly sensibly through a gate or across a stile, but where other tracks leading into that access land are not open to the public. The hon. Lady is saying that, no matter where the track is and how many other tracks there may be on to the access land, the public must have the right to use it. That would be unacceptable, especially in the case of a private track crossing someone's land-for example, their front drive, as I mentioned earlier-that gave access to land that might anyway have other access to it. It would be an unreasonable intrusion on the privacy of the individual and on the rights of private land ownership to suggest that people could, willy-nilly, go down any track as long as it terminated on a piece of access land.

The amendment is quite unnecessary, because the Bill already deals with the matter. Proper access will be given to access land; that is the whole point of the Bill. Taken at face value, the amendment would create a further unnecessary intrusion into the rights of private individuals. Rather than leading to the co-operation that is happening very satisfactorily in Wiltshire, as I have described, it would be likely to lead to greater confrontation. I therefore suggest that the Committee should not accept the amendment.

Mr. Mullin: I have listened carefully to my hon. Friend the Member for Calder Valley and I understand the objective underlying her amendment. It would allow access authorities to take action to safeguard or create routes leading to access land, even though those routes were not on the access land. Therein lies the difficulty. The amendment refers to

    a way leading to any access land

which would, by definition, not be on access land but on cultivated farm land, forestry or other land, such as the driveways of private houses mentioned by the hon. Member for North Wiltshire (Mr. Gray). It would not be on land to which the new right of access will apply.

We have often said that the consequences of increased access to our open countryside will be minimal, and that the new statutory right will be compatible with the continued use of the land for other purposes, such as farming and sporting activities. We cannot make the same general assurance in respect of other types of land. The amendment would, for example, allow access authorities to establish paths across intensively farmed land, solely on the basis that the way was necessary to provide reasonable access to the access land beyond. That might be a useful tool in the authority's armoury, but it would be unreasonable. As the hon. Member for North Wiltshire said, it would lead to unreasonable intrusion. Hon. Members have, throughout the Committee's deliberations, been at pains to stress the greater potential impacts of access on the improved, more intensively used lands below the moorland wall, and we understand and respect those concerns.

We recognise that there will be some areas of access land to which there is inadequate or no access. In such cases, it will be open to access authorities to negotiate access through the creation of permissive paths or new rights of way. In default of agreement, they may seek to create a new public path using their powers under section 26 of the Highways Act 1980. As my hon. Friend rightly predicted, clause 52 provides for the countryside bodies to be able to apply to the Secretary of State, or to the National Assembly for Wales, for a public path creation order under the 1980 Act, precisely for this purpose. That legislation provides for a proper assessment of the merits of the case for a new path. In addition, the woodland grant scheme and the countryside stewardship scheme-Tir Gofal in Wales-already include options for permissive access which could be used to provide links to new access land.

Where existing public rights of way leading to access land are obstructed, highway authorities have no need of recourse to chapter III. They will be able to apply the existing remedies available to them under highways legislation and common law, which will be considerably enhanced by the improvements effected by part II, to which we shall shortly turn our attention.

In conclusion, I regret that the amendment, while attractive, goes further than we can accept, and I ask my hon. Friend to withdraw it.
