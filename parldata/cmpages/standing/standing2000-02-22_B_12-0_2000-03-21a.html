You were not with us, Mr. Maxton, when we began the debate under Mr. Butterfill's excellent chairmanship last week, but we spent longer than I had expected discussing postcodes. There was some confusion about the postcode—what the hon. Member for Rutland and Melton (Mr. Duncan) described as the ``wretched postcode address file''—and the understandable concern felt by people when their address is not described accurately. Postcodes are made up of a series of routine instructions that enable mail posted in any part of the country to be delivered by the most efficient and economical route to its intended destination. The routine instructions are interpreted by the Post Office machinery. Along with Royal Mail staff, it now sorts video coding through optical character recognition machines.

The amendment would make it possible to ask for postcodes to be changed to reflect the county of the addressee.

Mr. Duncan: No.

Mr. Johnson: The amendment states:

    each postcode shall reflect the county in which the addressee resides.

The postcode is the combination of factors, such as SW1A 0AA. To change that to reflect the county in which the address resides would mean changing almost every postcode in the country. Apart from a few exceptions, postcodes do not reflect the county in which addressees reside. Changing postcodes would be as inconvenient as changing telephone numbers. Postcodes are now widely used; the Post Office needs them for its own purposes, but it is happy to share such information with other organisations. In fact, the census in 2001 will be based on postcodes.

People engrave postcodes on to their car windscreens as a security device; they put them on their bicycles so that, if they are stolen, they can be returned to them. Postcodes are used in many different ways and one of the reasons why they are so used is that they are reliable and change very little. The Post Office Users National Council investigated the matter some time ago and established a code of practice, which said that postcodes should be changed only when it was operationally necessary to improve the delivery service. That reflected the fact that changes in postcodes would be extremely inconvenient to customers and businesses.

Mr. Duncan: Can the Minister think of any postcode apart from London, which is a capital city, that does not reflect a county?

AMENDMENTS OF ENACTMENTS

Mr. Alan Johnson: I beg to move amendment No. 169, in page 76, line 9, at end insert—

    `Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (c.46)

    In section 11 of the Criminal Procedure (Scotland) Act 1995 (jurisdiction over certain offences committed outside Scotland), after subsection (4) there shall be inserted—

    ``(5) Where a person in any part of the United Kingdom outside Scotland—

    (a) steals or attempts to steal any mail-bag or postal packet in the course of its transmission by post, or any of the contents of such a mail-bag or postal packet; or

    (b) in stealing or with intent to steal any such mail-bag or postal packet or any of its contents commits any robbery, attempted robbery or assault with intent to rob,

    he is guilty of the offence mentioned in paragraph (a) or (b) as if he had committed it in Scotland and shall be liable to be prosecuted, tried and punished there without proof that the offence was committed there.

    (6) Any expression used in subsection (5) and in the Postal Services Act 2000 has the same meaning in that subsection as it has in that Act.''.'

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 170 and 171.

Mr. Johnson: The amendment is intended to ensure that a person in any part of the United Kingdom outside Scotland who either steals or attempts to steal any mail bag or postal packet, or its contents, is guilty as if he had committed such offences in Scotland, and is liable to prosecution, trial and punishment in Scotland without proof that the offence was committed there. That also applies to a person who, in stealing or attempting to steal a postal packet or mail bag, commits a robbery, an attempted robbery, or an assault with intent to rob. That is consistent with the Theft Act 1968, which applies to England and Wales, and the Theft Act (Northern Ireland) 1969. They cover the question of jurisdiction, so that for offences under the Bill, a person committing an offence anywhere in the UK can be prosecuted in any of the constituent countries. Therefore, this is a technical amendment to bring the legislation up to date.

Amendment agreed to.
