The amendments are part of the re-organisation of the different provisions of clause 138 and our biggest objection to them is the sneaking in of an unlimited right to intervene in circumstances where there is no additional requirement to give notice to the person being investigated.

Apart from anything else, that would be contrary to clause 2(3)(e) of the Bill. I suspect that some of the new amendments to clause 140 may have been prompted by one particular Investment Management Regulatory Organisation case that has been going on and which may have arisen from the Financial Services Authority nobbling either the Treasury or the Government draftsmen to tighten up and, as my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir Nicholas Lyell) said, reduce the potential for judicial review in this and other territories where the FSA acts when it does not have adequate justification to do so in relation to any suspicions and is using the powers for its own ends.

with no qualification whatever. Amendment No. 491 would correctly add to subsection (3) the words

    ``into the matter giving rise to the suspicion''.

Mr. Andrew Tyrie (Chichester): I should like to confine my remarks to the test of reasonableness, which has been removed from the Bill. The clause originally stated that an investigation can take place

    ``if the Authority has reasonable grounds for suspecting that'',

which is followed by a list of possible offences. Subsection (2) uses the same phrase. It is being replaced by the words

    ``if it appears to the Authority that there are circumstances suggesting''.

Anyone coming across that phrase will find a much weaker test that if the authority has ``reasonable grounds''.

I was talking to some lawyers last night, and I asked what was the difference between what the Government suggest in the new draft and a test of reasonableness. I also said that when the Opposition had tried to insert a test of reasonableness elsewhere in the Bill, Ministers had replied that the FSA was under a general duty to behave reasonably, and that nothing would be gained by adding a test of reasonableness. The original draftsmen, scribbling away in the Treasury Solicitor's department got it right; those who drafted the amendments—probably Treasury Ministers—have got it wrong. In a moment, I shall explain briefly the grounds for assessing whether the FSA has acted reasonably that I understand will remain once the phrases in question have been removed. However, before I do so, I shall refer briefly, without going into detail, to some of the most salient provisions for which the Government have either voted down or rejected amendments to specify in the Bill the application of a general test of reasonableness.

Under clause 2, which covers general duties, we said that the FSA should have a general duty to act reasonably. That was voted down. A further crucial provision was schedule 1, to which we wanted to add a phrase along the lines of:

    ``The authority must ensure that it acts in a reasonable, proportionate and fair way.''

The Government rejected that, too. They did not accept that the authority should have a duty to ensure that it acts in a reasonable, proportionate and fair way. In discussing clause 46, we argued that, in demanding further information from companies, the FSA should have a duty to act reasonably, and that a reasonableness test should apply to such a demand. The Government did not accept that, either.

This clause is being affected by a general change of tone on the part of the Government, which also affected earlier clauses. Clauses are being amended to remove sensible phraseology.

The amended wording—

    ``if it appears to the Authority that there are circumstances suggesting''—

leaves virtually no grounds for judicial review of the FSA. I understand that the general grounds for judicial review are, first, illegality—if the FSA does something that is clearly not in accordance with the Act of Parliament under which it operates. A second ground is procedural impropriety—if the FSA flagrantly flouts the procedures that it is expected to follow. I shall defer to my right hon. and learned Friend the Member for North-East Bedfordshire, but I suspect that a duty of propriety includes a duty to act fairly, involving a right to a fair hearing and the principle that no one should be judged in his own cause. I believe that that was what brought down the Pinochet case, as it was held that Lord Hoffmann was acting in his own cause.

A third ground for judicial review is irrationality—an authority must be shown to have acted irrationally. To put it another way, if it can be shown that no reasonable authority would have behaved as the authority has behaved, that would constitute grounds for overturning a decision or for judicial review. That third ground for judicial review is what I understand to be the Wednesbury test. I understand that as a result of the Government's amendments only that test will remain. It will be the only protection afforded to firms in the face of unreasonable behaviour by the FSA.

Firms will have to show that the FSA has not only acted unreasonably but has more or less taken leave of its senses, thrown a wobbly, or done something extraordinary for it to be caught by judicial review under the Wednesbury test. A lawyer described it to me last night as often being defined as a test of egregious unreasonableness, so it is extremely strong.
