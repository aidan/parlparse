Mrs. Liddell: The schedule sets out the detailed provisions for the appointment and terms and conditions of members of the new regulatory authorities for energy and telecoms. It also contains provisions relating to staff and procedural matters.

The schedule provides that each authority must consist of a chairman and at least two other members. The members of each authority are to be appointed by the Secretary of State on such terms and conditions, including remuneration, as he may determine.

During our discussions, we have repeatedly referred to flexibility and one of the reasons why it is necessary to amend the structure of gas, electricity and telecoms regulation is that in all those areas the pace of market change has been significant, not only through technology but with the liberalisation of markets and the opportunity to expand them for the benefit of consumers. In enshrining such flexibility in the legislation, we also want to ensure that the authority can change its shape in relation to the size of its membership so that as different sorts of expertise become necessary they can be found without having to revisit the detail of the legislation.

We already have interim arrangements and Mr. Callum McCarthy is in place as the Director General of Gas and Electricity Markets. He has put in place a non-statutory board structure to assist him with his regulatory functions. Apart from himself, the board consists of three executives who are employees of Ofgem and five non-executive directors who are known to be people of standing in the community: Lord Borrie, Lord Currie, Dr. Gill Owen, John Roques and Sir Nigel Rudd. The interim non-statutory board was appointed to advise Callum McCarthy. We want to ensure that the appointment of members of the new authority rests with the Secretary of State.

Different regulators may choose to configure the board differently and that is only understandable, not only in terms of how the work is to be conducted but in relation to changes in the industry. I referred to the way in which Callum McCarthy configured his non-statutory board. David Edmonds on the telecoms side chose an authority with four executive members, including himself, and a small number of non-executive members.

The shape of the authority must be whatever is right to do the job. Members of the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council and the Telecommunications Authority will be appointed to ensure that, collectively, the membership of the authority possesses a range of skills, experience and perspectives. It is highly likely that one member will have knowledge of consumer matters and others may have experience of technical matters. Decisions on particular appointments will not be made until the Bill has received Royal Assent, following the normal transparent procedures for searching for candidates of appropriate calibre. Existing members of Ofgem staff can be appointed members of GEMA and, likewise, members of Oftel staff can be appointed to the Telecommunications Authority. We want to ensure that high-calibre candidates are attracted and it would be foolish to ignore the enormous pool of expertise and skill among the current staff of the regulators' offices.

We have had a long debate on the terms of the authority's office and we have settled on a period of five years. Members may be removed from office on the grounds of incapacity or misbehaviour and they may be reappointed should the Secretary of State so choose. There is also a discretionary power to pay compensation for loss of office in the event of special circumstances. The authorities, like the individual regulators whom they will replace, will have to appoint staff and determine their terms and conditions. In order to do that, they must obtain the approval of the Minister for the Civil Service in the normal way.

Authorities are entitled to establish the necessary committees and sub-committees to regulate their procedures. Apart from the power to make statutory instruments, the authorities are permitted to delegate their functions to individual members, to committees of the authority and to employees.

In taking that first step, we may enshrine in statute a common-sense approach towards the establishment of regulatory authorities that are depersonalised and professional. Those authorities should put the rights of the consumer at the head of their list of priorities and take expertise and experience into account in a businesslike manner. That will secure certainty and transparency of regulation at an appropriately low cost.

The Committee has made good progress today, and I commend the schedule to it.
