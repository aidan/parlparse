Mr. Mullin: The hon. Member for Somerton and Frome kindly said that he did not disagree with the purpose of the clause; I think that, as always, he was trying to be helpful.

In the context of directions made for the purposes of fire prevention, amendment No. 478 would remove any requirement for the risk of fire to have arisen because of exceptional weather conditions. The hon. Gentleman suggested that conditions of high fire risk can occur independently of exceptional weather—for example, peat moors may remain vulnerable to the outbreak of fire long after the end of a drought. However, the cause of the high fire risk would remain the exceptional weather, even if those conditions no longer prevailed. The relevant authority would therefore still be able to make a direction if it were necessary for the purpose of fire prevention.

In other circumstances, the risk of fire may not arise in exceptional weather conditions. For instance, some types of vegetative cover may be flammable even in an ordinary summer. Such circumstances are not exceptional. By definition, they are not caused by unusual weather conditions; they are part of the normal fabric of the open countryside. I am not satisfied that they give rise to sufficient reason to restrict access to the land.

Referring to amendment No. 479, the hon. Gentleman said that the use of the word ``done'' was ambiguous and might not include contemporary operations. I am advised that the Bill does apply to contemporary operations.

Mr. Green: I hope that the amendments produce greater elucidation than we received on the previous clause.

It is a particular pleasure to reach the clause 24. Its purpose is

    Nature conservation and heritage preservation,

which many of us believe should be the main purpose of the Bill. It is a relief to be able to debate such matters. The clause introduces an important safeguard for wildlife and for heritage conservation of features found on open country. Wildlife and heritage features might be damaged by excessive or over-concentrated public access. At sensitive times or at sensitive locations, sites might be disturbed by a level of use that would not be enough to disrupt land management, which we discussed in our debate on clause 22. Clause 24, therefore, deals with more sensitive matters. For example, ground-nesting birds are known to be intolerant of disturbance, especially by dogs. Even with the Minister's oft-repeated point that we are simply talking about people who walk their dogs across open land, that activity could disrupt wildlife preservation.

I shall be interested to hear the Minister's response on the following issues. He will be aware that the Conservation (Natural Habitats, etc.) Regulations 1994 implement the EU habitats directive and the birds directive. They charge the competent authorities, which include the national park authorities and the countryside bodies, with ensuring that their consents will not have significant effects on the integrity of sites notified as special areas of conservation in the habitats directive or special protection areas in the birds directive.

That could be a broad requirement. The directives and regulations do not define significant effect, but Environment Agency guidance interprets it as an impact that excludes merely trivial or inconsequential effects. I would be grateful for clarification from the Minister on the way in which he would guide the relevant authorities to grant restrictions or closures to the right of access in the light of the legislative precedents already in place. Does he envisage such restrictions being commonplace? When we assess the effect of this potentially important clause, it is important for us to consider how often it will be invoked and for us to deal with the specific amendments tabled by me and my hon. Friends.

The express purpose of the provision is the protection of nature and the heritage. The important mechanism should be facilitated in every possible way. Amendment No. 335, to which amendments Nos. 336 and 337 are consequential with regard to clause 28, would protect the rights of all those involved to draw the relevant authority's attention to the needs of an area regarding closure or restriction for the purpose of protecting nature or the heritage. To leave the measure to the relevant authority, as the clause does, could lead to unintentional oversights. Such oversights could be insured against by the involvement of landowners, managers and others with an interest in the land. The Minister will surely agree that many of those people are likely to be the most sensitive to the needs of an area in terms of nature and heritage conservation.

The purpose of amendment No. 335 and the consequential amendments Nos. 336 and 377 is to widen the scope of the existing protection and to make it more likely that it would be effective in defending nature and heritage features. We all want to see them protected. I hope that the amendments are helpful to the Government's aim.

The one amendment that I have not discussed is amendment No. 338, which simply says that, if an applicant has an appeal refused, the response should come in writing. I am sure that the Minister would agree that that would be a simple courtesy, which should be a normal part of the act of any public body.

I hope that the Minister will take the amendments in the spirit in which they are offered. We agree with the Government that clause 24 provides an important set of protective measures for our natural environment and heritage. We simply seek to strengthen them by ensuring that landowners and other with an interest in the land on which sensitive features are situated can play a role in the features' protection.

Mr. Meacher: The hon. Member for Ashford said in a throwaway remark at the start of his comments that the clause is what the Bill should be about. That is not to say that other aspects of dealing with the countryside are not important and relevant. Nor is it to say that the statutory right of access and the amendment and modernisation of rights of way are not equally important.

As the hon. Gentleman rightly said—he tabled them—amendments Nos. 335 to 337 would amend clause 24 to provide a specific right of application for a direction in the interest of nature conservation and heritage preservation. They would also require consequential amendments to clause 28 to ensure a right of appeal against an adverse decision on the application. It is correct to say that there is no provision for an application to be made under clause 24. The absence of such a provision, however, does not prevent the owner of the land or anyone else interested in it from asking the relevant authority for a direction to be made. Relevant authorities will be obliged to give such a request reasonable consideration.

In the same way, if it appeared to the relevant advisory body—whether on its own initiative or in response to comments received—that the relevant authority should make a direction under clause 24 in respect of an area of land, it may make representations to the relevant authority. The authority will be obliged to have regard to the body's advice when considering whether to give a direction. There is no need for a special right of application for either the owner or the relevant advisory body.

The purpose of clause 24 is to protect the nature or heritage conservation interest of the land, in the context of the statutory right of access. The key role will belong to the relevant advisory bodies, which will give advice to the relevant authority. Whether a proposal for a direction under clause 24 originates with the owner, the advisory body, another person with an interest or a third party, chapter II provides the advisory body with a right of referral to the Secretary of State or to the National Assembly for Wales if the relevant authority declines to issue a determination in accordance with the advice. The right of referral under clause 27 will be sufficient to protect wildlife where, for example, the Countryside Agency and English Nature are unable to agree on how access to a sensitive habitat should be reconciled with its conservation.

The hon. Members for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy and for Ashford asked me how widely the application for a direction for the purposes of nature conservation is likely to be used. We would certainly expect, at least for the present, that the relevant authorities should give their reasons in writing. I hope that I have persuaded Opposition Members that I am as concerned as they are to ensure full and adequate protection for wildlife and nature conservation. We believe that the Bill will achieve that. For that reason, I ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Gareth Thomas (Clwyd, West): I should like to explore issues arising from the drafting of clause 28, which has caused me and perhaps other hon. Members puzzlement.

The clause is deficient in that it is unbalanced. It is unbalanced because it does not sufficiently take into account a proper balance regarding the interests of the users of land as defined by clause 41. We know from debates on clauses 6 and 7 that interest in land is defined legalistically to encompass those with a strict legal estate—a licence or tenancy in land—or sporting rights, but not those exercising recreational access. Although there is a right of appeal against a refusal to give appropriate directions under clauses 22 and 23 for those with an interest in land, as narrowly defined by the Bill, there is no corresponding and balancing right of appeal for those wishing to exercise recreational access under clause 2(1).

I wish simply to draw the attention of my hon. Friend the Minister to the concern expressed by many involved in the area, not least the Ramblers Association and the British Mountaineering Council. Those organisations put the case well. A document that the British Mountaineering Council has helpfully provided says:

    Experience clearly shows that restrictions—

Mr. Clifton-Brown: On a point of order, Mr. Malins. Is it in order to quote from a document that has not been circulated to all members of the Committee?

The Chairman: Yes, that is often done and it is in order for the hon. Member for Clwyd, West to do so.

 

Mr. Thomas: I shall continue.

The letter says:

    Experience clearly shows that restrictions—

as set out in clauses 22 and 23 to deal with land management and the avoidance of danger and fire—

the important word—

Why has no corresponding right of appeal been given to people who want to exercise their rights under clause 2(1)? If the Government are not prepared to grant a right of appeal to people who exercise recreational access rights, will they consider giving bodies that reflect the views of ramblers and others the right to make representations? If the appeal process is one-sided and solely in the hands of those who have a legal interest in land, it would be sensible to give a right of representation to other people.

We covered some of the argument when we debated my amendments to clauses 6 and 7, which outline the appeal procedure following a refusal to include land within a mapped area. I was told that to give a right of appeal to those who want to exercise recreational access rights would open the floodgates to a multiplicity of appeals. Surely that consideration is not relevant to clause 28, which deals with a different procedure. There is no question of the mapping exercise being delayed indefinitely.

The clause states:

    Sections 7 and 8 . . . have effect in relation to an appeal under this section.

Clause 7 allows local inquiries to be held. It also provides for the more informal procedure of hearings, which I am worried can be held in private. I was sceptical about using the phrase ``right to roam'' because it caused unwarranted alarm in some circles. However, having decided to give citizens the right to enjoy access to the countryside, why will the Government not protect that right? Why will they not accept that when they create rights that could be restricted or revoked, it is necessary to give both sides a right of appeal?

Mr. Mullin: The clause reflects the Government's determination to ensure compliance with the obligations in the European convention on human rights. As my hon. Friend the Member for Clwyd, West said, we touched on this argument when we debated clause 7. Clause 28 also reflects our recognition that those whose interests will be most affected by decisions on closure should be able to obtain a second opinion. We have considered carefully whether a right of appeal should be available more widely, perhaps even to members of the public. There is concern that without such a right of appeal, land might be subject to closure or restriction without an obvious reason. That might make some users sceptical about the closure regime. Clearly, we wish to guard against such a scenario, which is why it is vital that the criteria for exclusions or restriction should be transparent and readily understood. The fact that the Countryside Agency plans to consult on the criteria for closures or restrictions, including with the National Countryside Access Forum, will help to secure consensus about the reasons for restrictions.

The hon. Gentleman requested that members of the public should be given the right to make representations, but they already have that right. It will be possible for anyone with any interest to make representations without a provision being enshrined in law, and we would expect extensive and long-term closure proposals to be considered by local access forums, on which users will be represented, so we are not persuaded that it is necessary to grant a right of appeal. The right of appeal exists to help us to fulfil our obligations under the European convention. I hope that my hon. Friend would accept that landowners have a greater interest than any individual member of the public, so it is right that they should be given the opportunity to obtain a second opinion.

Mr. Thomas: None the less, the Bill creates certain rights for the public. I hear what the Minister says about the public having an opportunity to make representations at all stages, but will he clarify whether the public will be able to make representations when an appeal is lodged under clause 28?

Mr. Mullin: I cannot think of anything to prevent that from happening. In particular, extensive and long-term closure proposals will be considered and debated at local access forums, on which users will be represented and will be able to make whatever points they wish.

Were we to provide in the Bill for a right of appeal for anyone, it could add substantially to the costs and administration involved in operating the closure regime and could result in greater confusion and delay about whether the right of access applies to a given area of land at any one time. Provided that the criteria for restrictions are robust and justified—and are seen to be such—and that applications are fairly assessed against those criteria, I believe that the system of closures or restrictions will operate effectively and gain general acceptance.
