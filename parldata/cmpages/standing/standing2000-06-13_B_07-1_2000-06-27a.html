General functions of Chief Constable

Mr. Eddie McGrady (South Down): I beg to move amendment No. 200, in page 16, line 4, leave out from ``shall'' to ``any'' in line 7 and insert-

    `(a) insofar as practicable comply with the policing plan, and

    (b) have regard to'.

This simple amendment is designed to ensure that the Chief Constable is under a statutory duty, as far as is practicable, to comply with the policing plan that is drawn up by the Policing Board. The board's policing plan should be treated as an important and essential document, which sets out its strategy for training, education and the general development of a new police service in Northern Ireland. The Achilles heel of the Bill's proposed arrangements is that the Chief Constable merely has to ``have regard to'' the policing plan. That gives him a very wide discretion. If he has only to consider the plan, he can, in effect, do whatever he likes. No obligation is placed on him; he has only to take it on board. That is not good enough in terms of the new dispensation that we hope will apply to the delivery of police services in Northern Ireland.

At the same time, we accept that the Chief Constable must maintain his operational responsibility. That is why we do not favour obliging him to follow the plan, unless he has been given power by the board not to do so. That would be inflexible and wrong. The amendment merely proposes that he should be under a duty, as far as is practicable, to comply with the plan. That is a straightforward proposition, which emphasises the necessity of adopting an attitude whereby we have not only a regard for, but a duty to implement, the policing plan that is drawn up by the Policing Board.

I commend the amendment to the Committee.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. George Howarth): As my hon. Friend the Member for South Down (Mr. McGrady) explained, the amendment would require the Chief Constable to comply with the policing plan, in so far as is practicable. As drafted, the clause requires the Chief Constable to have regard to the policing plan to allow for any unforeseen changes in policing requirements. Let me make it clear that the Government expect that he will adhere to the plan as far as is possible.

Essentially, we are talking about a planning process. As my hon. Friend will know, given his long experience of such matters, plans are sometimes overtaken by events. No one can anticipate every eventuality, and references to complying with plans do not allow sufficient flexibility. Paragraph 6(4) of the Patten report stated that the police should develop the short-term, tactical plans for delivering the board's objectives, and the words ``have regard to'' give them the flexibility to do that.

My hon. Friend the Member for South Down will acknowledge that there is hardly a gulf between us. It could be argued that the phrase ``have regard to'' is a simpler way of saying

    insofar as practicable comply with.

Mr. Andrew Hunter (Basingstoke): The Under-Secretary is discussing an amendment that relates to clause 31(2), which will give the Chief Constable the flexibility to respond to unforeseen policing developments. Mr. Howarth: The starting point in this regard is the Patten report, paragraph 6.21 of which states:

In the circumstances, I hope that my hon. Friend will withdraw the amendment, which simply involves a difference of words. Our intentions are essentially the same, and I have clarified what the relevant expectations are. He will agree that the fact that I made such a statement in Committee is significant.

Mr. McGrady: The Minister's comments on the duties of the Chief Constable coincide with the amendment's sentiments. With that clear understanding, and in the hope that the matter will be fulfilled in practice with the relevant rules and regulations, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. William Thompson (West Tyrone): I beg to move amendment No. 236, in page 16, line 42, after ``person'', insert ``with experience of policing''.

The clause deals with the removal of police officers, who will have an opportunity to appeal. The Secretary of State can appoint a person to hold an inquiry and to report to him before he makes a final decision. If the amendment were agreed to, subsection (6) would state:

    The person appointed under subsection (4)(b) (or where two or more persons are so appointed, at least one of those persons) shall be a person with experience of policing.

If there is an appeals mechanism, the person to whom the police constable or police inspector would appeal would need to have some experience of the workings of the police, hence the amendment.

The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram): I understand why the hon. Gentleman has brought the proposal forward and that there may be fears that the person conducting the inquiry will not have the competence necessary to understand the issues at stake.

However, if there were to be a complicated fraud case, it might be argued that it would be best to have an accountant to conduct that inquiry. Strictly speaking, it would not be a policing issue, even though it involved a police officer in a discipline and appeal procedure. The clause has been drafted to allow some flexibility. The qualities that a person could bring to such an inquiry or appeal mechanism would be considered-it would be horses for courses. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will accept the reasoning behind the clause and will not press the amendment to a vote.

Mr. Thompson: I can see that, in certain circumstances, financial matters may need someone with experience in that field. Having heard the Minister's explanation, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

    `(3) No person shall serve as a member of the police service reserve on a full time basis four years after the entry into force of this subsection.

    (4) The Secretary of State may by order extend the period mentioned in subsection (3) if he is satisfied that there are compelling reasons related to the protection of public safety and public order in Northern Ireland to do so.

    (5) Before making an order under subsection (4), the Secretary of State shall consult-

    (a) the Board;

    (b) the Chief Constable.'.

The amendment deals with the full-time reserve and how the present community imbalance in the force can be redressed in a reasonable, orderly and equitable way. Lest I be misinterpreted, I should say that my party acknowledges the commitment shown by the vast majority of full-time reserve police officers to their duties and the sacrifices that they have made. It is not our intention to impugn that record of service in any way. However, we must deal with the phasing out of the full-time reserve in an orderly and proper fashion, so that we can implement other parts of the Bill that relate to community balance in the composition of the police service.

In the amendment, we propose that

    No person shall serve as a member of the police service reserve on a full time basis four years after the entry into force of this subsection.

However, we are aware that there will be difficulties, so subsection (4) would allow the Secretary of State to extend that period

    if he is satisfied that there are compelling reasons related to the protection of public safety and public order in Northern Ireland.

Subsection (5) would require the Secretary of State to consult the board and the Chief Constable.

It is interesting that the Patten report used the word ``disbandment'' only once, in relation to the full-time reserve. The Bill does nothing to address that recommendation. The implementation plan does not even provide a timetable for disbandment of the full-time reserve. In terms of one of the crucial elements of the new police service, there is a total blank. The amendment would require that the full-time reserve be disbanded within four years, but some flexibility is given to the Secretary of State. If there are compelling reasons why that requirement could not be met, they would have to be made public.
