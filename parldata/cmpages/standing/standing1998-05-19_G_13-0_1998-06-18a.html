Mr. Lansley: May I say what a pleasure it is to be a member of the Committee on the first occasion under your chairmanship, Mrs. Roe?

Clause 46 introduces us to the Competition Commission as a body. Schedule 7 fills out all the details of its composition and functioning, in so far as that is set out in the Bill. The commission is intended to have executive and judicial functions. Interestingly, in the European Union, the executive and judicial functions are exercised separately. The Commission is the executive, which corresponds to the role of the Office of Fair Trading and the reporting role of the Competition Commission, whereas the judicial functions, which will be embraced within the Competition Commission, are exercised by the Court of First Instance and the European Court of Justice.

It is not necessarily desirable for the executive powers proposed for the commission they include such wide-ranging powers as merger and monopoly reference, utility referrals and licence modifications which are proposed by regulators and referred to the commission to be incorporated into the same body, with judicial or quasi-judicial processes, for fear that activity on the reporting side of the commission would eventually compromise the objectivity of its appeal and tribunal side. The Under-Secretary will correct me if I am wrong, but I think that there will be instances of the commission's appeal tribunal hearing appeals against decisions made by its reporting side. It does not look as though the Minister is going to disabuse me of the notion that that is likely to happen.

The amendments do not seek to separate the functions into different bodies that would dismember the intentions of the legislation. They seek to work within the proposed format, but in a way that results in a better separation of powers within the commission. They would not prevent the senior, policy-making, managerial levels of the commission from operating coherently; the functions of chairman and deputy chairman of the commission would not, for example, be bifurcated into executive and quasi-judicial functions. However, under the schedule it is possible indeed, unless the Minister tells us otherwise, I imagine that it is contemplated that persons appointed under paragraph 2(1)(a) as appeal panel members could also be appointed by the Secretary of State as members of a panel dealing with general functions. We would therefore have commission members exercising both executive and quasi-judicial functions which would mix up the jurisdictions. Under paragraph 4, the chairman and president of tribunals are required to have legal qualifications, but even in that regard there is no apparent demarcation between members appointed to sit on the appeal panel and those appointed to carry out functions on the executive side. Persons appointed to be chairmen or even president of tribunals might, therefore, also exercise a function on the reporting, executive side.

The first purpose of the amendments is interrogative, to find out whether the Minister will give us assurances about the Government's intention to preserve a distinction between the membership of the reporting panels, which exercise general functions, and of the appeal tribunals. If the Minister is unable to give us satisfactory assurances, I would be happy to move the amendments in a substantive way. Under the schedule, the Secretary of State will appoint panel members and, in consultation with the Lord Chancellor and, where appropriate, the Lord Advocate, make appointments that require legal qualifications for the president and chairmen of tribunals, for example. Amendment No. 180 would give the Lord Chancellor, in consultation with the Lord Advocate, the power to make appointments to the commission, rather than the Secretary of State. I am sure that the Minister will be able to draw on specific instances of a Secretary of State making appointments even of a quasi-judicial character to tribunals. Before the Under-Secretary does so, will he tell us what is the balance of precedent? From what I know of the matter, the balance of precedent appears to be that appointments of a quasi-judicial character to appeal tribunals for example, to the financial services tribunal are made by the Lord Chancellor, together with the Lord Advocate, rather than by the departmental Secretary of State.
