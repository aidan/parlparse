Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause relates to personal pensions and certain self-administered occupational pension schemes. Under a defined contributions pension scheme--rather than a defined benefits scheme, where the pension is ultimately paid out of the pension fund itself, for example, a final salaries scheme--there is a requirement to purchase an annuity on retirement. In 1995, that requirement was mitigated by changing the law to permit income draw-down directly from the pension fund until the pensioner reaches the age of 75. That allows the pensioner to defer the purchase of an annuity for 10 years or more.

That reform was deemed to be necessary by the previous Government because of falling annuity rates. Annuity rates have continued to fall and, in January 1999, they reached a 30-year low. Seven years ago, a £100,000 pension fund could have purchased an annuity giving an income of £15,823 a year. A year ago, the same fund would have purchased an annuity giving about £9,700 a year. Today, it would purchase an annuity giving £9,494 a year, and even lower figures are derived from annuities that have an escalator or are index-linked. The Association of Unit Trusts and Investment Funds report, ``Income in Retirement--are Annuities the Answer?'', which was published in April this year, shows that a male aged 65 who is a stalwart smoker can expect £9,948 a year from a £100,000 annuity, a non-smoker can expect £9,104 a year, but an annuity with a 5 per cent. escalator will yield merely £5,925. The previous Government's decision to give pensioners the flexibility of being able to delay the purchase of an annuity until the age of 75 was welcome, and continues to be so. That is not merely because it enables pensioners to wait until annuity rates rise, which is a gamble. As events have turned out, such a gamble would not have paid off because annuity rates have continued to fall, and all the predictions are that they may fall even further. A more significant aspect of delaying the purchase of annuities, according to Clifford German, writing in The Independent, is that an annuity will be about 40 per cent. higher if taken out at the age of 75 than at the age of 65. That is because of lower life expectancy at the age of 75 than at 65. That has an even greater impact than being able to gamble on future annuity rates.

I represent a constituency with a large retired population, and I receive many letters urging the Government to remove the age limit of 75. Life expectancy has increased enormously over the past 20 years, and is expected to rise even further in the years ahead. That is welcome, and a great tribute to the success of this country. It therefore seems arbitrary to insist on an age limit of 75 for pensioners purchasing annuities. The new clause would remove that age limit altogether. One of my constituents wrote to me recently to say:

    ``I am one of very many pensioners who is unable to secure a satisfactory pension from my pension fund which I have worked hard to build up during a lifetime of continuous employment. As I have never enjoyed a Company Pension Scheme all my payments have been made into a Personal Pension Scheme which has been invested wisely, mainly in equities, to provide growth which has produced quite a large fund for me.

    However, I am now 68 years old, and would like to draw a pension but I have been informed...that with the present appallingly low rates of return in annuities I am best advised not to buy a pension at the present time.''

He continues:

    ``Under present legislation, I will be forced to purchase an annuity...before I reach the age of 75. This will mean that my investment strategy will then be dictated by the Inland Revenue with my annuity invested in gilts and involving the total loss of capital.''

He concludes:

    ``This seems particularly unfair for older pensioners as in my case as the larger part of my pension plan will be lost to my family irretrievably if I were to die after the age of 75.''

That raises the secondary point about removing the upper age limit--buying an annuity means that the capital is lost and that the pensioner takes a gamble that he will live for a sufficiently long period to gain more than the amortisation of the capital would deliver in normal circumstances. Many pensioners want to leave their capital to their children, and would be prepared to take a small reduction in income to enable them to do so. Other pensioners do not want to take such a risk, and would rather enjoy a higher income and not leave the capital to their children. That should be a matter for pensioners, and the new clause would provide the necessary flexibility without any jeopardy of creating a liability for the state as a result of pensioners squandering all their capital on a world cruise. That would not be possible, even under the current rules, as pensioners can only draw down an amount of income that enables the fund to maintain its value.

Another constituent wrote to me to say:

    ``We would appreciate it if you could press for better treatment for those in small self-administered schemes and those who defer taking annuities.

    As you know, the annuity rates are very low at the moment and show little sign of improving. Pensioners in most cases can obtain more income by managing their own affairs especially as they still have their capital sum intact.''

Pensioners are finding that they can achieve a better return by leaving their capital in the pension fund than by taking it out and purchasing an annuity.

That may seem odd, given that an annuity has the advantage of having an amortisation element in the capital, whereas the money left in the pension fund does not. There are circumstances, with which other members of the Committee will be more familiar than I am, in which annuity rates have risen not only because of low interest rates but because of an attempt by the annuity industry to recover margins following past mistakes, and because the annuity industry is probably under-supplied in relation to the level of demand. There are also worries about 20-year gilt rates in the marketplace, because of excess demand by the annuity industry for annuities for 20-year gilts. The constituent goes on to say:

    ``As the law stands, at the age of 75, pensioners must hand over the capital sum to an insurance company and take whatever the annuity rate is at that time and say goodbye to the capital sum.''

He concludes by saying that:

    ``the bulk of the fund has been saved by the pensioner and is truly his money. It does not seem just to take this away from him or his family.''

He makes a valid point.

The new clause has been tabled to deal with an important aspect of the Inland Revenue's powers, and this is not the first time that we have debated such matters. There is growing concern in some quarters that the Government, and specifically the Treasury, are too uncritical in accepting the argument for extending the Revenue's powers. I am sure that we all accept that a balance is necessary. The Revenue must have proper powers in order to ascertain tax liabilities and prevent tax evasion, wherever possible. However, that must be offset by proper concern for the requirements of justice and the principles of common law.

In parallel with the deliberations of this Committee, an interesting issue is evolving--about which the Economic Secretary knows a great deal--in relation to the Financial Services and Markets Bill. It is important that that Bill complies with the European convention on human rights. I think that all hon. Members would agree that the first financial services Bill, published last summer, did not fully measure up to the requirements of the convention. In that respect, I congratulate the Government on responding to concerns that have been raised We hope that the redrafted Bill will comply with the European convention on human rights.

I mention that only to draw attention to the importance of getting the balance right, which is not easy. However, there will be serious consequences if basic requirements of justice are overlooked as a result of any Government's concern to maximise tax revenue and to give Departments powers to achieve that. I believe that, in the area of legal privilege, the Government are in danger of ignoring the warning bells about extending Executive power. The question before the Committee is whether the Inland Revenue should be able to obtain documents that are legally privileged. Our new clause would make it clear in statute that the Revenue cannot obtain such documents.

For many years, it has been a feature of common law that a person who faces legal proceedings can take advice from a solicitor or a barrister in the certain knowledge that what he says to his lawyer, and the advice that he receives, will not be obtained by other parties to the proceedings and cannot be used against him. That is a longstanding principle, but statute law on the matter is somewhat unclear. However, nothing in existing statutes appears explicitly to remove that privilege.

Until now, the slightly ambiguous state of statute law has not mattered, because the Inland Revenue appeared to accept the principle that I have annunciated. However, there has been a recent hardening of its assumptions. In challenging the convention, it was assisted by a recent special commissioners case, which held that the Inland Revenue's statutory powers override the principle of professional privilege, and enable it to obtain correspondence and advice that passes between a taxpayer and his legal adviser. That is a significant development.

To obtain a glimpse of Parliament's intention, we must return to 1976, when the matter was last debated at length, in the Finance Bill Standing Committee. Regrettably, although the present Chief Secretary is a member of this Committee, he has yet to attend. In those days, the matter was deemed sufficiently important for the Chief Secretary, Joel Barnett, to participate in extensive debates in Standing Committee. He explained where the law stood and where the powers of the Inland Revenue should rest. He said:

    ``I should make it quite clear . . . that the purpose of this part of the schedule is not to require privileged and confidential documents, to be handed over to the Inland Revenue.'.--[Official Report, Standing Committee E, 10 June 1976; c. 686.]

The schedule to which he refers is now schedule 20, which is a familiar part of statute law. His words put the Government of the day's intention beyond doubt, but, unfortunately, they were never properly incorporated into statute law. As I said, that did not really matter, because the Inland Revenue agreed to abide by the thinking behind them.

The next significant event was the setting up in 1983 of the Keith committee, chaired by Lord Keith of Kinkel, to consider the powers--particularly the enforcement powers--of the Revenue departments. It received representations not only from the Inland Revenue but from the professions, the Law Society and, presumably, the Government of the day. It considered the pros and cons of the legal privilege, and recommended that

    ``Legal advice given in connection with a client's tax affairs should be protected from disclosure''.

It felt--and it was advised--that the situation was not clear cut. Much factual information that passes between a taxpayer and a legal adviser could properly be obtained by the Inland Revenue to ascertain the taxpayer's liability. However, the Keith committee was firm on the question of advice, and recommended that any proposals to restrict or exclude legal professional privilege should be specifically stated in legislation. However, that has never been done.

The Keith committee also said that the taxpayer should be represented at any hearing that establishes which category a document or piece of information falls into. If the Revenue claimed that a piece of correspondence that it wanted to obtain between a law firm and a taxpayer was factual, but a taxpayer's lawyer argued that it contained advice and was covered by privilege, the matter should be decided not by the Revenue but at a hearing at which the taxpayer is represented. That important requirement has never been stated in legislation, and the situation is extremely confusing.

We should add certainty to the statute book. Such provisions should conform to the assurances that were given by Ministers and by the Keith committee, which deliberated on the matter. It concluded that what Lord Keith regarded as the status quo should persist and be given statutory backing. If the Government do not accept the new clause or propose an alternative, there will be damaging side effects. If lawyers know that the advice that they tender to a taxpayer could be obtained against their client's wishes, they might not commit their thoughts to paper. The requirement is an inefficient and unnecessary restriction on the way in which lawyers do business.
