The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Ms Beverley Hughes): I beg to move amendment No. 345, in page 27, line 14, leave out 'local government' and insert 'relevant authorities'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 346 and 511.

Ms Hughes: We have reached part III, which deals with ethical standards. The clause requires the Secretary of State to specify by order the general principles that will govern the code of conduct of members and co-opted members of relevant authorities, and lists the relevant authorities to which part III will apply. Subsection (3) sets out the form of consultation that the Secretary of State is to carry out before making such an order.

The amendments will replace the requirement for the Secretary of State to consult representatives of "local government" with a requirement to consult representatives of "relevant authorities" when drawing up general principles. That has been introduced, in part, to ensure consistency throughout the Bill, but more importantly, the existing phrase could have restricted formal consultation on the members' code and employees' code to local authority bodies. We want to ensure thorough consultation on these important documents. The amendments will broaden the definition to include non-local government bodies such as the Association of Police Authorities, and unions representing the wider interests of relevant authorities that are not simply local authorities.

Mr. Loughton: As I said, we have no qualms about the amendments. I want to talk in more general terms about this part of the Bill.

Despite the fact that more than 400 Government amendments were tabled in the House of Lords, the selection paper shows that we are faced with scores more at this late stage of the proceedings. I accept that many are technical, but it reinforces the view that the Bill was cobbled together in a hurry and is still only partly finished--like a Spanish hotel, as my hon. Friend the Member for Eastbourne (Mr. Waterson) said on Second Reading.

The Bill is essentially about probity in local government. Some months ago, at the start of the Bill's passage, the Under-Secretary said that

    Effective democratic government relies on the bond of trust between the people and those who represent them, whether in local, central or regional government.

I am sure that we would all echo those words. Sir Jeremy Beecham of the Local Government Association has said that

    Councils must base change on what the local residents want.

Conservative Members would certainly agree with that. So far, however, our scrutiny of the Bill has suggested that the Government are not prepared to trust the people of local council areas who want to maintain the status quo because of their local circumstances, nor to guarantee people and the press rights of access to executive meetings. The clause imposes a national system with a whole new raft of investigators, adjudicators and quasi-judicial members of standards boards.

As was said earlier, the Bill appears to be about local government, not for local government. I pay tribute to the enormous amount of work that hundreds of thousands of councillors all over the country undertake, largely voluntarily, without which our democratic process would not function as well as it does. The Bill's underlying ethos is that we have to find ways of ensuring that more members of the public share that enthusiasm sufficiently to want to go out and vote for their elected representatives.

Despite the high-profile cases of abuse against which this part of the Bill is largely targeted, 99 per cent. of councillors are entirely straight and dedicated and perform their functions with the utmost integrity. The same is true of council officers. That is put into context by the annual report of the local government ombudsman, which stated that, in an average year, out of a total of 16,000, he would receive 60 to 70 complaints which either wholly or partly included a complaint about a member's conduct. The problem is not mainstream poor standards, but exceptions to the rule, yet 32 clauses deal with conduct of members in order to clamp down on the few offenders. That is overly prescriptive and amounts to an excessive degree of regulation. The vast majority of councillors who behave themselves and acquit their functions well will have to accommodate these provisions so as to carry on doing the good job that they were doing before.

The truth is that this part of the Bill has been necessitated by a tawdry catalogue of corrupt, unethical and politically motivated scandals in a wide variety of Labour-run rotten boroughs. [Hon. Members: "Westminster".] Well, if Government Members will wait, I shall give them many examples to their one. Labour rotten rotten borough syndrome means that the Bill is using a sledgehammer to crack a nut. Inevitably, the vast majority of councillors, who are straight, are tarred with the brush of suspicion implicit in the many extensive measures contained in these 32 detailed clauses. There seems to be an assumption of guilt before innocence is proven, and I have reservations about the draconian powers prescribed in the Bill for the ethical standards officers. Many of the proposals have been introduced simply to help the Labour party to clean up Labour-run councils, but every councillor will be subject to the same vast increase in regulation, checks and penalties.

We agree that the system should be tightened up and classified. We need a redefinition of the code of conduct set out in the Local Government and Housing Act 1989, which is couched in terms that suggest that councillors should never do anything that they cannot justify to the public. That is open to a wide variety of interpretations. Local government already observes high ethical standard. In the light of the publicity given to Donnygate-type scandals, however what is most needed is a disciplinary procedure that is clear, open and quick and will deal with those rare instances of serious wrongdoing. The proposals in part III are convoluted and time-consuming and go way beyond the proposals contained in the Nolan committee report.

In the report, Lord Nolan stated that

    The vast majority of councillors and officers observe high standards of conduct. The number of people who have used their position in local government for their own ends is small, compared with the vast majority who genuinely wish to serve their community.

Lord Nolan, of course, recommended that a new independent body, such as the Standards Board, be set up to arrange investigations, determine the substance of allegations and decide penalties. We have that, in some form, in the Standards Board; in addition to that, there are the adjudication panels and tribunals, the standards committees, the ethical standards officers with their extensive powers, statutory codes of conduct and a medley of new penalties.

More than 81 per cent. of local authorities have either established some form of standards committee, or are considering doing so. They are establishing structures that best suit local needs but they will now have a uniform code imposed on them. Part III removes even more local discretion. The new national code of conduct, which is especially pertinent to the opening clause of the part, will be subject to the negative resolution procedure. We have expressed our concerns about that before. The new code is to be drawn up with the help of the Local Government Association. What progress has been made on that? When will we see it? In the Joint Committee report on the draft Bill, there was a recommendation that the core of the code of conduct should be included in the Bill, so I would like more detail on what differences there will be in the statutory model code.

There are also problems with the potential overlap with the work of the local government ombudsman, as well as that of the National Audit Office. Will the Minister comment on the possibility of a double jeopardy scenario, in which the same complaint is investigated by different bodies? We also need further clarification regarding the stage at which a claim becomes sufficiently serious to merit criminal investigation and becomes, potentially, a criminal charge. There are interesting questions to be answered about the interplay with the European convention on human rights, which applies to various civil rights. I wonder whether Departmental lawyers have done any work on that, which might be applicable to certain parts of the Bill. I believe that that would be particularly relevant.
