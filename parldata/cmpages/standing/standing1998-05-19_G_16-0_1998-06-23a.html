Before I tackle the substance of criticisms of the Government's approach to over-the-counter medicines, I shall deal with some of the pertinent questions raised about the Government amendments. We have made no secret of the need to overhaul the schedule dealing with transitional arrangements. My right hon. Friend the President of the Board of Trade spoke about that on Second Reading. The right hon. Member for Wokingham suggested that the new schedule keeps elements of the old regime alongside the new prohibitions. I regret to say that he misunderstands the way in which the new schedule works.

The new transitional schedule deals with the way in which the Restrictive Trade Practices Act 1976 is to be dismantled. Transitional provisions are about the transition from the old regime to the new regime, but the right hon. Gentleman seems to have entirely overlooked the fact that we have taken pains to ensure that businesses are not burdened by an ineffective and bureaucratic RTPA for any longer than necessary. Hence, almost all registrable agreements made betweeen the Bill's receiving Royal Assent and the coming into force of the chapter I prohibitions will be non-notifiable under the RTPA.

When the prohibition comes into force, businesses will be fully free from their obligations under the RTPA. They will no longer slavishly have to furnish particulars of agreements; restrictive practices court orders will fall, and undertakings will cease to have effect. Instead, we shall have a modern efficient prohibition against anti-competitive agreements that is aligned with the European prohibitions.

The right hon. Member for Wokingham suggested that the new schedule introduces fundamental changes to the Bill. That is simply not the case. The changes are detailed and technical. The new schedule carefully and efficiently dismantles the existing regime, and ensures that businesses are able to make a smooth transition to the prohibition.

We are not keeping the Restrictive Trade Practices Act alive. Parties to most registrable agreements made after the Bill receives Royal Assent will not have to register them, and from the coming into force of the chapter I prohibition no particulars will have to be furnished under the RTPA. Where third parties would have had rights under the old regime, the Bill preserves them; they will continue to be able to take action for breaches of statutory duty under the RTPA and the Resale Prices Act 1976, but only in relation to the period when those Acts had effect.

The Bill also preserves the RTPA register. That is because the information on the register may be useful for some years after the new regime comes into force. Third parties may need information in order to consider whether to bring actions for the period when the old legislation still applied. The register also records which agreements are in receipt of directions under section 21(2) of the RTPA. As those agreements are excluded from the chapter I prohibition under schedule 3 of the Bill, the information on the register will be essential to determine the status of an agreement.

Some time after the new regime comes into force, the need to access information on the register will decrease. Hence, paragraph 10 of the schedule gives the Secretary of State the power to modify the requirements about the register.

Mr. Griffiths: These amendments deal with concerns about the risk of leaks of market sensitive information. They would reduce any such leaks to protect the interests of companies and their investors. They repeal section 83(1)(a) of the Fair Trading Act 1973, which requires that copies of a Monopolies and Mergers Commission report must be sent to every person named in the report as a person in whose favour a monopoly situation exists at least 24 hours in advance of the report being laid before the House and published.

Experience has shown that confidentiality requirements are not always respected. This provision of the Fair Trading Act therefore represents a real risk of damage to companies and their investors from leaks of price sensitive information. In future we intend that the practice for monopoly reports will be in line with that for merger reports. Named monopolists should be told the date and time of publication on the day before.

Mr. Redwood: On this occasion, I sympathise with the Government's intention. Leaks of such price sensitive information can be very damaging to those parties concerned. The problem would be worsened if those shareholders or traders in the marketplace who were party to such information tried to exploit it for their own gain. However, before we approve the Government amendments, I should like the Under-Secretary to answer one or two queries. It would also be helpful if the Under-Secretary could tell us what procedures will be put in place within the new competition authorities themselves, and within his Department. What is true for companies could also be true, through inadvertent lapses, for the competition authorities and for the Department itself. I trust that he will ensure that his procedures are tight and, most notably, that few people will see the material before it is published. Mr. Griffiths: I shall respond directly. Given that the right hon. Gentleman occupied this post, he will know that the merger reports regime does not allow 24 hours' notice. However, proper checks are made to ensure that the information considered by the authorities is accurate, and does not contain transposed figures and other similar typing errors. He will also know that merger reports are numbered copies. The Department of Trade and Industry, and its reporting bodies--in particular, the director general--have a creditable record. In my experience of the past 14 months, no leak of information on any merger report has occured, despite the widespread press speculation that was prompted by one board disclosing that it had made a bid.

The amendment deals with monopoly reports, which are treated differently in one crucial respect. All parties who may have submitted evidence to a Monopolies and Mergers Commission inquiry are entitled to see the report 24 hours beforehand. In a number of cases where not just a few but dozens of parties receive the report--for example, those concerning fine fragrances, newspaper distribution, and foreign package holidays--the procedure is neither leak proof nor watertight. The only way to avoid that problem is to bring the monopoly reporting regime into line with the merger reporting regime. That is the purpose of the amendment, and I hope that explanation satisfied all hon. Members.
