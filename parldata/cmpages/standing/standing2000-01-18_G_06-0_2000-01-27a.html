Clause 48

Permissible donors

9 am

Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): I beg to move amendment No. 53, in page 29, line 31, at end insert—

    `or who at his death was ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom and had within the previous five years been registered in an electoral register.'.

There is a slight tendency to jet lag this morning, but I hope nevertheless to focus the minds of members of the Committee on the amendment. Clause 48(3) deals with the disqualification of bequests as permissible donations where those who made the bequests were not registered on an electoral register at the time of death. The amendment would ensure that such people must, at the time of death, be

    ``ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom''

and registered on an electoral register for the previous five years.

That would make a practical, common-sense change, with which I hope the Parliamentary Secretary will agree. Many Committee members will know that people who become infirm or ill, perhaps seriously so, often change address in the last year or few months of their lives. They might enter a nursing home, or move in with relatives. Having had to act as an executor for a relative who died in similar circumstances, it is my experience that such people are not registered on the electoral register at that point. Often, their names are taken off the electoral register at their former address, where they may have lived for 20, 30, 40 or 50 years. However, for one reason or another, such people are often not registered anywhere at the time of death. There are various reasons for that. They might be mentally incapable, or so physically infirm that their carers decide that it is inappropriate to register them. Carers might even overlook registering the new address.

Committee members may want to reflect on our deeply paranoid fear of the misuse of postal votes in such circumstances. Perhaps a proper way to avoid misuse would be to register such people on the electoral roll to maintain the validity of bequests that would otherwise not be valid according to a common- sense interpretation of the Bill. The amendment would take account of those who have moved in the recent past and are no longer on the electoral register. It would not be right, and it is surely not the intention of the Bill, for bequests to fall simply because such people were not registered during the last year or two of their lives.

I hope that the Parliamentary Secretary will respond positively to the amendment. He should bear in mind that, as we speak, legislation is passing through the House that will introduce the rolling register. Its purpose is to keep the register up to date, but it is likely to be even more effective than the present system in deleting such people from their previous addresses. However, it is unlikely to be much more effective at re-registering them at their new addresses.

The amendment would provide a perfectly sensible safeguard for the intentions of people who, in many cases, have given almost a life time's service to a good cause, and have made a series of bequests. It would be strange if their bequest to the dogs' home or to the Children's Society could continue to be honoured, but their commitment to civic society via a political party could not be implemented.

Mr. Martin Linton (Battersea): I beg to move amendment No. 30 in page 30, line 30, at end add—

    `(9A) No donation by an individual under subsection (2) (a) above may exceed £250,000 in any financial year (as defined in section 36 (6))'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 31 , in page 30, line 30, at end add—

    `(9A) A donation by an individual under subsection 2(a) above which is required to be recorded under section 55 must be made by means of a personal cheque drawn on the account of the donor.'.

Mr. Linton: Amendment No. 30 would put a limit of £250,000 on the size of individual donations and amendment No. 31 would ensure that donors must write individual donations on a personal cheque on their own bank account.

If, as my hon. Friend the Minister said, the previous amendment was a piece of fine tuning, amendment No. 30 probably comes under the heading of re-„engineering. It is intended to deal with what many people see as the one major issue that the Bill has not addressed.

In recent years, public concern has centred on overseas donations and the non-disclosure of donations, both of which are well addressed by the Bill. However, worries about millionaire donors have not been addressed—although, of course, many such donors are overseas and will therefore be covered by the relevant provisions. It makes no difference if a £1 million donation comes from somebody who is resident in this country—a £1 million donation from a single individual will always be a cause for concern. The fact that the donation is disclosed, or that the donor is not foreign, does not address the central problem—that party treasurers, however high minded, will never be able to tread on the toes of somebody who has just given their party £1 million. However well intentioned it may be, a party that relies on big individual donations will always feel beholden to millionaire donors. It will always be nervous of offending them and anxious not to jeopardise the possibility of another donation.

Parties might be incorruptible in their own eyes, but the public will not necessarily believe that. The public believe that the integrity of parties had been compromised, as we saw with the Ecclestone affair. I believe that the Prime Minister behaved with the utmost integrity in seeking advice from Lord Neill and returning the donation, but justifiably came under attack. That is why I want to impose an upper limit on permissible donations from an individual.

I do not deny that the figure of £250,000 is arbitrary. Any figure that is suggested will be arbitrary. The Liberal Democrats, in their evidence, suggested a limit of £50,000 for individual donations. I think that that is a little too low because donations of £50,000 or £100,000 are unlikely to buy favours from a Government or be thought to buy favours. The risk of that, or at least of creating the perception of that, arises when donations of £250,000, £500,000 and more are made. My suggested figure is very generous. In Quebec, parties are not allowed accept donations of more than $3,000—£1,500. In the United States, the limit is $25,000—£17,000. There is some talk of increasing that to $75,000, but even that would bring it only to the £50,000 limit proposed by the Liberal Democrats. I propose a limit five times higher than that at £250,000, which would provide plenty of leeway and freedom for individuals who want to give money but stops short of the amount that begins to compromise, or is believed to compromise, the integrity of parties.

The Neill committee considered donation limits. It argued against a limit on donations for four reasons. First, disclosure should effectively contain the arms race between the main parties. Disclosure is desirable in itself and the Bill forces parties to disclose donations of more than £5,000, which is an important measure, but disclosure alone does not transform the situation in the way that some people believe. The United States has had disclosure for the past 25 years. It has not stopped large donations being made; it has made them more blatant. Some people might be deterred by the threat of disclosure from making a donation, but many others are not. I am not saying that there is anything wrong with those donations, but the mere fact of disclosure does not stop large individual donations.

 

Dr. Julian Lewis (New Forest, East): I thought that the purpose of the restrictions in the Bill was to stop corruption rather than large donations per se. Surely the test that should be applied is not whether large donations cease to be made, but whether the taint or suspicion of corrupt practices following from the making of large donations persists. If the hon. Gentleman is saying that people are happy to continue to make large donations in the full knowledge that everyone knows that they will have done so, what is wrong with that?

Mr. Linton: The test of disclosure is merely a test of whether the public know about the donation. They may not know about the strings attached or any understanding between the donor and donee. I do not want to reiterate history unnecessarily, but it is important to understand that many of the donations to both major parties in the past few years became public knowledge not because they were disclosed, but because the donor volunteered the information or it got into the press. Asil Nadir gave £450,000, John Latsis gave £2 million and there have been million-pound donations to the Labour party. The fact of the donations may have been disclosed, but we shall never know what the donor intended, what he hoped to secure from it or, what the recipient was told or led to believe in private. We will never know what possibilities of reward were held out.

Dr. Lewis: In that case, the limit will not achieve the objective. If someone can give as much as £250,000 openly, is the hon. Gentleman saying that the process will bring into the open any secret strings that might have been attached, whereas if the limit were higher, it would not? If someone is known to have given a large sum, it might be blindingly obvious that a favour has followed: but why should favours be any less concealed when the donation is below £250,000 than when above it?

Mr. Linton: The hon. Gentleman's faultless logic misses the issue of magnitude. Political parties are small organisations. The entire budget of the Liberal party is about that of a primary school, while that of the Labour party is about the same as—

Mr. Stunell: A secondary school.

Mr. Linton: Indeed. The Labour party's is similar to that of a small college, and even the Conservative party, with all its riches, does not have a budget to match any hospital in the country. Political parties are small organisations. Large donations are always in danger either of influencing us or of being seen by the public to influence us. Many Ministers spend their working lives juggling with billions of pounds— sometimes a Department's budget might be £40 billion or £50 billion. When they go home to their constituencies on Saturdays, they might be running a tombola in a local fete, collecting 50p off the public. There is a big disjunction between the poverty of political parties and the budgets that they bid for when they stand for election to run the country. In those circumstances, large donations can be seen to exert undue influence on the hard-pressed treasurers of political parties. It is important to understand that in any country, there will be important issues of public perception.

Let me continue with some of the other arguments advanced by Neill. It was argued that it would be easy for a donor to evade an upper limit by spreading resources among friends or by setting up subsidiary companies. In fact, clause 54 will make it illegal to use a friend as a conduit for a donation. The objection has already been dealt with.

Charlotte Atkins (Staffordshire, Moorlands): Would the clause cover the case of someone wanting to purchase the handbag of a former Prime Minister? If it were intended to purchase such an item for £500,000, would the clause prevent it?

Mr. Linton: I thank my hon. Friend for that interesting point. It would cover not only cash donations, but donations in kind. My hon. Friend would agree that the Bill makes it clear that donations in kind are to be treated in exactly the same way as donations in cash. When someone pays an excessive price, the excess will be treated as a cash donation. So £500,000 for a second-hand handbag at the North Dorset Conservative fete or auction would, apart from about £15, be treated as a cash donation. I may, of course, have underestimated the intrinsic value of the handbag: it may have been a Gucci or another of high quality. As my hon. Friend the Member for Staffordshire, Moorlands (Charlotte Atkins) said, the handbag of such a famous person would, even in the normal course of events, fetch a few hundred pounds. It would be up to the Electoral Commission to estimate and to agree with the treasurer of the party the excess that would be considered a donation.

It is important to understand that while the opportunities for evasion will always exist, they must not make us over cautious about legislation. In Quebec, for example, there is a limit of $3,000 or £1,500 on any donation from anybody, including businesses and trade unions. There was much talk in the early days of the big companies, which all support the Liberal party in Quebec, hiring tables at a Liberal lobster dinner and giving all their executives $3,000 to donate which they could then claim back on company expenses. Instead of giving $3,000, a company could in that way give perhaps $24,000. In fact some companies did that, but the Quebec legislature passed a simple law saying that individual donations had to be drawn on a personal bank account, which effectively stopped that practice.

That is the basis of amendment No. 31. Nobody argues that evasion does not exist, but just as with taxation there are ways of plugging loopholes. New laws sometimes have to be passed to do so. The fact that there are ways of evading tax does not mean that we should not have taxes, and the fact that there are ways of evading the limit on a donation should not stop us setting one.

Neill's third recommendation, to which we must play close attention, was that the proposals on tax relief would encourage parties to switch from a few big donations to a lot of small donations—the basic move we are trying to encourage in the Bill. Neill said:

    ``it is therefore unnecessary to introduce a new ban or a donations limit to produce the same result.''

In other words, the reason Neill gave for not putting forward a donations limit was that that objective would be achieved by the tax relief on donations. The Government have decided—I do not disagree with them on this—that the tax relief on donations has two major faults. One is that it is an indirect and barely disguised form of state funding, which not everyone would agree with. The other is that it gives an unfair advantage to parties that are likely to be supported by richer donors.

It is not my job, on this amendment, to argue the pros and cons of tax relief on donations, I merely point out that that central part of the Neill recommendations has been rejected. It was intended to achieve exactly what has been achieved in countries such as Canada, where, through the tax system, they have persuaded parties to rely on a large number of small donations rather than on a small number of large donations. Indeed, although the Progressive Conservatives in Canada fought that legislation tooth and nail, they instantly set up a data bank of small donors and replaced all the money that they had lost from large donations. Within a couple of years they were the most effective fundraisers in Canadian politics. All the parties in Canada quickly adapted their fundraising techniques, they forgot about the millionaires and put their energies into finding small donations.

That was Neill's objective and it is a worthy one. Neill hoped that it would be achieved through tax relief. That is why he did not suggest a donations limit. That tax relief has not materialised. The argument for a donations limit is now much stronger. Neill's objective was to get away from millionaire funding. He proposed one method. It has not been adopted therefore at the very least we should consider a direct limit on donations as a way of achieving the same result.

There is an argument in Neill, lest anyone thinks that I am trying to overlook this part, which says:

    ``Individuals should have the freedom to contribute to political parties, and the parties should be free to compete for donations.''

I do not deny that that freedom is essential. People want to express their support for political parties by giving money. That is not in dispute. What is in dispute, however, is the level to which individuals should be free to make donations and the level to which parties should be free to compete for those donations. In other words we do not want the give and take of politics and the free for all of fundraising to turn into a grubby searching after millionaire donations.

Before the election the Labour party had no choice but to try to match the fundraising of our main rival, the Conservative party, by seeking large donations of our own. No one denies that. I have already mentioned Ecclestone; Lord Sainsbury has also given donations of more than £1 million. No one denies that that has happened in the past. We are trying to make it impossible for it to happen in future.

In the past the Labour party's only argument against a donations limit was that it would stop trade unions or other large organisations giving large sums of money. The aggregate total of all the individual donations given by a single trade union may well be more than £1 million, but that was at a time when no legal distinction could be made between an individual donation, a company donation, a trade union donation and a friendly society donation.

That difference has been made in the clause. Subsection (2)(a) deals with individual donations and another subsection deals with company donations, which are subject to shareholder ballots. Parties are subject to the Registration of Parties Act 1998. Trade unions are subject to their own political fund ballots. The clause clearly differentiates between the various kinds of donations. It is now possible for the first time to put into legislation a limit on individual donations that does not have the unintended consequence of closing off the possibility of funding from companies or trade unions or corporate bodies of any kind.

We all know the mischief that has been caused by big individual donations. The hon. Member for Beaconsfield took a rather hostile tone on my amendment about tax exiles. I understand that, but he must realise that the objective of the Bill is not to gain party advantage but to put all parties on a level playing field. Nothing in the amendment is intended to do anything that harms any party. A donations limit will affect all parties in the same way, It will simply remove the possibility—the temptation if you like—of going for rich individuals, and will force us into the good habit of fundraising small amounts of money from members, subscribers, and supporters. They can still give quite generous sums of money, but not sums that will raise the spectre of influence and even corruption.

Over the past few years we have seen far too much of that kind of thing ever to want even to leave open the possibility that we could go back to it. When Lord McAlpine was treasurer of the Tory party he made no secret of the fact that in the late 1980s, as company donations dried up when companies understandably became more reluctant to give large sums to political parties, he deliberately switched to large individual donations. We only know the ones that have become public. I mentioned Asil Nadir and John Latcis, but British donors gave large amounts of money. Graham Kirkham is one example. Shortly after that he became Sir Graham and he is now Lord Kirkham. I am sure that is just coincidence. Many others have given large individual donations. Because there was no disclosure we do not know how many there were.

A researcher who has looked into Conservative party accounts from the 1920s and 1930s told me that it was the practice during those years to mark large individual donations with a code. It was not even the initials of the donor. The donor's identity was known only to the party treasurer. There was a letter in the correspondence in which the treasurer wrote to the donor, ``For goodness sake, I've told you before, don't write to me at head office, write to my home address.'' The practice of collecting large individual donations, being careful not to reveal the identity of the donor and receiving them at home addresses, goes back to the 1920s and 1930s.

No one will convince me or the ordinary voter that such party fundraising is not deeply unhealthy or does not open up a real danger of corruption. One has only to scrutinise the honours list to see at a fairly benign level what it can lead to. Indeed, in more recent times, Brigadier General Sir Brian Wyldbore-Smith who for many years was treasurer of the Conservative party, made no secret of the fact that in the 1980s he was fundraising from wealthy individuals from his home address.

There is a chapter of political fundraising that every party wants ended. This system forced parties into the arms of individual rich people—who may have been well motivated and gave money for idealistic reasons. We do not know, but it raised in the public mind a great suspicion that favours could be linked. This was all too apparent with overseas donors, who clearly could not have been buying peerages or knighthoods because they did not qualify for them. People obviously wondered what kind of reward they expected for their donation, but that is not very different from home-grown donors of this magnitude. The ordinary voter would not believe that they were all giving money for idealistic reasons, especially when they went to such great lengths to keep this knowledge from the public.

If we want a new chapter in fundraising in this country—all parties have an equal interest in that—we need not only a ban on overseas donations, and disclosure, but the one reform that is missing from the Bill, which is a limit on very large donations. That will force us all into the better habit of raising our funds from members and small donors.
