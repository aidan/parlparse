The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson): I, too, welcome you to the Chair, Mr. Malins. I recall that we were last together on the Select Committee on Home Affairs, and it is a pleasure to have you chairing our Committee today.

I shall answer the points raised during our debate on the amendment, and explain the provisions of clause 183. As Opposition Members said, the clause requires the FSA to establish a body corporate to manage the compensation scheme set out in this part of the Bill. Five compensation schemes currently operate in the financial services sector-the building societies investor protection scheme, the deposit protection scheme, the friendly societies protection scheme, the investors compensation scheme and the policyholders protection board. Those schemes will be replaced by a single scheme.

The Bill will enable the FSA to take forward the basic structure and characteristics of the current schemes, including the current levels of compensation that those schemes can pay out, and build the scheme around a new and more effective framework. That is consistent with the Government's overall commitment to reduce overlap between different regulatory bodies, thereby cutting unnecessary costs and the potential for gaps. Such measures should bring major benefits for business and consumer alike. Our proposals for rationalising the various schemes into one have received widespread support from the industry and consumer groups.

I agree with much of the thinking behind the amendment. It is clearly necessary for the FSA, when appointing the board of the scheme manager, to ensure that it appoints people who have relevant experience and expertise to ensure that the scheme operates effectively and efficiently. The necessary experience will, in many cases, include experience of the industry, but I see no advantage in making the Bill so specific about the constitution of the board.

First and foremost, board appointments need to be made on the basis of merit-I am sure that no one would disagree with that. I do not suggest that the FSA would be hard-pressed to find three directors of authorised firms who are sufficiently competent to perform those functions, but it would be unfortunate if the authority was ever unable to appoint a highly suitable candidate because the quota proposed in the amendment had not been met. I shall deal with those circumstances shortly and sketch out what might happen.

If we were to require industry appointments, the individuals appointed might be thought to have been put in place to represent the sector in which they worked; the three appointees would therefore have to represent three particular groups. That might work for the policyholders protection board, as we would have to look for candidates only from the insurance sector, or for the depositors protection scheme, which would have to worry only about the banks; but there are too many other groups-including insurance, deposit-taking and investment-for every sector to be represented. In any case, we are generally against that. Appointments should be made in the public interest, not to represent particular interests. That is our view on board appointments to the FSA; the same thinking applies here.

To the extent to which people from the industry are appointed to the board, I would question whether they have to be directors of authorised firms. For example-this is relevant to the points that Opposition Members have raised-a highly regarded chief executive of an authorised firm may decide to retire, but want to carry on working part-time. Of course, the FSA needs to ensure that the board has suitable experience and expertise, not only because it will wish to have the support of the industry and consumers, but because it would have failed in its duties in relation to the scheme if it did not do so.

We are not going further down what the right hon. and learned Member for North-East Bedfordshire called the mutualisation road. The provisions have been brought forward from the Policyholders Protection Act 1975, which was amended by the Policyholders Protection Act 1997. The long-term nature of long-term insurance policies means that special arrangements are needed to protect policyholders if their insurer gets into financial difficulty.

The policyholders protection board currently pays compensation in cases in which insurance companies are in default. In the case of general insurance, such as home or motor insurance, the type of loss that a consumer may face will be limited to the value attached to the time that a policy has left to run or the value of a claim that a policyholder needs to make under his policy. However, losses associated with long term insurance are addressed rather differently by the policyholders protection board, which has a duty to seek to continue coverage, compensating only when it is unable to do that.

When a general insurance company goes into liquidation, policyholders will lose the remaining period of cover for which they have paid a premium, unless they have a claim against the policy at the time of the insolvency. However, when a long-term insurance company becomes insolvent, the loss to the policyholder is much greater than the cost of the premiums for the unexpired term because, in many cases, life insurance is a savings mechanism as well as an insurance mechanism. In addition, changes in a policyholder's circumstances since taking out the policy may make buying a replacement policy difficult or expensive.

Therefore, when the scheme is liable to pay compensation, it may be required by rules to seek out another authorised firm that will take over the long-term policies or one that will issue a substitute policy. The scheme may also take an interim option, which will entail a receiver taking on the administration of the policies. That is essential to protect consumers who would otherwise face more worry and expense. However, substitute cover is currently restricted by limits on ordinary payments, which are 90 per cent. of the value of the amount for which the defaulting firm is liable. We expect that arrangement to be carried forward by the FSA. The organisational structure has changed somewhat, but the principles are the same.

The Bill allows the scheme to make compensation payments, should it prove impossible for new policies to be secured. However, its first move should be to take action under clause 187(3).
