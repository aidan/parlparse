It confirms the existing legal status of the Financial Services Authority. Paragraph 1(1) of schedule 9 to the Financial Services Act 1986 provides that

    ``A designated agency shall not be regarded as acting on behalf of the Crown and its members, officers and servants shall not be regarded as Crown servants.''

Mr. St. Aubyn: Before the hon. Member for Dudley, North rises with his usual alacrity, I acknowledge that amendment No. 65 is less than perfect. The notes on clauses set out the changes and paragraph 5 on page 51 makes a further reference to the Chancellor of the Exchequer when it might be argued that, for the sake of consistency, the Governor's name should also appear. The purpose of the amendment is to allow us to debate the principle of who should be at the peak of authority. There is also the argument that there should be a twin peak of authority and in that sense the amendment is not incomplete, because it would provide that both offices had an element of authority over appointments of those members.

The main debate is whether the Chancellor or the Governor should sit at the peak of authority of the new organisation. The Governor was formerly at the head of banking supervision, but it will now be the Chancellor. That is the key to the Treasury's motivation. Many people asked in May why the Treasury should be handing over power. Why did the Chancellor, as soon as he was elected after 18 years in opposition, immediately hand over his power to the Bank of England?

What has happened, as the Bill and, especially the clause, makes clear is that an element of operational control over interest rates has been transferred to the Bank of England, subject to the reserve powers and some of the other issues that we shall deal with in a moment. However, the Bank got a very poor bargain in return. We shall discuss later how, for the first time ever, it is losing its operation of the gilt market, but above all, it is losing its authority over banking supervision.

If, as the Government have said is their long-term intention, the country enters the European monetary union, the one area of authority that the Bank has under the Bill--operational control of interest rates--will be transferred to Frankfurt. Clearly, the Bank will be a loser all round. Clause 28, more than any other, will hurt the Bank. If I were the Governor and had considered resigning when I heard the announcement of the transfers of supervisory powers, it would be on this point that I would tender my resignation.

I am not denying that there are some good arguments for having a separate regulatory agency, one of which is that if the agency fails in its duties, it will not impact the central Bank's reputation and standing in the way in which it has been argued the reputation of our central Bank was impacted by regulatory failures in the past. A counter argument is that such effects are debatable. One of the outstanding achievements of the Bank of England in the time that the Governor has been at the top of the regulatory tree has been its avoidance of any major costs to the British taxpayer as a result of regulatory failures. One of the real concerns, which has been expressed in other clauses but which arises again here, is that under the new regime, with the Chancellor sitting at the top of the regulatory tree, there may be a much greater risk that the taxpayer will somehow end up picking up the tab.

Before continuing with that point, it is important to have an answer from a Minister. If regulatory failure in the past has been deemed to have affected the standing of the Bank and the Governor, do the Government accept that a future regulatory failure of the banking system will affect the Chancellor's standing? If such a failure occurs, do the Government accept that the Chancellor should consider his position and that he may well be forced to resign? If he is not prepared to be accountable for failures of the regulatory organisation, there is little point in putting him at its head.

On the other hand, if the Chancellor is accountable for failures, there will be tremendous political pressure to engage in measures to cover up such failures. Unlike the Governor of the Bank of England whose means of doing so are strictly limited, those of the Government may be wide. It could be said that one of the virtues of the system in the past is that the person who controlled the cash--the Chancellor--was distinct from the one who controlled the institution. That separation of powers between the Chancellor and the Governor made for a much healthier system of banking supervision and regulation.

The danger in the new arrangement is that it will be seen to have politicised the function of bank regulation. The danger of politicisation is that the credit of politicians can be exhausted far more rapidly than that of central bankers. As we have seen under the Government, there is a temptation in politics to say one thing and mean another. Before the election, it was intimated by the Prime Minister that there would be no change in the regime for PEPs and TESSAs. We have now discovered to our cost--it has affected hundreds and thousands of voters--that there will be some real changes to the regime.
