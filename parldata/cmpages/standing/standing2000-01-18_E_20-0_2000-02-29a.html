Mr. Hill: I shall be brief. In our view, amendments Nos. 390 and 449 have no practical effect. The hon. Member for Bath is right. It is well understood that in the statute references to the singular include the plural. Therefore, when clause 104 speaks of granting to ``another person'' the exclusive right to operate local services, it does not prevent a local authority from letting a series of quality contract contracts or letting a quality contract contract to a consortium including a number of different operators. The point is simply that bus operators at large would no longer be able to operate within a quality contract area unless they were party to the quality contract contract. To that extent, any operator under a quality contracts scheme enjoys exclusivity as far as his particular contract is concerned. The amendment would achieve nothing that is not already provided for in the Bill.

On the apparent contradiction to which the hon. Member for Bath alluded, ``authority'' covers the making by a single authority and ``authorities'' covers joint schemes. It is a drafting technique to make it clear that both types of schemes are covered. I hope that is helpful to the hon. Gentleman. As he desired, I have placed that on the record.

As the hon. Member for Poole explained, amendment No. 19 would amend the concept of a quality contracts scheme under the Bill. As drafted, the Bill is based on the principle that once a quality contracts scheme is in operation, local bus services can be provided within the area of the scheme only if they are within the terms of the quality contract. Thus, clause 104(4)(a) refers to an ``exclusive'' right to operate services to which the contract relates.

The amendment aims to remove that exclusivity. In drafting terms, it would not have the desired effect since other provisions in the Bill—notably clauses 104 and 109—would continue to apply. They disapply the general rights of bus operators under the Transport Act 1985 to run bus services within the area of a quality contracts scheme. However, it is not my intention to nitpick, so I shall address the point of principle.

The basic point about quality contracts as defined in the Bill is that they are an arrangement under which the local authority determines the bus network, frequency and fares, and lets a contract to a single operator or consortium to provide services to meet the local authority specification. This was discussed at some length on amendment No. 18. The presumption in such circumstances must be that the local authority decides what bus services are needed in a given area and to what standards they should be provided. The authority then secures those services by means of a quality contract for a given period of time, with payment of any necessary subsidy.

If it were possible, as is the apparent intention of the amendment, for other bus operators to continue to operate within a quality contract area, they would be able to compete with the contracted service, thereby undermining it. I cannot think that that is in anybody's interest. It would damage the commercial viability of the quality contract service and could, in the extreme, lead to its demise. It could certainly lead to the local authority having to pay more in subsidy, which would be bad news for local council tax payers.

As I have made clear, there seems to us to be no practical scope for a mix-and-match option in which commercial freedoms could co-exist with a quality contracts arrangement, The quality contracts scheme must, by definition, involve the grant of exclusive rights to operate within the area of the scheme and, with the best will in the world, we see no grounds for providing otherwise.
