Mr. George Howarth: It is not a matter of providing for no period--we must decide what constitutes a reasonable period. Clause 8(3) provides that a data controller who has already granted a subject access need not respond to a similar request from the same person unless, as the hon. Member for Ryedale said, a reasonable interval has elapsed since the first request.

What constitutes a reasonable interval will vary from case to case, but the issue does not rest on those words alone. The Bill, like the directive and the 1984 Act, does not specify ``a reasonable interval''. Instead, clause 8(4) lists three important indicators--the nature of the data, the purposes of the processing and the frequency with which the data are changed--to which controllers must have regard. They will help controllers judge what is and what is not reasonable.

The amendment would establish three months as a minimum reasonable interval between the first response and the subsequent request. It would thus build in an undesirable rigidity. Three months may be an inappropriately long period in some circumstances. In others, it may be too short, given the nature of the information and the processing.

The Government's approach--designed to retain flexibility--is better. It allows the right period to be determined in the light of all the relevant circumstances. It does not risk depriving individuals of the chance to exercise their right in an arbitrary way. That is key to our objectives.

The safeguard for trade secrets in clause 8(5) is drawn from recital 41 of the directive. It says that the right to information about the logic involved in automatic processing of personal data

    ``must not adversely affect trade secrets or intellectual property and in particular the copyright protecting the software''.

Recital 41 is a comment on the third indent of article 12(a) of the directive. Does the hon. Member for Ryedale still have the necessary material to follow the argument?

Mr. Greenway: I have just read the recital. Which article is it?

Mr. Howarth: It is article 12(a). In general terms, it allows the provision of information about the logic involved in automatic processing to be confined to fully automated decisions of the kind dealt with in clause 13 of the Bill. Clause 7(1)(d) takes that more limited option, so we are already narrowing right down the scope for the provision of information about the logic underlying automated processing as far as the directive allows us.

The Committee will note that recital 41 refers in particular to the ``copyright protecting the software''. That has no application to the limited right conferred by clause 7(1)(d). That right is limited to information about the logic involved in decision-taking. That does not include details for the software that may be instrumental in the application of the logic to the decision-taking process.

In the context of the limited right established by clause 7(1)(d), clause 8(5) gives full effect to the purpose of recital 41. Clause 8(5) limits the right provided by clause 7(1)(d). Clause 7(1)(d) creates a right to be given information by a data controller. It confers no rights to obtain copies of anything that the data controller has, nor does it confers any new rights on the data subject to use the information that he receives It is simply requires the data controller to produce some description of the factors and weightings involved in the decision taking.

It therefore leaves intact the law of copyright and other issues of intellectual property. As recital 41 says, the right that the Bill creates does not adversely affect intellectual property rights per se. The only legitimate concern that data controllers can have about that right is that it could force them to disclose information that is not generally known and that has some commercial or other value by virtue of that fact.

That is what we understand to be the essence of a ``trade secret'', whether or not the information is in copyright form or the subject of other intellectual property rights. It is information that needs protection from disclosure. Clause 8(5) gives it that protection from the requirement to disclose in accordance with clause 7(1).

Intellectual property law is not concerned to provide protection from disclosure per se. Information subject to intellectual property rights is, after all, generally in the public domain. The information is known, but its reproduction and use is restricted.

I hope that that explanation gives the hon. Member for Ryedale the reassurance that he seeks. Several examples of that approach are already on the statute book. For instance, the term ``trade secret'' has a clear pedigree. It is used in section 39 of the Radioactive Substances Act 1993 where it imposes a requirement to give public access to certain documents, except in so far as the disclosure of any such document would involve the disclosure of, for example, a trade secret.

Similar provisions apply in the Consumer Protection Act 1987, the Environment and Safety Information Act 1988, the Town and Country Planning Act 1990, the Environmental Protection Act 1990, the Food Safety Act 1990, the Water Industry Act 1991, and so on.

I am sorry to have to make hon. Members jump around in the Bill. Together, they make a series of purely technical changes that simplify and thus improve the Bill's structure.

As the Committee has seen, clause 12 makes provision for inaccurate data to be rectified, blocked, erased or destroyed. The fourth data protection principle in schedule 1 requires personal data to be accurate. Clause 12(5) contains a definition of inaccurate data. Paragraph 7 of part II of schedule 1, which interprets the fourth principle, contains an identical definition. That repetition could be confusing and it would be better to have a single definition. The purpose of the amendments is to achieve that.

Government amendment No. 123 makes the first of that series of changes by deleting clause 12(5). The later Government amendments will delete the corresponding definition in schedule 1 and replace them both with an identical definition in clause 64.

I apologise for the technical nature of that explanation. The changes are, however, necessary for consistency and I hope that they are acceptable.

I said all that I wanted to say about the terms ``inaccurate'' and ``misleading'' in our earlier debate on them. Amendment No. 53 raises that same point, and I have nothing to add. The Government prefer the existing formulation, which is drawn from the 1984 Act has worked well.

 

Mr. Greenway: Two points need to be made. First, the Minister tells us that, on reflection, he wants just one definition of ``inaccurate'', with a reference to it in the schedule. I have no objection to that entirely sensible approach; it is a belt-and-braces job. It is not necessary to have both definitions, and we have discussed similar issues on other amendments.

If the Minister accepts my argument on amendment No. 53, he will have to reconsider the wording of amendment No. 133 to clause 64; if he does not, we might have to return to the matter on Report if we want to persist with it. I am sure that we cannot technically seek to amend an amendment that has been moved, although we could table an alternative.

The Minister said that the existing provision had worked all right for 15 years. Given the increased use of computerised records, however, there is an argument for including the concept of incompleteness in the definition of ``inaccuracy''. As we have already mentioned, a person could have information on his file that was accurate as far as it went. However, it could be misleading because it was incomplete. For example, it could include a record of a county court judgment, but the reference would be incomplete unless it noted that the judgment had been discharged. Its incompleteness could make it misleading, which is why amendment No. 53 is phrased as it is.

Mr. White: The hon. Gentleman said that records could be incomplete, but when system analysts have tried to define data over the past 15 years they have been warned to take into account the provisions of the Data Protection Act 1984. It is unnecessary to add to the 1984 Act, which has worked well in obliging people to ensure that data comply with requirements.

Mr. Greenway: As I have said already, the approach to the 1984 Act for a long time has been, ``It's worked alright up to now, so why change?'' One of the benefits of the Bill is that it allows us to try to improve the regime. My point is simply that there must be many instances in which inaccuracy--that is, that which misleads--results from incompleteness.

Mr. Lock: One problem is that the information available to the data controller may not indicate whether a judgment, for example, has been discharged. How would the amendment comply with the fourth principle of schedule 1, which requires that data is accurate, if the data controller has only half the picture because of circumstances beyond his control?

Mr. Greenway: the hon. Gentleman makes a valid point. The corollary is that we must strike the right balance when we are discussing giving data subjects rights when inaccurate data cause damage and distress. The amendment is intended to clarify what might be misleading, and I think that incompleteness is one aspect of the problem.

However, as the Minister is about to delete the line to which my amendment would add the words, I see no point in persisting.
