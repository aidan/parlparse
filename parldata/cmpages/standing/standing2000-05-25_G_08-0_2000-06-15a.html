Mr. Philip Hammond (Runnymede and Weybridge): I beg to move amendment No. 170, in page 10, line 45, leave out ``made'' and insert ``served on the person registered.''.

Mr. Hammond: I should like to raise a couple of points on the amendments.

Lines 44 and 45 on page 10 refer to an order, variation or imposition having effect from the time when the order is made. The implication is that a registered person whose registration has been cancelled would be committing an offence at the moment that the justice of the peace makes the order. It is entirely reasonable and consistent with general legal practice that a person should not be put in a position where they might commit an offence without knowing it. Therefore, it is entirely reasonable to propose that the order come into force when it is served on the registered person-at the moment that he has notice of the fact, he must cease and desist from the activity as required.

In order not to allow such a provision to become a loophole that allows people to play games and to evade the notice server, we have tabled consequential amendment No. 172 to define ``service''. Therefore, if the person cannot be found, the notice can be nailed to their front door. That answers the obvious objection that the provision would be used to avoid the intended effect of the subsection. It is not acceptable to place a person in the position of committing an offence when unaware that the order had been made.

Amendment No. 171 would do something slightly different. Members of the Committee will have noticed that, as a result of debate in the other place, the Government saw the wisdom of inserting a provision in subsection (3) requiring that a registration authority seeking the cancellation of a registration should notify the appropriate authorities, which would include the health authority and the local authority social services department. There is an obvious reason for that: cancellation of a registration is likely to result in people who are being cared for finding that they have no place in which to be cared for, requiring the appropriate public authorities to make urgent alternative arrangements. That is entirely sensible, but if the appropriate authorities are notified at the time that the registration authority applies for an order to cancel registration, it is logical, consistent and, in fact, necessary to notify them, as subsection (5) states,

    as soon as practicable after the making of the order.

Therefore, the authorities would be informed that the registration authority was seeking cancellation, and subsequently that cancellation had been ordered by the magistrates court.

I can envisage the argument that, once the relevant authorities are aware that the process is taking place, they will engage with it, be closely involved, certainly know when the matter is going to court and be right on top of it, and that there is no need to notify them that the order has been made. Those of us who are aware of the pressures that health authorities and-even more so-local authority social services departments are under know that that is an idealised view of the world. The reality is that most people are trying to do about six jobs at once and it is quite possible that, having been notified that an application for cancellation had been made, they would lose sight of the exact process and timing of the court hearing. I hope that the Minister will consider that the insertion into subsection (5) of a requirement to serve notice on the appropriate authorities that an order had been made is a sensible change and consistent with those made in the House of Lords.

While we are discussing the issue of notice and cancellation, will the Minister reassure the Committee on the wider issue of the procedure for deregistration? It has been drawn to my attention that the mere service of deregistration notices by health authorities or local authorities under the Registered Homes Act 1984 may lead-not under the Act, but under the terms of contracts that registered homes hold with local authorities-to the automatic termination of the contract. That is my understanding of the reality, as built into many local authority contracts. The sequence of events is that a notice of deregistration is served by the health authority or local authority, the owner or manager lodges an appeal, but the contract ends immediately.

Notwithstanding any problems with bank and mortgage lenders that that may cause the person operating the home, by the time that the appeal is heard, the person is no longer operating a home, because he or she has no patients, no clients-all having been removed on the termination of the contract. Therefore, it is no longer possible for the tribunal to hear the appeal precisely for the reason that the Minister outlined when he moved one of his technical amendments on Tuesday: the legislation is phrased in the present tense, thus the appeal cannot be heard and, by definition, cannot be won.

I am seeking from the Minister a confirmation that he will use guidance or whatever other necessary means to ensure that the initiation of the deregistration process, either under clause 19 or under the more routine cancellation procedure in clause 13, will not be allowed to be used as an automatic ground for the termination of a contract by a local authority or health authority with care.
