This rather complex issue looks quite simple on the surface the clause refers to waste disposal authorities but a little history is needed to help the Committee understand its complexity. I will explain why I tabled this probing amendment.

Prior to the Environment Act 1990, waste disposal collection and regulation came under local authority control. As waste management activities are subject to a licensing regime, effectively, we had poacher and gamekeeper in the same camp. The 1990 Act introduced changes to the organisation of waste disposal and regulatory functions in local authorities, to separate regulatory and disposal functions. Three types of authority were constituted: the waste regulation authorities, which in England were mostly county councils; the waste disposal authorities which, because of the nature of things, were also county councils; and the waste collection authorities, which were district councils. The problem there is that with the unitary authorities in London and metropolitan areas, the three types of authority were still one and the same. Furthermore, as I have said, the county councils remained both regulatory and disposal authorities.

To offset that, local authorities in that position in England and Wales were required to take further steps to bring about the separation of the regulation and disposal functions. They had three choices. First, they could privatise disposal operations a choice taken by a number of them. Secondly, they could set up a joint operation with the private sector. A number of authorities, including Surrey county council, with which I am linked, are undertaking that option. It is currently in the process of setting up a joint operation, utilising the private sector through the private finance initiate, having been given Government approval and the go-ahead by the Departments that have responsibility. The third choice was to set up a local authority waste disposal company here, the usual ghastly local government phrase, LAWDC, comes into being. These are arm's length companies that are frequently the major choice of most local authorities in this position, but have to be run as commercial undertakings. They have a maximum of 20 per cent. local authority membership on the board; therefore local authorities are definitely a minority in LAWDCs. A large number of authorities, especially in London and the metropolitan areas, undertook this choice. In London, most formed partnerships of four or five authorities that then went into LAWDCs.
