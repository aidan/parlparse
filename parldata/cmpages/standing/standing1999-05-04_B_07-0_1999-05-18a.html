Clause 23

Rates of capital gains tax

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 120, in page 10, line 40, at end insert--

    ``(3A) After subsection (lAA) there shall be inserted--

    ``(1AB) Subsection (1AA) of this section shall not apply to trusts falling within paragraph 1 (1) of Schedule 1 to this Act (trusts for disabled persons), and the capital gains of such trusts shall be taxed at the lower rate.''.''. --[Mr. Heathcoat-Amory.]

4.30 pm

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No 119, in page 11, line 5, at end insert--

    ``(5A) The following shall be inserted after section 88A(3)(d) of the Finance Act 1989 (lower corporation tax rate on certain insurance company profits)--

    ``(e) chargeable gains''.''.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mrs. Barbara Roche): Before we adjourned this morning, I made the point that trusts for people with disabilities are already treated more favourably than other trusts, under the capital gains tax rules. They are treated in this way for reasons that all members of the Committee would endorse. They receive the full annual exempt amount set against their gains in any year, rather than half the annual exempt amount, which is available to trusts generally. I see no need to increase the complexity of the taxation of trusts by introducing a different rate of capital gains tax, and I therefore advise the Committee to reject the amendment.

Clause 23 provides that the rates of capital gains tax will be the same as those for savings income from 6 April 1999. Amendment No. 119 seeks to extend this principle to the rate of tax levied on insurance companies and friendly societies in respect of gains included in the share of their profits attributable to policyholders. There are special rules setting the rate of corporation tax charged on the profits of life companies and friendly societies. That part of a life insurer's profits on gains that will eventually benefit its policyholders is charged at a special rate at present equal to the basic rate of tax.

We are aware of the representations by the Association of British Insurers that the rate of corporation tax should be changed to reflect the change in the capital gains tax rate structure. However, the relationship between the corporation tax paid by an insurer on policyholder profits and the tax that the policyholders would have paid if they had made the underlying investments themselves is far from exact. That is because, among other reasons, the way in which gains on chargeable assets are calculated and the reliefs or deductions available are different for companies and individuals.

The Red Book shows that introducing quarterly payments will cost businesses £1.6 billion in year 1, £2 billion in year 2, £3.1 billion in year 3, and £2.2 billion in year 4. That is an extra £8.9 billion in taxes over the four years of the transition period. On top of that, businesses must find £5.2 billion for the windfall tax. If that were not enough, businesses must also pay an extra £15 billion to comply with all the new regulations that the Government have imposed.

The new quarterly corporation tax payments system more than outweighs the cut in the corporation tax rate. In 1999-2000, the introduction of quarterly payments will cost business £1,600 million. The benefit of the rate cut, according to the Red Book, will be ``negligible.'' In 2000-01, quarterly payments will cost business £2,000 million, while the benefit of the rate cut will be £700 million.
