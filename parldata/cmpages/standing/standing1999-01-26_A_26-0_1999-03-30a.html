New clause 19

Equality of Opportunity

    "(1) The Authority shall make appropriate arrangements with a view to securing that its functions are exercised with due regard to the principle that there should be equality of opportunity for all people.

    (2) After each financial year the Authority shall publish a report containing--

    (a) a statement of the arrangements made in pursuance of subsection (1) which had effect during that financial year, and

    (b) an assessment of how effective those arrangements were in promoting equality of opportunity.".

Good morning, Mr. Winterton. It is good to be here for the Committee's last sitting.

New clause 19 would impose a duty on the Greater London authority to ensure that it performed its functions with regard to equality of opportunity for all people. It would require the GLA to publish an annual report that set out the steps that it had taken in pursuance of the arrangements in subsection (1) and to assess how effective they had been in promoting equality of opportunity.

I quickly perused the White Paper again on the train this morning, and it is quite surprising that only paragraph 3.33 refers to equality issues. It states:

    "The GLA will be a local authority under the terms of the 1976 Race Relations Act",

which we debated last Thursday in respect of clause 265. The paragraph goes on to state:

    "It will have a duty to make appropriate arrangements with a view to securing that its functions are carried out with due regard to the need to remove racial discrimination and promote equality of opportunity and good relations between persons of different racial groups."

The Bill already contains clear responsibilities in respect of the Race Relations Act 1976, but we would like it to contain provisions to deal with the broader issues of equality of opportunity. That is what the new clause would do.

We hope that the Government will be able to take on board the new clause and the duty that it would place on the authority in delivering its functions and discharging its business.

We are as committed to that in London as we are in the rest of the country. Although the hon. Gentleman found only one reference in the White Paper, it was a clear reference representing a clear commitment. All the way through the process, in the Green Paper and thereafter, we have made it clear that we want to ensure that in our multicultural capital the authority responds to all sections of the community and promotes equal opportunities.

As a new authority, the GLA will have to observe the existing framework for equal opportunities that is imposed on local authorities. The GLA will be made subject to the provisions of the Race Relations Act 1976--as the hon. Gentleman has already indicated--the Sex Discrimination Act 1975 and the Disability Discrimination Act 1995. It will also be expected to carry out its functions, as do all local government and central Government bodies, without discrimination on the basis of gender, race or disability. More specifically, many of the Bill's provisions will ensure that the GLA gives equal opportunities the importance that they deserve. The provisions on consultation and openness are especially important in that respect.

The Committee has already discussed part II of the Bill, which sets out the requirement on the authority to consult in carrying out its policies. Following our debate, the Government undertook to consider those provisions further. We will return on Report with suggestions as to how we can more effectively carry through the existing commitment to ensure that there is meaningful consultation with all sections of the community in London. The Committee has also discussed the Bill's provisions on openness. The GLA will be fully accountable and open to Londoners. Provisions such as the mayor's monthly question time and the twice annual people's question time will ensure that the GLA is open to questions on all aspects of its policies, including equal opportunities. The assembly will have an important scrutiny role, and will have the power to mount investigations on any issue that is of interest to Londoners. That includes scrutinies of the mayor's functions, and would certainly allow for an investigation, or series of investigations, into equal opportunities if the assembly wished it. Those provisions will ensure that Londoners are fully aware of the policies that the GLA is pursuing and will allow them to hold the mayor properly to account.

Furthermore, the GLA will be made subject to the new best-value regime. Best value will require local authorities to put equality at the heart of their policies in delivering effective services and functions. No authority could be said to be truly effective if it discriminated against one section of the community, or in favour of another, in its delivery of services. That point was made very clearly by my right hon. Friend the Minister for Local Government and Housing during consideration of the Local Government Bill.

It is clear that the Government are fully committed to equal opportunities and that the GLA will have to act in accordance with them. Nevertheless, the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam raises important issues that deserve further consideration. Although it cannot be incorporated in its present form, we should like to give some further thought to the matter. As I said, we shall return on Report with further conclusions about the duties on consultation. In that context, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will agree to withdraw the new clause.

Mr. Burstow: I am grateful to the Minister for that positive response. However, the imposition of such a duty should not be viewed as impacting only on the way in which the authority consults. The new clause focuses on the appointments process of British members of the Committee of the Regions. When the Committee of the Regions was being developed, the appointments process was quite a contentious issue, at both the European and United Kingdom level. It was solved at the European level by deciding that the process should he decided by the member states--the principle of subsidiarity. In the United Kingdom, as I have already said, there was some debate between the then Government and Opposition, and the Opposition eventually won, so that the people serving on the Committee of the Regions were elected.

Local government is involved in the nomination process. The European international panel of the Local Government Association puts forward nominations to the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions, which then talks to the Foreign Office. The LGA has set down a number of criteria that it uses--such as political balance, regional distribution, gender balance, continuity of members who previously contributed significantly to the Committee of the Regions, and types of authority--and asks the political groups on the LGA to propose various members of its elected councils across the country. Each political group has a number of nominees in proportion to the votes cast in local elections. That is a fair way of going about the nomination process. At the final stage, the Foreign Office and the Council of Ministers ratify and approve those nominations.

The new clause seeks to develop that process. We want to ensure that Greater London has a specific role to play and that there are representatives from Greater London, at least two of whom are elected by the assembly. Surely that is a way of enhancing subsidiarity and democracy, and of ensuring that London's voice is heard on the Committee of the Regions. Representatives of London should be able to make a positive contribution to its debates.
