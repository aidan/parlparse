Mr. Letwin: We move now from amendments on which there was considerable common ground and about which there was no great concern on either side of the Committee to an amendment on which I fear that there may be disagreement—although I hope that we can persuade the Minister to reconsider now or later—and to an issue of some significance.

I am not certain why clause 10(1) refers to sections 2, 5, 6 and 18; it seems to us that it should have referred to sections 3, 5, 6 and 18. That is, however, a minor drafting point, which the Minister may illuminate. In any event, the amendment refers to sections 3, 5, 6 and 18, which deal with the possibility of the registrar refusing an application.

It is common ground between us that, although refusal does not signify a political party being cast into outer darkness, it is intended to be a massive inconvenience. Where circumstances are narrowly balanced, a refusal could affect results. It is, therefore fundamental to the operation of democracy that the refusal of an application is fair. I take it that, so far, we are on common ground.

Much has been thrust on the registrar in the Bill, which, appropriately, seeks to avoid cumbersome bureaucracy. That is a necessity because it would otherwise have been necessary to invent a cumbersome bureaucracy to avoid thrusting so much on the registrar. We will not argue that such a cumbersome bureaucracy should be invented. The reverse side of the coin is, however, that a decision that could have major implications for the result of an election and which is made by a single individual, albeit in discussion with the Committee—that is all common ground—must be open to further discussion. That should be possible in the event that a decision has adverse effects on a political party,which genuinely believes that it may suffer a reverse in an election of great national importance.

As the Bill is drafted—the Minister may correct me if I am wrong—the only recourse for a political party at that point would be to the courts. That is of constitutional significance. The grounds for judicial review are consciously limited, although they are ever-expanding in the hands of our judges. If the registrar had acted properly and reasonably and the result was subsequently strongly challenged by one of the political parties concerned, the party would be unable successfully to achieve the judicial review because the registrar would bring to his defence the fact that he had been reasonable, that he had properly considered the matter and that he had taken all due steps. The registrar could make a decision of national significance virtually unaided, although possibly in consultation with the Speaker’s Committee. He will be the sole person with responsibility for making the decision, and a judicial review would be the only recourse open to a political party that wished to contest it. Furthermore, the grounds on which a judicial review could be sought are consciously limited by the courts.

Conservative Members find that unsatisfactory in two respects. First, the necessity to involve the courts immediately following an adverse judgment by the registrar undercuts the Bill’s aim of limiting procedural complexity. As soon as one enters the courts, delay, costs and unpredictable results arise. Secondly, the fact that the grounds on which the court can intervene are limited, because the process would be one of judicial review, means that the substantive issue of whether the registrar has made an appropriate decision is not up for grabs at that point. That is a dangerous situation.

We hope that the registrar will be a thoroughly reasonable human being, who is well informed and will make decisions in line with a national consensus. However, that cannot be guaranteed in all cases. The burden on the registrar is therefore greater than any rational human being would wish to have thrust on them. The task is not a similar scale to that undertaken by, for example, a utility regulator, in which a firm’s profits and customer pricing are at stake. The President of the Board of Trade has frequently remarked that she has doubts about the propriety of a single regulator making decisions even of that kind. The registrar will deal with decisions that may affect the fate of the democracy—possibly almost irreversibly, depending on the policies of the parties that are contesting the election. For example, the Government’s wide-ranging constitutional reform programme means that the result of an election may fundamentally affect the democracy. There are, therefore, ample grounds for removing from the registrar the burden of making the final decision. A rational system of appeal should be installed, in line with long- established precedent for administrative action on this scale of importance in terms of the British constitution.

Conservative Members have sought to incorporate a method of appeal that is in the spirit of the Bill. Clause 10(1) stipulates that the Speaker’s Committee should be used as the source of advice for the registrar. We seek a minimal change that would alter the role of that committee so as to give it the additional role of a court appeal. I accept that that perhaps creates a slight tension: it may be difficult for the registrar to consult the committee given that it is the body to which an appeal may be made against his decisions. That may be an uncomfortable relationship for the registrar. If that is the Minister’s view, the remedy would lie in appointing another body to be the consultative body, while still using the laudable device of the Speaker’s Committee as the appeal body. For example, the Speaker herself, or designated officials, could be the advisory body. The Speaker’s Committee is well constituted to act as an appeal body.

The amendment states that the committee’s determination of the appeal “shall be final”. We want to restore an element of what we imagine the Bill aims to achieve—the minimisation of complex procedures and proceedings. The amendment would limit recourse to the courts, after providing a formal and ordinary and process of fair appeal.
