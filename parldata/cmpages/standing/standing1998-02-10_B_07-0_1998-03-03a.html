Clause 10, like clause 9, is unique to Northern Ireland because the funding arrangements are completely different there. In England and Wales the police grant is made up of two elements, one from the Home Office and the other from local authority funding. Police authorities in England and Wales function as local authorities and are subject to local authority finance arrangements. In Northern Ireland the position is entirely different. The Police Authority functions as a non-departmental public body and is subject to central Government financial arrangements.

The Secretary of State determines the annual police grant as part of the public expenditure survey exercise. The amount of the grant for policing is allocated after consideration of all the competing demands from other Departments. That is why the 1970 Act differs from the Police Act 1964 on which it was based and why the provisions in this Bill are different from the Police Act 1996.

The amendments are on a specific point. They seek to stop the Secretary of State seeing a draft of the estimates. They seek to allow the Secretary of State to see only the final authority approved estimates. I have a couple of comments on that. Secondly, the timetable for the processing of public expenditure bids within Government is extremely tight. As an ex-Minister, the hon. Member for North-East Cambridgeshire should be aware of the pressures relating to the conclusions of public expenditure bidding procedures.

The Secretary of State requires an indication of the police estimate—and all other major public sector spending programme estimates—as early as possible. That makes it possible for arguments to be advanced on behalf of particular budgets, so that various balances can be achieved. At present, Government officials work with the Police Authority and the RUC to arrive at an agreed bidding position before the official estimate is submitted.
