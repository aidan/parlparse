Mr. David Chidgey (Eastleigh): I shall speak to amendments Nos. 1 and 2, with particular regard to regional variation. I do not wish to revisit ground that the Committee has covered at some length. The debate has revealed key issues that must be addressed in our consideration of the national minimum wage.

The first whether significant regional variation in wages exists. The Committee has examined that question at some length. It can be said with all integrity that figures from the new earnings survey, from Government Departments and from the Trades Union Congress show that the north-east and the south-west have proportionally more low-paid workers than south-east England.

Secondly, what disproportionate impact would a national minimum wage throughout Britain have on regional employment costs and on regional economies? Economic analysis shows that, for small firms with fewer than 10 employees, the national minimum wage could increase employment costs by as much as 14 per cent. in the south-west, in contrast to 7 per cent. in the south-east. That variation is important.

Thirdly, the Committee has considered at length the whole thrust of the national minimum wage. The policy seeks to establish fairness and social justice and to lift people who earn low wages out of poverty and off benefits. I endorse those aims. I share the anxieties that Government Back Benchers have expressed about the need to end exploitation through low wages, and to end exploitation of taxpayers who currently support cowboy firms that pay poverty wages, which are topped up by benefits that are also paid for by taxpayers.

Hon. Members must recognise that a rigidly imposed national minimum wage, at a rate that will help the low-waged across the country, will pose a serious threat to small businesses in less prosperous regions. That is not to say that I subscribe to the purist economic theory that states that any rise in wage rates automatically leads to rises in unemployment. A wealth of empirical evidence shows that the introduction of a floor under low wages--a minimum wage--would not have such effects.

The evidence shows that increases in minimum wages can filter back into local economies, as local spending power increases at the margins. That would increase employment opportunities. The salient factor is that those local effects are heavily dependent on the level at which the minimum rate is set.

That is the real dilemma, not just for the Committee, but for the Low Pay Commission and, in due course, for Parliament.

There is wide variation in the proportion of people who earn low wages in different regions of Britain, and wide variation in the prosperity of those regions. Given that fact, how can a national minimum wage rate be set to achieve what appear to be the mutually exclusive objectives of removing social injustice while at the same time protecting small firms and employees from the consequences fo business failure?

In this invidious position, the responsible action should be to proceed with care and caution. The uncertainties must be investigated, examined and tested, and the body best placed to undertake such tasks in the Low Pay Commission. Therefore, I urge the Minister to widen the commission's remit, permit it to examine the impact, or otherwise, of regional variations on the national minimum wage, and allow the Bill to proceed with some degree of reassurance and certainty. I urge the Minister to consider the amendments seriously.
