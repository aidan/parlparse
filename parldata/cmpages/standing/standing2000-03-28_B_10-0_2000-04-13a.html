Clause 3

Power to extend to coastal land

10 am

Mr. James Paice (South-East Cambridgeshire): I beg to move amendment No. 241, in page 3, line 4, at end insert—

    `( ) In exercising any power in respect of coastal land under or pursuant to this Act, regard shall be had to the needs of flood defence and coastal protection.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider the following amendments: No. 242, in page 3, line 4, at end insert—

    `( ) In exercising any power in respect of coastal land under or pursuant to this Act, regard shall be had to the protection of wildlife and the needs of nature conservation.'.

No. 139, in page 3, line 12, at end add—

    `( ) An order under this section shall not be made until the Secretary of State (as respects England) or the National Assembly for Wales (as respects Wales) has consulted those interests likely to be affected by the order, has undertaken and published a regulatory impact assessment in relation to the proposed order, and has undertaken and published an assessment of the effects of a proposal for any such order on—

    (a) the environment, including any effects on the natural beauty, flora, fauna and geological and physiographical features of the land affected;

    (b) the use and management of land adjacent to the land affected; and

    (c) flood and coastal defence and coast protection in relation to the land affected.'.

Mr. Paice: We have severe reservations about the clause, Mr. Stevenson. As the amendments deal with specific concerns, I hope that you will allow a short clause stand part debate.

The amendments would introduce requirements that would have to be met if the Secretary of State intended to extend access rights to coastal land. The clause currently refers to

    ``coastal land or . . . coastal land of any description''.

That terminology is wide and open and covers a vast range of types of land, habitat and geology—a point with which I hope to deal in more detail during a clause stand part debate. Amendment No. 241 would require the Secretary of State to have regard to

    the needs of flood defence and coastal protection

in the exercise of any power. The Minister may say that the Secretary of State would do that anyway, but such provision should be included in the Bill to cover these crucial issues. Much of our coastline is extremely fragile and is open to erosion and significant flooding. We all know that particular regard must be given to the contribution by walkers to erosion. The Country Landowners Association has told us that it supports the amendments, as they would ensure that proper regard is given to such needs.

Amendment No. 242 deals with an issue that is, in many ways, much bigger. It would require the Secretary of State to have regard to

    the protection of wildlife and the needs of nature conservation.

I remind the Minister for the Environment of remarks that he made on Second Reading. He said that the priority of the Bill was

    to extend rights of access on foot, provided that such access does not damage the interests of wildlife, which must prevail.—[Official Report, 20 March 2000; Vol. 346, c. 724.]

I wholly support that statement, and it is already evident that we have strong doubts about other aspects of the Bill that compromise the ability of wildlife interests to prevail.

The 1979 European directive on the conservation of wild birds requires member states to take conservation measures, especially for specific rare and vulnerable species and for regularly occurring migratory species of birds. Such measures operate in part through the designation of statutory special protection areas by the Government on the advice of their statutory agencies. The designation is implemented through the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 and all special protection areas have to be notified first as SSSIs. Amendment No. 242 would ensure that appropriate recognition is enshrined within the Bill to keep it in line with European Union environmental rulings. Although there is much more public awareness of the need to protect the coastline—the disappearance of various bits of coastline is regularly mentioned in the media—the pressures are no less, and the provisions for access need to be balanced against the damage that access can cause to all aspects of our coastline.

Coastal bird colonies cannot cope with high levels of disturbance, either in the breeding season or when wintering flocks are present. The United Kingdom coast is extremely important for wintering birds. For instance, rock climbing can be very damaging to bird life, and most climbing cliffs are closed to climbers during the breeding season. We obviously have reservations about such access. There are many areas of particular importance; I shall not list them all, but they are to be found in all parts of the coastline of England and Wales and of Scotland.

The limits of coastal shoreline and marine zones are indistinct. That is why I mentioned the all-embracing terminology of clause 3, which refers to

    coastal land of any description.

Coastal land is difficult to define. Foreshores are a particularly susceptible ecosystem, and they are already being damaged by various external factors such as sewage discharge, nutrient run-off, boat mooring, sediment extraction, off-shore dumping and so on. That, too, must be properly accounted for. In short, although the Minister said that the interests of wildlife and conservation are paramount, it is essential that they are written in to the Bill.

Amendment No. 139 would require consultation to take place before the Secretary of State issues orders under clause 3. On Second Reading, the Minister undertook that any order of the Secretary of State to extend the right of access to coastal land would be

    subject to approval by each House of Parliament, and that no order will be laid until a full public consultation has been carried out and a regulatory impact statement has been prepared.—[Official Report, 20 March 2000; Vol. 346, c. 725.]

That is perfectly reasonable, but I ask the Minister to put action behind his words and to write words to that effect into the Bill.

I know that Henry VIII clauses are unpopular. Nevertheless, coupled with the environmental implications of clause 3, that undertaking, which was given and accepted in good faith, should be detailed in the Bill. The amendment would go a long way to meeting the understandable worry of environmentalists that the Secretary of State could extend the right of access without proper scrutiny or consultation, whatever the present Minister might intend. It is iniquitous that the Government would not be required to consult if they sought to make major extensions to the right of access.

Environmental regulatory impact assessments are also dealt with in the amendment. The need for environmental assessment prior to the extension of a right to roam over coast land is evidenced by the report of the UK biodiversity steering group, which includes a statement about boulders and rocks above the high tide mark. Page 302 of the report—I am sure that the Minister has a copy of it—states:

    recreational access can lead to the disturbance of rock-nesting birds. Tramping may also be detrimental to plant life.

On page 306, the report states that, for sand dunes,

    increasing tourism in coastal areas results in pressures from the development of public amenities and increasing levels of visitor pressure which leads to trampling of the vegetation, erosion of the dune systems and disturbance of breeding birds.

Those two statements underline better than I can the importance of undertaking a proper environmental impact assessment before orders made under clause 3 are laid before Parliament.

I accept in good faith what the Minister said on Second Reading, but, without looking into a crystal ball, I suggest that he will not be in his post for ever and a day. We do not know who will take his post or that of the Secretary of State. It could go either way. The Minister is right to flip his hand. I am not suggesting whether he will move up, down or sideways, but I suspect that he does not expect to be in the same job in five or 10 years' time. Certainly, we will be doing our best to ensure that that prospect is not even under consideration. His words, even if delivered in good faith, do not bind future Secretaries of State. Secretaries of State should be so bound, however. I have tabled the amendments to ensure that the obligations to which the Minister has verbally committed himself are written into the Bill.

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): The background to the amendments is that clause 3 allows the Secretary of State and the National Assembly for Wales by order to extend the statutory right of access to all or part of the foreshore and land adjacent to it. I recognise the force of the case that has been made. Coastal land is indeed rich in wildlife, and nature conservation bodies have expressed significant concerns that any proposal to extend or formalise coastal access might have adverse effects. Many sites are robust in the face of access and give little cause for concern but others are sensitive and could suffer negative impacts from human activities. Indeed, there is evidence that that has already happened in a minority of cases. Some types of coastal birds are especially vulnerable, often with considerable seasonal variation in sensitivity. Therefore, I understand the case that has been made and which needs to be answered. However, as the hon. Members for South-East Cambridgeshire and for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy (Mr. Llwyd) have said, we have already announced our intention to undertake full consultation and publish a regulatory impact statement before deciding whether to exercise the power to extend the right of access to coastal land. That will allow us to define the land affected and ensure that we take full account of all legitimate concerns, including conservation and coastal protection issues.

We recognise that the possibility of extending the right of access to coastal land raises issues of real significance. That is why clause 40(3) provides—these are not just my words as Minister for the time being, they are in the Bill—that a decision to extend the right of access in England will be subject to affirmative resolution in both Houses. That will ensure that there will be proper parliamentary scrutiny, and procedures in Wales will similarly provide effective scrutiny by the National Assembly.

Amendments Nos. 241 and 242 would require that, before extending access, the Secretary of State or the National Assembly for Wales should have regard to conservation interests and flood defence and coastal protection. We have already declared our intention to take full account of those matters before any decision is made about extending the right of access to coastal land. Of course, were we to decide to apply a statutory right of access to coastal land, the provisions in the Bill for closures on nature conservation or land management grounds would routinely apply to coastal land as they would to other access land.

The hon. Member for South-East Cambridgeshire made a point about the need to take account of the European protected conservation sites in complying with any coastal land order. I take his point. The Secretary of State and the National Assembly for Wales are already under a statutory duty to have regard to the protection of European conservation sites in exercising any function—for example, under the 1994 habitats regulations. The Secretary of State and the National Assembly for Wales will be under a new duty to protect SSSIs under the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 as amended by schedule 9. We have taken the matter fully into account.

I support the concerns behind the amendments, but they are fully and satisfactorily dealt with by the Bill. I am happy to repeat in the most positive terms our commitment that, before we lay a draft order before Parliament, we will publish a regulatory impact statement and undertake full consultations on the wide range of relevant issues, including those mentioned in the amendments. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will see fit to withdraw the amendment.

That is why we raised the issue of the directive on the conservation of wild birds. I accept the Minister's good will and therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Many of the other bodies that were consulted did not approve of the proposal. The Countryside Council for Wales responded:

    There should not be a statutory right of access to coasts, woodlands and watersides, due in part to the sensitive wildlife habitats found there, particularly along river banks, sea cliffs and in sand dunes.

It recommended that access should be expanded where appropriate by voluntary agreements backed up by grants. English Nature, the Government's statutory adviser on conservation issues in England, stated:

    Recreation may also have unwelcome impacts including physical damage to plants and sensitive geological features through trampling and erosion, collecting of biological or palaeontological specimens, changes in water quality, localised disturbance to wildlife, particularly birds, and negative by-products such as noise, artificial lighting, dog excreta and litter Development associated with recreation, especially in coastal areas, can have negative impacts including the loss of habitats and species.

The hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy referred earlier to ownership. As I understand it, the Crown owns about half the foreshore. The rest is owned by the Duchies of Cornwall and Lancashire, in those counties, the National Trust, local authorities and some private owners. Ownership of other coastal land, above the high tide mark, varies widely, but many coastal stretches are served by the rights of way.

Without repeating what I said in the debate on the amendments, I must remind the Committee that vast areas of coastal lands are sites of special scientific interest, or have special protection for other reasons. Widespread concern is felt about the impact not just of access but of all the other threats to our coastal land, which is why so much is classified in those ways. An example of coastal erosion is to be found at Orford Ness in Suffolk, close to where I spend much of my life. It has an important area of shingle spit and salt marsh that is rich in plant life and birdlife. Increased access to the area would have a negative effect on local biodiversity. I have referred to pollution, which is highly significant in the area. Marram grass is being encouraged in order to stabilise sand dunes that are being eroded by public access; it is clearly human disturbance that leads to dune destabilisation.
