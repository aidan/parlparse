Turning to the amendments. As the hon. Member for Ashford (Mr. Green) said, the amendments need not detain us long. They seek to add a number of new categories to the list of excepted land set out in schedule 1. I am afraid that I find none of them very helpful.

Amendment No. 47 would except land that is used to process waste materials. That strikes me as an odd activity to encounter on open country or registered common land, as the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath) said—[Interruption.] My pronunciation of ``Frome'' displays my lack of a classical education. It has never struck me that a waste recycling centre might be perceived as open country. We do not expect those facilities to be found on access land. In the unlikely event that they are, we imagine that the operation will fall within the curtilage of buildings.

Amendments Nos. 48 and 67 except land formerly used for mining operations that is being backfilled. That is seldom likely to occur on access land, for reasons that we have discussed. It is unlikely that a quarry or other excavated area would qualify as open country, still less if it were being backfilled with rubbish, which presumably would be generated by the waste recycling centre so imaginatively located in open country. In any event, applications for restrictions or closures can be made if necessary.

My hon. Friend the Member for Pendle tabled amendment No. 145, which would amend paragraph 4 to make it clear that the exception of land that is used for the getting of minerals applies only if the minerals are being worked. Amendment No. 203, tabled by the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome, would have the same effect. However, I reassure hon. Members that the amendments are unnecessary because the definition of excepted land in clause 1(2) is land that is

    for the time being—

I stress that phrase—

    of any of the descriptions specified in . . . Schedule 1.

If land becomes excepted, the right of access is suspended only for such time as it continues to meet one or more of the descriptions in the schedule. The right will be permanently lost only if land is removed from the conclusive map at the review, which is provided for in clause 10. If as hon. Members conjecture, mineral working ceases on access land, the right of access will resume. However, safety issues may make it necessary to impose restrictions or exclusions on land under chapter II, but that will be for the relevant authorities to decide. If the character of the land is changed as a consequence of the works so that it is no longer mountain, moor, heath or down, that will also be considered when the map is reviewed.

Amendment No. 60 would amend paragraph 4 to except land that is used for waste disposal. We have heard enough about waste processing activities. A real need may be addressed in applications for restrictions or closures. We certainly do not want to create a framework that might promote the dumping of rubbish on open country in order to except the land from the right of access.

Finally, amendment No. 68 would amend paragraph 10 to assign the same meaning to the word ``use'', which appears in paragraphs 3 to 7, as it has in the Town and Country Planning Act 1990. The definition of use in the 1990 Act is special. It makes a clear distinction, for the purposes of that Act, between development by way of building operations and development by way of

    material change in the use

of buildings or land. Material change of use often requires planning permission. The 1990 Act makes it clear that in that context ``use'' does not constitute building or other operations. There is no need to make separate provision for the purposes of excepted land. If we made such provision, it might mean that buildings on land used as a golf course or on land used for surface mining would not qualify as excepted land.

For the reasons that I have outlined, the amendments introduce unnecessary complexities to schedule 1. I ask the Opposition to withdraw amendment No. 47.
