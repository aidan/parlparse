I shall briefly explain the amendment and say why I should like the Committee to take it on board. The Government, in their press releases publicising it, the Bill, acknowledged that on certain issues it was difficult to draw the line, so they made the scope of the Bill as wide as possible. However, it should be recognised that the Bill may be drawn so wide that it could include people who should not be covered.

Subsection (2) sets out three circumstances in which a defence can be mounted. If we analyse the reasons why the Government have included those defences, we see that their motives were to exclude people who, for the sake of natural justice, should not be included in the provisions.

I am sure that all members of the Committee accept that any relationships that might fall within the wide ambit of the Bill should be condemned. Such activities were, at best, errors of judgment and, at worst, malicious exploitation for which there must be come-back on the perpetrator.

The review group examined the existing professional codes of conduct. It recognised that some were not of the standard of the best and work is now being undertaken urgently to improve them. the Bill is extending the criminal law to acts that were previously considered to be covered satisfactorily by professional codes, but are nevertheless to be condemned in almost all circumstances. In 1981, the policy advisory Committee of the Home Office considered whether the criminal law should be so extended and reported, but it did not consider that to be appropriate.

The Government drafted the Bill because of the outrageous occurrences that have taken place, particularly in children's homes, and because of the unease that is felt about exploitation. Nevertheless, it is important to recognise that, when drafting wide-ranging legislation, attention must be paid to possible defences. The provisions in subsection (2) are reasonable. The first states that it would be a defence for A, the accused, to prove that, at the time of sexual intercourse or other sexual activity he did not know and could not have been expected to know that B was under 18. We see that sort of defence elsewhere, including homosexual offences in Scotland, which have a statutory defence of ignorance of age.

Subsection (2)(b) would allow a similar defence if someone was ignorant of the fact that the person with whom he was accused of committing that offence was in a position of trust. Most relevant to this discussion, paragraph (c) allows an exemption or a defence if A was lawfully married to B at the time of the offence. That is a reasonable defence; indeed, it is difficult to see how a court could find such an offender guilty. It is possible that even professional codes might not cover such a situation.

The Government will be aware, however, that only those who are lawfully married can use that defence. The Bill clearly states that the two parties must have been married at the time of the offence; nevertheless, that defence is possible only for those who are, to coin a phrase marriageable. The purpose of the amendment is to examine whether the reasons for that exemption—other than the ceremony of marriage itself—can be extended to include those who are not married.

Only those in a heterosexual relationship can be lawfully married. Homosexuals cannot be married. However, under the marriage provisions of the Children Act 1989, children of 16 or 17 can be married if they have the permission of a parent or guardian. It is from that Act that I draw the provisions of the amendment.

This is not the time or place for an in-depth discussion of what discrimination exists under other Acts against those who cannot be married, particularly those, of any age, who are in committed homosexual relationships. Those wider issues may not be a matter for this Committee, but it should be noted that the matter has been debated in the House. Indeed, on Second Reading, when I expressed my concern about the lack of an extra defence for those who cannot lawfully marry, the hon. Member for Bassetlaw (Mr. Ashton) indicated that he saw no problem—in general terms; I do not wish to ascribe to the hon. Gentleman views that he may not hold—with extending such a provision.

If one accepts that the defence of being lawfully married provided in subsection (2)(c) can apply only to heterosexuals, albeit those who have the permission of a parent or guardian, one must therefore accept that it discriminates against people who have the same level of commitment to each other as a married couple and almost entirely the same relationship but who, whether or not they wish to get married, may not be married.

It is clearly reasonable for the amendment to ensure that the parent's or guardian's consent is given, because it was considered necessary under the Children Act 1989 and earlier legislation to protect 16 and 17-year-olds from marriages that might not be in their best interests. For that reason, I do not propose a wide defence for people in a committed relationship, because that would be difficult to define; I propose a narrow one.

The conditions to be met that would give such people the equivalent of the lawful marriage defence written in subsection (2)(c), include the consent of the parent or guardian, which is required by 16 and 17-year-olds seeking to be legally married. It is not unreasonable to include a provision that would allow them to be exempted—particularly those who are homosexual, who do not have the option of marriage.

The Bill criminalises an act that is legal in marriage. It therefore discriminates against people who, according to the rest of law, especially if clause 1 is passed, are capable of a consensual sexual relationship, but, because of marriage law, cannot marry.

Let us take the example of a 17-year-old gay person of either gender who has a relationship that may have been inappropriate when it started, but who is committed and who, if heterosexual, could marry with parental consent. That person and the partner should receive the same protection that the marriage exemption provides if they have obtained the parent or guardian's sanction. The reason why that sanction is important in marriage should be obvious. People who have the same level of commitment and parental or guardian consent should be considered to have a defence under the law.

If we do not want to discriminate, deliberately or indirectly against homosexual people, but want the same protection for 16 and 17-year-olds offered by the condition of the parent or guardian's consent for marriage, which implies consensual sexual activity, the amendment would not weaken the legislation but make it fairer. I cannot envisage any circumstances in which the parent or guardian's consent, as specified in the amendment, would not offer the same protection to 16 and 17-year-olds as the parent or guardian's consent that is implicit in the marriage defence, which is already included in the Bill. Anxieties have been expressed that the Bill as drafted discriminates against homosexual people. During the passage of the Crime and Disorder Act 1998, I understood that if the Government legislated on abuse of trust measures following the amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Bassetlaw (Mr. Ashton), they would do so in a non-discriminatory way.

I understand that the Government may not really want to exempt relationships sanctioned by parent or guardian, whether marriage or otherwise, and have been forced into including subsection 2(c) by the need to make the Bill compatible with the European convention on human rights. However, I ask the Committee briefly to examine those motives. The point of our commitment to the European convention on human rights is to recognise human rights, not to recognise that, like it or not, we have to comply. In considering the incorporation of the European convention on human rights and discussing how applicable it was to British law, I understood that the Government believed that those rights were worth safeguarding in British law. I understood that we would adopt a positive approach to compatibility with the European convention on human rights because we recognised those rights, not because we felt, without must enthusiasm, that we had to amend legislation to ensure that we complied. I would worry if the Government told us that subsection 2(c) was intended to achieve compatibility with the European convention on human rights, but that they would not include it if they had a choice other than mere compatibility with the convention.

If marriage and, therefore, consensual sexual relationships, are legal for 16 and 17-year-olds with the parent or guardian's consent, the human right to marry is worth writing in statute. The right to marry and have a consensual sexual relationship in that marriage is a human rights matter, especially under articles 8 and 14 of the European convention on human rights. The Government should therefore recognise, as a human right, a defence for people who cannot marry but whose parents or guardians consent to a relationship that is equivalent to marriage. It is hard to understand why people should be exempted from the remit of criminal law on the basis of a ceremony. That exemption is really made on the basis that parents and guardians are responsible people charged with overseeing the best interests of 16 and 17-year-olds, and have approved a marriage and, by implication, a relationship.

What concerns do the Government have in opposing the amendment? What do they object to in parents and guardians approving a relationship? What gives the Government the right to say that they know better than parents and guardians, while—laudably—impressing on parents and guardians the need for them to look after the interests of young people within the context of a family? It is anomalous for the Government to say that parents have the right to sanction marriage, with the long-term commitment that that involves, while bringing into criminal law relationships that have also been sanctioned by parents and guardians.

I return to my original point: no one suggests that relationships where one of the parties is put in a position of trust to the other, when or after the relationship starts, are to be welcomed. Indeed, there is a strong argument for the Government to consider amending the Marriage Act 1949 to ensure that the giving of parental and guardian consent to marriage cannot apply where a position of trust is involved. That would also remove any discrimination and some aspects of the lawful marriage defence in paragraph (c). The Government have to explain why they are introducing legislation that can be seen as discriminatory—I acknowledge that its purpose may not be to discriminate—and why they seek to intervene by means of the criminal law in relationships that have, perhaps reluctantly, been approved by parents or guardians.

I hope that the Government accept good notice of my intention to table an amendment on the matter on Report to ensure that we can have a full discussion on it. I would like to know how the Government can prevent the clause appearing discriminatory and how they can overrule the views of parents and guardians by using the full force of criminal law in difficult situations.
