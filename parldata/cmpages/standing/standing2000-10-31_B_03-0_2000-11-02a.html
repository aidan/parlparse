Mr. Mitchell: The amendments would widen the interests that the nominee has to take into account and open up information about the decisions taken throughout the process. It is important that such a process is subjected to accountability and that as much information is published as possible because hole-in-the-corner, secretive arrangements are likely to be damaging and less likely to be responsible arrangements that would protect the interests of all those involved.

Amendment No. 65 would introduce consistency into the Bill. Paragraph 26 of the schedule gives some rights to parties other than only the creditors, but paragraph 29, which is to operate in light of paragraph 26, does not give any rights to the directors and members of company to attend the meeting called by the nominee. Arrangements can be made that can affect the whole operation, yet such people do not have a right to attend the meetings. In the absence of the ability to know what is going on, members and directors are bound to have little opportunity to become aware of the consequences of the nominee's actions and their effect on the future of the company. At the very least, members of the company and its directors need to have the right to attend the meetings, so that they know what is going on. The amendment would further their rights, conceded under paragraph 26. Such rights make common sense. Such people have a vital interest in the future of the company's operation and in decisions taken by the nominee.

Amendment No. 66 is simply an information requirement because the Bill does not require the nominee to keep proper records of the meetings, which is again a deficiency in the Bill. If proper records are not kept, what accountability can there be? The schedule does not require the minutes to be circulated nor does it require the nominee to file with the registrar the decisions that have been made. Why not? People in the company need such information, as do creditors, not all of whom will attend the meetings. Stakeholders need it. After all, it concerns their future. Why is there not a requirement to keep records and to circulate them?

The result of the moratorium will inevitably mean some loss of public revenue—perhaps income tax, value added tax or corporation tax to the Revenue or to Customs and Excise. It might—indeed, if a slimming down follows, it will inevitably—lead to a loss of jobs and, therefore, to burdens on social security. It may result in arrangements that have consequences for current and future creditors of the company, who will want to know about the difficulties that the company has experienced and will want an explanation of what has been going on.

It is important that public information is available about the arrangements discussed and arrived at in meetings called by the nominee. Labour Members believe in open government; I hope that the Freedom of Information Bill will be made more liberal when it returns to the House of Commons. Such business affairs need to be open to prevent hole-in-the-corner arrangements from being arrived at in an atmosphere of secrecy with no requirement to publish. Such arrangements might be corrupt, although I believe that my hon. Friend the Minister has provided enough safeguards. The arrangements certainly would be self-interested and damaging to the interests of one section, whether it be employees or other creditors. The provisions more or less speak for themselves. In proposing them, I am asking for more light.

Amendment No. 67 would impose a requirement that the moratorium committee

    have regard for the interests of creditors, directors, members of the company, or any other person affected by the moratorium

to ensure that it acts for the benefit of all those affected by the moratorium. I have been justifiably critical of banks this morning. They will inevitably dominate proceedings in most instances, although not all. They are very powerful. They will usually be the major creditor and, therefore, a dominant influence on proceedings and nominees. Two powerful interests will be involved—the banks especially, but the nominee, too. There is always a danger that privileged parties to such arrangements will secure the outcomes that are most beneficial to themselves—the banks or, in terms of fees and arrangements, the nominee. The banks and the insolvency practitioner both stand to gain and, if they do—and if the process is slanted in their favour—by the simple fact of dominance it follows that, without a check in the Bill, and as night follows day, the other stakeholders will lose out. The amendment would prevent that by asking the moratorium committee to ensure that the interests of other stakeholders are safeguarded. Nothing could be simpler. I hope that when my hon. Friend the Minister rises to concede those powerful points he will take into account the need for openness and for protection of the other interests involved.

Amendment No. 65 is unnecessary. Once the Bill is enacted, we intend to provide in the insolvency rules a provision similar to that contained in rule 1.16 of the Insolvency Rules 1986 and rule 1.15 of the Insolvency (Scotland) Rules 1985. They provide that the convenor of the creditors' meeting should give notice to the directors of the company to attend that meeting. Additionally, if a director, employee or member of a company is also a creditor, the Bill already provides that they should be invited to the meeting. We do not think that more is needed, as the purpose of the creditors' meeting is for the company's creditors to take particular decisions. Paragraph 29 of schedule A1 obliges the nominee to summon to the creditors' meeting creditors of whose claim he is aware. That is a practical duty. Without summoning those

    of whose claim the nominee is aware,

he would have the potentially fruitless and expensive task of trying to establish whether there were other creditors of whom he was not aware. It is not clear whether the requirements would apply to the classes of people in the amendment, or merely to

    any other person known to the nominee.

Rule 1.24 of the Insolvency Rules 1986 and rule 1.17 of the Insolvency (Scotland) Rules 1985 currently make similar provision to that contained in amendment No. 66. For example, the chairman of the meetings is charged with the duty of preparing a report stating, among other things, the resolutions taken at a meeting and the decision reached. The report must also include a list of creditors and of company members who attend the meetings, and must be filed in court. If the company voluntary arrangement is approved, the report must be filed with the Registrar of Companies. It must also be sent to everyone who is given notice of the meetings. Those rules apply in relation to meetings at which a company voluntary arrangement is sought without the benefit of a moratorium. We will make similar provision in our revision of those rules, which will occur once the Bill has been enacted.

On amendment No. 67, a moratorium committee may be formed under paragraph 35 of schedule A1 when the company and its creditors have decided to extend the moratorium. It is important to note that the formation of a moratorium committee is not obligatory. It may be decided at the meetings that it is useful and helpful to establish a representative committee. However, we do not consider it necessary to prescribe its role and power in the Bill. Its functions should be a matter for the meetings to determine, in accordance with what the Bill provides. Obviously, such a committee will represent the interests of creditors and members of the company. We never intended to require the committee to represent the interests of directors or any other persons affected by the moratorium. We see no reason why it should do so. Indeed, such a practice could generate inappropriate conflicts. The only responsibilities placed on the moratorium committee by the Bill are to consider whether to approve any disposal of property or any payment by the company under paragraphs 18 and 19 respectively of schedule A1.

No such committee would have the power to override the Bill or the decisions or acts of the nominee or directors. I am glad that my hon. Friend the Member for Great Grimsby asked about the wider interest, which is very important. There is nothing to stop such a committee having regard to wider interests, but we feel that it would be inappropriate to impose an obligation to do so. I urge my hon. Friend to withdraw the amendment.

Dr. Howells: We must not forget the 99p.

As the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire said, the provision appears elsewhere in insolvency legislation, and it works well. It is supplementary to other general criminal offences of dishonesty, including, for example, as the hon. Member for North Wiltshire (Mr. Gray) said, theft.

If theft has taken place, the person involved can, as the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon suggested, be prosecuted for theft, no matter how small the amount involved may be. Several hon. Members asked why concealing or fraudulently removing part of the company's property worth less than £500 should not constitute an offence. The two offences to which the limit will apply are specific offences that apply only in the event of insolvency—in this case during the 12 months immediately before the moratorium commences and during the moratorium itself. If, however, misconduct that could amount to theft has taken place or another type of offence has been committed, that could be considered for prosecution as such and no minimum financial limit prescribed in the Bill would apply.

Similar offences exist elsewhere in the Insolvency Act 1986. For example, section 206 deals with offences in relation to companies that are wound up, and section 354 deals with bankruptcies. Both have a £500 minimum limit. In addition, such offences have had a minimum limit from at least as far back as the Bankruptcy Act 1914, as the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire said, when the limit was £10.

If we discovered that we could not prosecute people for those two offences in cases in which we thought we should be able to do so, we would review the £500 limit. Paragraph 10 of schedule 1 gives the Secretary of State the power to vary the minimum value of property for the purpose of those offences, and we would have no hesitation in reviewing that level if we felt it appropriate to do so. However, experience of sections 206 and 354 suggests that £500 is the right cut-off point, and it therefore seems perfectly reasonable to assume that it would be appropriate in this case, too.
