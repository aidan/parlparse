Indeed, the Economic Secretary and her colleagues should want to reconsider how best to go about the process of turning the Bill back into what it ought to have been.

The Bill's main problem is not of essence, but of accident. In essence, as the Economic Secretary said, its direction commands all-party support, as well as that of the PAC and most commentators that consider such matters. Given that it is 600 years—if my history is correct—since the invention of the normal accounting procedures that are used in the private sector, it is remarkable that we are only now debating whether they should be introduced in the public sector. It is clear, even to those of us who think of ourselves as part of the forces of conservatism, that change is overdue.

The Bill fails adequately to stipulate how definitions are to be reached. Accounts are of little value without accounting standards. There should be some means, external to the Government or the Treasury, of setting standards against which audit can be conducted. Many of the amendments that have been tabled by Conservative and Liberal Democrat Members—I was about to say our Liberal Democrat colleagues, having forgotten that they are actually colleagues of the Government—attempt to remedy that, and I am sure that we will have a long and fruitful debate about those mechanisms.

I enter the prayer that, after the Committee's proceedings have concluded, and perhaps following proceedings in another place, the Economic Secretary and her colleagues will find some means—without having to abase themselves or admit to any error—of providing an independent source of standard-setting, as Conservative and Liberal Democrat Members wish. It is not in the long-term interests of any Government—still less of democracy—to have a magnificent new accounting system that is an empty box because the standards against which accounting is conducted can be adjusted without limit and by administrative fiat. Although I am sure that our debate will be vigorous, I hope that it will not be a party political dogfight, but rather an opportunity for the Economic Secretary to persuade her officials to reconsider the matter.

As members of the Committee, we are all elected politicians, of whatever party, with a vested interest in clarity, accountability and democracy. Another set of people—civil servants—are properly employed doing a magnificent job, trying to run public services and especially the Departments of State. I am the last person to object to civil servants—my wife is one—however, the interests of officials cannot be ignored, as public choice theory rightly tells us. Officialdom as a whole has no interest in maximising the exposure of its activities to independent verification, but rather in minimising it. That is the experience of government over many centuries.

The purpose of this Committee and of Parliament should be to ensure that the innate tendency of any bureaucracy to protect itself and its own discretion is limited in such a way that the public at large, and Parliament in particular, can inspect what is going on, know what they are seeing and take action if they do not like it. Although I recognise that the Economic Secretary is subject to the siren voices once she gets back to the Treasury, I hope that my colleagues and I can persuade her that we are on the same side in this argument. It is as much in her interests as a Minister, a Government representative and a Member of Parliament, as it is in our interests and those of our electors, to ensure proper definitions. That will be a major issue for discussion, especially during our debates on clause 5.

There is another agenda, on which I do not want to dwell but which it is important to recognise. It is an agenda not of the Opposition, nor of the Liberal Democrats, although I think that I speak for the Liberal Democrats as well as for the official Opposition in saying that it is an agenda of the Public Accounts Committee.

Mr. Edward Davey (Kingston and Surbiton): The Liberal Democrat Opposition.

Mr. Letwin: The Liberal Democrat quasi-Opposition.

The agenda of which I speak is sponsored by the Public Accounts Committee, and we are deeply sympathetic towards it. It is not about definitions or standards, but about enforcement, although those concepts go hand in hand. Without proper independent definitions and standards, the enforcement of the resultant changing and fluid item would have little meaning. Moreover, there is little point in proper definitions and standards unless they can be enforced. That would involve the kind of audit that the National Audit Office and the Comptroller and Auditor General have been conducting for many years, and encompassing within it the whole scope of government.

The Committee benefited yesterday from a valuable briefing by the National Audit Office, which brought home to us how multifarious and messy government has become in recent years, and how many aspects of government are now conducted by bodies that will, if this Bill becomes law, fall outside the scope of audit by the NAO. I look forward to intense debates on several clauses, starting with clauses 4 and 5, concerning the extent to which the Comptroller and Auditor General should be allowed to become involved in those aspects of government that are not straightforwardly departmental, and how far he should have open and unfettered access throughout government. That is a matter of grave concern, and a case in which all Members of the Committee stand on one side of the barricades, while others stand on the other. This is not a case of senior members of the bureaucracy trying to protect themselves from accusations of impropriety. Permanent Secretaries and senior officials throughout Whitehall share the common aim of seeking to stamp out impropriety.

It is not clear to us that everything that needs to be done to permit resource budgeting to work properly has been done; however, I do not suggest that the opposite is so. The desire to move towards resource budgeting is a joint endeavour. We recognise how arduous an undertaking that will be, and can dimly intimate the huge complexities that will arise when the Department of Health and the Ministry of Defence, for example, begin to undertake it. The process may involve seismic shifts in the way in which British parliamentary democracy works. We appreciate that common concerns are involved in this vastly complex matter. We do not understand exactly what the Bill will permit or whether all that needs to be done to permit resource budgeting has been done. I hope that we shall be able to discuss that rather than debate it because from that discussion may come Government amendments to facilitate resource budgeting.

Finally, I speak for all my colleagues when I say that our approach to the Bill will be neither to delay it unnecessarily, nor to hasten it unnecessarily. Clause 4 deals with payments made in error into the consolidated fund. The amendment sets out to correct two aspects of a provision in the Bill that the PAC believes would weaken control over public money. In order to explain the need for the amendments, I need to set out the background and what I understand to be the intention of clause 4. As I said, it is fairly technical.

A fundamental principle of the system of parliamentary control over Government expenditure is that there should be a single fund into which general taxation is paid, and from which money approved by Parliament for Government to spend is passed to Departments. That fund—the consolidated fund—was set up in 1787 as, to quote the legislation,

    ``one Fund into which shall flow every stream of public revenue and from which shall come the supply for every service.''

However, Departments also generate income of their own—from fees and charges for services provided, such as driving licences. That income is termed appropriation in aid, as it can be used in support of a Department's spending. However, when Departments receive money additional to their parliamentary grant in that way, there are strict rules about what they may do with it. They may keep and use it only if it is an appropriation in aid authorised by Parliament through that year's Consolidated Fund (Appropriation) Act. That was the point of my comment on the Opposition's proposed amendment to clause 1. The receipt must satisfy the description of the appropriation in aid and its value must be within a specified limit. Any receipt that is not an appropriation in aid and not authorised by Parliament for use by the Government must be paid into the Consolidated Fund. Such income is known as a Consolidated Fund extra receipt.

Clause 4 deals with the situation in which a Department receives money that it thinks it is not authorised to keep and pays it into the Consolidated Fund, but then discovers that it was allowed to retain it. At present, such mistakes are corrected through an administrative procedure set out in Government Accounting, the Treasury's manual for Departments about money management. Under that procedure, known as short surrender, a Department deducts the amount previously paid in error from the next payment that it is required to make into the Consolidated Fund.

For example, if a Department had made an erroneous payment of £100,000, when it next had to pay £1 million it would instead pay £900,000. The accounts are balanced in that way. Clause 4 provides for the £100,000 to be refunded to the Department before the next payment is due. That is the clause's primary aim. The subsequent payment of £1 million would then be paid in full, since the mistaken payment would already have been corrected.

I understand from the Government's explanatory notes that the purpose of clause 4 is to remove a negative impact of the inability of Departments to recover immediately moneys paid in error. The behaviour pattern that the clause attempts to correct is Departments' delaying the payment of Consolidated Fund extra receipts until they are certain that none of the money can be recovered. The Government hope that establishing the power to recover amounts paid in error will encourage quicker payment of extra receipts.
