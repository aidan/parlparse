Government Amendment No. 652 is designed to improve enforcement of section 34, and to address the famous Grimsell lane case to which my hon. Friend the Member for Brighton, Pavilion (Mr. Lepper) referred. Many people have complained that that case will encourage large numbers of motor bike riders, in particular, to believe that they may ride with impunity on bridleways and footpaths irrespective of whether there is any evidence that vehicular rights exist over them. That is because in that case the prosecution was obliged to prove beyond reasonable doubt that, over the way in question, which was shown on a definitive map as a bridleway, there were bridleway rights only. In other words, it had to prove the negative, that there were no vehicular rights. However, a careful reading of the judgment in the case reveals that the defendants produced substantial evidence of the existence of vehicular rights over the bridleway in question.

Government amendment No. 652, and the consequential amendment No. 653, will deter the abuse of ways shown on the definitive map as footpaths, bridleways or restricted byways. Amendment No. 652 will, for the purposes of a prosecution under section 34, create a presumption that, where a way is shown on a definitive map as a footpath, bridleway or restricted byway, it is to be treated as being a way of the type shown. For example, if it is shown as a footpath, it will be presumed to carry only a right of way on foot. However, the presumption will not be conclusive. It will be rebuttable by prima facie evidence to the contrary. For example, in the case of a restricted byway, a defendant will be able to replace the burden of proof on to the prosecution to show that it does not carry rights of way for mechanically propelled vehicles by producing prima facie evidence of such rights. If the defendant were unable to do that, there would be no requirement on the prosecution to prove that such rights did not exist. We have, therefore, transferred the liability-the onus of proof-on to the defendant, and removed the requirement for the prosecution to prove a negative. We believe that that will be effective.

Mr. Meacher: I face the daunting prospect of speaking to a massive set of proposals, but I shall try to do so. I apologise to Committee members for the size and range of subjects that are involved. I have been advised that the only way in which we could amend the schedules is by using the approach that we have adopted. I shall try to assist the Committee by ensuring that we debate each item separately, and I hope that the matter will not become too muddled. I shall do my best to disaggregate it, and to respond to the relevant points.

I want to introduce the short new clause 16, and the rather long new schedule 4, which replace, revise and expand on the current clauses 49, 50 and 51, and schedules 6 and 7. The changes do not affect clause 52. I shall give a brief opening statement on the rationale behind the Government amendments and new clauses, and perhaps we can then move on to a wider debate on each of the points.

The revisions and additions to the Bill contained in the new schedule perform several functions. First, they refine the process of applications for public path orders and incorporate the procedures ralating to the determination by the Secretary of State and the National Assembly for Wales of appeals against councils' refusal to make or confirm orders following such applications.

11.30 am

Secondly they give schools a right of application and appeal in relation to orders to stop up or divert rights of way in school grounds to protect pupils and staff from harm. Thirdly, they fulfil our commitment to enable rights of way to be diverted where necessary to protect sites of special scientific interest. For the first time, local authorities will have a power-albeit a limited one that we expect them to use only rarely-to divert ways carrying vehicular rights, because it would appear pointless to safeguard sensitive sites from walkers, horse-riders and cyclists while disregarding the potential effect of vehicles.

It made sense to amalgamate the provisions in one clause and one schedule because they amend the same parent statute-the Highways Act 1980. The procedures for making the different, new categories of public path orders follow the order-making process that has been tried and tested by local authorities. In all cases, there will be an opportunity for anyone with an interest to object to orders affecting rights of way, and for objections to receive proper consideration in a hearing or public inquiry.

We have sought to place sensible restrictions on who may apply for a public path diversion or extinguishment order. In our consultation paper ``Improving Rights of Way in England and Wales'' we explained that the reason for introducing such a measure was to overcome the difficulties experienced by land managers in obtaining diversions in the interest of efficient management of their business, even when there was no apparent detriment to the public interest. We decided that the right of application should be restricted to those with an economic interest in working the land.

We have also thought carefully about the limits to be placed on appeals. For example, there is no right of appeal against a council's refusal to make an order, if the applicant declines to enter into an agreement to pay the costs of the diversion or closure, or when a council has no power to make an order because another council's consent is required and is not forthcoming. That is consistent with existing legislation. When orders are needed to divert or close rights of way for reasons of school security, the school proprietor will be able to apply and to appeal against a council's refusal to make an order. That applies to all schools in the state and private sector.

The new powers for special extinguishment and diversion orders-for crime prevention in designated urban areas and in school grounds-and SSSI diversion orders require the order-making authority to take into account other ways of addressing the problem that the order would be expected to alleviate. The police authorities must be consulted for order in respect of urban crime prevention and to protect the safety of school staff and pupils. Account must also be taken of the extent to which orders would be made consistent with a local authority's statutory crime prevention strategy. Local highway authorities will only be allowed to make site of special scientific interest diversion orders on application from English Nature or the Countryside Council for Wales. That will ensure that a full assessment of the risk to the SSSI is made first.

Authorities must also consider whether a traffic regulation order would solve the problem and whether it would be more convenient for the public. The powers apply only to rights of way that are in, part of, next to or near a SSSI. The Secretary of State and the National Assembly for Wales will have reserve powers to make orders, irrespective of whether English Nature or the Countryside Council for Wales has made an application.

I have been speaking fairly generally, but I hope that I have spelt out the structural background to the amendment and the new clauses. I am sure that hon. Members will now want to debate the issues in more detail.
