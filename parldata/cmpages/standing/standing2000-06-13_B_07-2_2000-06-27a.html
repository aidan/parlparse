On resuming-

Mr. Ingram: Some of the amendments in this group have not been addressed. I shall deal with amendments Nos. 86, 237, 238 and 239 and new clauses 6 and 7. The hon. Member for Montgomeryshire (Mr. Öpik) spoke to amendment No. 86 and the new clauses. The purpose of the other amendments is to remove the provisions that allow for qualifying candidates to form what is called a merit pool from which recruits will be selected. In effect, that would jettison the so-called 50:50 recruitment arrangement, as it would do away with the merit principle altogether. The Government consider it vital to retain that principle in selecting qualified candidates. The hon. Member for Montgomeryshire clearly considers it wrong and unnecessary and has a preference for what could best be described as a programme of affirmative action to encourage under-represented groups to apply to join the police service. Those measures are set out in new clauses 6 and 7.

The parties to the Good Friday agreement consider it imperative that a police service, if it is to be fully effective, should be representative of the community that it polices. There is nothing new in that. The RUC has for many years had a large number of affirmative action initiatives to tackle the lack of recruitment from nationalist communities. However, as things stand, the RUC is far from representative in terms of community background. There is no doubt that other groups, such as women, also suffer from under-representation, but the Catholic-Protestant imbalance was identified as, to quote paragraph 14.4 of the Patten report,

    the most striking problem in the composition of the RUC.

Patten's recommendations on how to tackle the problem are unequivocal and the Government have sought to implement them. We are aware and have acknowledged, as did Patten, that there are many complex reasons for low Roman Catholic recruitment. The precursor to a new, stable beginning is an agreed political form and a more stable security situation. Within that context, the Government believe that we must make every possible effort to redress the imbalance in the RUC. I do not think that anything separates members of the Committee in terms of the desire to do so; it is how we set about doing so that is the problem.

Neither Patten nor the Government intend 50:50 recruitment to represent an alternative to affirmative action. The report clearly states in paragraph 15.2 that

    The key to making that police service representative of those communities-indeed the key to the successful implementation of nearly everything in this report-is that the leaders of communities now actively encourage their young people to join the police.

The report made specific recommendations to that effect and others on similar themes, such as the need to target under-represented groups in recruitment advertising. The RUC has for many years implemented a wide range of affirmative action measures specifically aimed at increasing the recruitment of under-represented groups.

Mr. Ingram: I do not want to join in a party political knockabout. I appreciate the point that my hon. Friend made; perhaps we will have other opportunities to address the issue. He has clearly upset the hon. Member for Montgomeryshire who is, I know, a sensitive soul, which is why I have been so kind to him throughout our procedures-to ensure that he feels welcome. However, I will leave the sentiments of my hon. Friend as he expressed them.

I shall set out the background to our approach. I know that the Committee will accept that there is a fundamental issue involved. Only about 8 per cent. of the Catholic community is represented in the RUC, so there is a considerable way to go in trying to tackle the issue and, I hope, to find answers to it. There is no question that individual members of the nationalist and Catholic community in Northern Ireland are victimised and pressured when they seek to join the RUC. Many stories can be told of people having to visit their family in the dead of night, or being able to meet them only outside Northern Ireland. That is clearly a manifestation of opinions that any right-minded person would object to, but resolving it does not of itself answer all the questions-as Patten picked up. That issue goes back to our earlier debate about the prevailing cultural attitudes. The nationalist community's approach to the RUC-right or wrong-is not for us to judge, but we must recognise that it exists and seek ways of tackling it.

We would all hope that the measure would not be necessary, but based on his examination of events elsewhere-primarily in the United States, where there was a racial imbalance in some communities-Patten concluded that it took a considerable time to resolve. Very progressive action was needed by the authorities, but because of the in-built hostility and suspicion in communities it still took time to make progress.

The lesson that can be learned is that what we suggest will not of itself produce results unless other changes take place. The removal of the threat of intimidation is vital and it is important that those who speak for and represent the interests of the Catholic community give the message that its members should seek to join the police service. That would take on board some of the arguments made by the hon. Member for Montgomeryshire. If there was a tide of quality applicants to the RUC, people would be able to ask whether the legislative framework was necessary. Our judgment is that at present it is, but that does not negate the need for affirmative action as envisaged in new clauses 6 and 7. We need all the buttons to be pushed and switches to be flicked if we are to achieve the objectives.

The Police Federation has calculated that it would take 30 years to achieve a balance through such affirmative action. That is too long. We are aiming for a combined impact, to achieve a sharp boost in Catholic representation in the police service in the short term. Over time, such measures may become unnecessary- hence our intention to keep the recruitment procedure and the composition of the force under regular review.

Northern Ireland's Equality Commission has given its full backing to the concept of 50:50 recruitment against the background of the current political situation. The hon. Member for Montgomeryshire has constantly tried to specify the Equality Commission in the Bill as a body to be consulted. We consulted it, and it disagrees with him but agrees with the position adopted by the Government. I hope that he is therefore prepared to respect its opinion.

On amendment No. 11, I question the merit and the workability of applying the quota mechanism where such small numbers are involved. It would clearly have a negligible impact on overall composition and could in practical terms prove impossible to apply properly. More importantly, at such a low level, it virtually sets aside the merit principle.

For example, how could three-or five-vacancies be filled on anything approaching a 50:50 basis? Police support staff recruitment is generally not carried out on the basis of a large scale exercise to recruit pools of similarly qualified people. It is carried out geographically and covers a multitude of grades and skills. This diversity would be jeopardised by too rigid an application of the 50:50 recruitment principle. What if a finance director, a chief auditor, a personnel director and three personal secretaries are to be recruited? Personal secretaries are very influential, but could they sensibly be included with the heads of those services in terms of an aggregation approach? Would that serve Patten's aim? What if only Protestants apply for three scenes of crime photographers posts in Ballymena, or, later on, Catholics for IT support posts in Newry? When we discuss support services, we must recognise that we are dealing with small numbers of people and that to achieve a balance between them is difficult.

The other amendments have not been moved, and I am addressing the arguments that might have been advanced if hon. Members had been here in time to present them. They may not want to present those arguments after hearing mine.

The figure 10, in line 30 of the clause, is analogous to the figure specified in the Fair Employment and Treatment (Northern Ireland) Order 1998. Under article 48(2) of the measure, community background composition is not required of private sector employers. I accept that the two pieces of legislation are not exactly comparable. The Government have an open mind about that figure, if good arguments are presented for raising or lowering it, or for introducing flexibility. We have done our best to meet the requirements of the Patten report in that respect. Others have argued for a figure of 2; we have set it at 10, but the matter may be debated. However, we must bear in mind the principles that apply to private sector organisations in respect of community background composition. The Government are not adopting an unreasonable position in using the figure set in existing legislation. It is not a direct comparator, but it may be the best one around. Although I am inclined to do so, I hesitate to ask the Committee to resist the amendments, as we have not heard all the arguments.
