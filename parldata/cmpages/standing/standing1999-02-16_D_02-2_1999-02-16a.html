Clause 11

Appeals against decisions of Board

Ms Hewitt: We now come to five clauses dealing with the appeals process for national insurance contributions and related matters. It may help if I provide some background to clause 11.

When the Social Security Act 1998 became law, a new appeals tribunal was created to hear appeals on all decisions of the Secretary of State for Social Security in relation to social security matters. That was a substantial modernisation of the law and a simplification of the social security appeals process. We made a commitment at that time that we would look again at the approach to national insurance contributions in the light of the proposed transfer of the Contributions Agency to the Inland Revenue. After such consideration, we concluded that it would make much more sense for appeals on national insurance contributions, statutory sick pay and statutory maternity pay functions that were transferred to the Inland Revenue by the Bill to be heard by the tax appeal commissioners. The main reason for that decision was that the same issue will often arise in a tax case and a contributions case. A common examples arises from a dispute over employment status whether someone should be treated as employed or self-employed for tax and national insurance contributions purposes. Entitlement to statutory maternity pay and statutory sick pay is a similar case in point.

It is neither helpful nor efficient for the appellant to have to take his or her case to separate tribunals, one of which deals with social security and the other with tax, when the same matter and the same set of circumstances have to be judged. It is costly and worrying to the appellant, and it is also costly to employers, whose liability, especially to national insurance contributions, hinges on the appeal decision. The proposals under clause 11 and subsequent clauses ensure that appeals on contributions matters and on statutory maternity pay and statutory sick pay will be heard by the tax appeal commissioners. That decision has been welcomed by members of the business community, who clearly believe that enabling them and their employees to bring cases directly to the tax appeal commissioners, instead of to two separate bodies, will save them time and money. I hope that allays any fears about the clause.
