The essence of the amendments that we tabled and our approach to them is our concern, already expressed, that in formulating the concept of a national minimum wage, which is tied to or expressed as an hourly rate, for hours worked in a pay reference period, there are bound to be difficulties with other kinds of remuneration which are not so tied. The Government's exercise is to second-guess a series of market arrangements. In the past, it has been a matter for private bargain between employer and employee how pay and remuneration has been structured. They now have at least to flow through a gateway which is called an hourly rate for hours worked in a particular pay reference period. Given that parts of the remuneration--by definition, if it is piece work--are not tied to an hourly rate but to some measure of performance, some means must be found for fitting different cases to that template. I repeat that the first amendment is about piece work; others encompass a wider bonus or payment by result system, and gratuities.

I hope that the Minister will consider our comments carefully because it will be crucial to get these matters right in the regulations if, to use her colleague's phrase, the concept is not to be discredited by some anomaly or problem. The first problem is that some employees naturally work harder than others. Even when employees are equally assiduous, some are more effective than others. I suspect that if we had a needle-threading competition, however hard I tried, with the assistance of my hon. Friend the Member for Solihull (Mr. Taylor), I probably would not do quite as well as the Minister. It might of course depend on whether I was wearing my glasses. I realised that the onset of at least middle age, if not senility, had taken place when I first had to thread a fish hook and found it easier to do so without than with them. We all have different strengths and abilities. There are no doubt some things that I can do better than the hon. Lady. I might even say, modestly, that I can draft amendments better.

In similar work conditions, even if one assumes a similar degree of motivation and effort, some people will be better than others at carrying out tasks, especially repetitive tasks. That may be fine for employers. It may also be fine for employees who may get a better rate of pay and, assuming that the quality is maintained, contribute more to value added than less productive colleagues. That is a natural difference. But what about motivation? We all know that within a given labour force of more than one or two there will be considerable variations in how people feel about their work. That may depend on whether they have had a row with their boyfriend, whether they do not like their boss, whether they have read something that they did not like in the newspapers about what the Government had done to them or whether, indeed, they thought that the Government were taxing them so much that they had to work harder. Those considerations apply to people who are paid by the piece. Some will try harder, others will not: I bet that the former will resent the latter freeloading or getting the same pay for different results or attainments.

You, Mr. Atkinson, will remember the parable of the labourers in the vineyard. When the people who had worked an hour were paid the same money as those who had worked through the heat of the day, the latter were not too pleased. There is a long-standing perception that people work in different ways.

To summarise, the difficulty is of the Government's own making. They have prescribed against the grain of the market for a national minimum wage and they will set that by reference to an hourly rate for hours worked within a pay reference period which allows for salaries to be translated back into an hourly rate. That is still the most typical type of employment and will probably continue to be so.

However, they have failed to take account not just of piece-work systems--the subject of the lead amendment--or of payments by results more generally, but of the huge range of reasonable bargains that are struck within the free economic system. They will run up against generic differences in translating piece work into an hourly rate and, as they have acknowledged, they will have to think of ways of translating average performance, rather than individual performance, into an hourly rate equivalent.

I suggested that there may be times when the performance over time either of the group of employees or of the individual employee varies for entirely reasonable and understandable reasons. It would be foolish if the Government were to draft their regulations without those considerations in mind. They would score an own goal and risk discrediting the national minimum wage that they want.

As I said, the amendments are designed not to subvert the Bill or to create an artificial advantage but to probe the issues.
