Mr. Ross: Clause 6 specifies exceptions to its provisions, such as funeral processions. If such exceptions are to be made we should consider in more detail the categories of organisations whose processions and shows are not contentious. Those should fall outside the ambit of the Bill. No doubt the Minister will think of spurious reasons for not accepting that sensible view.

Amendment No. 40 would simply remove the reference to funeral processions from the clause. That is not to imply that we want the rigour of the law to be applied to funeral processions, although on the face of it that would be the effect. It cannot very well be judged when a funeral procession will take place. Certainly 28 days' notice cannot be given. We do not know who will be the unfortunate focus of such a procession in the next few months or weeks. That is, thank God, hidden from us.

Amendment No. 41 specifies certain classes and descriptions of procession that should be exempted, including funeral processions and processions to and from religious services--and no doubt the hon. Member for South Down could tell the Committee whether Corpus Christi processions such as those that take place in the Irish Republic are ever held in Northern Ireland. Other processions, such as fund-raising processions should fall outside the ambit of the Bill.

In addition to funeral processions, for which no one would dream of demanding applications for permission, the amendment lists the processions that should not be covered by the law. I have already mentioned

    ``a procession to and from a religious service''.

There are quite a few of those.

The next category relates to

    ``a procession by a band in a rural area where the purpose is for the collection of funds for the band.''

Many processions in Northern Ireland are organised by Unionist, Protestant and Orange bands and by nationalist bands, such as those of the Ancient Order of Hibernians, to go to the dwellings of their supporters where they play a few tunes and collect a few pounds. They get into cars and move to the next area. Under current law, because the bands move from one house to another, their efforts are caught by the general description of a procession. That is unnecessary. Such bands visit houses in areas where they are welcome and they cause no problems. I do not see why they should be lumbered with the weight of the law.

The next categories include processions organised by youth organisations connected with the churches and uniformed children's bodies such as the Girls Brigade, the Boys Brigade and the Scout movements. I say ``Scout movements'', because the Catholic Scout movement operates in Northern Ireland as elsewhere. Similar groups include the Girl Guides and the Church Lads Brigade, which is an Anglican body and does not have many groups in Northern Ireland. However, there are many Boys Brigade, Girls Brigade, Scout and Girl Guides groups in Northern Ireland.

Many of those groups are attached to churches, and the Boys Brigade, in particular, has a strong religious ethos. Such groups hold cycles of religious services every year. The Minister cannot seriously contemplate placing such organisations under the heavy burdens outlined in the Bill. A simpler procedure should be enough. All they should have to do is tell a police sergeant or a local police station of their plans.

The next group listed is

    ``a procession organised by a trade union''.

Some of them may be planned a considerable time ahead, but many trade union demonstrations and processions take place during a strike or as a protest against closures or job losses. I have never heard of a trade union holding a procession to celebrate jobs coming to an area, but they might not come to us often enough for that to happen. However, I am sure that the Committee understands the general picture.

Processions are also organised by the Royal British Legion. An atrocious attempt was made by a republican-IRA front organisation in Bellaghy to stop a memorial procession held there last autumn by the Royal British Legion. I include in the amendment

    ``a procession organised for the purpose of fundraising for a charitable purpose''.

There, I am thinking of the band procession that takes place in Garvagh in my constituency each year. It has always raised large sums of money for cancer research, a most worthy cause. There is no need to treat such a procession as potentially contentious. Everyone is welcome to the procession, and everyone comes. They are relieved of some of their hard-earned cash before they go away.

The amendment also refers to

    ``a procession organised on a cross community basis''.

I understand that such processions take place in some parts of the country. I do not see why they should be caught by the law. The same is true of

    ``a procession organised by civil authorities''.

We have lord mayor's shows and those organised by the chairmen of councils. Finally, I include

    ``a procession which is organised as part of a social function''.

Hon. Members will probably ask for examples. A church procession or a fancy dress procession could form part of a social function. Why should such events be caught by the Bill?

A large annual steam engine rally takes place in Northern Ireland. The proceedings end with a town procession of vintage steam engines.

Various vintage car rallies around the country entail a procession round the nearest town. A procession of vintage farm machinery and Clydesdale horses takes place in Garvagh every year and allows people to glimpse a bygone age. It is a popular tourist attraction.

As the Minister suggested this morning, a local football team might win a cup and want to celebrate with a procession. Although such a procession would be rowdier and more intoxicated than the other processions that I have mentioned, no one would want it to be caught by the measure. The list is endless. The category of processions that amendment No. 41 covers could be caught by the general language of the Bill.

Mr. Ross: The provisions have been available for many years and have never been used and I wonder why the clause has been included. There are all sorts of difficulties about what constitutes a band. We drew attention earlier to the possibility of a one-man band and ad hoc bands of people travelling along the street together but not part of a regular band. There is also the possibility of those in a band that was behaving in an unacceptable way changing their names and turning up week after week in different areas.

The fact that the provisions of the clause have been on the statute book for so long and have not been used clearly shows that the Government and the police recognise the grave difficulties in this area which are impossible to overcome. I would be grateful if the Minister could confirm or deny my views because we need to know what the position is and why this has been kept on the statute book when it serves no useful purpose.
