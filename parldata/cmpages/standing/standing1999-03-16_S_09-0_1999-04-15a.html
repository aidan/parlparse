Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

Mr. O'Brien: We need to speed up the system. The deportation system is manifestly slow and cumbersome and produces inequity. It needs reform. Those who have leave to remain that expires may apply to have it extended and can remain for the appeal provided that they make the initial application within the time limit.

The clause deals with those who have overstayed and who have not sought to apply to remain and are here unlawfully. Such people are not, in the main, much different from illegal immigrants in the sense that they have no right to be here.

Historically, once they have come to the notice of the authorities, many of them seek to use the deportation appeal process to delay removal.

Our aim is that persons who have no right to be here should be removed unless there are compassionate or other grounds for them to remain. We shall introduce immigration rules to allow such circumstances to be considered, but we believe that we should move quickly to remove people who should not be here.

Persons can avoid the provisions in the clause by making an in-time application for variation of their leave. They will then have an unrestricted right of appeal, in which they can rely on personal circumstances, no matter when they were last given leave. We are therefore dealing with a particular category of persons and we believe that the proposal is reasonable and fair.

If persons who have a decision made against them believe that some right of theirs has been infringed, they will be able to appeal under clause 47 and under the European convention; if they believe that there has been a breach of law, they will have the normal judicial review rights. We do not believe that we should produce a system that enables people to delay their removal from this country for ever by making a series of appeals.

Let us consider the effect of deportation in the circumstances that the clause outlines. Two issues are relevant to enforcement. First, we must ensure that enforcement proceedings are brought against those who are liable to them. Secondly, we should consider what happens after enforcement proceedings have been brought.

There is a difference between deportation and removal.

As the Committee knows from our earlier discussion, clause 6 will shift into the category of being liable to removal many people who would hitherto have been liable to deportation proceedings because of overstaying or breach of conditions, for example, by working when they had entered the country on condition that they would not work.

The consequences of deportation are more long term than those of removal. For example, a person who has been deported is prevented from re-entering the United Kingdom. That is important in view of the range of people against whom deportation proceedings may be brought. Those proceedings are not brought only against people who have breached control by overstaying, or those who have breached a condition, but against a range of other people who will continue to be deported under the new dispensation. They include people who have been recommended for deportation by the courts. That shows that deportation constitutes a serious step. Some people are recommended for deportation on the ground that their presence in this country is not conducive to the public good.

Amendment No. 80 deals with the longer term consequences of deportation. People who leave the United Kingdom with a deportation order cannot return while the order remains current. If they gain entry while the deportation order is still valid, they will have entered the country illegally. Therefore they can be arrested and removed promptly. The order has to be revoked before they can return. Every year, several appeals seek the revocation of deportation orders. A person who has been deported, must apply for the deportation order to be revoked. That places a significant hurdle in the way of those who try to return.

The hurdle does not exist in the case of removal proceedings, and would not apply to those who will be shifted into the removals category under clause 6. There are no formal procedures to restrict applications for return from those who have been removed as illegal entrants. Such people would have to satisfy the requirements of immigration law and rules.
