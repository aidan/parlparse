Political Parties, Elections and Referendums Bill

Clause 63

Special provision for Northern Ireland parties

Amendment proposed [27 January]: No. 46, in page 40, line 18, leave out 'a Northern Ireland party' and insert 'Northern Ireland parties'.--[Mr. Walter.]

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following amendments: No. 48, in page 40, line 19, after 'period', insert

    'not greater than 12 months'.

No. 49, in page 40, line 20, after 'section 48(2)', insert

    'to include citizens of the Republic of Ireland resident in the Republic (subject to compliance with the Republic's Electoral Act 1997) only'.

Mr. Robert Walter (North Dorset): Last Thursday, we had an extensive debate on the amendments to the clause, which deals with special provisions for Northern Ireland parties. I do not want to repeat my preamble, but we shall argue now and during the clause stand part debate that we are unhappy about making special provisions for Northern Ireland parties. In the light of the changed political circumstances in Northern Ireland, the Good Friday Agreement, the establishment of the Northern Ireland Assembly and the Northern Ireland Executive, and the markedly changed atmosphere in the north, we feel that such special provisions should not apply. However, the amendments suggest a number of compromises that would provide a way forward.

Although amendment No. 46 is a matter of semantics, the semantics are quite significant. It would replace the phrase "a Northern Ireland party" with "Northern Ireland parties", so that any special provisions that may apply in Northern Ireland would apply to all parties, rather than a particular party. As we said during the previous sitting, we fear that the clause might be seen to relate only to Sinn Fein, which is reputed to have received most of its funding from the United States.

Under amendment No. 48, any arrangement would not be specified by the Secretary of State, but time-limited to 12 months.

The most significant and interesting of our proposed compromises is one with which the Minister has some sympathy. As Neill and the Minister acknowledged, because some political parties span the border, difficulties might arise in the north of Ireland. Amendment No. 49 recognises that, and would limit the clause's provisions to citizens of the Irish Republic. The amendment would make it impossible for people from other parts of the world to make donations to Northern Ireland political parties. If the amendment were accepted, the clause would apply only to electors in the Irish Republic. The amendment is based on the principle that, if those electors were resident in the United Kingdom, they would not be regarded as aliens and would be entitled to vote in United Kingdom elections.

As Neill rightly pointed out, the Republic of Ireland is not a foreign country. Therefore, we are minded to suggest that the amendment is a way of addressing the dilemma in relation to the traditional funding of Northern Ireland parties without undermining the spirit of the Neill report or the basic thrust of the Bill, which is to outlaw foreign donations to United Kingdom political parties. I want to emphasise the United Kingdom aspect in that those elected to the United Kingdom Parliament serve and owe allegiance to the United Kingdom. Therefore, there should not be special provisions of the nature suggested by the clause for one particular part of the kingdom.

I am conscious that the Minister seems amenable to returning with amendments on Report. Perhaps those amendments will not be dissimilar to ours. I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Grieve: As my hon. Friend the Member for North Dorset (Mr. Walter) has just said, we have a responsibility to give the Bill detailed consideration. These two amendments offer another approach to the problem of funding in Northern Ireland. They address what seems to us to be a particular flaw in relation to this part of clause 63.

The problem with the way in which clause 63 operates is that it defines a Northern Ireland party as having one or more Members of the Northern Ireland Assembly or one or more Members of the House of Commons representing Northern Ireland constituencies. Although I have listened carefully to what the Minister said about other aspects of the clause and the attempt to ring-fence Northern Ireland politics, I confess that I still find this provision astonishing. Even if one leaves aside the unfairness that might be perpetrated in relation to United Kingdom parties organising on a United Kingdom-wide basis, the clause as drafted would effectively prevent any special provision being made for a Northern Ireland party that did not have Members of the Assembly or Members of the House of Commons. Therefore, the clause would effectively create sclerosis in Northern Ireland. For example, the idea behind the clause, as drafted, is that it should be possible, in Northern Ireland, for an established party to obtain funding from the Republic. If I understood correctly some of the comments that were made at the time of the Bill's introduction, it was even suggested that a party should be able to get funding from the United States of America, and that would trouble me even more. A party might want to set itself up in Northern Ireland on an agenda that appealed to many members of the Committee--one of non-sectarian, liberal democratic policies. I use liberal with a small "l", with due respect to the hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell). Why should such a party not be able to benefit from the same funding sources as a party dedicated to sectarian nastiness? A moment's thought makes one realise that that is nonsense. The Bill is therefore seriously flawed, even in terms of achieving what it seeks to achieve through clause 63, which, as the Minister already knows, does not entirely commend itself to me anyway.

The amendments address that issue, but go further. They also cover parties that may have a United Kingdom-wide operation but an accounting unit in Northern Ireland. My own party has such an organisation. Admittedly it is small, but there is a Conservative party in Northern Ireland. I have had the pleasure of addressing its annual dinner. It has put up candidates and on at least one occasion, as I think has been said, it came close to winning a seat in Parliament. Therefore, it is by no means a negligible force. In saying that, I appreciate that the party in government here, the Labour party, does not organise in Northern Ireland. I know that some of the Minister's colleagues regard that as being a great mistake; others may take a different view. However, it is wrong to exclude such groups.

If accounting units organise in Northern Ireland, I confess that I find it difficult to justify their being able to get funding from the Republic or the United States, but being deprived of the opportunity to transfer the funds, if they wish, to support wider party aims in Great Britain. However, I leave that to one side for a moment, because the amendments are designed to ensure that funding can be obtained by such accounting units. That essential change needs to be made to the clause.

Clearly these are probing amendments. I suspect that in not too long we shall move on to a clause stand part debate, where we shall express disquiet about the provision. I detected that the Minister was likely to go back to the drawing board on the entire clause, so I tabled the amendments as ideas to prompt his advisers into what I hope will be more creative thinking than what appears in the Bill. I hope that the Minister can therefore understand the logic behind the amendments.

Behind that logic, as a general principle, is the question of being equitable between political parties. In the wider sense we believe that that can be done only by treating Northern Ireland parties in the same way as any other United Kingdom party, and vice versa. In the narrower sense it means that we must ensure that parties that organise in Northern Ireland are not disadvantaged just because they do not at present have Members of the Assembly or Members of Parliament. If the peace process goes forward, as we all hope it will, over the next decade there is likely to be a sea-change in Northern Ireland politics, because, if there is peace, the issues that people have to address are likely to be very different in 10 years' time from what they are today. That in itself should ring an alarm bell about the way in which the clause is drafted.
