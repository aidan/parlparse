Mr. Hughes: The three new clauses relate to matters which arose in the Macpherson report following the Lawrence inquiry; it is therefore entirely relevant that they are being taken together. The first new clause is self-explanatory, and relates specifically to one aspect of performance indicators. The Macpherson report recommended that the exact words used in the new clause should be included as ministerial policy for all police services. Chapter 47 of the report, in a section entitled, "Openness, accountability and the restoration of confidence", recommends:

    "The process of implementing, monitoring and assessing the Ministerial Priority should include Performance Indicators in relation to"--

the items set out in the new clause.

I will not expand at length on why it is important at this time in the history of the Met and at the beginning of the life of the MPA to set out such objectives. Self-evidently, the MPA needs to get off to a good start. It needs to command the confidence of all the communities in London. Many Committee members, more than most hon. Members in other parts of the country, represent mixed-race communities and are very conscious of the fact that one in five people in London is of black, Asian or other ethnic minority origin. In my constituency, one quarter of my electorate are black, Asian, Chinese or from other ethnic minorities. For them, one of the tests of the new authority will be how it will responds to the failures that were identified by the Lawrence report.

It was mentioned earlier that by the work that we have already done to incorporate amendments into existing police legislation, we have in fact already provided for some targets to be part of the Met's work. There are performance indicators in existing legislation; they are laid down as an obligation of police authorities. We talked earlier--in a more confrontational manner, I admit--about the staffing and funding of the Met. One test that we discussed was efficient and effective policing. Existing legislation lays down various indicators by which that is to be judged. Performance indicators are now accepted as a means of judging that.

As I understand it, there are no formally agreed police indicators. There are Audit Commission indicators, which are used generally to ensure that the police service does its job well, and the inspectorate of constabulary has indicators, but Ministers may respond to the debate by telling us how specific and independently tested performance indicators could be introduced.

The reasons why these matters are important speak for themselves, but one test as to how satisfactory the police are is whether, as the new clause states in paragraph (2)(e), there are

    "equal satisfaction levels across all ethnic groups in public satisfaction surveys".

I compliment the Met on what it has done in recent years to ensure that it tries to respond to the concerns that have been expressed. To be fair, it was responding to those concerns before the Lawrence inquiry reported, but I have every confidence that the inquiry will push the Met further in that.

Because the new clause contains a verbatim quote from the Lawrence recommendations, it relates specifically to racial prejudice and disadvantage and fairness in a racial context. However, there is one other context in which it would be inappropriate not to mention fairness--the Met should also have targets and indicators on homophobic violence. That has been accepted in principle; perhaps the Minister could say how it can be incorporated in a second set of tests.

As well as a police community consultative group, Southwark has a forum in which matters of prejudice, policing and security are raised. It is very effective and allows gay and lesbian people in Southwark to express their views and concerns, which they find very valuable. Until recently, people felt that if they were members of any of those categories, they did not get equal service from the police. That has changed considerably and I think that the police want matters to continue moving in that direction.

New clause 70 raises a more immediate issue of greater concern and again draws directly on the Lawrence inquiry recommendations. It proposes that the Metropolitan police authority should report to the mayor and assembly on the recruitment of ethnic minority staff in both its civilian and uniformed sections--to pick up the point of the hon. Member for Ilford, North.

The Met has been working hard to increase the number of ethnic minority recruits. Given that one in five Londoners are from ethnic minorities, it should try to be a force that represents those communities, although I do not believe in having artificial targets and allocating places; that is the wrong way to proceed. From the most recent information I have, which comes from House of Commons answers given last June, of the 26,192 serving officers in the Met at that time, 3.3 per cent. or 861 officers came from the ethnic minorities.

I read in today's paper that the Met is concerned to ensure that it helps black and Asian officers to rise to the senior ranks. From memory, there are something like 18 or 20-odd senior officers of inspector rank, superintendent rank and above--we have one in Southwark--but so far few have risen as high as that. I hope that the Government will give some indication of their support for that. Certainly the Met's senior officers keenly want young black and Asian officers to join them. New clause 71 deals with the reporting of crimes. It suggests that the MPA should propose methods to encourage the reporting of incidents and crimes, including those of a racist nature, after consulting the mayor and assembly. I do not pretend that it is an original suggestion--it is a direct crib from the recommendations of the Lawrence inquiry. It was tabled to give the Government a chance to make an early response in this area, which I am sure that they will not find difficult in the light of the recommendations of Sir William Macpherson and his advisers. I hope that the Government will say that the measure will be included when it is appropriate to do so.

We must be careful in one respect about the implementation of the Lawrence report. As we begin to eliminate racism, as far as is possible, from individual officers and the police as a whole, so that black and Asian citizens in London can have every confidence in the police, we must ensure that measures are not taken that lead to the white community losing confidence in the police. It is a delicate balance, but we must achieve it. At the end of the exercise, when London's police have been inspected by an inspectorate, everyone must feel more confident.

I shall give an example. After the Lawrence inquiry, the police undertook an investigation, which has now ended, into the death of an 18-year-old white youngster, Robert Radford, in Walworth in my constituency. Someone has now been arrested and charged with manslaughter in connection with his death. The officers who dealt with the case were, like those who dealt with the Lawrence case, south-east London senior detectives. It was easy for the parents of Robert Radford to feel that all the attention was being devoted to crimes with a racial element--those with a black or Asian victim. I had to spend quite some time reassuring them that that was not the case, and should not be the case. As we build up the confidence of families such as the Lawrences and others from minority communities, it is important to ensure that we build up the Met's ability to respond to all crime in London. It would be no good winning the confidence of those with less confidence if that of those who had more was lost.

If we allow the Met to concentrate on the crimes and activities that are important to Londoners--by definition, murders, homicides, manslaughters and violent crimes are at the top end of the scale--we will do all our fellow-citizens a service. I hope that, in non-confrontational tones, I have persuaded the 1289 1290 Government to be sympathetic to the new clauses and that they can take them on board, thus giving the first positive response to the Macpherson inquiry recommendations.

New clause 69 arose from recommendation 2 of the Macpherson report. My right hon. Friend the Home Secretary has announced that he will use his powers under the Police Act 1996 to ensure that every police authority sets clear objectives to improve its response to racist crime and to establish effective ways of demonstrating fairness in all aspects of policing. The Home Secretary has powers under section 37 of the 1996 Act to fix objectives for police authorities, and to set performance targets to meet those objectives. Authorities must have regard to those objectives--known as ministerial priorities--and reflect them in their local policing objectives and local policing plans. Chief constables are obliged to have regard to those plans in exercising their functions. All those provisions of the 1996 Act are applied to the metropolitan police by schedule 17 to the Bill.

We aim to have in place a formal ministerial priority and targets by 2000-01, which, as I said, will predate the establishment of the MPA. Before we decide on the targets to support the ministerial priority, we will consult further, notably with ethnic minority communities themselves, and with police forces and authorities. The 10 targets in the new clause may not be the best ones to use. I notice that many of them are not targeted on outcomes. We think that it is right to spend some time ensuring that appropriate targets are set, and we also believe that it is better to set them in subordinate legislation, as that offers more flexibility to respond to future changes than is provided by primary legislation.
