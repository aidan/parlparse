Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): The clause deals with the serious and important subject of the abolition of the death penalty for a number of offences relating to treason, which are set out in the clause.

I want to make it clear that although I am speaking for the Opposition on the Bill, in my short speech I am expressing my individual views. This is free-vote territory for the Opposition. I understand that that is also the case for the Government, and that is absolutely right.

I understand the circumstances in which it arose in another place, and I have now given the matter careful thought, taking into account the views of my constituents. I have concluded that the clause should not be part of the Bill. Before I give my reasons for that view, I should like to broach two matters with the Minister. Will the Minister also respond to the following specific point? I understand that the offence of treason will be subject to a discretionary life sentence rather than the death penalty. If that is so, it will become a less serious offence than murder and will carry a less serious penalty. Treason will carry the same sentence as many other offences, which although serious in themselves, are less serious than the most serious offence of all, which is murder. Such offences carry a sentence of discretionary, rather than mandatory, life imprisonment. Is that right? Is not treason an utterly heinous crime?

We must consider what happens in practice. We do not want to return to ancient legal technicalities. The ancient law of treason made it an offence to violate the King's eldest unmarried daughter, counterfeit the Privy Seal, bring false money into the realm or slay the Lord Chancellor. Supporters of the euro or admirers of the Lord Chancellor can rest easy. In practice, modern Britain does not consider such crimes to be treason. I may be wrong again, but I understand that there has not been a prosecution in peacetime for an offence of treason carrying the death penalty since the 19th century. One of the last prosecutions for treason in peacetime occurred in the case of the Cato street conspiracy, which took place in about 1820. that was a bizarre historical episode in which it later turned out that many of the conspirators had been drawn into the conspiracy by a man who had been recruited to act as an agent provocateur on the direct orders of the Home Secretary of the day. Whatever we may think of modern Home Secretaries, none of us would lay such a charge against them.

In practice, we are considering the offence of treason in wartime. Prosecutions this century for offences of treason that carry the death penalty have all been made in wartime. Treason in wartime is the most heinous of crimes, although treason in peacetime is a more difficult issue. I have no hesitation in saying that treason in wartime is beyond the pale and must be treated as such. We shall debate the issue today, but I hope that the House will have an opportunity to return to it on Report and, perhaps, consider an amendment that will allow the death penalty to remain for offences of treason in wartime.

The circumstances of treason in wartime make it an especially heinous crime. One of my colleagues who is an eminent lawyer and one of her Majesty's counsel is opposed on principle to capital punishment for murder, but supports the penalty's retention for treason in wartime. I accept that millions of people oppose capital punishment on principle, but such opposition means little when the state is engaged in war. Terrible things happen in war. Thousands of innocent service men, women and civilians on both sides of a conflict may die through various acts of war. Why is it acceptable to countenance the death of an enemy soldier or civilian, but not the death of someone who betrays his country, or seeks to bring about his country's downfall, or to prolong the war?

In war, the highest priority must be given to protecting our service men and women in the field. That is my main reason for wishing to retain the death penalty for treason in wartime. Someone who betrays our service men and women in war not only deserves the death penalty, but should receive it as a deterrent to others of like mind.

Views differ on deterrence. However, in war, even life imprisonment—the new maximum sentence for treason—may not be much of a deterrent. All members of the Committee will be able to think of at least one occasion this century when, in world opinion, our country's downfall was imminent. On such occasions, life imprisonment would not have deterred someone who was minded to commit treason. In those years, when our brave soldiers, sailors and airmen were fighting, and civilians were suffering and losing their lives at home, we owed to them to have the ultimate deterrent as a protection, and to help save lives, defend the country and bring the war to an end more quickly.

That time may seem long ago, and remote to those of my generation and to younger people, but many of our constituents remember it. The last prosecution for treason, which carried the death penalty, arose out of the last war. One of those hanged for treason was William Joyce. His case has been used as an example of why we should not retain the death penalty for treason. On my admittedly incomplete understanding of his life history, I take the opposite view.

Some people claim that William Joyce was an unlucky man who had a posh accent, was a figure of fun and made funny broadcasts to this country during the war. I take a different view: I believe that he deserved his fate. William Joyce was a Nazi sympathiser who shared the beliefs and aims of the Nazi regime, did his best to put them into practice in this country before the war and went to assist Germany during the war. People refer to the legal aspects of the case, but William Joyce was tried in this country before a judge and jury who took 23 minutes to convict him. He had a fair trial, with full legal representation—that is more than can be said for the victims of the regime that he did so much to promote.

Most important, the man committed treason. He was part of the Nazi propaganda machine that was directed against this country during the war. He disheartened civilians and gave false information to service people. William Joyce had a full and fair trial. He deserved his fate because he was a traitor. Someone who commits treason in wartime commits the most heinous offence.

As a matter of conscience, I believe that the death penalty for treason should be available in wartime. Therefore, I shall oppose the clause.

When time limits set from charge to appearance in magistrates' courts and so forth are breached, proceedings are stayed under the provisions of the Bill. Clause 42 describes the sanction of staying proceedings. The Prosecution of Offences Act did not have such a sanction. What is the effect of staying proceedings if the time limit is breached? If proceedings are stayed, but the prosecution wants to continue to prosecute the charge, the only way in which it can do so is by instituting fresh proceedings after a direction. In the case of a Crown court, it would mean referring a voluntary bill of indictment. In the case of a magistrates court, it would mean laying an information. To cut out the technicality and jargon, this could be described as going back to square one, which we are afraid might happen. We are concerned about the length of time that it may take for a new prosecution to be instituted and to go through the necessary proceedings. I shall be asking the Minister to give us an indication of the length of time that he thinks it will take.
