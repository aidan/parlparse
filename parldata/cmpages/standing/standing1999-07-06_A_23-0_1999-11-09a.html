The starting point is our commitment to facilitating competition generally. Competition is one of the main drivers of economic growth and prosperity, leading to greater efficiency, innovation and choice. Greater competition at home is the key to greater competitiveness abroad. However, competition is not the be all and end all. If it were, we should not need a financial services regulator. The point of regulation is that there are issues to be addressed that cannot be dealt with by competition alone.

To take an extreme example, it could be argued that the entire system of authorisation is a barrier to competition, in that it prevents firms that might otherwise do so from entering the financial services industry. However, no one would seriously argue that authorisation should be abandoned for that reason.

The FSA will have good reason for wanting to keep certain firms out of the industry. Those firms' entering the industry would be bad for consumers and, by damaging consumer confidence, bad for the industry as a whole. When firms are within the regulatory fold, it is right that they should be allowed to carry on certain activities only within a regulatory framework. If consumers and markets are to be protected, financial services firms must, for example, have proper systems and controls in place and must have adequate financial resources.

The key point is that while the consumer is the ultimate beneficiary of competition, to secure this benefit he also needs to feel confident that he is not dealing in a market in which he is exposed to unethical, incompetent or unacceptably risky behaviour. For that confidence, he will look to the regulatory system.

In clause 2 we have set out the FSA's four objectives: the protection of consumers, market confidence, public awareness and the reduction of financial crime. These are the aims of regulation, the raison d'etre of the FSA. In meeting these objectives, the FSA may have to make rules or adopt policies that potentially impede or distort competition, such as imposing conditions on the way in which people can carry out financial services business. In order to ensure that, where it impacts on competition, regulation does not place burdens on firms or create market distortions greater than is necessary to achieve the regulatory objectives, the Bill puts in place both internal and external constraints.

I shall quickly outline the internal constraints, before turning to the external oversight arrangements with which this chapter is concerned. It is important to understand the way in which the two work together. There will inevitably be a number of ways in which the FSA could achieve its objectives. In making its choice, the FSA will have the duty of paying regard to what we referred to earlier as ``principles'', which are contained in clause 2(3). The objectives are about the purpose of regulation, and the principles are about the manner of regulation.

I am sure that Committee members are familiar with the principles, but I shall remind them of clause 2(3)(f), which contains the competition principle. That subsection states:

    ``In discharging its general functions the Authority must have regard to . . . the principle that competition between authorised persons should not be impeded or distorted unnecessarily.''

We fully agree with Don Cruickshank, who said that it is essential to get right the regulator's statutory duties. As he said, they drive the way in which the regulator recruits, organises and rewards its staff.

We are therefore reviewing the principle's operation—we said that we would do so in earlier debates—to ensure that it will constrain the FSA in the way in which we intend that to be done. The intention is that the FSA should be committed to choosing the regulatory solutions that appear to it to have the least anti-competitive outcome that is consistent with the need to protect consumers. We will in due course bring forward any necessary amendments.

As was (mentioned in the debate on clause 125, clause 125(7) currently has the effect of limiting the meaning of costs to compliance costs that are incurred by regulated firms in complying with the rules in question. The market changes over time and what is appropriate at one point may no longer be so at another, given market developments. The FSA will obviously need to address that on an on-going basis. Asking it to report on that matter in its annual report simply reinforces that requirement.

Those two changes will help to ensure that the FSA will continue to give competition considerations their full weight when formulating rules and policies and in their day-to-day regulatory activities.

The other side of the coin involves the external competition scrutiny arrangements, which we have put in place. That matter is the subject of chapter III of part IX, which we are currently considering. The reason for having that special regime is simple. If we did not have it, there would be no external scrutiny of FSA rules and practices. The Competition Act 1998 does not cover the rule-making functions of statutory bodies. The fact that we have this special regime is testament to the importance that we attach to competition in the financial services sector. It is important to have external scrutiny, but I would expect it to be called into play infrequently.

The FSA's own processes, which are highly transparent under the Bill provisions, form, as it were, the first line of defence in ensuring that competition is given full weight. The provisions of this part of the Bill form the second line of defence.

I suggest that we should debate the relevant mechanics in subsequent clauses. The Treasury then decides whether the rules are justified. We are proposing to make some changes to the arrangements in the light of Don Cruickshank's report, but I will deal with those matters when they come up.

The purpose of the clause is to define the scope of the competition scrutiny regime, by clarifying what falls within it.
