Clause 1

Unlawful interception

Mr. Heald: the amendments deal with the part of clause 1 that provides for the offences of unlawful interception, first in respect of public postal and telecoms systems, and secondly in respect of private systems. As the offences are drafted, the prosecution will have to show that an interception took place, that it had not been lawfully authorised and that it was intentional. The amendments would widen the offences to cover interceptions that are not only unlawful but reckless. The offences are one of the most important protections provided to victims of unlawful interceptions and will enable prosecutions to be made when authorities or other parties intentionally and unlawfully intercept communications.

Even as they are currently drafted, the provisions will concentrate interceptors' minds, but they will also provide them with protection for accidental interception of information without authority. The word ``intentionally'' provides the interceptor with a defence for mistakes and careless actions. We believe that it is perfectly right to introduce such protection, but we are concerned that the current drafting would also provide an interceptor with a defence for reckless interception. If an interceptor had a warrant or authorisation but grossly exceeded it, not caring less whether he intercepted other communications, he could defend himself by saying that he had not deliberately set out to do it, perhaps because he just did not give a fig.

It is important to penalise reckless interceptions, so we should be aware that the Bill gives interceptors wide powers and considerable flexibility. Clause 5(3) allows interceptions to be authorised in respect of all postal and telecommunications systems for a wide range of broadly defined purposes. The intercepted communication does not even have to be identified by the warrant. Clause 5(6)(a) provides discretion to intercept other communications. The list of people who may apply for a warrant is long and open ended. The Secretary of State can designate any person to apply, but he does not have to sign the warrant itself. A senior official may instead suffice, and he can quickly modify the document. In some instances, the interceptor does not even require a warrant when he believes that there is consent. In other words, consent does not have to exist; the interceptor must merely reasonably believe that it does. Clause 4 deals with external interceptions under mutual assistance agreements, for which no warrant is required.

Interception of communications is an invasion of privacy that should occur only in conditions that are tightly circumscribed by the House and which are necessary for effective crime fighting and the other purposes set out in clause 5(3). United Kingdom subjects are entitled to protection from overweening power and state interference. The amendments would ensure that interceptors do not act recklessly and would provide a counterbalance to the wide powers contained in the Bill.

The clause also affects internet users and internet service providers. Internet users believe that they own the internet system and that they have great freedom. I think that their confidence in the internet is currently justified. However, if interception is to be widely practised and sometimes undertaken recklessly, it will undermine confidence in the internet. It will also damage the interests of internet service providers, who share an interest in ensuring that interception is well targeted and incapable of being used in a broad and reckless way.

Certainly, stock market investors feel that it will become a widely used method of communication. If that is the case, it is important for the confidence of users and for our future as a country that trades on the net, that there should be no doubt as to the intentions of interceptors.

The question of recklessness has come up over the years in the courts, and there are classic judgments on the subject. I shall cite Lord Diplock, who touched on this in the Lawrence case. He ruled:

    Recklessness on the part of the doer of an act presupposes that there is something in the circumstances that would have drawn the attention of an ordinary prudent [and sober] individual to the possibility that his act was capable of causing the kind of serious harmful consequences that the section that created the offence was intended to prevent.

In this case, it is intended to prevent people intecepting deliberately. We are saying that mischief should cover any reckless act as well.

Lord Diplock said that

    the risk has been identified and the risk of those harmful consequences occurring was not so slight that an ordinary prudent individual would feel justified in treating them as negligible. It is only when this is so that the doer of the act is acting ``recklessly'' if, before doing the act, he either fails to give any thought to the possibility of there being any such a risk or, having recognised that there was such a risk, he nevertheless goes on to do it.

That is a sophisticated definition of recklessness, but it shows that the courts are able to decide what recklessness is.

In a judgment that is more relevant to the concerns of politicians, Lord Lane, as Lord Chief Justice, ruled in the Taylor case that a defendant is reckless if he acts in a way that shows that he ``could not care less''.

The last thing we want are interceptors whose minds are not properly focused on what they should be doing. We recognise that, in general, the authorities act responsibility and interceptions have been correctly handled, but we do not want to start a culture of slackness when the powers have been widened. We do not want interceptors who could not care less to act recklessly.

For many crimes, intention to cause the proscribed result or recklessness is sufficient to impose liability. A person who does not intend to cause a harmful result may take an unjustifiable risk and, if he or she does, that person is deemed to be reckless. The situation with interceptors suggests that not all risk-taking activity constitutes recklessness. In Smith and Hogan's ``Criminal Law''-the establised text in this context-the editor, Professor J. C. Smith, observes that, although a surgeon who performs an operation may take a risk, that risk is justifiable. In most cases, recklessness is not prima facie an offence for which the surgeon would be liable. Another example involves the promoter of a tightrope act, who would be aware, at some level, of the risk of harm. However, the fact that he was promoting that activity would not necessarily constitute recklessness.

In a case involving citizen's arrest, recklessness would not apply if the citizen drove a car dangerously in an effort to stop the criminal from getting away. Case law suggests that the well-meaning citizen would not, prima facie, be guilty of reckless driving.

I want to get to the heart of the matter. The amendments would improve the clause because the courts have clearly said that, if the act of risk taking involves no social utility-for example, a game of Russian roulette-the slightest possibility of harm would be enough to prove recklessness. At the other end of the spectrum, case law suggests that, if an act involves a high degree of social utility-for example, in the context of law enforcement, intercepting the traffic of criminals-only a high probability of harm being done would constitute recklessness. On the one hand, in relation to cases with no social utility, it is easy to establish recklessness, but, on the other hand, in the context of law enforcement and in relation to cases with high social utility, it is much harder to establish recklessness. I hope that the Minister will take that on board. He has not established adequate objections to the amendments. {**UF1**}To establish recklessness, it is necessary to show that the individual took an unjustifiable risk. Two forms of recklessness are interesting. The first is defined in case law as Cunningham recklessness, which requires proof that the individual was aware of the existence of unreasonable risk. The interceptor would have to be aware of a risk when seeking to intercept traffic under a warrant.

The other form of recklessness, Caldwell and Lawrence recklessness, requires a slightly different test. That test would be satisfied if the individual knew of the existence of the risk, or-this goes further than the Cunningham test-in the case of an obvious risk, if the interceptor had failed to give any thought to the possibility that a risk might exist. The Caldwell and Lawrence test is slightly more onerous. Those clear legal principles meet the Minister's objections to the amendments.

The interception provisions that are part of the convention were originally intended to ensure that law enforcement agencies will have the capability to intercept satellite telephone calls on the intercepting member state's own territory.
