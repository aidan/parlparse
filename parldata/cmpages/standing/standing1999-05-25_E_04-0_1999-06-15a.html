Youth Justice and Criminal Evidence Bill [Lords]

Clause 6

Establishment of panels

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 61 in page 6, line 9, at end insert—

    `(3A)(a) The youth offending team shall not appoint to the youth offender panel—

    (i) any police officer involved in the prosecution of the young offender.

    (ii) any person who appears to the youth offending team to be a victim of, or otherwise affected by, the offence, or other offences, in respect of which the offender was referred to the panel, or

    (iii) any magistrate who made the order referring the young offender to the youth offender panel.

    (b) No magistrate who is a member of a youth offender panel shall preside in any youth or other magistrates court to whom a young offender is referred by that panel.'.—[Mr. Greenway.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Paul Boateng): Mr. Stevenson, we have had in your absence an interesting and—you will be glad to hear—well-ordered debate. We have examined how the make-up of youth offender panels might best be guaranteed to ensure that all the requisite skills are in place in a way that does not create the sort of conflict of interest that might arise when a participant is seen to be parti pris.

In order to reassure the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenaway), let me make it clear that we take the composition of youth offender panels very seriously. That is central to the successful operation of the panel process and therefore to the success of referral orders. However, we are not minded to deal with the issue by way of statute, in that we do not think it necessary to include the hon. Gentleman's amendment in the Bill. It would preclude the appointment of police officers, victims and magistrates if they are otherwise involved in the case and it would preclude magistrates from hearing cases referred back to the court by the youth offending team if they are involved in the panel.

We do not accept the need to put that provision in the Bill, because guidance would be a better way of approaching the question of the composition of a panel. That guidance will need to be specific on the point that the hon. Member for Ryedale makes. It would be inappropriate for those involved to sit on the panel if the young person concerned is a party in a case on which a police officer is engaged or on which a magistrate is sitting. Similarly, the point is well made in relation to the victims of crime. Mr. Boateng: Clause 7 sets out those who may attend a youth panel meeting, among whom will be an adult of the young offender's choosing. Amendment No. 62 would remove from the Bill the panel's power to refuse such attendance. Amendment No. 63 would have two effects: to entitle a young offender to be accompanied by his or her legal representative when the contents of the contract are to be discussed, and to entitle parents and guardians to attend any panel meeting.

We are unable to accept either amendment. The advantage of the youth offender panel arrangement is that it enables the bringing together, around a table, of all those who could support a young offender to help him or her to avoid any further offending behaviour.

An offender will almost invariably be accompanied by one or both of his parents, and may also have with him other family members and significant adults, such as a teacher or a social worker. We also consider it important that a young person should be able to nominate a supporter of his or her choice. However, we are wholly opposed to legal representation at any stage of the panel meetings, and I will explain why in due course and in detail.

A young offender should be able to nominate his or her own supporter—someone whom he will be comfortable to have by his side at what is for him an important time. That is at the heart of the new dispensation, as far as the criminal justice process for young people is concerned. We need to be realistic about the situations that may arise. Young offenders may not make the best of choices. They may bring with them a friend of their choice who, it seems to the youth offender panel, is unlikely to be a constructive presence at the panel meetings.

Any of us who has any experience of the world and of children and young people, never mind the criminal justice system, knows their capacity to be negative and destructive when they set their minds to it. It must therefore be correct for the panel to have a right of veto. Nothing would be gained from proceeding in such situations, which would rob a young offender of the opportunity that the panel should provide.

I do not envisage that the right of veto will be exercised easily or often. Wherever possible, a young offender's choice should be respected, as will be made clear in the guidance to panels.

We have a fundamental objection to the other arm of amendment No. 63. As I have said, we do not believe that legal representation is a desirable objective in the context of youth offender panels, which are designed specifically to remove barriers between the young person and consideration of their offending. Legal representatives constitute a barrier that we wish to avoid. We want to create a forum where young people can be encouraged to come to their own conclusions about their offending behaviour and its effect on others. As we have discussed, a range of supportive adults will be there to help them to do that, as well as the trained panel members.

No member of the Committee can pretend that that is somehow the role of legal representatives. It is not what they are trained to do. Indeed, their whole culture—our culture as lawyers—is adversarial and in some ways in direct counterpoint to the approach and attitude that would cause the young person to become engaged with the process. Lawyers see themselves as a shield, not as a means of encouraging or engineering the involvement of a young person in a process that requires them to confront their offending and address the means of preventing them offending in future.

Therefore, we see the removal of legal representation from the panels as a positive step. We do not believe that it will have a negative impact on the young person. There is nothing coercive about the process. The whole point is that there should be a negotiation—that young people should be involved in the process and should, of their own volition, see its benefit for them. If they cannot see its benefit, it will do them no good anyway. Where it has not been possible to reach an agreement or enter into a contract, the matter will be remitted back to the youth court, where the young person will have the full benefit of representation. The panel's job is to bring together a range of interventions—the constituent parts of the sentence—which is designed to make the young person confront and address the issue of offending and reoffending.

When a young person feels, at the time or with hindsight, that he has been persuaded to sign up to more than is reasonable, he will have the opportunity to reopen discussions with the panel and, where necessary, to return to court for the matter to be decided by magistrates.
