Mr. David Lidington (Aylesbury): At the end of this morning's proceedings, I described my concerns about the impact of the Government's proposal on various aspects of public sector activity. They fear that the emergence in this country of a compensation culture along the lines of that in the United States will give rise to unnecessary and vexatious litigation, and that contesting legal action and making compensation payments will involve public sector bodies in unnecessary expense.

I hope that the Minister will be able to assure me that the Government have thought carefully about the implication of their proposal for every Department in Whitehall. I hope, too, that a proper consultation exercise has taken place between other Departments and the Home Office to ensure that the consequences of the amendment have been properly thought through. Every hon. Member is aware that the law sometimes has consequences that legislators did not intend. I hope, therefore, that the Minister can give me the assurances that I seek.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): I share the concern of the hon. Member for Aylesbury (Mr. Lidington) that we should not develop the compensation culture that has emerged in many other countries. We do not expect-indeed, there are no grounds to expect-that the Bill will result in that. There will be people, as there always, are, who make spurious claims under the Bill. However, the Government's approach has involved considerable consultation across Departments. Departments and public authorities will be able to resist spurious claims, and the fears raised about this aspect of the Bill are unlikely to produce any serious problems.

We understand the concerns that have been expressed. No doubt some lawyers could examine legislation and say that they could make a claim under this or that provision. However, that does not mean that the courts will entertain such a claim in practice. There is a clear onus on the complainant to show that a requirement or condition has been applied to him, that fewer people of his racial group can comply and that it is to his detriment that he cannot comply. Therefore, the onus on the complainant is quite great in the first place, and would prevent claims with no merit from succeeding.

The House of Lords considered the issue of the justification for indirect discrimination on 17 February in the case of Seymour Smith. The case was Regina v. Secretary of State for Education and Employment. There was an original appellant and cross-respondent. It was ex parte Seymour Smith and Another, but I will call it Seymour Smith.

The text of that judgment is very helpful to the Government and, indeed, to public authorities in considering this issue. It involves similar provisions in the equal treatment directive. The case was about whether a requirement that a person had to be employed for two years before he or she could claim unfair dismissal indirectly discriminated against women, since a smaller percentage of women are employed for two years or more. The case was decided on the grounds that the difference in terms of those that could comply was not statistically considerably smaller.

Given that that was a House of Lords judgment, it carries considerable weight. Although the case was not decided on the grounds of justification, it has, obiter, an important aspect which assists our consideration of the amendment. In his comments about rejected justification in relation to the case, Lord Nicholls said:

    The burden placed on the government in this type of case is not as heavy as previously thought. Governments must be able to govern. They adopt general policies, and implement measures to carry out their policies. Governments must be able to take into account a wide range of social, economic and political factors...If their aim is legitimate, governments have discretion when choosing their method to achieve their aim. National courts, acting with the benefit of hindsight, are not to impose an impractical burden on governments which are proceeding in good faith. Generalised assumptions, lacking any factual foundation are not good enough. Governments are to be afforded a broad measure of discretion.

The Government take some comfort from this recent indication of the courts willingness to address the issue of justification. Bearing in mind our strongly held view that our policies are fully justifiable, even if they have a disproportionate impact on different racial groups, we feel that there is little to fear from this being tested in the courts.

Turning to the point made by the hon. Member for Aylesbury regarding the number of claims that might arise, the Government do not expect a surge of vexatious claims, although a theoretical risk can always be identified. If we take claims of indirect discrimination handled by the Commission for Racial Equality under the non-employment provisions of the Race Relations Act 1976 as a guide for claims that may be brought under the proposed new section 19B, I believe that we should be reassured.

In 1997, only nine out of 456 complaints handled by the CRE were on the grounds of indirect discrimination, and in 1998 only three out of 479 complaints were on those grounds. Such numbers of claims are not large by any stretch of the imagination. That is the case under the existing provisions on indirect discrimination in the Act, which I should stress already applied to public authorities as employers, as providers of goods, facilities and services and in the field of education and training. We see no reason why in the long term the position should be any different in respect of public functions to which the Act is being extended.

That is not to say that the Act will not have any effect. It will certainly have a very beneficial effect on the way in which public authorities operate. What I am suggesting is that because of the way in which the law is operated, and given the strong indications by the House of Lords in that recent judgment, we do not think it likely that frivolous claims will be entertained seriously by the courts.

That brings me to the point raised by the hon. Member for Aylesbury regarding the defence under section 1(1)(b) of the Act. As his outline of the case law this morning reminds us, indirect discrimination is not a new concept. Both the public and the private sectors have been subject to it for almost a quarter of a century. The only problem, which the Bill should correct, is that some significant public sector functions were not included in the 1976 Act or were excluded by case law.

As I said in my opening remarks, the case law shows that the courts have given public authorities a fair wind. Recent comments by Lord Nicholls in the Seymour Smith case reaffirm that.

As for the hon. Gentleman's point about damages for inadvertent, indirect discrimination under section 57(3) of the Act, no damages are payable if a respondent proves that the requirement or condition in question was not applied with the intention of treating the complainant unfairly on racial grounds. The provision will apply to any claims of indirect discrimination that are brought under new section 19B. The provision will operate in such a way that the hon. Gentleman's concern about the compensation culture does apply, but the court could issue guidance or a directive about how the law could be interpreted.

The hon. Member for Aylesbury asked whether indirect discrimination would prevent the police from using stop and search that is intelligence-led. The Government's view is that nothing in the amendment would give rise to that. Stop and search will not be inhibited, whether intelligence-led or not, by reason of the amendment or the legislation as a whole. Police officers are required under section 1 of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 to have reasonable suspicion that an offence has been, or may be, committed in order to carry out a stop and search.

Under section 60 of the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994, to have reasonable grounds for suspicion or belief will depend upon the circumstances, but there must be an objective basis for it. A police officer who goes beyond his powers and stops someone solely on the grounds of his race or colour or otherwise treats someone unfavourably on such grounds could be challenged within the direct discrimination provisions in the Bill. I hope that I have dealt with some of the legitimate concerns that were raised by the hon. Member for Aylesbury.

A whole series of other cases give rise to our view that fair wind would be accorded public authorities in carrying out their proper duties, provided that there is some justification. I can cite a number of cases-although the strongest is Seymour Smith-including Panesar v. Nestle Co. Ltd [1980], a decision by the employment appeals tribunal, Kingston and Richmond Area Health Authority v. Kaur [1981] and the Board of Governors of St. Matthias Church of England School v. Crizzle [1993], each of which involved a decision of the employment appeals tribunal. In those cases, it was a matter of how the public authority would exercise its view of its policy, and the tribunal decided that the public authority had acted reasonably.

We do not, therefore, have anything to fear, because the provision will be applied in a reasoned way. It has been applied in many sectors of employment for a long time without serious problems. It is helpful, because it reduces the conditions or requirements that would be discriminatory, and places a burden on public authorities that are brought within the provisions of the race relations legislation by reason of the amendment not to discriminate. I suggest, however, that it will not create the grounds on which any of the fears that the hon. Gentleman has rightly brought to our attention may be justified. We do not expect a compensation culture. We have canvassed the views of other Departments; they, too, are satisfied that they can justify their policies. I hope that on that basis I have reassured the hon. Member for Aylesbury.

Mr. O'Brien: I shall deal first with whether the post will be part time. It is envisaged that it will operate in a similar way to the current entry clearance monitor-currently the very able Dame Elizabeth Anson-which is a part time and remunerated post. I am unsure whether she receives a full salary, but she is certainly remunerated for her work. She does not deal with individual complaints: she is not a court of appeal and does not have the ability to deal directly with complaints from the public, but she looks at individual files and decides how the entry clearance system is operating in practice. She is able to conduct unannounced visits. Similarly, the race monitor will be able to examine case files-in as random or systematic a manner as he or she requires-to establish how they are being dealt with.

I was asked what would happen if a discrimination were disclosed. First, it would be reported to Parliament and the circumstances in which it happened, if unacceptable, would doubtless be changed. It would become public. That shows that there is oversight and the ability to check how the provisions are operating. As I said, there could be unannounced visits.

It is not envisaged that the activities of the security services would be investigated in any way by the race monitor. There are other oversight provisions for the security services, which will remain in place. Separate legislation-passed about 18 months ago-deals with the way in which immigration operates in security cases. The race monitor is, however, likely to be informed about intelligence operations in the immigration service when they relate to criminal matters. In those cases, the monitor will have to operate carefully and brief Parliament about sensitive intelligence information. We shall choose someone who will be able to exercise the requisite degree of sensitivity. I hope that the Committee will support that.
