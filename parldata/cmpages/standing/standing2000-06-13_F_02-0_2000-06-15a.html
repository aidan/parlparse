Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): We debated designated members briefly at our previous sitting. The Minister's response on Tuesday was that they will do similar things and have responsibilities similar to company secretaries but that, because the statute book on company law does not state what company secretaries do, there should be nothing on the statute book to state what designated members do. We have been assured by the Minister and in Government notices that a guidance booklet on limited liability partnerships will be published to provide the missing details when the Bill comes into force. The summary of responses to the July 1999 consultation document, which I quoted at our previous sitting states:

Mr. Stephen O'Brien (Eddisbury): I shall begin by declaring an interest. I am conscious of the need not to transgress the no-advocacy rules.

On Second Reading, I urged the Minister to reconsider the clause's requirements about designated members. I want to raise five matters in that context. Limited liability partnerships are driven primarily by the competitive and international commercial needs of currently large professional partnerships. However, we should not lose sight of the fact that llps that have operated elsewhere in the world were rapidly and quite properly used for joint ventures in multinational manufacturing companies-partners came together for no premium, in addition, obviously, to smaller managed companies. Property companies that wanted to come together were particularly relevant in that regard. It is crucial to ensure that the provisions' impact on property companies has been thought through.

The first of the five points that concern me follows on from the comments of my hon. Friend the Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton and the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett)-we need to define the functions that are carried out by designated members. The role of a company secretary in a plc is not defined in statute, apart from the requirement to have one. However, useful custom and practice has grown, especially in the light of Cadbury, involving good corporate governance and the ensuing requirements. The lessons that we have learned during the past 10 years should also be involved. I say that in the context of the on-going consultation on company law reform that is being led by Department of Trade and Industry. That approach has to be viewed in the context of the overall consultation process on company law reform. One could argue that our debate should be seen as a precursor of the various matters that will be raised in that process.

Secondly, are two designated members needed? The Government's approach appears to involve the belief that it is necessary to have two designated members in a partnership business, in the absence of which the default goes to all members-that involves the culture of binding all the partners and a carry through from the past. I shall presently explain why the Government's approach may be slightly misconstrued.

Thirdly, an analogy has been drawn between what the designated members and the officer of a company will need to do. Those of us with corporate experience know that a rather vague concept is involved, in addition to the specific requirements involving directors-the fiduciary duties that are owed by a director are clear, but how does an officer bind the company? That has always been somewhat vague. The analogy between a designated member and an officer was discussed in the consultation document, which stated that it was too difficult to define a designated member because there was no equivalent definition of an officer. That is wrong-there is a large body of relevant custom and practice, which would be extremely helpful to a company secretary, and which clearly states the role and duties of an officer of a company. It might have been better to draw an analogy between designated members and a company secretary rather than to argue that the Government could not produce a definition in the absence of a proper definition of an officer in company law.

Fourthly, why cannot the functions that we presume are to be carried out by designated members-in the absence of a definition, this matter is linked to my third point-be carried out by an employee or other person concerned with the llp? An analogy could be drawn with company secretaries, who-at least in the case of larger companies-are rarely directors. There is often a real and proper reason for that.

Fifthly, on residency, the Minister said on Second Reading that it was not necessary for designated members to be resident in the United Kingdom, because the registered office was to be in England, Wales or Scotland. I presume that that will eventually, under separate provisions, apply to Northern Ireland as well.

On Second Reading, the Minister said that it was not necessary to provide a definition of a designated member because the position was similar to that of the officer of a company. However, a great body of knowledge now exists about the role of a company secretary that could be utilised in creating a definition. It has proved to be extremely helpful and important in the development of corporate governance and compliance over recent years. Without taking up the Committee's time by repeating myself, I listed, towards the end of my speech on Second Reading, what I thought should be the main headlines of the job description.

The Minister said that, for the avoidance of confusion, it is important to have a different name-that is, designated member rather than, say, designated secretary, which was the term that I used on Second Reading. On reflection, a better job title might be partnership secretary, which is often used in large partnerships. Limited liability partnerships will have a use and a context way beyond professional partnerships as we know them today.

The Minister said that the Government take comfort from the fact that designated members need not necessarily be UK residents. That fails to recognise the important advice role that a company secretary offers to the board of directors, especially in large enterprises that may be contemplating becoming llps. Some llps will have hundreds of partners-as I am sure that they will continue to be called in most cases-and will therefore need the strength, advice and guidance of somebody with deep knowledge of procedural and technical matters.

One matter that was not tackled in debate either here or in another place is the fact that the current proposals, which stipulate a minimum of two designated members, may lead not only to duplication-and therefore cost to the enterprise-but to confusion on the part of people who wish to communicate with the llp. Effective communication with a company generally requires a contact name.

It is intended that an llp should have at least two designated members who will carry out a number of administrative functions, some of which will relate to communications with Companies House. They will include the approval and signing of annual accounts, the filing of accounts at Companies House, the removal of auditors and, in the event of insolvency proceedings, providing any statements setting out the llp's affairs-for example, in relation to assets, debts and liabilities.

As I said at our first sitting, a designated member will be no more superior or responsible than other members. The way in which the llp is run and who makes the management decisions will be a matter for the llp, and we expect such issues to be covered by the llp agreement. I am surprised that someone with such vast experience of running a company as the hon. Member for Lichfield, who has traded with countries all round the world, should want the new vehicle to be so prescriptive in the way in which it sets out those agreements. He was right to disagree with the hon. Member for Eddisbury about the nature of being a designated member and the rather straitjacketed role that he was prescribing for that designated member as an officer of the partnership. Perhaps he will tell me later exactly what he meant by that.

I have been asked why there is no definition of a designated member. I have referred to comparisons between designated members and company secretaries, but designated members are not the direct equivalent of a company secretary. The Companies Act makes regular reference to functions that must be carried out by an officer or by an officer and director of the company. In applying the Companies Act to limited liability partnerships in regulations, it has been necessary for us to decide whether references to ``officer'' or ``officer and director'' should be to designated members and/or all members of the llp. There is no definition of an officer's role in that Act, and we cannot conceive of one that would make the designated member's role in an llp any clearer. Designated members are essentially defined by their functions, which will be contained in regulations.

I know that the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton has tabled an amendment to clause 17 and I hope that we shall deal with penalties then, but I want to outline the position, as I did at our first sitting. The application of the 1985 Act means that designated members in default will be subject to the penalties applied to officers of a company who are in default. The Act frequently imposes a duty on a company and then provides, where a breach occurs, that both the company and any officer in default are subject to a penalty. The various offences and penalties for each default are set out in schedule 24 and section 730 of that Act.

As I have said, in some cases the offence will also attach to all members of the llp, just as it may also attach to directors of a company. Clearly, therefore, the duty will be imposed on the llp, and any designated member, or any member in default, will be subject to a penalty along with the llp. For example, the designated members will be responsible for delivering annual accounts to Companies House. If they do not comply with that provision, they will be subject to a penalty in the form of a fine. They will also be liable to a fine if they fail to convene a meeting requested by the resigning auditor.

Other areas where designated members and the llp are subject to a penalty include the failure to deliver an annual return. Again, that would result in a fine. Similarly, when there is a failure to give notice to the registrar of the auditor's removal or to forward the notice of the auditor's resignation to the registrar, there will be a fine. However, as with companies, only the designated member, or the member who has knowingly and wilfully authorised the default, will be penalised. That is at the heart of the Bill. That is why we are interested in giving these partnerships limited liability.

Mr. Fabricant: I have several queries about the clause, which appears on first sight to be straightforward.

Subsection (1)(a) states:

    where a person becomes or ceases to be a member or designated member, notice is delivered to the registrar within fourteen days.

I have no argument with the fact that someone must notify Companies House when the membership of an llp changes, but does the Minister accept that the death of a member of a small llp could cause considerable disruption to the partnership? Did he consider allowing 28 days, or a longer period of grace, before it became an offence not to have made a change in membership of the partnership? In a large partnership, meeting the requirement within 14 days would not be a problem, but in a small partnership, when one of three or four partners has died, it could cause so much disruption to the partnership that the need to deliver the notice to Companies House within 14 days might be overlooked.
