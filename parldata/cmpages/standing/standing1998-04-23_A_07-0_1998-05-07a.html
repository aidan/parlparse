Mr. Fraser: I would like to speak briefly and coherently about amendments Nos. 20 and 22. My Hon. Friend the Member for North Norfolk (Mr. Prior) will speak to amendment No. 21.

Amendment No. 20 seeks to establish a scale or tariff of the fines to be imposed on the lottery operator for breaching the terms of the operating licence. The Opposition are in favour of a firm regulatory regime, which will establish a framework within which the operator can work and provide a transparent system that will gain the public's confidence.

Under the Government's planned regime, the director general and the lottery commission have the ultimate sanction of either revoking the operator's licence for failure to comply with its terms, or giving a new licence to another operator when the existing licence expires. However, we understand that the Government are determined to provide the regulator with the power to fine the operator for failure to comply with the terms of its licence.

The amendment is intended to make that process transparent by establishing a tariff or formula to[Mr. Fraser] indicate the level of fine to be imposed. The White Paper, "The People's Lottery", outlined the Government's proposal to

    "include a power for the Director General to impose financial penalties on the operator, where serious licence breaches occur."

We are concerned that clause 2, like so many other clauses, confers wide powers on the director general, which can be used at the discretion of individual office holders.

The Bill gives the operator no indication of what it might be fined for breaching the terms of the licence or even what might constitute such a breach. On Second Reading in another place, Lord McIntosh said that the operator would be fined only if there had been a serious breach of the licence. He said of the director general:

    "Now he will be able if there are serious breaches to impose a fine both as penalty and to recompense the good causes if the breach has reduced the amount of money flowing to them." [Official Report, House of Lords, 18 December 1997; Vol. 584, c. 731.]

There is no mention in the Bill of the severity or the gravity of offence that might prompt the regulator to fine the operator.

Mr. Spring: Does my hon. Friend agree that in general, when litigation results in excessive awards, it often diminishes the authority and credibility of the law? Amendment No. 22 would prevent the current or any subsequent operator from being held to account for any failure in the operation of the lottery resulting from circumstances that could reasonably said to be beyond its control. The grounds for appeal against financial penalties imposed by the regulator are not drawn widely in the Bill. They are: first, that the director general made an error concerning the facts; secondly, that there was a material procedural error; and thirdly, that the director general made some other error of law. The amendment would increase the scope for appeal by the operator by adding the grounds that the circumstances leading to the breach of the conditions of the licence were beyond the operator's control. If we compare the Bill's provisions with the grounds for appeal open to other bodies subject to a regulator, it is clear that the grounds for appeal are narrower in the Bill.

Section 57(1)(a) of the Railways Act 1993, section 30(1) of the Gas Act 1986 and section 18(1) of the Telecommunications Act 1984 all state that an operator can question the validity of an order if it is believed that the regulator has questioned his power in doing so. Such grounds clearly go beyond what the amendments or the Bill propose, but the Opposition think that there should be at least grounds for appeal in the case of force majeure.

Mr. Prior: I shall speak to amendment No. 21. It is the least controversial amendment that we have tabled, so I shall read it out. It states:

    "The Director General shall not impose a financial penalty if he is satisfied that the operator has agreed to take, and is taking, all such steps as it appears for the time being to be appropriate for the operator to take for the purpose of securing or facilitating rectification of the contravention in question."

The amendment does not seek to remove anything from clause 2. We wish to insert it at the end of the clause as subsection 15. In the event of a breach of the licence, the amendment would provide the operator with an opportunity to rectify the situation before financial penalties are imposed by the regulator.

The amendment is consistent with clause 2, the notes on which state:

    "The purpose of this clause is to increase the range of options open to the Director General for dealing with licence breaches so that he is better equipped with the full range of powers to ensure that the Lottery runs successfully."

Amendment No. 21 is consistent with that. It does not detract one iota from the powers of the regulator.

The amendment seeks to remove the possibility hopefully, a remote one of an unnecessarily adversarial relationship developing between the operator and the regulator. It is senseless for the operator to pay a fine when it rectifies a breach or satisfies the director general that it is about to do so.

Hon. Members on both sides of the Committee will surely agree that compliance with the licence is more important than penalising the operator for breaching the licence. We all want strong, independent regulation with teeth. That regulation should be transparent and clear for people who play the lottery. We do not want unfair and unprincipled regulation.

The wording of amendment No. 21 is similar to that governing contravention of the licence by operators in other regulatory regimes. The Minister will be familiar with section 55 of the Railways Act 1993, which refers to the powers of the regulator. It states that:

    "The appropriate officer shall not make a final order, or make or confirm a provisional order, in relation to the relevant operator if he is satisfied... that the relevant operator has agreed to take, and is taking, all such steps as it appears to the appropriate officer for the time being to be appropriate for the relevant operator to take for the purpose of securing or facilitating compliance with the condition or requirement in question".

The language that we have used for amendment No. 21 is almost identical to that and to wording found in Acts governing the regulation of utilities.

I would like to take the Minister back to February and March last year, when South West Trains was involved in a much-publicised case. The shortage of drivers meant that the organisation was cancelling trains on a regular basis, and, as a result, was breaching its agreement. The franchising director made a draft enforcement order, which required South West Trains to operate 98.5 per cent. of the scheduled service in April 1997 and to satisfy the director that the level of cancellations would not be repeated. Failure to do so would have subjected the operator to a fine of £1 million. In those circumstances, performance was improved and the threatened fine removed. That is the ideal outcome in such a situation. The imposition of a fine should be the last resort, because it may result in less investment in trains and rolling stock, fewer employees and a poorer service in the long term. The levying of a fine implies that the regulatory system has failed.

It is incumbent on the Government to ensure that the regulatory system works, rather than imposing a fine or a public penalty at an unnecessarily early stage. That is particularly important in the case of the lottery, for two reasons. First, public confidence in the lottery would be eroded by the wide publicity that a fine would receive.[Mr. Prior] Secondly, when the current licence expires, companies tendering for the new licence will take account of the fact that fines have been levied in the past and their bids will change accordingly. The more penal the regime, the higher the price that future operators will need to exact to take over the running of the lottery. That cannot fail to have an indirect effect on the good causes.

Mr. Banks: The aim of the amendments is to improve regulation of the national lottery to ensure that the public have full confidence in its operation. Regulation of the lottery is currently undertaken by a single individual, the director general, who is supported by staff from the Office of the National Lottery.

Clause 1 and schedule 1 reflected White Paper policy and would have established a panel to advise the director general on the selection of the national lottery operator. That would have brought collective expertise to the selection process, but the final decision would still have been taken by an individual the director general.

The amendments, however, go further to broaden and strengthen the regulatory structure. They abolish the office of the director general and remove the proposed advisory panel, and replace them with a National Lottery Commission, which will be a permanent collective body with responsibility for the selection of the operator and for on-going regulation. That will introduce a wider range of expertise and experience and so reduce the risk, actual or perceived, of conflicts of interest and regulatory capture.

The commission will have the same statutory functions and duties as the director general. Those include the new power to impose financial penalties on the operator for licence contraventions that is already contained in the Bill. The commission will have five members, who will be appointed by the Secretary of State.

We are determined to ensure that the lottery is given the most effective regulation possible to provide the objectivity, independence and transparency that the public rightly demand. When the Bill was introduced in another place it recognised the importance of widening the experience and expertise brought to bear on the selection of the lottery operator. It therefore made provision for an advisory panel to help the director general with that task.

However, we have re-examined that provision, and in the light of recent events we have concluded that we can do more to ensure that the public have confidence in the independence and objectivity of the regulator. In particular, a move away from a specific individual will help to remove possible criticisms that conflicts of interest, actual or perceived, threaten effective regulation.

The amendments therefore replace the post of director general and the advisory panel provided for in clause 1 and schedule 1 of the original Bill with a new National Lottery Commission. The commission will consist of five members to be appointed by the Secretary of State. It will be a permanent body that will take on the same statutory functions and duties as the director general. The role of its members will be to ensure that the lottery is run and promoted with all due propriety and that the interests of all players are protected. Subject to those duties, members will do their best to ensure the best possible return for the good causes.

The commission will be able to choose one of its members as a chair to run meetings and to act as a spokesperson for no more than a year at a time and no member will be reappointed immediately so that responsibility is rotated between its members. That will[Mr. Banks] avoid focusing attention and influence undesirably on any one individual.

The new commission will have in its armoury the power to fine licence holders that is already contained within the Bill. That fills a gap that was highlighted by the Select Committee on Public Affairs. Any fines levied will go to the good causes rather than to the Treasury, and the power to fine will apply in respect of existing licences as well as future awards. The commission will be supported in its work by existing Oflot staff. The amendments will provide for their employment by the commission on the same terms and conditions as they currently hold, except that the commission will not be a Crown body and its employees will not therefore be civil servants. Nothing contained in my proposals should be taken to be a criticism of the acting director general or his staff, whose professionalism and commitment are not in question.

The key provisions are new clause 2, which creates a national lottery commission to replace the director general, and new schedule 1, which is in three parts. Part 1 deals with transfers from the director general to the commission, part 2 deals with the constitution of the commission and part 3 deals with consequential amendments to the 1993 Act and other miscellaneous legislation. Amendment No. 32 lists the repeals resulting from the creation of the commission. Amendments Nos. 28 to 30 would allow the Secretary of State to make an order to bring the commission into being. I believe that those provisions, taken together, will ensure that the lottery inspires full public confidence.

Mr. Banks: I thank the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross and the hon. Member for West Suffolk for their contributions to the debate.

New clause 3 will add to section 33(3) of the National Lottery etc. Act 1993. It will allow the Comptroller and Audit General access to the lottery operator's records for the narrow purposes of auditing the national lottery distribution fund. The hon. Gentleman asked how that would be activated. The comptroller will approach Camelot after taking account of Oflot's work. There is no role for the Secretary of State whatsoever, as one would expect, in the independent actions of the CAG.

The new clause will meet the concerns raised by the Public Accounts Committee in its 20th report in 1996-97. The Government accepted that necessary access, sufficient to complete the audit of the national lottery distribution fund, was appropriate in their response to the PAC in July 1997. The new clause will, therefore, give effect to the PAC recommendation, while not setting any general precedent on the CAG's access to private bodies within regulated sectors.

The change is being introduced now, after careful consideration between other Departments and the NAO. Because of the need for wide consultation, it was not possible to introduce the new clause earlier. I apologise to members of the Committee for that, although I trust that they have had time to study the explanatory note that I issued on Wednesday 6 May and will understand the reasons. The new clause has the director general's support and the NAO is pleased with it, as is the PAC.

The new clause will allow the CAG to examine documents held by the section 5 licence holder and require written and oral explanations of it in carrying out his functions of examining, certifying and reporting on the accounts of the national lottery distribution fund.

The CAG's work will be complementary to the regulatory work currently conducted by the Director General of the National Lottery. In drafting the new clause, we were mindful that the extra compliance costs it creates should be modest. Therefore, it ensures that the CAG has a duty to take into account the work undertaken by the director general and the documents available to him through Oflot.

The access right to the operator will apply only to a financial audit. The change will not allow the CAG to conduct value-for-money scrutinies of the operator or set a wider precedent. That level of CAG access is consistent with that provided in various situations where it is necessary to audit accounts for which a public body is responsible.

I am confident that the change will reinforce transparency in the audit of the national lottery distribution fund, add to public confidence in the lotteryand ensure greater parliamentary accountability for the sums due to the good causes.
