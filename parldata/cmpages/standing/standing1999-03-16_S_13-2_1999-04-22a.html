In our previous debate, we touched on some of the matters that I wish to raise in relation to amendment No. 202. I will raise them again only to refresh the Committee's memory about what they are.

Amendment No. 202 concerns the circumstances in which a court may release a transporter after someone has applied to a court for its release. The court may release the transporter if satisfactory security is tendered or, as clause 23 (3)(a) states, if

    "there is significant doubt as to whether the penalty is payable and the applicant has a compelling need to have the transporter released."

Amendment No. 202 is straightforward: it would replace "significant" with "reasonable". Earlier, we had a debate about the meaning of words. However, there is a difference here which may be material in many circumstances, especially those relating to small firms.

We think that "significant doubt" is pitching the matter a little high. Committee members will remember that I made observations about a case in which there was some doubt, but not a significant doubt. The person in that case had a compelling need to seek the release of the transporter. I will not flesh out that example further, as I believe that Committee members will remember it.

I have already touched on the fact that I find it curious that the Government have put two completely different requirements in clause 23. One of those requirements is doubt as to whether there should be liability, the other a compelling need. It would have been far better for the measure to have been framed in an either-or way, as it will catch some people who have a significant doubt and want their transporter back, but cannot demonstrate that they have a compelling need. On the other hand, other people can demonstrate that they have a compelling need, but not a significant doubt.

Our amendment concerns the latter group of people, which is why it replaces "significant" with "reasonable". A compelling need is pitching it high. If someone really has a compelling need or particular reason for the return of his transporter, it is harsh to require him to show significant doubt too. The degree of doubt should be pitched lower.

The matter goes to the Secretary of State who concludes that it is more likely than not that the person is responsible and therefore the penalty is payable. That makes matters just about reconcilable. To leave the clause as it is will only cause confusion.

The concept of reasonable doubt is well known to the courts, so it will be easier for them to interpret than significant doubt. A High Court judge will hear the case--he will obviously be familiar with the term "reasonable doubt". In one well-known case, a leading legal text book states:

    "A reasonable doubt is a doubt based upon good reason and not a fanciful doubt, as the sort of doubt which might affect you in the conduct of your everyday affairs."

If someone can show that amount of doubt about whether they are liable to pay, their transporter should be released until the Secretary of State can fully consider whether it is more likely than not that the penalty is payable. That is a much better way of putting it, for three reasons: first, reasonable is a lesser threshold than significant; secondly, because it will be more familiar to the courts; and thirdly, it avoids the confusion of the courts prejudging something that will be decided by the Secretary of State.

Reasonable doubt is less than the civil standard of more likely than not, thus the two will be reconcilable; as the clause stands, they are not reconcilable. The heavens will not fall in or the earth open up if the Government accept the amendment, but it would make the measure more reasonable. I invite the Minister to consider it carefully.
