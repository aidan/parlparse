Requirement for private hire vehicle driver's licence

Question proposed [this day], That the clause, as amended, stand part of the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again proposed.

The Minister for Transport in London (Ms Glenda Jackson): It is a privilege to work under your chairmanship for the first time, Mr. Gale.

Earlier, I attempted to tackle some of the anxieties of the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst (Mr. Forth) about fees, their amount and the facilities to ensure that licensing would be entirely self-supporting. The right hon. Gentleman also mentioned consultation. It might be helpful if I gave the Committee the possible implementation timetable, and then dealt with fees.

If we assume that the Bill will receive Royal Assent in October 1999, the Secretary of State will formally agree the delegation of his day-to-day licensing powers to the Metropolitan police. The Public Carriage Office will have to form a project implementation team, begin recruiting and training staff, order the necessary computer hardware and software and start to plan and effect any accommodation changes.

Meanwhile Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions officials will have to get orders drafted, especially those that relate to transitional arrangements for existing operators, drivers and cars. The consultation period will then commence. That could take from December 1998 to February 1999.

Sir George Young (North-West Hampshire): When the Minister began her remarks, she said that the Bill would receive Royal Assent in 1999. Did she mean 1998?

Ms Jackson: I did. I am grateful to the right hon. Gentleman for correcting my slip of the tongue so speedily.

When the consultation period has concluded, the results will be analysed, and orders will be made and laid before Parliament. That could take until the end of July 1999. Some regulations could be made later.

When the requirements for minicab testing have been agreed, the Public Carriage Office will put vehicle testing out to tender. As the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst pointed out, there are many minicabs. There are too many to be tested at Penton street. The Public Carriage Office will also have to put out to tender drivers' badges and minicab plates, once their design requirements have been determined.

There will have to be a phased introduction of licensing. We established that this morning. However, if all goes well, operator licensing could start as early as June 1999 and be completed with all existing operators' applications processed 12 months later.

Minicab licensing could start by January 2000 and be completed in 12 months. Driver licensing depends on transitional arrangements and the rate at which criminal record checks can be carried out. That process could start by January 2000 and may be completed by December 2000. On the issue of cost, which the right hon. Gentleman had some concern about, we have already established that minicab licensing must be self-financing, that licence fees will be set by the Secretary of State, who will wish to be satisfied that the system is being administered with due regard to efficiency, and that there will be no cross-subsidy from taxi licensing to private hire vehicle licensing or vice versa.

I asked for information on licence fees outside London and a survey carried out by the National Association of Taxi and Private Hire Enforcement Officers in 1996 gives an indication of average licence fees in England and Wales outside London. The cost of private hire licences is as follows: vehicle licences cost £119; driver licences cost £48; operator licences cost £105. A comparison between taxi licence fees and the provinces give us good reason to suppose that the fees for private hire licensing will not be out of line with the provinces.

The average cost of taxi licences in the provinces was £131 for vehicle licences and £45 for driver licences. The fees in London in 1996 were £78 for a vehicle licence and £87 for a driver licence. There is quite a variation in the fees charged for minicab vehicle driver and operator licences in the provinces, but the PCO is already aware of typical figures. It would certainly have them in mind in drawing up licence fees for London. As I have already said, such fees must be approved by the Secretary of State. As the right hon. Gentleman said, there is no such thing as a free lunch. Certainly enforcement does not come free. The more pressure there is for greater enforcement, the higher the fees would have to be. I hope that that helps the right hon. Gentleman.

The key element in the amendment is the substitution of the word "shall" for the word "may" in line 15 of the clause. The clause will therefore impose a clear duty on the Secretary of State to require applicants for a minicab driver's licence to satisfy him about their topographical knowledge. I know that my hon. The amendments would improve the Bill. Amendment No. 81 touches on the fascinating subject of topographical knowledge. It would be respectable to argue that when one hires a minicab not a taxicab one is quoted the fare in advance, so it does not matter what route the driver takes. A driver may take a customer all around London, provided that the fare is agreed in advance.

That argument has considerable strength. The taxicab trade puts great emphasis in the fact as was mentioned this morning that when a meter is clocking up the fare it is of great interest to the customer that the driver has sufficient topographical knowledge to ensure that the taxicab travels from A to B as efficaciously as possible.

That does not apply to private hire vehicles. So it might be argued that a mandatory requirement for topographical knowledge is redundant for drivers of such vehicles. We must consider the extent to which we wish to continue differentiating in our minds, in statutes and in regulations the businesses of taxicabs and of private hire vehicles. It is not persuasive to argue that as taxicab drivers are proud of having the knowledge, it should be required of minicab drivers. We are discussing two different products, with differently structured fares, so that argument has no great strength.

In any case, my main contention is that it is not relevant to replicate the London knowledge of taxicabs so beloved by us all in the private hire vehicle trade. Mandatory topographical knowledge should not be replicated. That is why amendment No. 81 would seek to remove the reference to London, substituting a more restrictive requirement that would be based on the area on which minicab drivers propose to work.

I am trying to strike a balance that would reassure customers that drivers have reasonable knowledge of the principal area in which they operate. Of course, I accept that a lot of private hire vehicles' trade is out of that immediate area. One could take a more relaxed view of such trade, especially of the long haul trips to which knowledge of London as a whole might not be relevant.

The wording of amendment No. 81 provides a balanced judgment. Knowledge that private hire vehicles are required to have should relate only to the area in which they propose to work and should not replicate the London knowledge of taxicab drivers. The hon. Member for Ilford, North reinforced that point when she quoted from a survey that asked punters whether they wanted drivers to know a bit about the area they worked in. Everyone who responded to that question said surprise, surprise yes. I am sure that I heard her say that the key phrase was "local knowledge". So it seems as if that survey supports my point: customers, being ever sensible, recognise a difference between what they expect of private hire vehicle drivers and that they expect of taxicab drivers.

New clause 1

Hirings accepted on behalf of another operator

    ". (1) A London PHV operator (`the first operator') who has in London accepted a private hire booking may not arrange for another operator to provide a vehicle to carry out that booking as sub-contractor unless

    (a) the other operator is a London PHV operator and the sub-contracted booking is accepted at an operating centre in London;

    (b) the other operator is licensed under section 55 of the Local Government (Miscellaneous Provisions) Act 1976 (in this Act referred to as `the 1976 Act') by the council of a district and the sub-contracted booking is accepted in that district; or

    (c) the other operator accepts the sub-contracted booking in Scotland.

    (2) A London PHV operator who contravenes subsection (1) is guilty of an offence and liable on summary conviction to a fine not exceeding level 3 on the standard scale.

    (3) It is a defence in proceedings for an offence under this section for an operator to show that he exercised all due diligence to avoid committing such an offence.

    (4) It is immaterial for the purposes of subsection (1) whether or not subcontracting is permitted by the contract between the first operator and the person who made the booking.

    (5) For the avoidance of doubt (and subject to any relevant contract terms), a contract of hire between a person who made a private hire booking at an operating centre in London and the London PHV operator who accepted the booking remains in force despite the making of arrangements by that operator for another contractor to provide a vehicle to carry out that booking as sub-contractor.".
