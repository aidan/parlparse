New clause 1

Duties regarding availability of food

    `In providing advice under sections 6 and 7 the Agency must take into account the overriding importance of not restricting the availability of food to the consumer and that restriction of the availability of food should only take place when it remains the only solution.'. --[Mrs. Spelman.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mrs. Caroline Spelman (Meriden): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The purpose of the new clause is stop the unnecessary restriction of food availability to the consumer. Given that we are in Committee the day after the beef ban was lifted, it is right to place on the record the fact that we very much welcome that announcement. It is excellent news for industry. In the spirit of constructive opposition, we seek earnestly the recovery of the lost market to our livestock industry. I am sure that that is in everyone's best interests, and we hope that the Government will be ambitious for the industry in terms of its targets for restoring that market. That will be vital to many small and large producers who suffered immensely from the ban.

The lifting of the beef ban is an interesting backdrop to the new clause. The legacy of several food scares that have dogged the food industry over several decades has resulted in almost hysterical media reaction each time the safety of a food product is put into question. It is unrealistic to suppose that no such cases will arise in future, and the new clause is concerned with how the agency will cope with such instances. We want to encourage it to think of alternative options before resorting to the last solution of restricting the availability of food.

It is, however, right and proper that the agency should have the power to restrict food, because circumstances may arise when that is necessary. It is possible to imagine a scenario in which the science is unclear. That was true in the early stages of the BSE crisis; the science was not certain at the outset and, to this day, it remains unclear. Non-scientists sometimes place an unfair burden on Government scientists by asking them to give black-and-white, clear and certain answers. We know from the handling of previous food scares that that is not always easy. The agency will have to cope with scientific uncertainty when taking such decisions. However, the risk to public health may sometimes be considered insufficient to warrant restricting the availability of food.

It was instructive, during the most recent scare caused by Belgian food products that might have been contaminated with dioxins, that the Government should have issued a food hazard warning before taking the ultimate step of banning poultry, eggs and other food products from Belgium. The agency could consider issuing food hazard warnings before going the whole hog of limiting the availability of a food. In practical terms, however, as soon as official warnings are given, food retailers usually take swift action to remove products from the shelves in the interests of customer confidence.

It is important for the agency to consider carefully the practicalities of restricting the availability of foods. If the agency is over-cautious, public confidence in it will be undermined; if it is over-casual, however, it will be criticised for failing to protect public health. We understand the agency's dilemma, but it is important to get that balance right.

If the agency is urged to consider alternatives before reducing the availability of foods, it is more likely to carry out its prescribed general function under clause 7 of providing information and advice to the public on matters that could affect their capacity to make informed decisions about food. If the onus is placed on the agency to inform consumers and allow them to decide --if that becomes a strong pattern in its method of operating --it will be less likely to restrict the availability of food prematurely. That would be a grown-up way of treating consumers, who have had to live through a series of food scares about fats, eggs, poultry, cheese and meat --to name but a few.

British consumers have an almost stoic attitude; they believe that if they took much notice of the food scares that often circulate in the media, they would not eat anything. They have learned to live with those scares and to use their judgment about the risk to which they are exposed. Their ability to do that would be greatly enhanced by the provision of good, clear guidance and information by the agency on the relative risks involved. If the agency gains a reputation for giving sound, readily intelligible advice rather than for over-zealous decision-making on behalf of consumers, it will be more respected.

There are, of course, commercial reasons for taking a rather conservative approach to restricting the availability of food. Although I do not imply that commercial considerations should be anything other than a secondary reason --or even a third or fourth reason --for restricting food only as a last resort, restricting food prematurely can cause food businesses to lose large sums of money, as perishable stock is wasted and doubts about the safety of the product linger long after the Government have given the all-clear. Recent history has many examples of large sums of money being lost --for example, in the egg and meat industries --through a disproportionate fear of the risk attached to such foods.

The coupling of the provision of advice with a simple risk assessment would help the consumer to trust information given and decisions made by the agency. We debated risk assessment in considerable detail, and I do not intend to repeat any of that. We made several suggestions about how risk assessment might be made transparent and attached to the agency's decisions.

Risk assessment is integral if we are to find other solutions to the simple banning of foods. For that reason, we urge the Government to consider the new clause. The ethos of the agency should primarily be to inform and aid the consumer to make well-informed decisions about the risks attached to eating different foods, rather than to nanny consumers by making decisions for them or making decisions to restrict foods prematurely, unwisely, or in a way that might undermine public confidence in the agency.

 

Ms Jowell: I am delighted that constructive opposition now means congratulation for the Government, especially in relation to the successful negotiation of the lifting of the beef ban.

I listened carefully to the hon. Lady's remarks. I would like to respond to several perfectly sensible and probing questions that she posed, although we cannot accept the new clause because we already state in the Bill the clear, overriding objectives of the agency, which are:

    ``to protect public health from risks which may arise in connection with the consumption of food (including risks caused by the way in which it is produced or supplied) and otherwise to protect the interests of consumers in relation to food.''

The hon. Lady remarked on the nature of scientific uncertainty, which is an important subject. We dealt fully with issues in relation to risk and the associated status of scientific uncertainty when we debated that heated subject. The Committee heard arguments about the importance of creating a public climate in which people are able to understand and accept that not all advice can be certain. Advice is information based on the best available research at the time.

There are important ways in which the agency will contribute to improving the present position. One practical way will be to create a greater and better public awareness about the status of scientific research projects, some of which are better than others and some of which are better reported than others. I hope that we will introduce guidance for the public on how to interpret scientific research and see through some of the more inflammatory headlines.

The agency will operate in a climate in which public faith, confidence and certainty is extremely fragile. There is an important job to do in beginning to rebuild confidence in food safety, and improved public information will be an important step in that process.

It is absolutely key that the agency should act systematically and proportionately, paying due attention to the risks of taking or not taking action to protect the public, and leaving the public free to make their own decisions. As we have often said in our detailed scrutiny of the Bill, the agency's approach to such areas will be absolutely key to its reputation for reasonable and proportionate action and in building a consensus about the new approach to improving food safety in practice and engaging the public in judgments about food safety.

The new framework of risk, which we have discussed at some length and which is set out in some detail in relation to public health in the White Paper ``Saving Lives: Our Healthier Nation'', is a practical way forward in which the public will be engaged. We all accept that the agency will operate in a climate in which it will have to steer a steady course between the faddists at one extreme and those who would advocate minimal or no intervention at the other. The test of its capacity to deliver on its objectives and guiding principles will be whether it succeeds in doing that.

I have reiterated to the Committee the mission statement under which the agency will operate, but in safeguarding the public, due consideration of proportionate means and risk may sometimes mean that foods must be made temporarily or permanently unavailable. Mrs. Spelman: I have listened carefully to the Minister's reply, but I cannot understand how the agency's primary function --to protect public health --is incompatible with the purpose of the new clause. There is no conflict, so it does not cancel out our desire to shape the agency's culture so that it does not resort to banning foods too readily.

We want the Bill to state that that should be a last solution and we want the agency to consider all options before resorting to such a draconian measure. The new clause is based on a desire to prevent the agency from becoming too dictatorial. We shall therefore have to press it to a vote.

Duties regarding imported food
