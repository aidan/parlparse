Mr. Gibb: The clause seeks to end the use of quarter-end leasing companies, although it does not directly outlaw their use. The concept of quarter-end leasing companies is that a financing group or bank establishes a series of subsidiary companies--either quarter-end leasing companies with a year end of March, June, September and December or, if it goes one stage further, 12 year-end companies to cover every month, from January to December. A leasing company buys the asset on the customer's behalf by borrowing the money from its parent company--usually a bank or finance company--and rents or lends the asset to the customer in return for a rental income. That means that the leasing company has a stream of rental income and interest expense, and a tax allowance for the depreciation of capital allowances at 25 per cent. As a result, most leasing companies at the start of finance leases have a large taxable loss. That loss eventually unwinds as the lease goes through its term.

That loss is group relieved by the parent company against its profits and, therefore, the group as a whole obtains a benefit from the capital allowances. Leasing is often used because the customer has no capacity to use capital allowances. Either the customer is loss making and, therefore, claiming capital allowances would merely add to its losses and would not generate a benefit, or the customer does not pay taxes and so cannot take advantage of capital allowances. If the capital allowances remain with the leasing and banking company, their advantages can be passed on to the customer through a lower rental stream than if the company had merely bought an asset and rented it to the customer.

There are two principal benefits of using quarter-end leasing companies: first, whoever buys an asset half way through the year obtains an allowance for the whole year. The clause aims to get rid of that benefit. For example, the leasing company would have an asset bought in March purchased by the subsidiary leasing company with a March year-end. That March leasing company would obtain capital allowances for the full year until 31 March, notwithstanding the fact that the asset was only purchased in the last month of the year.

Secondly, there is a group-relieved advantage in having four quarter year-end companies in that the coterminous accounting period rules come into play. That means that the group will benefit from that group relief earlier than if the assets and tax relief had been obtained in the banking and leasing company itself.

I want to give an example of how that system works in practice. If a banking and leasing group with a 31 December 1996 year end had a subsidiary leasing company with a 31 March year end, and an asset was purchased by that company on 30 March, that leasing company would generate a loss in the year ending 31 March 1997. Three quarters of that loss--representing nine of the 12 months--would be offset against the profits of the banking group for the year ending 31 December 1996. The tax would be payable nine months after that date, on 1 October 1997, only six months after the leasing company had purchased the asset.

In effect, relief is obtained on the purchase only six months after the purchase, which is considerably earlier than would normally be the case. Normally, a company only pays its tax nine months after the year end--assuming, of course, that the assets were purchased right at the end of the year. The advantages of that system were referred to by the hon. Member for Kingston and Surbiton--the current quarter-end regimes mean that companies obtain relief, on average, after ten and a half months, whereas the clause will extend that period, on average, to 21 months. That is a considerable reduction in the time between expenditure on an asset and obtaining relief for tax purposes.

Leasing enables companies with losses to recover benefit from capital allowances that they would not otherwise have, and it allows those losses to be passed on through lower rentals. Another advantage, commercially, of leasing as opposed to outright ownership of an asset is the fact that the risks of ownership remain with the lessor. A maintenance or repair contract is often tacked on to a leasing contract. The classic example is that of care leases--a monthly rental of £300 or £400 a month is paid, and all the servicing arrangements and all other matters are brought into one contractual arrangement with the lessees.

The clause will end what the Inland Revenue regards as a loophole. A loophole is involved--technically--but it has been in existence for many years. The arrangements that leasing companies follow have been in existence for some time and a whole industry has built up around the ability to claim capital allowances for the whole year although an asset may be purchased only towards the end of that year. Indeed, the leasing industry arose from the fact that there were high capital allowances--of 100 per cent. in some instances--and high tax rates during the 1970s, which meant that many manufacturing companies had large taxable losses. They were making profits for accounting purposes, but they had 100 per cent. or 75 per cent. write-offs on their plant and machinery. That meant that for tax purposes, companies--especially manufacturing companies that purchased large quantities of plant and machinery--would often have large tax losses, which they would carry forward, or accumulate, from one year to the next. The leasing industry sprang up because the banks saw that large profits were being made on which tax was paid and that the manufacturing companies had large tax losses.

The leasing industry mushroomed because some clever person worked out that the benefit of those capital allowances could be shifted from the manufacturing companies to the banks through leasing. Since then, the leasing industry has become part and parcel of commercial activity in this country. Seeking to close that loophole is probably, on balance, a good thing, but it is not the sort of loophole that gives me any real cause for concern--it does not result in a genuine leakage of tax; it merely involves cash flow. However, the Government and industry see the lack of cash flow as a genuine cost because it has to be financed by borrowing, either through gilts, if the Government borrow it, or through commercial debt, if the private sector borrows it.

The clause will reduce the benefits that leasing companies have long expected and long enjoyed, and rentals will rise as a result because the margins in the leasing industry are fine, and the sector is competitive. New and non-profitable companies tend to be the ones that take advantage of finance leases. A profitable company can get better borrowing rates from a bank than are obtainable from a finance leasing company and can then buy assets and obtain capital allowances. That said, one great advantage of leasing companies is that their loans are more secure than simple loans. The lender--the lessor--retains ownership of the assets and that is an attractive feature of leasing companies.

Ending use of quarter-end leasing companies will damage the very companies that the Government claim to be trying to support in other Budget measures. Small and medium-sized companies and companies that are still expanding--and therefore making losses in their early years--are most likely to use leasing companies to acquire capital assets. The very companies that the Government help in clauses 42 and 43 are being wiped out by clause 44. That is another example of the Government giving with one hand and taking away with the other.

The Financial Secretary said that the playing field was not being tilted away from the finance leasing industry, but the clause does not change hire purchase rules, which allow ownership to go to the user of an asset straight away. The asset user is the technical owner and can claim capital allowances immediately and for the whole year, regardless of when, in an accounting period, the assets were purchased.

As a result of clause 44, if a company obtains an asset through finance leasing a month or three months before the year end, the finance lessor will obtain writing-down allowances equal only to the remaining part of the accounting period. For example, a leasing company with a March year-end that purchased an asset in February could claim only one twelfth of the 25 per cent. writing down allowance on the cost of that asset. But if the company was using the asset or had obtained it by hire purchase, it could obtain the full 25 per cent. no matter when it was purchased. Clearly an unfair advantage is being given to hire purchase arrangements, and direct ownership and leasing companies are being disadvantaged.

The Finance and Leasing Association believes that that will damage the industry. In a letter to the Financial Secretary, the director general states:

    ``It . . . makes finance leasing fundamentally uncompetitive with straight purchase or hire purchase by profitable companies.''

He goes on to say that exclusion of all leased assets from double first-year allowances will have similar effects.

The Finance Bill brings in great new benefits in clauses 42 and 43, in the shape of 50 per cent. first-year allowances for small and medium-sized companies with £11 million turnover, assets of £5.6 million or 215 employees. But those are the kind of companies that generally use finance leasing. The Government have gone to the trouble of giving such companies a special one-year period in which to obtain a double first-year allowance, while penalising them by making acquisition of those assets difficult; leasing companies are specifically excluded from obtaining double first-year allowances.

Another £14 million would be spent on distribution and cinema improvement. Lottery funding for film in the UK could exceed £100 million, provided that the film sector matched the funding. That represents an injection of more than £200 million into the British film industry. That does not take account of the Scottish film production fund, which offers financial support to those engaged in producing films that are short, unlike this debate, and in developing feature films.

As has been said, the British film industry is in a rosy state. Some may ask why we single it out for special treatment instead of other worthy charitable causes, against which parts of the Bill discriminate. Why do not the Scottish whisky industry, which the Economic Secretary might mention, or the English wine industry, on behalf of which I made a special plea, receive special treatment? I declare another interest: I was involved in film about the English wine industry. It was produced by the late Willy Rushton, another great bon viveur who enjoyed English wine.

The definition of a British film is flawed. The Bill does not challenge the definition of what qualifies as a British film in the Films Act 1985. The panel would challenge the premise for qualification. Like the Paymaster General, I shall not go over that ground in detail, although doing that would be worth while. If we had more time, and were not subject to a guillotine, we could do that.

Another problem relates to the changes in FIDs. Many UK multinational film production and media companies are large overseas earners, which are at risk form the loss of FIDs. They may be tempted away from being UK-registered companies, which would not qualify for some of the funding. I have already mentioned two large companies--Carlton Communications and Granada.

Before the safety curtain falls, I shall conclude by saying that the clause smacks of tokenism. It does nothing to solve the strategic structural problems of the British film industry. It is out of tune with the advisory committee's detailed findings. It fails to deal with the criteria for qualifying as a British-made film. It sets a precedent of subsidising a small part of British business without considering other special cases. We should avoid over-nationalism, such as that of the French film industry. In 1993, the Academie Franccaise certified that 55 per cent. of films shown in French cinemas had to be homegrown efforts. That is why not many people go to see them. That problem could arise in this country.

The Government should do their homework better. The clause is a good first attempt, with the right thrust, but it is also an appalling example of luvvy tokenism.
