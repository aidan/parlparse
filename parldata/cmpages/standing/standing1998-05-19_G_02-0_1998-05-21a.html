Amendment No. 1 would narrow the enormous scope of the principal clause. We shall provide evidence during the debate to show how its wide-ranging scope and powers need to be narrowed. I am sure that the Committee has grasped the fact that clause 2 covers one of the two central powers of the Bill. We must explore why it is so wide ranging. It should be narrowed to make the transition to and impact of the Bill more manageable for business and more sensible for customers in the marketplace.

My hon. Friend the Member for West Dorset (Mr. Letwin) will focus on the impact that our amendment, if successful, would have on reducing the substantial compliance costs that the Bill would impose on British business. We disagree with the Government's forecast for compliance costs; we believe that they will be many times higher than the amount that the Government have specified. One of the principal strengths of amendment No. 1 is that it would greatly reduce those prospective compliance costs, but I shall leave it to my hon. Friend to make that case in his excellent way.

We shall highlight the effect that the unamended Bill will have on the retail pharmacy sector. That industry is a good example of the dramatic impact that the Bill will have on small and valued local businesses throughout the country. My hon. Friends the Members for Buckingham (Mr. Bercow) and for South Cambridgeshire (Mr. Lansley) will concentrate their remarks on the consequences of the clause for retail pharmacies. They will support my amendment, which, if successful, would narrow the clause's impact on a range of businesses.

Amendment No. 1 would add a new subsection (9) to clause 2 and would limit preceding powers in the clause. As I have said, the clause is wide ranging. Under subsection (1),

    ``agreements between undertakings, decisions by associations of undertakings, or concerted practices which . . . may affect trade within the United Kingdom, and . . . have as their object or effect the prevention, restriction or distortion of competition . . . are prohibited.''

That is an entirely new departure in British law.

Under current competition law, which many businesses think is quite tough enough, one is allowed to carry on with an existing business or practice unless or until the competition authorities have investigated it and found it to be unreasonable; in which case, one is asked to desist from that conduct. Under current legislation, businesses that are worried about their practices have the option to register those practices as registered agreements, which gives them the comfort of knowing that proceedings will not be taken against them.

The burden will shift dramatically under the new regime. Businesses will have to satisfy themselves that nothing that they do could be construed as being anti-competitive. If they make a genuine mistake or error they can be prosecuted by the new competition authority and fined up to 10 per cent. of turnover for the infringement. The new law would therefore have a strong element of retrospection; it would impose much tougher penalties; and it would threaten far too many business practices--practices that might be perfectly reasonable under the new regime and which are perfectly reasonable today.

Amendment No. 1 attempts to identify a limited series of practices that are normal in British business and are deemed to be legal and satisfactory by the current competition authorities and about which the Bill should give reassurance. It is not an exhaustive list--I am sure that Members of the Committee can think of practices that should be added to it--but it is offered to the Minister, if he is listening, in the spirit of helping the Government to reduce disagreement with the business community and of helping to concentrate the Government's attack on those anti-competitive practices that they most dislike. If the Minister says that the prime purpose of the Bill is to ensure a more competitive bus industry, for example, and to prevent unreasonable concentration in local and regional markets in that industry, I would welcome that; I would ask for an explanation of how it would work, but if I were satisified that those were the consequences, I would accept it. However, if the bill is not amended, the Minister risks opening up to court action or cessation a series of business practices that he might, on reflection, prefer to continue.

Amendment No. 1 would apply to companies that share a licensed technology in whose development and promotion they are involved. Such an arrangement should be deemed, at least for a period, as pro-competition, rather than anti-competition. A company that is involved in developing new technology and ideas will want to enjoy a period in which it has the right to earn the reward for its investment and better thinking before there is a free-for-all in the marketplace. That is accepted in patent law, but I am not sure that the Bill meshes sensibly with that. An exemption should be made in the clause. The Minister may, having taken official advice, propose better words than I have crafted. It is important to specify an exemption for companies that spend a great deal of money on developing new technology, which would not otherwise be available to future generations. Sensible management of the market is normal practice and might be required to allow companies to develop their ideas and to ensure that they are produced in the United Kingdom, rather than being taken up by others elsewhere, where regimes differ.

The second suggestion in amendment No. 1, which would become new subsection 9(b), is to make it clear that clauses on price discrimination do not prevent the normal business practice of offering larger discounts for larger custom. It would be devastating for the Government if they could no longer enjoy bulk discounts on their purchases, on the grounds that to do so would unreasonably discriminate between categories of customer on the basis of size.

I hope that the Minister will say that he believes that such a protection is unnecessary and that the Bill, as drafted, will not jeopardise discounts to larger purchasers. If he does, that will be progress: our proceedings may help businesses when they judge what risks they run, and the Minister's words could, ultimately, be used as evidence in a court of law.

However, even if the Minister can offer that reassurance, it would cost him little to reinforce it--perhaps in words of his own choosing; again, I suspect that Government lawyers can better draft the amendment than I, as a non-lawyer can--because that would show business that the Bill does not constitute a threat to larger discount schemes, which are commonly used in business.

The third protection is in new subsection (9)(c). It is not clear in the Bill where a promotional discount ends and where predatory pricing begins. I am sure that the Minister and I would agree that predatory pricing is an evil. I trust that we would also agree that it is illegal under the current regime. The Minister may tell us that the Bill will make it even more difficult for businesses to get away with predatory pricing, and that it will be even easier to prosecute under the new legislation than under the old. I would be prepared to listen with an open mind to such a case.

If that is the Government's case, it is even more important that in the Bill and in the Minister's words, business is offered some reassurance that the common practice of promotional discounts is not under threat. Friend the Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell), has often made to me. I hope that the Minister will say something helpful about promotional discounts and on pricing under duress and stress when low prices and losses are clear evidence of an all-too-competitive marketplace--if there can be such a thing--rather than evidence of predatory pricing and unreasonable conduct.
