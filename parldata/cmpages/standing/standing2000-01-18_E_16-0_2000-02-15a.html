Mr. Jenkin: This morning is a time for refreshment and reinvigoration of our enthusiasm for the Bill. We now move on to what local authorities may do to promote public transport and transport in general in their areas.

Clause 92 sets out what is required of a local authority in drafting a local transport plan. We have a fundamental objection to the principle behind the clause in that it provides yet another imposition on local authorities. I sense that hon. Members on the Government Front Bench have not yet settled down to this morning's meeting. They are beginning to cough and splutter up their sleeves--if that is an expression one may use. When I talk about an additional imposition on local authorities I am, of course, talking about extra expense and bureaucracy--things beloved of new Labour. They have added £1 billion to the cost of running Whitehall with extra civil servants, political advisers, spin doctors and endless streams of paper in reports. They believe that reports and studies are a substitute for action and they want to impose that philosophy on local authorities. One would think that local authorities had never thought about transport before in their lives, such is the arrogance contained in the clause.

Local authorities spend considerable time considering transport, but now they will have to put such issues within a framework that is laid down by the Government and dictated by statute--a framework to which all local authorities will have to conform. Our objective is to make those plans voluntary, however laudable and constructive they may be. They should be not an obligation on local authorities, but rather an option for them to pursue. We believe in decentralising powers to local authorities, not centralising them on Westminster.

Since the election, all the legislation dealing with local authorities--in particular the Local Government Act 1999--and best value, has increased the controls on authorities. The Government say that they are sweeping away controls and getting rid of competitive tendering. In fact, whereas a limited number of services were involved in competitive tendering, now every service in local government is involved with best value and is subject ultimately to direction by the Secretary of State. The clause is about subjecting local transport plans to the direction of the Secretary of State.

The integrated transport White Paper made it clear that local transport plans must support the objectives of Government policy and that grants must be distributed to authorities on the basis of how well their plans conform to the Government's idea of a local transport plan. If policies that the Government do not like would be pursued under a plan, they would clearly not support it with the funding required. No doubt we shall return to the funding of local transport. By replacing "must" with "may" at appropriate points in the clause and elsewhere in this part of the Bill, the arrangements will be made voluntary instead of compulsory, which will be a victory for local democracy.

Under such a scheme, the local authority determines in detail what local services should be provided in the area concerned, and to what standard, and lets contracts with bus operators to secure those services.
