Clause 1

Meaning of ``stakeholder pension scheme''

Amendment proposed (2 March): No. 1, in page 1, to leave out lines 7 to 11 and to insert the words—

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 7, in Clause 2, page 2, leave out lines 31 to 33.

The Minister of State, Department of Social Security (Mr. Stephen Timms): The hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford (Mr. Davies) ranged widely in his opening comments on the amendments and drew in a number of points on subsequent amendments. It may be appropriate if I respond to some of those points, although I know that a number of them will recur when we come to later amendments.

I shall deal first with the hon. Gentleman's references to the recent proposal for increased flexibility in pension investment. There is no divide between the Department of Social Security and the Treasury on so-called LISAs. The consultation paper is clearly badged as a joint production of the two Departments. The aim is to improve pension provision and to ensure that better pension options are available to those who are able to save for their retirement. The proposed framework for stakeholder pension schemes will achieve that aim. The new flexible vehicle in the document will help by providing a new way in which to invest money in pensions.

The new arrangements will be an option for all types of pension scheme, including stakeholder pension schemes. The features of the investments in that arrangement will promote flexibility and transparency in any scheme based on it. The arrangements do not rival stakeholder pension schemes. They are not pension schemes at all, but can be used by pension schemes as a way of investing their members' money in stakeholder and other pensions.

The hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) anticipated my response, but implied that we are missing an opportunity by treating this new development as a relaxation of the investment rules rather than a new sort of pension. No such opportunity is being missed. The advantages of flexible investment that the hon. Gentleman wants in LISAs will be available in personal pensions and, indeed, stakeholder pensions, which use this new investment approach.

The hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford made much of the fact that the new investment proposal is not mentioned in the Bill. That is so, but the consultation paper makes it clear that the arrangements can be established by secondary legislation and that no primary legislation will be needed to authorise the new mechanism.

Our proposals for stakeholder pension schemes go much wider than the investment mechanism. In particular, it is crucial that schemes should be governed in a way that ensures that members' interests are protected. That is what clause 1 requires. A board of trustees is one long-established, well-understood way of achieving that, but we recognise, as we said in the Green Paper, that other structures could provide similar protection. Clause 1 also allows other forms of scheme governance to be approved. The Green Paper clearly limits alternative proposals and that is the basis on which we are proceeding. We shall not sell the members of stakeholder pension schemes short. The experience of personal pensions should be sufficient to show that additional protections are needed to ensure that savers get a good deal. We cannot allow a re-run of the mis-selling debacle. Any alternative to the trust structure will need to provide scheme members with comparable protection.

The hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford gave the impression that the Bill's proposals will force members of existing pension schemes to give them up. There is nothing in the proposals that will have that effect. There will be no requirement on employees to join a stakeholder pension scheme. Anyone who is in a personal pension will be able to stay in it. In many cases, it will be in their best interests to do so.

We are saying that employers should make access to a stakeholder pension available to any employees who cannot join an occupational scheme. That will lead to a major improvement in access to pensions. It should not be a significant burden on employers. There is no requirement on employers to set up and run schemes themselves. They simply need to identify a scheme that their employees can join and pass contributions from the employee's pay packet if the employee wishes them to do so. No employer would be in breach of the requirement if any of his employees opted to stay in an existing personal pension—doing so will be fine.

As to whether a personal pension can count as a stakeholder scheme, we have set out in the clause several conditions that stakeholder schemes should meet. There is nothing in those conditions that will stop personal pensions from being adapted so that they meet the conditions. I have no doubt that several providers will do exactly that.

The hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford criticised us for producing the Bill before the end of the consultation period on the Green Paper. As I explained in our previous sitting, there has already been extensive consultation on the idea of stakeholder pension schemes. In the Green Paper we are consulting not on the proposition itself but on the details of the way in which the schemes should be set up. We shall set out further details in secondary legislation. I assure all Committee members that when we draw up the detailed regulations that are consequential on the Bill we shall consult further.

Mr. Timms: As I said during our previous sitting, we shall issue—I imagine after Easter—a series of consultation documents about the detailed secondary legislation. Those documents will not be available much before the end of our Committee proceedings and the draft regulations certainly will not be available before then. I am afraid that I cannot give the hon. Gentleman the assurance that he seeks. However, a similar approach was adopted with the Pensions Act 1998. I believe that it worked satisfactorily with that Act, and I am confident that it will do so on this occasion as well.

I must return to the detail of amendments Nos. 1 and 7. The main effect of amendment No. 1 would be to prevent the addition of any further qualifying conditions for stakeholder pension schemes, beyond those already in the Bill. Consequential to that, amendment No. 7 would remove the requirement for schemes to declare that they have complied with such conditions.

The proposals to set minimum standards for stakeholder pension schemes have been widely welcomed. They will provide an important safeguard to members of the scheme, and they will help to encourage the many people who are wary of pensions to begin saving for their retirement. We have to rebuild trust in pensions and the Bill's safeguards will allow us to fulfil that extremely important task.

The clause sets out eight conditions in total which all stakeholder pension schemes will have to meet. They are the only conditions that we currently expect to apply. However, it would be supreme over-confidence to imagine that we have come up with a list that will never need to be added to. Committee members will appreciate that the financial sector changes rapidly, as does technology. It is entirely possible that we will need to change the conditions for stakeholder pension schemes in line with other developments.

We shall want to monitor stakeholder pension schemes when they start operating. Experience of the operation of the schemes may tell us that new conditions are needed.

We shall consult the pensions industry carefully as we set out the details of the conditions for stakeholder schemes. Those consultations could identify areas where we might want to add new conditions. The amendment would considerably restrict the Government's ability to respond quickly, by requiring that any new condition could be added only by new primary legislation. That would mean that we would risk the requirements for stakeholder pension schemes falling out of date, which would not be sensible. In tabling the amendment, Opposition Members may have been seeking to clarify what further conditions we have in mind.

Mrs. Jacqui Lait (Beckenham): I think that the Minister realises from the thrust of the amendments that we are looking for more flexibility. He refers to the need to create new conditions. Will those conditions include a less restrictive approach, and does he envisage getting rid of some conditions because of the changing market and the way in which new instruments have developed?

 

Mr. Timms: To provide reassurance for investors in stakeholder pensions, we need the conditions that are proposed in the clause. The hon. Lady makes the point that the amendment seeks to increase flexibility. However, it would seriously reduce flexibility because it would mean that we could do nothing else until we had a new Bill. Given the way in which those things are developing, that would not be a sensible approach. We want to stick with the eight conditions set out in the Bill, as they provide an essential minimum set of standards that will provide people with the necessary reassurance and confidence in pensions—a confidence that has been lost because of events such as mis-selling in recent years.

The clause could be used to specify some types of investment in which stakeholder pension funds could be held. It could also require schemes to hold records in a common technological format in order, for example, to assist data transfer.

Under amendment No. 1, schemes would not have to register with the Occupational Pensions Regulatory Authority to qualify as stakeholder pension schemes. I shall explain the reason why registration with OPRA is important. Stakeholder pension schemes will be required to apply for registration. To do so, they will have to give a positive declaration that they meet all the standards that apply. We are not proposing that OPRA should investigate each and every registration. In most cases, the declaration will be sufficient. Requiring schemes to make a positive declaration to OPRA will ensure that the Government and the authority know what schemes are purporting to be stakeholder pension schemes, and OPRA will be in a position to take swift, effective action if it becomes clear that the standards that we are aiming for are not being met.

We make it clear in the Green Paper that OPRA already has considerable experience in regulating the conduct of occupational pension schemes. OPRA will be able to regulate the conduct of trustees in stakeholder pension schemes in broadly the same way, dealing with reported problems as they arise. The register will also help employers to provide their employees with access to a stakeholder scheme. A comprehensive register will list all the stakeholder pension schemes available, without which the task of identifying a suitable scheme would be much more difficult. Difficulty inevitably adds costs, which places an additional burden on employers. I am sure that every member of the Committee would wish to avoid that.

The amendments would remove an important safeguard, and, to pick up the point made by the hon. Member for Beckenham (Mrs. Lait), an important flexibility to alter the conditions for stakeholder pension schemes. I hope that in light of that explanation and those assurances the hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford will withdraw the amendment.

The Minister was less than frank when he said that the Bill will not force employees to give up personal pension schemes or companies to give up schemes which they provide for their employees. Strictly speaking, that is correct. Companies can continue to provide a group personal pension arrangement for employees who choose it, as well as providing a stakeholder facility. Deduction from payroll is the essential distinction between the stakeholder pension scheme and previous schemes, and the Opposition are not opposed to that in principle. The employer's obligation to provide a stakeholder pension can therefore be fulfilled in parallel with any previous scheme.

The Minister knows, and might as well admit, that employers will be faced with the considerable new administrative burden of fulfilling their obligations under such legislation. A real danger exists—which the Government did not address when I drew attention to it on Second Reading, and which the Minister has not addressed this afternoon—that employers will say that they cannot administer several schemes. The tragedy is that the stakeholder provisions in the Bill do not require the employer to make any contribution other than providing the facility—he does not have to make any contribution whatever to provisions for the retirement of his employees. But the overwhelming majority of group personal pension schemes currently include an employer contribution. An employer who is exasperated by the Government forcing him to take on an additional administrative burden or to unwind his present arrangements might say to himself, ``I will do the minimum the Government require, and just provide a stakeholder pension''. That will severely disadvantage employees, who will lose the employer contributions that they currently enjoy. Alternatively, an employer might provide the group personal pension scheme to existing employees only, and new employees will have to contribute to the stakeholder scheme or get nothing. Existing and new employees would be substantially disadvantaged in both cases. That will not be a good day's work for pensioners or for aggregate pension provision in this country.

The Minister should acknowledge that although employers will not be forced to give something up, they will be forced to provide something new. It is my strong natural inclination to argue in principle that it is not for Governments to fix prices or charges. As we do not intend to put the amendment to a vote, I shall not argue differently today. It is not sensible for Governments to fix the price of electricity, beer, bus fares or rents. It has been tried in the past, usually by Labour Governments, with unhappy results. It has been tried elsewhere in the world with even more unhappy results—some sensational cases will come to all our minds.

In an efficient, competitive market, the rate of profit should be no more and no less than the market rate. If it were less than the market rate, no one would invest in the service, the service would cease to be provided and the volume of supply would fall until the point at which the price rises because the demand remains high. Alternatively, if the rate of profit were in excess of the market rate, competition would be encouraged, supply would increase to meet a given amount of demand, and the price would fall. Some markets may not be competitive—on the whole, financial services in this country are competitive. It is right for the state to take action if a market is not competitive, but it should do so through regulation and competition policy, not through price fixing.
