The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are discussing new clause 1—Comptroller and Auditor General: right of access to information—

    ` .—(1) This section applies for the purposes of the examination by the Comptroller and Auditor General of the accounts of government departments and other bodies under or by virtue of—

    (a) this Act, or

    (b) the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1921, or

    (c) any other enactment, or

    (d) any arrangement between such department or body and some other body which provides for the accounts of that body to be subject to examination by the Comptroller and Auditor General.

    (2) The Comptroller and Auditor General—

    (a) shall have a right of access at all reasonable times to all accounting records and other documents relating to—

    (i) such accounts, or

    (ii) separately, any money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant, and

    (iii) any payments funded by money that would otherwise be payable into the Consolidated Fund.

    (b) shall be entitled to require from any person holding or accountable for any such document any assistance, information or explanation which he reasonably requires.

    (3) Without prejudice to any other rights vested in the Comptroller and Auditor General, the rights conferred by subsections (2) and (3) are also exercisable against a receiving body to the same extent as similar rights are exercisable against the receiving body by a paying body.

    (4) Notwithstanding subsection (3), the rights conferred by subsection (2) are not exercisable against any paying body or receiving body the accounts of which are subject to audit under section 2 of the Audit Commission Act 1998 other than a body specified in section 98(1) of the National Health Service Act 1977.

    (5) The following provisions shall cease to have effect—

    (a) section 28 of the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1866 (access to departments' documents);

    (b) section 9(2) of the Exchequer and Audit Departments Act 1921 (application of section 28 of 1866 Act).

    (6) ``Paying body'' means any body which pays to a receiving body money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant.

    (7) ``Receiving body'' means any body which receives from a paying body money originating in the Consolidated Fund or Exchequer grant.'.

I start with the nub of the argument, which is the central point of the collision of ideas between members of the PAC and those who brief the Treasury. Our debates keep harking back to 1866, and the Economic Secretary said that we are now dealing with different questions from Gladstone's time. She is utterly wrong, as the Treasury knows. The machinery is different, but the questions remain absolutely the same. The Public Accounts Committee and what is now the National Audit Office were set up at a time of high corruption, profligate waste and public sleaze.

Issues do not go away. All that one can do is try to control them, and if they are not controlled properly, they get out of hand. They cannot be defeated by one good action by the Government or the National Audit Office. It is a continuing battle—essentially, as I hope to demonstrate, the same battle as in Gladstone's time. Gladstone's solution was to set up a monitoring system to scrutinise those who were profligate, wasteful or fraudulent, and to ensure that the public understood what was being done by those who purported to represent them. Such issues in Department after Department are still considered twice a week by the Public Accounts Committee. Depressingly, the same problems often recur in the same Departments.

The Minister believes that we can rely on Departments to get things right. The PAC held a hearing on Forward Catering, which used to provide £3 million of catering throughout the civil service and was about to be privatised. An entrepreneur was running it and we discovered that accounts had gone walkabout in three regions. Ghost workers were employed; they worked in one name in the morning and another in the afternoon to avoid national insurance contributions. The head of that powerful public body, with his deputy and one of their wives and daughters, secretly set up a private company, which made a bid in complete conflict of interest, without notifying his Permanent Secretary and accounting officer. Therefore, those who tell us not only that the Departments keep an eye on everything, but that an eye is kept on the Departments, had, in their own Department, a happy little group plotting a highly profitable privatisation of a public asset.

Miss Johnson: Before turning to the detail our amendments, I shall explain in general terms how clause 10 will enable us to prepare the whole of Government accounts and why they are so important. Clause 10 places a duty on the Treasury to prepare consolidated accounts for the public sector in order to meet the commitment given in the code for fiscal stability. To enable the Treasury to meet that requirement as efficiently as possible, it allows that Department to designate which public sector bodies will be covered and provides it with powers to request the audited information necessary to prepare the account.

Audited whole of Government accounts will improve the information available and support the conduct and monitoring of fiscal policy. The accounts will also improve accountability to Parliament and provide greater transparency for taxpayers. However, producing full audited whole of Government accounts will require greater conformity across the public sector in accounting policy, systems and procedures. That is a major challenge, so the Government have decided to adopt a staged approach, and will concentrate first on delivering audited accounts covering central Government Departments.
