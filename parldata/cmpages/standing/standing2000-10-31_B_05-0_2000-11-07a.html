No good purpose would be served. The contrary would apply if the Secretary of State were forced to apply to the court for a disqualification order in such circumstances.

The point of clause 6, which gained wide support when we consulted on it, is to take cases out of the court if the director and the Secretary of State agree that a period of disqualification is appropriate. That would help the courts and defendants, and it should result in earlier disqualifications. Where an undertaking has been given, it will be recorded on the register of disqualified directors, which is open to public scrutiny.

If amendment No. 4 was made, it would mean that if the Secretary of State considered that a director's conduct warranted a period of disqualification in excess of four years, he would have to apply to the court for a disqualification order even if the director was prepared to offer an undertaking for that greater period. That would defeat our intention to achieve earlier disqualifications in order to protect the public from unfit directors and to save the courts' time and the time and costs of defendants. I am sure that the Committee will agree that unfit directors should be disqualified as soon as possible. Being able to accept undertakings for an appropriate period, even for 15 years, is an important part of providing protection for the public.

The hon. Member for Christchurch asked whether I thought the provision would increase the number of disqualifications. We do not anticipate that being the case. At present, disqualification proceedings are instituted against all directors identified by the Secretary of State as being unfit and if he considers it appropriate in the public interest to institute proceedings. We do not expect the number of disqualifications to increase once the Bill is in force.

The hon. Gentleman asked a concomitant question about the explanatory notes, which do not refer to the saving mentioned in the regulatory impact assessment. The explanatory notes are not used to identify savings, which should be identified in the regulatory impact assessment; that is what I have done. The section in the explanatory notes that deals with the financial effects of the Bill is intended to point out expenditure that will fall on the Exchequer and it does precisely that.

As to amendments Nos. 5, 7 and 52, we do not believe that a statement of facts should be a precondition to acceptance by the Secretary of State of a disqualification undertaking. It is, after all, intended that the new provisions should allow for the disqualification of directors who agree that they should be disqualified—amazed though my hon. Friend the Member for Great Grimsby (Mr. Mitchell) is that anyone should agree to be disqualified. The Secretary of State will certainly seek to agree on the matter of unfit conduct before accepting an undertaking, in accordance with practice since the Woolf civil justice procedure reforms.

However, for a variety of reasons that may not always be possible. For the Secretary of State to be able to accept an undertaking only when a schedule had been agreed would be unduly restrictive and, in some instances, might run the risk of delaying the establishment of the protection that the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986 is intended to provide. It would be likely also to increase costs if there were difficulty in agreeing the schedule.

Under the new provisions, the Secretary of State could accept an undertaking only if satisfied that the conditions under section 6(1) of the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986 were met. In particular, he would need to be satisfied that the conduct of the director would make him unfit to be concerned in the management of a company. I should have thought that the disqualification of unfit directors as early as possible would be the issue of most importance to the public.

I turn now to amendment No. 6. The court will have discretion as to whether to make any order under new section 8A. We consider it appropriate to leave it to the court to consider what order, if any, to make, even if the application is made in the first two years of any disqualification period. The hon. Member for Christchurch may have overlooked the fact that under section 8 of the Company Directors Disqualification Act 1986, there is no minimum period for a disqualification order or undertaking. Not allowing someone subject to an undertaking under section 8—as it is proposed to amend it—to apply to court within two years of giving an undertaking could result in the disqualification period expiring before an application could be made. I am sure that the hon. Gentleman would agree that that cannot be right.

We do not consider it appropriate that the Secretary of State should, as new clause 1 would provide,

    publish guidelines as to the circumstances in which he may deem it expedient in the public interest that a disqualification order under section 6 shall be made

or a disqualification undertaking accepted. Each case must be considered on its merits and in context, and the extent of the overall unfit conduct should be weighed before a decision is made. That is not a process that could be distilled into guidelines. However, we offer guidance on what would constitute unfit conduct.

It is important to point out what clause 9 would achieve if it remained unamended. The clause amends section 218 of the Insolvency Act 1986, and provides that, in a winding-up, a court may direct the liquidator to report apparent criminal misconduct by past or present company officers or members of the company to the Secretary of State rather than to the Director of Public Prosecutions. It also requires a liquidator in a voluntary winding-up to report suspicions of criminal misconduct by company officers past or present or by members of the company to the Secretary of State rather than the DPP. Lastly, it provides that the Secretary of State may take powers under the Companies Act 1985 when investigating the alleged misconduct. In the case of a winding-up in Scotland, such misconduct would still be reported to the Lord Advocate.

The amendment would simply extend to administrators and administrative receivers the duty to report breaches that is now incorporated in clause 9. I hope that the Minister will either accept the amendment or explain why administrators and administrative receivers are excluded from the provision.

Dr. Howells: The amendments will largely replace the substance of clause 11. That has been done because what would have been new subsection (5) of section 421 of the Insolvency Act 1986 could have had an unfortunate effect. It could have prejudiced the marketability of property where the deceased's interest in jointly owned property passed to the survivor on death, irrespective of whether the deceased was solvent or not. Prospective purchasers of properties that involved an inherited interest could have been deterred from proceeding with a purchase because the deceased's severable share may have been treated as having vested in any trustee subsequently appointed. We would not want a disposal of a property by a surviving partner to be made unnecessarily difficult because prospective purchasers were concerned that they might not have a good title if the deceased's estate was insolvent.

The amendments are intended to address that problem and to ensure that the value of any interest in jointly owned property lost by the operation of the survivorship rules is still to be recoverable for the benefit of the insolvent estate and, therefore, for the creditors of the deceased insolvent. We propose to achieve that by enabling the trustee of the insolvent estate to make an application to the court for a monetary order against the survivor in such cases. The only purpose for which such an order may be made is for securing that debts and other liabilities to which the insolvent estate is subject are met—even if the deceased's interest in the property was worth more.

The scope of the order will be limited to what was lost to the estate when the beneficial interest passed to the joint owner on the debtor's death, even if the sum required to pay what is due in respect of the deceased's estate is larger. In making any monetary order, the court is to have regard to all the circumstances of the case, including the interests of the former joint owner as well as those of the deceased's creditors. However, unless the circumstances are exceptional, the court must assume that the interests of the deceased's creditors outweigh all other considerations. Additionally, we propose that such an order cannot be made if the petition for the insolvency administration order is presented more than five years after the death of the insolvent. Provision will apply only in cases where the petition for the insolvency administration order is presented after the new provision comes into force.

Clause 11, as amended, will continue to address the effects of the Court of Appeal decision in the case re: Palmer (Deceased). Although we have adopted a different way of achieving that objective, the creditors of an insolvent should not, as far as is reasonably possible, be in a significantly worse position where a debtor has died than they would be were he still living.

Dr. Howells: I shall answer the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire first and as candidly as possible. The amendment is so late because the issues it addresses are difficult and complex. I hope that the hon. Gentleman accepts that answer, because it is the only one I have.

Amendment (a) was tabled by the hon. Member for Christchurch. Hon. Members will appreciate that we must strike a balance between the interests of creditors who have not been paid by a deceased insolvent and a survivor who has become sole owner of what he or she formerly owned jointly with a deceased insolvent. We are not convinced that the creditors of an insolvent should be in a worse position if the debtor has died than if he is living. Limiting the application of the new provision as set out in amendment No. 36 strikes the appropriate balance. Prior to the Palmer case, it was understood that the survivor would automatically have been deprived of the deceased's interests, or the creditors would have got the debtor's entire share, with no thought been given to the survivor's situation or interest.

The new clause does not treat the survivor so harshly. It provides for the court to have the discretion to make a monetary order against the survivor; that order will deal with only so much of the value lost to the estate as is needed to meet the debts and other liabilities to which the estate is subject. A court will make such an order only after it has weighed the interests of both the survivor and the deceased's creditors. That balances the exercise. The court must assume that the interests of the deceased's creditors outweigh all other considerations unless the circumstances are exceptional. I hope, therefore, that the hon. Gentleman will ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

On the point made by the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon, there is no need for third parties to be protected. The clause does not provide for any alteration in the ownership of the property. It will be open only to the trustee of an insolvent estate to apply for a monetary order against the former joint owner; he will not be able to have the deceased's former share vested in him. If the survivor cannot pay over the sum that the court has ordered him to pay the trustee—because, for example, he has given the property away to a third party—it is up to the trustee to pursue the survivor in the usual ways. He would seek to enforce the order: he could, if necessary, make the survivor bankrupt when the disposal of the property might be challenged as a transaction at an under value or the trustee could seek a charging order to secure payment of the sum against the property of the former joint owner.
