We are determined that the referral order should focus on the group that is most likely to benefit--young first offenders. There are two reasons for that. First, we want to provide for early intervention to try to nip in the bud progressive offending behaviour, which worsens with each offence. If young offenders are to be diverted from becoming hardened criminals, it is essential to establish a process to nip such behaviour in the bud.

Secondly, young offenders who have not been through the youth justice system are more likely to respond to the approach that I have described. They have not had time to learn how to play the system. I talk to young offenders in institutions regularly, and I have found it instructive. Other Committee members may have had the same experience. When those young people are questioned, several things become apparent. For example, the same patterns recur in different offenders. It may seem paradoxical, but many young offenders are brutally honest about the process that led to their current circumstances. They may not be honest in other respects, but they will openly describe their lives and their experience of the criminal justice system.

I have found several common observations to be true. First, when discussing family relationships, it becomes apparent that many young offenders have never had a steady relationship with an adult male. That has several consequences, but it may not be appropriate to consider them now.

Secondly, many young offenders have not attended school regularly since they were 11. Some have managed to avoid attending school completely since they were 11. When they were not in school, they tended to learn the ways of the street.

My third observation has greater relevance to our discussion. If young offenders are asked about their experience of the criminal justice system, they say, almost invariably, that what happened the first time that they were in trouble set the pattern for their offending career. Most of them went to court expecting something serious to happen, only to receive a sentence that was less severe than expected, less relevant to their circumstances and less likely to modify their behaviour. We are trying to deal with that problem. Young offenders learn to play the system. They learn what is likely to happen to them. They learn dodges from their legal representatives, and ways of dealing with their predicament.
