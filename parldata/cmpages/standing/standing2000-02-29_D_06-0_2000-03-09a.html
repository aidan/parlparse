Clause 11 inserts a new section into the Army and Air Force Acts of 1955 to alter the process of electing trial by court martial. The availability of a right to choose to be tried by court martial, instead of being dealt with summarily, is long established. The right to make that choice was extended by the Armed Forces Act 1996, so that it became available to all individuals facing summary proceedings in the Army and Royal Air Force. In the Royal Navy it was accepted that this change should not be made, because of the need to be able to deal with minor disciplinary problems at sea quickly. Nevertheless, there is at present in the Royal Navy the right to elect trial by court martial for the more serious offences capable of being dealt with summarily.

As the hon. Gentleman is well aware, the Bill proposes to establish a summary appeal court. Apart from what the Government see as the intrinsic fairness of providing a proper mechanism for appealing against summary findings, this new procedure is necessary to put beyond doubt the question of compatibility with the convention. The advice we have is that the measures taken in the 1996 Act to extend the right of election to trial by court martial were not sufficient for that purpose. Although a step in the right direction, they meant that an individual who elected to be dealt with summarily was closing himself off from the possibility of eventual access to a compliant court. That is why we have introduced the right to appeal in the Bill.

However, we do not wish there to be any confusion between the new right of appeal and the existing right to elect trial by court martial. There could be confusion if we leave the procedures for electing trial by court martial as they are because, in the Army and Royal Air Force, the accused is not given the right to elect trial by court martial until after the commanding officer has found the charge proved and that procedure may be considered to have some of the characteristics of an appeal. So it is to avoid any confusion that we have drafted clause 11 to provide that the accused will have the right to elect trial by court martial prior to the commencement of any summary dealings. That will bring the Army and Royal Air Force into line with the procedure that already operates in the Royal Navy, where the accused has the right to elect trial by court martial at the beginning of the summary process.

We have confidence in the new arrangements for the simple reason that the new procedures in the Army and Royal Air Force will mirror those procedures relating to the point at which an individual may elect trial by court martial already in operation in the Royal Navy. The amendments are similar in effect to amendments that were tabled at the Committee stage in another place, although they go further this time in seeking to change the system in the Navy so that it falls in line with the other two services. We believe that our proposals should be left as it is because it will enable the accused to elect to be dealt with by a compliant court from the outset. My colleague, Baroness Symons of Vernham Dean, gave an assurance in another place that the new procedures will be monitored closely once they have been introduced. She undertook to consider options for remedial action if it became clear that there was a need for modification. I am happy to repeat that assurance today, but I am afraid that I cannot go further than that and I cannot accept the amendment.

Mr. Key: At present, accused people serving in the Army or the Royal Air Force may elect trial by court martial instead of being dealt with summarily by the commanding officer. In the Army and Air Force that right may be exercised only once the commanding officer has found the charge proved. Furthermore, a court martial has the power to award a greater sentence than the commanding officer.

The Armed Forces Act 1996 expended the right to elect trial by court martial as a result of concern that, on their own, summary proceedings may not be compliant with the European convention on human rights. It was considered that offering a wider right to ECHR-compliant court trial would meet such concerns.

The Bill reviews procedures for electing trial by court martial. From the outset, the accused will now have the right to be dealt with by a court complying with the convention. The accused will be offered that right prior to the commanding officer hearing evidence on the charge. The sentensing powers of the court martial are now being limited to those that a commanding officer could have exercised if he had the charge. That is dealt with in clause 12 and will ensure that the accused is not disadvantaged by electing trial by court martial.

The new procedure is damaging as it will slow down and delay armed forces' discipline. Many former service men have noted that the swift passage of military discipline is vital. If it is delayed, unit morale may be adversely affected. Furthermore, the Bill may encourage people to elect trial by court martial rather than by commanding officers, as the Defence Committee pointed out. That will have an adverse impact on commanding officers' authority, which is vital to unit cohesion and operational effectiveness.

Dr. Moonie: As Baroness Symons explained, we cannot condemn the clause on the basis that it is out of kilter with the civilian system. It is not always possible to make a direct comparison between the civilian system and ours. There, an accused facing the choice between trial by magistrate or in the Crown court does indeed face the possibility of a heavier sentence in the higher court. However, his choice is between two forms of trial that are both compatible with the European convention on human rights. There is that phrase again. I can almost see the Conservative Members' backs bristling. The choice is between two fully-compliant courts.

In the services, on the other hand, as has frequently been advanced, it may not be so easy to argue that summary dealings on their own are compatible with the convention. They can be made sound in convention terms by offering the alternative of trial by a compliant court and the right of appeal, hence the appearance of both of them in the form that we have chosen to put them in the Bill. The choise needs to be genuine and an accused is not compelled to put himself in jeopardy of greater punishment in order to secure their rights to a compliant court. To retain the power for a court martial to award a more severe sentence or, indeed, to allow the new summary appeal court to do so, would therefore not achieve the effect of bringing summary dealings within the scope of the convention.

We must remember, however, that election for court martial is not without risk from an accused's point of view. Although the court martial will be limited to the maximum sentencing powers available to the commanding officer, the accused would need to consider whether, within those powers, he is likely to receive a heavier sentence from the court or the CO. He will also face the risk, if he elects trial by court martial, that the prosecuting authority will consider that he could face a different charge. In this case, the accused may not be offered the option of election on a new charge in which case there would be no limitation on the sentencing powers of the court martial.

As was stressed time and time again, our main purpose is to ensure that the procedures for summary dealing remain as far as possible within the scope of the convention in order to permit the services to continue to be able to deal with matters summarily. We agree with Opposition Members that summary punishments are a good way of continuing: we are merely trying to make them compliant. The amendment would defeat that objective. It would place somebody in the position of having to gamble on whether they faced a more severe punishment. It would be improper to choose between a court that in some respects is non-compliant and one that is. Therefore, the amendments would not be of benefit.
