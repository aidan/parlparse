Mr. Bercow: My hon. Friend makes a sensible point.

I hope that there will be no disagreement about the significance of the clause's wording. It refers to ``additional remuneration'' for the period of deprivation during which the employee did not get what he or she was entitled to. That additional remuneration is calculated, in clause 15(2), as

    ``the difference between--

    (a) the relevant remuneration received by the worker for the pay reference period''--

in other words, what he or she got--

    ``and

    (b) the relevant remuneration which the worker would have received for that period had he been remunerated by the employer at a rate equal to the national minimum wage.''

It is unusual for me to attack the Government from the left, but I have to do so on this occasion--[Interruption.] I hear some sedentary chuntering from the Government Whip; we always welcome his remarks as they enliven our proceedings, even if their enlivening effect is greater than their enlightenment.

Mr Bercow: If the Minister would stop chuntering from his sedentary position, I could make speedier progress.

I want to relate the experience of a former colleague at a company for which I worked for several years. The individual concerned was entitled to a bonus for managing to attract a new client. She and a colleague were responsible for that and their contracts entitled them to a bonus payment of, I think, 2 per cent. of the contract fee from the client.

The employer argued the toss for ever and a day and refused to cough up the relevant sum. That was a most unsatisfactory state of affairs and they argued with him and harangued him and sought to persuade him that that sum was due. After a long period, the two of them were deemed to be entitled to the payment, but they received it a year or 18 months after they should have had it.

In the role of friendly counsellor, I asked whether they had requested an additional sum in interest, as one would hope for statutory interest on any sum of which one had been deprived. They felt so relieved to be getting the payment at all that they did not bother to seek any interest or additional sum to cover the inflation that had occurred--modest inflation, of course, as this happened under a Conservative Government.

Is there to be any consideration of the possibility of interest on top? That would surely be appropriate in circumstances in which--and I do not suggest that this happened in the case I have mentioned--the failure to pay the sum was deliberate.

The Minister referred earlier to different circumstances in which a sum was accidentally not paid and the failure to adhere to the duty of an employer was inadvertent. In my case, the failure was not only manifest and indisputable, but wilful. I am sure that we all agree that that is a lamentable state of affairs. I argue with an appropriate compassion and tender heart--as you, Mr. Atkinson, knowing me, would expect me to display--that in such circumstances, people who feel aggrieved should be entitled to some compensation.

The Minister prides himself on his compassion and on his track record of siding with the underdog and of the employee who is discriminated against, rather than the employer doing the discriminating. I would therefore be saddened if he failed to agree with me. I merely ask him to consider--I do not put it any more strongly than that--the possibility that the clause be amended. That is so manifestly reasonable that only an extraordinarily unreasonable person would fail to see the good sense of the argument that the clause might be amended to allow better treatment of the discriminated-against, and in some cases, long-suffering, employee.

The second example to which I should like to refer is that of someone who has been deprived of a benefit in kind. Should anyone accuse me of diverting from the main subject, the minimum wage--the question is still unanswered whether benefits in kind will feature as part of the national minimum wage. The matter has been effectively privatised: it has been subcontracted for policy advice to the Low Pay Commission on the ground that Ministers and, certainly, Labour Back Benchers are not willing to offer the Committee an authoritative view. Therefore, in clause 15 as it stands, there is a clear reference to ``relevant remuneration'', meaning

    ``remuneration which falls to be brought into account for the purposes of regulations under section 2 above.''

Benefits in kind might therefore apply under the national minimum wage in future.

Let us consider the example of an employee who is deprived, in whole or in part, of a benefit in kind to which he or she should contractually be entitled. Let us suppose for the sake of argument that the denial of that benefit is not an accident, or an innocent oversight, but a deliberate decision by an employer to deprive the employee of that to which he or she is entitled in the form of a benefit in kind.

Will the Minister consider whether the compensation that such an employee in due course receives for that denied benefit in kind is compensation at a level that would have applied in respect of the pay reference period if the benefit in kind had been awarded then? Or is he prepared to take a more generous attitude and consider whether it should be paid at the rate of which it would apply later? That question is not answered by clause 15 as it stands.

If the employee is to receive compensation for the benefit in kind only at the rate that would have applied if he or she had received it--which he or she did not--at the appropriate time, that is not much compensation if, in the intervening period, there has been a substantial increase in the retail prices index or a substantial rise in the cost, and therefore also in the value, of the benefit in kind. If the benefit in kind at the time of the discovery of the denied benefit is rather more expensive to purchase than it would have been if it had been paid on time, surely the loss should not be suffered by the employee? In such circumstances, it can be cogently argued that the responsibility for making good the difference, uprating the value and ensuring that there is no loss to the employee, is that of the employer.

The argument has been sufficiently made; I cited one example from my experience, which still sticks in my mind because I thought that those two employees were badly treated. In the end, they received something approximating--I do not consider it more than that--to justice.

These are important, serious points; I hope that the Minister will treat them as such. I should be delighted if he could accommodate my requests and those of my hon. Friends. If he is unable to give a commitment now--[Interruption.] This is an extremely serious matter. I hope that the hon. Member for Dumfries will not treat it with levity and that he and the hon. Member for Brighton, Pavilion accept that it is a matter of the utmost seriousness to the individuals who are discriminated against. At least the Minister has behaved as if he takes the point seriously. I hope that he will return to the matter at a later stage with an alternative proposal or reflect on it and discuss it seriously with his officials, who offer the wisdom of Solomon.

The hon. Gentleman's point is well taken. We have taken steps to relate clause 15(1) to other clauses. We must ensure that those categories of worker qualify for the national minimum wage. That is the purpose of those clauses.

The hon. Member for Daventry asked whether the mechanisms in the Employment Rights Act 1996 were sufficiently robust. Yes, they are. Technical issues are always involved when an employer fails to meet his obligations under contractual arrangements. The relevant authorities are well-accustomed to dealing with those problems, and appropriate procedures are in place. I hope that the Opposition accept the clause and that we can make progress.

Mr. Boswell: I said earlier that I rather enjoy serving on Standing Committees--never more so than at present. I hope that no member of the Committee feels that I have brought these books before me as protection from the Minister's brickbats--he gave an extremely reasonable response during our debate on clause 15. During our brief adjournments I have discovered ``Harvey on Industrial Relations and Employment Law'', which is a superb book. It puts me in mind of the young Keats and his famous sonnet, ``On First Looking into Chapman's Homer''. Having been introduced to the book, I am now hooked on it. I say that in a slightly jocular way, but important issues are involved, including civil liberties and the need to ensure that provisions are acceptable to businesses and are practical. The clause deals with those cases in which there is not the typical employee-employer contract of service, which is the normal industrial relation: it deals with the exceptions.

In that context, it is clear--and it is all the clearer for my hon. Friend's research; he elucidated it with his characteristic lucidity--that in order to accommodate the concept of the national minimum wage and to provide a legal base for prosecuting failure to pay it back, the Government have erected a legal concept that I shall, in shorthand, call a quasi contact. In those cases there is not an employment contract, but it is deemed to exist to cover the many people who are workers--to use the term of art--and who will be entitled to the national minimum wage. That stretches the concept of employment contract a long way, and signs of strain arise. However, I am not prepared to offer the Government an alternative approach. Their intentions are clear, and we need to examine the potential consequences and to establish whether any of them are difficult and need revisiting.

Mr. Hammond: My hon. Friend will correct me if I am wrong, but there is no need to impute the contract. Mr. Boswell: I understand my hon. Friend's point. My concern is that clause 16 should not have any unintended consequences. The clause enables a worker to enforce the national minimum wage even if he or she is not a worker in the generally accepted legal sense. As I have said already to my hon. Friend the Member for South Cambridgeshire, who is coming around the Chair, it is a matter of record that the Employment Rights Act 1996 takes a restricted view of who is a worker. Clause 16 will widen that to embrace other categories.

Rights will be acquired by those who have a lawful contractual relationship but the Bill will also require the courts to examine the relationship before giving a remedy. If the courts considered only what is set out in the 1996 Act, the extra cases would not be covered, so the concept has to be widened.

Several difficulties have arisen over what is or is not a contract of employment in judgments over the years. I promise not to expand on all of them, but they are enshrined in a fascinating book. It is worth pausing for a moment or two to appreciate the range and complexity of quasi-employment cases and deemed contracts. An interesting issue arises over trainees, which has never been finally resolved, about whether or not there is an automatic contract. I suspect that the hon. Member for Wentworth knows more about this than I do.

Given the evolution from the youth opportunities programme through the youth training scheme to the new deal, it seems as if the Government have privatised training rather more comprehensively than we did since they are paying subsidies to employers rather than meeting the costs of training from the public purse.

Agricultural gangmasters are another interesting issue and may concern the Committee later. The whole question of agencies for which there is no contract but where people are employed is also of interest.

My hon. Friend the Member for Solihull, who is a lawyer and has no need to consult weighty tomes as I do, knows that there is a potential problem affecting ministers of religion. Mr. Boswell: There are one or two points raised by the clause which fall outside the Government amendments on which we have just voted. Our concern on a number of clauses has been that although we respect the good faith of Ministers who genuinely intend not to oppress people, nevertheless the effect of what is before us might in certain circumstances give rise to oppression. I do not believe that Ministers want that; I am quite sure that neither my hon. Friends nor I want it. It is our duty to the Committee not to rush through the clauses to this Bill but to give them proper consideration and see whether they can be improved in the interests of the citizen.

In anticipating the rebuttal that we will no doubt receive, either via Excalibur or direct from the Government Front Bench, I do not wish to take from those sources the canard that Opposition Members are interested simply in giving shelter or sustenance to indifferent employers. That is not an adult way in which to discuss very important matters, most of which have a legal basis--the Opposition can return to their legal mode at any time--when the right balance of rights and responsibilities has to be struck.

Before I come to my specific anxieties about the clause, I should like to flag up another matter to Ministers. Ministers err by seeking to present the issues under discussion as a zero sum gain. Anything that the Opposition say that might by chance seem to favour an employer--I hope that that occurs only in defensible circumstances--is also construed as being at employees' expense. Although I did not comment on it, I noted with interest the Government's astonishment when my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham spoke of amendments that, it is fair to say, came from his wing of the party. They could be described as collectivist, as warm, caring and even as touchy-feely amendments. My hon. Friend was right. Some hon. Members are interested in the rights of employees, in civil liberties, and in achieving the right balance.

That is enough to preface. I suspect that some of my hon. Friends might also wish to comment on the clause. I shall draw two specific points to the Committee's attention.

The first line of clause 17 invites consideration of a hypothetical situation. The clause states:

    ``If an officer acting for the purposes of this Act''.

That is a good start, because it is tied to the national minimum wage, not to a general fishing expedition. The sentence deteriorates. It goes on to state:

    ``is of the opinion that a worker who qualifies for the national minimum wage has not been remunerated''

and so on. The Committee should pause on the concept of being ``of the opinion''. Anyone can have a huge range of opinions on a variety of subjects, as most members of the Committee do. Those subjects can range from the worthiness--or otherwise--of the national minimum wage to the question of who will win the cup this year.

Opinions derive from a variety of sources. Some members of the Committee may well receive them from tabloid newspapers. Others think them out for themselves, from first principles. Some Committee members seek for the evolution of a minimum wage about which they do not wholly know. Simply having an opinion does not mean that it is correct. It may be wrong. We all make mistakes from time to time. I invite the Committee to consider whether a legal term would qualify the opinion in question.

At the moment, the clause relies on self certification. An officer who is subject to legal action because of misconduct, for example, would no doubt be able to say in court ``Well, your honour, that is what I thought and it was my opinion.'', and that would be the end of the matter. No one else could look into my brain and tell me what my opinion was. The insertion into the clause of an adjective to qualify that variant noun, ``opinion'', would impart a test that might be transmuted through a judge or through the chairman of an employment tribunal. Potential words could be ``genuine'' or ``reasonable'', or the opinion could even be described as being ``based on proper grounds''.

 

Mr. Bercow: My hon. Friend has given examples of alternative and superior wordings that could have been deployed. Another alternative would be for the line to read:

    ``If an officer acting for the purposes of this Act has reason to believe.''

If that wording were deployed, objectivity would replace the unsatisfactory subjectivity with which the clause saddles us.

which might be a problem if an officer holds the opinion that there are such grounds, or if there is an arithmetical error--the sums are wrong. That is unduly restrictive. The words ``genuinely believes on reasonable grounds'' come close to the suggestion made by my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham.

This is not an attempt to subvert the clause. We want to ensure that opinions are subject to an objective test and are not exercised or certified unreasonably or oppressively.
