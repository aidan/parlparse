Mr. Nigel Waterson (Eastbourne): When we adjourned, I was discussing what effect, if any, the new power would have on the ground in local authorities. We have established that there is no extra money to back it up. I have discussed in some detail-I thought that the Committee deserved it-the letter that Lord Whitty wrote between Committee stage and Third Reading in the House of Lords, setting out his position. We have carefully studied that letter, and one can argue that there is no need for clause 3(2)-it is surplus to requirements. Lord Whitty notes that there is no limit on what councils can spend under the well-being powers, but he makes it equally clear that they are constrained by their overall budget. We were, therefore, rather taken by amendment No. 90 and have broken the habit of a lifetime to make common cause with our Liberal Democrat friends on the Committee-a serious lapse, which will not be repeated. We have done that because the neatest and most effective approach to clause 3(2) is to take it out altogether, certainly if one takes Lord Whitty literally.

In case anyone is labouring under the misapprehension that I am raising a purely Conservative gripe about the provisions, I should add that we have had a briefing from the Local Government Information Unit, which says in relation to clause 2:

    The recovery of costs, or making a charge for services, will be important issues. We have concerns about the impact of this clause We believe that councils need the assurance that they will be able to adopt suitable means of recovering their costs. This could be achieved by an amendment to clause 2(4).-[Official Report, House of Lords, 25 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1462.]

The unit goes on to suggest some wording. There is a genuine concern out there in the real world of local government that, if the clause is to survive more or less intact, there should be a mechanism for raising money for worthy causes.

It is perhaps worth taking a couple of steps back to consider the background to the debate on clause 3(2) in the House of Lords. Baroness Hamwee said:

    Our objection in principle is the tight grip which central government keeps on local government expenditure, a degree of control which we think is wrong, unnecessary and not appropriate for good government.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 25 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1462.]

I could not have put it better myself.

The Opposition have been listening to Britain, and as part of that exercise we have been listening to local government. We have formed the view-[ Interruption.] Yes, we did get it wrong when we were in government. We were far too prone to centralising and interfering in local government. Imagine our surprise and that of many people-not only Conservatives-in local government when we found that this Government had trumped us in that department and were moving in a more centralising and interfering direction.

Baroness Hamwee went on to ask in relation to clause 3(2):

    Are local authorities to be restricted by this provision from generating a surplus? I think that clarity in this matter would be helpful. There is a real anxiety that the subsection will prevent councils from raising partnership contributions to activities and could undo the good that the earlier part of the Bill will undoubtedly do.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 25 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1463.]

That is the key point. If the Government are clear about what they are trying to achieve in establishing the new power and if they are prepared to tackle all the risks that the lawyers might pose some way down the road-I described those this morning-why not ensure that the power makes a difference? Otherwise, this is all pure debate, and nothing will be delivered on the ground.

In the same House of Lords debate, which I commend to members of the Committee for its high standard, Lord Dixon-Smith said:

    We are back in the position that we were in on the Greater London Authority Bill. That Bill gave the Greater London Authority powers and then the Government immediately prescribed what I described as an ``anti-power''. Here we are again.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 25 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1463.]

He notes that the Bill gives the power to promote well-being-it is not an absolute power of general competence-but that it then says that authorities can do nothing about it. His reference to an ``anti-power'' is spot on: that is exactly what we have in the Bill. The Bill gives a power on the one hand, but makes it pretty useless to most local councils on the other.

Replying to the debate in the House of Lords on behalf of the Government, Baroness Farrington of Ribbleton said:

    Nor do we see merit in allowing authorities to use the well-being power in itself as a means of borrowing.

She added that there were good reasons for those ``controls'', saying:

    First, they ensure that authorities act prudently.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 25 January 2000; Vol. 608, c. 1466.]

Is that not the point? Does that not turn a penetrating shaft of light on the Government's true intentions towards local government? They are saying, ``Yes, you will have all these powers, but we don't think that you will use them prudently, so we will make sure that you use them in the way that we think you should.''

That attitude is reflected across the board in the Government's approach to local authorities, and it is nonsense. The hon. Member for Bedford (Mr. Hall) rightly said that councils want to get involved in serious issues such as homelessness. In the same way, people such as Chamberlain went into local government in the 19th century because it was expanding, finding its role and entering new areas of activity; that was seen as an exciting time to be in local government. How on earth can we hope to make a difference to the miserable turnouts in local elections and to the problem of attracting enough of the right sort of candidates if any ability that legislation gives councils to take action is immediately prescribed and hedged around with restrictions by central Government? That is at the heart of the problem that we face on clause 3(2).

It flows from the Government's approach that local authorities must find a way of funding some activities. The obvious solution is to charge for them, which is why we have tabled a raft of amendments and one or two new clauses. Section 137 of the Local Government Act 1972 is crucial in that regard, but it has been substantially recast by part III of the Local Government and Housing Act 1989. Section 33 of that Act already contains specific powers to allow local authorities to take

    such steps as they may from time to time consider appropriate for promoting the economic development of their area.

Presumably, some of the Bill is based on that predecessor statute.

Under section 35 of the Act, the Secretary of State may issue guidance to which local authorities must ``have regard''-there is that phrase again. The Secretary of State may limit expenditure on economic development, whether incurred under section 33 or otherwise, with reference to any financial year. At the moment, economic development by local authorities is subject to statutory powers in part III of the 1989 Act. That Act amended section 137 and made it clear that any such extension had to be in the interests of the area, part of the area or some of its inhabitants-it made no mention of people present in the area-and of direct benefit. As we heard, the financial limit was originally set at a 2p rate. It is now based on a multiplier linked to an area's population. Part V of the 1989 Act contains a statutory code relating to companies in which local authorities have interests.

That describes the basis of amendment No. 3, which, by reference to section 137 of the Local Government Act 1972, tries to tease out the Government's thinking on how local authorities will fund any significant activity under the new powers. We return to ultra vires. Lawyers say that the phrase ``in their opinion'', used in section 137 as amended, does not confer an unfettered discretion, according to cases such as Padfield v. Minister of Agriculture and that of our old friend the Wednesbury corporation in 1948. Local authorities will face two problems when considering new activities under this part of the Bill. First, will they still have an ultra vires headache? Secondly, will they be able to fund new activities, and if so, how? Ministers must answer those key questions, either today or some time.

In the meantime, we have proposed a solution in some detail. Much of it will be discussed later, so I shall not detain the Committee too long now. Our series of provisions would allow councils to charge for the new services. There is a slight difference between us and the Liberal Democrats-the word ``reasonable''-but we are raising the same issue, fundamentally. If the Government will not provide funds or allow councils to raise extra resources under the power, why, for goodness' sake, will they not let authorities make charges for certain discretionary services, if they want to do so? I commend the amendments to the Committee.

The Minister of State for Local Government and the Regions (Ms Hilary Armstrong): I hope that I can help the Committee in this regard, because I understand the concerns that Opposition Members are expressing. Before addressing the amendments individually, I want to tackle head-on the charge that the new power is something of a paper tiger, because I think that hon. Members have misunderstood what is possible and where the Government are coming from. I want to make it clear that councils will be able to raise money through council tax or the use of some of their grant to contribute towards the activities that the power will enable them to undertake.

However, the Government are anxious to make it absolutely clear that councils cannot use the power itself to impose new forms of taxation. It is interesting that the hon. Member for Eastbourne is not pushing a desire for new forms of taxation at local level. However, that would be the consequence of some of the amendments. It is important that hon. Members understand that we are not saying that no money can be spent, but that the authority will not be able to say that it wants to raise new funds merely by quoting the power. An authority will have to show that it is raising funds for activities pursued by means of the power and do so within the current local government finance structure.

Local authorities have an array of money-raising powers at their disposal at present, including the council tax. I remind hon. Gentlemen that in the past three years the Government have increased grants to local authorities by £6 billion, a real-terms increase of 7.8 per cent., in contrast to the previous three years when there was a real-terms cut in grant of 4.3 per cent. Councils will be able to raise money by using their existing powers to finance activity arising from the power; I hope that that is clear.

Sir Paul Beresford: The right hon. Lady has tackled a matter that the Opposition fully understand. However, amendment No. 110 is about the return, in charges or loans, of fees spent, not about their being used to raise money.

Ms Armstrong: I said that I would deal with the general issues first and then come to the amendments. We held extensive deliberations on best value in debates on a previous Bill, at which some members of the Committee were present. We considered how to ensure that the council worked out the priorities, and how they could be achieved most efficiently, with local people. I hope that hon. Members will not neglect that aspect of the debate; the power is in the Bill partly to aid that process. The new well-being power will allow authorities to explore new ways of working in partnership with other bodies to deliver better services and to make more efficient use of their resources.

Some of the best-value pilots discovered that authorities could not work with other authorities for greater efficiency by the common purchase of information technology systems, for example, because their legal advice was that to do so may be ultra vires. The power in the Bill gives authorities a clear way forward in that respect, and it will save money. I reaffirm that use of the power will not always incur more spending, but may be a means of reducing it.

In principle, the Government accept that authorities should have greater scope to charge for discretionary services. The Audit Commission published a report last September called ``The Price is Right'', which highlighted the importance of fees and charges, both as a source of revenue and as an instrument of policy. It stated that a consistent and logical framework was needed within which authorities should be allowed to charge for services. The Government accept that; we are currently considering the design of such a framework. We want to publish that framework as part of the local government finance Green Paper this summer, and then to consult on it. The problem with the amendment is that it would not give us the chance to do so; the issue has been quite confused and, as Conservative Members have said, it needs sorting out, but that should be through consultation with local government rather than by an amendment to the Bill.

We cannot accept amendments Nos. 110, 94 and 95; it is not a simple matter of legislating to allow authorities to charge for anything done under the well-being power, or to recover reasonable costs. We must give careful consideration to the effect that such powers may have.

Legitimate concerns have been raised about the impact of charging when authorities have a monopoly position. We must consider the impact of charging on competition with the private sector. The report last year of the Environment, Transport and Regional Affairs Committee recommended that charges should be means-sensitive. Those issues must be considered before they are encompassed in legislation.

In the meantime, there are good reasons to allow authorities to charge for what they do under the well-being power and we can use the powers in section 150 of the 1989 Act to allow such charges. That section may also provide a means to implement a wider charging regime following consultation on the Green Paper. We may not have to wait for further primary legislation before implementing suggestions that follow from the Green Paper.

The Bill is not about Government giving authorities greater powers to charge; the Audit Commission report found that councils rarely managed their charges well, and that there was little evidence that charges were driven by policy priorities or by systematic planning. In the Audit Commission's view, existing charging regimes are rarely used to their full potential; that is something that the authorities will need to tackle, and I hope that they will do so.

Amendments Nos. 90 and 3 would strike out clause 3(2), which stops local authorities from raising money, whether by precepts, borrowing or otherwise, and there are very good reasons for that subsection. Authorities should not be able to use the well-being power to impose new forms of taxation on individuals or businesses. The power to tax should be subject to the approval of Parliament. There is no merit in allowing authorities to use the well-being power as a means of borrowing.

Specific terms and conditions apply to local government's power to borrow, for two reasons: first, they help to ensure that authorities act prudently, so that a local community is not faced with an excessive burden of debt charges in future years and, secondly, they help to ensure that the Chancellor's objectives for the national economy are achieved, following the golden rule that borrowing should be only for investment, not for day-to-day spending, and the rule that public sector debt should be kept at a prudent and stable level over time. Committee members know that we are considering changes in how local government borrows as part of the local government finance review; however, the guidelines will remain relevant.

Amendment No. 3, which we cannot accept, would replace clause 3(2) with a new subsection, which would re-impose the spending limits in section 137 of the Local Government Act 1972. The new power is about freeing up the activities of local authorities the better to serve the needs and priorities of their communities. It includes the ability to direct spending towards local issues that require immediate action. It is not about placing limits on the amount that individual authorities can spend per head of the population. It is for local authorities themselves to decide how to spend the money that is available to them, taking into account the views of local people.

I am sorry that my explanation has been so lengthy, but I wanted to ensure that Members understood the Government's work on charging and the impact of specific amendments. I hope that the hon. Member for Eastbourne will withdraw the amendment.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. David Hanson): That was relatively short and sweet by comparison with the debate on the previous set of amendments.

In response to the hon. Members for Eastbourne and for Mole Valley, the Government have reflected on the position in the other place. However, despite the clarity of the explanations that we have heard today, we are still not able to accept amendments Nos. 8 and 1. I owe both hon. Members an explanation of the Government's thinking on the issues.

The Government want local authorities to be able to act in the interest of the communities, and to be certain of their legal right to do so. The hon. Member for Eastbourne made much of his listening exercise around England and Wales, and the United Kingdom at large. If he listened to anything that local authorities said, he will know that they want to know that they are properly accountable to their communities and that they can decide when, and how, to use the well-being power, whether inside or outside their own area.

The Government want local authorities to find ways of tackling local problems such as social exclusion and deprived neighbourhoods, and the economic development issues that are a factor in the power, which has been broadly welcomed. We want local authorities to take on the challenge, but I hope that hon. Members will accept that doing that will occasionally involve taking risks. There can be no guarantee that any approach will necessarily work. The power in the Bill is therefore drafted to confirm to local authorities that they can be sure of the lawfulness of their activity and will be able to prove in court that in their opinion the activity that they undertook was likely to promote or improve community well-being.

The concept is nothing new. The wording in clause 3 is similar to that used in other discretionary powers-notably section 137 of the Local Government Bill 1972. It is right to make the general powers discretionary. Local authorities are best able to identify the needs of local communities and consider the partnerships that they need with other local agencies to tackle problems. This is why many local authorities in England and-according to representations that I have received-in Wales, greatly welcome the general direction and thrust of clause 2.

The Government obviously think that local authorities should not be given the power to do absolutely anything. They will still be open to challenge on whether they have acted reasonably; that is entirely proper and right. However, if amendment No. 8 were accepted, local authorities would have to show that any proposed activity outside their area would actually-I emphasise that word-promote or improve community well-being before the activity could be undertaken. That would be a difficult if not impossible test, as there can be no objective test of whether the local authority could achieve its objective.
