The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Ms Patricia Hewitt): I am also looking forward to hearing my answers to the questions on this immensely important subject. However, before I turn to the interesting questions raised by the right hon. and learned Member for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir N. Lyell), I would like to deal with the Opposition's amendments.

Amendment No. 42 would extend the financial crime objectives to businesses other than those run by regulated persons. The broad effect of the amendment would be inappropriate. The FSA's remit clearly covers regulated activities and its powers apply to regulated persons. It would go beyond the proper remit of a financial services regulator to extend the objectives to cover other types of business--not necessarily within the financial system--run by non-regulated persons. That should be the job of other agencies tackling financial crime--the police, the Serious Fraud Office and the Department of Trade and Industry.

The hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) as well as the right hon. and learned Gentleman made the point that the clause might inadvertently exclude the activities of people who ought to be, but are not, regulated under the Bill. I readily undertake to look at that drafting problem again, although I have to say that it is not easy to get the boundaries right. We shall, as I said, attempt to deal with that problem, but we also need to limit the objective in some way to the FSA's natural sphere of activity.

I have more sympathy with the thinking behind amendment No. 43, but it is also unnecessary. The clause does not impose obligations on anyone other than the FSA. It does not impose a responsibility on regulated persons to prevent crime. It is all about the responsibilities of the FSA. The right hon. and learned Gentleman himself pointed out that clause 6(2)(b) requires the authority, in considering the objective of preventing financial crime, to have regard to the desirability of regulated persons taking appropriate measures. That defines what the authority has to do, but does not impose responsibilities on regulated persons.

We are all agreed that the reduction of financial crime is important and that the regulatory community has a key role to play. However, we also appreciate that we cannot ask business to take on tasks that it cannot reasonably be expected to perform. I understand concerns on that score, but I hope to reassure the industry--and right hon. and hon. Gentlemen- that those concerns have been met. Taking

    ``appropriate measures . . . to prevent financial crime''

does not mean that financial services companies should have to spend enormous sums on reducing financial crime to zero. That is not possible.

We have already made changes to this part of the Bill to respond to that concern. Following consultation, we replaced the original reference to ``adequate measures'' in the objectives with ``appropriate measures''. The amendment would have no effect beyond that.

Like the other objectives, the objective does not exist in isolation, When the FSA makes rules in pursuit of it, it must take account of the principles in clause 2. As part of that, it must have regard to the principle that a burden should be proportionate to the benefits, Indeed, we shall discuss that later in the Bill in relation to the cost-benefit analysis that will help to ensure that that balance is maintained.

Clearly, the FSA will not require all banks to model themselves on Fort Knox. As drafted, the objective will not give rise to the difficulties for authorised persons and regulated businesses which are of concern to Opposition Members.

I understand the aim of amendment No. 44. However, it is not helpful or necessary, and I shall explain why in detail.

There is a whole range of functions that the FSA will be able to exercise in relation to the reduction of financial crime, including giving guidance, mounting prosecutions, co-operating with other agencies and so on. We have specified in the Bill's early clauses the objectives to which the FSA must pay attention in exercising its functions. We have not tied any particular aspects of the FSA's statutory duties--including the objectives--to its functions as that would reduce flexibility. For instance, the rule-making power will be used in pursuit of all or any of the objectives; it will not be confined to one.
