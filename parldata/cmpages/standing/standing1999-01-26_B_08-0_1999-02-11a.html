The protocol has no legal status. In many respects it is similar to the concordats that are being negotiated between Government Departments and the new Scottish Parliament and Welsh Assembly. It is a new form of constitutional law, with no legal status at the moment. That has happened, for example, with the role of executive agreements between federal and state governments in the United States. It is one way in which European Community law is developing. Various different tiers of law exist. The document is, therefore, of extreme significance and has a bearing on the future interpretation of the clause.

With respect to the principles governing intervention by the Secretary of State, the protocol states:

    "The government will exercise intervention powers only when there is clear evidence that an authority is failing either to discharge its functions adequately or failing to meet its statutory obligations."

That is a good argument for removing the phrase

    "if the Secretary of State is satisfied"

from clause 13(1). The protocol says nothing about the Secretary of State being satisfied as to the point. It says only that it should be clear. The only body that can make the matter clear is the Audit Commission, since it has been charged with the responsibility.

The protocol states:

    "The Secretary of State will inform the authority of the reasons for intervention whenever using his powers under best value legislation".

That is laid down in the Bill. Further on, the protocol states:

    "The form and extent of intervention will"

reflect

    "the type and seriousness of failure"

and the need for effective improvement. That is a good instance of the principle of proportionality-something which is implicit in the competitive tendering legislation. Intervention can take place only when there is clear failure constituting a breach, and the intervention will achieve an objective. However, that is not in the Bill.

The clause nowhere provides that an order made under it would have to be proportional to the failure identified in the Audit Commission's report. It is important that it should be placed on record that that is what is intended. It is distressing that no relevant amendment has been tabled. Had we had the material more than a few days ago, we would have tabled appropriate amendments. Why does the Minister consider it unnecessary to provide for proportionality in the clause?

In dealing with timing, the protocol states that

    "the authority will normally be given the opportunity to make the necessary improvements itself."

That is comforting, but is like a gun being held to the head of the authority. Paragraph 5 states:
