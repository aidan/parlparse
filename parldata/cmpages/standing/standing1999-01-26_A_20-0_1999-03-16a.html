In 1986, in one of the debates that is usuaily--but not always--held every year in the Chamber on the policing of London, I first made the point that there should be a democratic police authority for London. I have repeated that point in each of the almost annual speeches that I have made since that debate in 1986. The clause not only reaffirms that we should have a police force in London--that is self-evident--but sets up a new authority for London. Instead of coming under the responsibility of the Home Secretary, the police will he responsible to a police authority, as is the case in the rest of England--apart from the City of London. That is an anomalous situation, to which we shall return later.

One of the failures of the present system is that the Home Secretary must be Mikado-like--he must speak to himself, as police authority for London, and then the police authority must speak back. The present Commissioner and the police believe that that is not in London's interests. London has no independent voice to put its case for policing to the Home Secretary, unlike the other forces in England, which have independent voices. Your force in Cheshire, for example, Mr. Winterton, speaks, independent of the view of the Home Secretary, to the Home Secretary as the Secretary of State responsible for policing. That has never been the case in London, which is a disadvantage from which the police in London believe that they have suffered. They have for several years called for a change.

The Secretary of State for the Home Department--the boss of the Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department, the hon. Member for Vauxhall whom we greatly welcome--is, as holder of that office of state, responsible for the police authority. It cannot be delegated to Ministers of State or Under-Secretaries of State. Yet we have had the nonsense that Secretaries of State have in the past left debates on policing London, even though they are supposed to be the accountable authority. I have criticised previous Home Secretaries for not sitting through the only occassions in the year for holding our police authority to account. The authority in the form of the Secretary of State, is held to account by Parliament, which obviously includes London Members of Parliament, although it is rather remote from the people of London.

When we had the review of policing legislation, we came, after wide debate, to a broad consensus that police authorities should be made up of a majority of elected councillors, a minority of magistrates and a third group whose members would be chosen by the other two groups.
