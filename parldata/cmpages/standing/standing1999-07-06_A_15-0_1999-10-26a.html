Mr. Timms: I welcome you back to the Chair, Mr. Atkinson, as we begin our deliberations on part V.

It might be helpful if I explained a little of the background to clause 53 and to this part of the Bill to put our simple amendments in context.

Essentially, part V provides powers for the Financial Services Authority to exercise a degree of control over people employed by authorised persons. Clause 53 gives the authority powers to prohibit a person from being employed to perform specified functions in the financial services sector. Later clauses require firms to obtain the FSA's approval before engaging staff to perform particular functions. They also give the FSA powers to issue statements of principle, with which approved persons must comply. Finally, they provide it with powers to discipline approved persons who are guilty of misconduct.

The measures are an important part of the authority's armoury, which will ensure that it can regulate effectively. They mean that it will be able to prevent people who are not fit and proper to perform particular functions—perhaps where they do not meet the required standards of competence—from putting people's money at risk.

That is not a new idea. Many existing regulatory regimes include statutory arrangements for vetting directors and senior managers. The self-regulating organisations under the Financial Services Act 1986 have introduced vetting and discipline arrangements for employees of investment firms. The council of Lloyd's has introduced similar arrangements under its byelaw-making powers.

Clause 53 carries forward and improves on arrangements that were provided for under section 59 of the 1986 Act. It gives the authority powers to make orders to prohibit individuals from being employed in the financial services industry. That is an important power if the authority is to achieve its objectives. However, we would not expect it to be used lightly or routinely.

The clause refers throughout to an individual performing functions rather than being employed. In part, that is necessary because some people who work for a firm, such as its directors, might not technically be employees of the firm. Modern working methods and employment practices mean that many complex and innovative structures exist. Outsourcing is common place. Many people work as freelancers; others work on a temporary contract and might be employed by their agency rather than the place in which they work. Those features of the workplace are as relevant to the financial services industry as they are to any other industry.

The situation is further complicated by the fact that larger international groups, in particular, structure their business in such a way that their subsidiaries carry out specific but complementary functions. That might involve an employee of one company managing part of the business of a sister company—the term matrix managers is used to describe that arrangement. The provision has, therefore, been drafted so as to be neutral as regards contractual or other relationships between the individual and the firm.

The Bill will enable the authority to exercise its powers in relation to a firm that carries on any regulated activities. The power to issue a prohibition order will, therefore, need to be sufficiently flexible to apply only to particular regulated activities specified in subsection (3)(a). It does not necessarily follow that someone whom the authority deems not to be not fit and proper to work in derivatives trading, for example, is not fit and proper to sell general insurance, as the two businesses are very different, so a prohibition order covering more than was necessary or appropriate would be excessive.

An individual may not be fit and proper to carry out certain functions, such as those performed by a chief executive or managing director. However, allowing that person to carry out a different, perhaps more junior function may not be a problem. Subsection (2) therefore enables the authority to specify the functions covered by the prohibition order in the order itself

Subsection (3)(b) enables the authority to make a prohibition order relating to different types of authorised and exempt people. The FSA may therefore specify that someone subject to a prohibition order may not be engaged to perform relevant functions for a bank or insurance company.

Factors specified in the clause as currently drafted will enable the authority to make a prohibition order as narrow or almost as broad as circumstances require. An order made under that power may be narrowly defined. However, in the most extreme cases, the FSA may find that so great is the risk to consumers and market confidence that it must make an order prohibiting someone from working in the financial services industry at all.

I anticipate that such a blanket ban will rarely need to be imposed, but I do not rule out that eventuality altogether, as such a ban is possible and, indeed, has been imposed using powers in section 59 of the 1986 Act. However, in the current drafting of the Bill, it is not clear whether a blanket ban would be possible and, were one to be imposed, whether it its validity would be challenged in the courts. We must address that risk in order properly to protect consumers' interests.

Government amendments Nos 324 to 327 are intended to remove any doubts about the authority's ability to make prohibition orders banning someone from performing any function in relation to regulated activity carried out by authorised persons. Our amendments ensure that the existing power is carried across to the new regime.

In seeking to deliver that certainty, we have identified changes that will simplify the drafting of the clause. I am keen to take the opportunity—
