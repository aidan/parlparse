Mr. Green: This large group of amendments essentially relates to various aspects of mapping. Most of the amendments are to clause 4, although others that are consequential to them amend clause 6. They would provide for maps of access land to identify points where the public can readily gain access. We have had many discussions about the necessity of providing walkers with information. Users, particulary new users, will need to be guided to places where they can safely park or to points where information on rights and restrictions that may apply is easily available.

Mr. Geoffrey Clifton-Brown (Cotswold): Does my learned Friend agree that it would be helpful if maps showed all other rights of way, particularly footpaths, because that would show how access land could be reached?

Mr. Green: It would. That is a good suggestion because the more information that maps can provide, the more enjoyment and satisfaction walkers-particularly new and inexperienced walkers-are likely to get from their day out. Many of the amendments seek to have access points clearly marked on maps by the Countryside Agency or the Countryside Council for Wales. If they are marked, it would be a straightforward matter for the Ordnance Survey to transfer that information to the published maps that are most likely to be used by the public. If they are not, the Ordnance Survey will have to obtain that information by doing its own surveys, which could lead to delays and more selective or limited information. I do not think that it is a matter of controversy between us that the more information there is on the maps, the better. The issue relates to our earlier debates on the identification and designation of access points, which I do not wish to repeat. It is reasonable that the Government should encourage the countryside bodies to include access points on draft and final maps of access land. I should be grateful if the Minister could give reassurance on that point.

It is important that information be provided on maps of access land, of other land to which statutory rights of access already exist, and of land dedicated for access. A clear distinction must be maintained between the different types of land because the management arrangements and the general restrictions likely to apply to them could vary under the Bill. Users and owners should be fully aware of their rights in regard to each type of land, and that will be possible only if all the land available for access, as a right of by dedication, is clearly identified on the same maps. I hope that the Minister will agree that it would be sensible for maps of access land to show the other land available for public recreation under agreements. That would be a desirable step towards an integrated access map that covers all access opportunites.

As I said, the amendments to clause 6 are consequential on the amendment to clause 4, and many of the clause 4 amendments make the same amendment to different parts of the clause, so I do not need to go through them all separately. Amendment Nos. 247 and 255 deal with the mapping duties of the Countryside Agency and the Countryside Council for Wales. If maps are to have practical value they should show access land, but that is not a requirement of the clause. I am sure that all members of the Committee would want the new maps to be accurate and informative guides; and the amendments would fulfil that objective.

Amendments Nos. 248 and 256 would complete the picture by specifying that excepted land should be shown. I am sure that all hon. Members want to prevent inadvertent trespass. Under the Bill, hazardous areas will be shown as open country. In debating what constitutes a hazardous area, we agreed about areas such as aerodromes, although we disagreed about golf courses. The danger is that areas that are clearly hazardous will be shown as open country, giving the impression that access rights apply when they do not. The amendments would not only help walkers avoid trespassing and hazards, but would help owners avoid breaking the law in siting notices for excepted land.

Amendments Nos. 249 and 257 deal with a similar point, in that the same false impression will be given when SSSIs are designated in areas marked on the map as open country. That could result in damage to those sites if walkers are misled by the maps.

Amendments No. 250 and 256 relate to land that has been excluded indefinitely. There seems little reason why that should be included as open country, and it would be more practical to mark it as excluded.

Amendments Nos. 251 and 259 are, perhaps, more ambitious. It seems sensible for the Government's maps to show land that will be regularly closed to access at predictable times of year. As the Under-Secretary will be aware, one of the most difficult and important detailed issues is the times of year that particular land will be closed to access. For the avoidance of inadvertent damage to wildlife in particular, it is important to put such information on the maps.

Amendments Nos. 252 and 260 relate to means of access. As the entrances to access land will be restricted to particular points on the perimeter when hedges and fences are in place, it would be sensible to mark those points on the map. Earlier, we discussed whether walkers might be forced to walk 500 m one way or another and back again, and the Under-Secretary managed to add that up to a 2 km walk. The way to avoid that is for the maps to show where the entrances are. Without such information, walkers may encounter problems, and may have to stay away or risk trespassing on land outside the perimeter while looking for entrances. The amendments would prevent that problem.

Amendments Nos. 253 and 261 would extend the Bill to cover new land provided for access under clause 15 that is not provided at the moment. Similarly, amendments Nos. 254 and 262 relate to land dedicated under clause 16. Under the Bill as drafted, that will not be marked as open country on the maps, which seems slightly perverse.

Amendment No. 263 relates to the important issue of different types of open country. The maps should distinguish between different types of several reasons. Doing so would enable people to know why the land is shown as open country and would help owners make an informed decision about whether to appeal, thereby avoiding unnecessary appeals. The information would be of interest to landowners, walkers and conservationists, and should be readily available. It will certainly be available to the Countryside Agency and the Countryside Council for Wales, as they will have to determine in which category land falls to become open country. That extra information would not give rise to more appeals. It would not be a ground for appeal if open country had been marked as down rather than health. That would be a practical way of improving the utility of the maps without unnecessarily clogging up process.

Further amendments in this group cover clause 6, but they are essentially consequential on the groups that have been dealt with. I hope that the Committee will agree that, if this group of amendments were adopted, walkers would be provided with a much more informative set of maps that would enable the new access regime to provide more good for more people. I commend the amendments to the Committee.

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): Once again, I understand the purpose behind the amendments, but I seek to persuade the Committee that that will be better achieved by the mechanisms proposed in the Bill.

The amendments would impose new requirements on the countryside bodies to identify separately characteristics, whether they are access points, SSSIs or excluded land. Before I speak to specific amendments, I want to say a few words about the purpose of the maps to be drawn up under clause 4. They are intended to provide certainty about what is open country or registered common land for the purposes of part I. They will be the authoritative source of information on that point. As I have repeatedly said, safeguards built into chapter I provide for landowners and others affected by the maps to be consulted on draft maps, and to have the right of appeal against provisional maps. The statutory maps do not identify excepted land and land subject to restrictions; we expect the countryside bodies to provide the public with information about that, and they are investigating how best that can be done.

There is, therefore, no difference between us about the need for the information to be put intio a useful form for walkers. The question is whether that information should all be included on the statutory maps, or whether that purpose would be best served by the maps that are-appropriately, in our view-drawn up by countryside bodies. The problem is that areas of excepted land and land subject to restriction may change over time. Given that change and flux, it would be impossible for the statutory maps to identify conclusively all the possible exceptions and exclusions.

The general principle of excepted land in chapter I rightly provides for the flexibility of the right of access, which in other circumstances the Opposition seem keen to endorse. Amendments Nos, 247, 255, 343, 248, 256, 348 and 353 would imply less flexibility because , after being mapped as such, excepted land would remain excepted, at least until the next map review, which may not be for a decade. The corollary, or course, is that the map would be conclusive envidnece of what was not excepted land, so the right of access would, for example, endure over downland even if the farmer chose to plough it up. Thus, the hon. Member for Ashford will appreciate that flexibility works both ways. The Opposition must decide whether they want flexibility for land to be taken in and out of excepted land status. The Bill makes that flexibility possible, but the amendment would introduce an unhelpful rigidity, which I am sure that the hon. Gentleman did not intend.

It would not be sensible for the maps of open country and registered common land to show land dedicated under clause 16, which would be called for by amendments Nos. 247 and 255 and by amendments Nos. 254 and 262. Where land is dedicated, the instrument of dedication will provide proof about the extent of the land that is subject to the dedication. However, I take the point underlying this little debate: it is important for the public to be able to find out about opportunities for creation on land dedicated under clause 16. As I said, we will look to the countryside bodies to publish more user friendly information, which shows not only the extent of open country and registered common land, but also land that has been dedicated and land from which the public has been excluded either temporarily or long term, a matter to which a string of amendments, which I shall not list, refers.

The hon. Member for Cotswold (Mr. Clifton-Brown) raised the matter of rights of way over access land; that is exactly the sort of information that it would be useful to show on the publicity material disseminated by the countryside bodies and others; indeed, that is its purpose.

The statutory maps are not the appropriate vehicle for conveying more and better information about access opportunities to the public. We doubt whether those maps will be referred to by more than a small minority of walkers.

On the additional point about SSSIs being shown on statutory maps, as statutory maps will be reviewed every 10 years, changes in access points, SSSI designations or new dedications could not be reflected on the statutory maps until the next review, which may be 10 years on. On the other hand, non-statutory publicity such as that produced by the countryside bodies will not be restricted in that way. It is a much better way to convey to the public what they need to know. We must be realistic about how widely statutory, rather than merely user friendly, maps will be used by the general public.

Amendments Nos. 249 and 257 would introduce an additional duty separately to identify SSSIs on the statutory maps. As I said, that information will already be available to those who need to identify sensitive sites; obviously the advisory bodies-English Nature and the Countryside Council for Wales-the Countryside Agency and, of course, landowners themselves. They will be able to use that knowledge, with a consideration of the merits of individual cases, to determine whether an exclusion or restriction of access is necessary in respect of a site under chapter II.

I return to the vexed issue of access points; amendments Nos. 252 and 260 with amendment No. 345 would require the countryside bodies to identify means of access on maps of registered common land and open country. As my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary of State said on 30 March, we support the provision of information points where people could go-and be advised to go-to find out about the access opportunities. We all stand by that. The amendment, however, is not the right way to go about it. We expect that access information published by the countryside bodies for the use of the general public will identify means of access where information has been supplied by the access authorities, or of course by landowners. That is not a new idea. It happens now in respect of much land subject to access agreements, so that the information is frequently shown on Ordnance Survey maps.

Amendments Nos. 253, 261, 351 and 356 would introduce an additional duty to produce maps of land already accessible to the public by existing statutory authority, in other words the so-called ``existing access land''. The rights of access to such land are preserved in this Bill by virtue of clauses 1(1) and 15(1). The Bill provides that any land that is open country or registered common land should be mapped, including land over which there is an existing right. That is because for well over 1 million hectares there are real uncertainties about the extent of such land. Disputes about such matters are for the courts, not the countryside bodies, to determine. Any land shown on the map will be access land, whether it is so as a result of an existing right of access, or a right arising from clause 1 of the Bill. That will provide the necessary certainty for walkers. However, again the countryside bodies will be best able to identify, on an advisory basis, existing access land where such information is both available and, importantly, reliable.

Finally, amendment No. 263, to which the hon. Member for Meirionnydd Nant Conwy referred, would require the countryside bodies to distinguish between mountain, moor, heath and down on their maps of open country. We do not think that is necessary. The requirement to make such a distinction on statutory maps is quite unnecessary because the real concern, both for the landowner and the walker, is whether or not the land is open country. That is what really matters. However, the countryside bodies will need to be able to justify their classification-either as mountain, moor, heath or down-including on an appeal under clause 6.

In conclusion, I think that we already agree that the countryside bodies will need to provide the public with information about the right of access and ancillary issues in a form that people can understand and use practically. The statutory maps are not intended to be that medium. I agree, therefore, with the purpose of the amendments. I am simply suggesting that they do not represent the right way to go about it.

The amendments would make the statutory maps much more bureaucratic and difficult to use. There would be a veritable wodge of overlays for each map to show all the information that the amendments would require. In many cases, the countryside bodies would be under a duty to show information that they were unable to determine. However, we share the desire to see that the public are better informed about the issues addressed in the amendments. I ask the hon. Member for Ashford to recognise that the proposals in the Bill, which I have tried to sketch out more logically, are the right way to go about it and I hope that he will withdraw the amendments.

Appeal procedure

Mr. Thomas: I refer the Under-Secretary and the Committee to clause 7(1), which provides that, in the event of an appeal-the right to which will be confined to those who have an interest in land as defined-two possible avenues exist. the first, a hearing, appears to be more informal than the second, which is a local inquiry. They seem to be alternatives, although the wording does not make that entirely crystal clear.

My amendments address the fact that it seems important, when a hearing falls short of a proper local inquiry, that the views of objectors and those who enjoy access to the countryside should be heard and taken into account. That should be the case if the alternative proceedings of a local inquiry are adopted. The amendment arises out of a perceived need to ensure balance and even-handedness between the interests of those who have a legal estate and those who seek access to the land.

I simply seek an amendment to clause 11(2)(j) to give the Secretary of State and the National Assembly order-making powers in relation to hearing representations from members of the public. Many feel strongly on the matter, and I would be interested to see whether my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary could help, or at least seriously consider the matter.

Mr. Heath: I support the amendments tabled by the hon. Before joining this Committee, I served on the Committee that considered the Freedom of Information Bill. I thought that we had reached the end of the Government's putting provisions in Bills to exclude members of the public who wanted to know what went on in their names. Mr. Gordon Prentice (Pendle): I should like to remind my hon. Mr. Mullin: I am sympathetic to amendment No. 317, which is about whether hearings should ever be held in private. The Government envisage that almost all hearings will be in public.
