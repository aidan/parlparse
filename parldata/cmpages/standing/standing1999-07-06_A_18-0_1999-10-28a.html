Let us assume that the operation of the AIM market is to remain with the London stock exchange. If companies outside London that required a listing, to trade in London, were asked for non-listing prospectuses, the FSA could, we believe, act as the alternative competent authority to the London stock exchange. So the need for non-listing AIM stocks in particular demonstrates the need for the amendment.

In the past few days, we have heard about some of the complications in the London listing markets which have driven two internet companies to seek listings on the Neue Markt and on EASDAQ in preference to London, or even to go for an AIM listing, because of what they claim are excessive costs, red tape and advisory fees. That is a cause of concern and, as the new markets become ever more competitive in their bid to build up business, concern will increase if we are unable to compete on a fair basis with other electronic markets around the world that are now taking over from the more traditional exchanges. So the amendment is still relevant and perhaps we were prescient in tabling it before the summer. I ask the Minister to take it on board.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): I also welcome you back, Mr. Atkinson.

The hon. Gentleman has made further interesting points. On his comments about competition from other exchanges in Europe, the Neue Markt in Germany and NASDAQ in the United States, the stock exchange is well aware of those growing competitive pressures. Not a few United Kingdom-originating companies of the type that he mentioned have chosen to list on NASDAQ. It is an increasingly competitive field, which is no doubt one factor behind the decision by the stock exchange to demutualise and to re-assess its status in order to face up to growing competitive pressures as effectively as possible. I shall return in a moment to the hon. Gentleman's points about AIM.

I hope to convince the hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends that the amendment is both unnecessary and incompatible with EC law. The relevant legislation is the prospectus directive agreed in 1989, which sets out the requirement for a prospectus when a public offer of securities is first made and is not an exempt offer— for example, because it is offered to no more than 50 people.

Any prospectus that is not exempt must meet the directive's requirements, which are implemented in the United Kingdom by the Public Offers of Securities Regulations 1995, familiarly known as POSREG. When an official listing is applied for as well as a public offer, the prospectus must be approved by the competent authority for listing. The requirements are the same as for listing particulars more generally.

When there is no application for listing, there is no requirement under European Community law or in the UK for the prospectus to be pre-vetted. Those issuing the securities remain responsible for meeting the requirements of POSREG. They include the directors of a company if it issues the securities. Failure to comply with the requirements is a criminal offence. When a prospectus contains false or misleading information that has led to loss, those responsible are liable to pay compensation. The matter is serious.

When issuers want to make a public offer in more than one EC country, they can voluntarily submit the prospectus to the competent authority for listing. Article 21(2) of the prospectus directive provides that when a prospectus has been approved by the competent authority for listing in one member state, it must be recognised as complying with the laws of' any other member state in which the public offer is made. That helpfully cuts down on unnecessary and expensive duplication.

The way in which the article is phrased means that the body that approves the prospectus must be the body appointed under the listing particulars directive, which is the competent authority for listing. It cannot be another body. The only circumstance in which another body could be appointed within the terms of the directive to carry out that function would require pre-vetting of all prospectuses.

Mr. Loughton: I am grateful to the Minister. The debate is becoming technical. Are AIM stocks—stocks that are not fully listed—included?

Mr. Timms: I shall refer in a moment to AIM stocks, which are a separate matter.

The hon. Gentleman will accept that pre-vetting of all prospectuses would be an unwarranted and unwelcome increase in the burdens on business. It is clear from the wording of the amendment that that was not the intention. Issuers would not be required to submit prospectuses for pre-vetting under the amendment, so a different competent authority would contravene the directive. That is the central problem.

AIM has nothing to do with official listing. The costs of admission to AIM are a matter for the London stock exchange as an exchange, not for the Committee to debate on the subject of the competent authority.

I hope that the hon. Gentleman accepts that his proposal would be contrary to EC law, assuming that we do not want to introduce massive bureaucracy, and that he will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Timms: The hon. Gentleman's latter point is right. It is necessary to pre-vet a prospectus if the company involved intends to circulate it around Europe and raise funds in other countries in addition to the UK. The competent authority may therefore vet the prospectus if the company's aim is to solicit investment more widely and therefore requires mutual recognition. A prospectus must be produced whenever new securities are issued, but it does not have to be vetted unless the company aims to solicit funds more widely. I hope that that answers the hon. Gentleman's point and that I have explained the position correctly; I am confident that I have.

Mr. Loughton: I do not wish to press the point, but I remain confused. It sounds as though a company that wants to trade on AIM in this country, whether it is based in the UK or on the continent, may solicit funds from UK investors without being subject to any prospectus requirements, and that only companies that want a full listing in London will be subjected to the rigorous prospectus standards. That is my interpretation of what the Financial Secretary said. If it is correct, there is one heck of a difference between the requirements for an AIM company and those for a listed company. However, as I said earlier, the only difference between an AIM-traded company and a fully listed company is that one is a junior, higher risk version of the other. Why, therefore, should one be subject to closer scrutiny than the other, especially in relation to the terminology of any prospectus that it circulates in order to solicit funds from the AIM market? I remain unclear about the requirements for non-listing prospectuses for AIM stocks.

Mr. Timms: I shall attempt to clarify matters further. The fact that prospectuses for new public offers that will not be fully listed do not have to be pre-„vetted does not mean that those responsible for producing them can do what they want. Prospectuses such as those to which the hon. Gentleman refers remain subject to POSREG—the Public Offers of Securities Regulations. Failure to comply with those regulations is a criminal offence. People cannot do whatever they like. The question is whether they require pre-vetting in order to circulate their prospectuses in other countries in addition to the UK. That is the distinction that must be made. If they do not, they can proceed, while remaining subject to the Public Offers of Securities Regulations. The exchange—in the example to which the hon. Gentleman refers, AIM—makes the rules for admission to trading. It is not a matter for the competent authority.

Mr. Loughton: I shall not pursue the point, or we shall be completely swamped in a welter of acronyms. I shall defer to the omnipotence of POSREG, about which I am sure we shall all go home and read more. It is a reasonable anxiety, and if Labour Members remain confused, they will raise questions later. Perhaps the Financial Secretary might like to consider the matter in a little more detail, but we shall not press the amendment at this stage. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Clause 93

Exemption from liability in damages

Mr. Loughton: I beg to move amendment No. 371, in page 42, line 33, at end insert—

    `(aa) to the competent authority, if the act or omission is shown to have been unreasonable, reckless or negligent; or'.

We have now reached a meatier part of the Bill and are revisiting our old friends unreasonableness, recklessness and negligence in the context of statutory immunity applied to the FSA. In the dim and distant past before the recess we had a lengthy discussion about whether the FSA, acting as the regulatory authority, should have statutory immunity. My right hon. and hon. Friends argued forcefully that it should not, but our view did not prevail.

Apparently the FSA, in its likely role as the competent authority for listing, will also have the full umbrella of immunity in dealing with its duties as the listing authority, just as other parts, divided by whatever Chinese walls we are promised in the FSA organisation, will act as the regulatory authority. We feel that that is highly unjustified, Obviously we thought that it was unjustified that it should have the degree of immunity in its regulatory activities. There is even less of a case to extend the immunity to its new found role as the competent authority for listing.

I do not know whether the stock exchange currently enjoys immunity from prosecution or compensation if it makes a muck of its job as the competent authority for listing. However, the stock exchange does not have that immunity, so it would be worrying if it were conferred on the FSA in its new role.

A company that seeks a listing and applies to the FSA as the competent authority for approval—it might not have to wait up to six months if it is lucky—takes part in a straightforward commercial activity. It pays a listing fee and agrees to abide by the rules of the listing requirements; in return, it is listed by the competent authority. As a quoted company, it is assisted in raising capital on the markets and by the respectability conferred on it.

However, let us imagine what might happen to a perfectly respectable blue chip company that applies to be listed by the competent authority, the FSA. The company might undergo all the necessary scrutiny, provide all the necessary paperwork and pay all the necessary fees up front. However, through no fault of its own—we covered this territory in part this morning—it might not be listed within six months because of negligence on the part of members of staff in the competent authority's listing department. That negligence would mean that the six-month period would be up and that the company's application was deemed to have failed, as we discussed on an earlier clause.

The Minister mentioned compensation and that it did not militate against the company reapplying for listing. However, there is the matter of timing, because the company might have lost an important window of opportunity. It will probably be coming to the market, as is often the case, because it wants to raise funds to take over another firm, launch an expansion exercise in another part of the world or introduce a new product in the market. If it does not receive access to listing within the time scale necessary to facilitate its fundraising exercise—that might be as much as six months—it stands to lose an awful lot of money. Under the Bill, however, that commercial company will have absolutely no comeback against the FSA, as the competent authority, as regards compensation for incompetence, negligence, recklessness or unreasonableness. That is more patently unfair than even the statutory immunity that has been extended to the FSA in the pursuit of its regulatory authority duties.

We are talking about an arrangement that is little different from a straightforward commercial transaction. If someone in the commercial world applied to become a member of a club or signed up to a group, and the relevant goods and services were not forthcoming, he would have recourse to the law to sue for compensation for the injury that he had been done. However, just because the listing authority happens to be under the umbrella of the FSA for the time being—it is unlikely that that will change in the foreseeable future, other than in extreme circumstances—it is tough luck on a company that legitimately applies for listing to the FSA, as the competent authority. That is entirely unfair.

We have, therefore, tabled the amendment, which would introduce specific exclusions from exemption for three straightforward misdemeanours on the part of the competent authority. I strongly urge the Committee to adopt it.
