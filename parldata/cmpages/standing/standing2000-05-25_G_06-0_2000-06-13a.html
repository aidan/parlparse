The Minister will not like me for asking him a question that goes back to one of our basic tenets, but I must put it to him: why should not the same circumstances apply in respect of a public hospital? With the application of clinical governance, we envisage that shortcomings in the performance of medical practitioners will become much more apparent to consumers of health care, whether public or private. That is one of the reasons why we have argued strongly that public and private health care should be regulated on a level playing field in respect of such shortcomings. Circumstances such as those outlined in the explanatory notes, in which the activity of a medical practitioner is found to have an abnormal set of outcomes, would cause considerable concern and might convince the Secretary of State of the need for an inquiry.

We believe that it is important to amend provision in respect of what happens after an inquiry is established. The purpose of both amendments is to empower the inquiry chairman-or the person who conducts the inquiry, whatever title he is given-to decide whether its findings should be published and to determine the way in which it should be carried out. If somebody has been empowered to manage the inquiry, he or she should also have power to decide the manner in which its outcome is published. In delegating power to the inquiry, the Minister should provide such an ability and not interfere with the outcome-interference that could involve preventing the report's publication. If a matter is so serious that a public inquiry is called for, the outcome of that inquiry should be published. Without the amendment, the Secretary of State can decide not to make the outcome public. In the light of the history of inquiries into health care shortcomings, especially in respect of children, and with the legacy that remains as a framework, it is important that public inquiries held under the Bill's auspices should be conducted in a way that gives the public access to their outcome.

Investigation of a medical practitioner is a very serious matter. A large number of people can be involved in such an investigation, some of whom might be less aware that they are implicated in an inquiry if its details and outcome are not made public. The issue is topical, as the activities of some medical practitioners have recently come to public attention. We know from practical experience that it is only when they do come to public attention that other patients come out of the woodwork, after recognising the name of the practitioner, casting their minds back to their own experience and realising that they were part of the same pattern. If the pattern had been spotted earlier, their experiences and those of others, which may have led to an inquiry being set up to investigate, might have been prevented. It is important to recognise that if an issue is of such a serious nature that an inquiry is established, the public have a right to know its outcome.

We understand why the Secretary of State may consider it necessary to hold part of the inquiry in private. It is clear from the Waterhouse report and other inquiries into cases of child abuse that it is important to protect the victims. Only when it is possible to hold the proceedings in private do vulnerable people feel able to give evidence of their bad experiences. We understand the need for the chairman of an inquiry to call for part of the proceedings to be held in private. However, once an inquiry has been held, nothing that can be learned from it should be hushed up or withheld from the public domain. Therefore, the person responsible for holding the inquiry should be the one who decides the most appropriate method of publication of the inquiry's findings. That person, who has sat through the entire inquiry, is better placed than the Minister, who has not been present throughout and therefore depends on a report at second hand, to take the decision on the best way of bringing the inquiry's conclusions to the attention of the public.

I am sure the Minister will comment on the fact that our proposal reduces the powers of the Minister in some respects, but in others seeks to ensure that the Minister has the power to act. It is important that the delegatory powers of the chairman of an inquiry should run consistently through to the decision about the publication of the outcome of the inquiry; the chairman is best placed to make such a decision.
