Police (Northern Ireland) Bill

Clause 52

Co-operation with police force of Republic of Ireland

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

4.30 pm

Question again, proposed.

Mr. Ken Maginnis (Fermanagh and South Tyrone): As I said earlier, it is not my intention to delay our proceedings on the clause. None the less, we have become confused about the meaning of co-operation in an operational and cross-frontier sense with regard to recruiting and other matters. I am sure that, by now, the Minister will have understood our worries, so perhaps he can sort out the confusion for some members of the Committee.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Northern Ireland (Mr. George Howarth): I welcome you, Mr. Benton, to the Chair this afternoon. The hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone (Mr. Maginnis) was right. I accept that the two matters have become confused, although that has happened unintentionally. The first issue concerns co-operation between the police forces in Ireland—north and south—while the second issue is of lateral entry under clause 52, which, as my hon. Friend the Member for South Down (Mr. McGrady) acknowledged this morning, is a recruitment measure.

Patten's recommendations 157 to 165 relate to north-south policing co-operation. They cover written protocols at recommendation 157, meetings and conferences at recommendation 158, secondment at recommendation 159, liaison officers at recommendation 160, training at recommendation 161, joint disaster planning at recommendation 162, joint investigations on cross-border incidents at recommendation 163, communications at recommendation 164 and joint databases at recommendation 165. As the hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone made clear, there is nothing between us on those issues. We debated earlier the sensible nature of the proposals and how they have been translated properly into the Bill. In some ways, they merely reflect what happens already and promote existing good practice. We have no difficulty with that.

The Government envisage an agreement with the Irish Government that will set out such matters and other sensible issues of co-operation. The board and the Chief Constable will then work out detailed protocols under the auspices of that agreement. Patten's recommendations 119, 127 and 128 deal with the encouragement of applications for lateral entry, including applications from the Garda. The Government support that, but nothing in clause 52 will enable the appointment of 500 Kerry men or even one Kerry man to be a police officer in Northern Ireland in any other way than such appointments can be made at present, which is through open competition. I accept that we are not currently dealing with such matters, but because of the confusion to which the hon. Gentleman referred, and with your indulgence, Mr. Benton, I thought it helpful to make that point clear.

I envisage the policing agreement between the UK and the Republic of Ireland dealing with such issues as equivalence in police rank, experience or professional qualifications in a way that would facilitate lateral entry—which would be good.

The provision deals with issues of co-operation that are common between European states and have a precedent in arrangements between the RUC and the Garda. It will not undermine the autonomy of the police in any way, but will enhance their effectiveness. I hope that, with those assurances and clarifications, the Committee will accept the clause.

Question put and agreed to.

Mr. McGrady: The amendments concern the management and the openness of the new police service. In the past, the human rights of many sections of the community have been abused, and others have felt that their rights were abused. That feeling has been compounded by the fact that the openness of legal, administrative, disciplinary, financial and other aspects of the review and revision of police conduct has been poor. The responsibility for creating the unhappiness and unease also lies with the prosecution service and the courts, the inadequacy of the formal and informal complaints procedures and the weaknesses in the police authority, which has statutory responsibility for policing.

The general duty on the Chief Constable to report and the ability of the board to inquire following a report are cornerstones of any accountable police service, because the powers of report and inquiry go to the heart of police accountability. Paragraph 6.22 of the Patten report says—

    the Policing Board should have the power to require the Chief Constable to report on any issue pertaining to the performance of his functions or those of the police service . . . The grounds on which the Chief Constable might question this requirement should be strictly limited to issues such as those involving national security, sensitive personnel matters and cases before the courts.

The word ``personnel'' is important there, because the Bill refers to ``personal'' matters, which are entirely different.

The Bill does not give due expression to the report's recommendations. In fact, it deviates completely from them. Amendment No. 32 would correct the Bill to reflect the report accurately. The Patten report suggested that the Chief Constable might not have to report on sensitive personnel matters. However, the Bill says ``sensitive personal'' matters, which is much broader than Patten's intention, at least in my interpretation. A personal matter can be anything, but a personnel matter falls within the confines of one's job or employment, or pertains to the force per se. A personal matter may not refer to the force at all. That restriction on the reporting function is excessive, hence the amendment.

Our amendment No. 33 would ensure that personal—or even personnel—matters did not include information about membership of registrable associations. Last week, the Committee considered the declaration of one's membership of, or involvement with, such associations. The amendment would ensure that the intention of clause 47 was not frustrated, as it might be by clause 55.

Our amendment No. 34 would exclude the exception, which was not envisaged by Patten, of

    a matter which is being investigated by a statutory authority.

That exception is unacceptably broad and should be deleted. For example, if the Audit Office conducted a value-for-money investigation into the conduct of the police in a case, paragraph (3)(c) could be used to prevent a report on the human rights issues, because the matter was being investigated by a statutory authority—the Audit Office. I would like to think that the Government did not intend that to be the position. The focus of the report or investigation by the Chief Constable could be entirely different from that of the statutory authority, although both reports related to the same case.

Let us take the Audit Office example a stage further. The report could be refused if the Audit Office investigation contained information relating to the police case, no matter how irrelevant or trivial. Again, we do not think that that was intended. The police service is a statutory authority, thus if a police investigation is established, its report could be refused. That is a contradiction in the Bill.

Amendment No. 35 would exclude an exception that was not envisaged by the commission. The clause refers to information that

    would, or would be likely to, prejudice . . . the administration of justice.

The need for an exception based on the administration of justice is not clear, because the clause already refers to exceptions relating to the likelihood of prejudicing proceedings that have been commenced in a court of law or the prevention or detection of crime, and the apprehension or prosecution of offenders. Again, the amendment is designed to cover an anomaly.

Clause 60

Time limit for complaints and

references to Ombudsman

Mr. McGrady: I beg to move amendment No. 55 in page 31, line 11, after `time''.', insert—

    `(2AA) Any regulations under subsection (2A) shall include an exception permitting the Ombudsman to accept any complaint or investigate any matter which he otherwise could not investigate by virtue of said regulations where in the opinion of the Ombudsman there are exceptional reasons relating to the public interest to do so.'.

The amendment would ensure the full independence of the ombudsman and that her office provides the necessary level of discretion to investigate past matters. The ombudsman should not operate as a truth and reconciliation commission and the office should not be diverted from its main task—investigating complaints of the past. We hope that the person appointed to the office—and her successors—will exercise discretion sensibly, bearing in mind budgetary constraints and other factors. The implementation plan states that the ombudsman should look into matters in relation to the past only in grave and exceptional circumstances or where there is fresh evidence.

The ombudsman should have a general power to look into past matters where it is—if I dare use the phrase—in the public interest to do so. The amendment is competent and reasonable and should gain the Committee's approval.

Mr. Ingram: The amendment would give the ombudsman an override provision to bypass the regulations under clause 60 where, in the ombudsman's opinion, there are exceptional circumstances relating to the public interest to do so. That was the basis on which the amendment was moved.

The Government believe that such a provision is unnecessary and that it would go further than we are prepared to go. The Government's underlying approach to Patten is that it offers a new beginning. The regulations that will apply to the ombudsman have been made available to hon. Members today. I do not expect members of the Committee to be in a position to deal with them in detail, but we are prepared to listen to any representations that they might wish to make. The ombudsman has been consulted on the regulations, and we have taken into account her views on the way in which she will perform her functions.

8.45 pm

Generally, the ombudsman will be able to consider cases in which the conduct complained of arose in the previous 12 months. In her first year of operation, we have agreed with her that the period should be 24 months; thereafter, however, it will be 12 months.

The ombudsman may take on cases provided—it is an important proviso—they have not already been dealt with. She may take over cases that are not closed, but she may re-open cases only in exceptional circumstances, which are defined in the regulations. That is our view and it is in keeping with what Patten said. It will avoid the ombudsman being inundated with complaints about cases that have already been dealt with.

The exceptions will include matters that have not been complained of previously, where it appears to the ombudsman that a grave or exceptional crime or disciplinary matter has been committed. In addition, it will be open to her to investigate cases where new evidence has become available, such as a witness coming forward, if she believes that it suggests that a grave or exceptional offence has been committed.

Where a case concerns a crime that has not previously been dealt with or where there is new evidence—this shows how broad the exceptions are—the Government believe that the ombudsman, as the new body charged with dealing with complaints, should be able to decide how it should be investigated. The regulations provide for that and aim to give her the widest possible discretion to deal with cases that need to be tackled, without opening the way for her to be inundated with requests to rake over cases that have already been dealt with.

That approach has been discussed with the ombudsman and she is comfortable with it. I repeat my opening comment: this is about a new beginning in policing. The ombudsman is clear about her main function, although I do not speak for her in this respect. The tenor of our discussions, however, has been about a new beginning. If she is constantly dragged back into the past, it might detract from our trying to develop that new future. We believe that that would be inimical to what Patten set out in his report and would not be helpful in trying to establish the new future for policing in Northern Ireland.

 

Mr. McGrady: Does the Minister know whether the ombudsman wishes to have greater powers than the regulations permit? What is her opinion?

Mr. Ingram: Discussions between the ombudsman and the Government would have to be protected—only she can articulate her view. The regulations have been drafted in line with her views and she is comfortable with her powers retrospectively to consider matters. The regulations allow her a wider remit in grave and exceptional circumstances. She is satisfied with what we are doing and the regulations are in any event open to examination. If hon. Members wish to comment on the proposal, we would have to take account of that, before consulting the ombudsman on whether she was comfortable with such suggestions. So it is not a closed book; we have given a precise definition of the general way in which her approach should be applied, while leaving a capability for examining the past in grave and exceptional circumstances.

We have married the desire to achieve a new future to the recognition that past matters may require further examination, on the basis of new evidence and on specific grounds. The regulations seek to provide for that. I hope that my hon. Friend will not press the amendment.

Mr. McGrady: The Minister has said several times in our debate, and in his comments on amendment No. 55, that a line should be drawn under the past. His colleague the Under-Secretary has also made that comment. The past exists and its consequences continue. It may be—we do not know until it happens—that some happening, event, misdemeanour or gross negligence that occurred in the immediate past is worthy of investigation. I find it hard to accept the notion that such investigation is allowable elsewhere but not here.

Secondly, there is a continuing theme that we cannot trust to the good judgment of the people we appoint to those important positions. We would be well served in many respects by most of the points that have been made but the responses seem to have missed the aim of the amendment. It is worth quoting the amendment again. It states that

    Any regulations under subsection (2A) shall include an exception permitting the Ombudsman to accept any complaint or investigate any matter which he otherwise could not investigate by virtue of said regulations where in the opinion of the Ombudsman there are exceptional reasons relating to public interest to do so.

That is not giving anyone a blank cheque in terms of inquiries. A responsible occupant of the office of ombudsman would treat his or her responsibilities under the amendment seriously and would act only in exceptional circumstances relating to the public interest. I do not see what is dangerous or offensive about the amendment and I invite the Committee to consider it favourably.

Mr. Ingram: I have tried to set out our recognition that the past exists. That is why we have provided for that exceptional allowance, which in the first year goes back to 24 months and thereafter to 12 months. That is wholly consistent with any other post of ombudsman that has been established. We respect the judgment of the ombudsman as much as that of any other ombudsman who has been appointed, but we are allowing for exceptional circumstances. Indeed, what we establish may be a form of protection for the new post. I have been living with the post of ombudsman since I first introduced it in the 1998 Act. The post does not emerge from Patten; the Government introduced it in the 1998 Act, where we tried to define its parameters. The development of that post was then picked up in Patten and has been further refined and defined.

Members of the Committee must recognise—I know that my hon. Friend would recognise it—that there are people who may want to rake over the past and forget about building the future. We do not have the perfect society in Northern Ireland—or, indeed, anywhere—and some people might use that office for their own reasons. The ombudsman must therefore have some parameters within which to operate. The provision will give her protection in making a judgment that is consistent with the law and the power that she has been given. It will allow her to get on with the job of looking to the future to deal with the new policing environment, in which she will be responsible for dealing with complaints.

Mr. McGrady: Does the Minister not believe that it would be appropriate and proper in exceptional circumstances, and for exceptional causes in the public interest, to allow the ombudsman to investigate?

Mr. Ingram: That has not been ruled out, but it has been defined, which will offer the ombudsman protection and allow her to point to the parameters of her power, so that if grave and exceptional circumstances or new evidence are involved, or if the case touches on a grave and exceptional issue, she may proceed. If the power were left open-ended and we provided a blank cheque, she would be obliged to respond to every case brought to her attention, and while trying to look to the future she would be trapped in the past, because so many people will want to drag her into examining its entrails.

The Patten report contains a vision for the future, in which the ombudsman plays a part, but we are allowing the recognition that, although the past may require consideration, a proven case must be involved. On that basis, we believe that we are giving the ombudsman a powerful remit. We are effectively covering my hon. Friend's points but are defining the matter differently.
