Amendment No. 130 would establish arrangements to monitor police performance in public order situations. It, too, is designed to ensure full and faithful implementation of the Patten report. The recommendation is also unusual because it specifically states that the Policing Board should have that role. Given our experiences over many years, it would be incongruous not to include the monitoring of public order situations in the list of 12 specified in the clause. The issue is crucial. We have seen how often the management of such situations affects the whole north of Ireland. We see it year after year, beginning around Easter and lasting until some time in October. Drumcree is not just a matter for Portadown. It concerns people throughout the north of Ireland and the well-being of the entire political process there. The purpose of the amendment cannot be achieved obliquely or by implication. The Bill implies it in places, but to obtain the Policing Board and arrangements that we want, we should make matters specific in the Bill.

The amendments faithfully represent the views set out by Patten. I am convinced that to omit from clause 2 any requirement to deal with the code of ethics, public order and interrogation centres would be to leave a huge gap-not just legislatively, but in terms of credibility.

Mr. Maginnis: I do not intend to take much of the Committee's time with the amendments. Amendment No. 159A refers to the board and to arrangements for the provision of administrative, secretarial and other assistance for the police by persons employed in the civil service. The amendment is simple. Arrangements should be implemented after consultation with the Chief Constable. That is reasonable; it makes sense that the Chief Constable should be able to submit his requirement to the board before decisions are taken on the deployment of civil servants to work with the police.

I doubt that I need to say anything on that matter with regard to amendment No. 160A. The amendment might evoke a little opposition because it would affect senior employees, who are defined by subsection (7) as those of

    such class or description as may be specified for the purposes of this section by the Board with the approval of the Secretary of State.

It is important that some criteria are applied to senior employees in relation to matters that we have already discussed. Such people should be disqualified from the support staff if, at any time, they have been guilty of an offence in Northern Ireland or elsewhere and have received a sentence of imprisonment, whether or not it was suspended. We believe that a member of the police support staff should have his employment terminated if he is found guilty of an offence after his appointment. Again, it is important that the bankruptcy criterion should apply to senior employees, who might handle large amounts of money or be responsible for financial matters. After our debate on the issue, I need do no more than emphasise the need for instituting some way of dealing with that situation in relation to police support staff.

Amendment No. 161A results from 160A and provides that subsections (6) and (8) shall apply to persons transferred to the employment of the board as they apply to persons employed by the board and to those who are not senior employees. The bankruptcy provision would not apply, but people who have a criminal conviction would be disqualified. I do not wish to take up any more of the Committee's time. The amendments introduce essential measures relating to staff who will handle the most sensitive issues with and on behalf of the police, where one would hope that propriety would be the order of the day.
