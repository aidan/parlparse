Clause 40

Variation etc on the Authority's own initiative

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 225, in page 18, line 30, after `firm', insert `or a Treaty firm'.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No. 226, in page 18, line 32, at end insert `or Schedule 4, as the case may be'.

Mr. Tim Loughton (East Worthing and Shoreham): Before lunch, we were tripping around the subject matter of amendments Nos. 225 and 226—the way in which the authority can vary or cancel commissions. Clause 40 contains further inconsistencies between European Economic Area firms and firms committed under the treaty. Amendment No. 225 would insert after `firm' the words `or a Treaty firm'.

As I understand the clause, the Financial Services Authority cannot exercise its case D powers in respect of an EEA firm—a non-united Kingdom firm established in the EEA that is authorised under a single market directive when the permission is granted automatically under schedule 3, which deals with EEA passporting rights that we have discussed at length. Such action is required by the directions. Surely the same exemption should apply to schedule 4, which covers firms that are committed under the treaty rights. We have also broadly considered that issue; it is what implements in United Kingdom law the passport granted to EEA firms under the treaty of Rome. We are concerned about consistency.

I should like to hear from the Minister, but the Treasury may not want to be limited on the basis that it will be allowed in the interests of the FSA and of the public good to stop incoming firms using their treaty of Rome rights to establish a branch or provide services on a cross-border basis, except where a directive provides otherwise. The amendment would subject the treaty firms to the same terms.

Amendment No. 226 would add the words

    ``or Schedule 4, as the case may be''

at the end of subsection (3) after reference is made to ``Schedule 3''. We may have missed something, but we had discourse earlier about how there seemed to be two ways of dealing with different firms that are permitted under the various schedules. I should be grateful if the Economic Secretary could explain the position and to know whether the clause may need amending by the Government's draftsmen.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson): The amendments start from the assumption that case D grounds for intervention—on behalf of the Director General of Fair Trading—should apply equally to a treaty firm as they do to an EEA firm. Case D grounds arise when the Director General of Fair Trading has informed the authority that an authorised person has done any of the things that are listed under section 25(2) of the Consumer Credit Act 1974.

I should refer to whether the factors in the case of a person who holds a licence under that Act are relevant to the director general's judgment of whether a person is fit and proper to carry on a consumer credit business. For ease of reference, I shall set out briefly what such factors cover. They deal with contraventions of any provision of the 1974 Act; offences involving fraud, dishonesty or violence, practising racial or other forms of discrimination and engaging in deceitful, oppressive, unfair or improper business practices. The purpose of case D is to ensure that the authority can exercise its powers in respect of a consumer credit business if the authorised person was also a licence holder under the 1974 Act. Enabling the authority to act in these circumstances is an important part of our attempt to ensure that the authorisation under the Bill for United Kingdom firms can legitimately be said to cover consumer credit for the purposes of ensuring that UK firms can exercise their passport rights in other European economic area states to the fullest extent.

As the hon. Gentleman noted, the grounds for intervention cannot be used to restrict a part IV permission granted to an EEA firm if the action would affect, other than incidentally, the exercise of an EEA firm's passport rights. Part XII of the Bill deals with intervention affecting passport rights.

The aim of the amendments is to ensure that carve-out from case D applies to a treaty firm in the same way as to an EEA firm. I have some sympathy with the hon. Gentleman's reasoning and am grateful to him for bringing the issue to our attention. However, with treaty firms, the extent of what is permissible under Community law is determined by the principle of the general good, which I understand was the subject of some discussion on Tuesday. The general good principle allows a host state to take action or impose requirements directed at a public policy objective only where the actions or requirement are properly directed at the objective, are proportionate and do not duplicate the effects of actions taken by, or requirements imposed by, the home state.

We think it possible that the authority could take an action under case D as drafted that would confirm the general good principle but would affect the carrying on of the treaty right activity in a manner that was more than incidental. We do not wish to rule out taking such action in those circumstances; we think that would be the amendment's effect.

I thank the hon. Gentleman for drawing this interesting and subtle facet of the clause to our attention but should be grateful if, in the light of my explanations, he would withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Loughton: I am grateful to the Minister for highlighting that subtle facet, if that is what it was, and for illuminating a little further the Treasury's thinking on it. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave. withdrawn.

Clause 41

Variation of Permission on acquisition of control

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): I beg to move amendment No. 228, in page 19, line 1, leave out

    `likely'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 229, in page 19, line 2, leave out

    `uncertain' and insert `likely to prejudice the continued satisfaction of the qualifying conditions by the authorised person'.

Mr. Flight: The amendments attempt to clarify, and so limit, the powers of the FSA where it is unclear what the relevant financial situation has to be before the authority can vary an authorised person's permission under subsection (2). The first amendment would remove the word ``likely''; the second would delete the word ``uncertain'' and substitute the phrase

    ``likely to prejudice the continued satisfaction of the qualifying conditions by the authorised person''.

Miss Melanie Johnson: I understand the thinking that led the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) to question the ground on which the power in clause 41 may be exercised. This power has its origins in the Insurance Companies Act 1982, and is generally relevant to prudential supervision but is especially relevant where an insurance company is taken over. The difficulty, or risk, that the clause addresses is that the takeover of a company creates uncertainty of a magnitude similar to when a company is first set up.

I can perhaps best illustrate the point with the following example. Let us imagine that a well-established insurance company has been taken over, perhaps by a firm with no track record in insurance, in the relevant type of insurance or, more generally, in doing business in the UK, whereas the company taken over has a good track record and a substantial business. Over the years, it will have managed to build substantial reserves to back the risks that it has written.

The regulator's concern would be that the new owners will inevitably bring a new business style. This may involve changing the way in which they assess and price risks. Even slight changes may materially affect the overall financial standing of the company, and it is quite possible that the new owners would not be aware of the potential impact of their policies. In such circumstances, the regulator may want to take steps—for example, by limiting the amount of business that the company may take on, until a track record has been established.

Another concern is that an insurance company may be an attractive target because of its substantial reserves. Although rules require the maintenance of a certain margin of solvency over and above the level of the estimated liabilities of the firm, it is common practice for insurance companies—for commercial reasons—substantially to over-reserve for their liabilities. It is because insurance companies maintain such high levels of assets that failures are often avoided during periods of a downturn in business. But there may be a temptation for an acquiring firm to want to put those assets to other uses—for example, to support a massive expansion of the company's business.

Those things are not bad in themselves, and there is nothing to stop companies from adopting new business strategies without a change of ownership. However, there is a potentially serious concern when a new owner with no established track record seeks to do those things.

In some case that would put into question compliance with the qualifying conditions for authorisation: but that will often not be the case, because the regulator simply does not have the knowledge about the new owners to take that view. None the less, there is a risk to the interests of existing and potential policy holders, and the interests of third parties who may need to claim against the insurance cover of those policy holders.

In order to reduce the risk, therefore, the insurance regulator will seek to take steps that will ensure that the business does not make any sudden changes to its business plan, or that if it is to make such changes the impact will not work to the material detriment of existing customers.

So what are the constraints on the exercise of the power? Clearly, as I have described them, the powers conferred on the Financial Services Authority by clause 41 are wide ranging.

In reality, the exercise of the powers is severely constrained. The authority will be able to exercise the power only in a way that is reasonable and proportionate. If it seeks to impose restrictions and conditions that are excessive and unjustified, the company concerned will have a right to refer the case to the financial services and markets tribunal. The authority will therefore need to have good reason to be concerned about the risks to consumers following the change of control, and it will need to have good reason for adopting any course of action. However, as I have explained, those reasons may need to address concerns that go somewhat wider than simply the likely compliance with the qualifying conditions.

This power, as drafted in the Bill, is widely used and highly valued by those currently regulating insurance companies. There is a real concern that restricting its use could place policy holders at risk.

I hope that Opposition Members will find that explanation helpful, accept the validity of the power on the terms presented in the Bill and withdraw the amendment.

 

Mr. Flight: I thank the Minister for her explanation. We are happy to withdraw the amendment. It remains unclear, however, whether the Government are happy about using the format from the insurance industry in a much wider context and applying it to areas other than insurance.

Miss Johnson: It is presented as a power to do something, but we are not prescribing the conditions under which it might operate. I was providing merely an illustration to convey the circumstances that we envisage as relevant. It is difficult to envisage all the situations that might arise.

Mr. Flight: I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Mr. Flight: The first amendment is a probing measure that seeks to establish when the FSA can use its power to vary or cancel a part IV permission in order to help an overseas regulator. The clause reproduces existing powers under the Financial Services Act 1986. Subsection (3) relates to help that that FSA could try to provide under a community obligation. Amendment No. 233 is also a probing amendment. We want to know whether paragraph (c) is a restricted example of a subsection (1) case or, as we think, an additional case not limited by the prescribed test in subsection (1).

On amendment No. 234, the FSA should not be told only to consider whether it is necessary to exercise the power to comply with a community obligation: it should be necessary for it to do so. It must exercise that power, which is the purpose of the amendment.

Amendments Nos. 235, 230, 231 and 245 would clarify the fact that subsection (4) is additional to earlier provisions. Initially, we thought that restrictions in subsection (3) ought to be included in subsection (4). However, on reflection, that may limit too much the circumstances in which help may be given. The wider the circumstances in which the United Kingdom may give help to an overseas regulator, the more likely is the United Kingdom to receive reciprocal help.

4.45 pm

Subsection (7) makes it clear that help may be given under subsection (4) only if the overseas regulator is of a prescribed kind. The Treasury does not want to restrict that further to the overseas regulator acting under specified circumstances. We go along with that.

Miss Melanie Johnson: The intention behind clause 42 is to give the FSA the power, when appropriate, to act in support of overseas regulators. To that end, it makes a distinction between two types of request: those in which the FSA is obliged by our commitments under the European Economic Area agreement to render assistance and those in which there is no such obligation.

Amendments Nos. 230 to 235 would interfere with that policy. Subsection (1) creates a basic power for the FSA to pass information

to overseas regulators of a kind prescribed by the Treasury. Subsections (3) and (4) set out the conditions under which that will work in cases in which the FSA is responding to a mandatory EEA request or a non-mandatory request. However, amendment No. 230 refers to a category of cases that fall outside subsection (1), which, by definition, includes all the requests that the FSA receives from overseas regulators.

Amendment No. 231 is based on a similar misunderstanding. There is no need to prescribe that requests under subsection (4) will be made by regulators outside the UK or will be of a prescribed kind because that has already been done under subsection (1).

Furthermore, it is not desirable to give the Treasury a power to specify the occasions on which the FSA may respond, as it is impossible to know in advance every type of case in which the FSA may wish to respond to a request from an overseas regulator. The four principles in subsection (4) are sufficient to provide the FSA with guidance on dealing with such matters on a case-by-case basis.

Amendment No. 232 reveals a misunderstanding of the twin-track approach taken by the provisions in relation to clause 42(1). The amendment would alter clause 42(3), which refers to mandatory EEA requests. The words that the amendment would remove are necessary, as they enable the Treasury to give guidance to the FSA about which categories of request it should regard as mandatory.

Subsection (3) gives the Treasury power to provide guidance on what constitutes a mandatory request. However, in practice it will clearly be necessary for the FSA to decide whether any request that it receives falls into the mandatory or non-mandatory category. Amendments Nos. 233 and 234 are unhelpful, as they remove the words that make that clear.

Amendment No. 235 is unnecessary, as it removes the words that make it clear that subsection (4) deals with non-mandatory requests, in contrast to the mandatory EEA requests that are dealt with under subsection (3).

Amendment No. 245 refers to the FSA's ``own initiative powers'', and makes it clear that those would be given and could be used only in circumstances specified in clauses 40 to 42. However, that is already clear in the present text of the Bill, so there is no need for the amendment. I therefore urge my hon. Friends to resist the amendment, should the hon. Gentleman press it to a vote.

Mr. Flight: We are in the delicate territory of EU and passport-related matters again. Our interpretation of the amendments is somewhat different from the Government's. That is not a huge political issue and we shall let it rest, but it would be helpful if the Treasury's and our advisers could get together to resolve the different interpretations of these matters. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Accordingly, the amendment would make it clear that there is no need for the own-initiative power actually to have been exercised before the right to refer to the tribunal arises. It is a matter of timing. The right to refer the matter to the tribunal must be spelt out in that way, because the decision notice procedures do not give a right to refer the matter to the tribunal, but only require notification of any right that the recipient of the decision notice has to refer the matter, and it is clause 52 that confers that right.

Mr. Timms: I do not think that the amendment would help. I understand the hon. I wish to reassure the Committee that, after the re-organisation of some of the clauses to which I referred this afternoon, it is our intention that there will be a clear path for anyone aggrieved under any of the provisions in part IV of the Bill to go to the tribunal and to seek redress there. Mr. Loughton: On the basis that the Government's intentions have been described as honourable, we can end the debate in a spirit of concord. I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
