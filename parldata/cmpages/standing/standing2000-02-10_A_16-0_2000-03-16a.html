Clause 33

Transfer of electricity licences

10 am

The Chairman: I call the Minister to move Amendment No. 33-I am sorry, amendment No. 589.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): I beg to move amendment No. 589, in page 31, line 3, at end insert-

    `(6A) The Authority shall give the Secretary of State not less than 28 days' notice of any proposal to impose a modification condition.

    (6B) If, before the expiry of the time specified in a notice under subsection (6A), the Secretary of State directs the Authority not to impose the condition, the Authority shall comply with the direction.'.

    Given that the House adjourned at 10 minutes to 3 this morning, I could well read this as amendment No. 33.

    The amendment provides a mechanism whereby the Secretary of State may veto a proposal by the authority to impose a modification condition on a licence that is to be transferred. The reason is that, as he or she will have created the standard licence conditions in the first place, he or she will have a responsibility, as with other condition modifications, to ensure that amendments made by the authority do not undermine the basis on which the conditions were approved by Parliament.

    There is a similar provision in the equivalent gas clause-clause 65-but it was overlooked in drafting clause 33.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): Clause 33 introduces a procedure enabling a licence to be transferred, in whole or in part, to another person, subject to the authority's approval. The amendment gives the Secretary of State a power to prevent such a modification. As the Minister said, that brings the provision into line with the Gas Act 1986.

The Opposition do not oppose the amendment. The transfer of licences from one company to another is essential in a dynamic market. In the years ahead, there will be consolidation and changes of ownership, as well as new entrants into the market. As long as there is no abuse of the dominant market position, the Government should do all that they can to ease such movements and transfers. The Competition Act 1998 will deal with any problems of abuse of a dominant position. Competition keeps management on its toes and is the lever that introduces new techniques and practices, which provide greater consumer choice and more opportunities as well as more efficient processes and lower prices.

I note that this is another of the many amendments that the Government have tabled in order to deal with bad drafting. There have already been up to 216 Government amendments, but there will be more to come.

Amendment Agreed to.

Mr. Gibb: Clause 34 ensures that the regulatory authority and the Secretary of State give reasons for, and publish, their decisions. The decisions to which the clause refers are the granting of an exemption, the revocation of a licence, the modification of the conditions of a licence, the giving of directions and the determination of questions and disputes in the making of a final order. However, there is no reference to the granting of licences in that respect.

Section 38A of the Gas Act 1995, which amends the Gas Act 1986, requires reasons to be given. It is to be replaced, under clause 67, with new section 38A. The original section implies that as well as enabling the licence holder to make representations-which clauses 34 and 67 do not-it applies to the granting of licences. Section 38A(3) states:

    This subsection confers power to provide that, where the Director makes a specified decision in relation to a licence holder, the Director shall as soon as practicable give to the licence holder a written notice explaining why it appeared to him to be appropriate to make the decision.

Subsection (7) states:

    In this section-

    ``decision'' means any decsion under this Part.

That includes the granting of licence. It continues:

    references to a licence holder include references to an applicant for a licence fee.

The 1986 Act thus requires the reasons to be given not only for modifications to licences, but for the granting of, or failure to grant, the licence. Will the Minister tell the Committee whether that is a correct reading of the legislation? If so, why have decisions on the granting of licences been removed? Why does the 1986 Act refer to representations by licence holders, but the Bill does not?

In competitive activities such as the supply of gas and electricity, there is, in effect, a presumption in favour of granting licences without discrimination to all applicants who satisfy the relevant conditions. Consequently, the only reason that can be given for granting a licence is that the applicant fulfils all such conditions. However, much is obvious from the fact of granting a licence, so there is nothing to be gained from a statutory requirement for GEMA to give reasons for such a decision.

The only licences to which the presumption might not apply relate to activities such as electricity transmission, where there are issues of geographical exclusivity. In such cases, the granting of a new licence encroaches on the authorised area of an existing licence holder. That is why, in such cases, provisions dealing with the granting of new licences enable an extensive consultation procedure, including the requirement that GEMA gives reasons why it proposes to grant such licences. It seems unnecessary to duplicate that requirement here. In the light of that explanation, I hope that the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton will withdraw his amendment.

I turn to amendments Nos. 507 and 553. Clauses 34 and 67 will require the authority to give reasons for regulatory decisions that involve the exercise of any decision-making powers included in a specific list. As the hon. Member for Lewes (Mr. Baker) said, the amendments would broaden that provision by requiring the authority to explain any other decision that materially affects the interests of consumers.

The amendment raises difficulties that may not be obvious at first sight, and it may assist the Committee if I briefly explain how we reached our position. We wanted to address the regulators' key decisions, and the response to consultation document did exactly that. It states:

    The Government confirms that it intends to introduce a duty on the regulators to publish reasons for key decisions.

The difficulty was in pinning down what was meant by a ``key'' decision. The most logical approach was to focus the test on the substantive effect of a regulatory decision.

The amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Lewes would, in effect, define a key decision as one that materially affects consumers. However, an approach that relies on the effects of a decision would leave the authority uncertain as to what decisions are subject to the publication duty. As the hon. Gentleman will agree, too broad an interpretation of what is ``key'' or ``material'' could result in an unreasonable burden. It would generate a vast amount of information, much of which would be of little benefit to those affected by the decision.

Valuable information would lay bare the regulator's approach to substantive issues, but the danger is that it would be lost in the chaff of irrelevant information.

Dr. Howells: Clauses 34 and 67 oblige GEMA and the Secretary of State to publish notices setting out the reasons for key decisions. In addition to a requirement to publish such notices, the clauses require a copy of the notice to be sent to the licence holder at the centre of the decision-at least in cases that involve a licence holder.

Taken together, amendments Nos. 608 and 611 are intended to clarify two matters in relation to that process. First, they clarify which types of decision trigger the duty to send a copy of the notice to the relevant licence holder. That includes all the decisions in clauses 34 and 67, except those to grant exemptions which clearly do not relate to licence holders and the determination of disputes referred to GEMA under section 23 of the Electricity Act 1989 or section 27A of the Gas Act 1986. The requirement to send a copy of the notice to the licence holder need not apply because the procedures for determination of such disputes already ensure that, as a party to the dispute, the relevant licence holder will be notified of the reasons for the determination.

Secondly, the amendments have been designed to reduce the risk that the requirement to give reasons under clauses 34 and 67 might unintentionally affect any independent right that those who are directly affected by a decision might have to be notified of the decision and the reasons for it.

The primary purpose of clauses 34 and 67 is to improve the transparency of decision-making for all those with an interest in regulation. To the extent that those directly affected by a regulatory decision have an independent right to receive reasons for decisions-perhaps under the developing principles of administrative law-nothing in clauses 34 and 67 should be taken as detracting from that right. That might be important, for example, in relation to a decision for which the reasons contain a good deal of confidential information. Much of that confidential information might properly be excluded from the reasons given under clauses 34 and 67 because of the application of the ``serious and prejudicial'' test in clause 34(4). However, there would be no reason to exclude that confidential information from the reasons given to a party directly affected by a decision if that information was only confidential because it related to that party. It would be wrong, therefore, if clauses 34 and 67 were interpreted so as to prevent reasons containing such information from being given to such persons if they would otherwise have a right to receive it.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss Government amendments Nos. 616 to 620, Nos. 630 and 631, and Nos. 633 and 634.

Dr. Howells: I beg the indulgence of Committee members in discussing this substantial group of amendments. Unfortunately, the provisions are rather complex, and I hope that I will be forgiven if I explain them in some detail. Given that they cover much of the next part of the Bill, I want to be as helpful as I can. They make an important change to the arrangements for connecting customers and embedded generators to the distribution system.

Clauses 25 and 27 introduce distribution licences, replace the two existing types of supply licence with one, and require that the same person may not hold both types of licence. As a result, there will no longer be any such thing as a public electricity supplier. That apparently straightforward change has far-reaching structural implications because so much of the Electricity Act 1989 is constructed around the obligations that are laid on public electricity suppliers and the rights they are given. It has therefore been necessary carefully to consider all those powers and duties and determine what is to be done with them. In each case, we have had to consider whether the relevant right or duty should remain a statutory provision and, if so whether it is a matter for distributors or for suppliers.

Clauses 36 to 39 and new clause 7, which I shall discuss later, deal with a very important aspect of distributors' duties and powers-namely, their duty to offer terms for a connection, which I shall occasionally refer to as their duty to connect. Sections 16 to 22 of the 1989 Act, which the clauses amend, deal with the existing duty of public electricity suppliers to give a supply. We now need to separate the provisions relating to supply and the provisions relating to distribution. On the whole, we thought it best to retain in primary legislation all the statutory powers and duties of existing public electricity suppliers that relate to distribution. Distribution remains, and is likely to remain for the forseeable future, a natural monopoly activity on which we all depend for electricity supplies. In the case of supply, given that there is now full competition, we felt it appropriate to adopt a more flexible approach. Some of the powers and duties that have hitherto been statutory will become matters for licence conditions or supply contracts.

I shall say no more about supply now, other than that we will shortly table amendments in the form of new schedules to replace schedules 6 and 7 of the 1989 Act. Those will spell out in detail the necessary statutory arrangements for supply and related activities such as metering. Sections 36 to 41 deal with the duty of licensed distributors to connect, as well as several associated matters-principally the financial arrangements relating to the duty and terms of agreements made in relation to the duty.

Amendment No. 615 inserts new sections 16 and 16A into the 1989 Act in place of the existing section 16. Subsection (1) of new section 16 places all licensed distributors under a duty to provide a connection when required, in accordance with such terms as may be agreed under the procedures provided for in section 16A. Under the subsection, a licensed distributor has a duty to offer terms for making a connection to anyone who requires a connection to premises or other distribution systems operated by distributors that are authorised by licence or exemption.

In the case of premises, the owner or occupier will be able to request that a connection be made. In addition, an electricity supplier, acting with the consent of the owner or occupier, will also be able to require a connection. Extending the duty to include requests made by suppliers on behalf of their customers is consistent with our general policy that customers-especially small customers-should, if they wish, be able to use suppliers as a one-stop shop for all of their electricity requirements.
