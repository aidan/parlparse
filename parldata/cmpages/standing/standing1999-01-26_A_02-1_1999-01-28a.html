Clause 10

Filling a vacancy in an Assembly constituency

Mr. Paul Burstow (Sutton and Cheam: I beg to move amendment No. 6, in page 7, line 13, leave out "six" and insert "three".

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 13, in Clause 16, page 9, line 36, leave out "six" and insert "three".

Mr. Burstow: The amendment highlights our concern about the provisions in respect of members resigning or leaving the authority for one reason or another and creating a vacancy for six months. We understand that this clause is modelled on the standard provision that one would find in most local authorities if a member leaves and creates a vacancy.

One of the most crucial times of the year for the Greater London authority will be in the period from Christmas to February, when the authority is deliberating on its budgets. The mayor will be making his or her proposals to the assembly and the assembly will be involved in moving amendments to the budget. An important process will be going on.

When the GLA is engaged in that process, a particular constituency, such as Croydon and Sutton, could go unrepresented. It is important that constituents are represented and have their views properly reflected in the assembly while it is scrutinising and checking the mayor's budget proposals. We therefore believe that a period of three months rather than six months is more appropriate when vacancies are to be filled in a new and unique institution. We hope that the Government might be willing to consider that point.

If three months is, for administrative reasons, inappropriate, perhaps another limit could be found. We feel that to deny local residents the benefit of a representative advocating on their behalf in the assembly and taking their views into account when the budget is being checked would be a shame, to say the least. We hope to receive a positive response from the Government.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Kate Hoey): I, too, welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Stevenson. I hope that the afternoon will be interesting.

I sympathise with the objective of the hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam to ensure that the people of London are properly represented by the people whom they have elected to the important offices of mayor or constituency member. However, I cannot support the effect that his proposal would have. If the hon. Gentleman thinks seriously about the matter I am not suggesting that he has not, but perhaps he would like to think about it a little more he will find that the proposal is impracticable. In certain, admittedly unusual, circumstances the provision could result in a mayoral by-election or a constituency member by-election, or possibly both, taking place only weeks before the next full, ordinary election is held.

That is because subsection (5) in line with the provisions for the rest of local government provides for a by-election to take place

    "no later than 35 days"

    after the date on which a vacancy is declared. In practice, because of the complex administrative arrangements that will need to be put in place particularly in the case of the London-wide mayoral election it will usually be necessary to use all of those 35 days to organise and hold the elections.

    Let us take 1999 as a hypothetical example and assume that the first Thursday in May 6 May is the next ordinary election date, and that the mayor dies or[Kate Hoey] resigns in the final week of the fourth month before the next ordinary election for example, on 31 January. Under the amendment, a by-election would have to be held not later than Friday 19 March. That would leave the new mayor in office for only six and a half weeks, or 38 working days, before having to represent himself at the full election.

    The cost implications of holding two major elections in close succession cost implications for the public and for the parties would be substantial. That has convinced us that the proposal is wrong and likely to be unfair. Although even the poorest of the major political parties might be able to afford the expense, there would certainly be a significant disadvantage for smaller independent candidates.

    I recognise that deciding at what point such cut-off dates should be introduced will always be arbitrary and cannot be a scientific act, and that it is therefore never easy to guarantee that the correct date has been chosen. However, I believe that the six months that we have included in the Bill strikes the correct balance between the need to ensure that Londoners and their interests are properly and effectively represented, and the need to avoid unnecessary, uneconomical and impracticable arrangements.

    The hon. Gentleman mentioned the budget, which is an important part of any local authority or Government cycle. However, under his proposal, the elections would be going on anyway and the member would not take his place in the assembly until after the budget procedures were completed. Although we do not want anyone to be unrepresented at any time, such things happen; they are a fact of life, and six months is a fair amount of time. I ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Burstow: I have listened to the Minister's arguments, and her concern over administrative difficulties is reasonable. We wished to test the Government's intentions by moving the amendment, and we hope that they will continue to consider a change from six months, although a decision will not necessarily be made today. In view of the Minister's comments, we shall reconsider the amendment and perhaps return to the subject at a later date. Therefore, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Ottaway: For reasons that are not clear, two issues have been put together in this group of amendments. I will speak first to amendments Nos. 138 and 149. Amendment No. 149 would amend schedule 3, which deals with vacancies on the party list. I will then speak about the issue of vacancies on the individual list, which is dealt with by amendments Nos. 136 and 137.

Under the proposals whereby London members are elected by means of the top-up system the additional member system and under the clauses that we have already passed, a vacancy can occur. If there is a vacancy for a London member elected from the party list of a registered political party, the next person on the list will move up into that slot to fill the vacancy. That is what we hope is intended, but we believe that there is a certain amount of ambiguity in the Bill.

Amendment No. 138 would make it clear that the list from which the candidate is drawn to fill the vacancy is the one that existed at the time of the election. Therefore if, following the elections on 4 May 2000, there were a vacancy in May 2002, it would be filled from the list that was filed in 2000. That point is not clear in the Bill, and amendment No. 138 would clear up the ambiguity.

Amendment No. 149 would stop the political parties fiddling and messing around with the list. It would stop them pushing up the list someone who they wanted to fill a vacancy. More to the point perhaps, it would stop them keeping out of a vacancy someone who was on the list, but whom they did not want to see in a position of elected office. The amendment, which would amend schedule 3, would ensure that there was no hanky-panky by the political parties.

The second category of amendments, which consists of Nos. 136 and 137, paves the way for new clause 8. The amendments relate to London members of the assembly elected as individuals. Under the Bill, if there is an individual member vacancy, it will not be filled. No one feels particularly comfortable with that, but I accept the Government's case that it would be hard to hold an election across the whole of London to fill a single vacancy on a proportional representation basis. It would, in effect, become a first-past-the-post election.

The amendments deal with what would happen if there were two vacancies among individual London members; that is a possibility, although I am not sure how likely it is. I can see that there could be one individual member vacancy on the list, but one could also conceive of circumstances in which there could be two vacancies.

Two vacancies would amount to 8 per cent. of the voting power of the assembly, and we believe that those vacancies should be filled. The amendments would provide for an election to take place to fill the vacancies on the list and to give the individual members the chance to be replaced. I hope that the Minister will consider those two modest proposals sympathetically and favourably.

If a vacancy occurs in a seat held by a registered political party, it will be filled by someone whose name was on the list that was submitted to the Greater London returning officer for the preceding ordinary election. That is the effect of the Bill. There will normally be more than one name on that list and the highest placed candidate not elected at the previous election will be successful. That is the clear arrangement.

Amendments Nos. 138 and 149 would prevent any possibility of names being added to the list of candidates after it had been submitted to the Greater London returning officer but before the election date. Under the Bill, if a list is submitted and a candidate dies or decides to change party during the run-up to the election, there is still the flexibility for a party to make an alteration at that point. However, there is no question of the addition of names to the list after the election. The amendment would remove the sensible degree of flexibility that is necessary in the period leading up to an election, and it is not necessary to achieve the hon. Gentleman's aim.

It is true that the proposed new section 36(2B)(a) of the Representation of the People Act 1983 includes a reference to the making of rules dealing with the addition of persons to lists. In practice, that will enable the rules to deal with the consequences of withdrawals by candidates at an early stage before an election.

I agree with the hon. Gentleman that it would be improper if people could be added to the list between ordinary elections. We intend to make that point absolutely clear in the regulations. That has always been our intention, and I hope that the hon. Gentleman will accept my assurance that the regulations will make that crystal clear. There can be no question of any name being added to a list after an election. The amendments would remove necessary and sensible flexibility in the period leading up to an election, so we do not believe that it would be appropriate to incorporate them in the Bill.
