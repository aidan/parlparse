The thrust of clause 3 is to hedge around with restrictions and limits this brave new power that the Government will give to local government. That is made clear in the clause's title, which states:

    Limits on power to promote well-being.

The amendment's purpose is to rein in the Secretary of State's power to restrict local authorities' freedoms to use the new power. As the Bill is drafted, it will enable the Secretary of State to issue guidance-that is one of the Bill's common themes-which will severely curtail the way in which local authorities can use the new power. The clause states that councils will be placed under an obligation to

    have regard to any such guidance-

in other words, they will not be able to act outside such guidance. I believe that that is the legal position. I am grateful to my right hon. Friend the Member for Skipton and Ripon (Mr. Curry), who on Tuesday gave a dissertation on the phrase ``have regard to''.

If our amendments are taken together with Government amendments Nos. 100 and 101, which we shall presently debate in detail, and with the relevant provisions in the Bill, including clause 3(3), a triple lock will be placed on councils' ability to use the new power. The only person who has a key to that triple lock is the Secretary of State. Again, there is a trend for power to creep upwards to the Secretary of State and the Department and for it to be taken to the centre.

First, councils will have to have regard to the guidance in preparing their community strategy, by which they will be bound. Secondly, they will have to have regard to further guidance about the way in which they will use the power. Our amendments are intended to deal with that matter. Finally, the Secretary of State will have the apparently arbitrary power to prevent an individual local authority from using the power for any specific activity.

The amendments would remove some of the chains from local authorities and give them greater discretion about the way in which they can use the new power without feeling the constant heavy hand of central Government on their shoulder. The provision is typical of the control freakery from which the Government suffer. I should have thought that they would have learnt their lesson from the recent local elections. The Government's approach may lead them into trouble. Many nice people are coming to the Committee Room twice a week for several weeks to learn about the powers that local government will be given. Why on earth not let local government exercise the powers? The Government are giving a new power and saying, ``This is how we want you to exercise it.'' There should be much greater local input into the power and the exercising of that power and the amendments go some way towards dealing with that.
