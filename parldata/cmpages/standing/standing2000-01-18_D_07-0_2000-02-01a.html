Mr. David Lidington (Aylesbury): I beg to move amendment No. 127, in page 96, line 17, leave out 'nine' and insert 'twenty-four'.

This is a probing amendment that would change the period of maximum permitted detention from the nine hours specified in the Bill to the 24 hours allowed under existing legislation. I am aware from both Lord Lloyd's report and the Government's consultation document that the power to extend the period of detention beyond more than a few hours is rarely used. Lord Lloyd suggested a maximum period of six hours detention. In their consultation document, the Government concluded that six hours would cause difficulties for the police and other agencies, but that they were prepared to consider a shorter period than the 24 hours currently permitted.

I wonder why nine hours was fixed upon. The current legislation does not seem to have been abused, and the vast majority of people who are stopped are detained for a matter of minutes. Only a handful of people, whose cases seem to merit detailed investigation, are detained for anything remotely approaching 24 hours. Is it really necessary to make such a change? Might not the change proposed in schedule 6 remove a useful backstop from the authorities, who derive a real advantage, if only rarely, from being able to detain someone suspected of terrorism for the permitted 24 hours? In all those circumstances, would it not be more prudent to retain the 24 hours maximum time currently permitted?

Mr. Charles Clarke: The current law provides that a person may be held for more than 12 hours only if a reasonable suspicion has been formed that the person is concerned in terrorism. The amendment does not include that important qualification, but I understand its probing nature.

We consulted the police about the period for which people may be held without charge under the port's powers. The police are alive to the need to balance their desire to detain people for long enough to allow them to conclude their inquiries with the need not to hold people for an unduly long time.

Bearing in mind representations made by the police, we have arrived at a maximum period of nine hours. We believe that that should be sufficient time for the police to conclude their initial inquiries, even across time zones. After nine hours, we believe that the police should have to choose between arresting the individual under clause 39 or for another offence, and allowing the person to go. As the hon. Member for Aylesbury (Mr. Lidington) said, nine hours is more than Lord Lloyd recommended. He settled on a maximum of six hours. In response to reasoned representations from the police, we decided to set a higher limit.

The most recent statistics bear out our view that nine hours is an appropriate point at which to draw the line. For the reasons given by the hon. Gentleman, the vast majority of people are held for under four hours. A smaller number is held for between four and eight hours. The number of people held for more than eight hours is tiny. Only 19 people were held for between eight and 12 hours, and one was held for between 12 and 24 hours. It is a tiny tail of people. The figures for previous years show a similar profile. The third objection, which is perhaps more serious than the other two, relates to the presumption of guilt intent. Subsections (1) and (3) of section 16A of the current Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989 mean that a person is found guilty of the offence if the possession of the item occurred in circumstances giving reasonable suspicion of terrorist intent. The burden of proof--here it is properly called the burden of proof--then shifts to the defendant. He must prove, on the balance of probabilities, that the item was not in his possession for such purposes. If he succeeds in raising a reasonable doubt about that, but fails to prove his innocence on a balance of probabilities, he will be convicted. Thus, he cannot even send the burden of proof back to the prosecution by raising a doubt, as one can in other parts of the law; his evidence as a defendant must pass the balance of probabilities test.

The consequence, if the defendant does not succeed in that, is that he can be convicted of an offence with a sentence of 10 years' imprisonment, despite the fact that there is reasonable doubt. In a country in which the Government are asking us to re-examine the concept of trial by jury--at present in Northern Ireland there is no jury trial--for a defendant to have to prove something on a balance of probabilities, and be open to conviction where there is reasonable doubt about whether he is guilty is a road down which the Government ought not go.
