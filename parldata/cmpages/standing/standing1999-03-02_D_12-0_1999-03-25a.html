We need to give the bereaved the financial resources that will offer them greater freedom. That is the nature of money. It gives people the opportunity to choose from the options that are available to them during bereavement. It gives them--this should not be underestimated--time to grieve, to come to terms with their loss, and to start the difficult process of rebuilding their lives. It also reduces their worries when dealing with loss and everything involved with bereavement. Anyone who has experienced bereavement, or seen a close relative or friend experience it, knows what it is like. The benefit helps to reduce their worries about other things--about where the next penny will come from, the bills, obtaining probate and getting the will sorted out. It eases their minds just a little bit.

The amendment would ensure that the £2,000 bereavement payment, which, under the proposals, will not be payable to widows over 60, will be paid to all bereaved persons between the age of 60 and 64. That is the sole purpose of the amendment. That would lend truth to the Chancellor's promise in the supporting documents to the Budget. The Chancellor was remarkably silent about the abolition of widow's bereavement allowance, but reference to it is contained in the various documents that were submitted with the Budget in the Inland Revenue No. 1 press release.

The matter is not referred to directly in the text, just as the Chancellor did not refer to it directly. It is frustrating to have to wade through so many documents to find out the precise position, which appears in an obscure note in points 4 to 6.

Hon. Members will recall that the Chancellor abolished married couples' allowance. As someone who has chaired many Finance Bills, Mr. McWilliam--I think you said it was two-- you will know that several maintenance benefits are tagged on to married couple's allowance, one of which is the widow's bereavement allowance. The other benefits, which are generally worth roughly the same as married couple's allowance, will be abolished from April 2000. That is fairly straightforward.

The Chancellor goes on to say that WBA is given to widows for up to two years. The Inland Revenue No. 1 press release, "Budget 99" states:

    "Widows will be more than compensated for the loss of this allowance by the proposed Bereavement Payment in the Welfare Reform and Pensions Bill."

That should be available to widows and widowers, and it should help taxpayers and non--taxpayers alike.

One could argue that the bereavement payment will make a small contribution to those who are below the age of 60. However, it will not be available for those aged between 60 and 64. The proposal will simply help the Government and the Chancellor. The anomaly relating to those aged between 60 and 64 might result in the Chancellor's misleading the House, which I am sure Committee members want to avoid at all costs.

Any consideration of the total resources available to a bereaved person must take account of factors other than the bereavement payment. Bereaved people will face other burdens as a result of the Budget. Many of them, especially those aged between 60 and 64, will be peculiarly reliant on savings. Various schemes will be open to them. Because of changes in taxation, those who derive their income from savings will continue to pay tax at 20 per cent., while those who receive payments through occupational pensions will pay tax at 10 per cent. A number of bereaved people between the ages of 60 and 64 will face a hefty loss as a result of the withdrawal of mortgage interest relief at source. A number of schemes exist whereby elderly people who are asset rich but cash poor can remortgage. According to my reading of the Chancellor's proposals, those who are already taking advantage of such schemes will be protected, but those who do so in the future will not.

During discussion of earlier amendments and clauses, my hon. Friends demonstrated the problems with annuities that arise from changes in the market and different interest rates. Who is to say that the current lull in such schemes will not recur, especially with regard to people aged between 60 and 64? The proposals, taken with the failure to deliver the bereavement payment to people aged between 60 and 64, work towards the extension of dependency. It is important for that age group to receive additional protection and, as it will affect mainly women, I shall refer to the plight of recently bereaved widows. The nature of society has changed since widow's bereavement benefits were introduced. The number of women in the work force who are actively engaged in full and part-time work has increased, but that is less true of older women. A woman who is bereaved in her early 60s is less able to turn to the other forms of income that are available to a woman who is bereaved at an earlier age.

A bereaved aunt of mine said to me shortly after the funeral that her life had not ended, but was entering a new phase. I thought that that was a brave thing to say. Other bereaved people take years to get through the process. This simple additional £2,000 payment will help them in the process of bereavement.
