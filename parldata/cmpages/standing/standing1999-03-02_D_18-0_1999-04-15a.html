Clause 50

Incapacity for work: personal capability assessments

Mr. Quentin Davies: I beg to move amendment No. 85, in page 49, line 9, at end insert

    "provided that in making the assessment under (a) and (b) above, account shall be taken of the types of jobs available in the locality considered.".

If the concept of elasticity of tolerance exists in this House, no one has ever suspected you of being guilty of exercising it, Mr. McWilliam, least of all I--as I have many reasons to know, both from my experience in considering this Bill, and from the pleasure that I have had in serving under your Chairmanship in the past.

It is my pleasure to speak to amendment No. 85, which proposes that when the personal capability assessment is made, account should be taken of the availability of work of the type of which the applicant is judged capable. It must be clear to all reasonable people that it would be cruel and unjustified to judge someone capable of work when, in practice, no such work existed. That claimant could therefore be deemed, theoretically, capable of a type of activity, although in practice there would be no chance of his exercising that activity, and he would also be denied benefit because he was deemed capable of that type of work. That would be an Orwellian trap into which the individual could fall. I hope that the Labour Government's Orwellian inclinations, so far as running the bureaucracy is concerned, are subject to limits, which will be imposed by common sense and by normal humanitarian sentiments.

A disabled person facing the prospect of a personal capability assessment will face a strenuous and worrying experience. We hope that those who are capable of some kind of useful activity--whether part-time, or a specialised activity, perhaps to be done at home--will be positively encouraged to undertake it. That is why we earlier tabled amendment No. 78--and were disappointed that the Government did not accept it--which proposed that the personal capability assessment should take place first and the work-focused interview afterwards. That would have left no doubt as to the capability of the person, and the work-focused interview could have taken place on the basis of an established medical report. The claimant could then have had a relaxed second interview, without worrying about whether he would be deprived retrospectively of benefit because of something he injudiciously said, or because of an over-optimistic statement about the type of work he would like to do, made at that first interview.

Unfortunately, the Government did not accept that proposal. It is therefore not clear whether the work-focused interviews will happen before or after the personal capability assessment. Inevitably, the claimant will therefore suspect that he will be gone over with a view to depriving him of benefit on both those occasions, and that any tricks that are available will be used against him. That would be an unhappy situation. There is also a suspicion that the personal capability assessment will be no more fair or effective than the all-work tests that they are replacing. I hope that we shall be able to discuss that in the clause stand part debate.

We do not want people to feel that the assessment is a trap, or an unpleasant charade, in which the assessor might find some activity in which the claimant might theoretically engage, even though it might be, for example, the assembly of jewellery that takes place in India or the south seas, and there would be no market for it here. The assessor might then say, "Well, you can move your fingers sufficiently to be able to do that, so you are capable of some form of work. Consequently, you are not eligible for benefit." The claimant then discovers, cruelly, that no such activity is available, either locally or nationwide. Similarly, one could devise various notional forms of employment activity for which there are no markets in the private, public or voluntary sectors.

We must be clear about the definition of availability for work, particularly about the meaning of those two important and related words, "availability" and "work". The Government must make a clear commitment with regard to the framing of the regulations, which should establish that what will be assessed will be the ability of the claimant to perform work that is actually available. Similarly, the Benefits Agency must make it clear to the doctors that it employs--whether directly or through a subcontract--that they are to make personal capability assessments on the basis of the availability of work that claimants can actually undertake. The Bill does not deal with that simple point, nor do the explanatory notes and glossy Government brochures such as the ironically titled, "A new contract for welfare: Support for Disabled People", provide any assurances. Given that the clauses that we are about to debate will deprive disabled people of some £800 million, the title of that Green Paper is a cruel joke. Such is the cynicism of the PR machine that the new Labour Government deploy.

We are trying to limit the damage that the provision will do. Disabled claimants or, in the case of personal capability assessments, interviewees--I shall not describe such people as "assessees", because I doubt whether that word exists--will be passive objects in the process laid down in the clause. They should have rights vis-ã-vis the state and should not be subject to an arbitrary process that will make fools of them and lead to senseless theoretical rulings on their capacity for work that will fail to result in their undertaking useful activities.

I expect that the Government will say that they share my sentiments; if so, I hope that they will accept the amendment. Such a hope has yet to be fulfilled during our deliberations. If the Government do not accept the amendment, we can only hope that our initiative will secure a clear undertaking from them. The 10 minutes of the Committee's time that the amendment has taken up would then have been put to good use. We would have secured a proposal that has not, as far as I know, been secured in any Government announcement. If they give that undertaking, we trust that they will fulfil it, and we shall monitor the situation carefully.

I shall not be drawn into a discussion about the relationship between the personal capability assessment and the all-work test because I do not want to damage our chances of doing so later. However, I fear that it is in the nature of things that the personal capability assessment, like the all-work test, will lead to a number of misunderstandings--I hope that there will not be too many, but there may be a lot. Disabled people who fail the assessment may feel a sense of wrong and of grievance, and perhaps even of bitterness.

The best way to minimise that risk is to ensure that the rules of the game are explicit and published in advance. Those rules relate to the basis on which the assessment will take place and the standing instructions of the doctors who will undertake the work. The decision should not be taken ad hoc during an assessment, by an individual doctor, according to how he or she happens to feel on a particular morning, or regionally in a benefit agency area. It would not he right for one approach to be taken in one area and a different approach in another--that would lead to misunderstandings and to a sense of grievance, which would discredit the process.

The rules of the game should be clearly established in advance and the instructions should be universal and known to people. They should also define availability, which I am sure they will do--the all-work test currently does--and work, which is the other important substantive in this context. If those steps were taken, there would be a lesser chance of matters going wrong, and the system would be perceived to be fairer and more effective.

The Government have said all along that their aim is to help disabled people. We pointed out that that is a pretty cruel thing to say, because much money will be withdrawn from them. However, I hope that the Government's statements about personal capability assessments are sincere: I hope that they really want people to be encouraged to take up any activity that they can do. I also hope that people who deserve to be supported by society will be, either with income replacement benefits or with the disability living allowance. The system should be fair and humane.

I see no good reason why the Government should resist the amendment. Alternatively, they could give us an equally effective and equivalent assurance. I have moved the amendment in the sanguine spirit that I usually display on such occasions.
