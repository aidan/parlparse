Mr. O'Brien: I can tell the hon. Gentleman that what he asked for in an earlier debate is what we seek to deliver--a two-stage process. First, the courts should decide whether a person should continue to be detained, and if they conclude that he should not, they should release him, subject to the normal immigration conditions. Secondly, if they are not satisfied with those conditions, they may be prepared to release him on other conditions. They can then consider whether particular conditions are appropriate.

The magistrates courts can consider a broad range of conditions, which may include a surety, which is a commitment to pay a certain amount of money should a particular set of conditions obtain--usually that the person does not turn up at the place or time that they are supposed to. Another possibility is a security, which is the depositing of a sum of money to obtain someone's release. A range of other conditions may be applied, which often include reporting to a police station at regular intervals, living at a particular address, informing the immigration service of any change of address within a particular time, ensuring that they do not go to a particular area or whatever else the courts might feel appropriate to ensure that concerns about someone's absconding are addressed.

If the courts feel that conditions are available to them to ensure that someone turns up at court or at an immigration hearing or interview, or reports to an immigration officer as requested, they will properly grant bail. If they feel that the conditions that are available to them do not sufficiently reassure them, the courts will then deny bail and the person will be detained. That is the range of options that we intend to make available to adjudicators in Scotland and to magistrates courts in England and Wales. As I have already said, because there are so few detainees in Scotland, it is better that adjudicators should deal with the matter there.

We are giving the courts a range of options, which will allow them properly to decide the future of anyone in detention.

We initially intended to have matters dealt with as they are normally dealt with in magistrates courts; lawyers would represent a person applying for bail, and the person who wished to make a bail application would nominate an appropriate lawyer, who would be a solicitor or barrister.

In magistrates courts, rights of audience are usually zealously protected by solicitors and barristers. Indeed, the High Court has seen considerable debate on the extent to which rights of audience should be granted.
