Learning and Skills Bill [Lords]

Clause 30

The Council

1.30 pm

Mr. Tim Boswell (Daventry): I beg to move amendment No. 168, in page 13, line 19, at end add

    'or in association with the Learning and Skills Council for England'.

I am pleased to see Committee members flooding back to their places for what I hope will not be an extended sitting, although we have important matters to discuss.

The amendment is intended to probe the Government's intentions towards what might, for simplicity, be called cross-border activities. I hasten to say for the avoidance of doubt, that that is not intended to signal either a derogation of devolution or a Welsh takeover of the English education system. Those of us who have studied in England have seen and admired the tremendous contribution that Welsh teachers have made to teaching in Wales and England. It accords with the generally good spirits with which this Committee has conducted its proceedings to suggest that teachers should have the easiest interpenetration across the frontier.

I have three concerns in this context, to which I hope the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State, Wales Office will respond. My first concern relates to institutional co-operation. When the Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment, the hon. Member for North Swindon (Mr. Wills) touched on that in an earlier debate about partnership and co-working, I was broadly satisfied by his remarks. I have previously discussed the quadrilateral that comprises: the two learning and skills councils, if I may use that summary phrase; the Secretary of State for Education and Employment and the Welsh Assembly; and the Wales Office and the Wales education department. He assured the Committee that those four elements would be able to work together and to exchange information and experience. We shall rehearse those matters further when we debate the inspectorates.

It is important for such practical institutional collaboration to continue. The clause states:

    The Council's functions will be carried out in relation to Wales only.

That implies that the remit stops at the border. I am sure that that wording was chosen to facilitate a wider understanding, and I hope that the Under-Secretary will give the appropriate reassurance.

My second concern involves the individual student, whose activities may be more sensibly conducted in another part of the United Kingdom or, if appropriate, abroad. For example, students with particular disabilities might receive better provision if they went to a specialist college in England. The converse might also apply--specialist provision might be available in Wales but not in England. The two learning and skills councils should be able to buy in to those services or to finance the activities of students who move cross the border to alternative provision, if that is appropriate.

My third concern involves institutional locations and the ability of a Welsh institution that is funded by the National Council for Education and Training in Wales to have an outstation in England, where it might serve the local community. Committee members may be familiar with what I believe is a piece of Austrian territory called the Kleinwalsertal, which is, in fact, part of the German customs area and uses German stamps, for the simple reason that it is inaccessible from Austria, except by climbing over a mountain. From my knowledge of them, that is not the case in the marches between Wales and England. However, there may be cases in which it is sensible for a Welsh college to have an outstation in England--or the reverse. It is not intended that this should be a unilateral provision.

There may also be outstations of educational establishments that exist for perfectly good reasons other than to serve the local community. Not far from my wife's family home in south Wales is Kilvrough manor, which was owned for many years by the Oxfordshire education authority. I do not know whether that is still the case. On the Gower peninsula, near the coast, it was used by students from Oxfordshire, near my own home. That kind of thing seems entirely sensible, and we should encourage such sensible collaboration across the frontier between schools.

We want an assurance from the Minister that there will be no artificial barriers to such exchanges put up by Government, learning and skills councils or individual educational institutions. We understand that there is a separate council for Wales with a separate remit, but people who wish, for educational purposes, to cross a boundary--which, I hope, is as invisible as possible--should be able to do so without let or hindrance.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Wales (Mr. David Hanson): The hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell) has picked on the right chap. He may be aware that my constituency is adjacent to England, and my home is six miles from the English border. If I go to the cinema or to Sainsbury's, I go to England. The airport that I use is in Manchester and the train station to which I go for a connection to London is in England. An awareness of cross-border issues is, therefore, second nature to me. I hope that I can give him the assurances that he seeks.

Amendment No. 168 is unnecessary. Generally speaking, it is right that the council in Wales will deal with Wales and the council in England will deal with England. That will not, however, restrict students resident in England who want to use facilities in Wales or students resident in Wales who want to use facilities in England. Both bodies will ensure that participation in educational activities on each side of the border is possible.

Mr. Boswell: The Minister has made a good start. He has described the legal position, which is that people will be able to travel from one country to the other. It is important, however, that functional delivery is concerted between the various bodies. It is all very well to say that the Ritz is open to everyone, but, if there is no funding for a particular activity that it would be sensible to do elsewhere, the Minister's generous commitment will not matter.

Mr. Hanson: I can reassure the hon. Gentleman that placing restrictions on the cross-border flow--the concern that lies behind the amendment--would be detrimental to the aim of offering learning opportunities according to where and when individuals wish to learn. That aim is shared by the Government and the National Assembly.

Mr. Phil Willis (Harrogate and Knaresborough): I am reassured by what the Minister is saying, but is he aware that there is a Judicial review case currently before the High Court about the Secretary of State's decision to impose the teachers' threshold arrangements on the National Assembly and, thus, on teachers in Wales? Does the Minister foresee a situation in which the Welsh council could decide on different arrangements for student support that would preclude students who, like him, live just across the border, from accessing some of the provision in Welsh institutions? That is part of the issue raised by the hon. Member for Daventry, and I should be grateful to hear the Minister's response.

Mr. Hanson: I understand what the hon. Gentleman said. Discussions are still going on in relation to teachers' pay and conditions. Regarding the amendment, I can reassure him that all students, from whatever side of the border and whatever institution, will be able to secure the proper provision and be funded accordingly. I hope that that clarifies the matter.

Mr. Graham Brady (Altrincham and Sale, West): The Minister has given an assurance that there will be no bar to students on either side of the English-Welsh border from travelling to institutions. I should like to return to the institutional point raised by my hon. Friend the Member for Daventry. Would it be possible under the arrangements in the Bill for a Welsh institution to have a branch on the English side of the border, or would that be precluded by this subsection of the clause?

Mr. Hanson: No. Should an English further education college wish to have, for example, an outward bound centre or a horticultural centre on the Welsh side of the border, we would welcome its participation. We would also joyously welcome the incoming students that it would bring. It would be funded by the Learning and Skills Council. The students might come from England or Wales and would be able to attend both branches.

We are discussing the institutional structures of accountability for funding, and the difference in policy implications for the Assembly and for the Learning and Skills Council. However, no student or institution will be prevented from receiving the tailored service that they require. I hope that that clarifies the hon. Gentleman's point.

In conclusion, when joint working by the LSC and the council in Wales is appropriate, the Bill already provides for the necessary powers in paragraph 15 of schedule 8. This carries forward section 82 of the Further and Higher Education Act 1992 which provides for the joint exercise of functions by the Further and Higher Education Councils for England and Wales. In practice, this covers joint working by, for example, the Higher and Further Education Funding Councils for England and Wales, and also the cross-border joint working currently being undertaken by the Further Education Funding Councils for England and Wales.

Finally, paragraph 15 of schedule 8, when properly informed by paragraph 17, will ensure that the provision in the 1992 Act is carried forward for both councils in England and Wales. I hope, in the light of my constituency background as well as my ministerial responsibilities, that the hon. Gentlemen will be reassured that we would not put anything into the Bill that would be detrimental to individuals or institutions on either side of the border.

 

Mr. Willis: I am grateful to the Minister for that clarification. I understand what he said in terms of the provision of courses. However, I want to raise the issue of student support. If a student with disabilities, for example, wished to cross the border to attend an institution in Wales--quite rightly, if it was the nearest institution--where would his or her funding come from in terms of support? Would it come from the English Learning and Skills Council, or from the one in Wales? Students studying for the post-graduate certificate of education at the university of Wales Aberystwyth receive only part of the golden hello, or training salary, unless they commit themselves to teaching in Wales. We would not want that situation to pertain up to level 3.

Mr. Hanson: I hope that I can reassure the hon. Gentleman that the resources follow the student. For example, if a student lived in Chester and attended Northop college across the border in my Constituency to do a horticultural training course, he or she would be funded by the LSC. A student living in Northop who attended a college in Chester would be funded by the Learning and Skills Council in Wales. The money will follow the student, and each student will be placed accordingly. The resources will come from either the English source or the Welsh source, depending on residency.

Mr. Boswell: The Minister set out to be both disarming and conciliatory. I shall do my best to be both of those things. He drew on his own experience, and I have a fellow feeling for him because I live very near to a county boundary. I constantly suffer as a result of cross-border anomalies, not least because I live in an English region that is close to another. English regions are assuming greater importance in the Government's agenda. Such considerations are real and, with the best will in the world, occasionally give rise to administrative glitches.

However, there is the residual concern of student support--of which I did not want to make a wider issue in this debate--which the hon. Gentleman helped to excite in my mind.

Although the position in Wales and England is broadly similar, the Minister cannot but be aware that, as a result of the deliberations of the Cubie report on higher education, the relationship between Scottish and English students who attend universities in Scotland and England is anomalous. Ministers have tended to defend that relationship by reference to the devolved system, and by arguing that discrimination within a member state is acceptable to the European convention on human rights and the Commission. Those arguments are partial and inadequate, even in cases where money follows the student across the frontier.

I have dealt with the wider context, but it was wise to do so at this time. I hope that Ministers will bear in mind the specific issue of student support, and concert policies more generally. We do not want the new structure and devolution to create difficulties in terms of flows. Ministers owe it to members of the Committee to keep an eye on the matter. They should work together, and with their respective bodies, maintain a single market for post-16 education and training--excluding the separate consideration of higher education--across this frontier. With those slight warnings and caveats in mind, and to make progress, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 31

Education and training for persons aged 16 to 19

Mr. Boswell: I beg to move amendment No. 169, in page 13, leave out line 26.

This is a probing amendment. It has nothing to do with destroying the opportunity for the council to provide proper facilities for organised leisure occupation. Far be it from me to suggest that that is a sensible idea. However, we want to ask the Minister to take a few minutes of the Committee's time to tell us what is in the minds of Ministers and, one assumes, the National Assembly for Wales in seeking to make provision for organised leisure occupation. That is something that we failed to do on the English clauses.

A little later in our proceedings we will debate the funding of grammar schools and sixth forms. One of the attractions of sixth forms, not just to Opposition Members but to many parents, is that the school environment can often provide a continuing and wider educational experience than is afforded by further education colleges. I accept that the duty to provide proper facilities for persons between the ages of 16 and 19 is without reference to the establishment. I should also say, before somebody points it out to me, that at present we have keyed in these extra-curricular activities only to education, but they might equally apply to training, which the next subsection deals with.

Be that as it may, a substantive point is involved. Whatever the establishment, but particularly where young people are concerned--I shall not reproduce this debate later in relation to adults, where the duties are slightly weaker--it should be possible for the CETW or the LSC in England to make provision for reasonable extra-curricular activities. That is not a bolt-on extra but an important part of young people's education. We want to encourage, it rather than diminish it, and to stimulate it rather than discourage it. Therefore, I hope that there will be no dissent across the Committee about the way in which it is done.

I want to add one other point, which springs from some points that I made on the last amendment about the external education facility for Oxfordshire education authority in Gower. By all accounts that is a sensible thing to have and a wonderful opportunity. In my county, Northamptonshire, there has been some concern about outdoor education activities at Longtown, which, as I recall, although I have not checked, is just on the English side of the border. Again we are taking people from the midlands to a more upland environment, where they can do more physically challenging activities. That seems to me highly desirable, and I hope that it will be possible for such activities to be conducted across the invisible frontier rather than confined to one country or the other.

I move the amendment simply to probe the Minister's intentions about the way in which leisure time activities, or, as I describe them, extra-curricular activities, fit in with the overall pattern of provision. It would be helpful if he could take us through his thinking.

Mr. Willis: I do not understand the purpose of the amendment, which on the one hand seems to argue that it is good to have this type of activity and on the other hand removes it from the Bill.

Mr. Boswell: The hon. Gentleman may be a more skilful draftsman than I am, but may I assist him straight away by saying that, in order to raise an issue, it is sometimes necessary to move its deletion, so that people like him, and I hope the Minister shortly--and, if necessary, in winding up, myself--stress the importance of having it in.

Mr. Willis: The logic of that escapes me, because one can make these comments in a clause stand part debate in the same way.

I welcome the provision. We have long wanted a level playing field between the further education and school sectors, and people have been attracted by the range of activities that comes with the educational package to stay on in sixth forms. The Government have made a breakthrough in recognising the necessity for activities such as physical recreation and sport, outdoor pursuits, team building and so on, and putting in legislation a requirement for the LSC to provide them. I welcome that and, whether or not the amendment is a probing amendment, I hope that the Minister will reject it.

Mr. Hanson: I am grateful for the support of the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough (Mr. Willis). It is our intention to resist the amendment, which I am sure the hon. Member for Daventry tabled merely to probe our intentions.

As the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough said, the effect of the amendment would be to remove the duty on the council in Wales to secure proper facilities for organised leisure-time occupation connected with the education of people aged 16-19. That is a flowery way of saying that it would be unable to undertake the outward bound activities, team work and range of activities that, although they may not lead to qualifications, help to build positive attitudes and team spirit, and provide people with physical outlets while doing other work. If the amendment were accepted, the council in Wales--and in England if the philosophy were applied there--would be severely restricted in providing a range of activities for developing skills for life.

I listened carefully to the hon. Gentleman's reasons for the amendment and it may be helpful if I explain the meaning of connected organised leisure-time. it is a phrase that dates back to the Education Act 1944, so it carries some weight and has a history.

More importantly, removing one part of the main duty of the council in Wales in respect of 16-19 year olds would put in doubt the national council's ability to provide the full range of educational opportunities for young people that I am sure Opposition Members want and which the hon. Member for Harrogate and Knaresborough supports. If the council lost its duty to provide facilities for more informal learning, it would lose its duty to secure the invaluable provision that enriches young people's lives and promotes social inclusion. A life-long habit of leisure-connected activities may lead to other valuable skills and development, and perhaps to careers and other opportunities or it may simply enrich life. Informal leisure-connected activities may also be the spur to go on to more formal learning and further education, and the will to learn and enjoyment of a subject may lead to formal qualifications in due course.

We discussed earlier Government amendments to clauses 22 and 23 which reflect the importance that the Government attach to the provision. We want there to be no doubt about that in the Committee. Increasing and widening the participation in life-long learning by including the socially excluded and disaffected are cornerstones of the agenda, both in England and, with the National Assembly, in Wales.

The amendment is contrary to the spirit of what we are trying to achieve and I hope that the hon. Member for Daventry will accept my assurance on the definition and withdraw it. It is important to progress in the way that I outlined. The amendment would also create an anomaly in that the national council would have a duty to provide such facilities in respect of training but not education. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will reflect on the matter and withdraw the amendment.
