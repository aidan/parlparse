New clause 3—The transferee company provisions (No. 2)—

    `(1) The single company granted a licence authorising it to provide air traffic services in respect of a managed area shall be—

    (a) an independent publicly-owned company,

    (b) a company which offers bonds for sale to investors,

    (c) a company that shall have the following duties—

    (i) to provide at all times a safe and orderly air traffic control system within the managed area,

    (ii) to plan efficiency improvements,

    (iii) to give to operators and owners of aircraft, owners and managers of aerodromes, persons travelling in aircraft and persons with rights in property carried in them the benefit of planned efficiency improvements, which may take the form of lower prices and improved quality,

    (iv) to achieve a commercial rate of return for the Crown,

    (v) to draw up a rolling five-year strategic plan incorporating financial, pricing and service quality objectives,

    (vi) to submit, on behalf of the board of the company, to annual meetings of stakeholders, the five year plan set out in sub-paragraph (iv).

    (2) The annual meeting of the company shall have the duty to consider, and if the meeting thinks fit, to approve, the strategic plan, and to establish performance objectives and to monitor the performance of the company.

    (3) The Secretary of State shall appoint auditors who shall have the duty of reporting to the annual meeting of the company on management performance relative to the objectives set at previous meetings.'.

The key aim of this group of amendments is to promote what is becoming a familiar acronym­an IPOC, or independent publicly owned company. IPOC is an oxymoron: being publicly owned, it is by definition not independent. It is therefore the epitome of the third way, so one wonders why the third way party did not choose it. It is a have-your-cake-and-eat-it option—a way of having all the benefits of privatisation without the commercial discipline. It is not a permanent option for NATS.

Public ownership will deny the benefits of commercial discipline. That means that the discipline will have to be applied through political control and control of the company's borrowing. If any publicly owned corporation takes on liabilities that are ultimately liabilities on the taxpayer, they must be agreed by the Treasury; immediately, the strings of political control are back on the organisation. There is currently a great vogue for bonds. I hate to point this out, but privately owned corporations can issue bonds too.

Some people are confused and believe that if a publicly owned corporation issues bonds, it is different from the Treasury borrowing money through the gilts markets or letting a company borrow from a national loans fund, as happens at present. They also believe that a bond issue is cheaper than raising equity finance in the city. In fact, the yield one has to pay on a bond is much higher than the average dividend yield on an equity share. In revenue terms, therefore, a bond is more expensive than equity.

In any case, those who want to issue bonds might as well borrow directly on the Government's balance sheet because the gilt yield is below the bond yield. If the objective is to have a cheap loan balance, one might as well issue more gilts and give the money to the company in that fashion. Bonds are greatly attractive to people who cannot be bothered to work it all out. We need some honesty about that.
