Miss Johnson: I hope that members of the Committee are sitting comfortably, because I propose to read quite a long note, which will cast new light on our amendments.

This is an important set of amendments, dealing with matters that I know are of great interest to the Committee, as they were to Lord Burns's Committee. The amendments relate to the Bill's key provisions on market abuse. I apologise for the fact that I shall speak for rather longer than usual, but I hope that my remarks will be of great interest to members of the Committee.

The starting point for the new regime is the importance of major financial markets, which sit at the heart of every modern economy and provide a mechanism for intermediation between savers and borrowers and between risk-takers and those who seek to hedge risk. They help to ensure the efficient allocation of resources, by channelling funds to where they are best employed. They also provide information on the fair market price of a range of assets. The prices that are set in the London commodities exchanges, for example, are used the world over in setting the terms of contracts between the producers and users of commodities. If the efficiency and liquidity of major United Kingdom markets are reduced, the effect is not confined to only a small group of market participants but is felt more widely. Ultimately, market inefficiencies impact on economic growth and the UK's overall prosperity.

UK financial markets serve more than the domestic economy. London is one of the world's three financial capitals. The City's expertise and innovation bring huge job and revenue benefits to the UK. That is a major factor in the UK's success story.

We are perceived to have fair and clean markets. We need to ensure that we keep it that way and, where possible, improve that reputation. That is vital in a world of rapidly changing global markets which compete for business.

We protect the financial markets in two ways. First, there are the criminal regimes for market manipulation and insider dealing. Those are both serious criminal offences, which carry a maximum sentence of seven years in jail and an unlimited fine. Secondly, there is the regulatory regime under which various regulatory bodies can take action against regulated persons for market abuse. However, there is a gap in the protections. The criminal law covers all market participants, but only a narrow range of serious criminal offences. The regulatory regime is capable of dealing with a wider range of damaging behaviours, but applies only to the regulated community.

Many commentators on the Bill have often exaggerated the differences between the new regime and what is possible under the current system. There are two key changes from the current position. First, the new regime extends the regulator's reach to cover all those who abuse the markets—both those who are regulated by the Financial Services Authority and those who are not. For example, where an unregulated person squeezes or attempts to squeeze a commodity market, that can have serious effects on other market participants and on the wider economy. However, that behaviour cannot be properly addressed at present. Secondly, we are trying to introduce greater transparency in this area for the regulated community.

It has often been overlooked by those commenting on part VII that under the existing system the regulator can take action for breach of rules or for breach of high level regulatory principles where abuse has occurred. It is worth recalling that the Joint Committee which examined the market abuse regime accepted in principle the need for a market abuse regime that complements the existing criminal offences.

We have set out the mischief on the face of the Bill rather than leaving the scope of the regime to FSA rules. The primary legislation sets an understandable outer boundary to the regime. That will be developed by case law providing greater certainty at the margins over time. We recognise that from the outset the new regime needs as much certainty as is reasonably possible. Financial markets move quickly and we do not want to inhibit innovation.

In order to provide greater certainty, clause 96 requires the FSA to produce a code of market conduct. It is intended that the code will set out the types of behaviour that are acceptable and unacceptable in a way which the Bill cannot do adequately in a changing world. The FSA consulted on a version of the code in June 1998 just before the draft Bill was published. It is now looking carefully at the many comments it received during that consultation with the help of a practitioner group and a group of exchange representatives.

The FSA intends to produce a revised version of the code and will consult on it next spring. That will, of course, take into account changes made in the Bill. As a result of the consultation responses on the Bill and the Joint Committee's recommendations we have made a number of improvements to the regime. I shall run through those briefly before moving on to today's amendments.

Following the consultation on the draft Bill it was suggested that the new market abuse penalty regime would be classified as a criminal regime under the European convention on human rights. The Joint Committee heard evidence on that from a number of witnesses and extended its investigations into the Bill to look at that question. In the light of consultation responses and the evidence of witnesses to the Joint Committee, we reviewed the question of the likely convention classification of the market abuse regime. We decided as a precautionary measure to introduce the convention criminal protections for market abuse penalties. Our arguments were fully stated in the written and oral evidence that we gave to the Joint Committee.

We recognised that there was a possibility that it would be classified as criminal for convention purposes and to avoid any risk of a finding that the Bill does not fully comply with the convention requirements, we decided to introduce the relevant protections. The first protection is against the use of compelled statements in proceedings to impose a market abuse penalty on the person who gave those statements. That is provided for in clause 144. The second protection provided under article 6 of the convention is the provision of subsidised legal assistance where a person has insufficient means and where the interests of justice so require.

We said in our memorandum to the Joint Committee of 14 May that we would look into how to make legal assistance available in cases involving the imposition of penalties for market abuse. We will table amendments in due course but I should like to take this opportunity to set out briefly what we propose.

We intend to provide for a stand alone legal assistance scheme. It will be entirely separate from the existing schemes for legal aid and will be administered by the court service alongside its role in running the tribunal. The scheme will be available to provide assistance to individuals who choose to refer a case involving a penalty for market abuse to the tribunal.

As the Government made clear, legal assistance is not generally available for tribunal proceedings as they are intended to be speedier and less formal than court proceedings. We do not intend to extend its availability under the Bill to cases other than those for the imposition of a market abuse penalty. It will be subject to both a means test and an interests of justice test in accordance with article 6 of the ECHR. Eligibility according to those tests will be determined by the tribunal. However, I should make it clear that the nature of market abuse cases is such that we would expect the interests of justice test to be met in the majority of cases.

The eligibility criteria will be set out in regulations to be drawn up by the Lord Chancellor's Department. The scheme will be funded from a levy that the FSA will raise from authorised persons. The money raised will be passed directly to the court service which will, as I said, be responsible for administering the scheme. The FSA will be the other party in all cases to have a role in that.

The levy will be raised by the FSA as part of its arrangements for raising fees from, and distributing penalty income to, authorised persons. The FSA will consult on the distribution of those costs, just as it will consult on the distribution of the penalty income.

Another change that we have made in response to consultation concerns is to provide that compliance with the express provisions of the FSA's code of market conduct is a complete defence against the charge of market abuse. If someone does something that is expressly permitted by the code, the FSA will not be able to take action. If the FSA thinks that the behaviour should be deterred, then it will have to change the code.

We also announced to the Joint Committee that we would be exploring two additional changes to the market abuse provisions. The first was to provide explicit protections for those who take reasonable steps to ensure that they do not commit market abuse. The second was to clarify that the regime will apply only to market participants.

The amendments to clause 95, which we are bringing forward today, would give effect to those two commitments and make several helpful clarificatory changes to the provisions. Let me explain what we have done and why.

First, let me deal with the changes related to the market participants issue. Concern was expressed during consultation and by witnesses to the Joint Committee, that the regime could catch people who were engaged in behaviour that had nothing to do with the market. An example is that the regime could catch striking copper miners since their actions could be said, although with severe straining of the language, to have had a distortionary effect on the market in copper futures, which would be a market protected by the regime.

Our regime was never intended to catch that sort of behaviour. The intention is to catch behaviour that does not live up to the standards expected by those who regularly use the market—hence damaging confidence in the markets. Looked at another way, only behaviour that does not live up to expected standards can damage confidence.

The Bill currently provides for that in clause 95(1)(c) by making a condition of abuse such behaviour as would be likely to damage the confidence of informed participants that the market is a true and fair one, or would be likely to damage confidence if it became commonplace. However, in the light of the concerns that were expressed we looked at how we could make it clearer that the regime did not apply as widely as some had feared.

We therefore adopted a different approach—to make it clear that it will be market abuse only when someone acts in a way that regular users of the market would consider to be a failure to observe the standard of behaviour reasonably expected of a person in his position in relation to the market. That would be the effect of amendment No. 346.

What regular users will expect will differ from case to case and market to market. For example, the market is likely to have higher expectations of a knowledgeable investor or an issuer than an ordinary member of the public. As part of that change, we have replaced the ``informed participants'' of the current provision with a hypothetical regular user of the market. The regular market user is defined by amendment No. 356 to be a reasonable person who regularly deals on the market. He has to be, effectively, the cousin of the courts' reasonable man. He represents the distillation of the standards expected by those who regularly use the market. Unlike the current ``informed participant'', he represents a single view. If people engage in behaviour that fails to meet the standards expected by the regular market user, that user's confidence in the market would be affected and he would be less likely to deal in it.

If they are to work properly, markets rely on people to observe proper standards. Where people do not do so, the markets can be damaged. For that to happen it is not a necessary condition that the person causing the damage intended to do so. Market integrity may also be affected by people acting without due care and attention. That is why I ask my hon. Friends to oppose amendment No. 139, tabled by Opposition Members.

The amendment would provide that, in the case of misleading impressions and distortions, abuse would occur only when someone intended to carry out the abuse. In many cases, people obviously intend to carry out their abuse. Where they do, penalties will be imposed under this regime. However, in relation to those who deliberately abused the markets, penalties could be higher as a result of amendment No. 360 to clause 99, to which we will return in due course. Rather like driving without due care and attention, people can cause a great deal of damage without intending to do so.

For example, the markets rely on those responsible for providing information to do so having taken due care to ensure that the information is accurate. If a company were to release information which misled investors, about, say, its current profits or planned activities, it should be possible to take action against it if the circumstances warrant it, for example if they were negligent. Similarly, if a trader does not take care to follow market rules or conventions, on disclosure, for example, and, as a result, the market is misled, it should be possible to take action.

I am not saying that, in such cases, market abuse would have necessarily occurred; I am saying that they should not be outside the scope of the regime. The Opposition's amendment would put them outside its scope.

People will be guided on what is abuse by their own understanding of market conventions and practices, and by the FSA's code of conduct. To continue the driving without due care and attention metaphor, the code will act like the Highway Code in giving guidance on the type of behaviour that is acceptable, and on what is unacceptable. Therefore, I hope that Opposition Members will not press their amendment.

It is worth recalling that, in paragraph 266 of its first report, the Joint Committee acknowledges the force of our argument against requiring proof of intent. The Joint Committee did, however, say that the regime should be modified so as to include some form of safe harbour for honest and careful persons. We provide that safe harbour in amendment No. 347. The amendment, as well as making changes to the conditions of abuse to take account of the changes introduced by amendment No. 346, also introduces two new safe harbours.

It proposes that in determining whether behaviour is abusive, regard must be had to the extent to which the person concerned took care to avoid engaging in abuse, or believed that the behaviour would not amount to abuse.

Whether someone took sufficient care, or had reasonable belief that his behaviour would not amount to abuse, will depend on the facts. That is true of similar provisions in the Bill. It is not possible, nor would it be sensible, to specify exactly what steps were sufficient. In one case it may be sufficient to rely on guidance from an exchange, but in another, it may be that formal legal advice is required from experts.

A specific safe harbour is also provided against a charge of market abuse through amendment No. 354 which provides that those who act in conformity with FSA rules cannot commit market abuse. This is intended to cover in particular things done in conformity with price stabilisation rules and Chinese wall rules. However, it is drafted widely to cover behaviour that conforms with FSA rules, on the basis that if people act in accordance with the rules, it would be wrong to proceed against them for market abuse.

The purpose of the Government's amendments is not to make significant changes to the underlying regime; like the changes that I have already explained, they are aimed importantly, at clarifying the Bill's intent.

Our amendment No. 347, as well as making changes necessary to take account of the move from the phrase ``informed participants'' to the term ``regular market user'', makes some additional changes to the conditions for the mischief. These will introduce greater certainty. In the condition dealing with information abuse, we have moved from the phrase

    ``relevant in deciding whether or not to enter into transactions'',

which is arguably too wide, to the narrower

    ``relevant when deciding the terms on which transactions . . . should be effected''.

This change narrows the clause's effect, providing that abuse will occur only where the information goes to the terms on which the transaction might be effected, rather than the potentially much wider question of whether the transaction should be effected.

In the second condition relating to false impressions, following responses to consultations we have moved from the mischief being to give a ``mistaken impression'' to the more familiar ``false or misleading impression''. Again, greater certainty is the aim.

I hope that, in the light of these changes, the Opposition will not press amendment No. 372. The amendment would make it a condition of information abuse that the person engaging in the behaviour knew, or ought to have known, that the information was confidential.

The point at issue—that the information has to be information not properly accessible to other market participant—is already dealt with. The information has to be information which is not generally available. Clause 95(7) makes it clear that information is to be regarded as generally available if it can be obtained by research or analysis. In addition, abuse occurs only if the reasonable regular user thinks that someone—the person engaging in the behaviour—has failed to live up to expected standards.

The problem with using a term such as ``confidential information'' is that it would place some people under a duty to treat information as confidential and others not. I fear that that would create more uncertainty than it would resolve. If I were to overhear a conversation between two directors about an upcoming company announcement, it would not be clear whether that information was confidential in my hands. However, if I were to deal on the basis of that information, I would be engaging in behaviour that we want the regime to cover. The code will, of course, be able to say something about when information is not generally available to the rest of the market and to give any necessary additional clarity.

Amendment No. 350 simply makes it clear that the two types of behaviour for which subsection (6) provides, which are to be regarded as occurring in relation to qualifying investments, are not the only types of behaviour that fall into that camp. For example, the production of a false press release that gives people a misleading impression of the price of a company's shares would be behaviour in relation to qualifying investments. Whether it would be abuse would depend on the facts of the case.

Amendment No. 351 is intended to ensure that clause 95(5)(b) covers what we intend it to cover. It makes clear that behaviour in relation to a commodity whose price or value is expressed by reference to the price or value of the qualifying investment also comes within the regime. Spread-betting is an example of such behaviour. A person could have inside information revealing that an issuer of shares is about to collapse. That person could take advantage of such information by making a spread bet on the movement of the price of the share in question. The bookmaker would quote a price that reflects his own book rather than the share price by reference to which the bet is made and which may not depend on the price of the underlying share, although it will be expressed by reference to it. The amendment makes it clear that spread bets and similar investments will be within the scope of the regime.

Amendments Nos. 349, 352, 353, 355 and 356 make changes that follow from the move to a regular market user as the person who represents the view of the standards expected by the market. Amendments Nos. 140, 141 and 389 to 391 are therefore unnecessary. I hope that Opposition Members will seek to withdraw them in the light of the changes.

The Committee will have noticed that the Government amendments remove clause 95(1)(c) from the Bill, and I think that hon. Members have a copy of the clause as it will appear when revised. Subsection (1)(c) is no longer needed as behaviour that is not in accordance with expected standards would damage confidence in the markets if it became commonplace. The provision would not, therefore, add anything to the clause. The changes to the clause should allay concerns put to the Government by the industry about the level of certainty provided by the new regime. We have received indications from industry representative bodies and their advisers that our general approach is welcome.

By unpacking the concepts in the clause, we can make it clear that the only behaviour that will be caught is that which fails to live up to the expected standards of the market. Behaviour that does not live up to those standards and which involves abuse of the market will most emphatically be caught.

The new regime is a big step forward in protection of the major UK markets. It will be good for professional users of the markets, for investors generally and for the economy as a whole. I commend the Government amendments to the Committee.
