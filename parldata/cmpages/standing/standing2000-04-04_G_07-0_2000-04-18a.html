Mr. John Burnett (Torridge and West Devon): I want to make a short point, which I believe is relevant. In other legislation that I have been involved in construing over the years, particularly fiscal legislation, the word ``individual'' is a term of art that means literally what it says. I join in supporting the point made not only by my hon. Friend the Member for Taunton but also by the hon. Member for Surrey Heath (Mr. Hawkins).

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Jane Kennedy): I recognise the probing nature of the amendment and am grateful for the opportunity to clarify the position and to reassure the hon. Member for Taunton on her specific point.

As my right hon. Friend the Minister of State said this morning, we are establishing new criminal offences that will bite on individuals who seek to work with children while disqualified. The scheme must focus responsibility on the disqualified individual; that responsibility must not be shifted, in any way, on to the employer. To give an extreme example, we do not want to impose a requirement on all organisations to check an individual's criminal record every time they employ someone or take someone on. That may be the responsible action in any given case, but it is vital that the checks are seen as part of a whole range of proper recruitment measures and a culture of vigilance. A culture that relied on checks and saw them as the be all and end all would be of no use to anyone.

Originally, the working group that developed the scheme proposed only one offence-for the disqualified individual who worked, applied for work or accepted work with children. In cases where an individual acted deliberately-maliciously, as it were-to circumvent the disqualification, the employer would have been caught by the standard ``aiding and abetting'' formulation. However, responses to the consultation exercise favoured a seperate offence-not to catch a wider range of culpability, but to make a clear statement that active collusion in the employment of a disqualified person must not be tolerated.

The aim of the clause remains the same. It is designed to deal with the individual who deliberately employs another person whom he or she knows to be banned-for example, a paedophile who tries to employ another member of his ring. It would cover an agency, such as a nanny agency, if the agent making the reference knew that the person was banned. The parent who employed the nanny would also be covered if they knew that the person was banned.

I hope that that satisfies the genuine and proper concerns that were raised by the hon. Members for Taunton and for Surrey Heath.

Mr. Burnett: If the director of a limited company that was running such an organisation knowingly infringed the clause by offering work, would he be liable, in effect, for the acts and omissions of the company for which he spoke?

Jane Kennedy: If it was demonstrated that the director knowingly promoted the employment of a banned person in circumstances that would be proscribed by the clause, he would be culpable and would be held responsible.

The offence in the Bill will cover those who are genuinely and truly culpable, not penalise the overall corporate organisation that may suffer as a result of an individual's actions. I therefore hope that Opposition Members will withdraw the amendment.

Clause 34

Disqualification in Scotland or Northern Ireland

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hawkins: The clause will apply the relevant provisions only in relation to disqualification in Scotland or Northern Ireland. I do not want to detain the Committee, but I wonder whether the Parliamentary Secretary could explain for my benefit the exact position of Scotland and Northern Ireland in this regard, because it may be relevant to subsequent clauses. We now have a separate Scottish Parliament and a devolved Assembly that is suspended, but which may return in the near future if our hopes for Northern Ireland are fulfilled. For the avoidance of doubt, the Parliamentary Secretary should explain the way in which the overlap will apply.

Jane Kennedy: I hope that I can provide that clarification. Statutory provisions equivalent to those contained in the Bill are not yet in place in Scotland or Northern Ireland, but I understand that both Scotland and Northern Ireland intend to establish equivalent schemes in the near future. The clause therefore provides a power for the Secretary of State to ensure that individuals who, under the law of Scotland or Northern Ireland, are subject to a prohibition or disqualification that corresponds to disqualification in the rest of the United Kingdom, are also disqualified from working with children in England and Wales. The clause is intended to allow the protections provided by the integrated system to be available on a UK basis in due course.

Although I cannot share specific dates and objectives with the hon. Gentleman today, we keep in close touch with Scotland and Northern Ireland on this issue. It is vital that the protections are UK-wide as soon as possible. In the meantime, the ban imposed in England and Wales will cover work in Northern Ireland.

Mr. Hawkins: I am grateful to the Minister for that clarification. It is helpful for those who read our proceedings to have that on the record.

Question put and agreed to.

The official Opposition are not alone in feeling strongly that the Government serve no good purpose by seeking to change the names of all these orders. Everybody in the court service at every level-lawyers, magistrates, whoever-has become used over many years to what a probation order, a community service order and all the others mean. There is no good reason for suddenly changing the name from probation order to community rehabilitation order. One does not have to listen to the official Opposition to believe that. In Home Office questions we belatedly persuaded the Minister of State, in answer to a question from my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham (Mr. Bercow), that in view of all the lobbying that the Government had received, the proposed new name of the probation service would bring it into ridicule. Almost every probation committee in the country wrote to the Government saying that the proposal was daft and the Government eventually conceded. The National Association of Probation Officers is at the sharp end and sent me a submission stating:

    The phrase ``Probation Order'' has international resonance and recognition. Indeed-

I hope that the Minister hears this important point loud and clear-

    most eastern European countries are currently setting up Probation Services with Probation Orders based on the British model.

At a time when the new democracies in former Warsaw pact countries want to copy the United Kingdom because the system works, along comes the new Labour Government with its passion for change for change's sake and making everything new for no good reason. They want to change the name of the order, which everyone understands, and replace it with another name, which will confuse everyone. At the moment, a young offender who goes before a court and is sentenced to a probation order knows immediately what that means. In approaching policy making and legislation, we must adopt a frame of mind that always views the ECHR as an obstacle to be overcome. The ECHR is designed to ensure that the imperative to create a safe and secure society is not also an imperative that is driven irrespective of the requirements of justice and of tolerance of diverse lifestyles. Some lifestyles may not be regarded as the norm, yet properly fall within the ambit of private concerns.
