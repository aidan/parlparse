Paragraph 29 shows that the Government seek to absolve themselves of responsibility for decisions on extended periods of detention, and to put that responsibility on to the judiciary. To return to my original point, the whole Bill begins to fall down in so far as it is too all-embracing. By trying to meld the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989 and the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1998 and by adding little bits around the periphery, we have almost lost the objective of anti-terrorism legislation. We are debating an important schedule. I am glad that the hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone (Mr. Maginnis) has now recovered--it was certainly very warm in here when we began. We will all be better for a slightly cooler atmosphere. I have a few questions regarding the schedule as a whole, but before I raise them, I want to say something to the Minister because this is the first opportunity that I have had.

I was gently chided last week for a throwaway comment in which I suggested, absolutely wrongly, that the Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office was junior to his colleague, the Minister of State, Home Office, who is currently in Westminster Hall. Now that I have put myself back in relative favour with the Minister, I shall move on. Schedule 7 raises several major issues. As the hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone pointed out, the system that the Government seek to provide moves away from the general detention provisions in the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984. In his inquiry, Lord Lloyd examined the issue at some length. Perhaps I may summarise his conclusions, which are in chapter 9 of his report, on detention. He recommended that

    "the police should retain the power to detain terrorist suspects for a maximum of 48 hours in right of arrest. If a further period of detention is required in any case they should seek judicial authorisation to extend the period of detention for up to two days, making four days in all."

Effectively, therefore, he came to two conclusions. First, the power to extend should be granted only on judicial authority and secondly, the maximum length of detention should be four days.

His other recommendations--colleagues will have had a chance to examine his arguments--were, first, that

    "applications for extension of detention...should be heard...by the Chief Metropolitan Stipendiary Magistrate"

in England and similarly placed judicial authorities in Scotland and Northern Ireland. Secondly, in relation to access to a solicitor--an issue that crops up in this schedule--Lord Lloyd states at paragraph 9.30

    "Once a lasting peace been established, however, the reason for maintaining a different regime in Northern Ireland will fall away. I therefore recommend that the provisions concerning access to solicitors and notification of third parties in Northern Ireland be brought into line with those already applying to terrorist suspects in Great Britain."

He made similar comments about bringing into the same system interviews, tape recording, fingerprinting and samples, namely, that post provisions should apply. His conclusion in paragraph 9.54 was

    "Accordingly, I conclude that the new legislation need not prescribe a special regime for supervision of detention of terrorist suspects."

It is interesting that the Government's response was to say that they would neither follow Lord Lloyd--they have not--nor justify the reason for the extended period. They conceded the point about judicial review, but did not make out an overwhelming argument for the extension of time point. This is dealt with in paragraphs 8.20 to 8.24 of the consultation paper Cmnd 4178.

I remind colleagues of the Government's comments in paragraphs 8.21 and 8.22 of their consultation paper:

    "While Lord Lloyd acknowledges that there have been occasions on which the police might have found it more difficult to bring charges had they not been able to detain the suspect for longer than 4 days, he suggests that, once there is a lasting peace in Northern Ireland, it ought to be possible to reduce the maximum period for which a suspect could be detained under the new legislation for a total of 4 days--i.e. 2 days on the authority of the police plus two days with judicial authorisation before charge or release. His views may well have been influenced by the fact that the practice has been that extensions in international terrorist cases have not exceeded a total of 4 days. The Government recognises that in international cases detainees have either been released, charged or the decision taken to deport them within the 4-day period."

I pause there. The Government confirm there that the evidence to date is that four days has been the period at which decisions have been made. However, they state that

    "there can be no guarantee that this period will always be sufficient, particularly given the Government's intention to introduce new arrangements for the granting of extensions of detention, as set out above."

That was a non sequitur. The Government, for a reason not justified anywhere, have said that there can be no guarantee about this period. Of course there cannot be, but there is no evidence that that period has not been sufficient. It seems to me that there is a burden on the Government to argue the case for change, especially as the person whom they asked to produce a report argued that there should not be a change, and justified his argument.

The Government then make a trite point:

    "The attendance of a detainee at an oral hearing before a judicial authority will inevitably reduce the time available to the police for making further inquiries and questioning him about the results."

Paragraph 8.23 then lists the sorts of reasons for extension of detention, which in a way are self-evident. They state that inquiries can be time-consuming, particularly if more than one person has been arrested, and conclude:

    "Terrorists, moreover, are increasingly trained to resist interrogation and often refuse to answer questions or otherwise co-operate with the police inquiries."

The burden is on the Government to make the case. There have been wide representations in favour of an extension beyond the PACE powers unless there is a very good case to the contrary. If the Government are praying in aid, as they have done, the fact that in terrorist cases so far--not just Northern Ireland cases--the practice has been sufficient, and if there is to be a judicial review power to extend, I hope that Ministers will reconsider the extended power of detention that the schedule provides.

The Government certainly need to make the case. They must do more than simply say that they may need more time. The argument that, in theory, terrorists are becoming better trained and more able to resist, and are more likely to be able to resist interrogation for four days than for seven days seems to need clear evidence to bear it out. It is self-evident that someone can be trained to resist answering for a certain time. One could argue for 10 days, 12 days or 14 days. There is no magic in a certain number of days. I know of no psychological evidence that people cannot be trained to stay silent for more than a certain amount of time. The Government need to come up with a different argument for the schedule. The hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone made a linked but different point, about where the authority should reside. I would like to register that point and come back to it later when we debate whether the authority to grant excessive powers should reside with the Secretary of State, a judicial authority, or another authority.

May I put it to the Government that the right to consult a solicitor should be given to all people in all circumstances? A person who does not have access to a solicitor is more likely to say something than a person who has access to a solicitor, who will advise him to stay silent. But under some clauses in the Bill staying silent will add weight to the guilty side of the scales. It is worrying that we are moving away in legislation from the presumption that everyone is entitled to be silent towards an ability of the courts to draw inferences from people's silence.

Again, if the Government argue that there should be a different right of access to a solicitor in these provisions, the burden is on them to show more than the simple reason that it may be a disadvantage to talk to a lawyer. By definition, one could argue the case the other way. Here are offences that carry particular penalties because they are defined as terrorist offences. Given the wide definition of terrorism that we debated on clause 1, it could encompass quite a few people. The Government must say much more about how they can justify the extended powers to deprive people of solicitors and where their support comes from, apart from the law enforcement agencies who clearly have an interest.

My next point is specifically Northern Irish. One of the themes of Lord Lloyd's report is that he would understand certain powers being held until peace is restored in Northern Ireland. He therefore makes the case that a particular regime is not required for the generality of terrorism. It is required only, as in part VII of the Bill, for the conditions in Northern Ireland until peace is achieved. It would be helpful if the Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office, could confirm that the Government intend that those provisions of the Bill that are not in part VII and do not cover the transitional period, but which contain a derogation from the PACE rules or from other normal rules, will be brought back to the norm when peace is achieved in Northern Ireland.

Excessive powers that go beyond what Lord Lloyd proposed in other parts of the Bill were intended to be only for a limited period. The problem is that the Bill does not provide for that. It provides that they will become part of the Act and, as I understand it, there is no separate renewable or time-limited provision. It would be possible later to introduce a time limit for some of the provisions, just as part VII has a time limitation.

My last question is about the places in which people are currently detained in Northern Ireland.
