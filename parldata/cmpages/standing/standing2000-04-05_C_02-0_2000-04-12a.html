The Chairman: Before we commence the clause 1 stand part debate, I shall make two announcements.

Notwithstanding what I said at the previous sitting about, as a general rule, not selecting starred amendments, I have selected amendment (d) to new clause 3. The new clause appeared on the amendment paper for the first time yesterday, so a starred amendment was the only way to table an amendment to it.

It might help the Committee if I explain how we should proceed on the new clause, the amendments grouped with it and subsequent clauses and amendments. I have grouped new clause 3 with the clause 1 stand part debate, because the new clause is clearly intended to replace the first four clauses. This point in our proceedings is the first at which the matter arises.

I will therefore be content to allow a wide-ranging debate on whether the first four clauses should be replaced by the new clause, and on whether the new clause should first be amended as proposed in amendments (a) to (d).

At the end of the debate, I shall put the question that clause 1 stand part of the Bill. If clause 1 is disagreed to, the Committee's intention will have been made clear and I shall not then call the amendments to clause 2 that I have provisionally selected. Of course, if clause 1 is agreed to, we shall proceed in the usual way.

New clause 3 and the amendments tabled to it are to be debated only under the clause 1 stand part debate. Any votes on them will be taken towards the end of proceedings and without further debate. They will be called formally when we reach the new clauses.

I hope that that is clear.

Clause 1

Interpretation

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following: New clause 3-Strategy relating to fuel poverty-

    `.-(1) It shall be the duty of the appropriate authority to prepare and publish, before the end of the period of twelve months beginning with the relevant commencement, a strategy setting out the authority's policies for ensuring, by means including the taking of measures to ensure the efficient use of energy, that as far as reasonably practicable the homes of all households on lower incomes can be kept warm at reasonable cost.

    (2) The strategy must-

    (a) describe the households to which it applies,

    (b) specify measures for ensuring the efficient use of energy, such as the installation of appropriate equipment or insulation.

    (c) specify interim objectives to be achieved and target dates for achieving them, and

    (d) specify a target date for achieving the objective of ensuring that as far as reasobably practicable the homes of all households on lower incomes can be kept warm at reasonable cost.

    (3) In preparing the strategy or any revision of the strategy, the appropriate authority shall consult-

    (a) the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority and the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council, and

    (b) such other persons as the appropriate authority thinks fit.

    (4) The appropriate authority shall take such steps as are in its opinion necessary to implement the strategy.

    (5) The appropriate authority shall-

    (a) from time to time assess the impact of steps taken under subsection (4) and the progress made in achieving the objectives and meeting the target dates,

    (b) make any revision of the strategy which the authority considers appropriate in consequence of the assessment,

    (c) from time to time publish reports on such assessments.

    (6) If the appropriate authority revises the strategy, it shall publish the strategy as revised.

    (7) Any strategy complying with this section which was published by the Secretary of State within six months before the passing of this Act shall be taken to have been published under this section.

    (8) In this section-

    ``the appropriate authority'' means-

    (a) as respects England, the Secretary of State, and

    (b) as respects Wales, the National Assembly for Wales;

    ``the relevant commencement'' means-

    (a) as respects England, the day on which this Act is passed, and

    (b) as respects Wales, the day on which this section comes into force as respects Wales.

    (9) In relation to any time before the commencement of section 3(1) of the Utilities Act 2000, the reference in subsection (3)(a) to the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority and the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council shall have effect as a reference to the Director General of Gas Supply and the Director General of Electricity Supply.'.

    And the following amendments thereto: (a), in subsection (1), leave out ``on lower incomes''.

    (d) in subsection (2)(d), after ``date'', insert

    ``not more than fifteen years after the relevant commencement''.

(b), in subsection (2)(d), leave out ``on lower incomes''.

(c), at end of subsection (4), insert

    ``and achieve the objectives and meet the targets set out therein.''.

Mr. David Amess (Southend, West): A great deal has happened since out last sitting, and I am pleased to say that it has all been good. In hindsight, we have achieved a splendid result due to the week in which we have further considered the repercussions of accepting the original new clause. I am pleased with new clause 3, which, as you have already told the Committee, Mr. Gale, replaces clauses 1 to 4. To be frank, my first efforts were somewaht amateurish. That is not new among those piloting private Member's Bills, but I do not think that the clause was in order as I first submitted it.

I am grateful for the further work done by the Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions, the hon. Member for Sunderland, South (Mr. Mullin), and his officials. I am only too well aware that Ministers and their officials are pressed at such points in our parliamentary cycle, when Bills build up along the Committee Corridor. But that pales into insignificance when compared with the way in which the Conservative party is pressed, running between Committees and trying to maintain a quorum. The Bill is important to my hon. Friends and me, so we regard the time that the Minister and his officials have spent on working towards what we all want to achieve as being worthwhile. We now have something much better than the original new clause.

One improvement is that the strategy in new clause 3 requires the Bill to include a target date for ending fuel poverty. All hon. Members think that that is fundamental. Many of us feel that, although Governments are always blessed with good intentions, a carrot must be placed in front of them to encourage action to put an end to fuel poverty.

Another improvement is that new clause 3 promotes the installation of insulation and energy efficiency equipment rather than only energy efficiency ideals. It underpins the Bill's overall objective. We want local authorities and private landlords to take advantage of various programmes, many of which are operated by companies in our constituencies. Furthermore, new clause 3 will include a description of the household to which the strategy will apply. On Second Reading and at last week's sitting, we referred to the difficulty of defining poverty. That troubed one or two of my hon. Friends but, by and large, new clause 3 reaches a splendid compromise.

New subsection (2)(d) is clearly a major improvement. The principal aim of the warm homes campaign is a target by which fuel poverty will be eradicated. We shall continue to call for that to be not more than 15 years. New clause 3 requires a strategy to be drawn up under the Bill to

    specify a target date for achieving the objective of ensuring that as far as reasonably practicable the homes of all households on lower incomes can be kept warm at reasonable cost.

Households on lower incomes do not include only people who are on benefits. Perhaps the Minister will say something about that later. In the light of last week's long discussions on the definition of fuel poverty, the new clause requires a definition to be included in a strategy that I hope provides enough flexibility for the Government to work out a good definition and meet the concerns that members of the Committee raised last week. That objective must now be part of the strategy and the relevant authority must take the necessary steps to implement the strategy. In other words, the strategy must aim to end fuel poverty and it must be implemented. Unless that is part of the Bill, we are wasting our time.

New clause 1 required that the strategy contained only objectives and target dates. That could be for the alleviation of fuel poverty rather than its eradication. New clause 3 calls for its eradication. I shall be honest with members of the Committee and admit that the provision falls short of the previous aim to set a 15-year target, as it leaves the final decision about the target date to the Government. Perhaps that is one concession that we have made to them. The warm homes campaign continues to believe that the 15-year target is sensible, an issue to which those on the Liberal Democrat Benches will refer.

While the exact target is a matter of judgment, I still believe that a target date of 15 years is sensible. It will allow the programme to pay for itself in its own lifetime. On Second Reading, some of my right hon. and hon. Friends were troubled by the costs of such a programme. We debated the money resolution, and I have furnished them with details of how the proposal will be cost neutral. Under the 15-year target, it should pay for itself, through savings in the national health service, public-sector housing and job creation. A 15-year period would have been sensible in creating new jobs. It is simply not possible to train hundreds of thousands of people to do just two years' work. A 15-year programme throughout the United Kingdom will generate up to 30,000 jobs, which my constituents in Southend, West and people in other parts of Essex will welcome. The 15-year target will also increase the action that the Government are already taking, which is realistic and practical. I do not want to be churlish. I recognise that, from the Minister's point of view, the Government are already doing a great deal.

It has been argued that a programme of less than 15 years would be sensible, and the hon. Member for Nottingham, South (Mr. Simpson) feels strongly about that. The Labour party in Scotland pledged in its election manifesto to eradicate fuel poverty in two Parliaments-about eight years. I should not be party political, but it has yet to detail how that will be achieved, and we look forward to that announcement. A clear commitment has been made to match that in the rest of the United Kingdom.

Furthermore, the most recent analysis of the 1996 English house conditions survey suggests that the number of households suffering fuel poverty may have declined. Assuming that fuel poverty in Scotland, Wales and Northern Ireland is as prevalent as that in England, the decline may be from about 8 million to about 6.3 million households. Whatever figure is arrived at, that is a huge number of people, and this is not a third world country. Whether the number of people who need proper heating is 8 million or 6 million, in a civilised society that is an absolute disgrace.

Regardless of which political party parliamentarians support, they should all welcome the efforts made in the Bill to eradicate fuel poverty. The hon. Member for Plymouth, Sutton (Mrs Gilroy) has suggested a 15-year target. I realise that some hon. Members believe that it is not worth signing early-day motions, but occasionally they cut their cloth when it suits them, and for the hon. Lady to have persuaded nearly 400 Members to sign her early-day motion is splendid. That proposal would have tackled 7.5 million homes over a 15-year period. The Minister is probably sick to death of hearing about it, but I believe that he signed an early-day motion three times, and we welcome his support. Had the 6.3 million households been tackled at a rate of 500,000 a year, as the hon. Lady proposed, it would have taken fewer than 13 years to eradicate fuel poverty.

Politics is the art of the possible, and last week, in my amateurish way, I may have been trying to persuade the Committee and the Government to support the impossible. Both sides of the argument have given a bit and taken a bit, but as the promoter of the Bill, I assure the Committee that I am now delighted with what we have arrived at with new clause 3, and I hope that other hon. Members will feel as I do and support it.
