The Minister of State, Department of Trade and Industry (Mr. Ian McCartney): I beg to move,

    That the remainder of the Bill be considered in the following order: Clause 32, Clauses 35 to 38, Clauses 40 to 42, Clauses 33 and 34, Clause 39, Clauses 43 to 53, New Clauses, Schedules 1 to 3, New Schedules.

The motion primarily concerns the handling of Clause 34, which could significantly affect the debate on that clause and clauses 33 and 39, which refer to clause 34.

Clause 33 applies the Bill's provisions to those in Crown employment. Clause 34 qualifies that application with regard to members of the armed forces and provides flexible provisions that are designed to reflect the unique status of service men and women. Those provisions include the potential to set a longer pay reference period for armed forces personnel; the facility to pay the hourly rate by reference to notional or average hours worked in that period; the flexibility to include or exclude particular benefits from the calculation and the freedom for the armed forces to keep their own record-keeping procedures and to use their internal service procedures for redress.

Unlimited liability for duty is essential to the operational effectiveness of the armed forces. All service personnel accept the need to be available for duty when required and recognise that duties will vary according to the needs of the service and that pay is not adapted to reflect work rates. The current operational circumstances facing our service men in a wide range of locations--Northern Ireland, Bosnia and the Gulf--underline the fundamental importance of maintaining that ethos and commitment.

In acknowledgement of that, service men and women bind themselves to a particular form of service that accepts that the requirements of the service are paramount. In return, it is incumbent on the Government to ensure that service pay is determined in a way that is fair and recognises those unique circumstances.

As the notes on clauses makes clear, in putting forward clause 34 the Government have sought to ensure consistency and universality in the application of the minimum wage. The flexibility built into the clause aims to include the armed forces in the ambit of the Bill without damaging their capabilities or undermining their operational and military effectiveness. We have been considering carefully the practicalities of applying the national minimum wage in the diverse circumstances in which the armed forces operate.

I ask therefore that the discussion of clauses 33, 34 and 39 be deferred as set out in the order of consideration. That will ensure that the Committee has the opportunity fully to debate and scrutinise the clauses and the Government amendments to exempt the armed forces, rather than having to wait for the Committee to report the Bill to the House, for Third Reading or for the Bill's consideration in another place.

The Government's view is that service men and women should not be disadvantaged by being exempt from direct coverage of the Bill. We have therefore examined the current arrangements provided by the Armed Forces Pay Review Body. That body offers a well-established system that has gained the widespread confidence of individuals in the service, who have no collective or individual bargaining rights, and who think the system fair. The body's annual review takes into account a broad range of factors, including comparability with civilian pay and the overall balance of advantages and disadvantages in service life.

The effects of implementing the national minimum wage will, therefore, feed through to service pay in comparisons with civilian sector jobs undertaken by the review body in formulating its recommendations to the Government. It is open to the Government to include in its economic and other evidence to the review body any factor such as the relationship of service pay to the national minimum wage or the situation of the lowest paid service men and women. The current review body arrangements are flexible and robust enough to provide a fair level of remuneration for the armed forces and ensure that service pay does not fall below the national minimum wage. By utilising the existing mechanisms for determining armed forces pay, we will be able to complement the arrangements in the Bill for applying the national minimum wage to other sectors without disturbing the armed forces' unique position.

The initial inclusion of the current clause 34 and the decision that I have announced today simply acknowledge the unique position of the armed forces, which has been recognised by all parties over the years. It should not be seen by Opposition Members or anyone else as a precedent for the treatment of any other groups. I should add for the avoidance of doubt that we intend that civilian employees of the Ministry of Defence will continue to be covered by the Bill.

For a person to be a home worker, the work that he is contracted to do is required to be carried out in a place that is not under the control of management of the employer. The definition of a home worker in the clause is too broad and is counter-intuitive. Most people would understand the term ``home worker'' to mean a person who works at home. As I understand the clause, it could cover a maintenance engineer who carries out his daily work on the premises of a third party. That is manifestly a place that is not under the control or management of the employer; equally, however, the engineer is not doing home work in the usual definition or understanding of the term.
