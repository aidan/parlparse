Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): When we broke for lunch, we were discussing clause 89 and why, during the consultation process—the Government boasted that it was extensive in the run-up to publication of the Bill—there was no mention between the Government and the consultees of the forthcoming White Paper on communications, especially as that White Paper had been heralded in the document, ``Regulating Communications: The Way Ahead'', which was published in June 1999. Second Reading on 31 January 2000 was just three days prior to publication of the Department of Trade and Industry press release on 3 February, which announced that the White Paper would be published in the autumn. It is the practice for such press releases to be in draft for several days prior to publication. Ministers will have been aware of the press release, which stated that the Government would make proposals for reforming telecommunications and broadcasting regulations. The words ``telecommunications'' and ``regulations'' were included in the draft press release that was circulated round the Department of Trade and Industry at the time of Second Reading.

It must have been in Ministers' minds that the White Paper was forthcoming, so it should have formed part of the dialogue between Ministers and civil servants and the consultees—the telecommunications companies. If Ministers did not raise the matter or if they did not know that the White Paper was forthcoming, they were negligent in that, but I find it difficult to believe that they were not aware of it. If they were aware of it and decided that the industry would just have to go through the process twice, they are guilty of gross maladministration. They cannot have it both ways; either they knew about the forthcoming White Paper and decided that it did not matter that utility companies would be involved in a huge amount of effort and cost, or they did not know, in which case, what on earth was going on in the Department of Trade and Industry? It would be helpful to know which of those two options applies.

The Government are trying to get out of a highly embarrassing situation and explicit exposure of their lethargic approach to the details of government and administration by seeking to mislead. The letters from the operators group and BT are hostile to the bid. The main letter, which was sent to all members of the Committee and to Ministers, states:

    we feel that the current Bill is inappropriate and that its underlying approach is flawed.

    the reality is that the Government, through the Utilities Bill, is stepping sharply backward, rather than advancing the regulatory model to take into account market developments such as convergence.

The third major point in that open letter is:

    The prospect of unlimited fines, with no full appeal mechanism, and communications companies being agents of social and environmental policy through vague or even undefined measures will severely lessen the UK's attractiveness for investment in communications.

That is a severe criticism of the framework of the Bill and it is not enough to say that a White Paper will be published in due course. The Minister said earlier that the framework of the Bill was unsuitable for the telecommunications industry.

Mr. Stunell: The amendment relates to the period for which the Secretary of State shall issue guidance. It aims to ensure that no Secretary of State—or no future Secretary of State, to be generous and fair to the current incumbent goes to sleep on the job of dealing with such important issues.

The amendment states that a review should take place on a set time scale; it should not simply be left to the Secretary of State to decide on the time that elapses between reviews, which are clearly needed. If one considers the issues at stake—the social and environmental matters relating to the gas industry the Secretary of State's guidance will clearly be significant. Furthermore, the environment in which that guidance must be made may change rapidly in the future. Changes are already occurring rapidly in the market structure, and there might be changes in social and economic conditions. Hon. Members of all parties are discussing how to make progress on social inclusion, which is of great concern to the Government, and certainly to Liberal Democrat Members. On Friday, in private Members' business, the House will debate the Warm Homes and Energy Conservation Bill. The Government are being subjected, at various levels, to pressure and lobbying to respond and introduce relevant policies.

In an undoubtedly rapidly changing energy market, and a rapidly changing social and cultural environment, it is proper for the Secretary of State's guidance to be kept up to date, and to be provided in a timely and appropriate way. The essence of the amendment is that, whatever else happens, the guidance will be reviewed and refreshed every five years. That five-year term is broadly consistent with the lifetime of a Parliament and ensures that every Government will have not simply the opportunity but the obligation to bring before the regulatory authorities and into the light of day their views about these vital matters of social and environmental concern. One way in which the inertia of Government functions is that it is possible, simply by inaction, for a Government to inherit their predecessor's policies. By failing to make statements or to comment proactively on a topic, they can allow matters to continue along a course that they had sternly criticised in the past.

The amendment is would make the process of government more transparent and place on a Secretary of State an obligation to come to the House and set out clearly to the regulatory authorities the guidance that he or she thought appropriate. Hon. Members would then have an opportunity to bring their views to bear, and a public debate could take place.

This is not a complex amendment with subtle, hidden side-effects. It is a straightforward amendment to ensure that no Secretary of State can hide behind his predecessor's actions or inactions, but must confront the issues and provide an opportunity and a forum for debate. I hope that it will find favour with the Committee.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): The amendments have two purposes. First, they define the time frame in which the Secretary of State should issue guidance, ensuring that new statutory guidance to the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority is issued every five years as a minimum. They also state that the guidance should be based on long-term policies and objectives.

The Government believe that regulatory stability must be maximised by avoiding the issuing and reissuing of guidance. Constant changes to the guidance would contribute to regulatory instability and cause the cost of capital to rise, to the ultimate detriment of consumers. It is not appropriate to impose an arbitrary frequency on the issuing of guidance, irrespective of whether new developments or policy changes require it to be reissued. Indeed, if the guidance is focused on the Government's long-term policies and objectives—amendments Nos. 227 and 243 seem to suggest that the hon. Member for Lewes would support that—it might not be necessary for the Secretary of State to issue guidance every five years.

The point common to both amendments is that guidance should be issued on a long-term basis. The Government set the social and environmental policy objectives that will be contained in the guidance, so the content of the guidance will depend on what those policies and objectives are. It would be madness if the Government issued guidance that was intended to last several years which did not include long-term policies and objectives.

If the hon. Member for Lewes examines the draft statutory guidance to the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority, he will see that it focuses almost entirely on long-term policies and goals. Therefore, we must ensure that guidance is a flexible tool that the Government can use in a variety of circumstances. Circumstances may dictate, for instance, that medium or even shorter-term goals are included in the guidance. The hon. Gentleman's amendments would prevent that. I therefore resist them.

 

Mr. Stunell: I am sorry to hear the Minister's approach. He says that he does not want to see advice chopping and changing. We understand that: a regulatory system must contain stability. I am disappointed by the Government's response to the amendments, and I hope that these are matters to which we can return, but I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
