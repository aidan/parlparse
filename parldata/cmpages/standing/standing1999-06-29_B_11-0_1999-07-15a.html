Food Standards Bill

New clause 6

Performance criteria

    `The Agency shall define and publish quantifiable criteria of its performance and shall include performance against those criteria in each annual report to Parliament.'.--[Mr. Paice.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Question proposed [this day], That the clause be read a Second time.

1.30 pm

Question again proposed.

The Minister for Public Health (Ms Tessa Jowell): Before we adjourned, I was answering the various points made by the hon. Member for South-East Cambridgeshire (Mr. Paice) and the right hon. Member for Penrith and The Border (Mr. Maclean). I referred to the inclusion in clause 4 of a reference to the agency preparing a report on its activities and performance, following the recommendation of the Special Select Committee. I said that we intended that the agency's annual report should not be self-centred or congratulatory, but be a rigorous assessment of the agency's performance and achievements.

The key issue raised by new clause 6 is how the agency's performance might be measured. We entirely agree about the importance of a transparent benchmarking of progress in such an important area of public health policy. It is important that the performance of the agency should be measurable, but we need to ensure that we measure the right factors. We have already discussed measuring the reduction in the level of food-borne illness, which might be an attractive and tangible way to gauge the agency's performance. However, the general view is that such a measurement might be unhelpful; we hope that the agency will not only tackle the causes of food-borne illness but ensure a more rigorous and systematic notification of it. One of the great public health problems in tracking the epidemiology of many food-borne illnesses is that reporting is erratic. We seek to rectify that.

I expect in the first instance that two performance measurements will be process measurements and specific outcome measurements. I hope that the agency will seek to engage the public in that debate. Every Department is accountable for delivering performance against its public service agreement. MAFF has a clear target to establish the Food Standards Agency and put in place a system to measure its performance in a way that will contribute to food safety. The public service agreement framework already provides one such yardstick.

The sort of performance measurements that we would expect the FSA to take unto itself could be classified under such headings as ``Progress in promoting HACCP'' and ``Quality of expert advice provided by the advisory committees''. I hope that there will be some scrutiny of the proportionate nature of the exercise of the agency's powers. That will be one of the board's early jobs.

Mr. Mark Todd (South Derbyshire): One critical way to measure effectiveness could be to measure the agency's work against the achievements of similar organisations. One of the agency's first tasks might be to find a basis for making that comparison, so that one could examine the performance of the FSA against that of similar bodies in other countries.

Ms Jowell: I thank my hon. Friend for that suggestion. It is certainly an area of comparative study, but the caveat must always be that conclusions about similarity should be based on comparisons of like with like.

I sought to indicate the broad territory within which I would expect the FSA board to consider the early targets against which performance could be measured and how they would appear in the annual report.

Mr. Kevin Barron (Rother Valley): My right hon. Friend will know that the Special Select Committee made a specific recommendation that the agency's annual report should state its annual progress not only on food safety, but on raising dietary and nutritional standards. I accept that the FSA will be independent, but can citizens expect to see those latter indicators included in the annual report?

Ms Jowell: The agency may seek to collect and publish that sort of information. For instance, the food survey run by MAFF, which will be taken over by the agency, provides a means of tracking national changes in the pattern of diet. That is clearly at the boundary, but it is the sort of information that the agency will collect and disseminate on the status of diet and the broader public health role of the Department of Health which we examined earlier.

In addition to the agency engaging the public in the development of performance measures, we would expect the annual report to be just that --a report on progress. As we made clear, the Select Committees will rightly be interested in it, and will want to exercise their power of scrutiny. Parliament, too, will no doubt want to debate the agency's report.

Mr. Geoffrey Clifton-Brown (Cotswold): Following on from the important comments of the hon. Member for South Derbyshire (Mr. Todd), is the right hon. Lady aware that the National Audit Office, as scrutinised by the Public Accounts Committee, already does a huge amount of work in comparing various agencies in respect of performance indicator levels? The agency will be involved in a huge range of performance indicator levels. Will the Minister make it clear that she expects the agency to work closely with the National Audit Office in its excellent work?

Ms Jowell: Yes, I can easily give that assurance. I would expect that to be to the benefit of the efficient running of the agency.

I have outlined the functions for which I expect the agency to identify its performance measures, and some of the pitfalls associated with doing so; the possible benefits of adopting measures for both process and outcomes; and the importance of the annual report in rigorously and self-critically recording progress against targets. I have described the other elements of parliamentary scrutiny that will be brought to bear on the agency's performance.

I conclude by reiterating a point that I hope is beyond all doubt. We expect the agency to define and publish its performance measures --what the public are receiving, or, as the hon. Member for Cotswold (Mr. Clifton-Brown) said, how the taxpayer's money is being spent. Making public that assessment of progress in meeting targets will be an important means of engaging the public in the discharge of the agency's functions.

Having dealt at some length with the case made in the new clause, having put much of the Government's thinking on the matter on the record and having made clear our wish that the nature of the targets should not be specified in the Bill but that we expect targets to be set, I hope that the hon. Member for South-East Cambridgeshire will see fit to withdraw the motion.

Mr. James Paice (South-East Cambridgeshire): I am grateful to the Minister for her response to the new clause and for the constructive way in which she has approached it. However, if everything is as good as she describes, I cannot understand the argument for not specifying the requirement in the Bill. In concluding, she made a remark about not wanting targets to be specified in the Bill. No one is proposing that the nature of the targets should be specified in the Bill. We merely propose that the agency should be required to establish and publish them. The Minister said that she ``hopes and expects'' that the agency will do that. I am not sure that that is sufficiently strong. It should be required to do so. I do not deny that public service agreements such as those from which she quoted are useful, but they are not the same as a publicly declared obligation on the agency, which would be the result of specifying the requirement in the Bill.

Having said that, we still have a little more work to do in considering the Bill, and I do not want to delay the Committee. I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the motion.

Motion and clause, by leave, withdrawn.

New clause 10

Duties of the Agency in relation to food supplements

    `. --(1) The Agency shall define what constitutes a food in accordance with section 1 of the 1990 Act.

    (2) The Agency shall decide what foods or food products are to be classified as food supplements in accordance with the definition in subsection (5).

    (3) The Agency may refer food supplements to the Medicines Control Agency to decide whether or not they should be submitted to the licensing authority for medicinal products to determine whether they should be licensed as medicines.:

    (4) Where a food supplement which has been referred under subsection (3) is not subsequently licensed as a medicine, the Agency shall retain responsibility for all regulatory matters relating to that food supplement.

    (5) In this section, ``food supplement'' means food, in the form of a capsule, tablet, powder or liquid, which--

    (a) contains any vitamin, mineral, fatty acids or amino acids,

    (b) if consumed, would supplement the intake of vitamins, minerals, fatty acids or amino acids from the diet,

    (c) is not a significant source of energy, and -

    (d) is not specifically claimed to have the property of preventing, treating or curing a human disease.'. --[Dr. Brand.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Dr. Peter Brand (Isle of Wight): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

I thought that it would be useful to take the opportunity that the Bill offers to clear up a grey area in the definition of food, foodstuffs, food supplements and borderline substances related to food. No doubt every member of the Committee has, like me, received letters about the threat that the Medicines Control Agency apparently poses to some traditional herbal remedies and food and mineral supplements.

I have no problem with the MCA's stepping in when a specific medical claim is made for a substance, no matter what it be, because by definition that would constitute a description of a medicine, so it is necessary to establish whether it does any good or, more important, whether it may do harm. However, I am worried that the MCA appears to have the power to act as prosecutor, judge, jury and executioner.

We now have an opportunity to build in some checks and balances. I am not suggesting that the FSA should control the MCA, but the FSA would be an appropriate vehicle to screen out references to the MCA, as would happen under new clause 10. The Minister for Public Health has said that that is a grey area, and we now have an opportunity to deal with it without further legislation.
