Mr. McLeish: I shall move on quickly to the substance of the vital amendments that we are considering.

Members of the Committee have asked why the anti-social behaviour orders cannot be applied to 12-year-olds in Scotland. There is little difference of substance between the two sides of the Committee. I do not underestimate the destruction and distress that children under 16 can cause in communities. That view unites the Committee. I have personal experience of such destruction. We are considering not only children but parents. The Bill applies a family concept to the problem.

Scotland has a distinctive system of children's hearings. They are not a soft option. A wide variety of disposals is available to them. They range from secure accommodation to the proverbial warning the use of the discipline of the children's hearing as a punishment. I do not want to cut across the sheriffs to make anti-social behaviour orders against under-16s.

My colleagues stressed that they did not want juveniles to be the only target of anti-social behaviour orders in England and Wales. A child and his or her parents are required to attend a children's hearing. The hearings are conscious of the needs of the child. It is in the child's best interests to confront his anti-social behaviour and its impact on communities.

Hearings can require compulsory supervision of the child and attach a range of conditions to that. For example, a child may be prevented from having contact with specific individuals, or groups of individuals. That might be useful in helping the child to break associations with undesirable elements, especially members of a peer group who damage the interests of the youngster. If there was a breach of a supervision requirement, the hearing would review its original decision and would consider the case for additional conditions and stronger measures.

However, even if the Scottish orders applied to under-16-year-olds, it would not be appropriate for them to be extended to the parents of the relevant children. It is a fundamental principle of Scots law that the parent should not be held responsible for the actions of the child. That principle was considered in depth by the Kilbrandon Committee in its 1964 report on "Children and young persons in Scotland" , which led to the children's hearing system and reaffirmed the principle. To serve an order on a parent because of the actions of a child, or to require the parent to ensure that a child complies with an anti-social behaviour order, would cut across that central principle.

I fully agree, however, that parents should be involved in decisions about their child and encouraged to support the child's future behaviour. The children's hearing system does that by addressing the child's problems in the family context. It also cements the family bond. I am sure that all hon. Members would support that in terms of family law and family policy. On far too many occasions, parents are not accountable for the activities of their children and the children are not responsible to their parents. That situation exists in our society, but the amendments would not help to remedy it in the Scottish context.

Under the children's hearing system, parents are encouraged to take part in the process of deciding what action should be taken in relation to their child, and are expected to support the child as he or she carries out the requirements of the hearing.
