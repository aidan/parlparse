New Clause 2

Appeals to the Employment Appeal Tribunal

    `. In section 21(1) of the Employment Tribunals Act 1996 (appeal from employment tribunal to Employment Appeal Tribunal on question of law arising under or by virtue of the enactments there specified) after paragraph (f) there shall be inserted--

    ``(ff) the National Minimum Wage Act 1998, or''.'.--[Mr. Ian McCartney.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

The Chairman: With this, we may take amendment No. 154.

Mr. McCartney: I beg to move, That the clause be read a second time. The relief is rushing through me.

The new clause provides that appeals on a point of law from proceedings before industrial tribunals under the free-standing provisions of the Bill will go to the employment appeal tribunal. The new clause adds the National Minimum Wage Act 1998 to those Acts in section 21(1) of the Industrial Tribunals Act 1996, which will be renamed as the Employment Tribunals Act by the Employment Rights (Dispute Resolution) Bill. Examples of the free-standing provisions created by the Bill are clause 11, which deals with access to records--an employee right; clause 17, which deals with an appeal against an enforcement notice--an employer issue; clause 20, which deals with an appeal against a penalty notice--another right for employers; and clause 22, which deals with detriment in relation to employee rights.

Without the new clause, the employment appeal tribunal would have no jurisdiction to hear such appeals and therefore it is important that the Committee supports the new clause.

Appeals under other employment jurisdictions, for example the Equal Pay Act 1970, the Sex Discrimination Act 1975, the Race Relations Act 1976, The Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992, the Disability Discrimination Act 1995 and the Employment Rights Act 1996, are heard by the employment appeal tribunal. It is a specialist employment court and it is sensible that it should have the jurisdiction to hear the appeals under this Bill. Such appeals can be made only on points of law.

Amendment No. 154 makes a consequential repeal to section 21(1) of the Employment Tribunals Act 1996, made necessary by the addition of this Bill to the list of Acts in subsection (1) of the 1996 Act. It is a sensible and logical addition, and I commend both the new clause and the amendment to the Committee.

New Clause 1

Hourly rate of remuneration (increase)

    `. Any hourly rate of remuneration determined by regulations made under section 2(1) above shall, at the end of one year after those regulations were made and annually thereafter, be increased by--

    (a) the rate of inflation; or

    (b) the percentage rise in the median of full-time hourly earnings during the preceding year,

whichever is the greater.'.--[Mr. Chidgey.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Chidgey: I beg to move, that the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause is clear and prescribes how the national minimum wage, once it has been established, should be reviewed and uprated over time. If we introduce a national minimum wage which is then fixed in tablets of stone, that wage will very soon, through due process and the movement of the economy, have little value in achieving its main objective which we understand is to protect the poorest people on the lowest wages.

We need some mechanism with which to review uprating of the national minimum wage. There are two routes to follow: we could either introduce a sporadic, almost ad hoc, process of uprating or, as the new clause suggests, we could have an automatic system of uprating which could be operated according to a separate formula. The two that I included in the new clause are the retail price index and the annual rate of inflation.

5 pm

Not surprisingly, there are two diametrically opposed views in the establishment as to whether there should be an automatic system for uprating the national minimum wage. On one side of the fence are the Confederation of British Industry, the Engineering Employers Federation and the Centre for Economic Performance, which are opposed to the principle of regular uprating. On the other side of the fence--again, not surprisingly--are the Transport and General Workers Union, the Low Pay Unit and other bodies which believe that the national minimum wage should keep pace with average earnings. Interestingly, the British Hospitality Association finds itself in a dilemma: it is worried about the possible inflationary effects that indexation of the national minimum wage could have, but it is also worried that regular uprating of the national minimum wage could cause serious economic shocks in its industry.

I think that the case for regular uprating of the national minimum wage is sound. Members of the Committee will know from their research that in the United States there are no automatic mechanisms for increasing the federal minimum wage. Therefore, any increases that are eventually introduced must be legislated for in their democratic process. That, over the years, has caused many high profile, politically acrimonious debates about the relative merits of keeping a minimum wage at all, which takes up considerable time in the legislative process.

The value of the national minimum wage in real terms and relative to the general level of earnings will be eroded without an automatic uprating mechanism. It should also be of concern to the Government, whose intentions regarding introducing a national minimum wage are clear and determined, that without some form of automatic uprating the national minimum wage could become a hostage to the fortunes of a future Administration which, given the passage of time and the nature of our parliamentary democracy, could well be hostile to the concept.

It would be very easy to allow the national minimum wage to become defunct by simply not allowing parliamentary time for the legislation that would be necessary to review it on an ad hoc basis. There is no point in the Committee agonising over the initial level of the national minimum wage, placing bets on what the Low Pay Commission will eventually recommend and spending months scrutinising the Bill as part of the process of introducing legislation if, when we have completed our labours and the Bill completes its passage through Parliament, we leave the level unchanged year on year without reflecting the normal functioning of the economy, generating increases in prices and earnings in a general way on a year-to-year basis.

The Government may believe that the Low Pay Commission will report on uprating. Perhaps I have not been following the proceedings closely enough, but it is far from clear whether the Low Pay Commission is intended to be a statutory and permanent body. If that is not the Government's intention, then the opportunity for regular review is removed.

Fundamentally, it should be clear that the national minimum wage should not only be set at a sensible rate, but should benefit from a sensitive system that provides regular uprating of minimum requirements in ongoing legislation.

New clause 4 deals with family members employed in a business and proposes the exclusion of such people from the scope of the minimum wage. It is principally small firms that this would affect, and our contention is that it would be inappropriate to inject the force of the law into family relationships in a small business. The most extreme example of what I have in mind--and of what my hon. Friend the Member for Buckingham had in mind when he tabled the amendment--would be a small retail business that has a single employee who is the wife or husband of the employer.

There will also be many instances, especially in ethnic minority communities, of members of an extended family working together in a business. In such cases, remuneration might take a complex form: the family might live together and share accommodation costs, which would not make it easy to determine whether the minimum wage had been paid. The Minister must know in his heart of hearts that the Bill is almost entirely irrelevant to such a situation, except in extreme circumstances such as a family dispute or family breakdown, in which this type of law should play no part. It would be impossible to determine whether a husband was paying his wife the minimum wage or whether payments in kind such as bed and board had been made available. Payments in kind would have to be taken into account in computing whether the minimum wage had been paid. If uncle, aunt, granny and cousins were living in the same house, it would be impossible to determine how many hours each of them was working.

To include this class of employees within the Bill's scope would be inviting employers and employees in such situations to ignore the law, to falsify records and to say whatever they liked. There would be no way of bringing them to book, and turning a blind eye to that would bring this law in particular and the law in general into disrepute.

Clause 14(2) already provides that officers would not require a person to give information that would have the effect of incriminating the person giving the information or the spouse of the person giving the information. That would preclude the Bill's provisions from operating effectively in situations in which a husband and wife were in an employer-employee relationship. The employment of close family members in a small business is a special class of employment and is simply not amenable to the imposition of legal constraints. Such constraints would not work in such a context and it would probably not be right to attempt to make them work.

New clauses 5 and 8 have not been tabled with the intention of disadvantaging disabled people or of depriving them of the rights that they share with other citizens. They have been proposed because as technology develops and as more and more workplace transactions are conducted electronically, there is the prospect that prejudice will be eliminated. More and more people will be working with others whose race, creed, gender, skin colour or disability will be genuinely hidden from them because those people will be electronic entities. They will have an electronic existence at the other end of a telecommunications link.

The reality is, however, that many disabled people will be less productive in their work because of their disability. Under those circumstances, it is right that society should compensate them, through state benefits or other forms of assistance. That will enable them to price themselves back into the labour market, by charging a competitive rate for their work. They will be competitive with an able-bodied person. That is an admirable objective, and one that I hope that the Minister will not attempt to misrepresent. It can only benefit disabled people and I hope that the Minister will accept the good intentions of Conservative Members and give due consideration to the principles behind the new clauses, which we have aired one final time in this marathon Committee.
