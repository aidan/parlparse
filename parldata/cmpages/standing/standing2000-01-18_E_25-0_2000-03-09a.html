The Minister for Housing and Planning (Mr. Nick Raynsford): We are approaching the part of the Bill that deals with charging schemes, so I shall give a brief overview of the contents of clauses 147 and 148.

Clause 147 sets out the practical measures that must be dealt with in charging schemes, such as designating the roads to be charged. Clause 148 deals with exemptions from charges. The clause requires any road user charging scheme to designate which roads are included in the scheme, explain what event triggers a charge, and set out the types of vehicles to be charged and the charges that apply. Those are the details about which the hon. Member for Poole (Mr. Syms) justifiably inquired.

The clause requires that charges must apply to using a vehicle as well as to "keeping" it or parking it on the road. The reference to "keeping" is necessary, as it will allow authorities to enforce schemes against parked as well as moving vehicles. That will allow permits in parked vehicles in a charging scheme area to be checked.

Clause 147 ensures that the road charging powers cannot be used to impose additional parking charges in an area. The clause also allows a charging scheme to provide for how a charge is to be recorded or paid. In practical terms, that means whether a charge is paid and collected in cash at toll points, or requires a paper licence, or is recorded and paid electronically. Schemes may require documents or licences to be displayed or equipment to be installed in vehicles. Those are all options because, as hon. Members know, such schemes can operate in several different ways.

Charging authorities will also be able to apply different charges for different types of vehicle, different days or times of the day, and different roads in a charging scheme. That will allow flexibility, for example, for charges to apply at peak congested times only, or for evenings and weekends to be uncharged. That point was raised by the hon. Clause 149 imposes penalties and liabilities. It does so because it is important that charging authorities have sufficient powers to enforce charging schemes fairly and consistently. Clause 149 covers penalty charges and the liability for penalties. If, for example, a vehicle is found in the area of the charging scheme without a required permit, the penalty charge will be issued. The liability would fall on the registered keeper of the vehicle as already happens with parking fines except when specified otherwise in regulations. These could transfer the liabilities say, to the hirer where there is a valid hire agreement for a vehicle, or if a vehicle has been reported stolen. Those provisions would mirror the provisions that exist in the case of parking charge.

Clause 149 also provides for criminal offences in situations where there is a systematic attempt to evade charges through using forged documents, by tampering with electronic charging equipment in a vehicle or by removing penalty charge notices from windscreens.

Clause 150 provides for rights of entry. Regulations will allow charging authorities power to authorise people to enter vehicles to check permits and electronic charging equipment and whether it's been tampered with. We expect that the great bulk of enforcement work can be undertaken from the outside of a vehicle, but we envisage the need for an occasional spot check to deter the use of forged permits or equipment that has been tampered with.
