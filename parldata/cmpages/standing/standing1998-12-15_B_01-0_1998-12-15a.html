Clause 1

Payment for hospital treatment of traffic casualties

Dr. Harris: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 8, after first `a', insert `direct'.

This is to seek clarification of what the Minister thinks would be a reasonable charge on insurance companies following road accidents. It was not clear to everyone on Second Reading—I was surprised to hear this raised by people outside the House—that the provisions apply only to third parties injured in road traffic accidents. Under clause 1, it is only third parties who can claim compensation from an insurance company. Most people assume that if they, as drivers, drive into a tree, they will be caught by the provisions. It is important for the Government to clarify that that will not be the case and that only in a proportion of road traffic accidents will insurance companies have to make a payment to the national health service.

It is therefore important to be clear that the liability of insurance companies to the NHS will be limited only to third parties who suffer injuries as a direct result of road traffic accidents. I can envisage a number of scenarios where that might not be clear. I am pleased to see the hon. Member for South Swindon (Ms Drown) serving on the Committee. She has great experience of the NHS—if not on the clinical side of its work, then certainly on the paper-chasing side because she was the finance director of the trust in my constituency where I worked briefly as a junior doctor. I am pleased to say that we had no run-ins there.

The hon. Lady will know that defining what treatment stems from an initial action or presentation to hospital is not easily separated from follow-up work that may or may not be related to an incident or condition that brought a person to hospital in the first place. For example, people who witness an accident but who are not involved in the direct impact may suffer as a result, perhaps from cardiac symptoms. The shock of witnessing an accident or being involved in a near miss has been known to precipitate cardiac symptoms and conditions—in the most brutal case, a heart attack. Similarly, people involved in an accident but who have no initial signs of damage may later report whiplash, although one might argue that that is a direct, albeit delayed, result of an accident, or post-traumatic stress. People involved in accidents often report that the biggest effect on their lives was not so much the flesh injury but the psychological effects of the shock of being involved in, or being witness, to it. Without this clarifying amendment it is not clear that only direct results of an accident, such as orthopaedic injuries, will be covered by the Bill.

The Minister may argue—I hope that he does not object to me pre-empting him—that the charges will cover what is defined later in the clause; that is, when a compensation payment has been made by an insurance company in respect of an injury or death. In other words, if an insurance company agrees that the condition for which a person is being treated is a result of an accident, that should be sufficient evidence for it to be covered by the Bill and for the NHS to be reimbursed by the insurer. Clause 1(1)(b) is then the precipitating factor. However, there will often be more than one pathology. An orthopaedic injury may result directly from the accident, but there may be other symptoms that are not so clearly a direct result of the accident—for example, post-traumatic stress—which may not be easily traceable to the initial impact. If an insurance company pays compensation linked to a flesh-and-bone or orthopaedic injury, but does not recognise other injuries which the claimant attributes to the accident, will the NHS be entitled to bill the insurance company for the cost of treating the latter injuries? That is not clear in the Bill and the amendment would clarify the matter by adding a further qualification requiring the injury to be a ``direct'' result of the accident. That would minimise the payments payable by the insurance company to the NHS. Liberal Democrats do not want the NHS to be deprived of funding, but, as we said on Second Reading, we do not support the principle of levying charges on users indirectly through insurance premiums. The amendment would narrow the criteria and prevent insurance premiums from escalating further than they are doomed to do. I should be grateful for the Minister's comments on that.

A third-party victim of a road traffic accident may be seen for follow-up as an out-patient and another condition may become apparent which later requires in-patient treatment. That condition may be due to the road traffic accident, or it may have existed prior to the accident without the victim being aware of it. People, rightly, expect compensation for injuries that deprive them of an income and may claim compensation. A condition may have pre-existed without the patient's knowledge and it may be difficult, with the best will in the world, to clarify whether it was caused by the accident or existed prior to the accident and was merely brought to light by hospital investigations following the accident. If such a condition requires out-patient treatment, there would be a flat-rate charge and no additional liability to the NHS; if in-patient treatment is required, it is not clear whether causation must be proven or whether the Bill relies on a compensation payment specifying a list of ailments and conditions, some of which only later come to light.

There are thus two problems. First, an insurance company may make a compensation payment ostensibly, but perhaps not explicitly, for an orthopaedic or soft tissue injury and other conditions may present later which are less clearly identified with the road traffic accident. Secondly, a condition may be picked up on arrival at hospital which cannot easily be seen to be a direct result of the accident, but could be. It is important to clarify the matter, because if the Minister cannot say which pathologies are to be picked up by subsection 1(a), Opposition Members will find it hard to understand the figures mentioned on Second Reading, which, I believe, the Minister intends to specify in regulations: £435 a day for an in-patient and £354 a day for an out-patient. The Liberal Democrats hope that those figures were not plucked from a hat. However, given that it is hard to see what conditions the clause will cover, how can the figures be anything other than a guesstimate?
