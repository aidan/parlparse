Clause 109

Rent factoring

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Dawn Primarolo: I hope not to detain the Committee for too long, but I must make one or two points about the clause. It deals with schemes that are, in substance, equivalent to loans that aim to generate tax relief on far more than the actual costs of borrowing the money. It ensures that such schemes will not succeed. The schemes transfer a stream of taxable rental income in return for a lump sum receipt, but with the underlying risk and benefits of owning the property returned.

In substance, the transferor is simply borrowing money and paying it out of the rents. The scheme's claim is that the transferor reduces its taxable income by the amount of the rents that it diverts, but does not have to pay tax on the lump sum that it receives in return. The clause puts it beyond doubt that the lump sum is taxable as income, so the avoidance equivalent to trying to get the tax relief for loan repayments as well as interest will not work.

Commentators have accepted that the Government are right to act against such a clear attempt at tax avoidance. The legislation has been drafted to apply only to transactions that, in substance, replicate loan financing and are accounted for as such. Other transactions that are genuinely transfers of risk and rewards of property ownership are not affected. We have received no representations that the clause does not target exactly what it is supposed to target. It is unacceptable that a person who in substance borrows money should claim that tax relief on amounts far in excess of the actual cost of borrowing. The clause as targeted applies only to schemes entered into after Budget day.

There have been suggestions that a game of cat and mouse is taking place between tax planners and the Revenue, which is expected to continue in the future. I want to make it clear, on the record, that the Government intend the clause to stop the abuse. We hope that our action, and the warning that I am about to give, is sufficient to achieve that. This is the warning: we will not hesitate to act against any equivalent schemes that are designed to obtain a tax reduction that is not justified by the true substance of the transaction. If we have to legislate to deter new schemes, and deal effectively with schemes in existence at the relevant time, we will. The Government are committed to ending the abuse in the tax system, and the clause is necessary and appropriate action. It puts it beyond doubt that rent factoring schemes cannot be used to avoid tax.

In June 1997, the Inland Revenue announced in its tax bulletin that the view of tax law taken by those entering into rent factoring schemes could be susceptible to challenge. The Revenue continues to process challenges that it believes to be incorrect in the view of the law. For some years, that has worked, but we have started to see renewed interest in such schemes. We are acting in the hope that we can put it beyond doubt that rent factoring schemes should not be used. Through specific targeting, we have kept the measure as narrow as possible. If it goes beyond that limit, we will have to return to the matter.

I hope that that makes it clear to those who read the record of this debate that the Government are determined to act in this respect.

Clause 110

Payments under deduction of tax

Dawn Primarolo: I beg to move amendment No. 368, in page 86, line 43, leave out ``payments of interest'' and insert

    `declarations under section 481(5)(k)(i) of the Taxes Act 1988'.

It would be helpful for me to place on record issues that are of concern, possibly to the Opposition, but certainly to a wider audience in the Committee. First, the clause is part of a package of measures to promote the UK as a competitive environment for business while improving the effectiveness of exchange of information agreements to help prevent individuals and companies from evading or avoiding tax.

The clause has three effects, which I shall detail briefly before turning to the substance to the amendment. First, from April 2001 it abolishes the pay and collecting agent schemes, under which UK paying and collecting agents currently deduct income tax when they issue interest on quoted UK eurobonds or foreign dividends and interest. Secondly, it ensures that in future all interst on quoted eurobonds will be paid free of withholding tax. Thirdly, it introduces a minor modification to the tax deductions scheme for interest under which building societies banks and other deposit takers deduct income tax from interest payments made to individuals-which is the subject of the amendment.

I turn now to the amendment. The Inland Revenue has been consulting City representatives about the detailed implementation of the deduction of tax measures in the clause and the new information powers, to which we shall come later, in clause 140. During the discussions, strong representations were made for transitional arrangements to be put in place for the proposed modification to the tax deduction scheme for interest arrangements. The purpose of the transitional arrangements is to allow payments of interest made gross to existing ``not ordinarily resident''-NOR-investors to continue to be paid without deduction of tax when the new NOR reporting arrangements come into effect on 6 April 2001.

Under the current arrangements, a bank or building society can pay interest without deducting tax to an individual who is not ordinarily resident in the UK if he or she holds a valid NOR declaration. If that declaration does not, for some reason, contain the address of the person signing it, the deposit taker may complete a deposit taker's certificate, as long as he or she has no reason to believe that the investor is ordinarily resident in the UK. Completion of a deposit taker's certificate will validate the NOR declaration and allow interest on the account to be paid gross. Where a valid NOR declaration is in place, the investor can request the bank or building society to omit from its annual information to the Inland Revenue details of the interest paid on the account.

From 6 April 2001, new information reporting arrangements introduced by clause 140 will come into effect. Banks and building societies will be required to supply information about all interest payments to individual investors. That will include payments made without deduction of tax to NOR investors. To ensure that banks and building societies have the information that we need, the names and addresses of account holders will need to be shown on NOR declarations. Those details could be included in the annual information returns that banks and building societies make to the Inland Revenue. That will put the Inland Revenue in a position to exchange information if we have appropriate arrangements with other tax authorities. That will help to prevent individuals from evading or avoiding tax.

Clause 110 as drafted would require banks and building societies to ensure that fully completed NOR declarations were held for all their existing NOR investors by April 2001. Financial institutions have said that they can see no practical way of achieving that. They would have to review all their NOR accounts to identify those that were validated by a deposit taker's certificate. In a significant number of cases, they would have to obtain new NOR declarations that contained the address of the person who signs.

Amendment No. 368 will meet those concerns by ensuring that existing deposit taker's certificates will continue to validate NOR declarations made before 6 April 2001. As a result, the requirement to obtain the investor's address will apply only to new accounts that are opened on or after 6 April 2001. Furthermore, we have discussed with City representatives how, in the context of the Bill's proposals, we should obtain the information that was needed for the appropriate exchange of information.

Following helpful discussions, we agreed with City representatives that the requirement for beneficial ownership details from April 2001 should be limited to new NOR bank and building society accounts, for which new NOR declarations are made on or after 6 April 2001, and to NOR investors in unit trusts and open-ended investment companies. To ensure that banks and building societies have that information to pass to the Inland Revenue, the City representatives agreed that all new NOR declarations that are made on or after 6 April 2001 must show the name and address of the person beneficially entitled to the interest on the account. We shall table the necessary amendments on Report.
