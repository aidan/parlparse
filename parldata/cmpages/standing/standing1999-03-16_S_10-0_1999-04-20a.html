As the hon. Gentleman will recognise, it is always difficult, as in many asylum matters, to distinguish people who are genuine from people who are not. Amendment No. 136 would require the Secretary of State to take account of the means of the person concerned in setting the amount of bond. Amendments Nos. 148 and 149 would prescribe the maximum level at which financial securities may be set, namely no more than 10 per cent. of the sponsor's or applicant's declared income.

The amendments are not acceptable for a number of reasons. First, the Bill is not the proper place for setting out detailed provisions on the amount of a financial security. That will be done in the immigration rules, for which provision is made in clause 7(6).

Secondly, amendment No. 136 is unnecessary, as the clause does not prevent consideration of the means of the person concerned in setting the level of the security. That is subject to the overall ceiling in the immigration rules, as envisaged in subsection 6(a). There are, indeed, arguments for the means of the person concerned to be considered. For example, security of a few pounds would clearly be inappropriate in the case of a multi-millionaire.

Thirdly, amendments Nos. 148 and 149 are incomplete. Do they refer to gross or net income? What are the criteria for deciding whether a security is set at the maximum amount or a lesser one? What supporting evidence will be required, and how will it be assessed? How will disputes be resolved? The amendments do not address those serious issues.

The principle underlying amendments Nos. 148 and 149 is that of a mechanical matching of the maximum level of a financial security to an assessment of an individual's ability to pay. We are not persuaded that that is necessarily the right approach.

I understand concerns that any financial security under our scheme must be affordable. It would certainly not serve our purpose to set security so high that a substantial number of those who might benefit would effectively be excluded. Equally, it is right to take account of means, otherwise the security would not serve its purpose. Small sums of money deposited would be unlikely to be substantial enough to make their prospective loss an effective deterrent against abusing a bond system simply to secure entry to the United Kingdom. Low deposits would also be unlikely to reflect the substantial cost to the taxpayer of enforcing the departure of those who overstay their leave.

The figure of 10 per cent. proposed in Liberal Democrat amendment would merely be a fee which would allow a breach of immigration controls. There must be a proper penalty which would make someone think seriously before breaching it.

Fourthly, the mechanical matching of the maximum level of the bond to the sponsor's or visitor's ability to pay, as proposed in amendments Nos. 148 and 149, does not work. Such matching would detailed evidence of the sponsor's or visitor's ability to pay. It would need to be assessed and verified by the visa officer through a means test, would offer scope for disagreement and dispute about the right level of bond in individual case and would generally lengthen and complicate the entire process, making it more expensive to run. If a bond scheme is to work, it must be quick and simple to understand and its administrative costs should not be too high for applicants.

We do not intend to take decisions about the maximum level of financial security or how it will be calculated in individual cases until we take account of views expressed in the consultation exercise that will take place later this year. We will then set out in the immigration rules the arrangements on which next year's pilot scheme will be based. Should we feel that there is a need to change those arrangement in light of the experience of the pilot scheme, the immigration rules will give us the flexibility to do so swiftly.

Mr. O'Brien: I shall deal first with amendment No. 83. The United Kingdom has international obligations under the 1951 UN convention and the European convention on human rights. In line with those obligations, where a visitor who was admitted to the UK after the provision of a financial security is subsequently recognised as a refugee here or is recognised as having a right under the ECHR not to be removed, it is proposed that no security should be realised and any security previously deposited or realised will be repaid. Where a visitor is admitted after provision of a financial security and subsequently applies for and is granted leave in another capacity, including exceptional leave to remain, that would nevertheless break the condition of the agreement that the applicant would visit the UK and leave the country. In certain circumstances, clause 8 will be used to continue security where further limited leave is granted. However, generally it will be appropriate to realise the security in such cases.

Although a number of the bond scheme's elements are subject to consultation, if it is to be a success, the terms of the provision are best kept simple and clear. The essence is that the applicant should visit the United Kingdom and leave on or before the expiry of his allotted leave, as agreed in the original undertaking. The bond is a contract which effectively states, "We will let you visit, but you have to leave because you promised Britain that you will go home."

The implication of the amendment is that the bond scheme for visitors will be used as a stepping-stone for another status under immigration rules. Any such impression would damage, perhaps fatally, the purpose, which is to facilitate entry of people wishing only to visit the UK.

On amendment No. 88, it would be undesirable to provide an enhanced sponsorship scheme for sponsors with refugee status or those granted exceptional leave to remain, although my fear is not so much that that would happen, but that the proposal misunderstands entirely the basis on which the bond scheme is presented. There seems to be a fear among the people who suggested the amendment to the hon. Member for Hertsmere that the bond scheme will be used to restrict access, whereas its purpose is to provide the extra reassurance that an entry clearance officer may require in circumstances where he might otherwise not be prepared to grant a visa. So, if I accepted the hon. Gentleman's amendment, far from being of benefit to a refugee, it would act to his detriment in that it is possible that a security might be used to enhance the likelihood of obtaining a visa.

It is important that hon. Members understand how the provision is intended to operate; I fear that it is not understood by some of the organisations that prompted the amendments. What will happen is that the entry clearance officer will look at an application for a visa and consider whether he is disposed to grant it. In some cases it is clear that he should not grant a visa, so he will not. At other times it will be clear that he should grant a visa, and, as now, it will be granted. There should be no circumstances in which a visa now being granted ceases to be granted or be made conditional upon the provision of a financial security.

The other category is that group of people about whom an ECO is a little doubtful in that he does not accept that they have shown that they are likely to leave the UK, but none the less feels that they have a genuine reason for the visit, and that his doubt can be assuaged by the provision of a financial security. We do not anticipate there being a great number of people in that category. The provision will enable an ECO to enable people in that marginal category, from whom a little more reassurance is required, to visit provided there is a bond or security which gives ECO the assurance that he needs to grant the visa.

If the amendment were accepted, refugees would be barred from using the option of that security to enable members of their family to visit. So, far from being detrimental to refugees, the bond system might be helpful in allowing their entry. I accept the point from the hon. Member for Hertsmere that some refugees may have financial difficulties, but the alternative to having a bond is essentially that a family member does not visit. That has to be understood. We are giving the ECO that extra reassurance that effective immigration controls will be maintained.

We are now discussing section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996. Clause 13 requires the Secretary to introduce a code of practice for employers on the measures that they may be expected to take to avoid unlawful discrimination.

Committee members may recall that section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996 made it an offence for an employer to employ people--including illegal entrants--who were in breach of immigration law. Section 8 of that Act also provided a defence for employers who had been shown proper documentation in advance.

Amendment No. 169 would remove the duty on the Secretary of State to introduce a code of practice for employers, and instead would give the Secretary of State a power to introduce such a code. That reflects our doubts about clause 13, the inclusion of which in the Bill comes as a surprise to those who listened to what the Labour party said about section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996.

We are not arguing for repeal of that measure. However, those who have heard what the Labour party said in the past may have expected such a repeal. Let us be clear. It is as plain as a pikestaff that, in opposition, the Labour party argued for the repeal of section 8 of the 1996 Act. It did not say that that the provision would be left in place with a code of practice tacked on, but that it would repeal it.

We are therefore dubious about the course of action that the Labour party has taken. The Government are obviously concerned about alleged illegal discrimination arising as a result of the matter. However, on 29 March I asked a written question about what research the Government had commissioned and evaluated into such discrimination. It turned out that they had not commissioned any such research and were relying on representations that they had received from others.

The Government told me that their proposals appeared in the White Paper "Fairer, Faster and Firmer--A Modern Approach to Immigration and Asylum" which was issued last July. I was told that comments from business organisations and others were invited at the time of publication. In a written answer, the Minister said:

    "No business organisations have been directly consulted, but consultations on the proposal have taken place with the Commission for Racial Equality, the Trades Union Congress and other interested organisations."--[Official Report, 29 March 1999; Vol. 328, c. 550.]

I do not know what the Trades Union Congress said. However, representations from the Commission for Racial Equality do not bolster the Government's case. To be fair, the Commission said that there have been cases of unlawful discrimination under the 1996 Act. However, it is not satisfied with the Government's proposed course of action. Its submission to the Committee states:

    "the Commission is not persuaded that translating guidance into a statutory Code of Practice, as proposed, will significantly affect discriminatory recruitment practices adopted to avoid conviction under Section 8."--[Official Report, Special Standing Committee, Other Memoranda and Letters Submitted to the Committee; c. 386.]

The Commission sets out in detail the reasons for taking that view and why a code of practice is inadequate. I shall not go into detail about that.

When Labour was in opposition, Labour Members did not confine their criticisms to section 8 of the Asylum and Immigration Act 1996 or to alleging unlawful discrimination. They criticised the measure for placing a burden on businesses--that may appear to be a new Labour criticism. I have a copy of a letter that the Financial Secretary to the Treasury wrote about section 8 when she was Opposition spokesman for small businesses. She sent the letter to The Daily Telegraph--a very new Labour thing to do. The letter stated:

    "Yet once again it is British small firms--who don't have extensive personnel and legal departments--who will be hardest hit . . . Labour believes the Asylum Bill adds to burdens on small firms and turns them into unpaid immigration officers. We opposed the measures during the passage of the Bill through Parliament and Jack Straw has made it clear that we would not operate them in government.

I do not know how many votes that pledge won from the readers of The Daily Telegraph. Anyone who voted Labour on the strength of it will be disappointed by the Bill.

Instead of removing burdensome regulations from small business proprietors, the Bill heaps more regulations on them through the code of practice. Businesses will not only be expected to avoid unlawful discrimination, they will also have to comply with the code of practice. They will have to do that as well as ensuring that they do not employ illegal entrants, for which section 8 of the 1996 Act provides.

It is interesting to note how much store the Government have set by business in the consultations that have taken place so far The Government announced the measure in their White Paper, "Fairer, Firmer and Faster", without any consultation with business. We are told that comments from businesses, organisations and others were invited when the White Paper was published. In a Parliamentary answer, the Minister said:

    "No business organisations have been directly consulted since then but consultations on the proposal have taken place with the Commission for Racial Equality, (CRE) the Trades Union Congress and other interested organisations."--[Official Report, 29 March 1999; Vol. 328, c. 550.]

So much for listening to business, large or small.

We are debating a code of practice that we have not seen. Once again, we are in the dark. The Government propose to hold consultations after the Bill is passed, to draw up the code of practice and introduce it through negative resolution. Doubtless, they wish to avoid debate on the code--it must be an embarrassing subject for them.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: We shall have the opportunity to debate the wider issues relating to section 8 of the 1996 Act in the stand part debate. I shall direct my comments to the amendment.

Our proposal for a statutory code of practice is intended to re-emphasise to employers their duty to avoid racial discrimination in recruitment practices. The present guidance, though useful for the generality of employers, is not perceived as being sufficiently effective, especially as some employers may be making more checks than required by legislation or making checks on individuals that are not applied to other individuals--that is, discriminating between persons, which is clearly contrary to race relations legislation. Hence the proposal for a statutory code, which will give added force to our efforts to guard against discriminatory practices.

The provision places the Secretary of State under a statutory duty to issue a code of practice. The Government place great importance on avoiding all forms of racist discrimination. We would not wish to detract from that commitment by removing the statutory duty to issue further guidance to employers. It follows that we cannot support the watering down of the proposal to guard against racial discrimination that is proposed in the amendment.

It is regrettable that Conservative Members should propose such an amendment, because it would undermine the strength of our commitment to racial equality, which I had hoped they would share. It is important that the Government should ensure that discrimination is tackled. We have looked at how the provisions of section 8 of the 1996 Act operate, and we clearly need to strengthen the current guidance, which is what we now propose.

If we are serious about tackling racial discrimination, we must do so, not "may" do so. Making it optional, as the Opposition propose, is not to be serious about dealing with it. We clearly have a growing problem, with racketeers breaching section 8 and employers employing persons unlawfully. Those problems must be addressed. However, we must also be serious about ensuring that the way in which those problems are addressed is not itself discriminatory. That is why we want to place a clear Parliamentary obligation on the Secretary of State do ensure that a code of practice is in place.

The present guidance is too weak. We want to be seen to be dealing with the difficulties that have resulted, but it seems that the Opposition do not wish to be serious about tackling the problems of racial discrimination.
