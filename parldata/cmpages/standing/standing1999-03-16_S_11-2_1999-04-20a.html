Mr. Mike O'Brien: I was dealing with specific points on the amendment raised by the hon. Member for Hertsmere, but I shall now deal with some of the broader issues.

It is not the Government's objective to fine anyone unnecessarily. I would prefer not to fine any lorry driver or carrier. Our objective is to prevent them from carrying clandestines and lorry drivers will not pay fines if they check their loads and do not carry illegal immigrants. They must accept responsibility for their cargoes.

Last year, about 8,000 illegal immigrants were brought into Britain in the back of lorries because lorry drivers failed to check their loads or because some of them accepted bribes. The going rate is about £2,000 for each illegal immigrant, so half a dozen of them stuffed in the back of a lorry would represent a lot of money. Some lorry drivers have certainly accepted bribes from traffickers and racketeers and carried illegal immigrants in the back of their lorries. We cannot turn a blind eye to that.

Other lorry drivers clearly fail to check their loads, and that constitutes negligence. Therefore, we must take a robust approach towards those who do not bother to check their loads or who accept bribes in order to get rich from trafficking in the often vulnerable people who seek to enter the United Kingdom.

One of the arguments of the hon. Member for Hertsmere in defence of his amendment was that some clandestines may be genuine refugees. About 17 per cent. of applicants, many of whom apply at port, are recognised as genuine refugees. By and large, clandestines are not recognised as genuine refugees. The British sometimes arrogantly believe that people come here because our human rights are better than those in other countries through which they pass, but under the 1951 convention, persons seeking refuge are obliged to do so in the first safe country to which they come. Britain is not generally the first country to which clandestines come. They may wish to come to Britain because they have relatives here, for example, and such reasons may form part of a later appeal for asylum, but the vast majority of people seeking asylum here will have their claim rejected. Many seek economic opportunity alone; they are undermining and abusing the asylum system. I emphasise that few clandestines are recognised as refugees and they should in any case have applied for asylum in the first safe country.

The argument that our proposals are a breach of human rights does not hold water. A human rights policy does not mean an open door for clandestines and illegal immigrants to enter the country in the back of lorries. We cannot have an open-door policy that allows people to come in via lorry drivers or to damage the cargo of reputable business people who want their goods freighted without damage. People travelling in the back of lorries often defecate in them or damage the goods in a way that destroys their value. That is happening much too often and we cannot allow it to continue on the basis that a few clandestines might turn out to be genuine refugees.

In the explanatory notes, the Government describe it as "a civil penalty", and the Minister may tell us that a safeguard of the criminal standard of proof is not appropriate in these circumstances. I am anticipating the Minister's reasoning. However, in the note provided to Justice by Ben Emmerson and Jessica Simor, doubt is cast on the Government's approach. Those lawyers consider that the penalties raise serious issues under article 6 of the European convention on human rights, the right to a fair trial, as well as under the first protocol, the right to peaceful enjoyment of property.

With regard to the amendment and the appropriateness of the criminal standard, Justice says that a penalty can relate to a criminal charge for European convention on human rights purposes even if it is defined as a civil penalty in domestic law. Intuitively that seems right, and it is good to see the legal experts confirm intuition. It surely does not matter what label the state chooses to pin to a penalty; it is the nature and character of the charge that matter. In the view of Justice, clause 18 is obviously designed to have a deterrent effect and to be punitive rather than to be pecuniary compensation for damage.

Justice would have been confirmed in that opinion if it had heard the Minister speak earlier this evening, because he said the same thing. He has not maintained for a moment that this was purely a form of civil compensation. He has told us time and again that it is intended to be a deterrent penalty.

Justice refers to the case of Ozturk v. Germany in support of its argument. It says that this case should be read in connection with section 25 of the 1971 Act, which, as the Committee will know by now, creates the criminal offence of intentionally assisting illegal entrants. The gist of Justice's argument, which hon. Members will find in the analysis of human rights compliance in the Justice memorandum, is that the provisions introducing a new form of carrier's liability in the clause are alternative charges in respect of the existing section 25 criminal offence. As Emmerson and Simor said, clauses 18 to 23 may be used to penalise individuals for conduct that also falls within the criminal sphere, thus frustrating the purpose of article 6 of the European convention on human rights.
