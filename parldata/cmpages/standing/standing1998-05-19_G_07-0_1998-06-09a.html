Competition Bill [Lords]

Clause 19

Abuse of dominant position by national newspaper

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

10.30 am

Mr. David Chidgey (Eastleigh): Good morning, Mr. Maxton.

This clause has caused considerable debate both in and outside Parliament. It deals with fair competition in the newspaper market and it is worth our reflecting on the importance of maintaining that. The market generates £700 million worth of revenue per year, but it is currently making an annual loss of £50 million. Many people believe that that is a direct result of the predatory pricing policy of The Times. Over the past five years, it has been argued that that policy has turned profitable quality daily newspapers into loss-making operations.

Mr. John Redwood (Wokingham): The hon. Gentleman makes an interesting point. Is he saying that the Liberal Democrat party believes that The Times is guilty of predatory pricing?

Mr. Chidgey: The specific answer is that that is the case that is being put. To answer the question of the right hon. Member for Wokingham (Mr. Redwood) more fully, the whole basis of my argument is to demonstrate the uncertainty that exists within that area of trade and the reason why the Bill needs to be precise to overcome it.

Mr. John Bercow (Buckingham): The hon. Gentleman referred to the uncertainty that exists. Although there may be uncertainty about outcomes and effects, will the hon. Gentleman confirm that it is his contention that The Times' intent is to engage in predatory pricing--in other words, that that is its motive?

Mr. Chidgey: It is my contention that the Bill does not provide a sufficient guarantee that such an intention can be restrained.

The pricing of The Times seems to vary greatly. For example, it retails on a Monday at 20p, while it costs 40p on Saturdays--

Mr. Redwood: It is 30p on a Monday.

Mr. Chidgey: It is now 30p. I shall continue on the theme of how the price varies. I know that the right hon. Member for Wokingham has a specific interest in the matter, but I want to continue with my arguement. The cost of The Times whether it be 20p on Monday or 30p on Monday, is still far below the production costs that most people in the industry believe to be the average.

Mr. Redwood: Again, the hon. Gentleman is making some interesting assertions. Will he tell us what it costs to produce The Times on a Monday and what costs on a Saturday? To make his case, he must know the answer to that.

Mr. Chidgey: That is not the case. The industry has a good idea of the average costs of newspaper production. The right hon. Gentleman must know that the cover-sheet cost charged by The Times is consistently below the costs of production.

Mr. Chidgey: I really must get on. I shall take interventions later when I have reached the substance of my argument. I want to say something before I answer questions. I appreciate the enthusiasm of members of the Committee, but perhaps they could temper it for a few minutes while I make some points, to which I hope the Minister will respond.

Under current legislation, it is unlawful for an enterprise to price its goods below cost only if it is the dominant undertaking in the market where the below-cost pricing occurs and if it is done to force a rival out of the market. Those conditions apparently do not apply to the national daily broadsheet market, which is why The Times has survived two recent Office of Fair Trading investigations.

The Competition Bill, particularly clause 19, offers a window of opportunity. The Bill presents the Government with the perfect chance to honour their pledge to tackle anti-competitive behaviour in the newspaper market. Clause 19 is necessary; without it, the Bill would not significantly change the test of predatory pricing. I know that the Minister will want to speak about that, but I want first to develop my theme.

Before clause 19 was added, the Bill would not have prevented a firm in the national daily broadsheet market with a substantial degree of market power from behaving anti-competitively. There is a precedent for having a more vigorous competition regime for newspapers in the Fair Trading Act 1973, which provides that the separate, tougher merger and acquisition rules for newspapers should be applied in other sectors.

The Office of Fair Trading is again looking at the behaviour of The Times and News International. Indeed, the director general is considering a case brought by The Independent, The Guardian and The Daily Telegraph. The fear is that the director general will be forced to reach the same conclusion as his predecessor did in 1993 and 1994, when it was found that there was no case to answer. The only comment that could be made then was that

    ``there is a fine line between aggressive price competition and predatory pricing.''

Without clause 19, the Bill would not provide a tight enough definition for the director general to reach any other conclusion. One of the reasons why previous complaints were rejected was that News International was said to have been engaging in a temporary promotional activity. That temporary activity has now been going on for five years.

We have already said that The Times seems to change its price from day to day. It is interesting, perhaps significant, that the price changes seem to occur just before Parliament debates the matter. I understand that the price of The Times was upped on the Monday before the amendment was moved in another place, and--surprise, surprise--it was upped again just before the Bill received its Second Reading in the House of Commons. That may be a coincidence; perhaps I am being cynical, but The Times can hardly be breaking even at a price of 30p a day. It must be losing money, especially as the industry reckons that the price on Saturday barely covers half the production costs. Without a change in the law, nothing prevents The Times--or any other newspaper--slashing its price as soon as Parliament takes the heat off it. The Bill may not be an ideal vehicle to deal with the problem, but it is the only legislative opportunity that we have. It is the only game in town.

We constantly hear that the consequences of the price war in the broadsheet newspaper market, which everyone seems to accept was instigated by News International, is that it threatens the diversity and plurality of opinion, not only in the quality newspaper market but, through repercussions, lower down.

Mr. Bercow: The hon. Gentleman makes an important assertion. He must either justify it or withdraw it. He spoke of a reduction in diversity and plurality having resulted from News International's behaviour. How does he square that assertion with the fact that there has been a 14 per cent. expansion of the broadsheet newspaper market since 1992?

Mr. Chidgey: I can satisfy the hon. Gentleman. As I was about to say, since the price war began five years ago there has already been one casualty in the mid-market sector--the Today newspaper. I am sure that there have been others. There has also been an effect on The Independent, in that it is no longer independently controlled. Hon. Members may recall that when The Independent was established, it was intended to be a completely independently owned newspaper, but predatory pricing has led to an acceleration of the reduction of choice in the newspaper industry. It was only when Mirror Group Newspapers Ltd., along with independent newspapers, took 46 per cent. of the shares of The Independent that it was saved from going out of business. I believe that it is now a healthy newspaper, but if that grouping had not saved the paper, it is unlikely that it would still be being produced.

Having set the scene, I shall not return to what I understand to be the Government's primary objections to clause 19. No doubt the Minister will be able to set those out in more detail, but I should like to refer to them now so that I can offer an alternative view. The Government appear to object to the clause on four grounds--although there may well be others.

The Government's view is that the Bill requires European jurisprudence to be followed and that, under European law, predatory pricing is illegal. The Government argue that the so-called Tetra Pak 2 judgment, which has been referred to many times in previous debates, extends article 86 of European law to apply to behaviour in a market other than one in which the defendant holds a dominant position. By implication, the Government claim that that would cover the anti-competitive behaviour of The Times in the newspaper market.

It has never been in doubt that European law outlaws predatory pricing, nor that the Tetra Pak judgment extends the scope of the law. However, the European Court of Justice held that the Tetra Pak judgment applied only in certain circumstances. Opinion has recently been sought from a leading competition expert in this country on whether the pricing policies of The Times would be unlawful under the Bill, in the light of the Tetra Pak judgment. Those extremely expert competition lawyers concluded that, in their opinion--in relation to the question of abuse, as well as that of dominance--the chapter II prohibition proposed by the Government will need modification if it is to be of assistance in dealing with newspaper pricing. The Government appear to have contrary advice.

At the very least, there is no settled legal opinion. Consequently, competition law needs to be clarified--if only in the form of a belt-and-braces clause, which clause 19 represents.

Mr. Andrew Lansley (South Cambridgeshire): If a competition lawyer has offered an opinion such as the hon. Gentleman suggests, that competition lawyer must have had an opportunity to assess whether The Times--presumably including its advertising returns--is pricing itself below average variable cost, or above average variable cost but below total average cost. Can the hon. Gentleman tell us at what point The Times is pricing itself?

 

Mr. Chidgey: Can anyone offer such an opinion? It is well understood that as the financial accounting of that group is less than transparent, and consists of many holdings in many countries, it is not easy to provide a specific numerical answer on the exact costs. However, I believe that certain assumptions, based on the industry's own trends and figures, are valid in forming that opinion. As I am sure that the hon. Member for South Cambridgeshire (Mr. Lansley) will know, leading lawyers and Queen's counsels do not come to such opinions lightly, and take such factors well into account before giving advice.

The Government's second claim is that clause 19 is unworkable. They say that the clause is drafted too widely and would make entrepreneurial behaviour such as special promotions unlawful. In particular, they argue that subsection (2)(b), which covers

    ``other action which may reduce the diversity and independence of the national newspaper press''

contains a catch-all phrase.

I do not pretend to be a lawyer of any rank, whether expert or otherwise. However, legal advice, supported by the noble Lord Borrie, a former Director General of the Office of Fair Trading, is that recognised legal doctrine requires subsection (2)(b) to be taken in the context of the other actions in the list of which it forms a part, and not in isolation. That allows the competition authorities the discretion to decide what behaviour would be unlawful. Therefore, legitimate marketing initiatives would not be caught by subsection (2)(b), but anti-competitive practices that reduce the diversity and independence of the press would be caught.

The Government have argued that it is not our responsibility to deal with such matters and that they are the responsibility of the OFT, or whatever. In opposition, Labour Members, including the Ministers responsible for taking the Bill through Committee, were--in all good faith and quite sincerely, I am sure--vociferous in their support for the then Government's action against anti-competitive behaviour in the newspaper industry. I strongly support that. It is a legitimate role of Parliament to take a view on what sort of action should be deemed unlawful.

One of the Government's major objections to clause 19 is that it is too specific. The Government have argued that competition law should apply to industry as a whole, and not to specific sectors. They argue that the clause would make the newspaper industry a special case, and would introduce a separate competition regime for that industry. Existing competition law, such as the Fair Trading Act 1973, which sets out tougher merger and acquisition rules for newspapers than for other sectors, acknowledges that newspapers are a special case. In her consultation document, which preceded the Bill, the President of the Board of Trade said:

    ``We do not propose to use the Bill to amend the FTA newspaper merger provisions. The original rationale for these provisions was that increasing concentration of newspaper ownership could threaten freedom and variety of expression of opinion in the press. We consider that this rationale remains valid.''

I could not agree more.

Mr. Redwood: The hon. Gentleman is being courteous. It might help the Committee if he could explain another issue. Subsection (2)(c) states that it would be an abuse if the newspaper were

    ``imposing selling prices below marginal cost of production on a presistent basis''.

A newspaper has two main sources of revenue--advertising and cover prices. Why is no allowance made for advertising revenue? Freesheets have a selling price of zero, so they are clearly sold well below the marginal cost of production, but might none the less be profitable. It is not a problem that the clause does not recognise advertising revenue?

Mr. Chidgey: The right hon. Gentleman makes an interesting intervention. I think that he is aware of my view on the matter. It has never been argued that clause 19 is the panacea to stamp out predatory pricing. We are arguing that it is the only game in town. If the Bill does not contain such a provision--which may be improved--we will not have the necessary belt and braces and will be missing our only opportunity to try to control such anti-competitive behaviour.

I shall now summarise the case for clause 19. On Second Reading, the Government claimed that the clause was unnecessary because European jurisprudence would have to be followed in line with clause 60. That requirement has never been in doubt. The real point is whether the relevant European case law--the Tetra Pak judgment--extends far enough to cover the type of anti-competitive abuse that we have witnessed in the daily broadsheet market; only then could we be sure that clause 19 is unnecessary.

As I have said, in the view of some experts in competition law the Tetra Pak judgment does not cover the broadsheet issue. In summary, the advice is based on the assumption that The Times can sell below cost only because it is supported by other activities--The Sunday Times, BSkyB, or whatever. It is not clear where that subsidy comes from because we do not have enough information on the accounting procedures operated by News International.

I understand that the Government's legal advice has been contrary to the expert advice that I have mentioned. Apart from asserting that the Government have taken such advice, they have not revealed its details--let alone published them. I understand that Ministers do not reveal confidential information, but the issue is important and is causing much concern in Parliament and elsewhere.

If the Minister is able to tell us today, I should like him to say whether the Government have asked a specific question: would the pricing policies of The Times be unlawful under the Bill given the Tetra Pak judgment? Given that the Government have not been able, or have refused, to answer that specific question, we must assume that is it unlikely that they have asked it. However, we must know the answer because, dependent on it, there might be one of three scenarios.

First, if the question has not been asked, it is hard to understand how the Government can expect Parliament, or even their own Back Benchers, to be reassured that the Bill covers such abuse. Therefore, the law must be amended, and clause 19 is the only option available. Secondly, if the question has been asked and the answer is that the Bill does not cover such abuse, clearly the clause is necessary, as both sets of legal advice--the Government's and the other experts--reach the same conclusion. Thirdly, if the question has been asked and the answer is that the Bill covers such abuse, the clause is still necessary because there is conflicting legal advice on the matter. That means that the only people who can gain from such uncertainty will be organisations such as News International and their lawyers.

Those are serious questions about the possible failure of competition law to control anti-competitive excesses, especially in the broadsheet market. The Government would be doing a great service if they placed the legal advice that they have obtained in the Library. That would clearly show whether such activities would be outlawed under the Bill. If the Government do not publish that advice, obviously the pressure on them will grow to accept a specific amendment containing similar provisions to those in clause 19. The Government claim that the clause is unworkable. If that is so, hon. Members from both sides of the Committee will be happy to assist in formulating a workable amendment along similar lines.

The reason I revisited ``Dangerous Estate'' for the purposes of this debate is because of the events relating to The Times between the years 1836 and 1856. The self-regulatory system restricts the ability of advertisers to say what they want in their advertising, through both the codes and the implementation of ASA decisions by CAP members. To that extent, the system acts to stop, restrict or distort competition.

However, the need for such a system in the absence of, and in preference to, full statutory control has not been challenged seriously in public by the advertising industry or consumer bodies. Broadly speaking, the system was accepted and blessed by the Director General of Fair Trading after an exhaustive inquiry in 1978 and it has been accepted and praised by Governments ever since, including the present Government and their predecessor. The decisions of the ASA are subject to judicial review by the courts.

Particulars of the codes have been furnished to the Director General of Fair Trading as required by the Restrictive Trade Practices Act 1976. Particulars of the underlying arrangements made by individual members to ensure compliance with the codes have also been furnished, although that is being checked. I believe that in most, if not all, cases directions have been given under section 21(2) of the Act.

The relatively secure legal position will be undermined in several ways when the Bill is enacted. Although the CAP has no reason to doubt that the OFT will continue to take a favourable view of the position under chapters I and II prohibitions, it is worried that parties who are aggrieved at the way in which the codes impact on their activities will, in future, be able to take legal proceedings against it for both injunctive relief and damages for breach of statutory duty.

At present, the activities of the ASA are capable of being reviewed judicially but, of course, judicial proceedings can be brought only with the leave of the court. No such filtering process will exist under the Competition Bill. Even the threat of litigation may have a significant chilling effect on the ability of the self-regulatory system properly to control advertising to the detriment of consumers and responsible advertisers.

The fact that section 21(2) directions have been given means that chapter I prohibition will not apply to the CAP's activities. However, that will be true only until there is a ``material variation'' to the arrangements. It is not clear to the CAP exactly what is meant by that phrase. In any event, it is both inevitable and desirable that the codes and their impact should be reviewed regularly and adapted, as necessary, to meet changing circumstances. It is likely therefore that, within a relatively short time after the Bill is in force, a material variation will occur and the exclusion in paragraph 2 of schedule 3 will no longer apply.

When that happens, it will be necessary for the CAP to apply for an individual exemption. Unless that is widely drawn, it is likely that the CAP will need to revert to the OFT each time that there is a variation to the codes or to its membership. In any event, an exemption can be granted only for a finite period. The net result will be an enormous administrative burden for the CAP, its members and the OFT. The chapter II prohibition will apply potentially to their activities as the Bill comes into force. Although they will be able to seek a decision from the Director General of Fair Trading that it does not apply, they will remain vulnerable to third party action.

I appreciate that the OFT has said that it does not expect to take many formal decisions and that the majority of cases will be settled by means of an administrative letter. Even if the CAP were to apply for a decision granting an individual exemption from the chapter I prohibition and a decision that the chapter II prohibition did not apply, it is not certain that the OFT will formally grant such decisions and that it will achieve the legal security that it needs to carry out its functions effectively.

The ASA is likely to be--the CAP may be--a public authority under the Human Rights Bill. Both may be exposed to judicial review in respect of any action infringing an advertiser's freedom of expression, which includes potentially all the ASA rulings that uphold complaints about advertisements. The advertiser will have the right, as under the Competition Bill, to claim damages. There is no requirement to show bad faith as is presently the case for a damages claim in judicial review proceedings.
