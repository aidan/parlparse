Mr. Clappison: As the Minister has been fond of saying, an anti-social order is a civil order. However, it is a criminal offence to breach it. Whether others regard such orders in the same way as the Government may be contested in future, but at present we must consider the penalty for breach. That is the question considered in the amendment.

The maximum sentence for breach of an anti-social behaviour order ranks alongside the maximum for some serious offences. At five years, it is more than the three years' maximum for affray, for example, and is the same maximum as is available for offences of violent disorder. I remind the Committee that, in the eyes of the law, offences of violent disorder occur when three or more persons use or threaten unlawful violence and whose conduct is such as to cause a person of reasonable firmness present at the scene to fear for his personal safety.

Five years is also the maximum sentence for offences of assualt occasioning actual bodily harm, or even of grievous bodily harm. Those are serious offences, in which people are made badly afraid or suffer real injury. Yet a person subject to an anti-social behaviour order would, in theory, lay himself open to the same sentence through any action that puts him in breach of the order. That need not be the same behaviour that cause harassment, alarm and distress, and which occasioned the making of the order in the first place. We have already discussed and firmly established that it need not be criminal behaviour, although a person could, of course, also be charged for any criminal behaviour that he committed.

The behaviour might involve the breach of an order, such as an exclusion order. I am sure that members of the Committee can think of many circumstances in which the mere breach of an order might take place. Someone might, for example, merely set foot inside an exclusion zone and thus render himself liable to five years' imprisonment--the same penalty as for the serious criminal offences that I mentioned.

It is worth reconsidering the issue. Amendment No. 15 attempts to make the sentence for breaching an order a little more proportionate by introducing a distinction between breach and aggravated breach. According to the amendment, someone who breaches an order by doing something that it prohibits him from doing would be liable to six months' imprisonment. That is not an unreasonable maximum for just breaching an order. Someone who breaches an order by acting in a manner that causes or is likely to cause harassment, alarm or distress would, however, be guilty of aggravated breach. In that case, we would follow the Government in imposing a maximum sentence of five years' imprisonment. In that way, we recognise the distinction between breaching an anti-social behaviour order and breaching an order by behaving in the way that was complained about in the first place and which gave rise to the order. That is an entirely reasonable distinction, and the penalty for a non-aggravated breach is in line with the penalties for breach of non-molestation injunctions or other injunctions under civil law.

Last week, the hon. Member for Wellingborough (Mr. Stinchcombe) spoke with some authority about the procedures for granting and enforcing civil injunctions. He might feel moved to tell us in the course of this debate whether he or any other member of the Committee knows of a civil injunction being enforced by anything approaching five years' imprisonment. In my limited experience of the civil courts, I have not come across such consequences flowing from the breach of an injunction. As I have already said, a person could be sentenced for any criminal behaviour that occurred when a breach took place.

I want to make one other point in favour of the amendment. Conservative Members want to help the Government avoid a successful challenge against anti-social behaviour orders under the European convention on human rights. We have outlined some of the problems that might arise in future and there could be a problem with the penalty. The fact that the breach of what is described as a civil order will give rise to such a heavy criminal penalty makes the order look far more like a criminal penalty than a civil order. The look of the order is important.

I shall briefly leave the head note, which concerns an appeal against the sentence to the Court of Appeal. This is what the Court of Appeal said:

    ``The question of whether or not [these offences] met the criteria laid down in section 1(2)(b) was, however, a different matter.

Section 1(2)(b) applies when the offence is a violent or sexual offence, which these clearly were, that only such a sentence would be adequate to protect the public from serious harm from the offender. The trial judge also made clear that he had in mind section 2(2)(b) of the Criminal Justice Act 1991 which is in the following terms:

    `(2) The custodial sentence shall be:--

    (b) where the offence is a violent or sexual one, for such longer term (not exceeding that maximum) as in the opinion of the court is necessary to protect the public from serious harm from the offender.'

The trial judge therefore had to consider whether or not, if he was going to impose a sentence which was longer than that which would normally be justified for the offence on its intrinsic merits, such a sentence was necessary to protect the public from serious harm from the offender. It was firstly necessary therefore to consider whether or not the term imposed by the trail judge of five years was longer than that which was justified on the intrinsic merits of the offence.

We have no doubt that the sentence imposed fell above the level that was merited intrinsically by the offence on each occasion, even taking into acount as the trial judge clearly did, and was entitled to take into account, under section 3(3) of the Act;

    `Any aggravating and mitigating factors in relation to the circumstances of the offence.'

The aggravating factors in that context, of course, were the pattern of previous offending, the repeating nature of this offending in relation to this one boy. Did it, however, fall within the criteria required under section 2(2)(b)? In our judgment it did not.
