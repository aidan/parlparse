Clause 40

Restriction on evidence or questions about complainant's sexual history

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 58, in page 29, leave out line 2.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss the following amendments: No. 59, in page 29, leave out lines 3 to 5 and insert--

    `(b) that it would be unfair to the accused to refuse to allow the evidence to be adduced or the question to be asked'.

No. 60, in page 29, line 17, at end insert--

    `or--

    (c) it is an issue of consent and the court considers it to be in the interests of justice to allow the evidence to be adduced or the questions to be asked.'.

Mr. Greenway: We now come to an extremely important part of the Bill; indeed, it would not be unfair to suggest that it was the most important and far-reaching, certainly in terms of its effect on victims and defendants in rape cases.

On Tuesday, the Committee agreed--there was a fair amount of consenus--that it could no longer be right under any circumstances for a defendant in a rape trial to ask the victim questions directly in open court.

This afternoon, we are reconsidering and redefining what sort of questions the representative or lawyer of the accused should be permitted to put to the victim in rape cases. We need not detain the Committee long in considering whether there is a consensus in favour of changing the law in that regard. Many in the legal profession undoubtedly believe that a change is not justified. One has only to study the debates that took place in Committee in the other place--particularly that on 8 February--to determine that many leading practitioners are not convinced that a change is justified. They include, of course, the Lord Chief Justice, Lord Bingham of Cornhill, and, to a fair extent, Baroness Mallalieu, who has long experience of prosecuting and defending in rape trials.

There is, however, a general sense among those of us who are not legal practitioners--this relates to the point that the Minister of State made when I lightheartedly asked whether not being a lawyer was a good or a bad thing in terms of dealing with the Bill--that aspects of rape victims' personal lives and sexual histories have been trawled through the courts on too many occasions. That has been done in a way that shocks us all and which we find wholly deplorable.

I want, therefore, to stress the seriousness and importance of the provisions. The first key question that we must answer as we approach clauses 40 to 43 is: does the law need changing? On balance, I am satisfied that it does. I hope that that will help the Minister when he reflects on our other questions and concerns.

Those who feel that the law does not need changing cite section 2(2) of the Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 1976, which provides courts with the discretion to decide which questions about a victim's personal sexual history to permit and which are relevant. Nonetheless, I agree with the Minister--I am sure that he will say this shortly--that the discretion in that legislation, which is 23 years old, has failed to protect many rape victims. Given some of the horrendous experiences to which women have been subjected in the courts--which, on any sensible balance, must be regarded as questioning wholly irrelevant to the case--we must be determined to do more to protect such women from such ordeals, as we did in clauses 33 and 34.

I shall cite one example, to which I referred on the Floor of the House at the time. Although the case related to problems with the rules of disclosure, the reason for those problems related to the law in the regard that we are currently considering. The Minister of State may be aware of the case; it was one of those cited by the Metropolitan police during the mid-1990s to demonstrate the need for change to the rules of disclosure of facts to the defence by the prosecution. A girl of 18 was gang-raped in a car park in west London. The five accused effectively escaped justice because the poor girl had suffered an identical ordeal a year earlier, and it was clear that that would be relevant to the defence--more to the point, it was clear that the defence would seek to exploit that fact. The Crown Prosecution Service rightly concluded that it would be entirely unreasonable for the girl to suffer that ordeal, so the case was discontinued.

If a girl who had been grabbed off the street and subjected to a terrifying ordeal of rape at the hands of several men was so unfortunate as to have that experience happen to her again, most of us would ask what on earth was the relevance of the first experience in considering the guilt of the accused on the second occasion.

I am sure that the combination of all those provisions will assist in such cases.

We could double the girl's age. She might he 20 and at the age of 12 or 13 have suffered sexual abuse at the hands of a relative--a parent, a brother, an uncle--or someone living in the house. All kinds of experiences come to mind. I find it difficult to see in what sense questioning someone, of whatever age, about horrific abuse against them on a previous occasion can be in any way relevant to the defence in such cases.

Ms Margaret Moran (Luton, South): A victim of repeated domestic violence and abuse in my constituency had a similar experience. Having had a stranger rape her two years previously, the evidence from that rape was used in evidence as a reason to dismiss the case of domestic violence. The Luton police said at the time that they suspected that the defence used her previous sexual history as

    ``a tactical course of action to keep the victim from giving evidence.''

Does the hon. Gentleman agree that that is a good example of why we should ensure that special measures apply to victims of domestic violence, so that they too are protected in such circumstances?

Mr. Greenway: I feel almost like the Minister having to defend the Bill. We considered that issue on Tuesday in respect of special measures. I remember arguing that domestic violence and the threat of intimidation from a person who had abused the victim by acts of violence would be a major reason for the court to agree that special measures for the giving of the witness's evidence would be appropriate. That is slightly different from the matter that we are discussing now. It is clear from the interventions that I have already taken that there is consensus across the Committee that the present situation is unsatisfactory. I am, on balance, satisfied that the answer to the question ``Does the law need changing?'' is that it does.

Having settled that the law should be changed, we come to another important question. Once the law has been changed, how far should the courts have discretion, or how far should Parliament lay out a structure that seeks to fetter that discretion? Given the experiences that have been described, in which the current law has not afforded protection in every case, how far should we trust the court to use such discretion as it has at its disposal to allow questioning of a victim about previous sexual history?

Assuming that discretion is to be narrowed, one then moves on to the question of what the test should be. What is the prime objective of allowing discretion at all? That is crucial in determining whether the Bill is adequate in meeting the requirements.

At this juncture, I should say that amendment No. 58 is a probing amendment, whereas amendment No. 59 has more substance in that it suggests an alternative wording to that of the Bill. It is a question of whether the discretion that the Bill provides is too narrowly drawn, sufficiently widely drawn or, to put it another way, meets the requirements of what we are seeking to do by providing the courts with any discretion at all.

It is fair to say that amendments Nos. 58 and 59 largely reflect the concerns that were expressed in the debate in Committee in the other place on 8 February. I have already mentioned Baroness Mallalieu, whose comments in that debate deserve substantial consideration by anyone who wants to study the matter in more detail. She pointed out that the crucial issue was fairness. She asked whether denial of cross-examination of victims about their previous sexual history is always fair to the accused. Amendment No. 59 seeks to address that concern, which is why it uses the word ``fairness''.

The Government have drafted a different test, which is contained in subsection (2)(b), which the amendment seeks to replace. Subsection (2)(b) provides that the court may not give leave for cross{**nrl**}examination unless refusal to do so could lead the jury to wrongly convict the accused. The court may not give such leave unless a subsequent inquiry into the case, presumably in the Court of Appeal, would result in the verdict being thought unsafe.

The philosophy behind subsection (2)(b) is capable of differing interpretations, which is why Baroness Mallalieu was right to ask Lord Williams to give examples of cases that the provision would cover. A further question has come to mind that was not discussed in the other place. When judges are deciding whether questions are to be denied, how will they know what answers would be given and the relevance that they could have? Will the Minister explain the purpose of subsection (2)(b)?

The Committee will discuss the detail of subsection (3) later. The Minister seeks to amend that detail, and I do not think that our opinions are wide apart in that respect. Subsection (5) is not a time-related exception that would allow cross-examination. When I first read the Bill, I wrote in the margin that subsection (5) might be referred to as the Owen Oysten subsection. The case was in my mind because a day or two earlier I had read a document circulated by the hon. Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours). The document supports Owen Oysten, whom, as the Committee may recall, was convicted of the rape of a 16-year old girl. I think that he may have been convicted on two counts. He contends that while the evidence adduced in his trial suggested that the young lady involved was of unblemished character and was beyond reproach, there is now evidence to rebut that view. If the prosecution brings evidence suggesting that the victim is of unblemished character and sexual purity but evidence to rebut that view also exists, I do not think that anyone would deny that such evidence should be considered and that it is relevant.

That may be the best answer to people who ask whether evidence of previous sexual history should ever be permitted to be adduced in court, and it explains why the Government were right to allow exceptions. The Minister will know that several people have suggested that we should go further than the proposed measures.

The Government clearly think that there are circumstances in which it is right for evidence of previous sexual history to be permitted to be adduced, and there is no great divide between us on that question. More pertinent to our discussion is the question of degree. In assessing degree, the final, perhaps overriding question to be determined is whether questioning would be relevant to the issues of consent or honest belief of consent. Clause 40(4) is hugely important in that regard because it makes it clear that questioning designed merely to impugn the character of the witness, to blacken her reputation, and which is of no relevance to the defence must be outlawed. We can all accept that point.

My real point is that, if the court considered questions relevant, it would be unfair to disallow them. It is difficult for us to anticipate every situation. Although some of us have experience of such criminal trials, none of us have the experience of other law practitioners. That suggests that we must be guided by what is fair. The interests of the victim and of the accused should be balanced.

In a large proportion of cases of women who are raped, the perpetrator escapes unpunished, and we cannot tolerate that. We know also that false and malicious allegations are made; some people have been wrongly convicted of rape. That suggests that, for all that we have the interests of the victim in mind, we must balance those with the interests of the defendant. Seeking that right balance is a matter of our determining whether the present wording is correct or whether it could be improved. I am happy to accept that the courts' discretion will be significantly limited. However, their discretion must be adequate--they must have flexibility. The courts would be better guided by the straight issue of fairness than by the wording of subsection (2)(b).

2 pm

Women--it normally is women although it might be a man--who are considering making an allegation of rape are all too often deterred from so doing, or from going through with the process of prosecution, because they are terrified of having their sexual history trawled through in a gratuitous way. I shall come to examples of the sort of cross-examination that has been allowed in the past under the 1976 Act formula, which the hon. Gentleman's amendments would restore and which will no longer be allowed under the Bill.

The intention of the Bill is to keep as much evidence of complainants' sexual behaviour out of trials as possible. That is vital if complainants are to pursue their complaints through to trial and not to feel that their privacy is being invaded to discredit and humiliate them. We must ensure that victims have faith in the criminal justice system and believe that their attacker will be on trial, not them and their history. Sexual history should be admitted only in very limited circumstances where it is really relevant to an issue at trial.

A clear framework is needed to govern when evidence of sexual behaviour can and cannot be considered relevant. No evidence that falls outside it should be admissible. The existing law--the Sexual Offences (Amendment) Act 1976--gives judges a broad discretion that any evidence that it would be unfair to the defendant to exclude should be admitted. No clear guidance was given on what that meant. That accounts for the disparity in practice in the various cases that has been identified in the law reports. That disparity makes it difficult for anyone seeking to counsel or advise rape victims to give them the confidence that they will not be subjected to this sort of intrusive and gratuitous cross-examination and the humiliation that all too often results.

We accept that there can never be complete certainty about that, but it is the absence of a reasonable degree of certainty that deters all too many people from coming forward and pursuing their complaints. Research evidence shows that sexual history evidence has been introduced in cases where it cannot be considered relevant, partly because the case law that the courts have built up in the absence of any statutory guidance permits it.

I shall take the Committee through some of that case law in detail because it is important that we all know what we are doing. The purpose of the Bill is to overturn that case law. No one should doubt that. We aim to overturn existing case law and impose a new statutory test on the relevance of previous sexual behaviour, especially to consent.

The Court of Appeal judgment in Viola in 1982, when it was ruled that the complainant's promiscuity was relevant to consent, will be overturned. It is our view that the fact that someone is or has been promiscuous does not take away that person's right to say no and to be able to draw on the full protection of the law to say no. We aim to overturn the judgment in Viola.

Similarly, the Court of Appeal judgment in SMS in 1992 will be overturned. That relates to the point, to which I shall return later, that my hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston made. In SMS in 1992 it was held that the judge should have given permission for the 14-year-old complainant to be cross-examined about the fact that she was not a virgin, even though the prosecution had not claimed that she was a virgin. If the prosecution had claimed that she was a virgin, it would have been permissible for the defence to produce evidence to rebut that. But the defence was gratuitously seeking to cross-examine the girl on her previous sexual history to suggest that if she was 14 and not a virgin, the chances were that she consented. That cannot be right. The 14-year-old was supposed to have consented to sex with a one-eyed, one-handed man in a grubby flat. In order to explain why she would choose to do that, the defence was to be allowed to gain from her the knowledge that she was not a virgin. How could the fact that she was not a virgin be relevant? The judge made a proper decision not to allow the defence to go down that path. Such a decision will be supported by the law in future, when the House passes the Bill--I hope that that will happen. The Court of Appeal finding will be overturned.

The Court of Appeal judgment in Brown in 1988 will also be overturned. In that case, it was held that the judge had to decide in each case whether

    ``the complainant's attitude to sexual relations could be material upon which in these days a jury could reasonably rely to conclude that the complainant may indeed have consented to the sexual intercourse on the material occasion.''

If the woman--it is usually a woman--has, in the opinion of the judge, a certain attitude, it is deemed right to cross-examine her on her sexual history.

I shall go into a little more detail about why we oppose the amendments. Previous sexual behaviour is not relevant to consent unless it is virtually contemporary with the offence or is so unlikely and so similar to the defence's version of events surrounding the alleged rape that it could not reasonably be considered a coincidence.

That does not include general evidence of promiscuity or of having had sexual relations with someone of a different race or of a different age. Attempts have been made to support the notion of consent in a specific instance by trawling the past. For example, the defence might say to a complainant: ``In the past, you had sex with a man older than you and who was of the same age as the defendant. In the past, you had sex with an Asiatic; the accused happens to be an Asiatic.'' That sort of questioning should not be allowed in relation to the issue of consent.

I shall give some examples from Sue Lees' research. Consent was the issue at the trial of Mulherne, a serial rapist. The complainant was asked about an abortion that she had had three years before the event and when she had last had sex. Of what possible relevance could that be to the issues at trial?

A complainant who had been raped by a taxi driver was asked about her relationship with her boyfriend, including how often she had sex with him and how long she had been going out with him. That was irrelevant to the issues at trial, and will no longer be permitted under clause 40.

I shall say a few words about strikingly similar behaviour. Examples of sexual behaviour evidence that might be admissible in relation to consent may include something along the lines of the example given by Baroness Mallalieu in the House of Lords. In that example, the defendant said that the complainant invited him to re-enact the balcony scene in ``Romeo and Juliet'' after meeting him in the pub. He subsequently climbed into her bedroom and had consensual sex with her.

That version of events sounds highly implausible and the complainant's claim that the defendant had broken into her room and raped her sounds far more plausible. Therefore, if the defendant had evidence of the complainant's previous sexual behaviour that was strikingly similar to his version of events, he should be allowed to introduce that evidence. However, if the defendant does not have such evidence and is unable to lay the grounds for that line of questioning, he will not be allowed to adduce evidence of previous sexual behaviour.

Under the provision, the following case reported by Sue Lees would not be allowed. At a trial at the Old Bailey in August 1993, the white complainant was asked whether she ``often slept with ethnics'', and what colour the father of her baby was, for no reason other than that the rapist happened to be black. We must never again allow offensive and wholly irrelevant questions of that nature in our courts.

Undoubtedly, hon. Members will be concerned about what is meant by the provision that allows evidence

    ``at or about the same time''

of the alleged offence, and will want to know its purpose. It is designed to allow in evidence behaviour just before the alleged offence, so that it can be seen in context. For example, the defendant and the complainant might have been having consensual sexual contact an hour before the alleged rape, on what amounted to the same occasion. That could be relevant to the jury's determination, and it should be allowed.

The hon. Member for Ryedale urges on us what has been described as an in the interests of justice gateway. our proposals have been deliberately tightly drawn. A wide interests of justice gateway would undermine the general premise that someone's past sexual behaviour is irrelevant to whether she consented on a particular occasion. That is why we cannot go down the road that the hon. Gentleman wants by having a general interests of justice gateway that would enable evidence to be admitted that we believe should not be allowed.

I draw the Committee's attention to the position in Scotland, where such a wide gateway exists. Research has shown that the gateway makes the rest of the legislation excluding sexual behaviour virtually redundant. That is why we cannot accede to the hon. Gentleman's amendments or the thrust of his oral arguments.

I shall now deal with the issue of honest belief in consent. It is a matter that has been raised by several hon. Members and it is right to give a specific and detailed answer.

Clause 40 highlights the distinction between the two defences of belief in consent and actual consent. This distinction is made for good reason. As I have said, consent is given, or not given, separately on different occasions.

But someone's belief may be affected by his previous experience and knowledge. Assumptions can be made on the basis of past experience, especially when there has been a previous relationship between the defendant and the complainant. There may be cases when a defendant realises, long after intercourse took place, that the complainant was not consenting, but that does not mean that his belief at the time was not genuine, based on some sort of misunderstanding, or misreading of the situation. The complainant's past sexual behaviour may sometimes be relevant to that belief. If it is, it should be admissible, because the defendant's honest belief in consent, as the law is currently framed--I stress that--can acquit him.

These proceedings and the Bill relate to criminal evidence. They do not relate to the substantive offence of rape. The Bill deals with criminal evidence.

The Government are aware of anxieties about rape and its definition. We should take a good, long, hard look at the offence in the light of contemporary knowledge and understanding of human sexual relations. We have therefore established a sexual offences review to consider the law on sexual offences generally, including the law on rape and consent. It will consider how to ensure that the criminal law meets and reflects contemporary views about the nature of human sexual relationships and the basis on which they should be subject to the criminal law.

The Bill will ensure that relevant evidence is admissible in relation to offences as they stand, and that is what should concern the Committee. Although I understand hon. Members' concern about the substantive offence of rape and other sexual offences, this is not the time or the place to try to make changes--through a side wind--in the law on rape. The Bill deals specifically with what is properly admissible in evidence.

Under subsections (3) or (5), evidence must either be relevant to an issue in the case or needed for the rebuttal of prosecution evidence. There is no reason why it should be admitted more widely; that is another reason for rejecting the hon. Member for Ryedale's amendment. Because the clause creates restrictions on the offence, there is a general provision in subsection (5) for the rebuttal of prosecution evidence to impugn the complainant's credibility; it is an important safeguard of the defendant's fair trial. If the prosecution asserts something and evidence can be produced that undermines it, it is right that that should be possible. If, for example, a prosecution's case was that a 14-year-old was a virgin and the defence could show that she was not, that would be fair enough--otherwise, the process would be wrong. It is important that evidence of a complainant's previous sexual behaviour is only ever relevant to her credibility if it undermines something that she explicitly claims at the trial. That is what the clause provides. Apart from that, sexual behaviour is irrelevant to credibility. The hon. Member for Ryedale will want to take that important point on board.

A judge, quoted in Ms Lees' research, directed the jury that a complainant was not a promiscuous person but a

    ``sober, sensitive and religious young lady''.

Such a comment, suggesting that if she were not sober, sensitive or religious, she would just have to take her chances as to how much belief she could inspire demonstrates the problem that we need to tackle.

That belief, and whether it is honest, will be affected to a greater or lesser degree by the defendant's previous knowledge and experience, if any, of the complainant. There may have been a previous relationship between them, or there may be an on-going relationship. The defendant may know of specific instances of past behaviour--with himself or others--which led him to believe that the complainant was consenting to sex with him on the occasion in question.

Under the existing law, evidence of the behaviour that led the defendant to form the view that the complainant was consenting can and should be admitted if the defendant can convince the court that it is relevant to his defence of honest belief, that his main purpose in adducing it is not to impugn the complainant's credibility and that its probity and value outweigh the adverse impact that it may have on the jury's view of the complainant.

Therefore, three hurdles must be overcome. Those hurdles, relating to the defence, will exist just as surely in future as they do now. However, added protection will be afforded by the Bill. The defendant's belief does not need to be reasonable; it need only be honest to succeed as a defence. However, the law allows the jury to assess the credibility of that belief in deciding whether it is honestly held. Should the jury conclude on the evidence presented that a defendant was reckless and could not care less about whether the complainant consented, it must find him guilty.

The wording of clause 40 does not permit a fishing expedition and does not allow the admission of tittle-tattle, bar room or cloakroom gossip or the suggestion that because the complainant had gone willingly with unnamed people in the past she was willing to go with the defendant. That concern was raised by my hon. Friends the Members for South Swindon, for Don Valley, for Stretford and Urmston, for Luton, South (Ms Moran) and for Basildon. My hon. Friends were right to raise such matters, which are foremost in the mind of any man or woman who, in certain circumstances, is concerned about their son or daughter--under the present law it could be either.

That is how the law makes a distinction between consent and belief in consent. Some may believe that such a distinction should not exist. That is a perfectly legitimate view; however, it is contentious. Were it to be accepted, there would be a fundamental change in British jurisprudence. That ain't going to happen in Committee this afternoon and will not happen in the course of parliamentary deliberations on the Bill. However, at some point, the House must wrestle with the matter.

Clearly, the full divergence of views on that matter is not reflected in the composition of the Committee. However, had that composition been otherwise, especially in relation to those silent ones in the Conservative party, there would have been a stout clash this afternoon. There has been no such clash. Indeed, I can think of at least one of my hon. and learned Friends who would have had his tup'penny ha'penny worth. Indeed, what he would have had to say would have been worth considerably more than that. I am a former colleague of his at the Bar, so he will not mind me saying that.

My hon. and learned Friend holds distinct views, as do other hon. and right hon. Members. However, I must ensure that the Bill achieves the right balance between the evidence and the Government's determination to end the abuse of the existing law that has led to complainants being deterred from coming forward.

I want to deal with another vexed issue that has been raised. My hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston forcefully and properly asked why a complainant's sexual behaviour in the past should ever be relevant to her consent unless it related to a previous false allegation of rape. My hon. Friend the Member for South Swindon correctly said that that was a different issue from the complainant's sexual history, but she concurred that sexual history can never be relevant unless it is raised in rebuttal. However, we are not currently able to accede to those views.

A complainant's sexual behaviour--either with the defendant or with anyone else--might in some circumstances be relevant. Our view is that it is not generally relevant to whether a complainant consented on the occasion in question. What is at issue--I have made this point time and again--is whether the complainant consented with the individual in question on the occasion forming the subject matter of the charge. Therefore, sexual history will not, and should not be, generally relevant. That does not mean that such evidence can never be relevant to consent.

I have been asked to give examples, so I will. I do so with some reluctance because it is always possible for examples to be misunderstood. I hope that members of the Committee are aware of that when they reflect on my examples or any of their own. That is why I prefer to root my remarks in existing case law. Everybody then understands where we are.

The complainant and the defendant may have been together for some hours during which the alleged offence took place. If they had consensual sex in that time and shortly before the alleged rape, evidence of that may be relevant to whether the complainant consented or whether the defendant believes that she consented to the alleged offence. It would be unfair to the defendant to exclude such evidence. Similarly, if the defendant can bring evidence or witnesses to show that he and the complainant had been engaged in a sexual activity--heavy petting, for example--other than sex shortly before the alleged offence was committed, that too might be relevant to the defence of consent.

I have another illustrative example. A group of youngsters go to a pub and two pair off during the course of the evening. They leave before the others, who later pass them on their way home and see them kissing, cuddling and fondling one another in a doorway or on a park bench. That evidence may be pertinent. Therefore, the defendant should be able with the agreement of the court--let us remember that the defendant must lay the ground for adducing such evidence--to bring witnesses to that effect at trial.

That does not preclude a complainant saying at trial that the petting took place, but that that was as far as she wanted it to go and that the defendant would not take her no for an answer. Of course, the complainant can say that. The jury must decide what weight to place on all the evidence. It would not be fair to deny the complainant the opportunity to test the defendant's assertion, or to prevent the defendant from introducing such evidence. It would not present the whole picture of events that took place on the night.

Evidence that the complainant had had one or more sexual partners in her life, last year, last month or last week would not be admissible as a defence of consent. The implication of such evidence would be that the complainant had consented to the defendant. On strikingly similar behaviour, it would not be relevant if the defendant could produce a friend to say that the complainant had had sex with him during the previous week after leaving the pub and indulging in heavy petting. It is important to make clear that that would not constitute strikingly similar behaviour. The Government are determined to get rid of the notion that consent to one person means consent to another and that sexual activity makes an individual more likely to lie. Both ideas are plainly nonsense.

I shall now consider the issue of similar evidence under proposed new subsection (3)(c). I have been asked why similar evidence under that provision is not ``strikingly similar''. The test under proposed new subsection (3)(c) allows the court to grant leave to admit evidence of sexual behaviour by the complainant that is so similar to the complainant's behaviour at or about the same time as the event that the similarity cannot reasonably be explained as coincidence.

There is a long history of the court's readiness to accept strikingly similar evidence. The best example is perhaps the DPP v. Boardman in 1975. The strikingly similar test has come under fire in recent years because it has been applied inconsistently. Therefore, we have chosen the test of unlikelihood of coincidence. That is not a new idea; it first surfaced in the Privy Council case of Makin v. the Attorney General of New South Wales in 1894--some time ago. It was most recently approved by the Court of the Appeal in the Groves case in 1998. We have chosen that test because we believe that it is tighter. We do not propose to use the phrase ``strikingly similar'' because proposed new subsection (3)(c) enables us, by introducing the language of the Groves case and that of Makin v. the Attorney General of New South Wales, to provide for additional hurdles that the defendant will have to overcome.

Under the test, the evidence that the defendant tries to adduce will have to surmount additional hurdles, apart from those for which the clause already provides. I shall outline the hurdles for which the clause provides. Under subsection (2), a refusal of leave might render unsafe the jury's conclusion on a relevant issue at trial; under subsection (4), the main purpose of the evidence must be other than to impugn the witness's credibility; under subsection (6), the evidence must relate to specific instances of sexual behaviour. The additional hurdles are that the evidence must be so similar to what happened during the alleged rape that the similarity cannot reasonably be explained as a coincidence, and that its probative value significantly outweighs the prejudicial impact its introduction might have on the complainant's evidence. That is important.

The gateway that the subsection creates is narrow, and intentionally so. It does not allow the court to admit evidence, even strikingly similar evidence, that, for instance, the complainant has previously enjoyed one-night stands, has had sex in the open air or in a car with different men on consecutive nights. That could be pure coincidence. It might be coincidental that sex was had in a car in a lovers' lay-by on one occasion, when it is alleged by the defendant that there was consent, and he can show that on another occasion she had sex in the same lay-by in the same car and the friend confirms that she consented and she admits that she consented. That would not be allowed in relation to this clause.

The clause permits evidence of consensual sexual activity that is unrelated to the alleged offence when it is so similar to the defendant's description events that it cannot reasonably be explained as a coincidence. We believe that that meets the requirements of justice and is fair to both the defendant and the complainant, but it is tighter than the previous test and is designed to be so.

I have been asked some specific questions, not least by my hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston, about children. For a child aged under 16 evidence about previous sexual behaviour cannot be relevant to consent because the child is deemed incapable of consent. It would not arise then in any event; it would not be allowed under the clause as drafted.

Where consent is not an issue, the Bill allows sexual behaviour evidence to be admitted only in the following circumstances. It can be admitted to explain or rebut evidence adduced by the prosecution about the sexual behaviour of the complainant or the events that form the subject matter of the charge. That is set out in subsection (5).

The evidence can be admitted if it relates to a relevant issue in the case and the court does not regard it as reasonable to assume that the main purpose for which it would be adduced is to impugn the credibility of the complainant as a witness. That is set out in subsection (4). It can be admitted if it relates to a specific instance or instances of sexual behaviour on the part of the complainant, That is set out in subsection (6). I know that that will be of particular concern to my hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston. I shall come in a moment to why it is unavoidable that such evidence might be admitted, albeit at the discretion of the judge on previous application and only where it is relevant and probative--when all those tests are fulfilled, but it might be admitted. Finally, evidence can be admitted if a refusal of leave to admit it might have resulted in rendering unsafe a conclusion on any relevant issue in the case. Those are the tests.

Subsection (4) excludes attempts simply to undermine the witness's credibility, an important safeguard against those who might want to claim that a secondary effect of sexual abuse of children is to render them sexualised, unreliable fantasists who may be more likely than most to make false allegations in the future. One cannot say, ``She was abused when she was a child, so she is a fantasist now and you can't believe a word she says'', although I know of attempts to adduce such evidence. That is not possible under subsection (4). Unfortunately, a child who has been victimised in that way is more likely than other children to be victimised by others in the same way in the future. That is one of the tragedies and evils of child abuse.

The definition of a child in clause 16 as someone who is under 17 includes children who are deemed capable of giving informed consent. Under subsection (3)(b), where the presence or absence of consent is an issue at trial, evidence of previous sexual behaviour will still not be admitted, even after the hurdles described earlier have been surmounted, unless the behaviour in question was so similar to behaviour that took place at or about the same time as the alleged assault that the similarity cannot reasonably be explained as a coincidence.

I said to my hon. Friend the Member for Stretford and Urmston that I would explain why we cannot rule out the possibility that a child's previous sexual behaviour, which may be non-consensual, may be relevant to a defence case. If it is relevant and it passes the hurdles in the clause, it will have to be admitted, as otherwise a miscarriage of justice might arise. Not only is the House committed to the notion of fairness between the defence and the prosecution but the burden of proof lies on the prosecution and is present even in the most horrendous of circumstances and horrific of cases. However horrific the circumstances and vulnerable the victims, the rule of law demands that the Crown prove its case and that the defendant do all that is necessary, so far as is compatible with the interests of justice, to contest and challenge the case brought against him. There is a right to a fair trial, and the court should hear evidence about relevant issues. It is a right to which the House is wedded, as it is wedded to the rule of law, and it is a right enshrined in the European convention on human rights. Consistent with that, the clause contains the checks and balances necessary to achieve our objective.
