Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): I beg to move amendment No. 39, in page 170, leave out lines 28 to 31.

This is a welcome schedule because it sweeps away some archaic schedule A rules, including the fact that rents are taxable on a due and payable basis rather than on an accrual basis and, with regard to the streaming of losses, the fact that landlord repairing losses are different from tenant repairing losses. In 1995, the schedule A rules from non-corporations were replaced with a more businesslike approach, and the same will happen in this instance. The rules that apply to businesses will also apply to schedule A businesses, which makes a great deal of sense.

This technical amendment would ensure that the Government's objective of creating an accruals concept that applies to schedule A will apply properly to the associated income. When the code was revised in 1995 for non-corporations, there was a debate about whether money siphoned into a sinking fund for future repairs to premises or property should give rise to a deduction for tax purposes. When money is put into a sinking fund, no expenditure takes place. The expenditure will occur in two, three, four or five years time when the property falls into a sufficient state of disrepair that the money needs to be expended.

Until the new code was introduced in 1995, the Revenue's view was based on the previous 1963 code. It believed that no deduction was available for a sinking fund until the money had been expended. That decision caused some hardship. When the 1995 code was passed, the debate ensued as to whether the Revenue would allow a deduction for a sinking fund on the basis of the long-standing case of Owen v. Southern Railway of Peru, which decided that if the provision could be calculated with accuracy, it should be possible to get a deduction for the expenditure. That debate was unresolved until this Finance Bill was introduced. My view, which is shared by people outside the House, is that it has now been resolved in the wrong direction.

New section 21A(3), which is in schedule 5 on page 170, refers to section 74(1)(d) of the Taxes Act 1988. Section 74 is the main provision which determines whether expenditure is deductible. Paragraph (d) specifically excludes

    ``any sum expended for repairs of premises occupied . . . for the purposes of the trade, profession or vocation, beyond the sum actually expended for those purposes''.

Therefore, any expenditure where sums were put into a sinking fund were not deductible.

It is slightly odd that new section 21A(3) incorporates that section into the new schedule A rules because the new rules purport to establish an accruals, business basis for the calculation of schedule A income. Schedule A income is supposed to be calculated according to the general rules that apply to the calculation of a trade's profits. It is wrong for those provisions to exclude expenditure siphoned into a sinking fund. I believe that the drafting of the Bill pertetuates an injustice.

The Government have an opportunity to put the rules onto a stable basis. They can resolve the debate, which took place after 1995, in the interests of business so that any money that is put aside for future repairs to a building can be deductible for tax purposes. The amendment would do that. It may be defective--one tables an amendment based on outside advice and hopes that it is technically sufficient--but even if it is not, I trust that the Government will accept my point. It is important for companies to obtain a deduction for sinking fund expenditure before the money is spent.
