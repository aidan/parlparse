Clause 69

Exemptions applicable to certain manual data held by public authorities

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Maclennan: The Bill extends individuals' right of access to their personal data held by public authorities. To the several classes of personal data to which individuals have rights of access under the Data Protection Act 1998 it adds an additional category; personal information held in a manual form that is not part of what the Data Protection Act calls a ``relevant filing system''. That could apply to an unstructured file containing items of information about an individual, or even passing references to an individual appearing on a policy file.

Generally, the standards that bodies must adopt in handing personal data are set out in the eight data protection principles. However, the Bill provides that the new category of personal data is covered by only by two of those principles, which require that people should be given the right to see data about themselves and that data should be accurate and, where necessary, kept up to date.

The clause gives public authorities a free hand to use unstructured personal data in breach of the remaining data protection principles. Clause 69 creates a new section 33A in the Data Protection Act 1998, exempting the new category of personal data from the majority of data protection principles. The clause allows personal data to be used unfairly and unlawfully; to be processed in a manner incompatible with any lawful purpose for which it was obtained; to be kept longer than necessary; and to be transferred to countries outside the European Economic Area, even if those countries cannot guarantee adequate protection.

The clause also permits the new category of personal data to be handled in breach of section 55 of the Data Protection Act 1998, which makes it an offence for a person to obtain or disclose personal data without the consent of the data controller, in this case the public authority.

I am really interested in why a high level of protection should be required for some manually held personal data, while there is to be virtually no protection in other instances.

Elizabeth France said in her response to the draft Bill:

    ``A practical problem arises from the extension of the 1998 Act to cover the extra personal information held by public authorities and the subsequent removal of many of the 1998 Act's key provisions from these data. An unnecessary level of complexity for both public authority and data subject is introduced by this . . . It would be simpler in practice as well as in the Registrar's view more desirable in principle to apply the 1998 Act in its entirety to the extra information brought within the scope of the 1998 Act.''

I should be grateful for the Minister's thoughts on those issues.

Mr. O'Brien: The issues raised by clause 69 are reasonably important and the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross is right to ask me to justify them. The clause is a necessary consequence of the way in which part VII of the Bill works. Clause 67 amends the definition of ``data'' m the Data Protection Act 1998 so that the Act applies to all recorded personal data held by a public authority. There is one small exception, in that clause 6 of the Bill limits the information brought within the scope of freedom of information access. An example is the BBC; information held for the purposes of journalism, art or literature is outside that scope.

The new definition of ``data'' is intended only to facilitate access to information for freedom of information purposes, not generally to broaden the way in which the 1998 Act applies to such information. Clause 69 therefore amends the Data Protection Act 1998 to disapply its substantive effects except for those relating to subject access and accuracy in the case of the relevant extended class of data.

Clause 69 will exempt specified information from some of the data protection principles and from the provisions in the 1998 Act relating to the unlawful obtaining of personal data. The extension of the definition of ``data'' to non-automated or unstructured records means that the 1998 Act will apply to, for example, incidental personal information on a policy file, or in loose papers.

Public authorities holding such data, but not in the form of personnel records, will have to obey the fourth data protection principle, which requires information to be accurate and, where necessary, up to date. A restricted version of the sixth data protection principle, which requires data to be processed according to the rights of data subjects under the 1998 Act, will also apply.

Individuals will have the right to subject access and, in certain circumstances, the right to rectify, to block, to erase or to destroy inaccurate data and to obtain compensation for damage and associated distress suffered as a result of inaccurate data or breach of the subject access rights. However, making such unstructured information subject to all the data protection principles would place a tremendous burden on public authorities. Similarly, the provisions in the 1998 Act relating to the unlawful obtaining of personal data are too restrictive in the context. The burden is not required by the EU directive, would be borne by public authorities rather than private bodies, and is not justified.

Clause 69 also provides that the extension of the rights of subject access and accuracy under part VII does not apply to personnel information held by public authorities. That will not have a significant impact on the rights of public sector employees to their personnel records. Most of the information, if held manually, would be in relevant filing systems and, therefore, subject to access rights under the 1998 Act. It would not be right to provide public sector employees with greater access to personnel records not contained in such files than private sector employees. To do so would be to provide enhanced employment rights by the back door.

The right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross says that the Bill does not give authorities a right to hold manual data while remaining in compliance with the data protection principles. It does not do that because authorities already have such a power. The Bill enhances the individuals' rights relating to the new limited category of information, grants them a right of access and imposes the principle of accuracy. It is, therefore, a step forward in the control of authorities' use of such information. A general extension of all the principles would not be justified.

The clause is a necessary part of a carefully constructed mechanism that serves to ensure that the public will have a comprehensive and sensible right of access to personal information.
