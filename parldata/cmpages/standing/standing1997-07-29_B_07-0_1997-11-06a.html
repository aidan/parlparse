We might not have needed to consider amendment No. 176 today, if--as I observed on Tuesday evening--the Government had accepted amendment No. 175. Unfortunately, they were unwilling to do so.

Amendment No. 176 seeks to ensure that appeals will be possible against the Secretary of State's decision either to suspend or to terminate payment, in the first case because of pending appeals, and in the second case because of failure to provide information or to submit to a medical examination. The suspension or termination of payment by the Secretary of State because of pending appeals makes the argument for the amendment similar to that for amendment No. 177 in some respects.

Amendment No. 177 seeks to add a further two cases in which there would be a right of appeal. First, it would allow appeals to be made against a decision by the Secretary of State not to take a decision where another relevant appeal was pending. Secondly, in paragraph (9B), an appeal would be allowed against a decision by the Secretary of State to call in a decision to be taken by an appeal tribunal or commissioner, where the grounds for the calling-in were that a relevant appeal was pending in another case.

Where relevant appeals pending is the reason for termination, suspension or calling-in, the Secretary of State's grounds would be that a relevant appeal would affect, in some way, the case being heard. The problem is that the Secretary of State might have misunderstood the grounds of the original case, so the case currently under appeal might not be genuinely relevant to the case being brought. If applicants cannot appeal, they will not even be allowed to argue that the Secretary of State has been mistaken in deciding what was at issue in their case. That seems rather unfair, and it could leave applicants in limbo for entirely irrelevant reasons. They might have to wait for their case to be heard, their payment might be suspended--even if it has been decided in their favour--or payment could be terminated. I hope that the Government will allow these further cases to be included, so that applicants have a right of appeal to argue that the case pending does not have the relevance to their own case that the Secretary of State believes it to have.

Amendment No. 176 deals with the failure to provide information and the failure to submit to a medical examination, as well as dealing with the issue of pending appeals. In this respect, the argument lying behind it is rather different, and does not apply to amendment No. 177. There might be very good reasons for the failure to provide information or to submit to a medical examination. It is possible that the information had been provided, and that the Secretary of State had either not realised or not accepted that it had been provided. We have all heard of cases in which files are lost, and in which information goes astray for a number of reasons. If applicants know that the information has been provided, and they are being prevented from receiving benefit because the Secretary of State believes that the information has not been provided, it seems unfair that the applicant is not able to make that point in an appeal. Family emergencies or delays in an over-bureaucratic part of the system could also prevent applicants from being able to pass on the necessary information sufficiently quickly to the officials who require it. Many things that are not the fault of applicants could make them unable to provide information quickly enough. It is therefore wrong that applicants cannot appeal against a decision that information has not been provided in the way requested.

Similar arguments apply to people who do not submit to medical examination in the expected period. Such people may have done their best to fulfil the requirement, but it is possible for doctors to be ill or unavailable on the relevant day. Some people have disabilities: that disability could worsen suddenly, making keeping an appointment difficult. Other emergencies could occur, or a bureaucratic slip-up could mean that people are not sent the necessary information.

The amendment attempts to build on the existing leapfrog procedures in the High Court in England and Wales. The effect of the amendments, however, would be to allow social security commissioners to do something which High Court judges cannot do. Under the leapfrog procedure a High Court judge can certify only that the necessary statutory conditions have been met, and that entitles the party who wishes to appeal to the House of Lords to petition the House of Lords for leave to appeal. It does not confer an automatic right of appeal; in 1996 one of the five petitions in leapfrog cases was rejected by the Lords. It is, therefore, a little-used power. The amendments would give commissioners the final say, however, about who can appeal to the House of Lords and they would also extend the leapfrog procedure to Scotland, where no such provision now exists for any type of case.

The amendments should be considered in relation to the role of the House of Lords as an appellate court. It is the highest court in the United Kingdom and deals with all kinds of cases. Its fundamental role is to give authoritative judgments on the most important and complex cases. Last year it dealt with only 43 civil appeals, of which just four related to social security law. Almost all types of appeal to the House of Lords require the leave of the House. Last year more than 80 per cent. of petitions for leave were refused.

It is an important feature of the House of Lords, as the supreme appeal court for the United Kingdom, that it is able to keep control over the cases that come to it, so that it deals only with those cases that are of genuine importance. The amendments would cut across that principle and would mean that the House of Lords' work load and priorities would be determined by others.

Mr. Rendel: I beg to move amendment No. 224 in page 11, line 11, after `not', insert

This is an important amendment. We are dealing here with the rights of people to be properly represented by others. We all know from our constituency work that sometimes those who need representation have not been able to be properly represented because their chosen representatives could not get hold of all the documentation or could not appear on the day for which the hearing was set.

The amendment seeks to ensure that where people appoint representatives to look after their interests, those representatives are themselves given all the relevant documentation as soon as possible so that, like the appellants, they can prepare themselves properly for the appeal hearing. The representative should also have a say in when the date of the tribunal hearing should be, because there is not much point in appointing a representative to look after one's interests if he or she cannot be there on the right day.

It is just as important that the day should be suitable for the representative as for the appellant. That is only natural justice. It is particularly important because of the short time in which some of these appeal hearings are set up. There is therefore a chance that the appeal hearing may be set on a day for which the representative cannot make himself available.

It is also true that quite a number of those who apply for benefit and who appeal as a result of being turned down are not necessarily the most articulate of people. To have a representative there to argue their case is very important. It is also a question of expertise. Their representative will know about the laws involved, about the details of the case, and about, for example, disabilities if it is a disability case; he will therefore be much more able to present the appeal than the appellant himself.

The needs of representatives are very important. It is only fair and reasonable that representatives should be able to be there. There is perhaps one final point that is worth making. While those who are appealing for some form of benefit may often find it relatively easy to attend tribunals on a given day--they may not be particularly busy and may unfortunately not have jobs themselves--their representatives are often very busy people who may find it particularly difficult to make themselves available at short notice for an appeal hearing. If anything, representatives will require more notice and more rights to say when they can and cannot appear than the appellants.
