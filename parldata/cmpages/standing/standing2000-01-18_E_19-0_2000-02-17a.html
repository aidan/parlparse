The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Keith Hill): Before we adjourned, I think I had said that this has been a genuinely interesting debate, and that I had gone out of my way to pay tribute to my hon. Friend, the Member for Scarborough and Whitby (Mr. Quinn) for his excellent speech and his help to the Committee in tabling the amendment. I am grateful to him and to other colleagues for their comments during the debate. I am a highly amenable Minister, anxious to please and I hope that my response will reassure my hon. Friend.

Local authorities should be alert to the positive and constructive offers and initiatives that are available from the bus operators. If necessary I should like to send out a clear message from the Committee that we expect a high degree of consultation between local authorities and bus operators. However, the emphasis on consultation is both implicit and explicit in the Bill.

Clause 94(4) makes special provision to ensure that, in drawing up its bus strategy, a passenger transport authority is to seek and have regard to advice from its passenger transport executive. That is right and proper: the clause recognises the respective role and functions of the two bodies. Most importantly, the clause deals with strategies. It therefore seems reasonable that it should be representative and accountable bodies and their executive arms—namely the PTEs—that should be consulted on the broad picture in developing the strategies. There is more than ample indication in the Bill that operators will play a central part in the process of implementing the strategy. It is difficult to see how the Government could give greater emphasis than it has to the role of bus operators.

Clause 95 already requires authorities to consult operators or their local representative organisations in connection with the preparation of the bus strategy. It follows that any reasonable authority, having consulted, must take those views into account. In the light of those provisions, it is argued that the amendment would not add anything to the Bill.

In addition, there are specific consultation requirements in connection with the making of schemes for quality partnerships, quality contracts and ticketing. Bus operators will have ample opportunity to make their views known, both on the generality and on the specifics. Judging by the remarks of my hon. Friend, the Member for Scarborough and Whitby, bus operators in Scarborough are ripe for involvement in a quality partnership. It is unnecessary to add further provisions to the Bill. I hope that my hon. Friend is fully persuaded by my remarks and will be content to withdraw his amendment.

The amendments would widen the definition of ``bus services'' in the context of bus strategies under Clause 94. The Bill as drafted defines bus services as services using ``public service vehicles''. The term is further defined in Clause 138—Interpretation of Part II—by reference to the Public Passenger Vehicles Act 1981, section 1 of which defines a public service vehicle as a motor vehicle adapted to carry more than eight passengers and used for carrying them for hire or reward; or not so adapted but used for carrying passengers for hire or reward at separate fares in the course of a business of carrying passengers. Amendment No. 447 would expand that to include other public vehicles providing public services, while amendments Nos. 496 and 497 spell out in detail categories of services that should also feature in the bus strategy, namely community and schools transport, taxis, private hire vehicles and dial-a-ride buses.

We have no objection at all to local authorities seeking to widen the coverage of their bus strategy to include reference to many of those services. Indeed, many will already be using passenger service vehicles—for example, community buses, school buses and dial-a-ride vehicles. We have anticipated the need to recognise links with other forms of transport in Clause 94(1)(c), which refers to additional facilities and services connected with bus services. Therefore, to the extent that other services or facilities are ``connected with'' conventional bus services, they are already provided for under the clause as drafted. Current guidance on non-statutory local transport plans already refers to the desirability of acknowledging links with health and community transport provision in what we have come to know fondly as ``joined-up thinking''. I believe that we are at one on that broad question; where we differ is on the desirability of amending the Bill.

In our view, Clause 94(5) rightly puts the focus on the bus services that must be covered by the strategy. They are what might be called ``conventional'' bus or coach services using public service vehicles. In that sense, Clause 94 provides a bridge between the local transport plan provisions in Clauses 92 and 93 and the new powers and functions of local transport authorities in respect of local services under Clauses 96 to 135. Let me repeat that there is nothing to prevent a local authority from giving its bus strategy a wider canvas to include other types of bus services, but we do not believe it is appropriate to make that part of the mandatory requirements in the bus strategy. I hope that I have satisfactorily explained our thinking to the Committee and that hon. Members will be content to withdraw the amendments.

I turn now to amendment No. 386. Subsection (4)(b) provides a defence for a bus operator temporarily unable to comply with the quality partnership scheme standards on account of circumstances beyond its control. It is a perfectly reasonable provision, which is common to many areas of contract law. Indeed, we discussed the question of force majeure in the context of licensing under part I. If we were to omit that defence, bus operators, through no fault of their own, would find themselves unable to enjoy the benefit of quality partnership scheme facilities, or in danger of breaching their bus registration conditions, simply because of a temporary problem.

For example, if a bus breaks down or is involved in an accident, an operator would have to decide whether to replace the bus with an older one that might not meet the quality partnership standards or, in extreme cases, leave passengers without a bus until the original one could be repaired or replaced. Subsection (2) provides an equivalent defence for local authorities for the provision of scheme facilities, which we have just debated.
