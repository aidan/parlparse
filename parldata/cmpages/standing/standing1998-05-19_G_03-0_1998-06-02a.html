My hon. Friend the Member for South Cambridgeshire (Mr. Lansley) moved amendment No. 85, which would leave out line 4 of clause 2, which refers to trade. By a process of dialogue, we discovered that his amendment goes deeper than even he had first imagined. He argued that the reference to effect on trade was otiose. He was right; it is. Anything that distorts competition is presumably intended to be caught by the Bill and the fact of affecting trade or otherwise is not material.

The Under-Secretary has been caught by the word “and”—an inclusive word—in much the same way as he had been caught earlier in our discussions by the word “or”—an exclusive word. The phrase which the amendment covers is of particular interest and goes to the heart of the Bill’s drafting problems. The clause was drafted by someone who had been studying in number, not in shape, what used to be article 85 of the treaty of European Union. There was a particular reason for that article containing an inclusive word—“and”; it was to demarcate the particular area of competition that should come within the competence of the competition authorities in Directorates—General IV. Such demarcation cover the area of inter-European member state trade and the type of competition.

I am sure that members of the Committee who are also members of European Standing Committee B would agree that there is every reason why the European treaty should be so structured. The Commission should be prevented from exercising its authority except in cases where the matters in question are not just of a distortion of competition, but affect inter-state trade.

Unfortunately, the Bill contains a transposition of a doubly vague phrase, appropriately crafted in the context of the treaties, into English law.
