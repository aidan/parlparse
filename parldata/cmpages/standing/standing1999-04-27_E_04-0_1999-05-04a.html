Amendment No. 40 proposes that "appropriate" should be left out of subsection (2)(a) and replaced by "necessary" . Examining the minutiae of legislation is often an exercise in semantics and about what certain words may mean. Doubtless, the Solicitor-General will tell us that it will be appropriate for satisfactory resources to be made available by the Lord Chancellor, pursuant to clause 6, for the funding of the services of the Legal Services Commission and the Community Legal Service. That matter causes us considerable concern. I am sure, however, that the Solicitor-General will have no difficulty in explaining some of the implications of the clause.

It is accepted that the existing legal aid scheme has many flaws, one of which is because of the element of it being a bottomless pit that a client who is financially eligible and has a meritorious case is entitled to legal aid. It is therefore demand driven; it is an important state provision to ensure that individuals should have access to justice. If more people require access to justice in one year than another, and so long as their cases fall within the merit criteria, they ought to be funded. Otherwise, they would be denied justice. Under the Bill, that system will be substantially altered. it will be based on the allocation of available resources. It will therefore be a cash-limited scheme. Not only that, but the cash limitation will depend, first and foremost, on how much has been spent or is being spent on criminal legal aid in that year.

Criminal legal aid will continue to be a bottomless pit, because the Government acknowledge the state's responsibility under the Human Rights Act 1998 to comply with the European convention on human rights. If the crime rate rises, or if the courts' throughput of work increases, the amount of money available for the Community Legal Service may decrease. That is a matter of considerable concern. The proposal to replace "appropriate" with "necessary" may seem a small change. By using "necessary" , I do not suggest the return of the bottomless pit. I seek to put an onus on the Government, the Lord Chancellor's Department and the commission that will administer the fund to justify the level of funding for civil legal aid.

I am fearful about the proposals. I say this in kindness to the Government, but if their budget projections are wrong, what will happen to those in civil litigation who are now entitled to funding? Shall we simply receive a letter from the Minister come January of the following year, saying, "I am terribly sorry, but no further funds will be available until the next financial year" ? Or will the Department impose a close budget and ratchet down the availability of funds irrespective of the merits of cases?

I would be happy if the Solicitor-General were to clarify how he sees the system working and in practice, rather than as a theoretical construct. I am convinced that there will be a crisis unless people receive funding for deserving cases that meet the Government's and the commission's criteria. I move the amendment to probe a little, and to ascertain how the Solicitor-General intends the system to operate. Nothing in the Bill says how much money the Government intend to make available for civil legal aid.

"Government Accounting" sets out the financial framework within which departments are required to operate. Sponsor Departments, which make funds available to non-departmental public bodies, are accountable to Parliament for the provision of funds, and are required to draw up clear, strategic frameworks for each of their non-departmental public bodies. It is dreadful that the Government have produced this legislation. The Minister says that it is for small and medium-sized firms of lawyers to pay that money, but they do not have it.

I want to address amendments Nos. 130, 131 and 132, which are in my name, that of my hon. Friend the Member for Montgomeryshire and various Conservative Members. We want to add to the list of cases exempted from the general prohibition on funding under schedule 2. The types of cases that we suggest are especially difficult to fund on a conditional fee basis those involving children and people suffering mental disability. Amendment No. 130 seeks to place in the Bill a number of different categories of personal injury cases for which legal aid might continue to be available. Cases involving children and the mentally disabled will involve their litigation friends being responsible for making decisions about conditional fee arrangements and taking out any necessary insurance on their behalf.

Litigation friends will normally be expected to give the court an undertaking to be personally responsible for any costs that might be ordered against the child or patient. There will be difficult decisions for litigation friends to make, although I concede that the situation will be improved if the success fee and insurance premiums are recoverable on a full indemnity basis. I raised that point with the Minister at Lord Chancellor's Questions some time ago. When he replies to this debate, will he assure us that they will not only be recoverable but recoverable on such a basis? It is not solely recoverability that I want the Minister to assure us of, but that the fee and premiums will be recoverable in toto.

The Lord Chancellor has suggested that high cost cases or those with high investigative costs may result in his making a direction to fund. However, lawyers advising clients need to be able to refer to the statute to see whether a case comes within the scope of legal aid without needing to check whether the Lord Chancellor has made a direction or is minded to do so. The amendment would incorporate those assurances in the Bill.

I accept that there have been problems with conditional fees. For example, disbursement funding has been mentioned, but new banking products are being developed to deal with that perhaps along the lines organised by trade union firms. Adverse selection has been responsible in large part for some of the hike in the insurance premiums. If anything, legal aid has distorted the CFA market because, under the present rules, before a CFA can be offered, the client must be offered legal aid if the case would fall within the existing rules, and that would distort the market.

As lawyers become more used to risk assessments, which have been the practice across the Atlantic for many years, they will become more willing to use CFAs. The object of the Bill must be to look to the interest of the client, not that of the lawyers. Clients want the same as the Government: a competent personal injury lawyer at a reasonable price. The present system has failed to ensure quality for clients who instruct lawyers under the legal aid process. An advantage of the CFA scheme is that, to gain access to CFA insurance, for example, the lawyer must be a member of the Law Society personal injury panel, and therefore have at least a minimum level of competence, if not rather more, to conduct a conditional fee arrangement. Other accreditation arrangements are expected, such as the Association of Personal Injury Lawyers college of personal injury, which will be launched later this week.

My experience in practice is that many people dabble in personal injury, often on the back of legal aid, who should not be doing such work. That experience has, if anything, been confirmed by my surgery experience. I am appalled by the quality of work that some of my erstwhile professional colleagues deliver to the public. For example, at my surgery on Friday, I heard about a legal aid case brought on behalf of a constituent who fell off a roof at work some six years ago. Unfortunately, he was not a member of the trade union. He applied for, and was granted, legal aid.

Having examined the facts, on the face of it, it seemed a good case. However, the employer did not have an insurance policy. He had contravened the Employers Liability (Compulsory Insurance) Act 1969, and was not insured. One of the first questions that a personal injury lawyer should ask is, if I win the case, where will the damages come from? However, the lawyers ploughed on regardless. They received a barrister's opinion that the case had a 60 per cent. chance of success. The trial date was set for this May, some six years on but the Legal Aid Board pulled the plug on the case a couple of weeks ago. Not surprisingly, the client was very wound up about that, and ended up at my surgery. The case should never have reached that stage in the first place. The client should have been given a much better analysis of the problems of the case at a much earlier stage. He should have been warned about what would happen, because it was inevitable.

That is one of the problems of the existing legal aid scheme. One of the lessons that we can learn from that case is about the benefits of block contracting and franchising, which will become the order of the day for other areas of work that will remain within the parameters of the legal aid scheme under schedule 2. Block contracting will provide quality control and price control. Moreover, the incentive under block contracting will not be, as it is now, to continue with a duff case at any cost because that is how the lawyer is paid. The incentive will be the opposite, as operates in trade union schemes to turn down bad cases early because the lawyer will not receive more money for continuing with them. From a client's point of view, it is far better to know where he stands at an early stage, rather than to have his hopes raised year after year only to have them dashed at the last minute.

I hope that the Minister will flesh out how the public interest scheme and the arrangements for high investigation costs will work. Perhaps he could respond to cases such as those raised by The Lawyer magazine, which expressed valid anxieties that should be discussed. I know that my hon. We must consider how to fund complex investigation cases, but the existing legal aid scheme is all or nothing. Some of the proposals in the Bill, such as allowing loans for high disbursement cases while expecting lawyers to fund work in progress, matches what happens in practice outside the legal aid scheme in trade union schemes or private cases. We can consider some of the more complicated industrial disease cases as high investigation cost cases. Several years ago, I handled what I think was the only case involving phosphorus tribromide poisoning. Luckily, it was funded by Unison, but it could have been a rather expensive case. I do not think that such a case has been brought since. I have great difficulty even in saying the name of the chemical. A lot of ground-breaking work is being done in industrial disease cases, such as linking some bladder cancers to exposure to industrial oils. Again, such cases may involve rather high investigative costs, which are often funded by trade unions. The private sector might be suitable for the cases under discussion.

We should also consider product liability cases, especially pharmaceutical cases, which are often lumped in with clinical negligence but are different. Traditionally, they have had a low success rate, but they are of high public importance and must be examined. We must also consider how to deal with new points of law raised by cases such as in the personal injury sphere the recent asbestos claim cases in the House of Lords, in which the victim has died and another tort-feasor may be involved, or the case recently handled by my firm, relating to rates of return, which had peculiar problems, partly caused by the existing legal aid scheme.

I should also like the scheme to be expanded to cover some of the areas that Opposition Members mentioned. However, tribunal work presents a problem compared with other litigation in that there are no cost orders in the normal course of events for employment or other tribunals. Although at the moment legal aid is, in effect, a loan as I have always tried to explain it to clients, using layman's terms for tribunal work it would constitute a grant and thus may be rather expensive. I hope that, if we can generate additional resources from the changes, we might be able to expand it to cover cases such as criminal injuries compensation appeals and motor insurance bureaux cases involving untraced motorists.

I am grateful to the hon. and learned Member for Harborough for flagging up new clauses 10, which I have tabled. If being able to do so means that we can produce another method of funding that ensures quality access to justice for people bringing personal injury cases, and that people who cannot secure conditional fee arrangements for complex cases are protected through a public interest fund or high investigation cost arrangements, I would be satisfied.
