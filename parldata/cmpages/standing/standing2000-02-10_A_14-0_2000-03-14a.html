Utilities Bill

Clause 25

Prohibition on unlicensed distribution of electricity

10.30 am

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): Although the clause prohibits the unlicensed distribution of electricity, its real purpose is to separate the licensing of electricity from the licensing of its distribution and supply. It does that by amending section 4 of the Electricity Act 1989, adding a fourth type of electricity industry licence—that of distribution—to the three sorts of licence for generation, transmission and supply. The clause redefines supply in a narrow sense, and defines distribution as solely the conveyance of electricity through low voltage lines. The Opposition supported that change on Second Reading, and we continue to support it. It is a tribute to the growth of competition in the supply of electricity since privatisation.

According to the Government's consultation document on the modification by the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority of the standard conditions of gas and electricity licences, which was published last week, there are now 50 licensees supplying electricity, of which 27 supply designated customers—that is Office of Gas and Electricity Markets jargon for domestic customers. The number of suppliers is huge and, by any assessment, constitutes a competitive market, which is why Ofgem has implemented its final round of price caps. However, to remove the price cap from supply, it is necessary to separate supply from distribution, which is not subject to such competition. Distribution comprises the wires from the national grid to people's homes in the local network; it is, in effect, a natural monopoly. By separating it from the highly competitive supply of electricity, the price setting of electricity supply can ultimately be removed. I would argue also that most of the remaining regulatory framework could be removed—but that, alas, is not the Government's view.

It is interesting to look back. I managed to dig out a copy of a pamphlet written by a chap whose name has featured before in our debates—Dan Corry, who is now a special adviser to the Secretary of State. Mr. Corry wrote a pamphlet, ``Regulating in the Public Interest'', in 1994, in which he said:

    At one level is the debate amongs the experts on the minutiae of the regulation process—

legal rulings and so on. He said that such debate tinkered with the regulation of the utilities. He then said:

    On the other level are the general, swingeing statements, often associated with commentators of the Left, that privatisation and the regulation process has allowed thousands of fat-cat executives to clean up, for the customer to be ripped off by the privatised utility companies charging extortionate prices and that we need to boot out all the regulators and start all over again.

The Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe might have said that. Mr. Corry said that his pamphlet

    tries to escape from these two byways to take us back to the reasons that we have regulation in order to see whether this leads us towards a different vision for the way forward.

That pamphlet is a blueprint for the Bill. I suspect that when the Government came to office, Mr. Corry handed it to the civil servants and asked them to draft a Bill. Therein lies the problem.

The pamphlet is revealing. Mr. Corry says:

    It is very important to reject this idea of competition as the only objective and vital to understand the significance of so doing for the future organisation of regulation.

He dismisses the debate between left and right, arguing against the importance of competition. Revealingly, he goes on to say:

    Once we accept that regulation is here to stay, we are into the world of how we should use it. This is a classic issue of interventionism in the workings of the economy.

The pamphlet illustrates the concept behind the Government's approach. They accept that regulators will be needed for some time in natural monopolies such as electricity distribution. Indeed, they accept that, if the utilities remain as natural monopolies, regulators will probablly be needed forever. Therefore, they want to use regulation to implement their broader agenda. The pamphlet reveals that the Bill is not about improving the energy sector or increasing competition in it, but about how the Government can use the regulatory mechanism to implement their social and environmental agenda. That approach will lead to higher costs for consumers, which is why the industry and other parties have difficulties with the Bill.

It is fascinating to consider how privatised electricity has reached its current state. Electricity is highly competitive: prices have decreased by 30 per cent. in real terms since privatisation, 27 firms are supplying domestic customers, there are 50 new independent power producers and the number of long-term one-year electricity contracts has fallen by a further 15 per cent. none of that would have happened if Labour Members' views had prevailed in 1988 and 1989, when the Bill that became the Electricity Act 1989 passed through the House.

At that time, the right hon. Member for Sedgefield (Mr. Blair), then shadow Secretary of State for Energy, said:

    Outside the Conservative party ... it is barely an issue that prices will rise because of privatisation.

He went on to say:

    I would have thought that it was ... virtually impossible that anyone would build a power station and invest hundreds of

    millions of pounds, unless they received a guarantee covering the capital costs, the fuel cost and, probably ... the operating costs, too ... the idea that we will have an influx of power stations, all competing on the grid, is nonsense.—[Official Report, 12 December 1988; Vol. 143, c. 683-84.]

In fact, 27 new power stations have been built, and they produce 18,500 MW. Electricity prices have fallen and are set to fall further because of competition, competition, competition and not because of regulation or even the £500 million new trading arrangements, which we support, but which are not responsible for the reduction in prices. The merger of gas and electricity regulation is not responsible for the savings either. Competition is delivering them to consumers.

Privatisation of the utilities was a huge leap in the dark. We trusted out philosophy and believed that putting monolithic state industries into the private sector would make them a degree or two more efficient. We hugely underestimated how much more efficient they would become. When I visited a Kent power station recently, I was staggered to hear that it and a nearby power station used to emply 1,200 people. Now they employ 300 people but produce the same amount of power—privatisation achieved not a 10 per cent. or 20 per cent. improvement in efficiency, but a 300 per cent. improvement. Labour politicians denied that power stations could become more efficient and even Conservative Members did not anticipate that competition in generation and supply would reach its current level.

Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): I have been following the hon. Gentleman's argument carefully, but I found it rather more difficult to follow his mathematics. A reduction from 900 to 300 is a 66 per cent. decrease.

Mr. Gibb: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman, who is probably right. We did not anticipate the current level of effiency, which explains my difficulties with the mathematics. Nobody—not the City, the Conservative party or even the Institute of Economic Affairs—anticipated the success achieved by the industry since privatisation.

The clause speaks volumes, as it separates the concept of public electricity supply into supply and distribution. It had been assumed that the regional electricity companies would always retain their regional monopolies. However, we were wrong. Supply, purchase and sale of electricity to consumers took off as a new business and rapidly became highly competitive as the laws were relaxed. Most of industry believes, as the Opposition certainly do, that the new, narrower natural monopoly—distribution—is likely ultimately to remain for the vast majority of consumers, but we should never underestimate the ingenuity of the private sector. Clause 35 provides for further alteration to the requirement by statutory instrument, as the scope for narrowing, refining and redefining the natural monopoly develops further.

We commend the Government for their decision to separate the licensing of supply and distribution. That is the ultimate accolade to the success of privatisation of the industry and, consequently, we shall not oppose it. However, we wish that the approach was taken elsewhere in the Bill. The over-regulatory and interventionist approach is potentially enormously damaging to the electricity industry. Mike Hughes, president of the Electricity Association, has warned that the Bill threatens the industry's long-term stability and will draw investment out of the sector. He said that the industry was concerned about the wide-ranging powers to be given to the regulator to change licence conditions and impose unlimited penalties, for example. He went on:

    I am concerned that all the forces in operation are very short term in their nature in an industry that needs long-term stability and considerable investment.

The Government have already withdrawn such clauses in respect of telecoms and water; I wish that they would do so for gas and electricity. Will the Minister inform the Committee of the outcome of the meeting on Thursday between the Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe and the Electricity Association? Might interventionist and highly regulatory clauses be withdrawn?

We support the separation of distribution and supply for licensing powers and shall not oppose clause 25.
