Clause 135

Authority's power to require information

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): I beg to move amendment No. 483, in page 63, line 14, after `without', insert `unreasonable'.

We now move to part X, to which clause 135 and the amendment specifically relate. It is complex territory, which concerns the powers of the Financial Services Authority to require the production of information, conduct investigations and have access to businesses. Those whom the FSA orders to comply face serious penalties for not doing so. Obviously, it is important that the FSA should be able to do its job properly. However, in relation to the traditional Anglo-Saxon areas of justice and liberty, refusal to comply with such orders is a criminal offence.

As my hon. Friend the Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton) said, the Government have substantially rewritten much of part X. Buried within that rewriting is, to some degree, a concealed extension of the FSA's powers, which goes beyond it having reasonable grounds to make such investigations. I wish to state upfront the Opposition's general feeling that the extension of those powers is unwarranted. Powers prescribed in the current draft of the Bill are sufficient for a professional job and for justice to be done. Many of the powers are Star Chamber powers. Since the beginning of our debates, we have felt the need to strike a balance in the Bill between the powers of the individual and the regulator.

Clause 135 gives the FSA a general power to require information and call for documents, which may be reasonably required for it to discharge its functions. Those requirements relate to formally authorised persons, those connected to them, exchanges and clearing houses. The clause should be absolutely clear and just. Amendment No 483 would change ``without delay'' in 135(3) to ``without unreasonable delay''. We are dealing with a criminal offence. ``Without delay'' means what it says. We consider that the qualification ``unreasonable'' is appropriate to achieve a proper degree of justice. Obviously, the amendment is not a major one, but it sets the tone for quite a lot of the amendments relating to part X.

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): Mr. O'Brien, I hope that you and Committee members enjoyed the short pause that we have had over the past week.

There is a little confusion on both sides of the Committee about which part of the Bill we are discussing. We are indeed discussing part X, not part IX or part VIII. The right hon. and learned Member for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir Nicholas Lyell) asked about notes, the title of which contained an error. It should have referred to part X and schedule 12, not part VIII and schedule 11.

I have some sympathy with the argument for amendment No. 483 made by the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight). However, I hope to persuade him that it is unnecessary.

First, let me say a little about the purpose of the clause and this part of the Bill. Hon. Members will appreciate that information is the key to effective regulation. The FSA must have access to detailed, up-to-date and relevant information in order to make good, regulatory decisions.

Therefore, this part of the Bill is vital to ensuring that the new rationalised regulatory system is effective. In keeping with our general policy of rationalising powers, part X represents a synthesis—put together with some care—of the rather confusing array of investigative powers exercised by the existing regulators. Initially, there was one kind of person doing one kind of activity, another power for another person, and so on. Part X now includes some detailed and technical provisions, but I hope that the Committee will bear in mind as we debate them that we shall end up with something a lot simpler than what is on the statute book at present—an important improvement.

What is equally important is that the powers in this part are not just simpler, but also more transparent than some of the powers currently relied on by existing regulators. All the FSA's powers, and the associated limits and safeguards, will be contained in the primary statute, rather than operating through contracts.

So the main point is not that the Bill arms the regulator with a wide range of powers, but that it provides the FSA with a single coherent set of powers, appropriate for a modern regulator dealing with developing markets, bounded by appropriate safeguards, and drawing on the best of what exists at present.

There are two broad categories of power under this part: powers to gather information or documents as part of the on-going supervisory process and powers to carry out investigations. This clause is concerned with the former. It deals with how the FSA goes about its daily business of supervising authorised firms and overseeing recognised exchanges and clearing houses. It provides the FSA with its basic power to require information or documents to be provided by an authorised person, a recognised investment exchange, a recognised clearing house, a formerly authorised person or a person who is connected to an authorised person.

The power replaces a range of similar information, gathering powers under existing Acts: the Insurance Companies Act 1982; the Financial Services Act 1986; the Building Societies Act 1986; the Banking Act 1987—section 39, to which I shall refer in a moment—and section 62 of the Friendly Societies Act 1992.

The information or document that the FSA can require under the clause is restricted to those documents or that information which it may reasonably require in connection with the discharge of its functions. Clearly, it is important that the FSA can access information and documents on a timely basis. Subsection (3) therefore provides that the FSA can require information or documents ``without delay''. The hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs, (Mr. Flight) suggests that we change that to ``without unreasonable delay''.

I sympathise with the intention behind the amendment, but I hope that I can reassure him that that is unnecessary. The term ``without delay'' appears in innumerable other places on the statute book. It is implicit that what is being referred to is an unjustified or unreasonable delay. Subsection (3) does not enable the FSA to require a person to meet some unreasonable or impractical requirement, nor do any of the other statutes that include that wording.

If we were to include the wording proposed by the hon. Gentleman, it could cast doubt on those other parts of the statute book where the word ``unreasonable'' does not appear. That would clearly be highly undesirable.

I hope that, in the light of that explanation and the fact that I have put on record the Government's intention behind the wording that we have adopted, the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs will feel able to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Flight: On the basis of the Minister having made it clear that ``without delay'' means here and in other places ``without unreasonable delay'', I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

The FSA would have no power to require information or documents on the basis of good reason. The problem there is that it would be shutting the door after the horse had bolted. How can we expect the FSA to supervise if it does not have the power to require information, or check on how things are going. I feel sure that Opposition Members would not really prefer a regulator which needs to wait until a problem has arisen. That would be out of line with all the principles and practice of sound modern financial regulation. The Bank of Credit and Commerce International and Barings have reminded us, if a reminder were needed, of the dangers of waiting until things have gone wrong before taking action. The lessons from those cases were widely drawn. We need a regulator that will be vigilant and pro-active in keeping its eyes open for trouble. I hope, therefore, that the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs will withdraw amendment No. 484.

I have some sympathy with the thinking behind amendments Nos. 485 and 487, which would prevent the FSA from seeking information under the powers conferred by the clause on someone who ceased to be authorised more than six years previously. The Government do not intend that a person who was once authorised should be subject to an endless stream of information for ever more, but nor can we afford to have the regulator wash its hands of an authorised person as soon as they cease to be authorised. What if, a few years later, concerns emerge that there has been large-scale mis-selling along the lines of the pensions scandal? To assess the extent of the problem, the FSA may need to have access to information from all those who were active in the market at the time, not just those who are still authorised.

For good reason, we have taken forward the existing approach under section 39 (8) of the Banking Act 1987, which applies the power to collect information to former authorised institutions under that Act. We considered whether there should be some cut-off date, but the difficulty is that any date we might choose is bound to be arbitrary. Pensions mis-selling provides evidence that there can be a real need for information about transactions carried out a few years—certainly more than six years—earlier. Instead, the power under the clause is circumscribed by the requirement in subsection (4), which limits it to

    ``information and documents reasonably required in connection with the exercise by the Authority of functions conferred on it by . . . this Act''.

Amendment No. 484 would delete that. If it is left in, that requirement ensures that a former authorised person will not be pursued endlessly with information requests. Only where the FSA has some legitimate need for information in connection with the discharge of its functions may it invoke that power.

The position under clause 136 is similar. That clause deals with reports by skilled persons, another very useful power modelled on section 39 of the Banking Act. Subsection (2) (d) of clause 136 applies the power to a person who was an authorised person, or a group or partnership of which an authorised person was a member ``at any relevant time''. The requirement under subsection (4) of clause 135, to which I just referred, also applies by virtue of subsection (1) of clause 136.

Adequate constraints are built into clauses 135 and 136, which will ensure that, while it is necessary for these powers to be capable of being exercised against former authorised persons, they can be exercised only for reasons connected with the discharge of the authority's functions. There is no possibility of former authorised persons being pursued with needless information requests for indefinite periods. In the light of that assurance, I hope that the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs will withdraw the amendment.

Miss Johnson: The clause gives the FSA and the Secretary of State the power to appoint competent persons to carry out investigations in more specific circumstances than under clause 137. It gives the Financial Services Authority and the Secretary of State the ability to look into possible contraventions by authorised and unauthorised persons.

This power is therefore appropriate where circumstances suggest that an authorised person has contravened a particular rule. Equally this power is exercisable where there are grounds for thinking that an unauthorised person may have breached the general prohibition on carrying on regulated activity without authorisation, or committed the holding out offence under clause 22, or breached the financial promotion prohibition under clause 19.

The power also captures behaviours and breaches which could be committed by either authorised or unauthorised persons, such as market abuse, misleading the FSA, making misleading statements or engaging in misleading practices, breaches of the money laundering regulations and insider dealing.

Finally this clause covers investigations in support of the approved persons regime under Part V. So an investigation under this clause can look into whether an individual is no longer fit and proper to be approved, or has failed to comply with a statement of principle under clause 61. The clause is currently drafted on the basis that the trigger for these investigation powers is where the authority or the Secretary of State has

    ``reasonable grounds for suspecting that''

one of the things listed in subsections (1) or (2) may have happened.

However, we have concluded that this wording imposes an unnecessarily high test for launching an investigation. It is very similar to the level of suspicion that a policeman must have to carry out an arrest, and which would often be met only after some initial investigation. This is too high a test if we want to have a regulatory system that responds quickly and effectively to possible wrongdoing. We make no apologies for wanting to be sure that investigations can take place as and when the circumstances suggest that they are needed. Nothing is gained by having a regulator that cannot respond quickly. Nothing is gained by encouraging wrongdoers to seek to prevent the launch of inquiries through procedural challenges in the courts.
