Before we start, I should make it plain that a number of these amendments relate to schedule 5, not clause 33. For the convenience of debate, we are taking them under clause 33, but they will, of course, be voted on formally when we reach schedule 5.

Miss Johnson: We have a quite formidable group of amendments to deal with. It will help everyone if I separate them into slightly smaller groups. First, however, I shall try to explain what we hope to achieve with schedule 5. I should warn the Committee that what follows is necessarily rather technical—especially so for 9 am on a Thursday.

Schedule 5 provides for the automatic authorisation of certain persons concerned in collective investment schemes in two particular cases. The first is persons connected to incoming passporting collective investment schemes under the UCITS directive. When those schemes meet the conditions set out in clause 235 in part XVI—that the scheme operators have notified the Financial Services Authority at least two months in advance they intend to promote the scheme in the UK, and that they have provided the FSA with a certificate from the home state regulator to confirm that the schemes comply with the UCITS directive requirements—they are therefore allowed to exercise their passporting rights under that directive; and schedule 5 gives automatic authorisation to their operators, trustees or depositories.

The provision is necessary because the passport rights under the UCITS directive attach to the scheme itself and, for those rights to be effectively enjoyed, we must ensure that the operators of such schemes can act in relation to the scheme without breaching the general prohibition.

The second case is of operators of authorised British open-ended investment companies—OEICs—which are affectionately called ``oiks''. The reason for that, too, is technical: an OEIC is its own operator. The OEIC is authorised as a product under part XVI; but, because, as operator, it will be carrying out regulated activities, it needs authorisation also as a person, or it will be in breach of the general prohibition.

Schedule 5 provides for the operator to get automatic authorisation, thereby avoiding the need for the same corporate body to have to go through two authorisation processes. The authorisation of the OEIC as a product will involve an assessment of the fitness and properness of the operator to act in that capacity because we believe that it would be unnecessarily burdensome for them to have to go through the separate processes of applying for permission and authorised-person status under parts III and IV of the Bill. Schedule 5 does not at present make that dual effect clear. The purpose of the Government's amendments is to rectify that, and to clarify exactly who gets automatic authorisation under schedule 5 and which activities they have permission to carry out.

As I have said, the schedule concerns two distinct groups. I shall deal first with the persons connected with EEA passporters under the UCITS directive.

Government amendments Nos. 206, 207 and 208 simplify the schedule. They make clear the direct connection between the authorisation of persons connected with, an incoming UCITS scheme and the recognition of such schemes under clause 235. They remove the direct reference to the UCITS directive, which is a necessary change, because that name will become obsolete.

Clause 235 details the requirements that an incoming collective investment scheme must meet in order to exercise its passport rights under the directive. Schemes that meet those requirements will be `recognised'.

Paragraph 2 of schedule 5 unnecessarily duplicates the conditions that a scheme must meet. The amendments remove that duplication and provide simply that, once a scheme has been recognised under clause 235, its operator, trustee or depositary will be automatically authorised.

The amendments also relate to a second category of persons—operators of OEICs. Amendment No. 236 provides that the operator of an authorised OEIC will be authorised. As I explained, the change is necessary to avoid an OEIC being subjected to two separate authorisation processes—one for authorisation as a product and one for authorisation to act as operator.

Amendment No. 209 details the regulated activities for which operators of OEICs will have permission to carry out as a result of their automatic authorisation.

Amendment No. 237 effects the third substantive change—the introduction of a new label, ``Part XVI qualifiers'', to describe persons authorised under schedule 5. Amendment No. 199 deletes the current label, ``UCITS qualifiers'', which will be replaced by ``Part XVI qualifiers''.

The amendments make it clear that schedule 5 relates to other products as well as EEA passporting collective investment schemes. The term ``UCITS qualifiers'' is therefore misleading, and is not sufficiently broad to cover all the categories of people who can automatically be authorised under schedule 5.

The new label refers to ``Part XVI'' rather than ``schedule 5'' because part XVI provides for the requirements that schemes must meet in order to be authorised as products, in the case of OEICs, or to be allowed to exercise their passport rights, in the case of EEA collective investment schemes. Automatic authorisations under schedule 5 will follow.

The rest of the Government amendments—with the exception of Nos. 240 and 211, which align the wording of paragraph 2(3) with that of paragraph 2(2) and clause 48—are consequential to the label change. They remove all references to ``UCITS qualifier'' in and schedule 5 and clauses 33, 45 and 352.

References to ``UCITS qualifier'', will be replaced either with the term ``Part XVI qualifier'', when the intention is to refer to both categories of persons authorised under schedule 5, or, when the intention is to refer only to persons connected to passporting UCITS collective investment schemes, with a reference to

    ``person qualifying for authorisation under paragraph 1(1)''

to schedule 5. I realise that that is not a catchy phrase, but, as I explained, the amendments remove the reference to the UCITS directive, because that name is likely to become obsolete, and replace them with ``Part XVI qualifier''.

I hope that I have made the purpose of the amendments clear, and that, in light of the amendments, Opposition Members will not press amendments Nos. 162, 213 and 214, which relate to provisions to which we are proposing substantial changes. Otherwise, our proceedings will become horribly disorganised.

I should like to say a few words about amendments Nos. 212 and 217, in the hope that Opposition Members will not press them, either. Amendment No. 212 is designed to give the FSA the power to withdraw permission given to operators, trustees or depositaries of incoming passporting UCITS schemes under schedule 5, thereby cancelling their automatic authorisation. However, directive does not enable such a withdrawal.

As I explained, a scheme that meets the requirements for recognition under the UCITS directive may exercise its passporting rights in the UK. Although the FSA has some discretion to prevent such a scheme from doing so, its discretion may be exercised only when a scheme's marketing arrangements do not meet the FSA's prescribed marketing standards.

If the FSA is unhappy with an incoming UCITS scheme, it must appeal to the scheme's home state authority. The home state authority will have the power to prevent the scheme from marketing aboard—that is why the FSA has an express power to stop UK UCITS schemes from exercising their passporting rights to market abroad.

Amendment No. 217 is unnecessary. It seeks to treat the permission that the operator of an authorised OEIC gets under schedule 5 in the same way as a permission under part IV. We assume that the purpose of that is to ensure that the powers in part IV, for example the FSA's power to vary a person's permission on its own initiative, can be applied to operators of authorised OEICs. As I have explained, the operator of an OEIC and the OEIC are one and the same. The power to vary the permission of an operator is therefore the same as being able to vary the terms on which the scheme itself operates. These powers will be provided for in relation to OEICs under the regulations to be made under clause 233. Members of the Committee will see that part XVI already provides the FSA with various powers in relation to unit trust schemes—for example, powers to revoke authorisation and to intervene. I assure the Committee that the regulations relating to OEICs under clause 233 will provide for a similar set of powers. I hope that hon. Members will withdraw their amendments.
