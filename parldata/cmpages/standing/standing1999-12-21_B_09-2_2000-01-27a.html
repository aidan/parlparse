Mr. Mike O'Brien: The Government agree that greater openness is necessary, especially in Whitehall, and in many other public authorities, too, but it must be balanced with other requirements. I have often listed those requirements, which, broadly speaking, fall into three categories—the need for an element of privacy, which may be relevant in this case; the need for an element of commercial confidentiality, which, again, may be relevant in this case, but is probably more properly covered by other clauses; and the effectiveness of proper public administration for the public good.

The assumption that freedom of information has a monopoly on righteousness is not sustainable. Freedom of information is right and proper, and we currently have too much secrecy in our public authorities. However, as all members of the Committee would accept, there are circumstances in which an element of Government efficiency is necessary and that require that certain matters should not, at least initially, come into the public arena. The right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross was right to say that government cannot be conducted in the atmosphere of a fish bowl. It is a matter of getting the balance right; the question is how that is done.

The Liberal Democrats have tabled an amendment that seeks to do two things. The first is to insert a test of objectivity, rather than reasonableness. There are problems with that, however, and I shall attempt to distinguish between the two tests. A reasonableness test is one where a public authority, which is given a range of decisions that it could reasonably and properly choose, is judged on whether it chose reasonably. The objectivity test is different and more problematic, in the context of the decisions with which we are dealing. It requires the official or the elected person taking the decision to consider whether someone else would take the same view of what constitutes reasonableness. The commissioner would have to consider whether the decision was objectively correct, substituting her view of the balance of the argument for the view of the person properly elected to make that decision. That is a worrying test. I would not go so far as to call it profoundly undemocratic; that would be overstating the case, because there are circumstances in which having an objective test by an unelected person has merit—judges are an obvious example. However, many decisions taken by public authorities are taken in a representative way and are not necessarily based on an entirely objective view of what the correct decision should be—they may be based on an ideological view, for example. Those who face a range of decisions on the issue of freedom of information should be able to ask themselves whether their choice of whether something should be disclosed is reasonable, given the range of reasonable options available, none of which is outrageous or irrational.

However, the test that puts someone in the position of having to judge what someone else might regard as objective is somewhat different and much more difficult to apply properly. We should have a test of reasonableness that is made by someone qualified to make it, so not just any old person makes that judgment of reasonableness but a person who has some locus or ability to make the decision—someone put in that position by reason of his capability. Although we may question the capabilities of some who are elected—as Members of Parliament that is our prerogative, as it is the prerogative of any citizen—the public has chosen them.

We have taken the view that the reasonable opinion of a qualified person set out in subsection (2) is the better and clearer test. The amendment would remove the concept of ``a reasonable opinion'' and leave in place the exemption on the grounds of the effective conduct of public affairs being subject to a similar ``would or would be likely to prejudice'' test as other prejudice test exemptions.

The Government consider that only a qualified person can have a full understanding of the issues involved in the decision-making processes of a public authority. Although the matters covered in clause 34 are arguably less sensitive than those in clause 33, and therefore a class exemption is not justified, we do not consider that it would be right for the prejudice caused by that sort of information to be determined by the commissioner. That would put her in the position of second-guessing every decision. Not only would it impose a large bureaucratic burden on her, it would mean that any judgment that she made would not have been made by a person who was elected to that position, however reasonable the decision might be.

At all stages, decisions must be reasonable. If someone makes a reasonable judgment but the commissioner, in an objective analysis, takes a different view, it might lead to conflict. To some extent, which side to take is a matter of judgment; one could probably argue for either side, but we prefer our approach. In a sense, the reasonable opinion of a qualified person is a compromise.

The commissioner can overturn the decision of the qualified person if she believes the decision to be unreasonable, but not otherwise. Within a range, that person is able to make a decision that is reasonable. If they make an unreasonable decision the commissioner can overturn it, but not if it is within the range of reasonableness.

The Government propose to balance the right to know against other rights—in this case the right of the public to have an effective public administration, but it could be instead a matter of privacy and whether certain information ought to be disclosed. Information about individuals might be requested—for instance, by the press—relating to cases in which a judgment taken by an official or a qualified person was perhaps not right. In matters like those, which in many ways strike at the heart of good administration, a compromise must be reached between the power of the commissioner to order the disclosure of information and effective public administration. The clause seeks to reach that balance.

I accept that it is a compromise and a matter of judging where to draw that line—as it is throughout the Bill. It is a compromise between the rights of privacy and confidentiality and of efficiency and freedom of information. It is all a matter of judgment, but the amendment highlights the nature of that compromise—that in the end one must come down on one side or the other. One of the reasons that I came down on this side is that it is important for people who hold elected office to be able to do their duty and carry out the job that the public put them there to do. Often that means taking tough and unpopular decisions. It may be about disclosing the personal details of an individual who is accused of particular offences. Does one disclose them when the press are demanding to know everything about that person? Where there is a reasonable range of options, the qualified person should approach the matter properly.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: If there is one place where a belt-and-braces approach may be justified, it is that of protecting ministerial collective responsibility. In many ways, that is the bedrock of the Government, of whichever party. It is a safeguard under our constitution that any Minister who makes a decision or speaks on behalf of a Department or the Government as a whole requires the collective consent of other Ministers to make that statement, propound the policy or deliver that decision or to fail to do so. That protection, which is for the citizen as well, must be ensured.

The right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross, who has some experience of such matters, pointed out that Government cannot be conducted in a fishbowl. Collective responsibility cannot be created in a fishbowl, but it must be created. Ministers are expected to advance departmental considerations in any discussion of policy outcome. Those considerations are properly proposed and the process of trying to reconcile them can be protracted. A Government may have to take account of other considerations, not least those of party, in the course of making decisions. There must be discussion and debate about how those can be reconciled before a collective decision is taken, and there is compromise on those matters, but perhaps not always reconciliation.
