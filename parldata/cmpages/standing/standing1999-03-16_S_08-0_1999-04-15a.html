Clause 2

Leave to remain

    `(2A) Only conditions of a kind that could be imposed on leave to remain given under section 3 may be prescribed.'.

Following in the footsteps of the Deputy Prime Minister, I was almost caught on the hop. The amendment is designed to obtain clarification from the Government. Why are the conditions set out in section 3 of the Immigration Act 1971 not specified as a particular requirement under this clause when they are referred to in clause 1? Section 3 of the 1971 Act specifies a particularly restricted set of conditions that can be applied or imposed on leave to remain. They include a time limit, a restriction or prohibition on employment, a requirement to register with the police and/or a requirement not to have recourse to public funds.

Clause 1 clearly states that the conditions can be applied on leave to enter, but I was concerned to find that they do not appear in clause 2, which relates to leave to remain. As it stands, I understand that the Secretary of State could impose a variety of different conditions way beyond those specified in section 3 of the 1971 Act. His ability to impose such conditions would not in any way be subject to parliamentary scrutiny and Parliament would not be able to determine what they involve in the same way as it did with the conditions in the 1971 Act.

I hope that the Government will accept the amendment and clarify that the conditions that apply to leave to remain will only be those specified in section 3 of the 1971 Act. If the Government do not wish to accept the amendment, will the Minister tell us whether the Government intend to go beyond the scope of those conditions or whether the clause will allow only the conditions that I have described to be applied? It would be a major issue if the law were changed to allow a new set of conditions to be applied without any form of parliamentary scrutiny. We should be able to consider whether we wish to allow the Secretary of State such power.

I shall keep my remarks brief, as we simply seek clarification. It may be that the wording of the Bill has given rise to these concerns and the Government do not intend to make substantive changes and impose different conditions on leave to enter. The organisations that I referred to want the statute to make clear what conditions can be imposed on leave to remain. Will they be the same as at present or do the Government intend to introduce new conditions?

Under section 3 of the 1971 Act the conditions that can be imposed on somebody granted leave to remain are a time limit, a restriction or prohibition on employment, a requirement to register with the police and a requirement not to have recourse to public funds while the leave to remain in this country subsists. Do the Government envisage that the order will contain different conditions? If so, that would put a different complexion on matters. The explanatory note does not pave the way for new conditions, but refers to the purpose of the clause as being

    ``to allow greater flexibility in the way in which foreign nationals . . . are notified of the leave to remain that they have been granted and the conditions . . . and time limits attached to that leave.''

Without going into the matter at too great length, we simply ask whether the Government intend to impose new conditions or to impose the present conditions in a more flexible and technological way.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): Clause 1 provides for a new section 3A to be inserted in the Immigration Act 1971. Subsection (3) of the new section would allow the Secretary of State to lay an order before Parliament to provide that, in specified circumstances, a visa or entry clearance is to have effect as leave to enter. An order under subsection (3) might provide, for example, for the number of times that a visa is to have effect as leave to enter and the conditions that apply to leave to enter.

Under subsection (5) of new section 3A only conditions of a kind that may be imposed on leave to enter under subsection (3) may be prescribed. Subsection (3) is concerned wholly with the situation where visa or other entry clearance might also act as leave to enter. We wish to restrict the conditions that might be imposed by the entry clearance officer when granting a visa as leave to enter. Subsection (5) of the new section 3A inserted by clause 1 coveres that. It limits the entry clearance officer to the conditions that might be imposed by an immigration officer at the port of arrival.

The restrictions on conditions to be imposed in the case of a visa also granting leave to enter apply in a limited and specific context. Clause 2 deals with a different and wider issue—the granting refusal and variation of leavev to remain—and covers a more varied range of circumstances. It is therefore wholly inappropriate to mix the circumstances in clauses 1 and 2. The leave to remain decision will be made by those workers in the United Kingdom and it is appropriate that the full range of conditions is available.

For example, in due course a requirement could be inserted that a person puts a smart card in a slot on returning to the UK after a period abroad, where the leave does not lapse on leaving the UK. The clause gives us the flexibility that we need. We do not seek to impose different conditions from those now imposed on people who have leave to remain, other than those brought about by new technology. People may have leave to remain to attend a college course which might go beyond the time restriction on employment and registering with the police. We may want to add a condition that they should attend their course, but for the moment we do not seek to introduce it in the clause. It would have to be done through a change in immigration rules, or through other provisions.

Mr. Clappison: We are coming to the point. Does the Minister agree with my analysis of the current restrictions on conditions that can be imposed on people granted leave to remain—this is the view of the expert organisations—and does he propose changing the type of conditions?

Mr. O'Brien: We do not at present propose to change the conditions that can be imposed by immigration rules. However, we want to be able to accommodate any changes in the way in which technology enables us to impose conditions. If, for example, a person had leave to enter and to remain, and we wanted to introduce a new way of ensuring continuance of that leave, we would want to be able, through the clause, to introduce new regulations to facilitate that. In a sense, it would be a new condition as the new method would be part of the process by which a person who had received the permission would be able to extend it. We are almost at cross purposes because what worried the hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam, and to some extent perhaps the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) was whether a possible series of new conditions would restrict a person's ability to do something which they are currently able to do. That is not our intention. We intend to enable ourselves to impose conditions that may be necessary to meet new technology. We do not know what they are because we do not know the nature of the new technology that might develop, but we want the Bill to give us the discretion to introduce orders at a later stage to allow us to take account of new technology. The hon. Member for Hallam need not concern himself with fears about lots of new conditions that would effectively restrict the liberty of persons given leave to remain. However, new technology may have implications on the conditions under which a person may remain. As I understand it, the hon. Gentleman's fears relate to new conditions being imposed on a person's freedom. That is not what we propose.

Mr. Allan: That is a useful assurance. However, from my reading of the Bill, the Minister can make an order providing for the imposition of such conditions. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Hoon: Hon Members will have noticed that clause 4 is one of the most important elements of the Bill, which is no doubt why the Home Office has entrusted it to a Minister from the Lord Chancellor's Department.

Clause 4 is intended to prevent foreign nationals from taking a job in a diplomatic mission in an attempt to evade our immigration control. Before 1988 any foreign national who worked at a diplomatic mission was exempt from immigration control. It was discovered that immigration offenders subject to enforcement action were using such employment to avoid being removed from the United Kingdom. The Immigration Act 1971 was therefore changed in 1988.

Under section 8(3) of the Immigration Act 1971, a person is exempt from immigration control as long as he or she is employed as a member of a diplomatic mission. Section 8(3A) of the 1971 Act, which was inserted by the Immigration Act 1988, provides that foreign nationals already here who are offered a job at a diplomatic mission, remain subject to immigration control provided that they remain in this country. We describe such individuals as locally engaged staff.

However, the current wording of Subsection (3A) of the 1971 Act means that if a locally engaged member of staff leaves the United Kingdom and then re-enters, he or she becomes exempt from immigration control. That is because they will have entered the United Kingdom as a member of a mission.

Foreign nationals seeking to evade immigration control have exploited that loophole in the past. Clause 4 will close it by making it necessary to be resident outside the United Kingdom, and not present in the United Kingdom, when a post at a mission is offered, in order that the section 8(3) exemption can apply. The clause will also prevent a person from benefiting from the exemption if he has ceased to be a member of the mission and is then re-employed there, unless the subsequent re-employment satisfies the requirements of the clause.

Foreign nationals seeking to evade immigration control have exploited that loophole in the past and the two amendments seek to close that gap in the law. Amendment No. 5 replaces ``person'' with ``member of a mission''. It is straightforward, clarifying precisely to whom subsection (3A) applies.

Clause 4 allows a person who entered the United Kingdom as a visitor to obtain a post at a diplomatic mission and benefit from the exempt provisions of section 8 of the 1971 Act. Although the persons would be living in the United Kingdom, they would ordinarily be resident elsewhere and therefore fall within the terms of the clause. Paragraph (a) of amendment No. 6 makes it clear than an individual must be not only resident outside the United Kingdom but physically outside it when offered a post at a diplomatic mission.

Paragraph (b) is intended to prevent a further abuse. When a person is offered a post while resident outside and physically outside the United Kingdom, he or she becomes a member of a mission and thus exempt from control. When such persons leave the mission they cease to benefit from exempt status. However, if they remain in the United Kingdom and are subsequently employed by the mission, without paragraph (b), such people could claim exempt status in respect of the subsequent post, even though the circumstances surrounding the appointment did not satisfy the requirements of the new subsection (3A). As another means of getting around the requirements of paragraph (a), that would be undesirable. I therefore invite the Committee to support the amendments.
