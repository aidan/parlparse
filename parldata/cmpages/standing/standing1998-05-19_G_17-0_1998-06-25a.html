Mr. Griffiths: I welcome you, Mr. Atkinson, to what I trust will be the final day of the Committee's proceedings.

New Clause 13 is obviously important. I intend to set out its rationale in some detail. I shall be pleased to deal with any serious points raised after I have concluded my initial remarks.

The new clause allows the Government to provide for vertical agreements and land agreements. For the benefit of the Committee, I shall outline the Government's thinking in respect of those types of agreement and how we intend to use the powers to be granted under the new clause.

I shall deal first with vertical agreements. We have said on several occasions that there is significant merit in dealing with vertical agreements as a whole under the chapter I prohibition. The power proposed in the new clause is important, as it will ensure that we are able to provide a regime that is of real practical value to business. We have discussed that approach with the Confederation of British Industry, which supports the new clause.

The Bill already includes provisions to make exclusions, but as we said on Second Reading, we have been considering whether additional flexibility will be required.

The vertical agreements task force has met several times during the past few months to consider that question. Working with the CBI, the Office of Fair Trading and the Monopolies and Mergers Commission, we concluded that the Bill's existing power to make exclusions may not be adequate to provide special treatment for vertical agreements treatment that is legally sound yet of real practical value to business. The new clause will enable appropriate treatment to be given specifically to vertical agreements. However, we do not propose to give special treatment to price fixing vertical agreements.

I shall explain the background to the need for additional flexibility. Vertical agreements cannot easily be compared with the other categories of agreement mentioned in schedule 3. Vertical agreements are often part of an overall network, such as a hamburger chain franchise agreement. It could be said that each agreement in an arrangement is made in contemplation of the others, as each dealer signs up to join the network.

The problem with vertical agreements is that they may be closely linked with horizontal agreements in an overall arrangement. We do not want the benefits to be lost simply because there is a series of vertical agreements of a similar kind in an overall arrangement. Flexibility over and above the powers in clause 3 is desirable to spell that out, for the certainty of business.

We have been considering with the task force how that power should be used. There will be advantage in providing for greater flexibility than is currently available under clause 3. It would enable us to prescribe more clearly than may be the case for straightforward exclusions how the Bill will apply to overall arrangements that include both vertical and horizontal agreements. The effect of using that power would be to ensure that parties to vertical agreements would not be subject to penalties for infringement of the prohibition. It would ensure that vertical agreements are not at risk of being rendered void because they are said to infringe the prohibition. Finally, it would ensure that vertical agreements would not be subject to third-party action in the courts. I shall deal with the question of safeguards against anti-competitive vertical agreements a little later.

Specifying how the Bill is to operate on vertical agreements makes it clear that we are not trying to change the way in which the prohibition itself should be interpreted.

As members of the Committee may know, the European Commission has been conducting a review of the treatment of vertical restraints. We have recently received a draft paper from the Commission that sets out the findings of that review. The paper contains descriptions of vertical agreements that could form the basis for a definition of vertical agreements that would benefit from the special treatment under the chapter I prohibition. The paper has not been made officially public by the Commission but copies are circulating widely in the business and legal community. Indeed, the hon. Member for Daventry (Mr. Boswell) referred to it earlier in our proceedings. The Commission is still working on those proposals. However, one definition used in the papers that we are considering is that of

    "agreements between two or more undertakings, each operating on a different economic level, in respect of the marketing of goods or services".

We are considering with the task force whether the general descriptions of vertical agreements contained in those papers could be copied, at least form the basis for the description of vertical agreements that would qualify for the special treatment under the chapter I prohibition. There may be merit in using the same language as that proposed by the Commission. Many UK businesses are already subject to EC competition law and it might be burdensome for them to apply two different tests at the EC and UK level when deciding whether they come within the scope of the UK and EC prohibitions. That is a matter we are actively considering with the task force.

The general description of vertical agreements proposed by the Commission are wide ranging. The existing EC block exemptions cover only vertical agreements for resale. The proposed EC definitions are far wider, covering service supply arrangements and supply agreements where the purchaser does not resell the products but processes them into another product, such as flour into bread. We do not read the draft definition proposed by the Commission as covering licences of pure intellectual property rights. We do not believe that there is a need to cover such agreements with the special treatment proposed. Licensing of intellectual property rights has been subject to article 85 in the UK for more than 25 years. A licence to use intellectual property rights, as such, is not restrictive it grants a freedom.

The European Commission proposal for a new wide-ranging block exemption is, however, subject to numerous qualifications. For example, the block exemption would not be available where the parties' market share exceeds a certain percentage and various restrictive terms do not qualify for the block exemption. That is in recognition of the fact that not all vertical agreements are benign; indeed, some can cause serious competition concerns.

We do not intend to copy all the various qualifications and conditions that the EC proposes. Nevertheless, we recognise that some vertical agreements can cause serious competition concerns that might not be readily dealt with by reliance on the chapter II prohibition or the complex monopoly provisions of the Fair Trading Act 1973. Clever lawyers may also test the terms of the special treatment and squeeze agreements within it which were not intended to benefit. In particular, we do not propose to give special treatment to price-fixing vertical agreements.

We believe that it is essential that the director general is able to claw back individual agreements, within the scope of the chapter I prohibition to examine them. It is the quid pro quo for a wide-ranging exclusion. That would be on similar terms to the power of clawback that already exists for many of the exclusions in the Bill. The test would be whether the director general considered the agreement to be subject to the prohibition in the absence of special treatment and was not likely to grant an unconditional exemption. If an agreement has such serious effects that it does not merit an exemption, it is absolutely right that the director general should be able to terminate the special treatment.

Once the agreement was clawed back the parties would be able to argue the case for an exemption before the director general. If he ultimately ruled that an exemption was not merited, the parties could appeal against the decision to the Competition Commission. The power of clawback by the director general is provided in subsection (3). We would expect the order to deal with such matters as the power to obtain information about the agreement and the giving of notice.

Mr. Griffiths: I can give my hon. Friend an assurance that there will be consultation on that matter. Indeed, we will consult the National Consumer Council on it.

I come now to land agreements. Similar considerations to those that arise in relation to vertical agreements arise in relation to land agreements. We do not believe that land agreements, so far as they relate to the creation of transfer of an interest in land, will be likely to have any appreciable effect on competition. There may be more concern about some covenants as to how land may be used, or which limit the freedom of the tenant in other ways, but the Government's view is that land agreements are unlikely to raise competition concerns.

We are, however, aware of concerns about exactly how the chapter I prohibition may apply to land agreements, and the effect of potential voidness on the property market. If we do not treat land agreements differently, we fear that uncertainty as to the application of the chapter I prohibition will lead to unnecessary notification of agreements and uncertainty in the property sector. Under article 85, the lack of clarity has been ameliorated by the general expectation that restrictions in land agreements are unlikely to affect trade between member states and hence to fall foul of article 85. That limitation will, of course, not apply under the chapter I prohibition.

We therefore believe that the powers of the new clause in relation to land agreements will enhance the clarity of the application of the prohibition, reduce the burden on business of unnecessary notifications and enable the better concentration of regulatory resources on areas of competition concern, without undermining the effectiveness of the prohibition regime itself.

I commend the new clause to the Committee.

I was surprised by the Under-Secretary's decision to omit intellectual property altogether on the grounds that an intellectual property agreement was unlikely to be anti-competitive. It is quite possible for such an agreement to be anti-competitive. Indeed, those who wish to challenge in the marketplace would say that such agreements are almost always anti-competitive.

The case for such an agreement is that the public interest is served by protecting an individual's intellectual property for a period of time. Such protection makes it worth that person's while to go the extra mile and apply, for example, for patent protection. The law recognises that companies and individuals need to conduct a great deal of research and to spend considerable sums up front before money can be recouped from the market. Through the patent, a period of time is therefore given for the exclusive exploitation of intellectual property.

One also finds anti-competitive elements in intellectual property agreements between companies. For example, it is common practice for a company that has developed a particular idea to discover in the marketplace that the large customer that it wishes to sign up refuses to be supplied exclusively by one producer. The solution is to license one rival, not four or five rivals making it harder to make a profit in return for the effort put in.

Why is the Under-Secretary not worried about such agreements? Part IV

On and after the starting date

Chapter I

General

    Duty of Director to maintain register etc.

    10. (1) This paragraph applies even though the relevant provisions of the RTPA are repealed by this Act.

    (b) which fall within sub-paragraph (4);

    (c) which immediately before the starting date are the subject of proceedings under the RTPA which do not cease on that date by virtue of this Schedule; or

    (i) one to which the RTPA applied:

    (ii) subject to registration under that Act;

    (iii) a non-notifiable agreement for the purposes of that Act.

    (3) The Director is to continue on and after the starting date to be under the duties imposed by section 1(2)(a) and (b) of the RTPA of compiling a register of agreements and entering or filing certain particulars in the register, but only in respect of agreements of a kind referred to in paragraph (b), (c) or (d) of sub-paragraph (2).

    (c) as at the starting date no entry or filing has been made in the register in respect of the agreement.

    (5) Sections 23 and 27 of the RTPA are to apply after the starting date in respect of the register subject to such modification, if any, as may be prescribed.

    (6) In sub-paragraph (2)(d) "court" means

    (a) the High Court;

    (b) the Court of Appeal;

    (c) the Court of Session;

    (d) the High court or Court of Appeal in Northern Ireland; or

    (e) the House of Lords.

    (a) in relation to a continuing application under that section; or

    (b) so as to allow an application to be made under that section on or after the starting date in respect of a continuing application under section 1(3) of the RTPA.

    (2) "Continuing application" means an application made, but not determined, before the starting date.

    RTPA section 26 applications

    (2) If an application under section 26 is determined on or after the starting date, this Schedule has effect in relation to the agreement concerned as if the application had been determined immediately before that date.

(1) Even though section 35 of the RTPA is repealed by this Act, its provisions (and so far as necessary that Act) are to continue to apply in respect of a person who, immediately before the starting date, has a right by virtue of section 27ZA or 35(2) of that Act to bring civil proceedings in respect of an agreement (but only so far as that right relates to any period before the starting date or, where there are continuing proceedings, the determination of the proceedings).

    (2) Even though section 25 of the RPA is repealed by this Act, the provisions of that section (and so far as necessary that Act) are to continue to apply in respect of a person who, immediately before the starting date, has a right by virtue of subsection (3) of that section to bring civil proceedings (but only so far as that right relates to any period before the starting date or, where there are continuing proceedings, the determination of the proceedings).

Chapter II

Continuing Proceedings

    The general rule

    14. (1) The Chapter I prohibition does not apply to an agreement at any time when the agreement is the subject of continuing proceedings under the RTPA.

    (2) The Chapter I prohibition does not apply to an agreement relating to goods which are the subject of continuing proceedings under section 16 or 17 or the RPA to the extent to which the agreement consists of exempt provisions.

    (3) In sub-paragraph (2) "exempt provisions" mean those provisions of the agreement which would, disregard section 14 of the RPA, be

    (a) void as a result of section 9(1) of the RPA; or

    (b) unlawful as a result of section 9(2) or 11 of the RPA.

    (4) If the Chapter I prohibition does not apply to an agreement because of this paragraph, the provisions of, or made under, the RTPA or the RPA are to continue to have effect in relation to the agreement.

    (5) The repeals made by section 1 do not affect

    (a) continuing proceedings; or

    (b) proceedings of the kind referred to in paragraph 11 or 12 of this Schedule which are continuing after the starting date.

    Meaning of "continuing proceedings"

    15. (1) For the purposes of this Schedule "continuing proceedings" means proceedings in respect of an application made to the Court under the RTPA or the RPA, but not determined, before the starting date.

    (2) But proceedings to which paragraph 11 or 12 applies are not continuing proceedings.

    (3) The question whether (for the purposes of Part III, or this Part, of this Schedule) an application has been determined is to be decided in accordance with sub-paragraphs (4) and (5).

    (4) If an appeal against the decision on the application is brought, the application is not determined until

    (a) the appeal is disposed of or withdrawn; or

    (b) if as a result of the appeal the case is referred back to the Court

    (i) the expiry of the period within which an appeal ("the further appeal") in respect of the Court's decision on that reference could have been brought had this Act not been passed; or

    (ii) if later, the date on which the further appeal is disposed of or withdrawn.

    (5) Otherwise, the application is not determined until the expiry of the period within which any party to the application would have been able to bring an appeal against the decision on the application had this Act not been passed.

    RTPA section 4 proceedings

    16. Proceedings on an application for an order under section 4 of the RTPA are also continuing proceedings if

    (a) leave to make the application is applied for before the starting date but the proceedings in respect of that application for leave are not determined before that date; or

    (b) leave to make an application for an order under that section is granted before the starting date but the application itself is not made before that date.

    RPA section 16 or 17 proceedings

    17. Proceedings on an application for an order under section 16 or 17 of the RPA are also continuing proceedings if

    (a) leave to make the application is applied for before the starting date but the proceedings in respect of that application for leave are not determined before that date; or

    (b) leave to make an application for an order under section 16 or 17 of the RPA is granted before the starting date, but the application itself is not made before that date.

    Continuing proceedings which are discontinued

    18. (1) On an application made jointly to the Court by all the parties to any continuing proceedings, the Court must, if it is satisfied that the parties wish it to do so, discontinue the proceedings.

    (2) If, on an application under sub-paragraph (1) or for any other reason, the Court orders the proceedings to be discontinued, this Schedule has effect (subject to paragraphs 21 and 22) from the date on which the proceedings are discontinued as if they had never been instituted.

Chapter III

The transitional period

    The general rule

    19. (1) Except where this Chapter or Chapter IV provides otherwise, there is a transitional period, beginning on the starting date and lasting for one year, for any agreement made before the starting date.

    (2) The Chapter I prohibition does not apply to an agreement to the extent to which there is a transitional period for the agreement.

    (3) The Secretary of State may by regulations provide for sections 13 to 16 and Schedule 5 to apply with such modifications (if any) as may be specified in the regulations, in respect of applications to the Director about agreements for which there is a transitional period.

    Cases for which there is no transitional period

    20. (1) There is no transitional period for an agreement to the extent to which, immediately before the starting date, it is

    (a) void under section 2(1) or 35(1)(a) of the RTPA;

    (b) the subject of an order under section 2(2) or 35(3) of the RTPA; or

    (c) unlawful under section 1, 2 or 11 of the RPA or void under section 9 of that Act.

    (2) There is no transitional period for an agreement to which paragraph 25(4) applies.

    (3) There is no transitional period for

    (a) an agreement in respect of which there are continuing proceedings, or

    (b) an agreement relating to goods in respect of which there are continuing proceedings,

    to the extent to which the agreement is, when the proceedings are determined, void or unlawful.

    Continuing proceedings under the RTPA

    21. In the case of an agreement which is the subject of continuing proceedings under the RTPA, the transitional period begins

    (a) if the proceedings are discontinued, on the date of discontinuance;

    (b) otherwise, when the proceedings are determined.

    Continuing proceedings under the RPA

    22. In the case of an agreement relating to goods which are the subject of continuing proceedings under the RPA, the transitional period begins

    (a) if the proceedings are discontinued, on the date of discontinuance;

    (b) otherwise, when the proceedings are determined.

    Provisions not contrary to public interest

    23. (1) To the extent to which an agreement contains provisions which, immediately before the starting date, are provisions which the Court has found not to be contrary to the public interest, the transitional period lasts for five years.

    (2) Sub-paragraph (1) is subject to paragraph 20(3).

    (3) To the extent to which an agreement which on the starting date is the subject of continuing proceedings is, when the proceedings are determined, found by the Court not to be contrary to the public interest, the transitional period lasts for five years.

    Goods

    24. (1) In the case of an agreement relating to goods which, immediately before the starting date, are exempt under section 14 of the RPA, there is a transitional period for the agreement to the extent to which it consists of exempt provisions.

    (2) Sub-paragraph (1) is subject to paragraph 20(3).

    (3) In the case of an agreement relating to goods

    (a) which on the starting date are the subject of continuing proceedings, and

    (b) which, when the proceedings are determined, are found to be exempt under section 14 of the RPA,

    there is a transitional period for the agreement, to the extent to which it consists of exempt provision.

    (4) In each case, the transitional period lasts for five years.

    (5) In sub-paragraphs (1) and (3) "exempt provisions" means those provisions of the agreement which would, disregarding section 14 of the RPA, be

    (a) void as a result of section 9(1) of the RPA; or

    (b) unlawful as a result of section 9(2) or 11 of the RPA.

    Transitional period for certain agreements

    25. (1) This paragraph applies to agreements

    (a) which are subject to registration under the RTPA but which

    (i) are not non-notifiable agreements within the meaning of section 27A of the RTPA, or

    (ii) are not agreements to which paragraph 5 applies; and

    (b) in respect of which the time for furnishing relevant particulars as required by or under the RTPA expires on or after the starting date.

    (2) "Relevant particulars" means

    (a) particulars which are required to be furnished by virtue of section 24 of the RTPA; or

    (b) particulars of any variation of an agreement which are required to be furnished by virtue of sections 24 and 27 of the RTPA.

    (3) There is a transitional period of one year for an agreement to which this paragraph applies if

    (a) relevant particulars are furnished before the starting date; and

    (b) no person has acted unlawfully (for the purposes of section 27ZA(2) or (3) of the RTPA) in respect of the agreement.

    (4) If relevant particulars are not furnished by the starting date, section 35(1)(a) of the RTPA does not apply in relation to the agreement (unless sub-paragraph (5) applies).

    (5) This sub-paragraph applies if a person falling within section 27ZA(2) or (3) of the RTPA has acted unlawfully for the purposes of those subsections in respect of the agreement.

    Special cases

    26. (1) In the case of an agreement in respect of which

    (a) a direction under section 127(2) of the Financial Services Act 1986 ("the 1986 Act") is in force immediately before the starting date, or

    (b) a direction under section 194A(3) of the Broadcasting Act 1990 ("the 1990 Act") is in force immediately before the starting date.

    the transitional period lasts for five years.

    (2) To the extent to which an agreement is the subject of a declaration

    (a) made by the Treasury under section 127(3) of the 1986 Act, and

    (b) in force immediately before the starting date,

    the transition period lasts for five years.

    (3) Sub-paragraphs (1) and (2) do not affect the power of

    (a) the Treasury to make a declaration under section 127(2) of the 1986 Act (as amended by Schedule 2 to this Act),

    (b) the Secretary of State to make a declaration under section 194A of the 1990 Act (as amended by Schedule 2 of this Act),

    in respect of an agreement for which there is a transitional period.

    Chapter V

    Extending the transitional period

    36. (1) A party to an agreement for which there is a transitional period may apply to the Director, not less than three months before the end of the period, for the period to be extended.

Chapter VI

Terminating the Transitional Period

    General

    37. (1) Subject to sub-paragraph (2), the Director may by a direction in writing terminate the transitional period for an agreement, but only in accordance with paragraph 38.

    (2) The Director may not terminate the transitional period, nor exercise any of the powers in paragraph 38, in respect of an agreement which is excluded from the Chapter I prohibition by virtue of any of the provisions of Part I of this Act other than paragraph 1 of Schedule 1 or paragraph 2 or 9 of Schedule 3.

    Circumstances in which the Director may terminate the transitional period

    38. (1) If the Director is considering whether to give a direction under paragraph 37 ("a direction"), he may in writing require any party to the agreement concerned to give him such information in connection with that agreement as he may require.

    (2) If the end of such period as may be specified in rules made under section 51, a person has failed, without reasonable excuse, to comply with a requirement imposed under sub-paragraph (1), the Director may give a direction.

    (3) The Director may also give a direction if he considers

    (a) that the agreement would, but for the transitional period or a relevant exclusion, infringe the Chapter I prohibition; and

    (b) that he would not be likely to grant the agreement an unconditional individual exemption.

    (4) For the purposes of sub-paragraph (3) an individual exemption is unconditional if no conditions or obligations are imposed in respect of it under section 4(3)(a).

    (5) In this paragraph

    "person" has the same meaning as in Part I;

    "relevant exclusion" means an exclusion under paragraph 1 of Schedule 1 or paragraph 2 or 9 of Schedule 3.

    Procedural requirements on giving a paragraph 37 direction

    39. (1) The Director must specify in a direction under paragraph 37 ("a direction") the date on which it is to have effect (which must not be less than 28 days after the direction is given).

    (2) Copies of the direction must be given to

    (a) each of the parties concerned, and

    (b) the Secretary of State,
