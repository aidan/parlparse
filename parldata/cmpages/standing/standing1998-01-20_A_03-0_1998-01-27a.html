Mr. Don Foster (Bath): I beg to move amendment No. 4 in page 2, line 4, at end insert

    "(5) The LEA and the governing body shall exercise their functions in relation to subsection (4) so that no non-infant class in the school is thereby disadvantaged.".

As the first to speak this morning, Mr. Gale, may I welcome you to the Chair. I am sure that the wise guidance given by Mr. McWilliam will be continued by you.

I intend to be brief. The amendment is important. When debating the previous group of amendments, both sides agreed that class size matters and that our desire was to see class sizes reduced. The Committee also accepted that although it is important to focus our attention initially on class sizes in key stage 1 the infant years it is also important to have regard to class sizes in key stage 2. Indeed, we referred to evidence showing that class size matters for pupils of all ages in primary and secondary education.

The purpose of the amendment is to ensure that reductions in class size at key stage 1 do not adversely affect class sizes at key stage 2. The amendment would ensure that, in reducing class sizes in key stage 1, local education authorities and governing bodies would not increase class sizes at key stage 2.

I remind the Committee that, at column 78 of the report of our previous sitting, the Minister said:

    Later, when we discuss amendment No. 5 to clause 2, which has been tabled by the hon. Member for Bath, we shall be giving assurances that we shall monitor and take steps to guarantee that children in junior years are not affected detrimentally by our policy."

I hope that, when replying to this brief introduction, the Minister or his hon. Friend the Under-Secretary will say what steps the Department intends to take to guarantee that children at key stage 2 will not be detrimentally affected. However, it will not be easy because of the difficulties to be faced at key stage 2. It is interesting to note that, at column 79, the Minister said:

    Obviously, as the class size pledge rolls on year by year, it will benefit eight, nine and 10-year-olds." [Official Report, Standing Committee A, 11 January 1998; c. 78 and 79.]

That may or may not be the case. Even if the Government want to see class sizes at key stage 2 reduced, it is possible that the measure will have a detrimental affect. For example, we are well aware that an appeals panel could require a school at key stage 2 to take additional pupils. That may be particularly likely at popular schools, because parents will have tried to get their children into the school at key stage 1 but failed as a result of the class size limit, and will therefore have another go at key stage 2. Admissions appeals panels decide cases at key stage 2 on a different set of rules.

The funding arrangements for key stage 2 will also be different. Under most local management of schools formula, the smallest amount of money per child is spent at key stage 2. There could be a problem in that, given their limited resources, schools might not be able to reduce class sizes at key stage 2, even if the Government want them to do so. Therefore, there could be a squeeze on key stage 2.

In several other circumstances, pressure could be put on key stage 2 because of the policy of reducing class sizes at key stage 1 without the comment that we seek in the amendment. I shall be brief to give the Under-Secretary an opportunity to provide the assurances that we seek. If she does not give us those assurances, we will press the amendment to a Division.
