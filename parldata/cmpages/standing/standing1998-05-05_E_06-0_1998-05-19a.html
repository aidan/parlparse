Clause 32

Unrelieved surplus advance corporation tax

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): I beg to move, amendment No. 31, in page 18, line 39, at end insert

    `such provisions to consist of a simple regime limiting the amount of brought forward surplus ACT which can be offset against a company's mainstream corporation tax liability in any year to 15 per cent. of that company's corporation tax profits.'.

The amendment would introduce some simplicity into the Bill, which is riddled with enormous complexity. It is an attempt by outside bodies and Opposition Members to encourage the Government to consider how the measures will be implemented in practice and how business will cope with them. The regulations that were published last week -- they are complex and run to some 18 pages--set out the shadow advance corporation system that will replace the current system. Many outside commentators believe that the new system is too complex and even tougher than the current regime. As I said at our previous sitting, Robert Turnbull of KPMG has stated that

    ``The regulations contain tougher rules than the present system and some companies might find them difficult to use.''

The amendment would replace the plethora of 18 pages covering the new shadow ACT system and in its place would limit the amount of surplus ACT that can be offset in any one year to 15 per cent. of the company's mainstream corporation tax profits. To me and outside bodies, that seems a much simpler method. The Confederation of British Industry believes that that figure should be 10 per cent. and the Chartered Institute of Taxation believes that the new shadow ACT regime is both unfair and unnecessarily complex and that the figure should be 15 per cent. The system proposed in the amendment would be fair.

A number of companies in this country together have £7 billion of surplus ACT which they seek to offset against their mainstream corporation tax liability. That is a significant sum which has not emerged from thin air. It is hard cash which, over the years, those companies have paid to the Inland Revenue at the end of the quarter in which they paid a dividend. It is grossly unfair that that money should be difficult to offset against their mainstream corporation tax liability. The previous Government introduced the foreign income dividend scheme to try to help companies with large overseas interests to offset that money against their tax liability. It is unfair to say that that is a cost that they must bear because those companies do an enormous service to this country. They invest overseas, sometimes in risky ventures, and bring enormous income to this country from those ventures. It is wrong to say that we do not want those companies to offset that £7 billion against mainstream corporation tax liability. The Exchequer cannot afford to lose £7 billion of revenue, but that money belongs to companies and we should be trying to ease its recovery over the forthcoming years.

My particular concern is that there is a hidden agenda; that the Exchequer and the Inland Revenue do not want that money to be recovered. It would be helpful if the Paymaster General could confirm that no time expiry limit will be placed on surplus ACT and that the Government do not intend now or in the future to say ``From now on, you have three, four or 10 years in which to reclaim this surplus ACT.'' Indeed, the tax faculty of the Institute of Chartered Accountants has said:

    ``We would welcome confirmation that existing ACT surpluses will be preserved indefinitely.''

The Government have given companies an option: either they can adopt the burden for administering the complex shadow ACT system or they can write off their surplus ACT and not use the new system. That is an invidious option. Companies should not be faced with such a choice. It is their right to recover the money. It should not be a matter of adopting the burden or losing the money. The system should be simple. It should enable companies to recover surplus ACT by following easy administrative arrangements. Companies should not have to make the invidious choice between losing many millions of pounds of surplus ACT and incurring many tens or hundreds of thousands of pounds in administrative costs when they try to reclaim the money.

I urge the Committee to adopt the amendment, which has the backing of outside professional bodies, because it simplifies matters. It would enable companies to recover what is theirs in a fair and equitable system for dealing with the vast £7 billion of surplus ACT. I look forward to hearing what the Paymaster General has to say about the expiry of surplus ACT.

The Paymaster General (Mr. Geoffrey Robinson): I must point out to the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) that the amendment is almost certainly defective, but he knows that. He has tried to make a point that was raised by some companies involved in the FIDs argument which we had last year when we discussed the previous Finance Bill. I am sure that all of us still have vivid memories of that.

It is a little disingenuous for the hon. Gentleman to suggest that his proposal is done in the name of simplicity. The previous Government's partial solution to the problem of surplus ACT was the foreign income dividend which came into effect in 1994. No fewer than 24 additional sections from 246A to 246Y--we almost got to Z--were inserted into the Taxes Act. Many were extraordinarily complex. For example, section 246I dealt with foreign source profit and distributable foreign profit and section 246B rejoiced in the title of notional foreign source advance corporation tax.

We are getting rid of all that. The hon. Gentleman will be pleased to know that we are going to simplify the system in a way that the previous Government's half-baked scheme did not. Their scheme created no end of problems. It was not taken up by many companies because of the tax credits to which they were committed and the bias towards distribution that that involved. Companies did not take up the option on FIDs to anything like the extent that was envisaged.

We have solved the problem of ACT. We are also committed to dealing fairly with surplus ACT held by companies when ACT is abolished next April. The amendment would not do that for two reasons, which I am sure the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton will want to know. Apart from being technically defective, the amendment would prevent some companies from getting back temporary ACT surpluses as quickly as they had anticipated. That would be a double-edged sword, because it would reduce the limit for set off against corporation tax bills from 20 per cent. to 15 per cent. of tax profits. In contrast, our shadow ACT system will substantially preserve companies' existing expectations. Given that £7 billion is theoretically outstanding, such companies cannot realistically expect to recover all their ACT surpluses.

The amendment would also enable other companies with long-standing ACT surpluses, much of which they may have already written off in their accounts, to recover more of it than would be possible under existing rules. That would therefore constitute a relaxation of the rules. Moreover, the cost to the Exchequer could run to several billion pounds over several years. That is simply not on the cards. If the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton were to talk to companies about the matter, he would find that they largely accept the Government's proposals.

Companies that have built up large amounts of surplus ACT are multinationals such as RTZ and Billiton, which we are pleased to say entered the London market in 1997, and which have little in the way of UK profits. Companies with long-standing ACT surpluses, which they have written off because there is no realistic chance of recovering them, are precisely those that will benefit from the abolition of ACT.

Mr. Gibb: The hon. Gentleman says that companies such as RTZ have written off surplus ATC. They may have done so for prudent accounting and auditing purposes, but they hope and expect one day to recover money that they have written off in their minds.

Mr. Geoffrey Robinson: Well, I cannot read their minds. Perhaps the hon. Gentleman, who is a distinguished accountant, is also a distinguished Freudian or Jungian who can penetrate the inner recesses of the minds of industry tycoons. However, the surplus ATC is written off in their books. It would be unfair to benefit such companies still further by spending huge amounts of the Exchequer's money to improve the prospect of recovery of surplus ACT that they have written off.

The amendment would prove arbitrary and unfair, it would be excessively costly to the Exchequer, and it would itself be unfair on other people who pay tax. I therefore urge the Committee to reject it.

 

Mr. Gibb: The Paymaster General did not respond to my key question, about whether the Government plan to introduce an expiry date for surplus ACT. Given his comments about writing off ACT, such companies should be worried that the Inland Revenue might decide in the future that ACT is irrecoverable. It is an important question because ACT is hard cash that companies have paid to the Exchequer.

Mr. Robinson: I apologise to the hon. Gentleman for not mentioning the matter. I can reassure him that, at least for the duration of this Parliament, there will be no time limit. We have given similar commitments on personal and corporate tax matters. However, we beg to differ with his other point.

Mr. Gibb: In view of the confirmation given by the Paymaster General, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Schedule 4

Interest payable under the Tax Acts by or to companies

Mr. Nick St. Aubyn (Guildford): I beg to move amendment No. 36, in page 164, line 35, after `section', insert

    `shall provide that where any company has overpaid tax in an earlier period and underpaid tax in a subsequent period interest on the overpaid tax arising at the same time as interest on the underpaid tax shall be calculated at the same rate and otherwise.'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take the following amendment:

No. 30, in page 164, line 48, at end insert--

    `(8) No interest on overdue tax shall be charged in respect of underpaid quarterly corporation tax payment in circumstances whereby the under-payment resulted from an increase in the corporation tax rate or other changes to the law determining a company's liability to tax.'.

Mr. St. Aubyn: Amendment No. 36 speaks for itself. I am grateful to have been given a copy--which was on general distribution--of the Labour party policy unit document on these clauses. The Government are generous in letting us know their thinking. I need only quote from that document--[Interruption.] It is on the Table, if any hon. Members do not have a copy. It says:

    ``The purpose behind the change is to enable large companies to cope better with instalment payments of corporation tax, by avoiding penalising them if they make honest mistakes over their instalment payments.''

I am delighted that the Committee is of one mind on the purpose of this schedule and related clauses. I assume that the Labour party policy unit is still the mind of the Government, of which there is plenty of evidence in other areas. There is no reason to suppose that that is not the case in this instance.

If an instalment system on a current-year basis is introduced, it is inevitable that companies making such instalments will have to make untested assumptions about what the profits for the period just ended have been. It is in the nature of such a process that, sometimes, they will make an overpayment and then wish to make an underpayment in the following period, so that the total amount of cash taken out of the company is no more than the bare minimum required to meet the new law.

Under self assessment, if an individual makes an overpayment of tax, there is a general mechanism by which, if he makes a subsequent under payment, the discrepancies in the two amounts may be offset. I understand that no such mechanism is available to companies. If a company has overpaid in an earlier period, and then wishes to underpay in a subsequent period and have that acknowledged by the Revenue, the company will have to go through a formal process of getting the Revenue's agreement to the fact that it overpaid in the earlier period before payments for the two periods can be settled up and netted out, one against the other, in the company's account with the Revenue.

That seems to be somewhat unfair on companies, as, in effect, they will be asked to undertake a new process of self assessment in future. Therefore, it is vital not only that the interest that they earn on an earlier overpayment corresponds to the interest that they are charged on a subsequent underpayment, but that the two rates of interest are absolutely equal, so that, as the document from the Labour party policy unit aspires, there is no penalty on the company. At present, as I understand it, the rate of interest that will be paid on an earlier overpayment will still be some 2.5 per cent less than that rate of tax charged on the subsequent underpayment. Given that we are talking about the largest 20,000 corporate tax payers in the country, who account for 85 per cent of all corporation tax revenue, 2.5 per cent will be levied on some very large sums of money. That is a substantial point. The amendment would simply hold the Government true to their declared intentions that there should be no interest penalty in the case where the overpayment happens in an earlier period.

I anticipate that the Paymaster General will rise to say that the Opposition are advocating use of the mechanism of regulations for which we have criticised the Government during the debate. Our response to that is, if there are to be regulations--and we do not favour regulations being used to the extent to which the Government use them--it is appropriate that they should be used to fine tune the mechanism by which interest is levied or paid on tax. That area is sufficiently obscure and minute to be dealt with appropriately by regulations.

When, in a previous debate, we asked the Paymaster General whether there was any area that he did not envisage being covered by regulations, he was stumped for an answer. The Government have a wildly different view on the use of regulations in Finance Acts, but I am sure that we would both agree that in dealing with a complex area such as this, technical regulations can give effect to our intentions, but the intention must be clear. The intention is apparently clear in the Government document from which I have quoted, and the intention is clear in amendment No. 36, which would amend schedule 4, so that those large companies that will have this penal system imposed on them should at least be sure that if they are cautious and slightly overpay in an earlier accounting period, they will not face a further interest penalty of 2.5 per cent for doing so.

Amendment No. 30 seeks to deal with situations where an inaccurate estimate has been made, not because of the difficulties of estimating the current-year profits or current-year tax liability of a company, but because of a change in the law such as a change in the corporation tax rates. Member for Sevenoaks on the issue of transition. I was not really speaking about the transition for big companies from the ACT system, with all its complications, to the in-year instalments basis, but the hon. Gentleman is right, and if he checks the regulations he will find that they contain adequate transitional arrangements, whereby a negative cash flow for companies over that period will be offset against the on-going benefit--a net present value added benefit of around £8 billion from the changes in corporation tax. The general welcome for our proposals suggests that we have got that right.

I was referring not to the transition of big companies from one system to another but to the much more difficult situation in which a small company, approaching the £1.5 million level, suddenly finds itself catapulted into a completely new set of arrangements for in-year instalments. If the hon. Gentleman checks--I think that it is in section 3 of the regulations--he will find that we set out a series of criteria that will apply over a period of time before a company can move from one system to another. Medum-sized companies were taken out of the in-year instalments system as a result of consultation with the CBI and the industry generally. We have made adequate transitional arrangements and, with the one exception of the provisions relating to cash flow, the new system has received widespread support.

The hon. Member for Guildford raised a fair question about the construction industry, but one should consider profit only where it arises. If one starts to consider profit on work in progress before the contract is completed or a clear accounting point in the contract is specified, one gets into terrible problems. That creates tremendous dangers of overstating profit, or understating it, but more probably the former. Therefore, a company should consider profit only when it crystallises--when the contract finishes or reaches a particular stage that is known in advance and which the company will have in its budget. The pattern of profits throughout the year, or for two to three years in the case of a long-term contract, will be set out by the company and it will pay tax in respect of those profits, and not in respect of any irrelevant considerations.

Of course, if the contract goes horribly badly, the company will adjust its profit forecast in relation to the difficulty that it is experiencing with the long-term contract, and pay less tax. If the contract goes especially well and the company uprates its profits on the contract, as and when those profits emerge--or, more naturally, at the end of the contract--the company will pay tax, according to its forecast, when the profit crystallises.

In all those ways, it is clear that the system is workable. In the second year, once the transitional period has been completed, it will yield up to £2 billion to companies with a large net present value. In the interim, there is an element of negative cash flow because, as I have said before, that is a one-off effect. The benefit of the reduction in corporation tax is an on-going benefit, and as the Government have committed themselves to those levels of corporation tax for the whole of this Parliament, it is a pro-business Budget and a pro-business reform of corporation tax.
