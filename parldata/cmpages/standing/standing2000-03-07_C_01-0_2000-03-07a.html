Clause 1

Right of carers to assessment

Mrs. Spelman: I beg to move amendment No. 4, in page 1, line 13, at end insert—

    `(1A) The Secretary of State (or, in relation to Wales, the National Assembly for Wales) shall issue guidance to local authorities as to how they are to satisfy themselves as required by subsection (1) above in cases where the person cared for has refused assessment.'

The amendment is separate from the second set of amendments. I am pleased that they have not been grouped together, because amendment No. 4 relates to an assessment of a cared-for person, whereas amendments Nos. 1, 2 and 3 relate to assessment of the carer. It is therefore right that the two groups should be separate.

This is a probing amendment, which would require the Secretary of State to issue guidance to local authorities on how to satisfy themselves that carers regularly provided a substantial amount of care and ensure that such an assessment had regard to a person's ability to provide care. There could be practical problems unless the clause is amended. Realistically, it could be difficult to carry out an assessment without practical guidance. The assessment of a cared-for person would have to be undertaken at arm's length, so to speak, if the person refused to allow an assessment to be made. That would mean that local authorities might have to take things on trust. How could an objective assessment be made at arm's length? Without adequate guidance, would the cared-for person feel that such an assessment was intrusive? Would such an assessment have to rely on a standard estimate of what was required for a particular condition? Without guidance, the right to assessment could result in a strain on the relationship between the carer and the person receiving care. That point came out on Second Reading.

Providing the right to assessment will arouse a level of expectation, as my hon. Friend the Member for Eddisbury (Mr. O'Brien) said on Second Reading. We are concerned that those expectations could be exaggerated unless the right to assessment is accompanied by guidance. That should include the capacity for local authorities to revisit the question of the cared-for person's needs and the carer's ability to provide for them, because, as the explanatory notes to clause 1 say, the assessment needs to consider the ability of the carer ``to continue to provide'' care.

One problem that befalls carers—this is no doubt one reason why the hon. Member for Stalybridge and Hyde introduced the Bill—is that they become burnt out. Although an assessment may establish that a carer is coping with a low level of support, the carer may, without any appreciable deterioration in the cared-for person's condition, become less able to meet that person's needs. In addition, the condition of the cared-for person may deteriorate. Unfortunately, many people who require care have conditions that deteriorate over time, hence the need to revisit the assessment. I hope that the guidance that we seek would contain that provision.

Such guidance would also help to ensure consistency in standards of care, and the Minister made it clear on Second Reading that he was concerned about that. I am sure that we all want standards of care to be, as far as possible, uniform throughout the country. Someone in one local authority area may compare notes with relatives or friends in another local authority area only to find that provisions vary. That is unacceptable and leaves the carer feeling badly done by. I hope that guidance will reinforce consistency in standards of care, so that such variation can be avoided.

Without such guidance, how will local authorities determine accurately at arm's length whether the cared-for person qualifies? Will the hon. Member for Stalybridge and Hyde describe the circumstances in which an assessment would be carried out without guidance? Calling for guidance from the Secretary of State would place more responsibility on central Government to solve the problem of arm's-length assessment.

It is somewhat unfair to ask local authorities to solve that problem without guidance. It is a Government responsibility to give clear guidelines on how such assessments are to be carried out, so that there is not a wide disparity between the methodologies used by different local authorities.

Mrs. Spelman: As I said earlier, these amendments are a separate set, which deal with assessment for the carer. They are probing amendments and I hope that they will raise and refine important issues about issues surrounding the Bill.

Amendment No. 2 may seem a small change—from ``may'' to ``shall''—but it is important because it strengthens the requirement on the Secretary of State to give directions about the manner in which an assessment is made. It would make it less optional for the Secretary of State to do so and begs the question of who is really in charge in making the assessment. Will the process be controlled by the Secretary of State or will the power rest with local authorities? As long as that remains unclear, there is a danger of conflict. For example, will there be penalties if the local authority lacks the resources to carry out a proper assessment? By reinforcing the Secretary of State's powers to give direction, the proposal makes the position clearer.

Amendment No. 1 would remove the potential for conflict of local authority powers to decide the appropriate form and manner of assessment co-existing with the powers residing with the Secretary of State, who ``may'' give direction. For example, if local authorities are inundated with demands for assessment, which is possible, they may need to change the form and manner of such assessments. There may be many variations in the form and manner of multi-agency assessments. For that reason, and to clarify the position, we advocate that the word ``may'' should be changed to ``shall''.

Amendment No. 3 is also important; it reduces the option of dealing either with the form or the manner of the assessment, but calls for both to be subject to clear direction. Mr. Pendry: I have already said, Mr. Stevenson, that the Minister may wish to catch your eye. He may inform the Committee more clearly than I could of one or two aspects of the provisions of clause 1.

The clause enables local authorities to provide certain services to direct carers, following a carer's assessment. The services to carers are not defined in the Bill. The local authority may provide any services that it thinks will support carers in their caring role or help to maintain their health and well-being. As was said on Second Reading, such services may take the form of physical help or could involve assistance around the house and other forms of support such as training or counselling for the carer.

The clause has generated some sensible amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Meriden. I hope that the Government have responded to them with some effect. The debate has been informed, intelligent and sensitive to issues of concern for carers and the people for whom they care. However, anxiety has been expressed not just here but on Second Reading that the devil is in the detail. The clause sets out new freedoms and flexibilities for local authorities. I mentioned that freedom on Second Reading, and it runs right through the Bill. It allows local authorities to decide what services will best meet carers' needs and help them to maintain their important role. However, the Bill also places a limitation on that freedom. In order to respect the individual liberty of disabled, vulnerable or frail older people, carer services delivered to the person cared for may not include anything of an intimate nature. That safeguard is intended to ensure that the new freedoms for carers do not disempower disabled people.

The clause allows for exceptions in prescribed circumstances. Flexibility was built into the Bill in case circumstances were identified in which it would be helpful to users and to carers. The Bill provides the power to make regulations on what counts as a service of an intimate nature. Such services might include dressing, feeding, lifting, washing or bathing a person who is cared for. Discussions about the extent of intimate services are on-going and involve the Department and carers' organisations. The limitation of the new freedom is designed to ensure that local authority support for carers does not become a tool to erode the hard-won independence of disabled people. Some services that carers view as assisting them in their role could be delivered for cared-for people under community care legislation. I am thinking of home help, cleaning, laundry and other such services. Some carers may prefer to be recipients of services, and might want to access the flexibility of a direct payment that would allow them to buy into a service at a convenient time.

As the Minister said, the provision has caused difficulties. Carers have indicated that they do not want to be charged for services. That point has been made before. I welcome the fact that the Minister will consider tabling an amendment dealing with the subject on Report. He will doubtless refer to it when he responds.

Cared-for people will not be forced to accept services that they do not wish to receive. The hon. Member for Runnymede and Weybridge expressed concern about that on Second Reading. In some circumstances, however, the provisions may accept a level of contact with social services that is not threatening and helps the person who provides the care. Carers welcome the opportunity to benefit from services that meet their needs and are delivered to them in recognition of their valuable role. The clause enables local authorities to provide support to carers, whom it will benefit when the cared-for person has refused services by allowing the local authority to deliver to the carer the widest possible range of non-controversial support, perhaps of a domestic nature. It will benefit carers who want greater flexibility and control on how services are delivered to better enable them to continue to lead their own lives. I am thinking especially of carers looking after elderly parents who have their own families and of those with outside interests or jobs to hold down.

The issues are complex, and I am sure that we shall become increasingly aware of that complexity as we discuss the Bill. I did not know about it when I started formulating my proposals. Not all carers are the same and the circumstances in which they care vary. Local authorities generally welcome the proposals, as they will give them flexibility to support carers in new ways. For the first time, the needs of carers will be met. Sensitive support will be delivered and we will recognise the difficult personal circumstances in which carers often care.

Specific issues have been raised. The hon. Member for Meriden described clause 2(3)(a) as a flaw in the Bill. It is not designed to make the Bill more complicated or confusing, or to impose restrictions on services that could be provided to carers; far from it. Subsection (3)(a) attempts to find a way, which is currently not available, to provide services that benefit a disabled person—the person who is being cared for—to the carer in a way that might be more convenient for both of them. We could all think of examples of that, especially in relation to laundry services or home help.

It is true—my hon. Friend the Member for Stalybridge and Hyde was right to mention this—that after the Bill was published, many people expressed concern that the clause might allow a local authority to charge a carer for services for which the cared-for person has been assessed. I accept your stricture, Mr. Stevenson, that we will discuss charging later, but there are serious anxieties, which we acknowledge, about how subsection (3)(a) will relate to charging. It has always been reasonable for a local authority to charge for services that are delivered to a disabled person, and it will be acceptable for a local authority to pose a reasonable charge in relation to carer services. It would, however, be unacceptable for a carer to be liable to contribute towards charges for their own services and those of the disabled person. The hon. Member for Meriden, who was concerned in particular about subsection (3)(a), will find it helpful to know that the Government intend to introduce a suitable amendment on Report to make it clear that the carer cannot be charged, or be assessed as being liable to a charge, for community care services that are provided for the disabled person. The clause is important. We intend to amend subsection (3)(a) on Report to clear up its ambiguous meaning. I hope that hon. Members are satisfied with that reassurance.

The hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam raised the technical matter of the nature of intimate services, which is mentioned in subsection (3)(b). He drew a parallel between the Bill and the Care Standards Bill. The Care Standards Bill does not purport to contain an exhaustive definition of personal care. During its passage in another place, it became clear that it might need to be amended. The hon. Gentleman should not read too much into the style and approach of the two Bills. The Carers and Disabled Children Bill broadly follows the approach of the Carers (Recognition and Services) Act 1995, on which similar concerns were raised. Regulatory powers give the Government and local authorities greater flexibility to adapt if there is something wrong with the way in which the rules are working. I shall not pretend that they are not inherently difficult.

It is obviously difficult to set specific and workable criteria for intimate and highly personal services. The greater flexibility that the regulation-making procedure gives to iron out the problems provides the best way to attempt to resolve the issues. We must address the issues, for a simple and obvious reason. Such intimate services might involve physical contact with a cared-for person with a disability. It is right for such a person to have the final say on the care services being extended to them. No member of the Committee would want it any other way. We are challenged with finding the right way in which to sort out the issue in the Bill that my hon. Friend the Member for Stalybridge and Hyde has introduced. We must resolve it because reasonable concerns are being expressed about it. I should give members of the Committee notice that we intend to improve clause 2 on Report.

The hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam possibly stretched the scope of the debate by asking when the Government intended to issue their advice on continence services. That issue is not only for local authorities but for the national health service. We intend to issue the continence guidance as soon as possible. We want it to be effective and to lever up the quality of services for people who need that help and care from the NHS and social services.

I urge the Committee to support this clause, which is a very important part of the Bill.
