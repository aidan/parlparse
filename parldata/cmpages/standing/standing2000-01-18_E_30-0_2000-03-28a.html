Mr. Hill: I propose briefly to explain the purpose of the amendments and to respond in due course to the debate on the Opposition amendments. I hope that that will be for the convenience of the Committee.

We made it clear early on that although railways legislation is reserved to Westminster, the devolved Administrations have a particular and legitimate right to be involved in influencing the Strategic Rail Authority's development of railway policy. We made a commitment very early that one board member of the SRA should take particular account of Welsh concerns and one of Scottish. We have now been asked to make those commitments clear on the face of the Bill and that is what the amendments achieve. They also make technical changes to improve the drafting as regards the appointments of the chairman. Amendment No. 815 makes it an obligation for the SRA to consult with the Scottish Ministers and the National Assembly for Wales before it formulates its strategies and as part of keeping those strategies under review.

The Secretary of State will remain the person who makes the appointments to the board of the SRA, but one member will need to be appointed after consultation with the National Assembly for Wales and one after consultation with Scottish Ministers. Appointments to the board are of individuals and a broad range of experience and expertise is needed, as well as the need to take account of specific interests such as the concerns of Wales and Scotland. The Secretary of State is in the best place to appoint a balanced board taking account of its needs if he appoints all the people. Then, the individual strengths can most simply be balanced.

On the consultation by the SRA with the Scottish Ministers and the National Assembly for Wales, we expect that the SRA will need to consult with a wide range of bodies before formulating its strategies, including local authorities, industry and other interest groups. It would not be practical, nor is it necessary, to specify those groups on the face of the Bill. Should it prove necessary to direct the SRA on whom to consult, that could be achieved through the Secretary of State's directions and guidance.

Primary legislation is devolved to the Scottish Parliament, while in Wales, a Crown body has devolved power over secondary legislation and has no primary legislative role. Mr. Hill: As we enter this important schedule, it may be convenient for the Committee, if I say a word about its purpose before dealing with the question raised by the hon. Member for North Essex. Clause 179 and Schedule 13 set out provisions relating to the appointment, tenure and remuneration of members and staff of the authority, certain procedural matters, and provide for the financing of the authority.

Part I of Schedule 13 makes standard non-departmental public body provision for the conditions of appointment for members and staff and part II makes standard provision for the funding of a NDPB. As for part III, it is worth noting the following with regard to funding and borrowing. The authority will mainly be funded by grant from the Secretary of State. It will be entitled to borrow money from the secretary of State and, temporarily, from other sources such as overdraft or bridging facility with the consent of the Secretary of State and the approval of the Treasury. Restrictions on borrowing are set out. This mix of powers will enable the authority to be a flexible, proactive body in the promotion and development of the railway.

The borrowing limit is set at £3 billion, or such other sum as the Secretary of State may, with the approval of the Treasury, specify by order made by statutory instrument. Provision is made as to the terms of any loans from the Secretary of State and the requirements for accounts and audit of Government loans are set out. The Secretary of State is allowed to guarantee sums borrowed by the authority and the procedures that must be followed to facilitate this are laid down.

With regard to the payment of surplus sums, Mr. Gale, the Secretary of State may require the payment to him of sums that are received by the authority, in particular those that are surplus. This will ensure that the authority cannot hold large surpluses that properly belong to the public purse.

Part III of the schedule enables the authority to give the Secretary of State assistance and advice about any matter in respect of which any of its functions is exercisable. Part IV sets out standard NDPB procedures to be adopted by the authority in respect of its business, committees and so on and the declaration of members' interests. The provisions on members' interests are based on those in the Regional Development Agencies Act 1998. I hope that this setting out of the broad objectives of schedule 13 has been helpful to the Committee.

The amendments would change the arrangements for possible payments for loss of office. They would prevent compensation being paid in special circumstances in the following cases: first, where a member of the SRA cease to hold office; secondly, where the office of franchising director is abolished on the setting up of the SRA; thirdly, where a member of a rail passengers' committee ceases to hold office on abolition of the committee; and finally, where a member of the British Railways Board ceases to hold office because of a reduction in the size of the board or its abolition. I realise that these are probing amendments, but they would, if effected, be unfair to the people involved. The Government are denying that this is an opportunity to set up any matter arising out of the termination of the relevant appointment.

These are standard provisions dealing with public appointments. The powers would be used only in special circumstances. For example, the last franchising director cannot be obliged to take the appointment of chief executive of the SRA that is to be offered by virtue of paragraph 1 of schedule 25. In the unlikely event that the franchising director elects to turn down that appointment, there must be discretion to pay compensation for loss of the predecessor office. The power may be used if boundary changes led to a change in the number and membership of rail passengers' committees.
