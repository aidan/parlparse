I want to spend a few moments discussing section 242 of the Taxes Act 1988, which clause 20 would abolish. That provides relief for claiming franked investment income tax credits against losses in a company. That is part of the imputation system, properly called the partial imputation system because there is a disparity in the credit rate of 20 per cent. on the full corporation tax of 33 per cent. or 31 per cent. That means that double taxation is not altogether avoided, but the aim of the partial imputation system is to avoid companies being taxed more than once on their profits. A United Kingdom company that receives a dividend from another UK company does not have it taxed. Part and parcel of the imputation system is the avoidance of double taxation on company profits. The dividends received are thus known as franked investment income--the income has been franked and had tax paid upon it.

The essence of the system is that profits are taxed only once. In addition to the avoidance of double taxation being implicit in the system, ACT tries to avoid a double cash flow effect. People confuse ACT with the double tax credit system. ACT--advanced corporation tax--is simply a cash flow facility so that the Government can avoid having to wait nine months after a company's year end before they receive any corporation tax. ACT is payable 14 days after the end of the quarter in which a dividend is paid--the quarters are March, June, September and December--with an extra one for each company's applicable year end. The ACT is paid to the Government as soon as a dividend is paid.

Where one UK company receives a dividend from another and then pays that dividend to its own shareholders, if it were not for the franking of dividends the companies would have to pay out that cash flow disadvantage twice. A dividend would be paid by the first company, which would pay ACT to the Exchequer. The dividend would be received by the second company but, when that company paid the dividend to its own shareholders, it would, theoretically, have to pay another chunk of ACT. To avoid that disadvantage arising, the imputation system has a system of franking the dividend that is paid, so that franked investment income coming from the first company can frank the dividend being paid to the second. In effect, there is no requirement for the second company to have to pay any ACT.

We have heard a great deal from Labour Members about the distorting effect of the imputation system, but it is not a distortion.

Suppose that a company generates lots of profits, but the managing director decides, for some reason, that instead of distributing profits to the shareholders as he should, he will invest the money in some groundnuts scheme in Africa. That managing director is, in effect, defaulting on the people who lent capital to him, just as if one borrowed money from a bank and failed to pay the interest on it. Dividends are no different from interest on a loan.

The consequence of failing to pay a dividend is that a company's share price will fall. If a share price falls and the company seeks to raise further capital in future, it will find that the cost of capital rises because it has to issue new shares at a greater discount to take into account the fact that dividends were not paid on previous capital. Investors will want some form of risk premium to compensate for that. The whole notion that paying dividends is somehow distorting or bad for business is utter nonsense. The concept in Will Hutton's ``The State We're In'' that raising funds through the equity markets is in some way an Anglo-Saxon method of raising capital and is not as good as the continental method, by which businesses are funded by debt, is utter nonsense. There is no real distinction and the equity method of raising capital is very effective.

It is far better for companies to pay out dividends--as they should do and as they have committed themselves to do--than for the capital markets to decide where best to reinvest that capital in the future. They may well be totally satisfied with a company's record and they may be happy to reinvest capital in the company in a future rights issue or in a future shares issue, but they may decide to invest their capital elsewhere in the stock market; they may decide to invest the dividend flows in venture capital--in emerging companies that are just starting up.

The essence of the imputation system is central to the success of British business. It means that flabby, inefficient management is not allowed to succeed and that aspiring, new companies with new ideas are able to raise capital in the marketplace.

If a company has a loss and is not able to pay a dividend, section 242 of the 1988 Act provides it with effective relief during that difficult phase. The essence of the relief, which clause 20 will abolish, is that the company can offset the franked investment income credit against the loss and obtain repayment from the Exchequer of the tax credit, which is effectively equal to 20 per cent. of the amount of the loss that it has given up in exchange for the repayment. That is useful relief for companies that are going through a difficult phase. Of course, those companies hope to be able to return to a profit in the future.

Section 242 is an unusual relief because it can be undone in due course--when a company decides that it is no longer in a loss-making position, and most companies hope that they will not remain loss-making for ever. The relief can be undone, the tax credit can be repaid to the Exchequer via the ACT bill and losses can be reinstated and offset against future profits. Companies may want to do that because the temporary relief that can be claimed under section 242 gives relief only at 20 per cent. for losses, although corporation tax losses are really worth 33 per cent.--or they will be worth 31 per cent. when the Bill is enacted. Claims are made in the knowledge that they can be undone, that losses can be reinstated and that full relief will be available in the future at 33 per cent. or at 31 per cent.

Clause 20 will abolish that valuable relief for accounting periods beginning on or after 2nd July. The unwinding provisions that I have just described end straight away on that date. Many companies in this country have put in claims under the provisions of section 242 to have their franked investment income credits offset against their losses and they have received the 20 per cent. relief, but they put in the claims in the full expectation that in due course--in two, three or four years' time, when they return to profitability--they will be able to undo the claim, to reinstate their losses and to obtain relief at the full corporation tax rate. Had they known that the Government were going to abolish the ability to undo that claim, it is very likely that almost no company in this country would have put in a claim in the first place.

It is grossly unjust for the ability to unwind claims to be removed forthwith on 2 July 1997. I do not understand why the Bill is being rushed through. The Institute of Chartered Accountants in England and Wales believes that there is no need for haste, and it is not required as a result of the terms of the Provisional Collection of Taxes Act 1968.

Again, we must ask why that is happening and what the consequences will be. As I read the clause--I am sure that the Economic Secretary will let me know if I am misreading it--all companies will find themselves unable, if they run into losses, to gain the benefit of ACT interim relief. Indeed, a company that makes a loss and does not in future become profitable to the extent of having to pay mainstream corporation tax will never be able to claim that relief. New Labour has dreamed up an extraordinarily perverse and anti-progressive, even regressive, form of taxation, under which the least profitable companies will pay tax at a higher rate and the most profitable companies, which will ultimately pay mainstream corporation tax on their profits, will be able to gain the full measure of relief.

It will not have escaped the attention of the Economic Secretary that, on that basis, some companies will go under. Those companies that make losses will be unable to gain the benefit of the 20 per cent. relief on their ACT that they can gain today, which is deducted from dividends received. That financial contribution, that cash-flow contribution, might have made all the difference to their survival.

What the Economic Secretary has conceived in clause 20--it is an effective achievement--will increase the volatility of the earnings of British companies, removing what might otherwise have been an effective smoothing of their earnings, thereby increasing the risk of bankruptcy. That is not a very positive achievement, but it is remarkable. The new Labour--or, if she prefers it, socialist--Economic Secretary has brought to the House a piece of regressive or anti-progressive legislation, which will result in companies paying tax at a higher rate if they do not make a profit or if their profits are lower.
