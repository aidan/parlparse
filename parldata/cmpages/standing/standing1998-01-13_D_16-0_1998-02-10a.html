Such a person gives advice for a fee and is liable for any consequences that may flow from that advice, in particular negligent consequences of bad advice. There is also a reference to those who give advice in circumstances where the Bill says there is no question of a payment being made, which includes law centres and citizens advice bureaux.

There is a contradistinction between the advice given for money and that given necessarily freely. Until 1963, the treatment of the advice given in each case would have been entirely different. The giver of advice given for a fee was liable for the consequences and wrongness of the advice. The giver of free advice was rendered in no way liable in negligence. Gratuitous advice was of no consequence. In other words, the advice that one did not pay for is worth what one paid for it--nothing. Then along came the interesting case, with which I know the hon. Member for Aberdeen, Central (Mr. Doran) is familiar, of Hedley Byrne and Co. Ltd. v. Heller and Partners Ltd. The learned law book says:

    ``that decision of course remains a landmark which shattered the assumption that the tort of negligence did not extend in any circumstances to the protection of purely economic interests.''

We are talking about the protection of economic interests. To some degree, the part of the Bill that concerns unpaid advice remains dependent upon that case remaining a cornerstone of English law. The case was a great surprise at the time. I was a law student in 1964, and it was considered to have turned important principles upside down.

I shall not discuss the point any further, but I offer the Government the thought that part of their Bill depends on a controversial decision.
