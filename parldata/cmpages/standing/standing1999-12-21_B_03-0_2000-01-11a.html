Freedom of Information Bill

Clause 1

General right of access to information held by public authorities

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 22, in page 1, line 24, leave out subsection(6).—[Mr. David Heath.]

4.30 pm

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): I welcome you to the Chair, Dr. Clark, and wish you a happy new year.

I was reminding the Committee that clause 75 makes it an offence for an authority to alter or destroy records with the intention of preventing disclosure. The effect of the amendment would be to remove clause 1(6) from the Bill. That subsection provides that the information communicated to an applicant under clause 1

    ``is the information . . . held at the time when the request is received, except that account may be taken of any amendment or deletion made between that time and the time when the information is to be communicated''

as long as the

    ``amendment or deletion . . . would have been made regardless of the receipt of the request.''

The amendment is intended to cause authorities to preserve a record requested under freedom of information legislation regardless of any previous intention to destroy it. However, by deleting clause 1(6), the amendment would leave unclear the crucial time for determining the communication of information to the applicant.

Further difficulties would remain even if that defect could be remedied by retaining the first half of clause 1(6) and deleting the lines on page 2 of the Bill, because the subsection is not simply concerned with the destruction of records, but enables an authority to update and correct information that has been requested. In many cases, information held by authorities will require constant updating and revision in the light of changing circumstances and more up to date analysis of facts. Without the provision, authorities would be obliged to communicate unrevised information to applicants without any additions or deletions that would be required to bring it up to date. The amendment would therefore have the effect of requiring authorities to communicate incorrect and out of date information to the applicant.

Far from being otiose, as the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath) suggested, the clause is important. If it were deleted, normal record management within large operations would be almost impossible. They could never update; they would have to maintain things as they always were. The amendment does not achieve what the hon. Gentleman seeks. The best way to protect against anyone who might seek for mendacious reasons to prevent information from getting into the public area is clause 75, which provides the sort of protections that the public needs. On that basis, I ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I, too, welcome you to the Chair this afternoon, Dr. Clark.

I listened with great care to the Under-Secretary's reply, as always, but am none the wiser for having done so. There seemed to be a certain lack of logic in what he said. Nothing in the subsection prevents an authority that produces information from adding an additional piece of paper to say, ``By the way, this is now incorrect or has been superseded by later information that we will provide as well.'' Nothing in the Bill restricts a public body from providing information. Therefore, suggesting that the amendment would prevent a public body from correcting, amending or updating information is simply wrong.

There would be a short time between the application for information and its being sent to the applicant, but it would not upset the workings of even the most sophisticated filing system to keep a matter that had been inquired into on file during that brief period. The Under-Secretary referred to clause 75, but he has not made it clear why there could be a procedure for updating files—it would be quite a proper procedure if the subsection were to remain—that would effectively take another class of information outside the remit of the Bill. The amendment would also prevent any accusation under clause 75 that something had been done intentionally to circumvent the Bill, as there would clearly be a regular updating or deletion programme and the intent would not be to remove a specific piece of information from public scrutiny.

For that reason, I am not entirely satisfied with what the Minister said, and I hope that he will discuss the matter with others to establish whether the subsection achieves the objectives that he has ascribed to it. I believe that it does not and that its deletion would not have the results that he suggested. We will want to return to the matter later in the Bill but, at this stage, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment No. 36 is intended to raise the question of information that is not written down or recorded. The Bill's right of access is to information ``recorded in any form'', as set out in the interpretation clause, clause 82. That seems fair enough, but what about information that is known to people in a public authority but has not been recorded? That may sound rather an abstruse definition, but when one stops to think about it, certain rather important classes of information may be included in it, such as information that relates to action or inaction by a public body. Mr. Mike O'Brien: My hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central (Mr. Fisher) has presented a valid and interesting case, but it is unfortunately an extremely difficult issue. We have considered it and studied how it has been addressed in New Zealand. The amendment would extend the right of access to unrecorded information, but we cannot support it—certainly not in its current form. There are difficulties regarding the enforcement of such a right of access to unrecorded information.

Under the Bill, the right of access applies to information recorded in any form, and we believe that that is the only practical solution if the Bill is to be effective. A public authority cannot be said to hold information or be required to communicate it accurately if no record of the information exists. How can the commissioner investigate a failure to provide information in respect of which there is no record of its existence? I accept my hon. Friend's point that information may be known to individuals which is not written down. It may have been the subject of discussion, but was not recorded. How will the commissioner determine that, except by calling witnesses before her—assuming that the commissioner is Mrs. Elizabeth France—and questioning them about what they know, when they knew it and why they did not record it? She would no doubt have to record information from all the testimony and then try to determine what the information was. That is difficult, especially as some of that information could be rumour, unsubstantiated or inaccurate comment, or merely hearsay. The Bill is not intended to promote a right of freedom for misinformation; it will provide access to recorded information about which people have a right to know.

To some extent, there is an assumption, which I accept can be challenged, that the public authorities will record everything. It may be that some public authorities will choose not to do so for all sorts of reasons. We do not want to create a situation where that process is encouraged. We need to ensure that information is available to the public when it has been recorded. Some people insist that extending the right of access to unrecorded information will encourage officials deliberately not to record sensitive information to evade the right of access. The only way in which to stop such abuse is to give adequate protection to sensitive information when the public interest demands it. Officials will then have no fear about recording information and adequate audit trails will be maintained. That is what the Bill will do. Under clause 13, the public interest in disclosing information should be taken into account.

My hon. Friend the Member for Stoke-on-Trent, Central makes a valid point, but the Bill is not an effective way of dealing with the circumstances that he described. That would put the onus on the commissioner and local authorities to deal with unrecorded information. In order to proceed sensibly and coherently, the best approach is not to include a right of access to unrecorded information, but merely that recorded information will be the area to which we wish new rights to extend.
