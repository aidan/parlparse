I have received representations about the Bill from the National Youth Advocacy Service, from colleagues involved in child care law with whom I worked when I was a social worker, and from organisations concerned with domestic violence, which are worried that courts do not take a strong enough view of the impact of domestic violence on children's lives when, under section 8 of the Children Act 1989, they decide the crucial issues of contact and residence. That is a serious concern.

The amendments reflect my concern for the need to establish children's rights at the heart of the service. The concept of children's rights directors is fairly new. It is embodied in the Care Standards Bill, which is in another place but will soon reach this House. We should reflect on whether children's rights directors could have a role in CAFCASS. They could certainly have a role in relation to children in this country who are looked after and who live away from home. That includes children who come before the courts in family proceedings anyway.

As we are establishing a committee structure for CAFCASS, it is important to consider also the possibility of establishing a children's rights committee. I do not wish my remarks to be construed as derogatory to the Lord Chancellor's Department, which is a fine, important Department of State, but establishing such a committee would bring children's rights into the heart of a service that has no experience of managing a children's service or a social work service. Given the difficulties involved in bringing children before courts, we would all agree that a court is not the ideal setting in which to resolve many of the issues that affect children. It should be a place of last resort—a point that needs to be emphasised in the new service.

Part of the background here is the as-yet non-implementation of part II of the Family Law Act 1996. I am sure that there are good reasons for that. There was a debate on the Floor of the House last week to which my hon. Friend the Parliamentary Secretary replied. Research is taking place to consider the implications of the 1996 Act, section 11 of which brings Childrens Act principles firmly into family law. The 1996 Act refers to the necessity for courts to have particular regard to the wishes and feelings of the child in the light of his or her age and understanding. It also lays down the general principle that, in the absence of evidence to the contrary, the child's welfare is best served by regular contact with whoever has parental responsibility. This is a fluid, complex area of law.

It is important to recognise that this country has a major commitment to the United Nations convention on the rights of the child, particularly article 9, which gives voice to a child's right to have contact with both parents, provided that this is plainly in his or her interest, and article 12, which gives credibility to the idea of the child's voice being heard. These are extremely important aspects of law. Even if there were not a fluid background to this complex issue, there would, in my view, be sound reasons for putting children's rights at the heart of the new unified service. It needs to be proactive and child-centred rather than reactive and court-led. This is a major opportunity to build on the framework created initially by the Children Act 1975 with the guardians ad litem provision. This would build a preventive, integrated, inter-disciplinary service that is not triggered simply by court proceedings but can develop as a service for children in many and varied circumstances. There is a need to create a service to which a child can have direct access, as an active participant, rather than as the passive subject of remote, strange and often frightening court proceedings.
