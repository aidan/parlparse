New Clause 15

Detention of Girls Under 18

    `An offender who is convicted of any offence punishable with detention and who is a girl under the age of 18 shall be kept separate from adults and shall be detained in a separate institution, or in a separate part of an institution also holding adults, unless, after assessment and in exceptional circumstances, it is held to be in the best interests of the offender to be held with adults for a temporary period.'. [Mr. Allan.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Richard Allan (Sheffield, Hallam): I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The principal purpose of the new clause is to make it unlawful for girls under 18 to be held with adults in prison except in the most exceptional circumstances. It is necessary to bring Britain into line with the United Nations convention on the rights of the child, article 37 of which deals with this issue. We recognise that the Government entered a reservation about exceptional circumstances, but we believe it is now time to take the issue forward.

In some senses, we rehearsed this debate when we discussed previous amendments on 15 and 16-year-old boys being held in Prison Service institutions. However, there are differences for women under the age of 18. The first is the numbers involved. Between 60 and 80 were in the system at any one time in 1997, with a total of 300 held in custody over the year. The smaller number of girls held in detention means that the Government would be able to achieve the goal of removing them from the Prison Service system.

Specific young offenders institutions are not available for women in the same way as they are for men. Young women offenders are put into women's prisons and are not held in separate institutions for young offenders. Again, that is because of the numbers of involved.

I refer the Committee to the excellent report "Lost Inside" that was produced by the Howard League and Baroness Masham of Ilton. It highlighted many of the issues relating to the position of young women in prison and it interviewed a prison officer who said:

    "Prison doesn't deter them. When they leave here they have a criminal record so employment is impossible for them and mostly they will only have a hostel to go to where they'll get little support. If they have a drug problem they will go back to shoplifting or prostitution to fund the drug habit. It's a circle. It's sad. They don't know any other life."

Such testimony from those involved in the system shows the extent to which it does not serve the wider public or girls under 18.

We hope that the Government will make a commitment to holding such women in local authority secure accommodation. That is far more appropriate except for the most severe offences. Occasionally serious crimes will require a prison sentence, but most girls are convicted of ordinary, run-of-the-mill offences. Local authority secure accommodation is most appropriate for them.

I also refer the Committee to the thematic report on young prisoners, which was conducted by the chief inspector of prisons, Sir David Ramsbotham. He expressed grave concerns about putting youngsters into main Prison Service institutions and wished to see separate institutions set up.

Amazing problems are caused by the mixing of adult and juvenile prisoners. For example, the Howard League came across a 15-year-old who was placed in a cell next to a woman convicted of procuring 15-year-olds for prostitution. Such coincidences are unfortunate, but they are inevitable if the mixing of adult and juvenile prisoners takes place.

The new clause would make it clear that young women should be taken out of Prison Service institutions. I understand the Government's reservations about similar proposals for young males; they did not want to accept a provision that they could not deliver. However, a small number of young women would be covered by the new clause, so it outlines an achievable goal. It would enable the Government to tackle the issue and they would receive widespread praise if they accepted it.

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): It is well worth discussing this subject. It is important to bear in mind that the new clause covers the detention of girls aged 15, 16 or 17, and would prevent them being kept in prison alongside adult offenders. By now, the Committee will be well aware of the Opposition's views about mixing the age ranges; our views apply to young women as well as to young men. We have advanced strong arguments that 15 or 16-year-old young men, or boys, should not be held on remand in adult prisons. The same arguments would apply to young women.

We should take the issue seriously. It has often been said, under Governments of all political complexions, that young girls look up to older women, who mother them while they are in prison. The hon. Member for Sheffield, Hallam (Mr. Allan) gave an example from the Howard League report of a 15-year-old girl who was put in a cell next to a woman whose offence was the procurement of 15-year-olds for prostitution. That example illustrates the argument.

We should debate the matter to consider how we might deal better with young women who are in need of secure accommodation. That brings us back to the question of the availability of local authority secure accommodation as an alternative to prison. Again, the Committee wil be well aware of the views of Conservative Members on that subject.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O' Brien): As the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) has rightly pointed out, this is a serious issue and I have some sympathy with the anxieties expressed by the hon. Member for Hallam.

On the face of it, the notion of detaining vulnerable young girls with hardened adult prisoners seems unacceptable. If all under-18s were vulnerable and all over-18s were hardened criminals, we should certainly need to ensure that they were not held together. However, as we know, the world is not like that. We should remember that important point and keep it in the forefronts of our minds when considering the proposal.

The real issue for the Prison Service is to minimise the risks of holding vulnerable prisoners with those who might have a negative influence on them. We are all aware that neither vulnerability nor negative influence are absolutely correlated with age. We must be careful to ensure that we do not inadvertently put in place, for apparently good reasons, what might turn out to be impractical and possibly arbitrary cut-offs that adversely affect those we want to help.

The new clause covers two issues: first, that of accommodation and, secondly, that of mixing offenders of different ages during activities. The root of the problem is the small number of girls aged under 18 that the Prison Service has to deal with. There were only 91 such girls within the Prison Service estate at the end of March this year. They are, therefore, a minority, wherever they are placed. However, we are duty bound to do our best for such girls under the circumstances.

The Prison Service is currently considering the possible solutions to the problem. There are a number of alternatives. The first alternative is to create a separate secure estate specifically for juvenile females that would parallel that being planned for juvenile boys. A population of about 91 girls might justify the establishment of only two separate units. Such units could well be of a suitable size to provide effectively the range of programmes that might be required under the proposed detention and training order. That is the plus side.

The down side is that the establishment of only two units would separate many of these girls from the valuable support of their families. As the hon. Member for Hallam will know, the Select Committee on Home Affairs, in its report on juvenile crime, pointed out that contact with families is very important in reducing the likelihood of the offender re-offending in the future, when he or she leaves custody.

The two units would probably have to be placed so that there was one in the north and one in the south of the country. With only two units there would be no possibility of the girls moving to open conditions as part of their preparation for release, as is possible at present. Those are two serious down sides, which make the creation of two sites one in the north and one in the south unacceptable.

Mr. Allan: I understand that Lord Williams of Mostyn announced in another place that the Government's policy was to set up two units one at HMP New Hall, near Wakefield, and the other at Holloway.

Mr. O'Brien: I shall come on to our planned approach to that. The hon. Gentleman seemed to be talking about different institutions, but the setting up of units is also being considered.

Another option would be a single juvenile estate, for both sexes. The advantage would be that a larger estate would overcome the geographical problems I mentioned and allow effective remedial programmes to be provided. However it would entail problems. The mixing of boys and girls in regime activities might raise more difficulties than mixing girls and women would particularly, perhaps, in cases where there had been sexual abuse. Girls' needs might be more akin to those of women than those of boys. That solution would therefore not be ideal.

A further option would be to make use of the secure training centres, when those became available, or local authority secure accommodation. The first secure training centre came on stream in April. Both options raise the issue of capacity. The STC estate will comprise 200 places when completed and will probably be most suited to younger children. The local authority secure estate is already used for a number of purposes: welfare cases, section 53 custodial sentences and some remands. The secure remands that we discussed under clauses 89 and 90 will make use of those facilities. The question that this alternative raises is whether it is right to hold girls who are nearly 18, who might be difficult and might have convictions for serious offences, with much younger boys and girls.

The last option is to create several separate units to accommodate juvenile girls in the women's prison estate, and to ensure that any mixing of regime activities is closely monitored and controlled and is in the best interests of all concerned.

The issues are difficult. I am sure that we will all have the interests of juvenile girls at heart in trying to resolve them. However, the upshot is that there is not at present a simple answer; the new clause would make the situation worse by restricting the options even further.

One of the strengths of the proposed detention and training order is the built-in flexibility. The ability to place offenders in custodial facilities best suited to their needs is a feature of the new arrangements. The proposed new clause would restrict how the new order might operate in practice, as it would require a girl serving a detention and training order to be transferred to adult facilities on reaching 18. That would necessarily interfere with the purpose of such a sentence, which is not what the Government would want.

We must also remember that there are those who may need to be accommodated elsewhere. Within the small population of female under-18-year-olds that we are concerned with, there are some girls who may pose a particular risk to the rest. They have to be accommodated somewhere and allowed to associate, but it may not be appropriate to house them with other under-18s. Section 1C of the Criminal Justice Act 1982 allows, on a case-by-case basis, for named juveniles to be sent to a prison rather than a young offenders institution for a specified reason. Placement in a mother and baby unit is one acceptable reason, as is the need for medical or psychiatric care.

The Prison Service has a responsibility to protect young and vulnerable prisoners from detrimental influences, but it also has a duty to look after all prisoners with humanity and to help them to lead useful and law-abiding lives in custody and after release. If the service is to achieve those aims, all prisoners must have access to meaningful activity, such as education or training and appropriate programmes to help them address their offending behaviour.

As I have said, the question of how best to meet the needs of young offenders is complex and there is no quick-fix solution. The hon. Member for Hallam has already warned of the dangers of quick-fix solutions. If we supported the clause we would be in danger of thinking that there was a quick-fix solution when there is not.

There are very small numbers of sentenced under-18s in women's prisons. The average number at any one time is around 90. The need for these girls to be near their families has to be balanced with the need to be with others of their own age. Work is in hand to address the issues of age mixing in female prisons and the regimes provided for these young offenders.

The Government, when debating this issue in the other place, promised to report back to Parliament on progress when the new detention and training order was implemented, and this will be done. In the mean time, a considerable amount has been achieved by the Prison Service in separating those sentenced to detention in a young offender institution from those over 21.

A framework for enhancing the regimes for women and female young offenders in prison is part of the major initiative on regimes being undertaken by the Prison Service this year. The impetus for this work has come from a number of sources, not least from the strongly held belief shared by the Prison Service and the Government that custodial sentences are not an end in themselves but can make a real difference to the offender and to the public. The aim is to provide positive and constructive regimes which prevent offending.

Again, I fully understand and share the concern expressed by the hon. Members for Hallam and for Hertsmere and those in the other place about the inappropriate mixing of vulnerable female prisoners with other female prisoners who might have a negative impact on them. However, I feel that the arrangements we are seeking to put in place will provide sufficient flexibility to allow us to continue to work toward addressing the issues. I must stress that I recognise that we are not there yet. We still have a way to go.

While I can endorse the concern about the need to separate under-18s from women who may have an adverse influence on them, we need to ensure that we do not undermine the flexibility that can enable us to address the real problem of the under-18 females, both through our review of juvenile secure accommodation and through the Prison Service's review of its policy on age mixing. Therefore, I do not believe that the amendment is either necessary at this stage or the best way to address the needs of vulnerable female prisoners.

I hope that I have set out clearly the Government's position, and that in the light of what I have said, the hon. Member will not seek to press the new clause.

 

Mr. Allan: I am grateful to the Minister for his courteous reply and in particular for two factors that he brought out: first the Government's intention to report back on this issue and secondly their open recognition that not all the work has been done. The issue is a growing difficulty. The Minister said that 90 offenders in this category were in prison at any one time. My understanding is that in 1992 that figure was more like 25. Clearly the number of young women being sentenced to custodial sentence is growing all the time.

Some of the measures in the Bill may drop the lower age limit down to 12 for detention and training orders, for example. It will continue to be an issue that need urgent attention. New Clause 17

Increase in sentences for aggravation based on sexual

orientation (No. 2)'

    `(1) This section applies where a court is considering the seriousness of any offence.

    (2) If the offence was aggravated for the purposes of this section, the court

    (a) shall treat that fact as an aggravating factor (that is to say, a factor that increases the seriousness of the offence); and

    (b) shall state in open court that the offence was so aggravated.

    (3) An offence is aggravated for the purposes of this section if

    (a) at the time of committing the offence, or immediately before or after doing so, the offender demonstrates towards the victim of the offence hostility based on the victim's actual or presumed sexual orientation; or

    (b) the offence is motivated (wholly or partly) by hostility towards members of a group based on their sexual orientation or based on their membership of, or association with, such a group.'. [Mr. Allan]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Allan: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

There are three areas that I should like to cover in setting out the reasoning for the new clause. We should like to spend a little time on it, as it is important.

First, I wish to examine the background and why the clause is necessary at this time. Secondly, I will talk about the phrasing of the new clause and how it would fit into the Bill. Thirdly, I will refer to august and learned support for the changes that we are proposing.

A survey by the Stonewall group entitled "Queer Bashing" covers the background to the new clause. I will quote some of that report's key findings and examine some of the examples given in it.

The most important finding of the report which was a comprehensive survey covering a several thousand people was that one in three men and one in four women have experienced some form of violence. Those figures will shock anyone who comes across them. Thirty-two per cent. of those surveyed had been harassed in some way and 73 per cent. had been verbally abused. They felt that that was as a result of their sexuality and was because of what they were.

I will quote some examples from the survey that are relevant to our debate. The first concerns a lesbian from Yorkshire who went to Ripon for the weekend with her lover. She says:

    "we went out for a meal. We passed two lads sitting on a wall. Shortly after, we retraced our steps. . . The guys started shouting at us. . . One of the men came running after us . . . He kicked my lover in the back of the head.

    We ran across the road to the pub for refuge. We went to the bar and ordered a drink then they came in, pushed us to the ground, all the time kicking and punching and they started to drag us out of the pub."

The victim was unable to see what was happening because her jacket had been pulled over her head. She remembers:

    "I was in the pub doorway holding onto my lover's legs. Her head was on the pavement. I covered her body with mine . . . We ran . . . to the house we were staying in. During the fumbling for the key, one of the men came running down and continued to kick and punch us."

Fortunately, the women got into the house, and called the police. Both of the men involved were arrested. the victim says:

    "I got over my physical injuries within about a week but the metal stress caused is still there although I do feel much better.

    It's the fourth time I've been queerbashed since I came out in 1980."

The mental scars remain with individuals who are assaulted in such cases. Their sexuality will not change, so they know that every time they are in a similar situation they will be exposed to similar dangers. The new clause addresses that issue. It would ensure that, in addition to physical assault, mental anguish is recognised in open court.

My second example deals with people who are harassed in their flats and who are forced to move on. It concerns a gay man and his lover who live in a flat in south-west Britain. He says:

    "A group of local boys, aged 16-20, took great pleasure in hurling abuse every time myself or my partner left our flat. On several occasions objects were thrown at us, including tomatoes and stones. Windows were broken and graffiti was written on the block we lived in."

On another occasion the man remembers being

    "jumped by three lads, one wielding a snooker cue. I was badly injured resulting in hospital treatment and time off work. The incident was reported to the police but nothing ever came of it. They were in fact very helpful and sympathetic, but because I couldn't give descriptionsthey were powerless."

The man continues,

    "Most of the physical scars have gone, except for a few on my face, but it still haunts me to this day."

I hope that the Bill will provide some remedy for such situations through the anti-social behaviour orders. I was pleased that in an earlier debate Ministers clarified the fact that the orders are intended to provide protection for those who are picked on because of what they are, and are not meant to be used to victimise such people.

We believe that offences that involve assault and criminal damage as in the tales that I have just recited should be tried in court, regardless of any order that is issued. New clause 17 would come into play when such offences came to court.

The Government's official position on attacks is crucial. My hon. Friend the Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey (Mr. Hughes) asked questions about this issue on 22 April 1998. The Minister of State's response was:

    "From the information collected centrally, it is not possible to determine offences or incidents of homophobic crime" [Official Report, 22 April 1998; Vol. 310, c. 636.]

which is one of the key issues addressed in subsection (2)(b) of the new clause. It is a serious problem when a Minister of the Crown, responsible for the oversight of the criminal justice system, is unable to provide the figures because the criminal justice system does not make provision for such information always and consistently to be taken into account when courts consider relevant crimes. We are keen to ensure that whenever a crime has a homophobic element, an open declaration of that fact is made in court. We feel that the climate is right, with a new Government formed by a party that has long professed an interest in promoting tolerance and fighting bigotry. We recognise shared objectives in that respect, which is why it is appropriate to bring the proposal forward when the Government are taking action elsewhere against intolerance as in the race clause in the Bill.

We also recognise that even the Conservative party has now come out as a champion of equality and an enemy of bigotry. We were particularly impressed by the comments of the former right hon. Member for Enfield, Southgate at the Conservative party conference, which took us some way forward. We may hear from his successor, the hon. Member for Enfield, Southgate (Mr. Twigg) later in the debate. However, we are not merely trying to test the Government or force them in a macho way to adopt our proposals and claim a victory: we genuinely want to achieve a step forward on the issue. It is not a partisan, point-scoring issue: we want to achieve a result.

The new clause is phrased with great care so as not to dilute the excellent provisions elsewhere in the Bill for dealing with racially motivated offences. We considered whether to replicate the wording of the race provisions exactly but decided that that would not be helpful. We thus considered the other avenue of what happens in the court process.

We were pleased to contribute to expanding the scope of racially motivated offences when my noble Friend Lord Dholakia persuaded the Government to accept an amendment on racially motivated criminal damage. As I said, we are in no way seeking to dilute such provisions; we want only to provide the prosecutor with a vital new tool in dealing with homophobic offences. We considered other groups that may suffer from intolerance and bigotry, but rejected the option of expanding the provisions more widely. I know that if a provision is expanded widely enough, it can cease to have any meaning whatever. However, in view of the evidence from Stonewall and others, there is a specific and defined problem of homophobic incidents, which can be tackled through legislation. Many thousands of our fellow citizens have direct and painful experience of the problem.

As I have said, the solution to the problem need not dilute other provisions. Giving the prosecutor a tool to use in an offence of this kind will not detract from the fact that he has a different tool to use in a racially motivated offence. There will not be much crossover. The Crown Prosecution Service will be dealing with a particular offence, and if a prosecutor is dealing with a homophobic crime he will be able to use the new tool provided in the new clause.

I said earlier that I wanted to quote from an august and learned source in support of our proposals. I wish to cite the current Home Secretary who wrote the foreword to the book on queer bashing and is explicit on the seriousness of the problem. He states:

    "The violence and fear to which gay people are subject is something which diminishes us all."

He also rightly said that it is not just a matter of law and order, but about underlying barriers of discrimination. He also made a useful speech to Stonewall on the eve of the general election last year. He launched the survey and I support the sentiments that he expressed. He said:

    "If we want to be tough on discrimination we have to be tough on crime too."

He pointed out that the poor, those from ethnic minorities and those who appear to have different life styles, such as gay and lesbian people, are more likely to be victims of crime than the general population.

The Home Secretary added:

    "As Stonewall's survey shows, only too graphically"

similar gangs to those who attacked Stephen Lawrence in the most tragic circumstances in London

    "have gone in for `queer bashing' and in some cases their victims too have been left for dead. Often, it starts in an apparently small way, with bullying. They begin with taunts and insults. They graduate to graffiti and criminal damage. Then, as they discover just how ineffective the law is in dealing with `lower level' disorder, they become intoxicated with the power they have over the community...It is why we are committed to a strategy of `zero tolerance', to make the streets safe for everyone, regardless of the colour of their skin or their sexual preference."

Mr. Edward Leigh (Gainsborough): I am listening with interest. Anybody who beats someone up because of his or her sexual orientation does something that is very wicked. However, anybody who beats someone up for any reason does something very wicked. It is wicked to beat people up because they are old, frail, have money or because they are black, brown, Protestant or whatever. How does one differentiate between the various motivations? Mr. Allan: I do not disagree with the hon. Gentleman's sentiments about the difficulties involved in defining levels of wickedness. There are two ways in which a court can pass judgment on a crime. The first is to consider the individual circumstances and pass a sentence relative to them. For example, if an elderly person were beaten in the most heinous circumstances, every normal court in the land would consider the victim, the assailant and the nature of the assault and pass a heavier sentence.

The second approach is to have enshrined in legislation particular offences or particular directions to courts that cover categories of people. Categories of people should be covered when two clear criteria are established. The first is that there is evidence that that category of people is regularly offended against purely and simply on the basis of what they are. Older people who are assaulted by muggers are not necessarily assaulted because they are old; they are assaulted because they are weak and the mugger wants to target a weak person.

The new clause makes it clear that hatred must be expressed at the time of the offence. It would not be a matter of guesswork. Assailants would make it clear that they are assaulting someone because he or she is queer or is black. Such offences take place and the categories of people involved can be clearly defined.

The second crucial criterion is based on the fact that public policy should send out a message to the victims in particular. Such crimes are drastically under-reported and it is crucial that all our citizens, no matter what their background, have confidence in the criminal justice system. One way of doing that is to make it clear that if such offences are taken to court, a statement will be made in open court. Whatever the sentence imposed, a statement will be made to show that it was a crime of bigotry and of hatred.

The new clause passes those tests. We considered expanding its scope to cover other groups; religious groups would pass similar tests and we are still considering tabling provisions for them. The community covered by the new clause passes the criteria that I have set out. It requires protection and it must have confidence in the criminal justice system. We welcome the interest that the Home Secretary has shown and the fact that he quite properly bracketed that community with other groups who suffer similar violence from frequently the same bigots.

The problem with the new clause is that it starts to promote a particular group in society for protection. All groups in society should be protected. Blind people should be protected. Old people should be protected. Religious groups should be protected. The nature of our society should surely be that we are all equal before the law although we are all very different. The severe difficulty with the new clause is that if the hon. Gentleman singles out a group, other groups who might feel threatened in particular circumstances will also demand protection. The hon. Gentleman might say that that was fair enough; that a group might feel threatened because of its racial origin. However, other groups of which we are not immediately aware might also feel threatened. The courts should be given full powers to deal with that and to impose lengthy sentences. I have listened to numerous accusations of changing and stealing political clothes, so I wish to quote Juvenal about Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus, two brothers who were noted revolutionaries in ancient Rome.
