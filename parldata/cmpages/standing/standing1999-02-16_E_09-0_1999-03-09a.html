Unfair dismissal of striking workers

Question proposed [this day], That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking the following: Government new clauses—Unfair dismissal of striking workers.

Government new Schedule 1—Unfair Dismissal of Striking Workers—

    `Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992

    1. The Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 shall be amended as follows.

    2. In section 238 (dismissals in connection with industrial action) after subsection (2A) there shall be inserted—

    ``(2B) Subsection (2) does not apply in relation to an employee who is regarded as unfairly dismissed by virtue of section 238A below.''

    3. The following shall be inserted after section 238—

    ``Participation in official industrial action.

    238A.—(1) For the purposes of this section an employee takes protected industrial action if he commits an act which, or a series of acts each of which, he is induced to commit by an act which by virtue of section 219 is not actionable in tort.

    (2) An employee who is dismissed shall be regarded for the purposes of Part X of the Employment Rights Act 1996 (unfair dismissal) as unfairly dismissed if—

    (a) the reason (or, if more than one, the principal reason) for the dismissal is that the employee took protected industrial action, and

    (b) subsection (3), (4) or (5) applies to the dismissal.

    (3) This subsection applies to a dismissal if it takes place within the period of eight weeks beginning with the day on which the employee started to take protected industrial action.

    (4) This subsection applies to a dismissal if—

    (a) it takes place after the end of that period, and

    (b) the employee had stopped taking protected industrial action before the end of that period.

    (5) This subsection applies to a dismissal if—

    (a) it takes place after the end of that period,

    (b) the employee had not stopped taking protected industrial action before the end of that period, and

    (c) the employer had not taken such procedural steps as would have been reasonable for the purposes of resolving the dispute to which the protected industrial action relates.

    (6) In determining whether an employer has taken those steps regard shall be had, in particular, to—

    (a) whether the employer or a union had complied with procedures established by any applicable collective or other agreement;

    (b) whether the employer or a union offered or agreed to commence or resume negotiations after the start of the protected industrial action;

    (c) whether the employer or a union unreasonably refused, after the start of the protected industrial action, a request that conciliation services be used;

    (d) whether the employer or a union unreasonably refused, after the start of the protected industrial action, a request that mediation services be used in relation to procedures to be adopted for the purposes of resolving the dispute.

    (7) In determining whether an employer has taken those steps no regard shall be had to the merits of the dispute.

    (8) For the purposes of this section no account shall be taken of the repudiation of any act by a trade union as mentioned in section 21 in relation to anything which occurs before the end of the next working day (within the meaning of section 237) after the day on which the repudiation takes place.''

    4.—(1) Section 239 (supplementary provisions relating to unfair dismissal) (shall be amended as follows.

    (2) In subsection (1) for ``Sections 237 and 238'' there shall be substituted ``Sections 237 to 238A''.

    (3) At the end of subsection (1) there shall be added ``; but section 108 and 109 of that Act (qualifying period and age limit) shall not apply in relation to section 238A of this Act.''

    (4) In subsection (2) after ``section 238'' there shall be inserted ``or 238A''.

    (5) At the end there shall be added—

    ``(4) In relation to a complaint under section 111 of the 1996 Act (unfair dismissal: complaint to employment tribunal) that a dismissal was unfair by virtue of section 238A of this Act—

    (a) no order shall be made under section 113 of the 1996 Act (reinstatement or re-engagement) until after the conclusion of protected industrial action by any employee in relation to the relevant dispute,

    (b) regulations under section 7 of the Employment Tribunals Act 1996 may make provision about the adjournment and renewal of applications (including provision requiring adjournment in specified circumstances), and

    (c) regulations under section 9 of that Act may require a pre-hearing review to be carried out in specified circumstances.''

Amendments (e), (f) and (h) deal with reasonable steps. The essential reality of the employer-employee relationship is that the employer dismisses people. He or she makes the decision, and issues the dismissal letter and the P45. Employees do not sack themselves, and unions do not sack their members on behalf of employers.

In assessing the rights and wrongs of a dismissal, it is essential that the employer's behaviour is examined. The tribunal must, first and foremost, consider whether an employer has acted fairly in the case. We have drafted a schedule, which provides that it is up to an employer who dismisses an employee outside the eight-week period to show that he or she has acted fairly. The complainant must show that the employer has acted unfairly. The test of fairness is whether an employer has taken all reasonable procedural steps to resolve the dispute.

The amendment would blur the issue and lead to confusion. That does not mean that tribunals cannot or should not take account of the behaviour of other interested parties. They could and should do that. We are providing for assessing whether the employer has acted fairly. The employer does not live in a vacuum.

Amendment (g) is about negotiating in good faith. Again, we may share a common objective: we, too, want to encourage negotiated settlements. I think that the hon. Member for Daventry concedes the advantage of ensuring that the new protections against unfair dismissal may offer some incentive to the parties to resume negotiations before dismissals.

We have tried to draft such incentives in a way that is clear, objective and reasonable. The hon. Gentleman, however, wants to add an important caveat to our wording. In effect, he wants the tribunals to judge whether the parties—the employers or the unions—had acted ``in good faith'' when they offered to open or resume negotiations. In reaching a judgment, the tribunals would therefore have to assess the motivations of the parties. It is notoriously difficult to apply a good faith test. That would lead to countless arguments about what may or may not have influenced either party's behaviour, and would inevitably widen the scope for the parties to appeal against the tribunal's decisions.

Our proposals deal with the sort of problem that the hon. Member for Daventry raised but without resorting to tests of good faith. Under our proposals, the tribunal would be asked to consider whether an employer had taken reasonable steps before dismissing an employee. Whenever a procedural step was proposed, the tribunals would have to consider whether it was reasonable in the circumstances. If an offer was made to resume negotiations, the tribunals would have to judge whether it was reasonable for the employer to make an offer to resume negotiations, or to refuse to accept an offer to negotiate from the union. They could not judge on the facts whether an offer was reasonable.

For example, an employer might offer to open negotiations on the last day of the eight-week period and sack the work force the next day. The union would have no reasonable chance to respond to the offer, let alone begin the negotiations. Such an offer would be empty and would not represent a reasonable step. That point was made by the Confederation of British Industry and the scenarios painted by the hon. Member for Daventry are catered for in full. The amendment is therefore unnecessary and unworkable.

Amendment (i) deals with unofficial action. It has been a long-standing feature of trade union law, and the policy of this and previous Governments, to discourage unofficial industrial action. Such action can be particularly damaging to employment relations. It is by nature difficult to control and resolve. The main agency for negotiating a settlement—the union—is absent.

Under existing law, some protections are given against unfair dismissal if workers are dismissed selectively when taking official industrial action. Those protections do not apply when the action is unofficial. Subsection (4) of section 237 of the Trade Union and Labour Relations (Consolidation) Act 1992 describes what would happen if official action became unofficial because the union had repudiated it. The protection against selective dismissal would be withdrawn at the end of the next working day following the repudiation. We have adopted the same time limit in relation to the new protection that the Bill introduces.

There is no evidence that the existing time limit has caused problems in the past. For example, no one has suggested that the time period is too short for the workers concerned to learn of the repudiation, or that there is insufficient time for them or the employer to adapt their behaviour.

I am pleased that, in this regard at least, the hon. Member for Daventry, appears anxious to extend the employment rights of striking workers. However, his good intentions are misplaced on this occasion. I cannot support the amendment.

Amendment (j) relates to reinstatement orders. Tribunals cannot issue reinstatement orders until the strike is over. It would make no sense for them to issue such orders when the employees are unwilling to work. The hon. Member for Daventry and I are agreed on that matter: the question is when the tribunal should be empowered to issue reinstatement orders. We propose that the tribunal could issue such orders from any point after the action has ceased. He wants to put that period back by a week. It is important to remember that these are employees who have been dismissed unfairly. When the strike is over, most will be ready and willing to return to work, so what purpose will be served in delaying their return? We hope that in the majority of cases, employers will voluntarily offer to reinstate the workers following the cessation of action. They will not wait for a tribunal to issue an order of reinstatement when the few cases in which they do not would not impose a further delay in relation to workers' entitlements.

The hon. Member for Daventry may argue that employers need time to prepare for the return to work of their employees. However, he should bear in mind that tribunals will not make an order for reinstatement until the industrial action has ended. We are therefore giving employers the duration of the action to make provision for the return of their workers. How much more time do they need? Reasonable employers, having suffered damage to their business operations, will want normal service to be resumed as soon as possible.

It is up to tribunals and the parties to determine how to deal with these cases. However, tribunals already have the power to delay holding the full hearing until what they regard as a suitable time. Tribunals are prepared to exercise such powers especially when the parties ask for a delay. Tribunals can hear a case and decide whether dismissal was fair or unfair or they can delay ordering the remedy until a later hearing. Tribunals may choose that course when they think that the parties can agree a remedy between themselves. That option may be very attractive to tribunals when considering dismissed strikers, because part of the settlement of the strike may involve reinstatement.

If tribunals award compensation while the strike is continuing, it might be low. For example, the tribunal can award compensation for loss of earnings between the day of the dismissal and the hearing if the worker was on strike all of that time. The hon. Member for Altrincham and Sale, West asked when an employee can be dismissed under normal disciplinary procedures after industrial action has begun. He cites the case for procedures to be invoked before the action is started. In such circumstances, the principal reason for dismissal is unconnected to industrial action. The individual will not be able to claim unfair dismissal just because he or she was taking industrial action at the time. The dismissal would relate to the behaviour of the individual concerned. The tribunal would examine such dismissals in the usual way, judging whether an employer had acted reasonably in the circumstances.
