Clause 1

General right of access to information

held by public authorities

Mr. Mark Fisher (Stoke-on-Trent, Central): I beg to move amendment No. 18, in page 1, line 12, at end insert—

    `(1A) A public authority shall take all reasonable steps to assist any person in seeking to exercise any right under this Act.'.

I am delighted that the Government have placed the statement of general right of access to information at the head of the Bill. It is not a purposes clause; no doubt we shall have an opportunity later to consider the virtues of that, which I would press. However, the present statement of a right to access is a move in that direction. The purpose of the Bill is to help people and organisations obtain access to information to which they previously did not have legal rights, and it should therefore do all that is reasonably possible to that end. It is a matter of attitude. Other freedom of information regimes which have informed the debate in this country, and which have been examined by the Government, vary in their shaping of the law. However, their experience, which, in the case of Commonwealth countries such as Canada, Australia and New Zealand, stretches over 18 years, shows that the manner of implementation—the Government's attitude—is every bit as important as the law itself.

Clearly, the Committee's responsibility is to prepare the law as well as possible, but I expect that it will also give frequent attention to the matter of implementation, which is very important. This aspect of the Bill needs strengthening. There could be a much more positive approach to helping people and organisations gain access. Some organisations and people, such as big media companies, experienced and skilled investigative journalists and large charities and campaigning organisations, will have no trouble. They will have legal advice. They already understand the relevant legislation and they will not often be inconvenienced by difficulties over access. However, the Bill is, as the Home Secretary and Under-Secretary stressed on Second Reading, for individuals as much as organisations, and it will be difficult for those individuals to become familiar with it and find their way round it. It is a new measure, providing new opportunities, and its very novelty means that people will not have any experience and will not know their rights. No one ever pretends that laws passed by this House are presented in a way friendly to the members of the public who might read them. Hon. Members of many years' standing struggle through and need much hand-holding help to understand all the implications of the drafting of Bills.

The Bill will not be user friendly for the public. I accept that the Government have done what they can to make it literate and clear, but no legislation, for good legal reasons, is transparent to the public. Therefore, we must do everything that we can to ensure that the public know what information is available to them and how they can easily access it. To produce legislation of which we are proud, the Government need to provide easy access to it by implementing a large public information and advertising exercise comparable to the previous Government's launch of charter marks. Although we must take the trouble to open up people's minds to what is available, they still need help to know their rights—to understand what is and is not available to them—so that they know how to apply for access to information.

It is clear that much can go wrong. Various clauses identify what constitutes an invalid request. Clause 1(3) entitles a public authority legally to turn down an inadequately identified request. Clause 7(1)(a) and (b) respectively allow requests to be rejected if they are applied for orally rather than in writing and where a correspondence address is not provided. Clause 11 makes it clear that a request can be rejected when the cost of complying exceeds the appropriate level, which is, understandably, not defined. Those would be innocent, easy mistakes for individuals to make.

Behind those four specific tripping points we may discern the Government's attitude. Although proud of the Bill, they want people to go through hoops or risk a request being turned down. That may be necessary in some circumstances, but as a general attitude that informs the Bill, it does not seem helpful or right. Amendment No. 18 puts a statutory obligation on public bodies to assist individuals or authorities to make a request. After all, the people who hold and form the information are the experts. It is not beyond their wits to understand the intention behind an application. It would not greatly inconvenience Government Departments or public authorities or bodies to have the good manners to assist, in a co-operative spirit, anyone who genuinely wanted information. Many aspects of the Bill deal with frivolous or vexatious information, but anyone who genuinely wants information should be able to look to the authority to which they are applying for help.

My hon. Friend the Minister will probably say that clause 17 allows for a code of practice, but that is not the same thing; it is unenforceable. Although the previous Government's stab at regulating legislation in their code of practice was not bad, will all agree that a code of practice contains weaknesses. People will need help, especially in the early states and that is a crucial consideration. The legal obligation for authorities to assist them should not be covered by a code of practice, but included in the clause. An amendment, such as mine, is necessary to require bodies to have a legal and statutory obligation to assist authorities or individuals.

Problems with the appeals mechanism, which is dealt with in clause 15 and elsewhere, will make the need for that more apparent. Clause 50 allows requests to be rejected if an applicant was slow to complain or suffered because of undue delay. Furthermore, under clause 15, the body from which information is sought is under no obligation to inform applicants of their rights of appeal. Such matters are small and niggling, but they form a colour wash throughout the Bill whereby authorities can challenge an applicant and say, ``You must justify to us that you know what you are asking for; what it is all about; that you know how to apply and what your rights are, and how to appeal if you are turned down.''

In the early stages, the Government will have the task of helping members of the public to understand the potential of the Bill and not placing small barriers in their way. The duty to assist under amendment No. 18 will help individuals along those lines as does other freedom of information legislation. For example, under New Zealand law, there is a duty on every department to give reasonable assistance. In Australia, there is a duty on agencies to take reasonable steps to assist. Under legislation in Ireland, public bodies have a duty to give reasonable assistance. It has not been found that such legislation places an enormously cumbersome, onerous or expensive additional obligation on public bodies.

The amendment is realistic and constructive. I accept that it is asking a lot of the Government to accept the first amendment that has been tabled in Committee, but if they could, it will be a genuine case of the Government demonstrating their desire to ensure that people will benefit from the Bill. For that to take place, a statutory obligation should be placed on public bodies to assist applicants.

In her oral evidence, Elizabeth France said, in criticism of the draft Bill.

    ``I think what I find lacking, when I look as the potential enforcement authority, at the FOI Bill is what I could use to hang a view that a discretionary decision had been improperly taken. If you have not got something which says `One of the weights and balances is the desire to have as much access as possible to public information' . . . then you are lacking something to go with the weight on the other side which says that there is a fundamental right to privacy where it is personal data and you lack something to assist decisions on whether a disclosure is obligatory or discretionary.''

The Government have heard those arguments before and have modestly responded to the desirability of a purpose clause with two minor modifications to the original draft. The first is to change the long title so that the phrase ``to make provision about'' becomes ``to make provision for'' the disclosure of information. The second change, which I advocated, was to the order of clauses, making the right of access the first. The Public Administration Committee did not, however, consider those changes adequate. It noted in its report that

    ``there is still no clear presumption in favour of disclosure.''

We are not treading on wholly uncharted territory in advocating the development in question. Some countries that have introduced freedom of information legislation have already taken that course, and I draw the Committee's attention to the example of New Zealand. Its Official Information Act 1982 included just such a purpose clause. That is not precisely the basis of my new clause but it sets out a broad approach that I would recommend the Committee to adopt. It is worth noting that in 1992 a leading commentator noted that the purpose clause

    ``ensured that the number, tenor and generality of the exemptions would not overshadow the raison d'etre of the Act.''

That, it seems to me, is a valuable thought to keep in mind in considering the Bill, for although the right of access is set out in four lines, it appears at first blush to be heavily counterbalanced by the huge number of exemptions that constitute a large part of the Bill, running to more than 12 pages. There is a question of discretion. Deciding the bounds of public interest and where the duties for and against disclosure should be balanced to conform with the purpose of the Bill is not straightforward. Where there is a need for a decision, and the arguments for and against disclosure are clearly finely balanced, a purpose clause will make it simpler to come down on the side of publication, if that is the legislation's intention.

In a New Zealand case following the use of that provision, the judge said:

    ``If the decision-maker, be he Minister or departmental head or Ombudsman or Judge, adjudicating in a claim of denial of right, is in two minds in the end, he should come down on the side of availability of information.''

That ought to be the position here. The judge was able to say that because the New Zealand Act provided guidance in that respect. The Act exemption contained statutory public interest tests; the ombudsman has often referred to the purposes clause in the Act when considering those tests. In one case, the New Zealand ombudsman said:

    ``I also noted that the Trust received public funding. I pointed out that the Official Information Act is an instrument to promote accountability . . . it could go without saying that one of the primary elements of the accountability of Ministers and officials is their responsibility for expenditure of the funds voted by Parliament. The various provisions of the section headed `interpretation'{**?h 1pt**}''—

that is, the purposes clause—

    ``must, in my view, be read with that purpose in mind.''

We should bear in mind several helpful examples from that jurisdiction when considering the appropriateness of the amendments.

In this country, we have given specific and clear protection to the individual's privacy, not merely by the Data Protection Acts, but by article 8 of the European convention on human rights. That makes a counter-balancing general right and purpose, which we should entertain.

My amendment would emphasise that the Bill was intended to extend progressively the public's right to information, as is the case under New Zealand law. There is probably common ground between the Government, others and myself on that point. It is not a once and for all situation—interpretation will be required, rules will have to be drawn up and the operations of specific authorities will have to be adapted to comply with the new openness and accountability that the Bill is intended to promote. It is clear from clause 17 that the intention is for authorities to produce publication schemes setting out the information that they publish and intend to publish. Those will have to be reviewed from time to time.

Such progressiveness is part of the argument for the enshrining in law of a purpose, in line with which development is intended to occur. The amendment specifies four interests that the Bill is intended to promote: the accountability of public authorities, informed public debate and public affairs, public participation in decision making and public understanding of the powers, duties and operations of public authorities. Those interests informed the attitude of the Government when they first published their proposals. Most recently, the approach towards the purpose clause was set out in paragraph 105 of the paper entitled ``Your Right to Know—Background Material'', published in January 1998 by the Cabinet Office, which was then responsible for the Bill. It was envisaged that

    ``The definition of `public interest' for these purposes might be set in terms of certain stated objectives of the Act e.g. in promoting better informed discussion of public affairs and the greater accountability of public authorities.''

In making those proposals, I am merely following the Government's logic so clearly expressed in that document.

I said earlier that I would mention a couple of examples in which purpose clauses had been incorporated recently in our legislation. The Arbitration Act 1996 states:

    ``The provisions of this Part are founded on the following principles and should be construed accordingly—(a) the object of arbitration is to obtain the fair resolution of disputes . . . (b) the parties should be free to agree how their disputes are resolved . . . (c) in matters governed by the part the court should not intervene except as provided by this part.''

Similarly, section 37 of the Government's Crime and Disorder Act 1998 stated that:

    ``It shall be the principal aim of the youth justice system to prevent offending by children and young persons.

    (2) In addition to any other duty to which they are subject, it shall be the duty of all persons and bodies carrying out functions in relation to the youth justice system to have regard to that aim.''

Although purpose clauses have not always been regarded as necessary or helpful, that has increasingly become a part of our jurisprudence. The officer principally made responsible by the Government's decision regards it as a part that we should incorporate.
