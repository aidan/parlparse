Dr. Vincent Cable (Twickenham): I beg to move amendment No. 48, in page 2, line 38, at end insert--

    '(c) promoting education to develop the skills people need to interpret and use information about financial products and the financial system; and

    (d) promoting awareness of the Authority, the compensation scheme and the ombudsman scheme.'.

The Chairman: With this we may discuss amendment No. 49, in page 2, line 38, at end insert--

    '(2A) In pursuing its public awareness objective, the Authority shall submit annually to the Secretary of State for Education and Employment a report on its progress.'.

Dr. Cable: The amendments relate to the requirement on the Financial Services Authority to promote public awareness, and would specify more precisely the FSA's educational role and how that should be monitored.

It may be objected that the amendments are worthy but unnecessary. I shall therefore spell out why they are necessary in relation to the functions of financial services markets. It is generally understood by consumer groups and others that major gaps in information and knowledge prevent retail markets for financial services products from working well. The Office of Fair Trading recently produced a report entitled "Consumer Detriment", which characterised the market selling financial products in terms of imperfect information.

The financial markets do not work well, as information is deficient in a number of ways. Much product information is complex. Transactions are often undertaken once in a lifetime; they are not like a weekly shopping expedition to the supermarket, and require particular knowledge--which is often lacking--at a particular stage in life.

Perhaps most importantly, the commission-driven nature of the retail business frequently creates misleading and distorting information. I am sure that many of us have experience of buying a personal pension policy. I bought such a policy about 10 years ago and was confronted by a salesman who told me that the first two years of contributions would be used to make up his fee. When I queried that, he told me, "Well, this happens whatever policy is bought. Our competitors hide the commissions; nevertheless, they are present." Millions of people have bought complex products in that opaque market.

That is why more is needed to meet the FSA's public-awareness objective than merely stating that it is a general aim and loading more information onto customers. A key problem in the market is that information is complex and so, in itself, is not helpful. The amendments would make that process more specific and would guide the FSA, ensuring that successive generations of FSA officials understood the concrete implications of the objective.

Amendment No. 48 would require the FSA to be concerned with developing consumers' skills, not merely pushing information at them. Those of us who follow the FSA's activities know that is already consulting quite extensively with the Department for Education and Employment, the university for industry and various consumer bodies. That is all very worthwhile and much appreciated.

Amendment No. 48 would explicitly oblige the FSA to help develop skills and would ensure that that obligation was embedded for future generations. We have built into the amendment a specific requirement that the FSA should make people aware of its complaints procedures and the ombudsman. Last week, we had an extended debate on the difficulties and limitations of the complaints procedure. The FSA should, at the very least, make its consumers aware of the existence of that procedure. There are deficiencies in the structure. It is a captured organisation to some extent.

Amendment No. 49 spells out the educational requirements more specifically. As I have said, the FSA is already making a considerable effort to raise schoolchildren's awareness of financial products and the difficulties that they may have later when they transact financial services. Survey data suggests that a large number of people leaving school acknowledge both the importance of the subject and the great deficiency in the education that is currently provided. We suggest a small amendment which requires the FSA not merely to report its education activities in its annual report, but to submit that report to the DFEE. That provides a cross check with the work of the curriculum council.

We share a common belief here. There is no enormous controversy about the importance of public awareness. We all acknowledge that the FSA is performing a useful role at present in building up its public awareness activities. The amendments aim to make those obligations more specific and to ensure that when the FSA comes under different management in future there is no backsliding in its commitment to those ends.

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Ms Patricia Hewitt): I am grateful to the hon. Member for Twickenham (Dr. Cable) for tabling the amendments and explaining his concerns. All members of the Committee, and certainly the Government, are committed to promoting greater public awareness and the education of consumers. The public awareness objective is perhaps one of the most radical innovations among the Bill's objectives. In drafting the clause we have made the objective quite general so as not to tie the FSA down to any particular course of action.

When we consulted on the draft Bill last summer, a large proportion of respondents commented, both on the regulatory objectives as a whole and on this one in particular. Some who came to the issue from the consumer perspective were worried that it did not go far enough. They could envisage all kinds of useful ways in which the FSA might be able to improve public awareness. Those who were concerned about the implications of the objective for the industry thought that it might require the FSA to do more than was appropriate for a regulator. They were also concerned--in my view, reasonably--about whether a body founded by fees levied on the regulated community should add to those costs in pursuing this general objective.

I mention that because it underlines the fact that a balance has to be struck. I do not believe that the consumer bodies were seeking to suggest that the FSA's main focus should be on education rather than regulation. Nor did the industry argue that the FSA was not well placed to give helpful advice and information to members of the public. Indeed, some well known institutions have educational initiatives themselves.

What we have tried to do in Clause 4--I think we have achieved it--is to strike that balance between the enormous number of things that the FSA could do and the reasonable concerns of the industry. We have given the FSA considerable flexibility about how to meet its objectives. It might, for instance, want to issue publications or publish advertisements on its own initiative. It might want to work with other bodies, such as the Office of Fair Trading and consumer bodies, to produce information that consumers would find helpful. It might want to work with citizens advice bureaux and it certainly might want to prepare information that could be used as teaching resources in schools, or to direct people to other useful sources of information and guidance. What will be appropriate will depend on the circumstances.

The FSA does not have a free hand and it will be constrained in meeting its objectives by the principles in clause 2. That is right, because the regulated community should not be viewed as a bottomless pit for funding general awareness and educational activities. There is a clear consensus, however, that the FSA has a valuable role to play and that a better educated public will have wider benefits for the effectiveness and the effective working of competition.

In our debate on competition and competitiveness, the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham (Mr. Loughton) said:

    "The most effective form of regulation and the most effective way of bringing about competition is by the education of the consumer. There must be an educating function so that the consumer--for whom we are here, after all--can make appropriate and informed choices".--[Official Report, Standing Committee A, 8 July 1999; c.48.]

I agree entirely and that objective is spelt out in clause 4.

The objectives are expressed in general terms because they relate to a wide spectrum of activities and people. The danger with the amendments is that trying to make the objectives more specific runs the risk that they will apply only in limited circumstances. Other elements would then have to be added to deal with other circumstances. Instead of having a broad objective that could be interpreted by the FSA--and, if necessary, by the courts--we would end up requiring a much longer Bill and a much longer clause in which everything that the FSA could do is spelt out. We would all agree that the Bill is long enough, without going down that road.

Amendment No. 48 relates to what the FSA might do. Clause 4(2) defines the FSA's public awareness objective as

    "promoting public understanding of the financial system"

and amplifies that definition by making it clear that that general objective includes

    "promoting awareness of the benefits and risks associated with different kinds of investment or other financial dealing"

and

    "the provision of appropriate information and advice".

The fact that certain elements are not listed does not mean that they are not covered: clause 4(2) is simply illustrative. We believe that the clause as a whole, with its illustrative paragraphs, certainly cover, in the words of the hon. Member for Twickenham's amendment,

    "promoting education to develop the skills people need to interpret and use information about financial products and the financial system"

I am not persuaded of the value of repeating something that is already inherent in the clause.

It is worth noting that the FSA has also agreed about the benefits of using the education system to promote awareness. Some hon. Members may be aware that the FSA is already working with the Department for Education and Employment--for example, in considering the effectiveness of the national curriculum in England and how financial education might be incorporated in it. Ideas being considered include financial examples--learning about interest rates when teaching mathematics and including financial services in proposals for personal, social and health education. The FSA would not, of course, be involved with teaching those subjects, but it would be able to help with the development of teaching resources and the curriculum. The FSA is already applying the objective in precisely the flexible way that we want.

The proposed new paragraph (d) would bring promotion of the FSA and the compensation and ombudsman schemes more clearly within the scope of the awareness objective. Again, those are already covered by the generality of the clause. It is worth referring briefly to clause 127, which gives the FSA a clear function of giving advice and information about several matters, including its functions under the Bill and

    "any other matters about which it appears to the Authority to be desirable to give information or advice."

Those matters undoubtedly include both the compensation and the ombudsman schemes. Amendment No. 48 is not necessary because the Bill already provides what it seeks, in clauses 4 and 157.
