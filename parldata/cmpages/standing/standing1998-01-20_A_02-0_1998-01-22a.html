The amendment would ensure that the balance of advantage of having smaller class sizes rests with the parents of those children who are directly affected. I have been involved in the education system, in one way or another, in London, in Cornwall and now in Surrey, and I know of no one, of any party, who maintains that class size does not matter. However, that is not the same as saying that class size should be the overriding consideration. Other factors are important.

In its press release of 4 December 1997, the Department for Education and Employment announced the result of a survey. Indeed, it was given quite a spin by Labour spin doctors. They applauded the fact that 35 per cent. of parents cited lower class sizes as their top priority. However, the same press release stated that 38 per cent. of parents regarded a greater emphasis on basics as being the top priority and that almost 30 per cent. of parents wanted schools to promote greater potential involvement. It was with the aim of promoting greater parental involvement that we tabled the amendment.

It is worth bearing in mind that the survey was based on responses to a leaflet distributed in supermarkets and high street stores. The press release pats itself on the back by saying that the response showed that the top priorities for parents were the Department’s top priorities. Well done indeed, but that was a self-selecting response. I imagine that most of those who responded to the survey would be inclined to agree with it. We cannot read an endorsement of the Government’s plans into the result.

In moving towards smaller class sizes, we need to ensure an element of parent power as well as state power. The problem of the 31st child has been mentioned, but we should also remember the problem of the sibling and what happens when parents want their children to go to a Church school. Religious education is dealt with more thoroughly in clause 54—sorry, I mean clause 56!

It is worth noting the Government’s aims. The White Paper states:

    “We have pledged to reduce class sizes for 5, 6 and 7 year-olds to 30 or below within the next five years.”

That is an example of a slogan being put before sense. To promise to deliver class sizes of 30 or below for all children in that age group is to make an effective and catching slogan, but many parents regard the possible side effects to its implementation as unacceptable.

The White Paper says:

    “Research evidence shows the importance of class size for younger children.”

Some research may show that, but there is plenty of conflicting evidence. In the London area, the boroughs of Bromley and Richmond have some of the highest educational achievements, yet they also have some of the largest class sizes in the area.

There is also a debate about how much a class size needs to be reduced before there is any tangible effect. My hon. Friend the Member for Tiverton and Honiton (Mrs. Browning) suggested that a figure of 24 would have to be reached. In the opening summary of the scholarly paper written by Blatchford and Mortimore in the Oxford Review of Education, the authors state:

    “It is concluded that there is now firm evidence of a link”—

between class size and educational attainment—

    “but only in the early years and only with classes smaller than 20. The evidence supports the reduction of class sizes in the first years at school, especially with disadvantaged pupils, but much still needs to be researched.”

Indeed it does. The authors highlight the findings of three other eminent researchers who flatly contradict their own findings. In 1990, Tomlinson found that

    “Reducing class size to the point where student achievement would likely benefit . . . is prohibitively expensive.”

In 1989, Slavin wrote:

    “The evidence . . . suggests that . . . reducing class size will not itself make a substantial difference in student achievement.”

In 1992, Burstall wrote:

    “The outcomes of this research effort (into the connection between class size and educational attainments) having been conflicting, inconclusive and disappointingly meagre.”

I am indebted to the excellent Library research paper 97/136, which was produced for members of the Committee. It cites research from the United States, including the STAR research project in Tennessee, of which it says:

    “In that study pupils in small classes (13-17)”—

in roughly the five to eight-year-old group—

    “performed significantly better than pupils in regular classes (22-25).”

The Government are not talking about reducing class sizes to those levels—the clause would not achieve that, especially if it is unamended.
