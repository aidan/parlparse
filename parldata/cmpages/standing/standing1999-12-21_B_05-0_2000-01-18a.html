Clause 11

Exemption where cost of compliance exceeds appropriate limit

Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 33, in page 6, line 38, at end insert `substantially similar.—[Mr. Maclennan.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Chairman: I remind the Committee that with this we are taking amendment No, 34, in page 6, line 40, leave out from `person' to end of line 42.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): It has been suggested that this provision will prevent members of campaign groups from seeking information from public authorities, but I do not believe that it will have that effect. Where such people ask for identical information, they will receive it, unless the cost of doing so in one case exceeded the limit, because there would be only one cost associated with finding that information. The cost of sending out the information will not fall within the calculation of the limit. Where such people ask for different information, for the purposes of the provision, they will not be acting in concert. The provision will apply only where each person seeks a subset of the same information, and the limitation cost to the authority is thereby evaded. Those circumstances will be prescribed in regulations.

Under freedom of information regulations, the amendments could inhibit public authorities from carrying out their main tasks. In particular, smaller public authorities could be prejudiced. The circumstances in which aggregation will be permitted will be set out in the regulations. Should problems arise, they will be subject to annulment by Parliament, so I ask the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross (Mr. Maclennan) to withdraw the amendments.

Mr. John Greenway (Ryedale): I beg to move amendment No. 102, in page 6, line 44, at end insert

    `divided by a whole number, prescribed in regulations, which is greater than the total number of requests.'.

We have been discussing the extent of which aggregate requests for information could fall foul of a cost aggregation. Given the Under-Secretary's comments, I suspect that subsection (4) may be subject to different interpretations. We have no quibble with attempts to reduce public cost and to avoid several members of a campaign group making the same request for the same information. However, subsection (4) does not make it clear that the appropriate limit will increase where several items of information are requested. Such a circumstance need not involve a number of campaign members asking the same question. An individual who seeks redress after an accident or other incident may want to discover what relevant information is available.

We assume that the Government have in mind some form of sliding scale. Clearly, where several items of information are requested, one cannot roll up the appropriate limit according to the number of requests. In such a case, the appropriate limit should differ from that which applies to a single request. Regulations on fees and aggregation should not enable public authorities to refuse to supply the same information to, say, five or six people who share the same problem, on the ground that the appropriate limit has thereby been exceeded. Although it would be wrong for the appropriate limit to apply only in respect of each and every application, the provision of a sliding scale would be a suitable halfway house.

We toiled over the appropriate wording for the amendment. It would require the cost of complying with requests for information to be

    ``divided by a whole number, prescribed in regulations, which is greater than the total number of requests.''.

That would provide a sliding scale for assessing the appropriate limit. It would give comfort to those who are concerned—as, to some extent, are we—that subsection (4) will work to their disadvantage by enabling public authorities to shield themselves from providing information on the ground that the aggregation of such requests has exceeded the appropriate limit. That would not be right, but nor would it be right for the appropriate limit to apply to each and every request. There must be some aggregation.

We all want to avoid wasted cost, and the amendment is perhaps the way to deal with it. I commend it to the Under-Secretary.

Mr. O'Brien: I accept that the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) is trying to be helpful.

Mr. Stephen Day (Cheadle): That is the point at which the hon. Gentleman is most dangerous.

Mr. O'Brien: The hon. Gentleman says from a sedentary position that that is the point at which the hon. Member for Ryedale is most dangerous, but I am sure that his attempts to help are genuine.

The hon. Member for Ryedale has suggested a sliding scale, but, if I may be self-critical for a moment, the problem is that the Bill is complex. It is supposed to be a people's Bill, and one would have liked it to be readily intelligible to the ordinary member of the public who seeks information, but we must get the balance right. Given the variety of public authorities and the various circumstances in which information might be requested, the Bill has to be complex. However, a Bill that is already complex should not be unnecessarily complicated further, and I fear that that is what the amendment would do.

The amendment would prevent a public authority from refusing to deal with a series of requests from one person, or from different people who are, or appear to be, acting in concert, if the unit cost of each request is less than the appropriate limit, which will be set in regulation. Because the amendment would require the total cost to be divided by a number that is greater than the total number of requests, the unit cost will be less than the actual cost. Without the amendment, an authority will be able to refuse to reply if the aggregate cost of doing so exceeds the limit.

The purpose of subsection (4) is to prevent an applicant or a group of applicants evading the appropriate limit by packaging requests in a series of smaller ones. The Government believe that people should have a right of access to information at a low cost, but that that right should be subject to sensible limits. We have already announced that we are minded to set the appropriate limit for authorities at £500, and to restrict the cost that can be included in that calculation to the marginal cost of locating and finding the information in question. For information to be disclosed under clause 1, we have further made it clear that the charge to applicants will not exceed 10 per cent. of those marginal costs.

Under the Government's proposals, an applicant would receive a subsidy from the taxpayer of about £450 before the cost limits came into play. We believe that it would be wrong for individuals to ask the taxpayer to provide a greater subsidy than £450 per request. That is a substantial sum and we want to ensure a proper balance. We can do that by limiting the subsidy to 10 per cent. of the marginal costs. We shall provide some subsidy, but it will not be excessive.

I was asked whether it would be wrong, in particular circumstances, to aggregate the costs of five or six people. We cannot provide for that; the regulations are intended to deal with requests that are divided into a number of separate requests as a means of defeating the cost of the application. I accept that the hon. Member for Ryedale seeks to be helpful and that his suggestion has some benefits, but such a means of dealing with requests does not command the Government's support.

Mr. Greenway: That is helpful. Perhaps I did not listen intently enough when we debated the matter on earlier amendments to clause 11—or perhaps the Under-Secretary did not explain it in such explicit terms. He now says that subsection (4) is meant to prevent people avoiding the appropriate limit by dividing up their requests into segments. I take it that the regulations will give priority to ensuring that public authorities cannot abuse the system in the other direction by falsely aggregating what are clearly stand-alone requests.

Mr. O'Brien: I can confirm that.

Mr. Greenway: That is excellent. I could not agree with the Under-Secretary more when he says that we should protect the interests of taxpayers. Indeed, during the stand part debate on clause 8, I resisted the temptation to say that for 90 per cent. of the cost to be borne by the taxpayer was a pretty hefty subsidy—so it is. Equally, however, we support the Government's objective. If a new culture of information is to be available to people, they should not be put off by the excess cost of applying for access to information.

We have probably flogged the matter sufficiently, but it has been helpful further to clarify the fact that subsection (4) will not be used as a means of preventing people from obtaining information to which they are entitled. It is a preventive measure, ensuring that people do not avoid the appropriate limit by dividing up their requests into various segments. If that is all that is intended, we are greatly reassured. We need to protect public authorities from abuse, but we must ensure that the regulations reflect the fact that public authorities should not use subsection (4) as a means of perpetrating abuse of bona fide applicants by wrongly aggregating requests. We are happy with the Under-Secretary's reassurance that that will not be the case. As I said earlier, we look forward to seeing the draft regulations. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.
