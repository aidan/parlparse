Mr. Forth: I accept your guidance, Mr. Wells, for which I am grateful.

Occasionally, we hear references to issues before us in the House or in Committee being matters of froth. Usually, that expression is only a term of art, or even a term of abuse--but in this case it is an accurate description of the Bill that we are about to discuss. Much of the debate will revolve around froth--or, to put it more accurately, the head of certain beers.

I emphasised the word ``certain'', because we must take into account the enormous number of beers that are available to beer drinkers in this country. I have done some homework on the matter. I concede that some Labour Members' experience of such matters far outweighs mine. In fact, many of them outweigh me--probably because of their appreciation of this subject.

When I looked up the figures, I found to my astonishment that, in this country, there are about 370 variants of draft cask-conditioned beer, 335 non-cask conditioned beers, 290 lagers, 430 light pale and export ales, 25 stouts and 30 brown ales. That shows the problem confronting anyone who seeks to standardise the delivery of beer, especially given the huge variety of outlets and products. That would be bad enough, but matters are further complicated by the head on beer.

Members might be interested to know that I have been given figures that show the variation in the head of beers, which is a mixture of liquid and gas, not just liquid, wherein lies another of the problems with which we must grapple today. There is 3 per cent. liquid in the head of a pint of Fuller's London Pride; 5 per cent. in a pint of Fosters lager; 25 to 33 per cent. in a pint of Guinness, 40 per cent. in a pint of Mansfield Smooth; 50 per cent. in a pint of John Smith Smooth. We are confronted by a variation in not only 1,500 kinds of beer--from draft cask-conditioned to stouts--but from 3 to 50 per cent. liquid in the head.

Mr. Forth: I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman; he helps to show the difficulty with which the Committee is faced in considering how to maximise customer confidence in the amount of beer that people receive in their local or any other place where beer is sold. The difficulty arises partly from the way that beer is traditionally delivered and partly--in contradiction to the sponsors' desire, which I well understand--from seeking to maximise customer confidence and satisfaction. There is no disagreement between us on that. The disagreement arises in trying to identify the best way in which that can be done. I believe that the existing arrangements are satisfactory, which is why I tabled the amendments.

Apart from anything else, I do not understand where the dissatisfaction arises. Before the Minister does so, I shall draw the Committee's attention to a letter that arrived by some mysterious process on his desk this morning from the Local Authorities Co-ordinating Body on Trading Standards, the body for which he has responsibility. Having seen these modest amendments, which I tabled on Monday and which appeared on the amendment paper yesterday, LACOTS told the Minister--I think that I paraphrase the letter fairly--that it does not like the amendments at all. LACOTS believes that the amount of beer dispensed can be measured satisfactorily under the Bill. However, I contend that it is more reasonable, fairer, and more efficacious for trading standards officers to measure several different items in measuring the amount of beer dispensed. Not only the trade but trading standards officers acknowledge that there is a degree of variation in the amount dispensed.

The trade responds to that point by saying that it is very much aware of its need to please its customers, who are discriminating and who want to ensure that they are getting full measure. The trade says that if customers feel that they have received less than full measure, it will always provide a top-up to satisfy them that they have had what they should have.

Against that background, we can readily see that if trading standards officers were to check measures on the basis of only one item, that could lead to a false conclusion. The purpose of my amendment is to allow--indeed, to insist on--a degree of variation by enlarging the sample size to ensure that a proper measure is taken. That is not unreasonable, given what I have said about the enormous variations in the product itself--bitters, lagers, stouts and so on--and in the head, which is the matter of contention.

Let us for the sake of argument consider a flat product--I use that term in a rather non-technical sense--such as milk. The Minister may correct me, but I think I am right in saying that milk bottles, which some of us still have delivered to our doorsteps--I am happy to say that I do--do not all contain exactly a pint; there are overages and underages. Other products that we drink are rarely, if ever, subject to the degree of precision sought under the Bill.

Milk is not an effervescent product--again, my language is not strictly technical--it is not grassy, or whatever. The head of the beer provides the real complication, as it contains a volatile mixture of gas and liquid. That means that it is difficult to take a precise measure of what has been dispensed.

If we then consider the different possible configurations of the container--whether there should be a line marked on the container; how far above that line should be allowed for variation in the head; what allowance should be made for the collapse of the head over what period of time--we see that considerable complications and difficulties arise in finding one simple, standard, elegant way of measuring beer, which is dispensed in a huge variety of ways. It may be dispensed by the traditional hand-pull pump; there may be free flow into a glass with a measure; it can be metered.

The Bill is an attempt--wrongly, I submit--to find one way of providing the consumer with a measure of confidence.

I ask the Committee to recall the fate of section 43--as was--of the Weights and Measures Act 1985. In the run-up to the 1992 general election, the Conservative Government acknowledged that there was a problem with short measures. A date of 1 April 1994 was set for section 43 to come into force, but many in the trade complained that serving a full measure would force up prices dramatically. The Government therefore cancelled a commencement date for section 43 in exchange for the voluntary agreement to which the right hon. Member for Bromley and Chislehurst has referred.
