Clause 9

Education committees to include parent representatives

10.30 am

Mrs. Angela Browning (Tiverton and Honiton): I beg to move amendment No. 131, in page 9, line 15, at end insert

    "(g)which bodies the parent representatives report to and the mechanism by which reports can be communicated.".

The amendment relates to a matter that was touched on briefly in our previous sitting, namely, the mechanism by which parent representatives report and then communicate their reports to other parents and take soundings from them. It is not clear how the mechanism will be put into practice. We believe that such a procedure should be part of the Bill, hence the proposed additional paragraph (g) outlined in the amendment.

There seems to be confusion among the governors and the governing bodies about the exact remit that such parents will have. I am not saying that we do not welcome the involvement of parents, but there must be clear guidelines on the reporting procedures, stating which body the parents represent. For example, a parent who may sit on a specific governing body of a school could not be deemed to be representing that body, given the corporate significance of the governing body. How does the Under-Secretary envisage parents carrying out their roles and how representative will they be of parents' views? How will those views be communicated to other parents, so that the parents can be truly representative of others rather than acting as individuals expressing their personal perspective? Will the reports be communicated by written means or through meetings?

Mrs. Theresa May (Maidenhead): I echo the comments of my hon. Friend the Member for Tiverton and Honiton (Mrs. Browning). If the Under-Secretary casts her mind back to our previous debate on the clause, she will recall that we pressed her on the issue of parental representation. We discussed the importance of ensuring that parents who sit on education committees represent parents, not the governing body.

We have accepted the practical matter of how to elect a group of people who are representative of the parents the Government proposed that they be elected from among serving parent governors. Ministers have made it clear that, although parents on the education committee would have been elected from among parent governors, they would be wearing not governing body hats, but parent representative hats. It is necessary to take that debate further this morning and to receive clarification of how we can ensure that parent representatives elicit the views of other parents to put to the education committee. We do not want them simply to put forward their own views or those of the governing body, given that the views of parents will differ across different phases.

It is important for parent governors to understand what issues affect other parents. For example, parent governor representatives from a primary school may reflect the views of parents in the secondary sector and vice versa. Although the Under-Secretary accepts that the representative speaks with a parental voice and not a governing body voice, it is important that the Committee should know how that parental voice is to be determined and what liaison and communication will take place to ensure that the views put forward are genuinely the parents' views and not the views only of a school's governing body.

Mr. Phil Willis (Harrogate and Knaresborough): The Liberal Democrats support the amendment. However, although we want the Government to accept the sense of it, we hope that it will not be pressed to a vote.

We want to know how parent representatives will report back. We are especially concerned that action zone forums may not include a local education authority representative. We therefore want to know about the inter-relationship between the parent representatives on education committees and on action zone forums.

The bill could be huge. Who will foot it?

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Education and Employment (Ms Estelle Morris): The Government are sympathetic to the thinking behind the amendment. It is important that those elected to represent parents on education committees do not serve in isolation. We shall resist the amendment because we do not want primary or secondary legislation to restrict the mechanisms that could be used to allow feedback or to listen to parents. The mechanism might vary it could be anything from a newsletter report to issuing a summary of what has happened in the education committee. A local newspaper could run a monthly[Ms Estelle Morris] column and, in some areas, it might be appropriate for one newspaper to report on all schools in a local education authority area. If we define in legislation how representatives can inform parents, we may restrict them from adopting mechanisms that are more appropriate in their particular circumstances.

In the light of my comments, I hope that the amendment will be withdrawn. We shall issue guidance to make it clear that we expect feedback from governors and that we want parents to know how to get in touch with their representatives on the education committee. We shall set out a series of ways by which feedback can be achieved, but without restricting people to using that list. We shall invite them to be imaginative and creative.

We want to see examples of good practice models of how feedback could take place. If governors think that they have a better or more effective way of feeding back information about their activities to parents and other governors, I should want them to be able to adopt and develop it. That good practice could then be used in the next series of guidance notes that we issue to governors. That is why we do not wish to put it in primary or secondary legislation. We want to see how the system works. Nobody knows the best way of feeding back information. I am happy to put on record the Government's firm intention that parents' representatives should not operate in isolation. Appropriate ways of listening to parents and feeding back information will be made clear in guidance.
