Amendment moved [this day]: No. 370, in page 7, line 34, at end insert—

Mr. Nick Gibb (Bognor Regis and Littlehampton): One of our concerns about the new regulatory regime is its scope for duplication. The consumer council investigates any complaint that it receives. However, the Bill establishes the council as a partial, partisan and pro-consumer organisation that represents consumers' interests. As the council has such a role, any investigation that it conducts will be unlikely to be seen as purely objective. If the consumer council feels after conducting an investigation that action is required, it will have to pass its report to the authority, which will take appropriate action. However, can the authority rely on the objectivity of such a report, given the partisan nature of the organisation responsible for it? If it cannot, it may have to investigate the initial complaint all over again, which would be a waste of time and money. The amendment would prevent that risk, as well as the danger of double jeopardy.

The regulatory authority, the Competition Commission and the consumer council have investigatory powers. The Competition Commission can impose fines of up to 30 per cent. of turnover and the regulatory authority can impose unlimited fines. The amendment would therefore avoid a double jeopardy as well as the double investigatory time and expense. It seems that when the Bill was drafted, not much thought was given to the interaction of its various provisions or to the relationship between the Competition Commission and the regulatory authority. The amendment is intended to flush out from the Government a statement on how the relationships between the consumer council, the regulatory authority and the Competition Commission will work in practice. Will the Minister explain how the Bill avoids double jeopardy and repeated investigations of the same complaint?

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton (Mr. Gibb) makes an important point about duplication. In providing for concurrent functions for regulators in the Competition Act 1998, the Government were clear that the exercise of those functions had to be well co-ordinated between regulators and the Director General of Fair Trading and that, most importantly, double jeopardy had to be avoided. That is why section 54 of the Act gives the Secretary of State power to make regulations for the purpose of co-ordinating the performance of concurrent functions under the Act.

The Government have made regulations: the Competition Act 1998 (Concurrency) Regulations 2000, which were laid before Parliament on 8 February and came into force on 1 March, when the Competition Act prohibitions came into force. Regulation 7 of the statutory instrument contains a provision for the avoidance of double jeopardy. I hope that the hon. Member will accept that the regulations addressed his concerns and ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Gibb: The Minister's statement is helpful and I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
