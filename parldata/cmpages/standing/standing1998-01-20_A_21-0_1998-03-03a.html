School Standards and Framework Bill

Clause 114

Dissolution of Funding Agency for Schools

4.30 pm

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mrs. Angela Browning Tiverton and Honiton): The Committee already knows that funding for GM schools will be changed significantly, and we now come to the dissolution of the Funding Agency for Schools. Will there be an overlap between the two types of funding? For example, after the Bill receives Royal Assent, when will the provision apply? At present, GM schools need to plan for the next year or so; they need to know when their funding arrangements will change so that they can ensure that there will be no gaps in the system. Will the Minister explain the timing of the change?

The Minister for School Standards (Mr. Stephen Byers): The effect of the new framework under clause 114 is that there will no longer be GM schools, as a result of which there will no longer be the need for the Funding Agency for Schools. The Government's intention is that the work of the funding agency will cease formally at the end of the financial year, March 1998-99. The agency will be run down thereafter and several people will have to be in post to finalise and discharge its final liabilities and responsibilities.

We expect that the financial regime for GM schools for the new financial year starting in April 1999 will be determined in the period running up to that time. GM schools will probably be informed of their budgets in January or February. We shall debate an amendment later this evening to allow the Department to make contributions to grant-maintained schools to cover the period between April and September when the new school framework will not be in place. We will need a mechanism by which to continue to pay grants to grant-maintained schools during that final period.

Mrs. Browning: I should like to deal with the period between March and September and the salary for existing staff. Do the Government intend to replace the funding on a like-for-like basis, or will there be an averaging out to GM schools? In other words, will schools suddenly find that they have to give notice to a teacher at that time, or will there be a guarantee that whatever mechanism is in place in those intervening months will ensure that staffing levels will be maintained, certainly until September? We have heard that the change in structure will mean that, in many cases, GM schools will have to dispose of members of staff.

Mr. Byers: It is premature, in March 1998, to give commitments about budgets for a school in April 1999 if only I could. The hon. Lady will know that that is impossible and it would be misleading for me to try to do so. I am not saying that only in relation to grant-maintained schools; I cannot give such a commitment for any school under the new framework because it is too soon to say what the budgetary arrangements will be. Two principles underpin our approach: first, that there should be as little disruption as possible to schools as they go into the new framework. That applies to financial arrangements as much as to organisational matters. Secondly, and specifically, the Government's objective is to level up, rather than down. Transitional arrangements will be put in place to provide a safety net, so that the difficulties that might otherwise occur will at least be reduced.

The Government are conscious of the uncertainty that change always creates. We shall be mindful of that; indeed, we are holding discussions with groups representing the grant-maintained sector, to ensure that the new system will meet their particular requirements and deal with any anxieties that they may have.

I hope that I have satisfied the hon. Lady about the arrangements that we want to put in place. The Funding Agency for Schools will not be discharging any responsibilities after March 1999.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 116

Publication of inspection reports

Mr. Don Foster (Bath): I beg to move amendment No. 191, in page 84, leave out lines 26 to 31.

You will be delighted, Mr. Gale, to hear that I intend to be brief, not least to demonstrate for once that the Minister is wrong. Many of us were somewhat taken aback when, only a few minutes ago, the Minister said that a subsequent debate was likely to occur "later this evening." We hope that it will not be that much later.

Amendment No. 191 is a probing amendment. Clause 116 creates a new section 42A to the School Inspections Act 1996. Subsection (3) states:

    "For the purposes of the law of defamation any report published by the Chief Inspector under any of those provisions shall be privileged unless the publication is shown to be made with malice."

I hope that the Minister can explain why the chief inspector should be so protected.

I realise that many hon. Members feel that the chief inspector's remarks are sometimes intemperate. I am in a difficult position because, only a few days ago, in his annual lecture, he referred to "My very good friend, Don Foster". I am not sure whether I should be happy with that appellation. However, in his report, he not only attacks the Chairman of the Select Committee on Education and Employment, but accuses Professor Robin Alexander of an "excess of academic unworldiness". He also accuses Professor John MacBeath of Strathclyde university, who is also a member of the Minister's standard task force, of "intellectual shoddiness". [Mr. Don Foster]

Many of us are worried that some of the chief inspector's remarks, not only in his remarks in his annual lecture and at other public meetings but in his reports, appear rather intemperate and are sometimes not backed up with the necessary intellectual rigour. It therefore seems appropriate that those who feel maligned by the chief inspector should have the opportunity for redress.

Subsection (3) is an odd provision. I hope for a clear explanation of why it is necessary to give the chief inspector such protection.

Mr. Byers: The provision is necessary because we propose a far wider distribution of Ofsted inspection reports than normal; such reports usually have a fairly limited circulation. The reports are published in pursuance of a legal duty imposed on Her Majesty's chief inspector, so he will automatically be covered by qualified privilege. That simply means that he will not be liable for his comments, provided that they are made without malice.

The clause allows Ofsted reports to appear on the internet that is what we mean by "published by electronic means" so the reports will have a far wider distributiion. Our legal advice is that because of that wider distribution, qualified privilege will not automatically apply to the chief inspector. We seek to ensure that the present legal cover given to the chief inspector for Ofsted reports in their limited circulation will apply when we move to a far wider distribution by making reports available on the internet.

We are giving Her Majesty's chief inspector no special protection; we are just ensuring that the same protection that at present applies to the limited circumstances in which Ofsted reports are made available will be extended to their far wider distribution, in which the chief inspector is not under a legal duty to make reports available. There is benefit and merit in making Ofsted reports easily accessible to parents, teachers and other people who are interested in reading reports on individual schools. That is why we need the specific provision in the clause to ensure that qualified privilege will also apply in those circumstances.

I hope that I have been able fully to explain why the words are in the clause, and that the hon. Gentleman will feel able to withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Don Foster: I am grateful to the Minister for at least giving the Committee an explanation of why the words are included. What he has said is that there is protection for the more limited distribution of the inspector's reports, and that with wider distribution, the words must now be included in the Bill.

Of course, the Minister could have taken the opportunity when considering widening the distribution of such reports to reconsider whether the chief inspector should have such a level of protection. It is interesting to note that inspectors can be deregistered under the Education Act 1997, which amends the Education Act 1996, for producing a "seriously misleading" inspection report "without reasonable explanation". Inspectors can lose their job for producing such a report; it appears that the chief inspector can produce such a report and not lose his job.

Nevertheless, given that we now have on the record an explanation of the reason for the provision, which is what I sought by moving the amendment, I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.
