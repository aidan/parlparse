Amendment proposed [this day]: No. 68, in page 107, line 43, leave out from `paragraph' to end of line 45.—[Mr. Grieve.]

The Parliamentary Secretary, Privy Council Office (Mr. Paddy Tipping): I was about to explain in detail the purpose of paragraph 2(1)(b). Before I do so, I reiterate that the regulations that are to be made by the Secretary of State after consulting the commission are very narrow. They relate solely to the additional information about the donor that must be included in a donation report. During this morning's sitting, I was concerned that the Committee was being given the impression that there were hidden ulterior motives which gave the Secretary of State wide-ranging powers. If that were so, members of the Committee would be right to be concerned. However, it is not the case, as I shall spell out.

The purpose of the provision is that where a recordable donation is made by an individual, paragraph 2(2)(a) of schedule 5 requires that the donation must include the address of the person as shown on the electoral register. However, under regulation 49(2) of the Representation of the People Regulations 1986, the addresses of certain service voters, voluntary mental patients and overseas electors are not included in the register.

The purpose of the regulation that makes the power that we are discussing is to enable the provisions to be made in respect of the addresses of such donors. It would be unwise to put those provisions in the Bill, given that they are, to some extent, dependent on other provisions set out in subordinate legislation. If the Secretary of State wants to change the situation, this enabling power allows him or her to do so. On that small, narrow and precise point, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will consider withdrawing the amendment.

Mr. Dominic Grieve (Beaconsfield): The Minister's explanation is cogent, and I understand what is intended. I accept that it may be impossible because of the number of pieces of subordinate legislation. Nevertheless, will the Minister consult his officials to find out whether it would be difficult to encapsulate the precise areas of legislation that the measure is designed to address? Mr. Tipping: There has already been one area of improvement in the Bill today as a result of inquiries from the Opposition. If we can make it better, we will.

The submission of the circumstances in which the donation was made and the submission of accounts places an onerous burden on those who are making the returns—although it is not so crushing that it should not be imposed. Under paragraph 4 of the schedule, in the case of a quarterly report the relevant date for the donation is the date on which the donation was accepted by the party or the accounting unit. I have no quarrel with that. However, paragraph 4(3) makes it clear that, in respect of weekly reports made during elections, the relevant date will be not the date on which a donation was accepted but the date on which it was received.

I can see the underlying purpose of that provision. By waiting until the date on which the donation is accepted, the election will have long been finished. Nevertheless, I question the reasonableness and usefulness of a weekly report during a general election campaign, because it will present a potentially misleading picture. The return will state that the party has received X amount of money, but it will not have been possible to scrutinise that. A correction might be included in the subsequent quarterly report, showing which sums have been rejected, but in the meantime the party will believe that it is receiving certain amounts of money, even though it has been unable to put them to use. That is the crucial issue. The party may have money in the kitty, but it will be unable to use it until it is satisfied that it can accept it.

I should be grateful for the Minister's views on this matter. What is wrong with saying that even the weekly reports should declare only sums that were accepted, and which the party can therefore use for general election campaigning purposes? My hon. Friend the Member for Glasgow, Pollok (Mr. Davidson) asked about the difference between receiving and accepting. Earlier today, we discussed the precise meaning of ``receiving''. It might involve posting an item through the front door of central office or new technology could be used to show that it had been received. Acceptance marks the point at which a party has established that a donation is legitimate. However, if that cannot be done within 30 days the money has to be returned.

Parties, businesses and other organisations spend money that they do not have; that is how they are run—they plan their expenditure and income. A donation can be received and spent before it is accepted and before the seven-day period has elapsed. The requirement that donations that are received during a campaign period be reported is an important restraint. Such information will have to be made public to electors.

The hon. Member for Hazel Grove (Mr. Stunell) asked us to consider a scam or other such mischief making. He gave the example of a newspaper that set up an American business man who made a donation to a political party. He said that the donation was publicised and, in a circular manner, news of the donation appeared in the newspaper. That is certainly possible. However, I remind the hon. Gentleman that other parts of the Bill make such deceptions a criminal offence. The business man would be liable—he is a third party and he is involved in the deception. I also suspect—I shall check on this and write to him—that the newspaper's editor and proprietor would be liable. That mechanism would stop such a scam.

The situation is complicated but the balance is right. I believe that Committee members agree that it is important to have a different regime—quarterly rather than weekly—during general elections. Small parties may, in effect, run their campaign on promises, which may not materialise. The restraint to use in those cases is the fact that such parties would face civil law prosecution—they would have debts that they would have to pay. That mechanism would stop small parties from carrying out such unprincipled spending.

Mr. Walter: In this debate, we shall revisit a topic that we discussed this morning. We shall debate it with more force, however, unless we are comforted by the Minister's response.

Amendment No. 64 is fundamental. It hinges on the philosophical discussion of part I, which I am sure will take place on the Floor of the House, on what kind of body the Electoral Commission should be. The wording in clause 60(1) is

    ``The Secretary of State may, after consulting the Commission''.

We feel that if the commission is to have force, the Secretary of State should not merely consult the commission but should be required to act on its recommendations. If commissioners are to be wise men, good men and true, who will look dispassionately at our electoral system—and in whom we can have confidence—the Secretary of State should not have the power simply to override them. If that were the case, amendment No. 66 would relate to the Secretary of State's powers. The amendment stipulates that:

    ``The Secretary of State shall not make any order under subsection (1) above unless a draft of the order has been laid before, and approved by a resolution of both Houses of Parliament.''

We are considering matters that might constitute significant alterations to the implementation of our electoral law as it relates to this place, the European Parliament, the Scottish Parliament, the National Assembly for Wales and the Northern Ireland Assembly. One could argue, although I do not want to do so in relation to the amendment, that such orders should be laid before the Scottish Parliament, the National Assembly for Wales and the Northern Ireland Assembly. However, I hesitate to suggest that any orders that the Secretary of State comes up with should be laid before the European Parliament because, in this context, we are dealing with domestic electoral matters.

The Bill is designed to establish an electoral system in which we—Members in this place, the Welsh Assembly, the Scottish Parliament and the Northern Ireland Assembly and the electorate—have confidence. The Electoral Commission will be an important body, and it should be powerful, independent and above the political fray. Orders that the Secretary of State makes about our electoral procedures should have the commission's recommendations. They should be given when Parliament has approved the orders that were laid before it. Clause 61

Reporting of multiple small donations

Mr. Grieve: I beg to move amendment No. 45, in page 39, leave out lines 25 to 32.

This is a probing amendment which raises an issue of concern. That concern will increase when we come to schedule 6, which contains a similar provision in relation to the payment of smaller sums to associations of political parties. I am flagging the less important issue, but I thought that we should consider it now as well as later.

A donor who gives small donations, with an aggregate value exceeding £5,000, must make a declaration at the end of the year and report those donations to the commission. When someone is obliged to make a tax return, that obligation may be firmly implanted in his mind by his accountant. I am concerned that, unlike having to make a tax return, the person who is required to make a declaration of donations will not necessarily have that knowledge unless the issue is widely publicised, yet a failure on his part could attract draconian penalties.

I appreciate that the issue will vary according to the sorts of donations that are made, but when we discussed the matter at a previous sitting, there was some laughter, perhaps at the expense of my constituency association, about the largesse with which people may sometimes engage. We were interested to discover from a recent libel action that the owner of a large department store took out £120,000 a week in petty cash for his own use. That may be an extreme example, but it is surprising how people with wealth and means sometimes distribute, legitimately, money in cash—for example, putting £500 in a bucket when it circulates at a charity concert—without keeping an audited record of what they have done. There may not be many people who make small donations to a political party with an aggregate value of not less than £5,000, but I shall return to the matter when we come to schedule 6 because a limit of £1,000 could present a problem.

I want to raise the issue of principle. I accept that we should be concerned to ensure that we do not introduce a loophole behind which people may start to make substantial aggregate donations of well over £5,000 and forget about them. However, we should bear it in mind that those aggregate donations ought—unless they were less than £200—to have been picked up by the donee at the time that they were received.

Is it reasonable to place such a burden on an individual donor, with the penalties attached to a failure to comply with it? Granted, there is a clause that will protect him if he unknowingly makes a false declaration. However, it will not occur to some people to make a declaration at all. The sort of person likely to be caught by the provisions does not have lunch with the party treasurer and write out a cheque for £10,000 as a result. Rather, they are the sort of people who have quite a lot of money and who are content, when the occasion arises at a fundraising gathering of the association, to pay either by cheque or by cash. They would not necessarily carry out such an audit of their expenditure at the end of the year.

It is noteworthy that clause 61(3) reads:

    ``The report must be delivered to the Commission by 31 January in the year following that in which the donations were made.''

How will that tie in with individuals' financial years, when they make their tax returns? A regular donor to a party of small sums paid by cheque is likely to work out his aggregate donation when he goes through his accounts to fill in his tax return at the end of the year. What publicity or educational mechanisms does the Minister envisage to alert people to their duty under the provision?

The Minister may not have answers to all those questions, but we must have a moment's pause for thought on the issue. We are dealing here not with £100,000 donors—often individual donations negotiated with the party that the individual will always remember having made. It will be easy to remind such people of their duty in this respect. We are dealing instead with wealthy people who are in the habit of dipping into their pockets for a whole variety of things, and who could easily exceed that aggregate total by, perhaps, half a dozen donations a year.

We shall return to the matter when we discuss schedule 6. I accept the need to maintain transparency, and I accept the logic that there may be a duty on donors. However, I am anxious about how this will work in practice and how, if it is implemented, individuals will be reminded of their obligations, so that they do not fall foul of the criminal law.
