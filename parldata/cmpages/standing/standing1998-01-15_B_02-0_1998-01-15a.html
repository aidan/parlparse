New clause 1 Advance notice of counter demonstrations to public processions ". (1) A person proposing to organise a counter demonstration to a public procession shall give notice of that proposal in accordance with subsections (2) to (4) to a member of the Royal Ulster Constabulary not below the rank of sergeant by leaving the notice with him at the police station in whose area of responsibility that procession is proposed to start. (2) Notice under this section shall be given not less than 14 days before the date on which the procession is to be held. (3) Notice under this section shall (a) be given in writing in such form as may be prescribed by regulations made by the Secretary of State; and (b) be signed by the person giving the notice. (4) The form prescribed under subsection (3)(a) shall require a person giving notice under this section to specify (a) the nature of the counter demonstration; (b) the number of persons likely to take part in it; (c) the arrangements for its control being made by the person proposing to organise it; (d) the name and address of that person; and (e) such other matters as appear to the Secretary of State to be necessary for, or appropriate for facilitating, the exercise by the Commission, the Secretary of State or members of the Royal Ulster Constabulary of any function in relation to the counter demonstration. (5) The Chief Constable shall ensure that a copy of a notice given under this section is immediately sent to the Commission. (6) A person who organises or takes part in a counter demonstration to a lawfully determined public procession (a) in respect of which the requirements of this section as to notice have not been satisfied; or (b) which differs markedly in nature from that in the notice given under this section, shall be guilty of an offence. (7) A person guilty of an offence under subsection (6) shall be liable on summary conviction to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 6 months or to a fine not exceeding level 5 on the standard scale, or to both.".

New clause 2 The Commission's powers to impose conditions on counter demonstrations ". (1) The Commission may issue a determination in respect of a proposed counter demonstration to a proposed public procession imposing on the persons organising or taking part in it such conditions as the Commission considers necessary. (2) Without prejudice to the generality of subsection (1), the conditions imposed under that subsection may include conditions as to the lining of the route of the proposed procession or prohibiting counter demonstrators from entering any place. (3) Conditions imposed under subsection (1) may incorporate or be framed by reference to (a) the Code of Conduct; or (b) any other document (i) prepared by the person or body organising the procession in question; and (ii) approved by the Commission for the purposes of this section. (4) The Commission may, in accordance with the procedural rules, amend or revoke any determination issued under this section. (5) In considering in any particular case (a) whether to issue a determination under this section; (b) whether to amend or revoke a determination issued under this section; or (c) what conditions should be imposed by a determination (or amended determination) issued under this section, the Commission shall have regard to the guidelines. (6) The guidelines shall in particular (but without prejudice to the generality of section 5(1)) provide for the Commission to have regard to (a) any public disorder or damage to property which may result from the counter demonstration to the proposed procession; (b) any disruption to the life of the community which the counter demonstration may cause; (c) any impact which the counter demonstration may have on relationships within the community; and (d) any failure of a person of a description specified in the guidelines to comply with the Code of Conduct (whether in relation to the counter demonstration to the proposed procession in question or any previous counter demonstration). (7) A person who knowingly fails to comply with a condition imposed under this section shall be guilty of an offence, but it is a defence for him to prove that the failure arose (a) from circumstances beyond his control; or (b) from something done by direction of a member of the Royal Ulster Constabulary not below the rank of inspector. (8) A person who incites another to commit an offence under subsection (7) shall be guilty of an offence. (9) A person guilty of an offence under subsection (7) or (8) shall be liable on summary conviction to imprisonment for a term not exceeding 6 months or to a fine not exceeding level 5 on the standard scale, or to both.".

 

Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): Welcome back, Mr. Welsh. It is nice to have you with us after your disappearing act this morning. I was ending my remarks when the gong went. I was speaking about clause 10 and the changes proposed in my amendments Nos. 88 to 118, but not No. 105. That list of amendments would ensure that counter-demonstrations were included in every case when processions were to be considered by the Secretary of State.

Before concluding, I want to refer to new clauses 1 and 2, which are important. As I said earlier, new clause 1 almost replicates clause 6, which deals with advance notices. Clause 6 covers advance notice of public processions; new clause 1 would also include the organisers of counter-demonstrations to public processions. The wording is similar, but there are some key areas that I want to point out to the Committee.

The advance notice under new clause 1 is 14 days; the Government propose 28 days in clause 6. We shall discuss the proposal for 28 days' notice later. Our proposal of 14 days' notice seemed reasonable, as after the organisers of a parade had submitted their application to the local police, it would be passed to the commission within a day or two and would then be public knowledge. That would give at least 14 days for the organisers of a counter-demonstration to make their views known. The application form, or whatever it is to be called I believe that it has yet to be designed will require that the person giving notice under that section of the Bill should specify the nature of the counter-demonstration. The commission should know what it is faced with before it makes a decision. Is it a sit-down protest? Will it be a peaceful protest? Will it come close to the marching parade? Such details should be submitted so that the commission can make sense of the situation.

The commission should also be told the number of persons likely to take part in the counter-demonstration. If the organisers of parades have to give the numbers of those expected to take part, it is only reasonable that protesters against parades should do the same.

The wording about the arrangements for the control of parades is similar in new clause 1 and clause 6, as is that about the name and address of the person making the submission. It has been suggested that it is difficult to specify the people involved in protests and counter-demonstrations. I would agree with that if the protests were spontaneous, but many such events in Northern Ireland are not. The organisers of the counter-demonstrations are part of the so-called residents' organisations, which are well known to the police and to most people in Northern Ireland. If they continue to organise protests and counter-demonstrations, it seems fair and reasonable that they should make clear their intentions early enough to enable the police and the commission to take a view and reach a determination.

Furthermore, if the organisers of the counter-demonstration were made to put their intentions on paper, their submission would become the basis of the decision. If the commission deemed a peaceful protest, or a protest involving a sit down, to be acceptable, those elements would become agreed terms and conditions. Breaking them would amount to an offence. As things are, in discussions and negotiations with the commission, groups could say one thing and do something different on the day. There would be no recourse to the law. The organisers of the demonstrations would shrug their shoulders and say, "It just got out of hand on the day. Our intentions were to hold a peaceful protest within the law." That is not acceptable any more. The survey findings show that the vast majority of people, from all sides of the community, do not think that it is reasonable for protest demonstrations to take place if a parade has been given the legal go-ahead.

New clauses 1 and 2 are my attempts to put some meat on the bones to tie down the intentions of the counter-demonstrators, so that proper decisions can be taken. New clause 2 would give the commission powers to impose conditions on counter-demonstrations. It could do that only if it had information and knowledge about the organisers' intentions. The advance notice is thus a logical part of the approach. The new clause is similar to clause 7 in its wording but there are slight differences, particularly in subsection (2); my wording provides that conditions imposed under subsection (1) "may include conditions as to the lining of the route of the proposed procession or prohibiting counter demonstrators from entering any place."

One could think of other relevant situations, perhaps, but those two govern the interface between the parade and the demonstrators. The line of the route is obviously a critical area, and key points and locations on the route could also feature in violent confrontations if demonstrators so intended.

The latter provisions of the new clause provide the commission with powers of sanction against a group organising a counter-demonstration, much along the lines of the powers to invoke sanctions against parade organisers who deviate from their stated intention set[Mr. Malcolm Moss] out in the advance notice. The offences set out in the new clause are similar to those in clause 7.

Anyone studying the history of the past few years must recognise that the main problem is connected with demonstrators and protesters. Resident groups, plainly fronted and acknowledged by Sinn Fein-IRA, have been the main cause of the trouble. Thus legislation to deal with the problem should focus less on parades than on the protesters. The Bill should deal not so much with perceived difficulties with parades as with the difficulties caused by protesters.

My view is that we do not really need a Bill because the law in Northern Ireland is comparable to the law here in England. The same criteria exist on the mainland as in Northern Ireland. The main problem is that the interpretation and implementation of the law has been different in Northern Ireland.

If people want to hold a parade in England, they go to the police and agree a route with them. The police then police the parade. No protester would be allowed to get near it, to intervene or to interfere with it. Certainly no protester or group of protesters would be allowed to sit down in the road, block the parade and prevent it from taking place. Protesters would simply not be allowed to have offensive weapons and threaten to use them. That just would not happen here in England.

In Northern Ireland, however, the police have refused to deal with demonstrators in the same way. Under existing Northern Ireland legislation there are five or six articles that deal with people breaking the law, but the police have very rarely used the powers that they possess.

Some people believe that Dr. North's report is wonderful. I do not, but even the report makes the point that it is incomprehensible why so few people in Northern Ireland have been prosecuted under existing law. It states: "We are clear in our minds that there is a need for an effective offence to penalise those who act to disrupt parades and that the events of the past two years point to undoubted evidence of such behaviour. We are less clear from the evidence before us whether the paucity of prosecutions under Article 7 lies in a defect in the law or simply in an unwillingness to bring prosecutions under that particular provision."

That is Northern Ireland's problem the failure of the police to police parades according to the law.

There is an annual parade in the village of Mountfield in my constituency to the local Church of Ireland church. That parade has been going on for years without any problem. This year the organisers gave their notice to the police as usual. However, certain troublemakers and Sinn Fein sympathisers decided that this year they would cause trouble. They went to the route of the parade, threatened to sit on the road and block it and threatened violence. Under the law the police should have prevented that: they should have allowed the peaceful parade to proceed through the village to the church for the religious service.

What happened? The police, under a misinterpretation of the law stopped the parade. They said, "You can't go up because by so doing you will cause a breach of the peace." That seems a foolish argument and a perverse interpretation of the law. Not only that, but the police issued the peaceful people who were marching with the document which said that they were the troublemakers who were acting illegally and that they were being stopped under the Act when they had done nothing at all and were in a perfectly peaceful parade. That is our problem in Northern Ireland the failure to police parades properly.

The Bill, instead of reinforcing the existing law, will bring in a new law to change the whole situation and makes it far easier for people to protest. They will be invited to protest and to gather to stop parades. They have discovered that once they have got away with it they will continue to get away with it. That is the problem in Northern Ireland.

In the village of Pomeroy the local Orange parade on the way home each 12 July parades a certain section, mainly the Protestant section of the village. It goes around the square in Pomeroy and back down to the hall. For many years some people have protested about that parade those who do not like us because we are British, who do not like the Union Jack, who do not like the British way of life but who are quite happy to take the Queen's money. They protest and the police block the road. They were not allowed in and the parade went through as normal, quite peaceably. Now the police do not block the route and the protesters come in and sit down and the police block the legal and peaceful parade. That creates problems because it means that the law-abiding citizen is denied his right to march the Queen's highway. It is therefore essential to deal with the counter-demonstrations and counter-protesters. There is nothing in the Bill to deal adequately with them. Changes need to be made so that those who wish to demonstrate and protest have to go through a legal process similar to the one to which those who wish to hold proper lawful parades have to undergo.

The Bill contains codes of practice, rules and regulations for those who wish to hold peaceful parades. It contains nothing of the sort for protesters or counter-demonstrators. They should be subject to a code of practice that requires them to give notice that they will parade. They should have to go through procedures that are similar to those for people holding lawful parades.

It is difficult for provisions on counter-demonstrators to have much beneficial effect. Most protests take place suddenly and unexpectedly and many protesters are brought in to create trouble. However, if the Bill made some effort to deal with them, we might have more confidence in the Government's resolution on solving the problem.

Unionists and law-abiding people see a Bill that deals almost exclusively with their parades, but does nothing about troublemakers. It gives Unionists very little confidence in the Government whom they are under at this time. It increases their perception that the Government are more interested in placating Republicans and Sinn Fein than in protecting the rights of the people of Northern Ireland. It is a continuation of the Government's perceived policy of removing, as far as they can, those things which are part of the British way of life and the British emblem from the people of Northern Ireland.

Those are real perceptions and if the issue is not tackled with great care, there will be far more problems this year than there were last year. There might have been 15 or 20 trouble spots last year, but, if the Bill is enacted, there will be 30, 40 or 50 or even more in the year that lies ahead. It is essential that the Bill is amended and that protesters, as well as law-abiding citizens, understand that they have to abide by certain strictures of the law.
