The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 177 to 180, Nos. 182 to 185, No. 187 and Government new clause 5--Abolition of sentences of detention in a young offender institution and custody for life.

Mr. Boateng: The amendment will abolish the sentence of detention in a young offenders institution--DYOI. Following abolition, all convicted defendants aged 18 or over at the time of conviction will be sentenced as adults.

Since the separate custodial sentence of DYOI is being abolished for 18 to 20-year olds, the need for a separate life sentence provision known as "custody for life" for this age group will no longer exist. The amendment will therefore also abolish that sentence. It is a technical change and will make no practical difference to the length of time served or to the arrangements for determining release. Following abolition, 18 to 20-year-olds will be subject to the same life sentence as adults--life imprisonment. We consulted on these proposals last year and there was broad agreement on their content.

The Criminal Justice Act 1982 currently provides that a court may pass a sentence of detention in a young offender institution on an offender aged 18, 19 or 20 when he or she is convicted of an offence which, in the case of a person aged 21 or over, would be punishable with imprisonment. Sentences of detention up to the maximum permitted period of imprisonment for an adult convicted of the offence in question may be passed in those circumstances.

The Crime and Disorder Act 1998 modified the sentence of DYOI with the creation of the detention and training order for young offenders aged 10 to 17 whom the courts decide need a custodial sentence. Detention and training orders came into effect on 1 April 2000, and one effect of their introduction is the reduction in the number of young offenders who remain sentenced to DYOI--some 6,000 sentenced 18 to 20-year-olds as at 31 March 2000.

To legislate for the abolition of DYOI will provide a number of benefits, which the hon. Member for Surrey Heath (Mr. Hawkins) may like to take into account. It will enable the courts to sentence all adult offenders--those who have reached 18, the age of majority--to sentences of imprisonment. That will increase the Prison Service's flexibility to manage its estate and should make it easier both to provide constructive regimes for young adult offenders, such as those which best meet the individual needs of that age group, and to keep them closer to their homes, thereby helping to support work on reducing recidivism. There is the advantage for the hon. Gentleman: the Prison Service will have greater flexibility. It will be able to tailor the regimes more accurately to the needs of a wider range of younger people susceptible to rehabilitation and keep them near their families. That is much better than an artificial distinction between those up to the age of 18, those between 18 and 21 and those older than 21, which is simply not justifiable in this day and age. It is much more important to build on the evidence base, to find out what is appropriate and to make sure that it is received. The broader groupings will bring about more effective regimes, which will have the benefit of enabling us to deliver welfare to work programmes to increase young offenders' employability. It does not mean that 18 to 20-year-olds will be mixed indiscriminately with older offenders, as appears to be the concern of the hon. Member for Surrey Heath. It is most likely that there will continue to be some separation for most offenders, but there will no longer be a rigid age divide.

We are also including powers--primarily in subsections (2) and (3)--providing for transitional arrangements. We will need to deal carefully with those offenders currently sentenced to DYOI and custody for life whose sentences will continue to run in the period immediately following implementation, while also ensuring that the flexibility flowing from abolition will have immediate impact. There is a balance to be struck, as I hope that the hon. Gentleman and his hon. Friends will appreciate.

The amendment makes careful provision for those who fall into that group. For example, in disciplinary matters they will not be disadvantaged by the change, as changes to be made to the prison rules will ensure that they will remain subject only to the maximum punishments that would otherwise have been available for their age group rather than those currently available in respect of prisoners aged 21 or over.

The other amendments in the group are purely consequential. Members of the Committee should be aware that some further minor amendments built around the measure will be taken at a later stage. I also assure hon. Members that we will address issues such as ensuring that vulnerable young adult prisoners will not be put at risk and that movement of young adult offenders between YOIs and prisons will take account of their regime needs before the changes are implemented. We are not rushing into this. We shall be in a position to ensure that the maximum benefits accrue to the young people at the earliest possible stage.

It is for those reasons and with those assurances that I hope that the Committee will give the new clause and its associated amendments a fair wind.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 186, 188 to 190, No. 175 and Government new clause 4--Life sentences: tariffs.

Mr. Boateng: The effect of new clause 4 is to bring our legislation into line with a key finding of the European Court of Human Rights in the case of Thompson and Venables on the way tariffs are set in cases of detention during Her Majesty's pleasure. As the Home Secretary made clear in his statement to the House on 13 March, we are bound to give effect to that judgment, and the Bill provides an opportunity to make the necessary legislative changes.

The background to this issue was the appalling murder of James Bulger, a terrible crime that stunned and shocked us all. The series of judgments in the courts here and the proceedings before both the Commission and the European Court of Human Rights could not have made the grief of his parents and family any easier to bear. It would not be right to have any discussion on this issue without putting on record, I believe on behalf of the whole Committee, our profound sympathy for that family for all that they have had to endure.

The clause changes the way in which a tariff is set in cases where a young offender is sentenced to be detained during Her Majesty's pleasure as a result of committing murder under the age of 18. That function is currently carried out by the Home Secretary, which the European Court of Human Rights has found to be in breach of article 6 of the European convention on human rights. In future, under the clause, that role will be performed by the sentencing court.

The clause is drafted so that provisions on tariff setting by courts will appear in the Powers of Criminal Courts (Sentencing) Bill, the recent consolidation legislation on sentencing. The scheme of the new provision suggests that it should sit with other sentencing matters rather than with provisions dealing with release in the Crime (Sentences) Act 1997.

Under new section 82A to the Powers of Criminal Courts (Sentencing) Bill, a court will determine the period that must be served before the parole board can consider the offender's release in two sets of circumstances: first, when a court passes a discretionary sentence of life imprisonment or custody for life--that is, where the court is not required by law to pass a life sentence but where that is the maximum sentence for the crime--; or, secondly, when an offender is sentenced to detention during Her Majesty's pleasure.

In determining the minimum period to be served, the court will have regard to the seriousness of the offence, the effect of crediting time on remand if a determinate sentence had been imposed and the fact that long-term prisoners are entitled to be released on licence before they have served the full term of their sentence. This re-enacts the existing factors under the Crime (Sentences) Act.

The period set by the court will be capable of being appealed by the offender or referred by the Attorney-General on the grounds of undue leniency, as is currently the case with court-set tariffs.

The court must set a tariff unless it believes that life must mean life because of the seriousness of the offence. This reflects a practice direction issued by the Lord Chief Justice in 1993 in the context of the original version of these provisions in the Criminal Justice Act 1991. The Thompson and Venables judgment and others have underlined the importance of this principle for cases involving detention during Her Majesty's pleasure or a discretionary life sentence. It therefore seems sensible to make this explicit. Subsection (5) provides that, in the extremely unlikely event that a court considers that life must mean life, in the case of a person under 18 when they committed their offence, the Home Secretary will determine the period that must be served before the case can be referred to the parole board, which will determine release. That maintains the position under the Crime (Sentences) Act 1997.

The new clause has effect in relation to sentences passed after the coming into force of the clause. Arrangements have already been put in place to deal with those sentenced before its commencement and to review existing tariffs in cases of detention during Her Majesty's pleasure. Legislative change is, however, needed to allow courts to set tariffs under this sentence in future. The proposed clause will have that effect and will preserve current statutory arrangements for existing cases. This will enable tariffs in existing cases to be reviewed and, if necessary, re-set under the transitional arrangements announced by my night hon. Friend, the Home Secretary, in his statement to the House on 13 March.

I should like to add a few words about the provision made in paragraph 2 of the new clause. The Thompson and Venables judgment points strongly towards the sentence and consequent arrangements of detention during Her Majesty's pleasure applying to all those given a life sentence under the age of 18. This is underlined by the abolition of the separate sentence of custody for life, as we propose in new clause 5. The sort of case that might be covered by the amendment is highly theoretical--offences involving killing under the Geneva Conventions Act 1957 and the Genocide Act 1969--but the possibility of a person receiving a mandatory life sentence for an offence other than murder and committed while they were under 18 cannot be excluded altogether.

I should make it absolutely clear, to avoid any doubt, that the clause does not affect the position of adults who commit murder. They will continue to receive a mandatory life sentence and the Home Secretary will continue to be responsible for both tariff and release decisions. Nothing in the Thompson and Venables judgment affects that position. The European Court of Human rights has drawn a distinction, which I am sure that the Committee would support, between the nature and purpose of detention during Her Majesty's pleasure and discretionary life sentences on the one hand, and the mandatory life sentence for murder committed by those over 18 on the other. In doing so, it has affirmed the compatibility with the ECHR of the role of the Home Secretary in setting tariffs and determining release where an adult has been convicted of murder.

The role of the Home Secretary in determining the minimum period to be served by an adult convicted of murder and the timing of their release, reflects the unique nature of the mandatory sentence of life imprisonment imposed in the light of the particular severity of that offence. That role serves a useful function, underlining the distinct nature of this sentence, enabling the Home Secretary to bring his experience across all murder cases to bear in setting tariffs in individual cases and enabling the wider concerns of the public to be reflected in release decisions. We do not believe that that role should be removed.

Mr. Boateng: I beg to move, that the clause be read a Second time.

The amendment is a technical measure designed to improve the efficiency of the criminal justice system. The Access to Justice Act 1999, consolidated in the Powers of Criminal Courts (Sentencing) Bill, introduced powers to enable the Crown court to reserve to itself when imposing a community sentence all potential breach proceedings in respect of that sentence. That was a measure requested by the Council of Circuit Judges, who argued that judges would have greater confidence in community sentences if they knew that the Crown court would be able to deal with any breaches that ensued.

In order to expedite proceedings, the 1999 Act further provided that a case going to the Crown court in such a way should not first be heard in the magistrates' court. The summons would be obtained from the magistrates' court, but then the hearing would take place straight away in the Crown court.

However, if the offender falls to appear at the Crown court when summonsed, the Crown court has no power to issue a further summons or a warrant for his arrest. Instead, the probation service needs to apply to a magistrates court for such a further summons or warrant. The Government consider that there would be an advantage, in the interests of speeding up justice, in circumventing that to enable the Crown court to issue the further summons or warrant. The new clause provides those powers, and I commend it to the Committee.

    "(bb) a person nominated by a person appointed as chief education officer under section 532 of the Education Act 1996".'

Brought up, and read the First time.

 

Mr. Boateng: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The parenting order under the Crime and Disorder Act 1998 provides a new means of supporting parents in exercising proper responsibility for their children's behaviour, especially when the child or young person has been convicted of an offence or when children do not attend school regularly. The order will be implemented nationally on 1 June 2000. One of our best debates in Committee was on parental responsibility; although we differed on some of the issues, there was a free and full exchange of views, which helped our work enormously.

The new clause extends the range of persons who may serve as a responsible officer under a parenting order. It is currently limited to a probation officer, a social worker of a local authority social services department or a member of a youth offending team. The new clause will allow local education authority staff working outside youth offending teams to serve as responsible officers under a parenting order. The additional flexibility will be useful when a parenting order is made in a case in which the local education authority is already working with the child and the family concerned to secure the child's regular attendance at school--we considered that issue earlier in our deliberations. In those circumstances it may be helpful for a member of staff of a local education authority already involved in the case to carry on his or her involvement by acting as a responsible officer under the parenting order. In so doing, he or she can support the parent in complying with and benefiting from the order and helping to ensure that the child attends school. The Committee will recall that the thrust of our earlier discussion was how best to avoid a situation in which the parent failed to carry out those responsibilities and the full rigour of the law was invoked. The new clause makes it easier to work effectively with the parents of children who fail to attend school. I commend the proposal to the Committee.

Mr. Hawkins: I agree with the Minister that we had a good debate earlier on parenting issues, but I have reservations about how the new clause may operate. Although the vast majority of those who work for local education authorities are dedicated people, independent inquiries reveal that individual officers are sometimes criticised for things that they did under pressure of work. Not everyone who works in a local authority bureaucracy is of the same high calibre as the best.

The Minister talked about flexibility, and, as hon. Members said earlier, that word can cover a multitude of sins. The power in the new clause may be too wide, and I hope when the Minister responds, under the doctrine of Pepper v. Hart, he will say something that clarifies the limit under which chief education officers may operate when it comes to whom they appoint. We are discussing a very serious matter--taking parenting responsibility under parenting orders--and we need to be absolutely certain that the people concerned have the right qualifications, experience and ability. It would be helpful, therefore, at the very least, for the Minister to say how the Government envisage the categories of people working for local education authorities will be included within the proposal. Do the Government intend to issue guidance notes on the matter before the Bill is enacted?

In practice, in years to come, we may find that the measure gives too wide a discretion. I do not suggest that the Government are mistaken in introducing some discretion, which may be welcome, but they may wish to consider expanding the proposals, perhaps in another place or on Report, or providing further clarification in the Bill, so that the power is not completely unrestricted.

I hope that the Minister will undertake to think about the matter further, as he has done on previous occasions, and perhaps to consult chief education officers about whether further clarification is needed.

Mr. McWalter: In my constituency the Government have been kind enough to fund for two years a pilot project with a charity called ACE, which uses members of the community who are highly regarded for their work with young people to work closely with the chief education officer in reducing truancy and other behaviour disorders. I am grateful that the charity works especially on primary school truanting.

The hon. Member for Surrey Heath asked which employees of the local education authority would be involved. The wider provisions of the clause will enable us to utilise the community's resources; retired people, for example who have the capacity to take an interest in young people's welfare. Many education authorities are interested in the experiment, which I hope interests the hon. Gentleman; the flexibility in the clause will make participation easier.

Mr. Boateng: I am grateful for the contribution of my hon. Friend the Member for Hemel Hempstead (Mr. McWalter), which highlights what can be done by those with a range of skills, experience and expertise. We seek sufficient flexibility to be able to tap in to those qualities. For example, we can utilise the experience and expertise of a local authority's education welfare officer or educational psychologist, who may have formed a relationship with a group such as ACE. At present, such persons cannot be responsible officers under the Act, but in future they will be able to take that responsibility.

As a former social services Minister, I understand what motivates the anxieties expressed by the hon. Member for Surrey Heath. However, at present, any local authority social worker can be appointed; it is not limited to a small cadre of specially qualified or trained social workers. Any social worker working alongside a probation officer or a member of a youth offending team can currently be appointed to serve as a responsible officer. The hon. Gentleman will understand that the best safeguard lies in ensuring that not only is the range of skills available, as I believe is the case, but that the expertise and knowledge of the management and staff at local YOT level can be tapped into to identify the best person to take the role of responsible officer. Let us not forget that, ultimately, the court will specify the responsible officer when making the order. The court, not the chief education officer or any other chief officer--although their input, where relevant, will be welcome--will finally decide who is a suitable professional to carry out the role. The decision will lie with the court, which will have in mind the task of making sure that the responsible person is just that--a person suited by virtue of experience, knowledge and qualification to carry out the role. With that assurance, with full understanding of the spirit in which the hon. Member for Surrey Heath made the point and bearing in mind the contribution of my hon. Friend the Member for Hemel Hempstead, I hope that the Committee will give new clause 9 a fair wind.
