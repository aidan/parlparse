The intention of my amendment is to reduce the spending limit for parliamentary elections. I am trying to avoid a situation where parties can spend a lot of money on marginal constituencies and almost nothing on constituencies that they believe they have no chance of winning or are almost certain to win. I do not need to tell hon. Members that there is no such thing as being certain to win. Many people went into the last election believing that and were disabused of the idea. However, it is observable from election returns that the parties spend far more on marginal seats than on safe or unwinnable seats. That may be an efficient use of resources for them, but it is not a fair spread of resources for voters. Those who live in a seat that one party may describe as hopeless, unwinnable, an electoral desert or with not a snowball's chance in hell, or which the opposite party describes as safe, rock-solid, a fortress or a bastion, can be sure that they will be almost completely ignored. The candidate may come along with an election address and perhaps one or two disconsolate supporters. However, we all know that those who live in marginal seats will be inundated with coachloads of canvassers. The slightest inquiry to the candidate will immediately be attended to and any amount of paper will come through the letterbox. Mr. Linton: It is indeed miraculous.

Having decided to recommend a national spending limit, the Neill committee then discussed the relative merits of what it calls an integrated or separated limit. The report says:

    ``An integrated limit would be one in which the national and constituency limits were combined to produce a single global limit''.

That is what I am trying to achieve in the amendment. The report goes on:

    ``The amounts of money that the parties were permitted to spend nationally would not be treated, for legal purposes, as a single national pool but would instead be divided up pro rata among the parties' candidates in individual constituencies.''

Thus, if a party had a national poster campaign, the cost would have to be divided between all the constituency candidates for seats in that region. The limit would clearly have to be much higher than the present limit. Indeed, it would be sensible to accept the figure in the Bill of £30,000 per constituency and turn that into an overall limit for local and national spending. The Neill committee decided against that in the end, but said:

    ``One advantage of this approach is that it would be `traditional'. It would build on the existing system, modifying it only in order to reverse the modification that was itself introduced by Tronoh Mines.''

That is an important point, because in the first half of the last century the parties believed that to be the approach. Indeed, little was spent on national campaigns; most election spending was at the local level. As recently as 1950, it is related in the Neill report, Violet Attlee drove her party leader round in an Austin Seven with one security man, and that was almost the total cost of the Labour party's national campaign. I dare say that the Conservatives spent a little more, but it was at a low level.

In 1952, a Malaysian tin mining company called Tronoh put a small advertisement in a Cornish newspaper, in which it advised its clients to vote Conservative because it was concerned about the threat of nationalisation. The judges' decision that the advertisement did not constitute an election expense, which surprised the parties at the time, opened the spending floodgates for national campaigns. The general election of 1955 was the first television election. National campaign expenditure now accounts for more than 90 per cent. of all election expenditure. Local expenditure accounts for a mere 10 per cent.

The Committee can doubtless see that the anomaly that began with a 1950s legal case has grown into the dominating factor in election expenditure law. The Neill committee and the Bill accept that anomaly, and a separate limit is set for national campaign spending. It would be more effective to revert to the law as it should have been before judges misinterpreted it, and set a single limit for local candidates and national campaigns. Parties would be unable ruthlessly to concentrate spending on marginal seats and ignore others, and the interests of the voter would be paramount.
