[Mr. John Butterfill in the Chair]

Initially, the commission seemed to be an advisory body that could also negotiate, arbitrate, talk to various factions and try to reach agreement between those who are at variance on the streets of Northern Ireland. Unfortunately, the Bill turns it from being an advisory body into one with legal authority. That amounts to a massive change which will impinge on the Chief Constable's decision-making power, and we should not go down that road lightly; we must give it very careful consideration.

Objections to processions in Northern Ireland are seldom spur-of-the-moment. We are told that the leadership of Sinn Fein-IRA spent two years preparing for consultations. In those circumstances it follows logically that much of the decision-making can be made only when true knowledge of the facts is made available to the commission and to the security forces.

The Chief Constable more than anyone else knows the security background and understands the dangers that are inherent in any given situation. He also knows the identities of individuals who are involved. If we want clear evidence of his knowledge and ability to advise, we need only consider the decision of the Orange Institution on 12 July when it voluntarily withdrew from some processions. Members of the institution who saw the Chief Constable and other senior officers will confirm that it was clear that if the processions were to proceed, there would be violence and bloodshed on the streets. In such circumstances, the only person whom the loyalist bodies are likely to believe is the Chief Constable and his senior officers. Loyalists are extremely unlikely to believe an appointed quango which does not contain a single person whom they could regard with any confidence as independent and competent to make the right decisions or put forward their point of view.

Given that the Chief Constable was able because of his background knowledge and the information that he was able to impart in the run-up to 12 July to persuade the Orange Institution of the merits of not continuing with some traditional parades down some traditional routes, it is clear that he is the person best fitted to convince people of the merits of the case in hand. Can the Minister assure us that members of the commission will be given comparable knowledge? Will they have access to reports on the security background of particular parades? Without that knowledge, will they be able to make a valid decision that they can persuade other people is the correct one in the circumstances? If they are given such information, will they be empowered to impart it to other people? If so, that will raise many security concerns.

In those circumstances, I suggest that the Chief Constable, with his knowledge and with the knowledge possessed by the people with whom he is talking, is in a far better position to convince Loyalist orders.

In Northern Ireland, this is seen as an anti-Orange Bill. We should not sweep that fact under the carpet; that is how it is perceived. It does not matter what the Minister says is the truth. That is the perception, and perception counts on the streets of Northern Ireland, as Ministers well know.

It is time to draw back and give careful consideration to the Bill's consequences. Since it was discussed and just before it was debated in the House, we have been provided with more evidence on the source and the nature of the opposition to processions. I refer to the Apprentice Boys procession in Londonderry shortly before Christmas. Information was clearly available to the police that enabled them to take preventive action and launch a pre-emptive strike against Sinn Fein-IRA, which was clearly intent on mayhem and violence and, indeed, massive violence it caused. It did that because it wants to be able to determine who walks up and down the streets. If the Minister does not understand that, I assure him that that is the perception of people in Northern Ireland.

The information that the police had on that occasion was accurate. Would that information have been given to the commission? If the commission had been given information that people of evil intent were willing to ensure that violence and trouble would take place on that day, what view would it have taken? Would it have[Mr. William Ross] taken the same view as the police? If it had come to a different decision from the police, would the police or the Secretary of State have overridden it?

Lay people, no matter how well meaning they are, do not have the professional qualifications of senior police officers. They do not have the long experience and detailed understanding of what they will have to deal with. No matter who is selected for the commission, they will not be capable of taking as sensible, as balanced, as wise, or, as is sometimes necessary, as firm decisions as the police.

The result is that we need to pause and think through all the consequences of the proposed legislation. My concern, and that of the Committee and the House in general, should be greatly increased by the fact that the Rev. Roy Magee, the only person on the commission who had any standing with those most affected the Loyal institutions in Northern Ireland has resigned over the changing nature of the commission's powers.

One other member of the commission is a leading member of the SDLP and naturally will not enjoy the confidence of the Unionist population. When we see that that woman, who will inevitably bring her political baggage with her, is now to be the predominant voice in Londonderry where she lives, and will determining what the Apprentice Boys will do in their native city, we need go no further to make people think seriously about the consequences of going down this road.

The Rev. Roy Magee has done a great deal for Northern Ireland over the years. He has enjoyed the trust and confidence of the most violent and dangerous people in the loyalist community. I do not believe that the Government or the House have come to terms with the effect of his resignation, of the host of questions that it has raised in that community, of the dangers that flow from it and the fears that are aroused. They have not seen his replacement yet and, quite frankly, trying to find someone to replace him who will enjoy his standing is a task that no one can contemplate with any confidence.

This is a bad Bill and the Minister knows that; it makes changes and gives powers to people who are not qualified or fit to exercise them. It will cause the most horrendous difficulties. The commission will be put in the position of making decisions that it does not itself have to enforce: these will have to be enforced by the police. That will put the police on the street in a position of which the Chief Constable will not, to put it mildly, always fully approve. That is wrong.

While it was barely acceptable to some people that there was an advisory body, it is much less acceptable to a much larger number of people that we are creating a body with these executive powers to make criminals out of people who believe they are simply exercising their traditional rights and privileges. We are going too far. The capacity of the commission to appear as an independent arbitrator and acceptable neutral voice went out of the door with the Rev. Roy Magee. It did not have much credibility anyway in the eyes of those who are most affected.

Giving the commission these powers creating an organisation that will make and hand down decisions in defiance of the wishes of a great section of the population in Northern Ireland is not the way to bring peace and stability. It is a road to more confrontation and difficulty. It opens the door to the most violent elements of society to capitalise on it because they are saying quite bluntly, "The IRA by their violence and confrontation have created this body and therefore we will do the same." It is not a road down which I want the Government, the police or anyone else to travel. The proposed legislation, which has been accepted in principle by the two Houses, could be passed, but it should not be implemented until we see how things work out over at least the coming year.

That means that not only will the commission have information on what is likely to happen, but a clear statement of intent will be required of the counter-demonstrators. If they do not adhere to what they say they will do, they will break the law as defined in other clauses. Similarly, if parade organisers do not do what they say they will do, they will be guilty of breaking the law and will be subject to a penalty.

Clause 7 deals with the commission's powers to impose conditions on public processions, including the power to ban them. New clause 2 would give the same power to the commission to set conditions for counter-demonstrations or protests or to say that, in absolute cases, protesters cannot counter demonstrate or that they cannot counter demonstrate in the form that they say they want to.

Clause 10 refers to the Secretary of State's powers to prohibit processions.
