Magistrates' Courts (Procedure) Bill [Lords]

Clause 1

Statement to be served to enable accused to plead guilty without appearing

10.30 am

The Parliamentary Secretary, Lord Chancellor's Department (Mr. Geoffrey Hoon): I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, leave out lines 20 and 21 and insert--

    `(a) in a case where a statement of facts as mentioned in subsection (3)(b)(i) above was served on the accused with the summons, that statement;

    (aa) in a case where a statement or statements as mentioned in subsection (3)(b)(ii) above was served on the accused with the summons and the court does not otherwise direct, that statement or those statements;'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss Government amendment No. 2.

Mr. Hoon: With your leave, Mrs. Dunwoody, I will explain the nature of the proposed amendments, together with the impact of clause 1. That will avoid the need for me to speak again to clause 1 stand part. The amendments will make sense only if I explain the purpose and intent of clause 1.

The Chairman: The Chair highly approves of clarity and brevity. I am sure that the Minister will bear both those points in mind.

Mr. Hoon: Thank you, Mrs. Dunwoody. I shall do my very best.

The Bill's purpose is to amend existing legislation to reduce the number of adjournments in minor summary offences, principally traffic offences, in the magistrates courts. Section 12 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980 allows the police to issue a summons for minor summary offences. The summons will include what is known as a statement of facts, which is a concise summary of the facts supporting the accusation. The summons will also include forms describing the procedural steps to be taken by the recipient of the summons.

On receipt of the documentation, defendants have three options. First, they may plead not guilty by post or in person at the magistrates court. A date will be set for trial, which will proceed later.

Secondly, the defendants may choose to plead guilty by post or in person. As long as the statement of facts with the prescribed form was issued with the summons, the case may proceed. If no such statement was served with the summons for any reason, the case cannot proceed under the section 12 procedure of the 1980 Act, except where the defendant is unequivocally willing to plead guilty. No guilty plea can be accepted and the case must be proved in full. The documents or evidence to prove the case must satisfy the stringent provisions of the Criminal Justice Act 1967. Detailed statements must be set out and prepared in full or witnesses must come to court to give evidence. A further effect is that the Crown Prosecution Service must take responsibility for prosecuting the case even though it is not actually being contested.

The harm that the Bill seeks to address lies in the third option. It is very common that someone who has been summoned simply fails to respond or appear in court. The difficulty is that the 1980 Act does not allow the statement of facts alone to be sufficient documentation on which to try an absent defendant. All the documentation has been sent out, but the court has no knowledge of whether it was received by a defendant who simply chooses to ignore the summons.

The court must assume that the charge is contested if there is no response. The court must therefore take steps to obtain sufficient information to try what is apparently a contested case. Where the defendant ignores the information sent to him or her, the court is forced to treat the case as if it were a not guilty, disputed case. That is essentially the harm that this modest legislation, especially through clause 1 seeks to address.

The Bill will allow witness statements to be served on the defendant with the summons as an alternative to the statement of fact. It will permit someone who is in receipt of a summary or of a full statement to be dealt with by the court, even though he has simply failed to respond to an invitation by the court to attend to be dealt with.

Clause 1 essentially establishes that either document will be satisfactory for the court if a guilty plea is entered by post. If the defendant fails to respond to the summons, the witness statement--the full details--can then be used to prove the case on the listed day in the defendant's absence. That will save the considerable procedural inconvenience of having to transfer the case to the CPS to deal with it as if there were a not guilty plea. It will therefore save considerable time and expense.

That is the state of play as revealed by the Bill as originally drafted. If we agreed the Bill without the proposed amendments, it would mean in practice that the full section 9 statement--the full details proving the case--would have to be read out. However, the Bill is designed to save time and to avoid the inconvenience of further adjournment. Reading out the full statement in each case obviously might be a time-consuming exercise. It might be necessary even when the defendant has unequivocally demonstrated to the court that he or she intends to plead guilty.

Having attempted to explain, I hope, clearly and briefly, the impact of clause 1 as drafted, I should like to turn to the amendments. Several practitioners in the field have brought the Government's attention to the problem of clause 1 as drafted. The Government have carefully considered what practitioners have said, and believe that the amendments will further improve the procedure that the Bill establishes.

When a case comes to court, the amendments will allow the court clerk to give an oral account of, or to summarise, the witness statement that has been served on the defendant, if the defendant has written to the court to say that he wants to plead guilty. Perhaps I should explain why reading out a witness statement might be a time-consuming exercise. Inevitably, reading a full witness statement will take longer than reading a statement of fact, because it is a thorough and detailed account of what a witness saw, heard and did. It will have been prepared as an instrument of proof in the event of a not guilty plea for proving the case in the absence of a defendant who fails to respond.

For example, a speeding case statement will include details of all steps taken to check the accuracy of a calibrated speedometer in a police vehicle. If there were, for example, 100 speeding cases before the court on a particular day, it would be necessary to read out the same details for each of the 100 cases. Clearly, the evidence is technically necessary to rebut any suggestion that the equipment is faulty, but such detail is not necessary in a guilty plea case. When many similar cases are dealt with on the same occasion, the time taken can be excessive. It is important to find a way to short-circuit the problem.

The problem also occurs, for example, with statements for the offence of overloading a vehicle. The statements may contain lists of the permitted and actual weight for each of the several axles of a heavy goods vehicle. If a person admits that he overloaded a vehicle, it should not be necessary to read out in exhaustive detail all the permitted and actual weights.

The Bill as originally drafted would undoubtedly reduce the number of adjournments, and therefore save the court's time. However, there is a risk, which has been drawn to our attention, that each guilty plea could take longer than it does at present if each witness statement were read out verbatim. It may not take long to recite the full statement in a single case, but the cumulative effect in a busy court of reading out each and every statement in full could reduce the time and costs that the new procedures would otherwise save.

The amendments stipulate what is to be read out to a court by the court clerk in guilty plea cases. What is read out will depend on whether the summons is accompanied by a statement of facts or by a witness statement. When a summons is served with a statement of facts, the court clerk will simply read out that statement to the court, as is done at present.

When the summons is served with a witness statement, the clerk will read that statement aloud to the court, unless the court gives a direction that the clerk should give an oral account of so much of the statement as is not read aloud. The requirement for the court to give a direction means that it will always control the proceedings and that the court clerk may not summarise or give an oral account of what is in the statement without the court's express authority.

Clause 2

Evidence of previous convictions in motoring cases

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

11 am

Mr. Hoon: Clause 2 also simplifies the process of securing evidence before the court in summary and road traffic cases. It allows previous convictions for endorsable offences to be proved on the evidence of Drive and Vehicle Licensing Agency computer records for defendants convicted in their absence who have not forwarded their driving licences to the court.

This problem compares with that in clause 1. Where someone who simply ignores a summons is convicted at the end of the process, including adjournments if needed, the court does not have a driving licence available. Although the endorsement details on all licences are replicated on the DVLA computer print-out provided to the court, they cannot be used in the absence of a licence unless the defendant is served with at least seven days notice of the court's intention to cite previous endorsements.

That leads to an absurd situation in which everyone knows the previous convictions and endorsements, but the case has to be adjourned for permission to be obtained to deal with those previous convictions in the way that they could have been dealt with if the licence had been available. That is a considerable waste of time and public resources.

Clause 2 is designed to deal with the problem by removing the requirement to notify a convicted offender of the court's intention to take into account previous summary convictions as detailed in the offender's driver record as held by the DVLA. Where the offender fails to respond to the orignial summons or to deliver a driving licence to the court, magistrates will be able to take previous endorsements into account without adjourning to serve notice of their intention to do so. It is a modest change but it will significantly improve magistrates court procedure.

Unless anyone specifically requires it, I shall not go through the details of subsections (1) and (2) which are essentially technical and consequential provisions.

Clause 3

Power to issue warrant for arrest of accused after conviction where disqualification proposed

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Hoon: This is a further procedural refinement to the way that summary road traffic offences are dealt with, and it relates to the disqualification of defendants from driving.

Magistrates courts may disqualify a defendant from driving in his absence by following certain procedural steps, although the bench may wish to compel him to attend court to hear the reasons for the disqualification. The relevant powers are contained in section 13 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980.

When a defendant is convicted following sworn oral evidence from witnesses, a warrant can be issued. Increasingly, such cases are decided using section 9 written statements, which I mentioned earlier, and which are served on a defendant prior to his trial. They are mostly admissable to the same extent as oral evidence, but they do not meet the section 13 requirement that information should be substantiated on oath. That means that at present courts must secure the attendance of a police officer to substantiate the information, whether singly or in batches. That wastes police time and court time and it is often entirely meaningless because the officer may have no personal knowledge of the matters about which he swears. We are trying to modernise our court procedures, which currently assume that every case is contested and can be sworn to by an officer with personal knowledge of the facts. When magistrates courts deal with a large volume of relatively minor motoring offences, in practice that is not the case.

Similar difficulties arise when a defendant has pleaded guilty in person on a previous occasion, and was convicted without consideration of the evidence, or when the court has accepted a written guilty plea under section 12 of the Magistrates' Courts Act 1980. The Bill amends section 13 of the 1980 Act, so that in the case of a convicted person, the court can issue a warrant for a disqualification hearing without the need to substantiate information on it. That removes the need for a police officer to give up valuable time to attend a court to swear to details of which he or she may or may not have personal knowledge. We hope to free that officer to pursue those who need to be brought before the courts because they have committed offences.
