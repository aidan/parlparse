Patten recommended that the Secretary of State should set

    longer term objectives and principles

for policing instead of the two to three-year objectives to be set by the Policing Board. Patten also stated clearly that the

    Government should retain the power to set principles and broad overall objectives for policing. But the Policing Board should have the power to set the objectives, priorities and performance targets for Northern Ireland

policing

    in both the 3-5 year strategy and the Annual Policing Plan.

The Patten report said that it could see no reason whatever

    for government to second-guess the Board in these matters in the way that

the Bill provides. However, clause 22 places no limits on the sort of objectives to be set by the Secretary of State. He could even set annual objectives for policing in Northern Ireland. That is not desirable, it was not Patten's intention and the Bill should not allow it. The Bill allows the Secretary of State to retain the power to second-guess the Policing Board, which is exactly what Patten set out to prevent. It does not define what are long-term objectives-that is a matter of good policing practice taken from other police authorities and structures-but it is clear that he should not be involved in the three to five-year or annual objectives set by the board.

That is the purpose of the amendment and if the Committee wishes to endorse and pursue Patten's intentions, it will accept that the amendment is reasonable and proper and that it fulfils the purpose in which we are engaged.

Mr. McGrady: I thank the Minister for welcoming me to the Committee, although I doubt that I fit his description of me as a striker. I was always a pacifist by nature, and my performances on the sports field left much to be desired.

The amendments follow the theme that my party has pursued with regard to this part of the Bill, in trying to make it compatible, as far as is possible, with the Patten report. Patten stated that the Policing Board should have the power to set the objectives, priorities and performance targets for Northern Ireland in the three and five-year strategy, and the annual policing plan. However, the Bill omits any provision empowering the Policing Board to set performance targets. The Police Authority of Northern Ireland has strongly criticised that omission, and correctly argued that the setting of performance targets is a vital tool in the police planning process. Indeed, the Police (Northern Ireland) Act 1998 specifically empowers the Police Authority to set such performance targets, so there is no reason why that vital function cannot be set out in the Bill.

The Secretary of State has laid before the Policing Board draft regulations that would empower it to set performance targets. That is welcome but it is not enough. In respect of policing in Northern Ireland, the Secretary of State has reserved the express power to set performance indicators and performance standards not in the regulations, but clause 26(3), even though Patten saw no reason why the Secretary of State should second-guess the Policing Board on such matters. If there is sufficient room in the Bill to empower the Secretary of State to set such indicators and standards, why cannot the same be done for the Policing Board? That is what Patten recommended, and the main purpose of amendment No. 2 is to grant to the Policing Board the power to set such targets.

Amendments Nos. 3 and 4 are particularly important in terms of the way in which the Bill is currently drafted. Clause 24(2) will allow the Secretary of State to make regulations setting out what should be included in the policing plan, and again, he has made available draft regulations. They are not a matter of debate this morning, and we agree with much of what they contain, but they are none the less mere regulations-they have no statutory authority.
