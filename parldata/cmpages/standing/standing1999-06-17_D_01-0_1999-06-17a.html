Mr. Gary Streeter (South-West Devon): May I begin by warmly welcoming you to the Chair, Mr. Maxton. It is the first time that I have been a member of a Committee under your chairmanship, and I look forward to your jurisdiction over the next few weeks. I am sorry that you will have such a troublesome Government Front Bench team, but I know that Conservative Members will be extremely well behaved and courteous throughout our proceedings.

It is important that we pass the sittings motion and are prepared to sit on Tuesdays and Thursdays to discuss the Bill, line by line. This is the first time since 1 May 1997 that I have been a member of a Standing Committee, but there seems to be a lot of them over there and not many of us over here. I wonder if the Committee Clerk has the numbers right; perhaps you can look into that, Mr. Maxton.

We must have ample time in which to scrutinise the Bill. At first sight, it appears to be a technical Bill. It is, in one sense, but what it will put in place causes us considerable concern. The measure has huge ramifications, and it is important that Parliament has the opportunity to study it. I am delighted that my hon. Friend the Member for Hertford and Stortford (Mr. Wells), the distinguished Chairman of the International Development Select Committee, is in our midst, because the Select Committee has taken a great interest in the privatisation of the Commonwealth Development Corporation. His wisdom and input into our proceedings will be important.

We are concerned about two key issues. As we said on Second Reading, we strongly support the Commonwealth Development Corporation and agree profoundly with its objectives when it was set up in 1948. Its purpose was to promote economic development in the countries in which it operated—which then were mainly in the Commonwealth, but it has now spread its wings a little. We are worried that, under the Bill, the developmental objectives of the corporation—[Interruption]—a word from No. 10, I think—might be left behind. The CDC, in its new guise, might slip the moorings of those important developmental objectives and its vital role of investing in pre-emerging markets that the whole House has supported for 50 years. We must have enough time to explore whether the Secretary of State, in putting together the Bill, has adequately secured the developmental objectives of the CDC that we want to see continued into the 21st century.

Our second concern is, as we lawyers say, in the alternative; it is an equal but opposite concern to that which I have already expressed. The step has been taken to create a third way company—a public, private partnership. It is a Companies Act company and 75 per cent. of its shares will be owned by the private sector. It will be burdened with many regulations, obligations and Government controls. We want to examine whether the new creation will be of interest to the private sector. We want to explore who will invest in it and, given its strange make up, whether the Government are likely to get a fair price for it. It will be neither one thing nor the other. It will not be a private sector, fully fledged company, nor will it any longer be in the public sector. We want to probe whether taxpayers will receive a fair return on their investments over the past 50 years. We need adequate time to explore whether the Government have successfully found a third way for the Commonwealth Development Corporation. I do not think that they have, or that such a third way exists. I fear that the new CDC may be the worst of all worlds: it will attract little interest from the private sector and will therefore be undersold, but will be moved away from its developmental objectives over the next few years by shareholder pressure.

Having seen the tax amendments, I shall require the Government to assure us that the Bill has been considered carefully for hybridity. There must be sufficient time for us to be satisfied that a clear legal opinion on the Bill's hybrid status has been given to the Government, and that we find that opinion satisfactory. This a public sector Bill creating a private company that retains elements of public sector control but has a unique tax status. We shall need time to explore that issue over the coming weeks and months, as the Bill is complicated and technical. The fact that the tax amendments on which the Bill stands and falls were tabled only two days ago is not good enough. We are getting specialist advice on them, but it has left us short of time to do an adequate job.

There are a whole host of other issues that we want time to consider, such as what happens to existing guarantees and whether the Government intend to produce fresh ones, either when the CDC is a Companies Act company or when it has investors. Also, has risk passed from the public sector to the private sector under the Bill? Of course, we hope that the company succeeds, but if it goes horribly wrong in five or 10 years' time, do the Government intend to bail out private investors? We do not know such things at the moment, so a statement on risk would be extremely helpful. Is the Bill simply a way for the Chancellor to break his own spending rules, or is it a genuine attempt to find a new way forward on public-private partnerships?

We should like to hear more from the Government about when they intend to float the company, what their plans are about going to the market, and what advice they have received from experts on that subject. What price do they expect to achieve? We know that in their financial plans for the Department for International Development for the next few years, a sum of money has been allocated for the sale of the CDC, and we should like to hear what figures they have in mind.

How will the golden share be used? How does the Secretary of State intend to exercise her control in relation to the company? What will happen if it does something that she does not agree with, such as makes an investment that she profoundly feels is a mistake? Will she intervene, and what will happen if the other shareholders disagree with that? Have the Government considered the opportunity for litigation in that arena?

Some supporting documents suggest that the company will be able to borrow in the private capital markets after the Bill goes through. It can currently borrow from the Treasury at zero rates. We need to know more about its future borrowing intentions. As I suggested, we will have to examine in great detail the tax implications of the company. It is strange to take the significant step of suddenly producing amendments two days before a Standing Committee that will create, for the first time that I am aware of, a special tax status for one private-sector company that will make it unique in this country's legal history.

We shall also need to explore how the company will increase its return for investors from 7 per cent. or 8 per cent.—or, in the past year, 0 per cent.—to the sort of levels—15 per cent. or 18 per cent.—that private investors will expect.

If we can be satisfied on all those points, of course we shall support the Bill. We want the CDC to succeed, and we want private-sector investment in this important arena. We believe in the CDC and its vital role in investment in emerging and pre-emerging markets. We are worried, however, that the Government have not done their homework, that their thinking on the matter is not adequate and that they might not have found a new, third way forward but might achieve the worst of both worlds. As we proceed in exploring the Bill over the next few sittings, we hope that the Government will satisfy us that all our anxieties can be alleviated. We very much look forward to that.

I also hope that it will not only be Opposition Members and hon. Members on the Labour Front Bench who speak, but that contributions from some of the wise and experienced Labour Back-Benchers who are members of the Committee will help to enhance our proceedings. I know that you have no discretion in the matter, Mr. Maxton, but I hope that you will encourage them to make their own contributions as we proceed.

The Secretary of State for International Development (Clare Short): I am delighted to sit under your stewardship, Mr. Maxton—there could be none finer. I hope that all the wise and fine members of the Committee will participate in our deliberations—although I hope that none of us speak at unnecessary length. I agree with the message expressed in all the contributions of Opposition Members: we share the interest in getting this right. This is not a matter of crude party political difference. We are making a very important change and I hope that we will all work on getting it right. To follow the point raised by the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford, if details emerge through our deliberations, we wish to improve them. We share the objective of making the change in the CDC sucessful; let us scrutinise whether we have got it right and modify things if necessary. I agree with the hon. Member for South-West Devon that we should take the time to do the job well; I hope that it is a matter of weeks, not months—given the quality of intellects present in the Committee, I think that it will be. We can probably do the finest job necessary in a relatively short period, but which is sufficient to examine all the technical measures properly.

As everyone has said, the question is whether we are securing our development objectives properly. I say to the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford that, whatever the history of previous Governments considering changes to the CDC, the Government—and I as the relevant Secretary of State—came to the matter afresh. As I said in my evidence to the Select Committee, I was not under any pressure for the matter to be solved in order to raise funding for the development budget. It was a question of considering whether the CDC was as good as it could be or whether we could use it to increase investment in the poorest countries in the world.

Our objective is genuinely to use the CDC's fine track record of investing in markets into which the private sector rarely goes. As the hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford knows better than anyone, the CDC is structured, as a public institution, not to compete with the private sector; it is expected to get a lower rate of return than that expected in the private sector. In some senses the CDC operates with one hand behind its back, almost as a last resort lender, being instructed to go into places the private sector will not go. However, while we want to support reforms in the relevant countries that invest in human development through education and health care, we also want to help countries create a more successful economic performance so that they have the growth necessary to reduce poverty. Such countries therefore need to be attractive destinations for private sector investment.

The question is whether the CDC could attract private sector investment, expanding its investments in those markets and bringing in private sector funds. More investment is in itself a good thing, but if in addition, private sector investment could achieve a decent rate of return in those countries, that would act as a bridge for other parts of the private sector to cross into them; CDC would prove that profitable investment is possible in those markets. That is why we have to give birth to a new creature. If we simply privatised the CDC, without imposing constraints on where it invested, the market would take it out of the poorest countries because no other investment organisation has a significant presence in those markets. We had to create something new, to enable us to stay in the places where the private sector tended not to go and get more investment into them. If we did not entrench the development objective, the normal forces of the market would in time have taken the CDC out of the poorest countries. So that is our objective, which a series of provisions in the Bill are intended to secure.

The hon. Member for Hertford and Stortford asked whether there was any danger of the CDC being oversold or undervalued. We must ensure that that does not happen. The indefinite retention of a 25 per cent. golden share means that the golden share on taxpayers' interest is not just a transitional interest to get the CDC privatised. We, as taxpayers, retain a substantial continuing interest in the CDC because we have an indefinite quarter stake in the company; we therefore have an interest in getting a decent rate of return from investments and do not want the CDC to underperform or undersell. At the same time, we do not have an interest in simply offloading the whole thing and getting some kind of return back into the Exchequer.

I accept the criticism that the tax arrangements are late. How can I put this delicately? I am sure that all the Inland Revenue officials are extremely dedicated public servants with enormous, onerous responsibilities, but they are not the speediest officials in the public service. From the beginning, we made it clear that our objective was simply to secure a level playing field. Left to itself, the CDC would have to go offshore in order to compete with other investment companies for private sector funds, because of the different tax treatment there. That seems wrong, in practice and symbolically, and I was adamant that a major instrument of British development policy should not be headquartered offshore. I said when we discussed the matter that maybe we should have it in Africa and our advisers said, ``No way, you can't have it in Africa, the Cayman Islands, or wherever.'' They said we could put it in Ireland; it seems peculiar to suggest that a major instrument of British development policy should be headquartered there. Ireland is committed to development and its performance is becoming stronger and stronger.

We therefore wanted tax treatment for the unique body of the CDC which would not make it more privileged than other investment organisations but would put it in exactly the same position for tax purposes, which meant that it had to be headquartered in Britain. We made that objective clear in the House of Lords and on Second Reading. Now all the technical work has been done and approved by the necessary people, although I agree that the tax proposals have been tabled late—they are covered only in clause 27, so we have some time—and it is a case of examining the details. I shall provide to the Opposition, if they would like it, a detailed technical briefing. We could arrange that fairly soon and I hope that it helps. We can therefore look at the tax proposals properly and responsibly and make up a bit for their late tabling.

The hon. Member for South-West Devon asked about risk. Because we are retaining indefinitely the 25 per cent. share, any risk will be shared. Golden shares and Government holdings have often been transitory, but this is indefinite—subject to future Governments or Parliaments wanting to do something different with the CDC, such as privatising it completely, although there would be difficulties in doing that, as our discussions will show. The question of when the corporation is to be floated depends on how rapidly we can restructure the balance sheet and get a track record with a good rate of return. The CDC is moving more into equity investment and less into lone investment because that increases its rate of return but also because it has the desirable quality of committing it to improving the management and capacity of companies; The CDC is not just taking a return on a loan but wants successful, creative companies. When it has that track record—emerging markets are stabilising a bit, which is relevant to any proposals to float some or all of the 75 per cent. of CDC—we can consider flotation. We want to get it right for the success of the company, for the return of the taxpayers' money and for responsible investors.

There is a sense in which the money that wil be generated from the CDC has already been discounted. The Government have agreed to the proposition so that when the return comes in it will go the the Exchequer. We already have the budgetary commitment to spend more of our GDP on overseas development, moving towards the 0.7 per cent. UN target and we do not have the pressure of having to raise money by a deadline so that we have to move at a time that is not necessarily best in terms of achieving our objective. That is important; it would be sad if we had to rush to do a less good job because we had to get the money into our budget in order to fulfil other commitments. We are not under that pressure.

The hon. Member for South-West Devon asked an important question on the use of the golden share. The hon. Member for Richmond Park, the Liberal Democrat spokesperson, asked about how the ethical code is to be incorporated. The public-private partnership has deliberately been incorporated into the Bill and its structures from the beginning so that that cannot be subject to political interference. Its make up will not be unpredictable for those who wish to invest in it. The golden share will exist from the beginning. Two directors will be appointed by the Secretary of State, but that is to install the ethical code and make it impossible to change it—not that there will be a majority, which means that the board cannot be made to do things against its commercial interests.

The rules on investment in poor countries are entrenched and enforceable—70 per cent. in poorer countries and 50 per cent. in Africa and south-east Asia. I shall put before the Committee a certain exchange of correspondence that will enforce those rules; it is another belt and braces provision. Nothing about the company will be unpredictable when investors buy into it. Nothing will depend on a Secretary of State's whimsical interference. The partnership, its behaviour, its ethical code and where it will invest will be entrenched in its structure, so that investors will know what kind of beast they are involved with. It will not be dependent on political decision making, which we are advised will make the corporation successful.
