Dawn Primarolo: I hope to answer the questions raised by the hon. Gentleman and show him that his amendments are not necessary. I accept the points on which he seeks clarification.

Clauses 141 and 142 allow the United Kingdom to make new tax information exchange agreements with other countries to exchange information for the purposes of income tax, capital gains tax, corporation tax and inheritance tax. They also allow the Inland Revenue to disclose information under the terms of those agreements—a point on which the hon. Gentleman seeks clarification. The agreements are part of a package of measures designed to enhance international exchange of information which, as I said, the Government believe is the best way to tackle cross-border evasion and avoidance of tax. The agreements contain safeguards to protect the use and disclosure of information; they will improve the current arrangements and help ensure that taxpayers pay the right amount of tax at the right time in the right country.

Amendments Nos. 462 and 463 seek to limit the scope of the new tax information exchange agreements by requiring them to contain certain express safeguards to limit the use of information, protect taxpayer confidentiality and prevent the use of tax information exchange agreements in trivial cases. The amendments are not necessary because the safeguards that are already applied to information exchanged under the UK's existing arrangements will also be applied in due course to the new tax information exchange agreements.

We regard the proper protection of taxpayer confidentiality as essential in maintaining the integrity of the tax system. That principle applies in our dealings with other countries, which is why stringent safeguards are built into our current legislation and practice to protect taxpayer confidentiality. As I said in the previous debate, we focus only on information that is relevant to tax purposes. For example, all Inland Revenue officials are subject to strict statutory and common law duties of confidentiality. That duty is such that they are permitted to supply information to other tax authorities only when obliged to do so under the terms of a treaty or the EU mutual assistance directive. Before passing information to another country under the mutual assistance directive, the Inland Revenue must be satisfied that the other country is bound by rules of confidentiality no less strict than those that apply in the UK. For all our existing exchange of information arrangements, the receiving country is required to use the information only for tax purposes.

The need to protect taxpayer confidentiality and to ensure that information is used only for tax purposes is firmly embodied in clauses 141 and 142. In particular, proposed new subsection (2ZA) in clause 141 and proposed new subsection 220A(5) in clause 142 provide expressly that information will not be disclosed unless the Inland Revenue is satisfied that the confidentiality rules applied by the other country are no less strict than those applying in the UK. The Government will ensure that the agreements contain safeguards to guarantee that information can be used only for tax purposes. It is important that the new tax information exchange agreements will come into force only once approved by resolution of the House, in the same way in which we approve double taxation agreements.

As there is an opportunity for scrutiny, the amendments are unnecessary. They would require that information received by the UK authorities be treated as confidential, but that would add nothing to the common law and statutory principles of taxpayer confidentiality, which prohibit disclosure of taxpayer information received other than in tightly drawn circumstances. In some cases, disclosure is subject to criminal sanction.

The Law Society of Scotland suggested amendment No. 466. There has been considerable debate about situations in which information will be considered to have been obtained in privileged circumstances. In that sense, the amendment relates to amendment No. 467. The clause provides for an exception to the rule when information comes to the adviser with a view to furthering a criminal purpose. The phrase

    Items held with the intention of furthering a criminal purpose

has been the subject of consideration by the courts, although it is unclear who must have knowledge of the criminal purpose.

The amendment reflects the view that legal professional privilege could not be claimed when the adviser was in possession of the information with the intention of furthering the criminal purpose. If the adviser had no knowledge of the criminal purpose, the doctrine could still apply. English law, through the case of Francis and Francis v.
