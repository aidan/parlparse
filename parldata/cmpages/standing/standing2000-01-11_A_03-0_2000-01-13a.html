Mr. Davis: Good morning, Mr. Hood. You cannot imagine what a pleasure it is to see you this morning. Perhaps it is as well that we are not discussing congestion charges today.

Before I deal with the amendments tabled in my name and in those of other members of the Public Accounts Committee, I reiterate the point that I made during the previous sitting that these are all-partyóone might almost say non-partyóamendments. They are parliamentary amendments. They are also in the name of the right hon. Member for Ashton-under-Lyne (Mr. Sheldon), and therefore reflect the views of the Public Accounts Commission as well as the PAC. They also reflect the views of the Comptroller and Auditor General.

The combined experience of the people who have tabled the amendments is enormous. The right hon. Member for Ashton-under-Lyne was my predecessor on the Public Accounts Committee for 14 years, the longest ever sitting serving Chairman, and he fought on these issues for much of that time. His experience, coupled with that of the other signatories and the Comptroller and Auditor General, is extensive in the fields of accountability to Parliament and of the measurement and control of Government performance. On those grounds, I would argue that these are well judged amendments.

The thrusts of amendments Nos. 24 and 25 are rather different, so I shall deal with them separately. Amendment No. 24 is clear: its purpose is improve openness and accountability in the Government's financial reporting.

Clause 5(1) establishes the form of the financial statements that Departments will be required to provide to Parliament for the use of their funds. As it stands, the clause specifies that amounts must detail

    ``resources acquired, held or disposed of by the department during the year, and .Å.Å. the use by the department of resources during the year.''

What does that mean? One interpretation is that the first phrase refers to a balance sheet and the second to an income and expenditure account. That may be the intended reading, but we cannot be sure. The terms ``income and expenditure'' and ``balance sheet'' have meanings that are clearly understood not only in the world of accountancy, but among Members of Parliament and the public, so why not use them? They have formed the basis of financial reporting for almost a century. They are stable and will not change dramatically. Their explicit use would bring the Bill more into line with commercial accounting practice, which, after all, is the aim of resource accounting. The Companies Acts require a set of commercial accounts to include a profit and loss accountóor, in terms relevant to the Bill, an income and expenditure accountóa statement and a balance sheet. The Bill should impose the same requirement.

Paragraphs (a) and (b) are not sufficient for the preparation of resource accounts. The appropriation accounts that resource accounts will replace are currently subject to a statutory requirement that the Bill will repeal without reinstating. Appropriation accounts are, by definition, cash expenditure accounts. Accounting standards require a set of commercial accounts to contain a cash flow statement. The Bill should specify the same for resource accounts, so that the existing statutory requirement for an account of cash use is retained.

As was noted during an earlier debate, cash will remain a key control, even under resource accounting and resource-based supply, and will still be subject to separate authorisation by vote of Parliament. However, the Bill will not require Departments to provide Parliament with continuing information on their use of cash. That is a glaring omission. Parliament's first responsibility is to raise taxes, which are raised in cash, not resources. Taxes are accounted for as cash; indeed, they will be so accounted even under resource accounting. If the financing side of the equation is accounted for in cash, Parliament is entitled to clear information on the use of cash on the expenditure side. To give another example, the public sector borrowing requirement is also accounted for in cash.

The Minister may say that one intention behind resource accounting is to encourage Departments to think in wider terms than cash when deploying their resources. However, to Parliament and the Government, cash is still an important control. It will continue to be subject to a vote of the House that is separate from the vote that covers resources. A clear statement should be given to Parliament explaining how that cash has been used.

No successful or well managed company would operate on the basis of control of profits or income without keeping an eye on cash, and we should not do so either. The amendment deals with, that. Instead of the Bill's uncertain and incomplete specification of resource accounts, the amendment would require each set of accounts to contain a statement of income expenditure, a balance sheet and a cash flow statement. Those three statements have formed the basis of financial reporting for half a century, and two of them have formed that basis for a century. They provide a logical and robust framework, and it is difficult to envisage a need to change it for many years.

The Bill will also repeal a requirement in the original legislation for each appropriation account to be accompanied by a statement that explains the use of cash that exceeds that which is authorised by Parliament. That is an important provision. There is little point in Parliament specifying cash and resource limits if it receives no clear feedback about breaches of those limits. The Bill should reinstate a provision requiring an explanation of variations between amounts that are voted for Departments and amounts that are used by Departments. That is why the amendment would require an explanation of variations between cash and resources that are used and amounts that are voted.

The Economic Secretary may say that it is intended that resource accounts should show such variations. Without statutory provision, that would be done at the Executive's discretion. We have seen time and again that Parliament's rights in this regard should not depend on the Executive's discretion. That is not because we distrust the present Government but because the Bill will establish the framework for the foreseeable future.

The Minister rightly said that the letter that was sent out early last year contained fictional accounts for the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food. However, Parliament has not yet seen a full set of resource accounts in which the relevant information is provided clearly. It has not been assured that the new system will provide as much transparency and accountability as the previous system.

The amendment's proposed new subsection 1(a) would require the income and expenditure account and the balance sheet to show a true and fair view. It is a statutory requirement in the commercial world that directors of a company who draw up a financial statement should present a true and fair view. In central Government, the permanent secretary, in his role as accounting officer, occupies a similar role to that of a director of a company, and he or she be under similar obligations. It is he or she who puts his or her name to the accounts and who signs them off before passing them to the Comptroller and Auditor General for audit. It is anomalous that clause 6 requires the Comptroller and Auditor General to examine the accounts

    ``with a view to satisfying himself . . . that the accounts present a true and fair view'',

but that there is no requirement on Departments to ensure that accounts present such a view.

Those who are accountable for expenditure should be under a statutory obligation to follow the accepted rules, and the Comptroller and Auditor General should assure us that the rules have been followed. The Bill's failure to require Departments to produce financial statements that present a true and fair view is a major omission.

Amendment No. 25 addresses an incredibly significant issue. It is linked with an amendment that I have tabled to clause 6, which would provide for the Comptroller and Auditor General's examination of performance reports. The revolutionary element of resource accounting, as originally envisaged, was the link between resources used and performance delivered. Its aim was to provide the public and Parliament with information on what they get for their money rather than just how much money was spentófocusing on outcomes rather than inputs. Performance reporting by Departments is therefore one of the most important developments to come out of the Government's modernisation initiative. Speaking personally, I count myself as one of those who believe in the high quality and effective delivery of public services, and view this as possibly the most revolutionary reform of Government for a long time. It holds the prospect of dramatic improvement in the delivery of public services to those whom the Government want to help, including the most vulnerable in society. It will focus on how many people are cured rather than how much money is put into the health service. It will focus on how well we teach children, rather than how much is spent on education. It will focus on how well our justice system works, rather than how much money we spend in legal aid and so on.

The Bill covers issues that were the original focus of Parliament in the control of supply. In this sitting, which is one of the first in the new millennium, the future purpose of Parliament will focus on the extent to which we can deliver good public services to British citizens. That is a vital component of resource accounting and is, in many ways, more vital than the commercial accounting practice that we are introducing in the Bill.

The point that strikes me is that the Government have changed course. Performance reporting was integral to the accounts at the outset. At every stage of parliamentary consideration of the initiative, the importance attached to performance measures and their independent validation was paramount. I am referring not just to the Public Accounts Committee but to the Treasury Select Committee and the Procedure Committee. They argued in favour of the importance of these measures and independent validation by the Comptroller and Auditor General of the National Audit Office. That is why the amendment focuses on sending the information to him.

If we are to have good, clear accounts for the public sector, we must have a system of good, clear performance information to go with it. Modern management of public finance demands that Departments show what has been achieved with the money provided by Parliament. The Bill is silent on that. My judgment and that of the Public Accounts Committee is that this is a missed opportunity of mammoth proportions. The technology is now available to measure performance at a micro level, as well as a macro level, creating an unprecedented opportunity to identify and spread good practice, improve value for money and so on. The Government seem to have recognised that, and are developing an extensive performance measurement regime, but almost independent of that, they have moved the goal posts with respect to the link to resource accounts.

Integral to resource accounting when it was first introduced was a requirement on Departments to produce a statement known as an output and performance analysis, in which they reported their performance by objectives. That was an integral requirement, not an option, and it was to be produced and published within the financial reporting cycle. That would have established a clear link between performance and resources. It would have enabled Parliament to make well-founded judgments about the effectiveness and use of resources in pursuit of Government policy and would have been in the public interest.

By not incorporating in the Bill the requirement to produce output and performance analyses, the Government have broken the direct link between performance and resources, which was a cornerstone of the resource accounting initiative. That would have ensured a direct comparison between resources expended and output achieved, and would have provided valuable information to Parliament as it considered future spending programmes. The amendment would ensure that Parliament had that detailed performance information.

Elsewhere in the public sector, bodies are required by statute to provide performance information. The Government, rightly, are imposing a rigorous, independently audited performance measurement regime in local government. They are pouring millions of pounds into the Audit Commission to finance the new audit regime, but we have yet to hear a well founded argument why central Government should be different. That relates to the issue of independent validation and performance information. In local government, the best value performance regime will be rigorously audited; indeed, the Audit Commission is expanding massively to do that. However, the Government have refused to match that discipline in central government. The failure of the Bill to provide the Comptroller and Auditor General with the information to allow him to examine a report on departmental performance information will undermine its credibility and usefulness. That is a tragedy for the Government and for Parliament.

The Government are undertaking a large amount of performance information creation. Research done by a Liberal Democrat Member on written questions in the past few weeks showed that 2,500 new performance measures were being created. We should consider the risk involved in that, which applies to any Government. Under this Government and the previous Government, there have been disputes about the nature of information. Most recently, those disputes have been in relation to national health service waiting list figures, tax burdens and numbers of policemen. Under the previous Government, disputes were in relation to unemployment figures, and other figures. All Governments are subject to the temptation to put the best gloss on their performance. That is perfectly understandable political behaviour, and a part of life in a democracy. However, it is vital to the proper operation of democracy that the public, and especially Parliament, can rely on a firm factual basis of information from Government.

There is a significant risk that, if this amendment or a similar one is not accepted, the thrust of the performance measurement aspect of resource accounting will be jeopardised by sterile disputes over whether a particular figure is right or a piece of propaganda. Such disputes will undermine the public credibility of those numbers. If that happens, the managerial usefulness of the figures will be undermined, even within the Department. The Department will start to view them as numbers with which the Minister can defend himself at Question Time, rather than a proper measure of the Department's achievements.

The amendment, in my judgment, is one of the most important that I will table in this Committee. It offers the hope of a major step forward for parliamentary accountability and a major step forward in the Government's modernisation programme, on the presumption that that programme is designed to provide the best service for the British public in the future.
