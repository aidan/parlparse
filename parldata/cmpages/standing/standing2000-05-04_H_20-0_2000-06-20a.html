At present—I think that I am right in saying that it is since the 1960s—under the ruling in a case involving Bowater, the amount of tax credit that one receives in relation to dividends remitted from one of one's overseas subsidiaries is proportional to the ratio of that dividend to the underlying profit of the subsidiary. To put that into easy numbers, if I, as a UK-headquartered company, had an overseas subsidiary with a profit before tax of £100 and a dividend-to-payout ratio of two, so that I was delivering a dividend of £50 back home, and if my overall tax bill in that country were £30, the amount of tax that I could relieve in relation to that dividend would be one half—50/100—of the £30 chargeable against the total profit. In this example, that would be £15. There would be nothing wrong with that.

However, paragraph 8 contains a redefinition of the profit before tax. Line 44 on page 506 describes ``profits available for distribution''. I shall try to explain how the effect of the redefinition will be significantly to reduce the amount of credit available in relation to the dividend.

In paragraph 8(2), new subsection (6)(b)—the first two lines on page 507—states that the definition of the profits available for distribution—in my example, the £100 figure—should make

    no provision for reserves, bad debts or contingencies other than such as is required to be made under that law.

``That law'' means the law of the foreign jurisdiction.

Let us turn to an example—I shall not cite that of Zimbabwe, because it is a bad and controversial one—which I should stress is invented. Although I have no precise knowledge of tax laws in Uganda, it will have a set of such laws. Perhaps Ugandan law specifies what provision must be made for reserves, bad debts or contingencies in the tax accounts of Ugandan firms, but on the basis of my professional knowledge of adjacent countries, I suspect that it does not. Certainly, many countries do not make such provisions in detail.

In countries where the law is not specific—an interesting example is the UK—auditors tell the company what is a reasonable provision for

    reserves, bad debts or contingencies.

Or, more accurately, auditors discuss with the board possible reasonable provisions. My right hon. Friend the Member for Wells, who has considerable experience in such matters, will know that, on the whole, after a little negotiation and argy-bargy, boards will largely accept their auditors' view of a fair provision for contingencies such as unfunded pension liabilities. That is a perfectly ordinary and proper commercial proceeding. Over-provision and under-provision would lead auditors to qualify the accounts, so in general, companies follow their auditors' advice about what is a proper provision. That is particularly true of countries with undeveloped company law in which almost nothing is specified, and where virtually all matters are decided by auditors.

Let us consider the problem of a UK-headquartered multinational that has a subsidiary in a country where the law on such points is unspecific. The subsidiary makes a perfectly proper provision or reserve against bad debts or contingencies, which auditors specifically endorse—indeed, perhaps implicitly demand—but the law does not demand. In such a case, for UK double tax purposes, the multinational would have to count its profits as significantly higher than its stated profits, because it would have to subtract reserves or provisions made in that year that were mandated not by local law, but by its own auditors. Having subtracted the reserves or provisions and hence increased its profit before tax—my £100 figure, say, rising to £200—it would reduce the proportion of the profit before tax represented by the dividends remitted to the UK, if the dividend pay-out ratio on its genuine accounting profits were to remain constant. In doing so, it would diminish its ability to claim UK tax back for tax paid in the foreign jurisdiction.
