I am prepared to take as long as is necessary to ensure that the Bill emerges from the Committee in a thoroughly analysed and perfected form. That is our job to improve legislation. I doubt, however, whether many Bills that have come before a Standing Committee are as long and as complex as this one, or have so many ramifications for local government and for the conduct of important matters relating to London, such as transport, the environment and many others. Of course, I want orderly and constructive progress, but I doubt whether our consideration can, as the Minister hopes, occur in the full detail that the Bill merits.

I hate to go back over past days, but the Committee should understand that sittings motions can be very significant. In 1984, the Civil Aviation Bill would have artificially limited the number of air transport movements into Heathrow to 275,000 a year. The Government of the day had a vast majority. It was not as big as that of the present Government, but it was sufficient to ensure the usual rapid and constructive conduct of business. The Committee denied the Bill a sittings motion, even though it had been agreed to with a huge majority on Second Reading. The Bill was killed thereby. The amendment is as important as any that will be discussed during the Committee's proceedings. As members of the Committee are aware, the assembly will be elected by a form of the additional member system, whereby 14 members will be elected on a constituency basis and 11 members will be elected from top-up list. Candidates who properly submit their names for a list, which can be as short as a list of one, can be elected to the assembly.
