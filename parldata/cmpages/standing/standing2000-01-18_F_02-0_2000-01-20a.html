Clause 1

Maintenance calculations and terminology

Amendment proposed [18 January], No 1, in page 1, line 8, at the beginning to insert the words--

'(A1) Subsection (1) shall not come into force until such time as the Secretary of State shall have published in the London Gazette, an estimate prepared by him of:

    (a) The number of maintenance calculations he expects to perform (whether as a result of the operation of section 4 of the 1991 Act or as a result of the operation of section 6 of the 1991 Act) in the period of 12 months following the coming into force of subsection (1);

    (b) The number of maintenance calculations referred to in (a) above in which the amount of child support maintenance would be more if that maintenance calculation were to be undertaken in accordance with Part I of Schedule 1 as amended by this Act rather than in accordance with Part I of Schedule 1 to the 1991 Act prior to any amendment by this Act;

    (c) The number of maintenance calculations referred to in (a) above in which the amount of child support maintenance would be less if that maintenance calculation were to be undertaken in accordance with Part I of Schedule 1 as amended by this Act rather than in accordance with Part I of Schedule 1 to the 1991 Act prior to any amendment by this Act; and

Angela Eagle: Let me give the hon. Lady the information for which she asked; perhaps then she will be happy.

Of the one and a half million children now on the CSA's books, only about 300,000--this is a well-known figure--are enjoying the benefit of maintenance, and of those, fewer than half are getting all the money that is due to them. We estimate that the new, simpler child support system, together with the introduction of a £10 maintenance premium to income support and the 100 per cent. disregard of maintenance in working families tax credit, will mean that more than one million children will stand to gain. Many of them will have the benefit of maintenance for the first time. They include about 600,000 children in the poorest families on income support.

The amendment asks for figures in relation to applications for maintenance in the first 12 months of the scheme, but it is not strictly possible to provide that information in the form requested--obviously, we cannot know in advance the personal circumstances of the people who will be coming to the Child support Agency for a calculation of their liability in the first year of the new scheme's operation. The estimates are therefore contingent on those assumptions.

Angela Eagle: If the hon. Gentleman would let me make some progress, he might decide that he does not need me to write to him.

In all, we estimate that the Child Support Agency will complete in the region of 300,000 new maintenance calculations in the 12 months following subsection (1) coming into force. Of those, around 200,000 of the non-resident parents will be in work and, as I said, assuming that future applicants maintain the characteristics of the current case load, we estimate that around 65 per cent. or 130,000 cases will have a lower maintenance liability than they would have done under the current scheme, and 35 per cent. or 70,000 cases will be assessed as having to pay more.

Of the 100,000 cases where the non-resident parent is on benefit, we estimate that around 50,000 will have a lower assessment, because of the reduction in the flat-rate contribution for those on benefit from £5.35 to £5, and that 50,000 will pay more, because the current exemptions from paying the minimum amount will have been removed in most cases.

In other words, of the 300,000 non-resident parents coming on to the CSA's books in the first year of the new scheme, around 180,000 are likely to have to pay less maintenance and around 120,000 will have to pay more. We estimate that in only a small number of cases--we think fewer than 5,000--will the amount of maintenance remain the same, whether it is calculated under the new or the current rules.

In total, around 60 per cent. of non-resident parents will have a lower assessment under the new scheme. That does not mean--this is one of the basic errors, to put it gently, that was made in the speeches that we have heard--that 60 per cent. of parents with care will be worse off. As I said, it must be borne in mind that assessments currently bear little relation to amounts paid. Only 40 per cent. of those using the CSA's collection service pay everything that is due and overall only about 66 per cent. of maintenance due is collected. The new scheme will mean that more men will pay more of what is due and, hopefully, more quickly. That, combined with the maintenance disregards in income support and working families tax credit, will mean that the large majority of parents with care--around 85 per cent., we think--will be better off at the point when they first come to the CSA.

There are bound to be differences in maintenance liability between the new scheme and the current complicated formula. The Hon. Member for Gainsborough (Mr. Leigh) almost argued that we should not do anything because we will change maintenance liabilities. His speech was an argument for doing nothing. But we cannot learn from the lessons of our experiences with the Child Support Agency to date to make the system fairer and more effective, and to get maintenance money flowing to the children who deserve it, without changing the liabilities.
