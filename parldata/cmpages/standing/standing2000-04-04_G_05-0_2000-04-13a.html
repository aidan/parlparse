Schedule 4

Meaning of "offence against a child"

Mr. Hawkins: I beg to move amendment No. 81, in page 49, line 13, at end insert--

    '(ee) an offence under section 47 of that Act (actual bodily harm),'.

All members of the Committee recognise that offences committed against a child are among the most upsetting and worrying matters that arise in our society. I have referred previously to inquiries into the sad and tragic events of child abuse, such as the recent Waterhouse inquiry into events in children's homes in Wales.

It would be helpful to add the offence of actual bodily harm to the list of offences in the schedule. It would be difficult for anyone to suggest that an offence of actual bodily harm is not serious. During my work at the Bar, I have both prosecuted and defended many people tried, and, in some cases, convicted, of offences of actual bodily harm. The Minister will know from his professional experience how often someone is initially charged with an even more serious offence under section 18 of the Offences against the Person Act 1861--causing grievous bodily harm--or an offence under section 20--wounding--but, because of the evidence, is ultimately convicted of the lesser offence of assault occasioning actual bodily harm. As the Minister and many other hon. Members will know, that may nevertheless be an extremely serious matter. We were therefore surprised that it is not included in the list of offences in schedule 4. It might be possible to go even further and add common assault to the list, but we have not gone that far. We suggest that the list of offences might usefully include offences under section 47--assault occasioning actual bodily harm--in addition to the more serious offences already listed. If it does not, someone who has been involved in a serious crime will not be subject to the Bill's regime, and that would be unfortunate.

I do not expect the Minister to accept that the Opposition are right, but we hope that he will take the matter seriously, as he has done for several previous matters, and will examine whether the Government might reconsider it and, perhaps, introduce their own amendment in another place or on Report. We hope that the Government will take it seriously, and we look forward to hearing what the Minister has to say. I have every confidence that in dealing with such a serious matter the Minister will understand that we tabled the amendment with serious intent, and we hope that he will give it the serious attention that it undoubtedly warrants.

Mr. Boateng: The amendments to schedule 4 are important, and the Committee and, in due course, possibly the House will want to discuss them with great care. Schedule 4, in conjunction with clause 24, establishes the circumstances in which a person is deemed to have committed an offence against a child--there is a range of offences--and thus to qualify for a disqualification order. It will help the Committee if I explain the Government's thinking behind the schedule and clause 24. It is necessary to examine the two together, for obvious reasons.

The common feature of the offences is that the victim is a child. Someone who is charged with or has committed such an offence can therefore be identified as posing a risk to children. The offences are listed in schedule 4, and, as we discussed on the previous amendment, the list is capable of being altered by affirmative resolution.

We have limited the ban to people who are convicted of one of the listed offences, and who serve a minimum prison sentence. Those two criteria must be seen together, as they form a dual key. We want the ban to be as strong as possible. The aim is that it should always be imposed by the judge unless he specifies in court reasons why the offender does not represent a danger to children. That minimises the chance that it may not be applied in highest risk cases. The offences listed need to be those that would normally carry such a risk, which is why care has been taken to identify those that should be included.

9.45 am

We have had to face the issue of proportionality. The ban will prevent the individual from taking part not only in paid work with children, but in a range of activities such as helping out in clubs or school-based activities, including those in which his own children participate. We believe that the ban should be imposed only by a judge, and only for a serious offence that receives a severe sentence. The hon. Member for Taunton and I have exchanged views on proportionality in the Chamber in relation to other legislation. It is a subject that we must have in mind when considering the schedule and the clauses on which it impacts.

There are two safeguards to ensure that offenders do not fall through the net. If they worked in an area covered by the Secretary of State's bans, they could be banned in a wider range of circumstances than those before the criminal courts, in which the judge can rely on only the details of the criminal offence and any reports. Although other convictions, including ABH if the provision were unamended, might not carry a ban, they would appear on the individual's criminal record. Therefore, they could properly be taken into account before a decision was made to take someone on as a volunteer or employee. Working with children falls under the exception provisions of the Rehabilitation of Offenders Act 1974, and thus convictions and cautions cannot ever be spent, I am sure that we all agree, in terms of the high standard that we expect from people who work with children, that that should be the case.
