Mr. Boateng: May I start on this bright and early morning by welcoming you to the Chair, Mr. Cook, and to what we very much hope will be the last lap—[Interruption.] well, I may have spoken too soon—of what has been a good-humoured and worthwhile Committee stage.

The amendments provide for a lower age limit of 20 on the requirement to register under the Sex Offenders Act 1997 in all three jurisdictions—England and Wales, Scotland and Northern Ireland. That means that those committing the new offence who are under 20 do not have to register. Our starting point is that someone is working with young people who have been identified as sufficiently vulnerable to need the protection of the new offence, and who abuses that position of trust should be subject to registration.

There is an argument that, given the consensual nature of the sexual activity, the offence of abuse of trust differs fundamentally from other sexual offences and, hence, registration is not appropriate in any circumstances. I am not convinced by that. However, there is an important difference between culpability, which applies to all those committing the offence, and risk, which may not. Under the Bill, abuse of trust in specified circumstances should be viewed as criminal, provided that the offender is over 18. Under the Bill, a 19-year-old who has a relationship with a 17-year-old in respect of whom he is in a position of trust is properly guilty of an offence.

However, judged in terms of risk to the community, which is the principle underlying the Sex Offenders Act 1997, an age limit of 20 can be justified. The risk to the community is much less if a 19-year-old has consensual sexual relations with a 16 or 17-year-old, irrespective of the relationship of trust. The age limit achieves consistency with schedule 1 of the 1997 Act, which exempts certain offenders from the requirement to register, depending on their age and the age of their victim. For example, an offender who is under 20 and who is convinced of unlawful sexual intercourse with a girl aged between 13 and 16 is not required to register. It would, therefore, be anomalous if those under 20 were required to register for the abuse of trust offence, but not for unlawful sexual intercourse.

For the reasons that I have outlined, the amendment is necessary.

I am sure that the amendment will find favour with the Government as it is in a similar vein to the previous set of amendments. It also relates to issues of risk and consistency, in particular that of whether courts should have the discretion to put someone on the register. We agree with the Minister that the register exists to prevent further risk to the public. The people whom we want on it are those who are likely to reoffend, so that we have an active register in which the police, probation service, and so on have active control over those who are on it.

On the other hand, we do not want people put on the register who are very unlikely to reoffend. The Minister identified those under 20 as a blanket category of such people. We believe that other categories—not blanket but discretionary categories, which would have to be decided by the courts—might also be identified. For example, a young teacher has a relationship with a 17-year-old. The teacher is taken to court under the abuse of trust legislation and may receive a caution, fine or other appropriate punishment, but the couple may subsequently marry. That is not inconceivable; teachers have in the past married their pupils. The abuse of trust is reprehensible and should be punished, but to put such a person on the sex offenders list for some years because he committed a consensual offence with a person who is now his spouse would not benefit the system or the community.

The courts should have discretion, given that cases will often come up some time after the offence was committed and that events such as a subsequent marriage may have occurred. The courts will be able to judge whether the offender is a serial offender who might get a job in another school and abuse more 16 and 17-year-olds, or whether the incident was a one-off, perhaps leading to a substantive relationship, and there is no risk of further offences.

We need such a discretion and the working of the amendment is one way of achieving it. When the previous Minister of State, the right hon. Member for Cardiff, South and Penarth (Mr. Michael), wanted to include a similar discretion in the Sex Offenders Act 1997, the distinguishing feature that he used was whether the person had been given a custodial sentence. That is a proper test because the courts give custodial sentences for serious serial offences and are more likely to give a caution or small fine in the discretionary cases to which I have referred.

I am not wedded to the wording of the amendment, but it sets down the important principle, which the Government should accept, that, in line with the logic of the Minister's comments on the previous set of amendments, individuals who pose no subsequent risk may be caught by the legislation. To put such individuals on the sexual offenders register would be a waste of the time of the police and the probation service and would provide no benefit to the public.

Dr. Harris: I shall make general points in response to the Minister and a specific point about why it is vital that the new clause is included. I was not convinced by the Minister, despite his initial kind remarks. His general point was that the proposal is too much, too soon and that it is an attempt to move immediately to a full equality agenda.

Many Members want to see an end to discrimination. I recognise that the Bill is not the way to bring about full equality immediately. Ministers have other matters to consider, and if we were discussing a general sexual offences Bill, we would be here for weeks. We have the opportunity to deal with matters relating to the age of consent that fall within the remit of clause 1. I will explain why it is vital that we do so. Certainly, the relatively small measure proposed in the new clause would give a statutory defence to homosexuals that is already available to heterosexuals. It is not moving too fast, too soon. The proposal is integral to the issues covered by clause 1; otherwise, it would not have been selected for debate.

Some of us, including many Labour Members, see that the presumption should be in favour of equality, so I reject the Minister's charge that the provisions in the new clause would be going too fast. The perilous discrimination that results in people finding themselves in the dock has existed since time immemorial. Caution is always required when convicting young people of sexual offences because of the shame that accompanies conviction.

In the climate of decriminalisation of the younger party, the possibility of blackmail and the clampdown already referred to, we must preserve the right of 16, 17, 18 and up to 24-year-olds engaged in homosexual acts to have the same defence and human rights as heterosexuals.
