I also welcome the clause because it is one of the few to introduce greater flexibility to the operation of the Greater London authority and Transport for London. It will enable the various players to move around assets, rights and liabilities in a way that could empower Transport for London and the GLA to do their job more effectively. I am attracted by the clause because it begins to make the job of mayor for London more worth while. However, despite its value, I, like my hon. Friend the Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey (Mr.Hughes), have not been tempted to put my hat into the ring for that job.

    "The Secretary of State shall, before making an order bringing into effect the provisions of this Act",

and I am sure that members of the Committee took it to mean that.

The information that we ask for should be available to the Committee as we debate the clause. The Minister will say that aspects of that information are already published in annual reports and elsewhere in the public domain. However, they should be brought together in a coherent, structured way so that we can see what Transport for London will be worth and what assets it will hold.

I hope that the Minister will say clearly when the Government intend to publish the information, even if they do not accept the amendment. I also hope that he will tell the Committee how they intend to publish it. In the balance sheet that would presumably accompany such a publication, will they show clearly the types of assets, liabilities and rights and how they differ?

I shall be specific on that point: new clauses 42 to 44, which we shall debate later, will limit the marketability of Transport for London's assets by defining some of them as key system assets that cannot be used for collateral security. Those assets will have a different nature on any balance sheet. I hope that, when the Government publish the list of assets, liabilities and rights that we ask for, they will indicate such differences. That will be important to the people of London, to interested parties looking at the accounts and to future mayoral candidates.

It is important that the information is published as soon as possible because it might play a major part in the mayoral elections. Mayoral candidates might want to put to the people of London their ideas of how those assets could be used. They will be the most valuable part of the GLA's portfolio, and it is important that candidates can say how they will use them for the benefit of Londoners.

Before the last election, there was a discussion about the potential for privatising London Transport, an idea put forward by the previous Conservative Government. During that debate, there were huge variations in the estimates as to how much London Transport and London Underground were worth. If I recall correctly, the Government of the time put a figure of £800 million on those assets, while estimates of up to £13 billion were made elsewhere. When such a huge range of valuations exists, the Government must be specific about what they think the assets are worth, so that those estimates can be challenged and form part of the debate with the mayor.

Large amounts of land and buildings in the capital will be owned by the Greater London authority. Local communities will wish to know the views of assembly candidates on the future use of those assets. Employees of the relevant bodies will wish to know the rights and frameworks surrounding the pension rights of those working for the new authority.

Some of that information is gradually being prepared. In 1997, the Treasury published the national asset register, the so-called Domesday book, which sets out a number of the assets to be transferred. For example, it tells us that the net fixed assets of the department of the Traffic Director for London are £0.28million, and those assets will be transferred. It gives a very limited breakdown of the assets of London Regional Transport and gives the value of LRT's net fixed assets as £7,485.2 million. I could go on.

The so-called Domesday book does not meet the demands of the amendment, because it does not give the sort of detail to which Londoners are entitled. The original Domesday book was more comprehensible and detailed than the Government's more modern attempt.

Central Government are bringing in new accounting systems. Reforms were triggered by the previous Conservative Chancellor of the Exchequer to bring in resource accounting. Those important reforms, which were welcomed in all parts of the House, enable us to look more closely at the capital assets of the public sector and to make proper decisions about them. We can then find out what the civil service and the Government are doing to the capital base of our country.

Given that that reform is well under way in central Government, and is being implemented in other areas of the public sector such as the national health service and local authorities, I hope that the Government will ensure that the new authority will play its part in that financial revolution in the public sector. The starting point is to have the information published.

I wish to ask the Minister some specific questions. When will the Government publish that information? What estimates can they give to the Committee today of the value of the total assets and liabilities of the Greater London authority and of Transport for London? What breakdown would they be prepared to give to the Committee? What accounting rules and standards will the new body have to follow? Will it work to the new resource accounting rules, or will it have separate rules? Will it take the benefits of accruals accounting, and how will that be linked to the information that we are demanding today? I look forward to the Minister's response, and I hope that the Government, in their professed spirit of openness, will provide us with the information that we require.

Clause 151

Discontinuance of a network service

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Ottaway: The clause deals with the discontinuance of a network service. Those of us who live in London regularly hear, usually via a constituent, that a bus service is about to be withdrawn or varied. Rarely is one notified of that in advance by London Regional Transport, London Buses or the appropriate authority.

I should simply like to put down a marker--I hope that TfL will take a more user-friendly approach to the variation of bus services. Such variations happen continually in south London and the authorities take a highly aggressive stance. For instance, a bus service that used to take children from the south of the borough of Croydon to the north of the borough was changed with the result that children had to stand at intersections for up to half an hour on the way to or from school waiting for the next bus to come along. If anyone from the future TfL should read these remarks, he or she should note that a more user-friendly style of consultation would be much appreciated.

Mr. Raynsford: The hon. Gentleman raises an issue of obvious significance to any London Member of Parliament. All of us have experienced problems when changes of services have been introduced in a way that did not appear to be sensitive to, or pay sufficient regard to, the views of the local community. That, as he quite rightly stresses, causes a lot of irritation, and we should ensure that TfL does better in the future than LRT has done in the past. I share that concern. We have made clear commitments to consultation, but the requirement to consult depends greatly on the consultation being conducted in the spirit in which we would like to see it done.

Inevitably, in a city like London, where a lot of changes take place, there will be a constant need to reassess the effectiveness and appropriateness of routes, and to change routes to provide a better service. This will inevitably be a continuing process. I can only endorse the hon. G's concerns that there is effective consultation, and that the letter of the law, which we are approving in the clause, should be interpreted in a spirit that pays heed to the real concerns of people all over London about having a say with regard to the services on which they depend and which are provided for their needs.
