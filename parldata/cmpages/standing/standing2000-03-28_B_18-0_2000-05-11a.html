Mr. Heath: I intend to be relatively brief this morning. This is an important group of amendments. Clause 54(1)(f) provides that local highway authorities should consult

    such other persons as they may consider appropriate.

The amendments seek to qualify, in a variety of ways, those ``other persons'' who should be consulted about the preparation or review of a rights of way improvement plan.

Amendment No. 599 seeks to add the local access forum to that list. I am confident that the Minister will resist it, just as he has resisted every other attempt to have the words ``local access forum'' included in the Bill. That does not prevent me from being persistent.

I am a half-owner of amendment No. 644. I shall not speak to it. Instead, I shall allow the hon. Member for Stafford (Mr. Kidney) to speak to it, as he beat me to the table by about three seconds. Inspiration obviously struck us both at the same moment.

It is transparently obvious that the categories of persons to be consulted should include those who represent conservation and recreation interests, but there must be a read across from part I about access to open countryside and the rights of way network; it must include landowners. The amendments seek to explore the reasons why the Government left it entirely to the discretion of the local highway authority or the national park authority to decide which bodies were appropriate. I say that not because I want an exhaustive or prescriptive list, but because it is self-evident that various other categories should be consulted. I believe that they would be better mentioned in the Bill rather than later in regulations or guidance.

Mr. David Kidney (Stafford): As the hon. Member for Somerton and Frome (Mr. Heath) said, he tabled an identical amendment immediately after I tabled amendment No. 644.

Clause 54 requires highway authorities to consult a number of bodies on their rights of way improvement plans—including, if a long-distance route passes through the area, the Countryside Agency or the Countryside Council for Wales. The corollary is clear: if no long-distance route passes through the area, the highway authority need not consult those bodies.

The Minister's intention may be meant as a kindness to the Countryside Agency or its sister organisation in Wales, but it is an unnecessary kindness. The briefing that hon. Members received from the Countryside Agency says that the agency positively wants to be consulted about every rights of way improvement plan. Although the briefing received from the Countryside Council for Wales does not use the same words, it says that someone should be responsible for the overall coordination of improvement plans and that if it is not the Countryside Council for Wales or its sister organisation, the Secretary of State should do so under the provisions of clause 61. The Secretary of State might wish to be relieved of that obligation.

There are good reasons why the Minister should accept amendment No. 644 and allow the two organisations to which it refers to be consulted on all rights of way improvement plans. They relate to consistency and quality. None of the consultees specified in the clause will receive every rights of way improvement plan as a matter of course. The amendment would add to the clause the only consultees that would see all the plans and which could compare them, ensure consistency, promulgate best practice and draw attention to those who are lacking their duties. The amendment is, therefore, modest but desirable.

Mr. James Paice (South-East Cambridgeshire): I rise to speak to amendments Nos. 607 and 645, which were tabled in the names of myself and of other Opposition Members. Clearly, there is a general belief that the list contained in the clause is inadequate, and various ways of amending it have been proposed.

Amendment No. 645 would require bodies that appear

    to be representative of owners or occupiers of the land

to be included in the consultation. Such a requirement is a natural courtesy for the parties whose land is affected. Furthermore, it would be a travesty of justice if owners and occupiers, or their representatives, were not informed of what was happening and woke up one morning to find that a change had been made in relation to a footpath or a right of way across their land, even though they had not been consulted. The amendment is self-explanatory.

Amendment No. 607 seeks to achieve an aim that is generally in line with Government thinking, as it would ensure that the same phraseology is used in different pieces of legislation. It would include in the list of bodies to be consulted any organisations prescribed by the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981 to receive public path and definitive map orders.

Obviously, the 1981 Act contains a list of such organisations, although I would suggest one or two additions. The fundamental point is this: if that Act deemed it right for particular organisations to be consulted on public path and definitive map orders, precisely the same list should be used for consultation on rights of way improvement plans.

The idea for amendment No. 645 came from a group of organisations: the Byways and Bridleways Trust, the National Federation of Bridleway Associations, the British Horse Society, the Cyclists Touring Club and the South Pennine Packhorse Trails Trust. Those organisations worked together on the proposal, which drew the 1981 provision to my attention. Its inclusion in the clause is an eminently sensible idea, and I hope that the Under-Secretary will take this opportunity to provide consistency between two similar items of legislation.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Chris Mullin): I bring good news. I have no difficulty with the aims of amendment No. 644, which would ensure that the Countryside Agency and the Countryside Council for Wales were consulted during the preparation of all plans, rather than just on those which affect areas where there are national trails. I entirely agree with the sentiments behind all the other amendments. We expect local authorities to consult widely in the preparation of their plans and on the draft versions. However, the amendments collectively show the difficulty of prescribing every organisation that should be consulted and provide a wide range of possibilities. We believe that local authorities should have some discretion in deciding whom they should consult, but we accept that there may be a case for ensuring that certain key organisations, such as those which represent user groups and land managers, can give their views at an early stage in the plan preparation process. We consider it better to include a regulation-making power in the Bill than to list the organisations in the clause, as amendment No. 645 proposes, or to link them to bodies that are currently entitled to receive notice of orders, as amendment No. 607 apparently seeks to do.

We are, therefore, willing to consider amendments Nos. 595 and 596, which were tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Pendle (Mr. Prentice), as well as amendment No. 644. Clause 56

ENFORCEMENT OF DUTY TO PREVENT OBSTRUCTION

No. 643, in page 30, leave out lines 9 to 11 and insert

Mr. Mullin: Clause 56 introduces a new procedure, which will allow members of the public to enforce through the magistrates court a local highway authority's duty to prevent as many obstructions as possible. Currently, a complainant must go to the High Court. We hope that that will encourage local authorities to be more active in keeping rights of way free from obstruction.

Government amendment No. 646 is, in part, a restructuring exercise. It makes it clear that the complainant requests, rather than requires, the removal of the obstruction, as, under this provision, that is not a matter for the courts. It also makes it clear that a local highway authority will not be relieved of its duty to serve appropriate notices under new section 130A(5) if the complainant is wrong about any matters to which the notice relates. For example, new sections 130A and 130B will apply only to obstructions that are without lawful authority. A member of the public might not be able to establish for certain that an obstruction is unlawful, but we would not want that to cripple his whole application.

Amendments Nos. 585 and 586 are related. They would remove the requirement that notice may be served only in respect of obstructions to particular types of highway: footpaths, bridleways, restricted byways and ways shown as byways open to all traffic on a definitive map. They would also allow for an order to be made by a magistrates court in respect of any highway for which a local authority was responsible. That would considerably widen the operation of clause 56 and could lead to highway authorities being swamped with notices, but authorities must retain a reasonable degree of control over their priorities. Part II is principally, although not wholly, about rights of way, which we believe are the best focus for clause 56.

10.15 am

Amendment No. 643 would restrict the application of clause 56 to rights of way that have been recorded on definitive maps, so whereas the previous set of amendments sought to widen the powers in the clause, this amendment would narrow the new powers. We are constantly being told that there are parts of the country in which few rights of way have been recorded, and the amendment would provide an incentive for local authorities not to record rights of way. Only once a right of way were recorded could a member of the public take action under these provisions to enforce compliance with a highway authority's duty to prevent obstructions. That would undermine the provision's objective, which is to encourage local highway authorities to take effective action to tackle obstructions to rights of way, whether or not they are recorded on definitive maps.

I agree that we do not want people to bypass the definitive maps procedure by applying to the courts under clause 56 to resolve disputes about the existence of a highway. However, the Bill includes provisions to reduce such opportunities, and those provisions relate to the situation in which the existence of a highway is seriously disputed. For the reasons that I have given, I invite hon. Members to withdraw amendments Nos. 585, 586 and 643.

Mr. Damian Green (Ashford): The Under-Secretary's description of amendment No. 643 is slightly unfair. He said that the clause provides important new powers to tackle obstructions on rights of way but, as I am sure he is aware, there is a risk that the clause could cause contention and undermine attempts to secure a complete and accurate definitive map, which must be the goal that we all seek.

As it is currently drafted, the clause provides that the new powers can be exercised in relation to any public footpath or bridleway, whether or not it is shown on the definitive map. As the Under-Secretary admitted, that means that someone could try to secure the removal of an obstruction from a path, the status of which is disputed, without being required to clarify its status by seeking a modification order. In other words, the clause could provide a means by which to assert rights of way without going through the proper procedures. If an authority were to act on an application relating to a right of way that was not shown on a definitive map, it would be accepting that the path sustained public rights, a fact that might be disputed by the owner or by other local people. If, subsequently, the authority did not make a definitive map modification order to add the path to the definitive map, contention over the path's status would continue indefinitely.

Surely, good government should recognise the importance of clarifying the status of paths before taking action to remove obstructions on them. The amendment would resolve those problems by providing that the new power to remove obstructions would be exercisable only in relation to paths shown on definitive maps. If someone believed that such a path existed and that it was obstructed, their first action would be to seek a modification order to add it to the definitive map. Once that had been done, action could be taken to remove the obstruction, with everyone involved knowing that the path had formal status as a public right of way. Apart from the practical preferability of the procedure, the amendment would ensure that the provisions in relation to footpaths, bridleways and restricted byways would be brought into line with those proposed for byways open to all traffic under new section 130A(2)(b). The new power would be applied consistently in relation to all public rights of way.

I take it from what the Under-Secretary has said that he is not minded to accept the amendment. However, I urge him to reflect on the fact that it would add the consistency lacking in parts of the Bill as currently drafted.

Mr. Mullin: I beg to move amendment No. 647, in page 30, leave out lines 38 to 41 and insert—

    `(b) so far as reasonably practicable, on every person who it appears to them may be for the time being responsible for the obstruction, a notice informing that person that a notice under subsection (1) above has been served in relation to it and stating what, if any, action the authority propose to take.

The amendment relates to new section 130A(5). Although it is technical, it serves to protect in the first instance the anonymity of the complainant. Amendment No. 647 would ensure that local highway authorities were not under an absolute duty to serve notice on the person responsible for any obstruction. The duty is tempered. It would require them to serve notice as far as is reasonably practicable as an authority might be unable to identify the person concerned. Finally, the amendment widens the duty on the local authority, requiring them to notify whoever appears in their view to be responsible for the obstruction. The present wording of the clause covers only those who may be required to remove the obstruction and therefore may not cover all those responsible for a lawful obstruction.

I hope the hon. Members will agree that this is a sensible amendment and will accept it.
