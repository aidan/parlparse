The Minister for Transport in London (Ms Glenda Jackson): What a pleasure it is to have you in our company for this afternoon's sitting, Mr O'Hara.

Just before we broke, I said to the hon. Members for Croydon, South (Mr. Ottaway) and for Sutton and Cheam (Mr. Burstow)--who, I regret, is not in his place--that what I had to say on amendment No. 1102 would make pleasant hearing for them. The hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam referred to the necessity of having a flow chart with regard to directions and gaining assent to planning applications. We will be happy to include such a flow chart in our forthcoming guidance.

Amendment No. 1102 would require the mayor to have regard to the provisions of a borough's development plan and to the spatial development strategy when exercising any power that the Secretary of State may give him to direct refusal of a planning application.

I am certainly in accord with the thinking behind the amendment as it refers to the SDS. The main purpose of giving the mayor the ability to have an input into strategically important development control cases is precisely that he can defend the policies and objectives set out in the SDS and present the pan-London view. As a matter of general administrative law, however, the mayor must have regard to the mayoral strategy when exercising the powers that the Secretary of State confers on him. We shall make that clear in any guidance that is issued. It is, therefore, not strictly necessary to specify it in the Bill.

The same applies to the borough's development plan, which, as a matter of administrative law, is likely to be a material consideration for the mayor. Provided that a borough's UDP is up to date, it should reflect and be in conformity with the mayoral spatial development strategy. That said, the spirit of the amendment appears to be consistent with our intentions and with the operation of the plan-led system. The appropriate place to convey the points that I have mentioned may be in the secondary legislation that will define the mayor's powers, rather than in the Bill. I would, therefore, like to take the amendment away and consider it further. With that commitment, I trust that the hon. Member for Croydon, South will withdraw it.

I want now to deal with the points that the hon. Gentleman raised when he spoke to the other amendments that were tabled in his name. Amendments Nos. 1099 and 1110 would give sole responsibility for ensuring that the objectives of the SDS are achieved through individual development control decisions to the local planning authority. They would not, however, secure the right balance between strategic and local interests. Even with a plan-led system, the success or failure of strategic policies is often ultimately determined through individual development control decisions. For that reason, it will be important for the mayor to be consulted on the limited number of applications that raise issues of genuine strategic importance and, in extremis, to have the fallback power to direct refusal if important strategic concerns cannot be resolved. The second amendment, No. 1110, would eliminate the mayor's fall-back power to direct refusal and is, I regret to tell the hon. Member for Croydon, South, unacceptable. The first amendment, No. 1099, is unnecessary. As a matter of administrative law, the SDS will be a material consideration in the determination of planning applications in Greater London. There is therefore no need for the Bill to make express reference to that. Section 70 of the Town and Country Planning Act 1990 requires the local planning authority to have regard to material considerations when determining applications. The Act does not specify what the material considerations are. There is obviously an enormous number of them, and to begin setting them out in the Act would lead us into a never-ending task. However, existing case law provides that policy statements made by the Secretary of State are a material consideration. There can be no doubt that the statutory SDS will also be a material consideration for the purposes of section 70.

Amendment No. 1101 is also unnecessary. The Government have stated repeatedly that the mayor will be involved only in applications of strategic importance. Simply stating that in the Bill, would surely create a fertile area for confusion and conflict. That is why we propose to set down clear and unambiguous criteria for such cases in secondary legislation. On 15 March, the Government's detailed conclusions on the types of applications that will be sent to the mayor were announced. Applications will be sent to the mayor for comment and as a last resort he may direct refusal. The Government's proposals for ensuring that the mayor sees only applications of genuine strategic importance are clear, so the amendment is unnecessary.

Amendment No. 1103 seems to be an attempt to create a mayoral exclusion zone in the heart of London. Currently, only one building in London, commonly known as the NatWest tower, is higher than the proposed 150m threshold. St. Paul's cathedral is a mere 111m high. I find it hard to accept the Opposition's suggestion that developments of a similar height to the Barbican towers, which are 123m high, or the stock exchange, which is 100m high, both of which are clearly visible from many parts of London, are not of strategic importance. We find unacceptable the Opposition's belief that no matter how large a proposed development, it and the attendant employment or transport implications should be ignored and will not be of wider significance unless the proposed height exceeds a specified maximum.

The Government's detailed proposals for defining applications of strategic importance are somewhat more carefully considered. It is important that the mayor has an opportunity to consider large-scale proposals throughout London, but in recognition of the greater scale of development and capacity for change, separate thresholds for triggering consultation with the mayor are proposed for the City of London, whose thresholds will be higher than those of surrounding areas.

The threshold for consultation on high buildings in the City is 75m, compared with 30m elsewhere in London, except for sites fronting the River Thames. Similarly, the threshold for consultation on large-scale buildings is higher in the City, where it is set at 30,000 sq m, than in the rest of central London, where it is set at 20,000 sq m, or elsewhere, where it falls to 15,000 sq m. We believe that our proposals strike a fair and sensible balance between allowing the mayor to have a say in key planning applications and recognising that the vast majority of applications are properly a matter solely for local planning authorities.

Amendment No. 1104 is another attempt to restrict the type of applications on which the mayor could direct refusal, in this case by preventing consultation of the mayor unless the proposal in question does not accord with the development plan. It would therefore limit consultation to so-called departure applications.

While we accept that proposals that do not accord with the plan may be of particular interest to the mayor, provided that they are of sufficient scale and importance we do not believe that the scope of the mayor's consideration of applications of strategic importance should be restricted only to such proposals, even under a plan-led system. Just because a proposal is in accord with the development plan, it does not automatically mean that it does not require detailed consideration either by the local planning authority or by other appropriate bodies. There will always be plans that are insufficiently up to date or which are simply silent on the types of development proposed.

As we make clear in our detailed proposals for defining applications of strategic importance, applications above a certain scale can potentially have an impact over a much wider area and should be considered by the mayor. Similarly, the mayor should have an opportunity to consider the strategic implications of proposals on defined key strategic sites or involving an important strategic policy. Amendments Nos. 1103 and 1104 would harm the mayor's effectiveness in achieving his or her strategic objectives.

On amendment No. 1229, it would not be appropriate to give a power to direct the granting of planning permission to the mayor. Such a draconian power would be inconsistent with the national planning system--not even the Secretary of State has such a power--and would create the potential for the mayor to ride roughshod over a local planning authority with no right of appeal against such a decision.

I assure the hon. Member for Croydon, South that the mayor will have much opportunity for positive action. In particular, the mayor's spatial development strategy is an excellent platform from which the mayor can encourage and channel London's strategic development. In addition, despite fears to the contrary, we expect that the mayor will want actively to support many of the applications of which he is notified by the boroughs, particularly where they will contribute to the future well-being and prosperity of London and Londoners. I would therefore ask the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.
