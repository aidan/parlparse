Clause 1

General purpose and definitions

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 14, in page 1, line 25, at end insert—

    `, except where the buildings or structures have the function of controlling pollution.'.—[Mr. Green.]

Mr. Graham Allen (Nottingham, North): On a point of order, Mr. Hood. Would it be in order for those members of the Committee who have not pre-empted your decision, to remove their jackets?

The Chairman: I understand that the Committee had a kind and considerate Chairman this morning and I shall continue in that vein. Members of the Committee may remove their jackets.

Mr. Damian Green (Ashford): As a Committee member who pre-empted your decision, Mr. Hood, I am doubly grateful for that ruling. I welcome you to the Chair, particularly in the light of your decision to carry on that liberal, environmentally-friendly policy, which will help to prevent us from polluting the air.

Before lunch, I said that I hoped that the Government would accept the amendment, which is in line with the National Farmers Union's wish to relieve certain farmers of an unnecessary burden. The amendment would change the Bill's definition of environmental pollution. Currently, the clause states that

    ```air' includes air within buildings and air within other natural or man-made structures above or below ground.''

One can see why that definition has been included. It will avoid legal doubt about whether air inside buildings falls within the scope of the definition of pollution. For many purposes, that is sensible. However, were it applied to certain agricultural buildings, it might prove inadvertently damaging.

Many livestock farmers use metal tanks to store slurry before spreading it on the land. Through the natural course of events, slurry's ammonia levels are relatively high. Slurry tanks contain the air pollution—

The Chairman: Order. Members of the public should not take papers from the Table.

Mr. Green: It is far better for the ammonia to be contained in the slurry tanks, so that it does not pollute the air—in the popular sense of the term—with unpleasant and possibly dangerous smells. The Environment Agency has taken an increasing interest in such structures, which, as the Minister will know, may be required to be roofed to contain foul air. Clearly, it would be perverse to regard air that is contained by such buildings as pollution. Such air will be regarded by the Environment Agency as pollution where it is not contained, but, unless the amendment is accepted, it will be regarded as pollution even where it is contained.

There are other, similar cases. A power station that is fuelled by chicken manure stores it in a building that is deliberately kept under negative atmospheric pressure. Again, the building itself controls the pollution. Clearly, therefore, it would be wrong for that air to be described as environmental pollution. Mushroom composting facilities are another agricultural example—perhaps less vivid than the chicken manure power station—of buildings potentially being employed as pollution control measures.

The amendment would ensure that air in buildings or structures that are themselves part of a system for controlling pollution are not regarded as falling within the definition of environmental pollution. Obviously, if the air escaped from the building, a problem would arise and environmental pollution would occur. We are seeking not to redefine air, but merely to point out that if it is kept properly inside a building it should not be described as pollution. I hope that the Minister will accept the amendment.

Mr. Tom Brake (Carshalton and Wallington): Again I rise in support of an amendment that has been tabled by the official Opposition. It would be entirely counterproductive if a building designed to reduce and control pollution were to be subject to regulation that precluded the building from serving its proper function. If the Minister can satisfy me that that will not arise, I shall of course gracefully withdraw. If he cannot, I should like to return to the matter at some point in the future.

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): I understand the motives behind the amendment and I hope that I shall be able to satisfy hon. Members who have spoken in favour of it. The matter has apparently bothered the National Farmers Union, which is worried about a possible impact on the way in which certain agricultural processes are regulated—especially, as the hon. Member for Ashford (Mr. Green) said, in respect of mushroom compost manufacturers. All of us in the Room are experts on that subject. Fears have been expressed that unnecessary costs may be imposed on the processes by which compost is produced for use in mushroom growing.

I am sure that no member of the Committee would doubt that it is right that pollution and noxious smells that escape from a regulated installation and affect the environment outside must be controlled. Sometimes, that control will need to take account of the quality of the air inside a building, although I recognise that it may not always be possible—for whatever reason—to prevent emissions escaping under a doorway or through a window or vent. That is the basis on which the current regulatory regimes—which were, quite correctly, established by the previous Government—operate. The Environmental Protection Act 1990 stipulates that

    ``the medium of air includes the air within buildings and the air within other man-made structures above or below ground''.

The Bill merely rolls forward that definition: we are not seeking to make any new demarche.

I assure the hon. Members who tabled the amendment that it is not the role of the current or new pollution control regimes to regulate the air within buildings if it does not pose problems to the external environment. Where an operator has built a structure to contain pollution and it is performing satisfactorily, there will be no question of regulators imposing additional requirements just because the air inside the building is poor. That is not the case now and it will not be the case. There are, of course, health and safety regulations designed to protect people working in such conditions, but that is a separate issue.

Mr. Green: I am grateful for the Minister's assurance. As he said, that is the existing situation and regulators have not intervened in it. He spoke as though he could give some new reassurances. Does anything other than custom and practice lead him to say that regulators will not conceivably intervene in future?

Mr. Meacher: No, I am not offering any new protection. I am simply saying that the Bill is rolling forward a provision that has operated perfectly satisfactorily for the past decade. We do not propose to change it and there is no reason why it should not continue to operate in exactly the same way in the future. There is no reason why operators should fear that changes will occur that affect that.

Mr. David Drew (Stroud): Will my right hon. Friend comment on a particular problem that affects my constituency? If the amendment were passed and if some equipment—I am thinking particularly of a filtration plant—were faulty, am I right in supposing that it would be exempt from action being taken against it and that, even though the biological process may be what is wanted, because the equipment was faulty no action could be taken against that company or plant? That would be deleterious to achieving the right process.

Mr. Meacher: I think that the answer is that if it did not impact on the external environment, no action would be taken under these provisions. If, however, the equipment was working unsatisfactorily—in other words, if the building that had been constructed to contain the pollution was not working satisfactorily—I would expect the Environment Agency to intervene. However, I do not believe that there would be any question of taking action against the owner of the installation, so long as there were no noxious or negative effects on the local environment.

I hope that the hon Member for Ashford is reassured by my reply. I genuinely do not believe that there is a problem. We are speaking to each other for the reassurance of the National Farmers Union. There is no reason why matters should change. I am not aware of any cases in the past year wherein this has proved to be a problem and I therefore hope that the hon. Gentleman will withdraw his amendment.

Mr. Green: I am grateful to the Minister for his response. As he said, it is a question of reassuring those at the sharp end, who have genuine concerns. I am, however, happy to accept his assurances, with the obvious caveat that we will continue carefully to monitor the situation. I dare say that the fact of the amendment and the debate on it will bring the issue to the attention of those outside this place who are most concerned and we will keep in close contact with them. If necessary, we will return to the issue at a later stage but for now I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Green: The amendments would fulfil a twofold purpose. First, they would make the Bill more practical and prevent it from being so far stretched that its provisions became ludicrous or fell into disrepute. Secondly, they would ensure proper scrutiny.

Amendment No. 9 would add the word ``significant'' to the line about damage to property being one of the events that can cause regulatory intervention. We had a full debate on the use of the word ``significant'' in another context—[Hon. Members: ``A significant debate.'']—a fully significant debate. Similar considerations apply to the amendment and we do not want to go over old ground.

Significant measures come up under the group of amendments. I would like the Minister to address the question of the areas in which the directive, and the measures in the Bill that flow from it, go beyond the existing controls rolled up, broadly speaking, in the 1990 Act. There is much concern among many of the groups and industries that will be most affected by the Bill and it will be convenient for the Committee if we discuss issues that arise now.

The Minister will be aware that about 2,600 more installations will be regulated under these provisions than were regulated under the 1990 Act. Some that were regulated under the previous measures will fall out of the scope of provisions—there are clearly complications in both ways. Large intensive pig and poultry installations and those for the manufacture of food and drink products are the unregulated installations covered by the directive. One point especially relevant to the group of amendments that the Minister has already made is that part of the purpose of the Bill is to tidy up. Different regimes for the regulation of different types of pollution are in force; one of the benefits of the proposed changes will be that the situation will be tidied up.

Who, in principle, could argue with that? It is more sensible that people have certainty and thereby are enabled to run their businesses better. We have already discussed deregulation, so let us not go over that ground, but certainty in regulation is a virtue in terms of letting businesses know what is coming.

My fear, which I genuinely hope that the Minister can put to rest, is that one effect of the Bill, which would be prevented by the amendments, is the regulatory equivalent of mission creep—regulatory creep. Regulations can be established and extended without, necessarily, any expression by Parliament that that should happen. Several new installations will be covered and a wide range of existing measures have been brought together—regulations for the integrated pollution control regime, for the local air pollution control regime, for control of water pollution and of waste sites. That step comes on top of the addition of new regulations concerning pig and poultry installations and food and drink plants.

One does not have to be unduly cynical to think that, with such a wide range of new regulatory powers being brought together, there is at least a possibility of creeping extra regulation. I hope that the Minister and I can agree that that would be bad for business and also not good for the environment. As we have discussed, we all want decent and practical environmental controls, but allowing them to spread so that they become illegitimate is likely to encourage bad behaviour by some on the fringes of some industries. That would have a damaging effect on the environment and on respect for the law.

Amendment No. 10 would insert an appropriate level of parliamentary scrutiny into the procedures. After this morning's discussions, I am not sure that the Minister and I are destined to agree on what such a level should be, but the amendment is consistent with others that we have tabled. At as many stages possible, we wish democratic rather than bureaucratic control over the regulations to be exercised. I shamelessly steal the elegant phrase used this morning by my hon. Friend the Member for Altrincham and Sale, West (Mr. Brady), who crystallised the issue well. That is part of ensuring that the regulations are legitimate so that people accept and enforce them willingly, rather than feeling that they are being imposed from above. There should be at least some democratic control over the regulations.

The amendments have the twin aim of increasing the practicality of the regulations and the level of parliamentary scrutiny. On that basis, I commend them to the Committee.

 

Mr. Meacher: As the hon. Gentleman said, we had a considerable debate this morning. I am sorry that you were unable to participate, Mr. Hood, although I am sure that all members of the Committee will tell you that it was comprehensive and exhaustive, if not exhausting. I made it clear in that debate that the Government have no wish to place the burden of regulation on businesses whose pollution is insignificant. Again, however, Parliament should not be required to decide matters case by case in advance.

We in Parliament are not well placed to make regulations about what is significantly offensive. Despite the slightly casual remark by the hon. Member for Ashford at the outset of today's proceedings, it is not appropriate for the House to make such a decision. How does one encapsulate in a regulation the awful stench that arises from a maggot farm? I am sorry to mention hikers in the presence of the hon. Member for Billericay (Mrs. Gorman)—I am sure that they will find Billericay a wonderful place to walk over—but how does one capture the offence that is caused to hikers or local people in the Peak district when they find that a stream has turned bright orange because of effluent from a dye works? The fact is that we cannot do so. The regulator must consider such issues case by case, following consultation with local people.

The hon. Member for Ashford asked whether we ran the risk of regulation creep. As I understand that interesting phrase, it means that if we extend the existing parameters of regulations and set new ones, the regulations will somehow grow beyond them. They will not; they will be subject to consultation with local interests. If the Environment Agency sought to extend its regulatory powers beyond what is prescribed—I do not believe for a moment that it would—there is absolutely no question that local interests, including industry and small businesses in particular, would tell it where to get off.

Ms Joan Walley (Stoke-on-Trent, North): I want to draw my right hon. Friend's attention to an issue in my constituency, to which I referred in passing on Second Reading. It relates to the use of liquid condensate from a rendering plant as fertiliser on grazing land. We are talking about definitions. Does my right hon. Friend agree that the new powers of prevention will mean that smells that arise from that offensive practice can be dealt with in a wholly integrated way? If so, we will be able to consider not only the installation that produces the liquid condensate, but the smells, nuisance and harm that are caused in the surrounding areas. Is that not also an example of the issues that the amendment raises, with which we now need to deal in an integrated way?

Mr. Meacher: That is a good example of the type of environmental pollution that the Bill covers. There is no doubt that immense discontent and aggravation can be experienced in residences close to some rendering plants. Some rendering plants are well organised, while others cause terrible odours that are detectable from a great distance. It is right that such plants should be regulated, not only in terms of the processes going on inside the building, but of their impact on the neighbourhood.

The question is whether Parliament should decide on the degree of offensiveness and whether that should be a matter for regulation. The current systems work via the regulator, who is subject to principles laid down by this House. He takes account of the circumstances of the installation concerned and consults widely with all the local interests, which are listed in clause 2(4). That is how the current systems work and that is how the new one will work. The definition comes from the Environmental Protection Act 1990 and it has worked well in practice.

I am not suggesting that the regulator makes arbitrary decisions. He is an experienced professional, whose decisons are informed by comprehensive guidance about what is the best available technique. He also takes account of local consultation. There is, and will always be, a right of appeal against the regulator's decision. There are many instances of such appeals being made, and in some cases succeeding.

I feared that the point might be raised that regulators would try to ban the normal smells that characterise the countryside. They hav not done so and there is no intention that they should do so.

To return to the point made by the Opposition, whom I am trying to meet half way, Parliament can, however, take a view on which processes are capable of causing significant enough offence to human senses to warrant regulation. That is what we are doing in the Bill, which will continue the practice under the 1990 Act whereby the list of industries or companies regulated can be added to or subtracted from by regulation subject to the negative procedure. However, it is then for the regulator to decide, on a case-by-case basis, whether pollution is significantly offensive and warrants palliative measures. It would not be sensible to establish first, which those installations were, and then separately—additionally—how they were to be regulated in a further order, as the amendment seems to require.

I repeat the fears that I expressed in our first lengthy debate, that the amendment—with which I have some sympathy—would create a wholly unnecessary additional parliamentary stage before regulations dealing with the control of pollution could reach the statute book. it would place a restriction on the flexibility of the system—although I am sure that that was not the intention behind it—because Parliament would simply not be able to carry out those individual local consultations. The only way in which the system can operate properly is by having those fine and nice judgments made in the local neighbourhood.

The amendment would represent a retrograde step in the control of pollution. The system works and I would be the first to complain if it did not. As they say, ``If it ain't broke, don't fix it.'' I hope that, for those reasons, the hon. Member for Ashford will withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Green: There is a definite point of agreement between the Minister and me, in that we now know where we disagree. He wants to put powers into the hands of the regulators, and we want to put powers into the hands of Parliament. No doubt the debate will continue. Scrutiny and practicality are important points of principle and I urge the Committee to support the amendment.

Mr. Graham Brady: (Altrincham and Sale, West): I do not wish to put myself in bad odour with the Committee by detaining it for long. I had not intended to speak on clause stand part, but a significant point has arisen on which the Minister may be able to put my mind at rest quickly.

Mr. Meacher: That is certainly an interesting proposition, and one that had not previously occurred to me, but having taken advice on the matter, the answer is, rather surprisingly, that theoretically such practices could be regulated, although I think that, for reasons that I am about to mention, it would be an extremely perverse and obscure way of regulating genetic modification.

The primary purpose of the Bill is to regulate the effects of large installations, as we have regularly stated, but the Government have tabled an amendment to include controlling activities, as well as installations and plant, and I suppose that, technically, the effects of pollination from GM crops could be regulated on that basis. Clause 2 gives the Secretary of State an enormous raft of powers; there is considerable scope for improvement, which the amendments would provide.

The Government should make it clear how much power they will take for themselves, not only in the initial stages of the application of the transposed directive, but in future. We assume that the new regime is likely to last longer than the previous one. We have moved from national regulation to European regulation, and, if I may say so in the presence of my hon. Friend the Member for Billericay, we must assume that regulation on a European scale will continue.

Mrs. Teresa Gorman (Billericay): It is a pity that you have not been here all day, Mr. Hood, as it is difficult to resist the temptation to lampoon the Bill and all its ridiculous implications. In this country, under common law, cannot people with such grievances—most of which are busybody matters—take them to the local courts for a magistrate to resolve? Notwithstanding his toleration of European regulations, does my hon. Friend agree that most of the matters being debated today could be resolved under our intrinsic, good old English legislation and law, without leave to introduce legislation that ties everybody up in knots and allows people to get busybody inspectors to do their dirty work?

Mr. Green: I agree with my hon. Friend to the extent that most cases are clearly local and, although they need to be decided within a wider framework of law, they could be decided locally. One of the aims of many of the amendments has been to avoid including trivialities in regulation. Much of the large-scale pollution with which the Bill deals is inevitably cross-border. My hon. Friend will know as well as I do about the figures that are quoted on the amount of pollution that arrives on our coastline from other countries, and the amount of acid rain that lands on other countries as a result of coal being burned here.

Of all the issues on which there is clearly a European locus, environmental protection has always seemed to me to be the most obvious; it is entirely reasonable that there should be Europe-wide legislation in this sphere. I agree with my hon. Friend that that makes it all the more important that there should be a proper degree of scrutiny by the British Parliament of the regulations and laws that will apply to British people. That is not a theoretical point—it is very practical. One of the most important aspects of regulation is that it must be legitimate. If people feel that their lives are being changed, or their businesses damaged, by an arbitrary power over which they have no proper redress, they will begin to resent it and then try to evade it. That is particularly unhelpful in respect of environmental protection.

The amendments would ensure that future parliamentary scrutiny is proper and powerful. There is some instructive history in that respect in the Bill's passage through another place. I concede that the Bill has progressed: the Government started off with a bad set of proposals, which got better every time their lordships got their teeth out and bit it. However, the process has not yet gone far enough, which is why we are trying to make further improvements in Committee.

For example, the amendments would extend to all the regulations the privilege that the Government have deigned to allow to the first regulations that will be debated under the clause. I am waiting to hear what the Minister has to say about the rationale of allowing one regime of scrutiny for the first regulations and another for future regulations—beyond the fact, of course, that it is administratively convenient. We have already heard too much today from the Government about the impracticality of Opposition proposals, but no admission that the basis of their desire to retain the Bill's existing wording is simple administrative convenience. For reasons that I have already rehearsed, that is democratically unacceptable. I hope that the Minister will devote some time to explaining why it is proper to have one type of scrutiny for the first set of regulations and weaker scrutiny thereafter.

During the Bill's passage in another place, protests were made by agricultural and industrial bodies, as well as their lordships, and a degree of consultation has consequently been introduced. The Secretary of State will be required to consult before regulations are made. In answer to the question, ``Is that enough?'', I would say that it is not. Although such consultation is a necessary condition for ensuring appropriate and sensible regulation, it is not a sufficient on its own. Parliament is able to bring to bear a wider perspective, and that must happen. Otherwise, one may well ask what we are doing here. To some extent, the Minister sold the pass this morning, when he responded to a previous group of amendments by saying that small businesses that were worried about the matter could talk to their Members of Parliament about it. The problem is that if their Members of Parliament have no input into the regulatory procedure, those businesses are wasting their breath—breathing out in a polluting way, thus breaking the terms of the Bill. Under the Bill's provisions, there will be no point in hon. Members making representations. However, certain corporate bodies will be allowed to represent themselves to Ministers, which in itself creates an imbalance.

It is clear that the big boys—those who are involved in large industries or groups of large industries—who have professional trade associations and are tuned in to the business of government will do better out of this than small businesses; they know how to play the Whitehall game, when to intervene and what language to use. One knows of instances of big companies in an industry using environmental regulations to try to damage small companies in the same industry. It is always easier for big companies to adapt. If the regulations can be written in a way that suits their purposes, then for perfectly understandable commercial reasons, that is what tends to happen. It is often the small businesses—which may be collected together in federations but which tend by definition not to have spare staff for lobbying—that look to their MPs, as well as to their federations, to lobby on their behalf. Part of our purpose in seeking to introduce the various safeguards in this group of amendments is to allow Members of Parliament to fulfill their proper function of representing the interests of their business constituents.

I urge the Minister to take these arguments seriously.
