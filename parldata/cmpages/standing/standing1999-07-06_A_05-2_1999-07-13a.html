Ms Hewitt: We are looking at the market confidence objective, which is fundamental to regulation. In addition to generating employment and work, the financial services sector has a key economic role in channelling savings and investment into finance for our industry. For that to happen efficiently and competitively, there must be confidence in the market. Consumers who entrust their savings in the market, industries that raise capital, and businesses that enter into and invest in the UK financial services sector, must have confidence in the market.

It is not simply a question of confidence in markets and exchanges but in the financial system as a whole, including regulated activities that take place inside and outside markets and exchanges. Amendment No. 41 would alter the definition of the financial system, so that the market confidence objective would no longer be to maintain confidence in regulated activities but in authorised persons in so far as they engage in regulated activities. If I understood the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs correctly, the Opposition are suggesting that the FSA should maintain confidence in individual banks rather than banking, and in individual insurers rather than underwriting. If that is not their intention, I have to say that that would be the effect of their amendment.

The hon. Gentleman also seemed to suggest that, under clause 3, the market confidence objective will fail to cover the entire banking sector. That is not the case. As described in clause 3(1), the market confidence objective is designed to

    ``maintain confidence in the financial system.''

In subsection 2, the financial system is defined simply as

    ``the financial system operating in the United Kingdom''.

It includes, but is not confined to, regulated activities. Indeed, although that definition is not confined to paragraphs (a), (b) and (c), paragraph (c) specifically refers to

    ``other activities connected with financial markets and exchanges.''

Therefore, the entire banking sector is covered, and is meant to be covered, by the market confidence objective.

Sir Nicholas Lyell: Just for clarification, is the banking sector swept up only in subsection (2)(c)?

Ms Hewitt: No, it is swept up, as it were, by subsection (2)(c) and by the fact that the definition of the financial system includes paragraphs (a), (b) and (c). Of course, that does not constitute an exclusive definition. It simply means that, for the avoidance of doubt, the financial system will include those specific paragraphs.

Sir Nicholas Lyell: I am listening carefully and, if the Economic Secretary will allow me, I shall intervene when she has had a further chance to respond.

Ms Hewitt: The amendment would appear to have little positive effect, and suffers from three defects in respect of its likely impact on the market confidence objective. First, it could be interpreted as setting an objective for the FSA to maintain confidence in all individual firms: in other words, in the authorised persons who are engaged in regulated activities. That is not what we are seeking to achieve, and I doubt whether Opposition Members want to achieve that. The objective of the regulation is to maintain confidence not in particular players, but in the financial system generally. FSA regulatory action may involve some loss of confidence in a specific firm expressly to maintain confidence in the market as a whole.

The second difficulty is that regulated activities under the Bill are not carried on only by authorised persons. They may also be carried on by persons who are exempt from the requirement for authorisation or by appointed representatives of authorised persons who are not themselves authorised. There is a risk that the amendment would narrow significantly the definition of the financial system and that the FSA's objective might be attributed as being only to maintain confidence in a subsection of the financial system. I doubt that that is the intention but I fear that it might be the effect.

The third problem is that regulated activities may be carried on improperly by people who are neither authorised nor exempt. The FSA is certainly not in the business of maintaining confidence in regulated activities carried on by persons who are neither authorised nor exempt. Part of the FSA's responsibility for maintaining confidence in the financial system as a whole requires it to ensure that unauthorised persons who are not subject to an exemption do not carry on regulated activity. Policing the general prohibition is an important part of achieving that objective. The amendment would cast doubt on that difficulty.

On the next substantial issue that was raised, the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham asked about the relationships in the memorandum of understanding between the Treasury, the Bank and the FSA in relation to the maintenance of overall confidence in the system and the avoidance of systemic risk.

The memorandum is, of course, a public document. It establishes a framework for co-operation between the three institutions. It is based on and spells out four guiding principles: clear accountability, based on clearly defined responsibility; transparency, with responsibility made public for all to see; no duplication of the roles of the three institutions; and the regular exchange of information.

The importance of the memorandum of understanding lies in the creation of the relationship between the three organisations--of which the FSA is only one--and in the way in which it must operate. The relationship is not new. Even before the transfer of responsibility for banking supervision from the Bank of England to the FSA under the Bank of England Act 1998, proper oversight of the stability of the financial system required close co-operation between the Treasury and financial services regulators. The fact that so many of those regulators are now to be joined in a single authority improves and simplifies the mechanics of that co-operation at a stroke.

The memorandum of understanding established new transparency, although not in response to a new need. The regular exchange of information, some parity of responsibilities and close co-operation between the institutions involved have always been fundamental aspects of ensuring the stability of our financial system. It was never the case, as the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham perhaps implied, that the Bank could discharge the relevant responsibilities on its own.

Ms Hewitt: I shall explain the respective responsibilities of the three institutions. The simple answer to the hon. Gentleman's question is that, in the event of a threat to the financial system that required the three institutions to work together, the line of accountability would, as always, be to Parliament through Ministers. Treasury Ministers would be answerable for the way in which the system and the set of relationships that the memorandum of understanding created dealt with that systemic risk.

The three institutions have different responsibilities and different expertise. The Bank is charged with oversight of the system and its infrastructure. That includes the payments system, which is a crucial part of the system as a whole. The Financial Services Authority supervises banks, security houses, insurances companies, investment markets and the clearing houses. It therefore has a close understanding of the way in which firms are exposed to each other and how excessive exposure to risk in one might cause a chain reaction in other elements of the system. The Treasury has overall responsibility for the regulatory structure and the stability of the economy. However, it is also the ultimate source of any funds that might be needed to launch the lifeboat in the rescue operation to which the hon. Gentleman referred.

When they read the memorandum, hon. Members will note that it makes it clear that it is for the Chancellor of the Exchequer to consider whether public funds should be committed to any support action. That arrangement is not new, and it is as it should be. It means that Ministers would be accountable to the House for an important decision either to commit public funds to rescue an institution and maintain confidence in the whole system, or not to commit public funds if it were felt that that was the right course of action.

The memorandum sets out in some detail the way in which the standing committee operates. As the hon. Member for East Worthing and Shoreham acknowledged, there are good reasons why the minutes of the meetings between the three institutions are not made public. He sought to draw a distinction between discussions on the position of individual institutions and discussions on matters of more general importance to financial stability. I am not sure whether that distinction is viable. It is in the nature of financial stability that one should be wary of making public without careful thought whatever matter of concern has arisen, lest that triggers the crisis of confidence about which one is worried. I am sure that practitioners in the financial community would readily concur with that.

Mr. Loughton: If whoever monitors the banking system decides, for whatever reason, to bring in more banks with lower capital adequacy requirements, do they not have a duty to the underlying consumer to say, ``The money that you placed with those banks is slightly less safe than it was, because we have, in effect, diluted the security of the banks now handling your money.''? How would that duty be weighed against the fact that they do not want to cause systemic risk? Which argument is relevant: the transparency argument or the propping up the system argument?

Ms Hewitt: We are going far wide of the amendment. The answer to that question lies in the fact that the Financial Services Authority has to balance the objective of consumer protection, which will clearly have a bearing on capital adequacy ratios for banks, with the principle of promoting competition. It also has to take into account the objective of maintaining confidence in the financial system. The authority will have to balance the relevant objectives and principles against each other and weigh them up as it decides a particular case. For example, the hon. Gentleman suggested the case of a foreign bank seeking to enter this country. The authority will have to decide whether lower capital adequacy ratios would be justified in the interests of competition an of giving consumers more choice, even though that might involve a downside in terms of the protection of consumers who put money into any such institutions and thereby have implications for the market confidence objective. It is a balancing act. If the Treasury or the Bank of England were worried about the FSA's attitude to capital adequacy requirements for new entrants, and felt that an issue of systemic risk could be involved, I have no doubt that the matter would be discussed between them under the memorandum of understanding.

I was asked about the mechanics of how the Barings crisis would be dealt with under the memorandum of understanding. We all hope that such a case will not occur again, but if it did it would be extremely important that any decision was taken on the basis of a full understanding of the facts. In the case of Barings, the Bank of England was not the only regulator involved in supervising the institution. Indeed, the overseas subsidiary that was racking up spectacular losses was a futures trader rather than a bank. That illustrates the fact that one cannot draw hard and fast distinctions between different sectors of the modern financial services industry. The decision would be taken in the light of the circumstances, using all the relevant information that was available to all the institutions involved.

What they want is confidence not in products--I think that they understand that the Government cannot guarantee that--but in what might be called the micro-economy. Put another way, they would like micro-confidence in the economy. For example, when they receive a share certificate after buying a share, they want to be confident that it represents part of the ownership of a company and that it gives them rights to vote at the end of a general meeting, to receive a dividend and to receive party assets of the company in the case of a wind-up. They also hope that it limits their liability. That is extremely important: before the great company law reforms of the last century, people were very unwilling to invest because it meant unlimited liability. As everyone probably knows, Sir Walter Scott wrote all those wonderful books to discharge the debts that he incurred by being a partner or investor in a publishing company that went bust. It was nothing to do with him, it was not his fault, but he was jointly liable for all those debts. The great step forward was when, under one of the Gladstone Administrations, company law was reformed so that people could not lose more money than they had put into a company in the first place.

People want to be confident that that remains the case when they buy a share and receive a share certificate. They want to know that when they buy a pension, someone will pay them the money or buy the annuity for them when they retire. If it is entirely unrestricted, the authority must spread its wings, or it might be assumed to take on objectives and responsibilities that go well beyond what we have always assumed are connected with market confidence in the more traditional sense.
