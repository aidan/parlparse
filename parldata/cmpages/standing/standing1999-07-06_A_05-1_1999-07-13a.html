Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: We now come to the important issue of statutory immunity, on which we have touched in previous debates. I am glad to have the opportunity to explore the issue further. In some ways, it goes to the core of the Bill and certainly to the status of the FSA.

Amendment No. 22 would limit the statutory immunity granted in the Bill and make the authority liable for acting unreasonably, recklessly or negligently. The Government presently want to grant the FSA a substantial degree of statutory immunity going well beyond that. Our starting-point is that it is objectionable in principle for any organisation to be above the law.

The monarch has always been considered to be above the law because it was the monarch made the law in earlier times and it was thought unsuitable for them to be subject to their own actions. Immunity of various sorts was practically automatic and went with the job. That concept has been eroded through time and the remains of Crown immunity have gone.

Only a few years ago, no matter how many cockroaches were found in NHS kitchens, no one could do anything about it because the hospital could claim Crown immunity. That has ended, rightly; I hope that the cockroaches have gone, too. It should appeal to parliamentarians because we operate under the important principle of equality under the law. For example, although the police enforce the law they are not above it; they are subject to it and can be sued.

The Government must make the case for statutory immunity for the FSA, and they have not yet done so. The Joint Committee examined the matter at length; several witnesses raised the subject and were asked about it. The Committee concluded that it was happy with the proposals for immunity

    "provided that the complaints procedure is strengthened as we recommend below."

We debated the complaints procedure this morning and the Government did not accept the Joint Committee's three recommendations. First, they did not agree that the appointment of the investigator should require the approval of the Lord Chancellor; secondly, they did not agree that the investigator should be able to launch investigations into complaints received directly, because the Bill as drafted provides that complaints should reach the investigator via the authority; and thirdly, the Joint Committee asked the Government to consider seriously whether the investigator should be able to award compensation. Admittedly, the Government will come back to us on that, we hope with some better news, but as things stand they have not accepted the complaints procedure recommendation. It therefore follows that the Joint Committee's agreement to the proposals for the FSA's immunity no longer applies. That is important.

The link between statutory immunity and the complaints procedure was raised constantly by witnesses. There was a good deal of grumbling about statutory immunity, but they could see the force in a limited degree of immunity, provided, they emphasised, that the complaints procedure was strengthened, made fully independent and able to award financial compensation. That point was made before the Joint Committee and has been made in writing, and privately, to me and, probably, to other members of the Committee. In the light of the inadequacy of the complaints procedure in the Bill as drafted, we should look particularly closely at its provisions concerning immunity.

The Government say that they are simply rolling forward the arrangements in the Financial Services Act 1986. That is true, but this is a different Bill and the FSA is a different organisation. The financial services markets have been largely self-regulating and many of the people servicing and serving on the Committees are themselves practitioners. They asked to be given a degree of immunity from prosecution if they were to give their time--voluntarily and freely in many cases. They felt that it would be an unacceptable risk if they were to serve on regulatory committees when they could face legal action. Whether that reason was justified, it certainly provided much of the thinking behind the development of the law to date. We now have a Financial Services Authority that has far greater powers and is run by full-time professional staff. Therefore, that reason for immunity no longer applies. The Government have not granted absolute immunity, but only when the FSA acts in good faith. To put it another way, statutory immunity does not apply when it can be shown that the FSA has acted in bad faith. It is notoriously difficult to prove in court that someone has acted in bad faith; essentially a subjective judgment is made.

It is not clear to me whether the immunity covers reckless behaviour. If someone in a position of authority behaved recklessly towards me, I would certainly allege that he had acted in bad faith. That might be a matter of dispute. Our amendment would put the matter beyond doubt. It would explicitly remove statutory immunity when there is reckless behaviour.

Perhaps, more importantly, we also seek to remove statutory immunity when the act or behaviour is unreasonable or negligent. In many cases, the FSA might make mistakes that would not amount to a malicious campaign against a firm or individual, but which would constitute a sin of omission. It would have failed to take proper care and attention. Therefore, it would have acted negligently and cost the victim substantial sums of money. This morning, we asked for such cases to be investigated by the complaints authority and for financial compensation to be paid if the investigator wanted that to be done. Our suggestion was turned down, so the only route left for the person or firm involved is to sue at law. If the FSA is able to show that it acted negligently and the Bill makes it clear that such a defence will give it complete immunity, that will be unfair and unjust.

When the Economic Secretary and others appeared before the Joint Committee, they pointed to foreign experience. In particular it was said, without complication, that the Securities and Exchange Commission in America enjoyed legal protection. I am not a lawyer, still less an international lawyer, but I have made inquiries. In truth, the position is a little more complicated than that presented to us. I am advised that regulators are routinely named as respondents in appeals of their decisions. That differs from suits against agencies themselves. Regulators are generally immune from such claims unless instances of egregious bad faith and intentional misconduct against a firm can be shown. Mere mistake in judgment will not do.

Therefore, not only the SEC, but individual regulators, can in certain cases be the subject of legal action.

I am also advised that, in practical terms, such actions are extremely rare. In general, action is taken against the SEC as a corporate body. However, that is not easy or common and a detailed route has to be taken if one wishes to bring a case against it. If one deviates from that route, the SEC can claim immunity. I hope that that explanation assists the Committee. It may not be entirely clear, but it shows that this is a complex matter and that the SEC does not enjoy blanket immunity as was claimed during the Joint Committee inquiry.

The force of that is doubled by the Government's failure so far to respond satisfactorily to the complaints recommendations of the Joint Committee.
