Mr. Michael J. Foster (Worcester): On a point of order, Mr. O'Hara. We made relatively slow progress this morning, so I intend to move a new sittings motion later this evening.

New clause 21

Stalking, and flushing from cover

    `._(1) A person does not commit an offence under section (Prohibition of hunting, &c.)(1) if he uses a dog to stalk a wild mammal, or to flush it from cover, for the purpose_

    (a) of obtaining food for human consumption, or

    (b) of controlling the number of a particular species in a particular area, in order to safeguard the welfare of that species in that area, or

    (c) of protecting sheep or other livestock, fowl or game birds from attack by wild mammals.

    (2) Subsection (1) applies to a person only if he takes reasonable steps to ensure that the wild mammal is shot as soon as possible after it is located or emerges from cover.

    (3) In this section "cover" does not include cover below ground level.'._[Mr. Michael J. Foster.]

Those of us who are interested in such matters know that in the middle of Salisbury plain there is a colony of stone curlews, one of the rarest birds in Britain. Brought up, and read the First time.

Mr. Michael J. Foster: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The new clause caters for escaped animals and adds some flexibility to deal with circumstances that may arise. It gives exemptions to the offence created in new clause 3. For example, new clause 13 will cover a dog being used to hunt an animal, including fierce animals that may have escaped from captivity, such as a tiger from a zoo or even the infamous Bodmin beast_or is it the beast of Bodmin?

Subsection (2) implies that reasonable steps must be taken to ensure that the escaped animal is recaptured or shot once located. It does not provide an excuse for dogs to continue the chase and tear an animal apart. Recapture is possible. Hon. Members opposed to the Bill may mock, but it is preferable to shoot an escaped animal with a drug to put it to sleep and to return it to captivity, if suitable. That is a better option for those who care about animal welfare than having it shot and killed. The new clause does not give people an excuse to continue the chase with a pack of dogs to catch the quarry and tear it apart, limb from limb, as happens with mink hunting.

Subsection (3) is fairly clear. It ensures that the exemption does not apply to the hunting of foxes and hares that have escaped from captivity. We want to prevent fox and hare hunting, even when foxes or hares escape from captivity.

Subsection (4) provides exemptions for situations that may arise in everyday circumstances. The Bill will not apply to deer, boar or mink, unless an animal has escaped from a zoo or farm. However, I refer Committee members back to subsection (2), which provides that the animal should be recaptured or shot once it has been located. The new clause will not excuse the hunt of a carted deer that has been taken to a location, where the lock has accidentally come off, and the deer may be deemed to have escaped. Such accidental release leading to a hunt would not be permitted under the legislation.

Mr. Cawsey: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

It falls to me to continue to play a helpful role in the Committee. I have done my best over the past few weeks to be helpful and to ensure that we return good legislation to the House, for which it will thank us.

He is most concerned about the new clause, which relates to common land and national parks. An area of the Lake district was discussed earlier. If the new clause is rejected, it will not be possible to hunt foxes with hounds in that area. The normal alternative to hunting on farmland is for the farmer to go out and shoot the fox_that is how most foxes are dispatched, despite assertions to the contrary by the Bill's opponents_but there are no farmers on common land. We do not want lots of people who cannot hunt with dogs wandering around with shotguns in national parks, which have hundreds of thousands of visitors each year. The Committee should discuss the matter and reach a conclusion.

 

Mr. Foster: We partially discussed this matter when we considered an amendment that would exempt certain geographical areas from the offence of hunting. I see no reason why an exemption should be made for hunting in national parks.

We are told that sheep farming suffers from fox predation. If there is a local problem, farmers should use the most effective, efficient and humane method to control fox predation, which, all the evidence suggests, is by shooting. Concerns have been expressed about national parks because tourists flock to them, and rightly so. It is interesting to note that hunting with fell packs in those areas does not take place in July and August. If one relied solely on hunting with dogs, there would be, in effect, no pest control in those areas during those months. Clearly, however, that is not realistic. If there is a problem during those months a farmer will rightly deal with it as quickly as possible by shooting the fox. It is common practice to ignore tourists who happen to be in the area.

More important, evidence has been brought to my attention that explains why people go fell pack hunting, which is different from following a hunt on a horse. I want to quote an opinion from 1987_I must apologise that it is from the previous decade, but it is still topical. In a television programme called "Face the public", Chris Ogilvie, a huntsman of the Coniston Foxhounds, was asked about his fox hunting activities. He stated:

    "I don't hunt foxes to control them and neither does any hunting person".

The host then asked:

    "So what do you hunt them for?"

Chris Ogilvie replied:

"We hunt them for sport."

The host asked, "For enjoyment?" Chris Ogilvie replied, "Yes." The host asked, "For pleasure?" Chris Ogilvie replied, "Yes...for pleasure."

That is what happens with the current fell packs in the national parks. I do not believe that the Committee, or the House, can allow that to continue. I therefore urge that we reject new clause 7.
