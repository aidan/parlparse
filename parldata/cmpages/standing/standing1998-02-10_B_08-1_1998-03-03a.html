The issue relates to the phrase ``shall have regard to'', which refers to the annual policing plan. One wonders how different Chief Constables might interpret those words in the clause. It also relates to the way in which that interpretation will be perceived by the general public, which the Police Authority is supposed to represent.

The clause strikes at one of the key issues in the Bill because it relates to matters that deal with the Chief Constable's operational autonomy. Successive Chief Constables have been criticised by members of the public for actions that they have or have not taken in the historical past. In political terms, it is a key area. One side of the community would want some operational control introduced because it feels that, in the past, there has been a cop-out by Secretaries of State who say, ``This is an operational decision. There is no politics involved. Therefore, we leave it to the Chief Constable to decide what to do.''

That issue has been taken up by the CAJ. In its submission to the Committee, the CAJ pointed out the difficulties to which I have just alluded. It would not wish the control of the police to be subject to any partisan, political control. However, it feels that the wide-ranging powers in clause 19 give Chief Constables enormous power. The CAJ maintains that such power has been subject to abuse--that is its interpretation of actions by previous Chief Constables, but, nevertheless, it is a strongly-held view. In particular, the CAJ refers to marches and events such as those in Drumcree and, very recently, in Londonderry. Those issues were operational ones, which, in the CAJ's view, were open to some abuse in the past.

The Police Authority would like to make it clear to the public that it is in charge of the annual policing plan. Clause 17 makes it clear that it is responsible for issuing that plan. Although the Chief Constable submits a draft of that annual policing plan, the Police Authority can amend it. It does not have to return to the Chief Constable to approve such changes. If that policing plan has the ownership of the Police Authority and if the Chief Constable deviates from it in any way under the powers given to him under the clause he will be accountable to the Police Authority. That is exactly what the amendment seeks to achieve. If the Chief Constable deviates from the annual policing plan he should consult with and seek the approval of the Police Authority.

It may be argued that we need to be flexible and that we never know what is around the corner in Northern Ireland in terms of policing and the security situation. I fully accept that a plan must contain contingency sections. If there was a severe deterioration in security, the Chief Constable would obviously have to meet that challenge. I do not see any problem in writing that contingency option into the annual policing plan. I am sure that it will be because that is common sense. But deviations beyond that which are not within the contingency section are more important in this context.

It is right and proper that the Police Authority is seen to have full ownership of the annual policing plan. The amendment makes it clear that these deviations shall be discussed with the Police Authority and that its approval shall be sought for their implementation. If the Chief Constable is not accountable for the policing plan to the Police Authority, then to whom is he or she responsible? That is not clear in clause 19.

The accountability factor may be exercised in the reports that must be submitted under clause 47(2)(a) and clause 48(3). But one could wait weeks, if not months, for reports and we are talking about major deviations from the plan in the shorter term which need to be discussed with the Police Authority. It believes that the amendment would help it to exercise the responsibilities for which it is tasked in clause 17 with the annual policing plan.

There is, arguably, an ingredient missing from clause 17 and the amendment would restore that deficiency. To illustrate my argument, clause 17(3) rightly requires the Chief Constable to submit a draft of the plan to the Police Authority. Subsection (4) allows the Police Authority either to adopt the draft plan that the Chief Constable submits or to amend it, having consulted the Chief Constable. Under clause 17 the plan is to cover policing priorities for the year, the financial resources expected to be available and any objectives and performance targets which have been established either by the Police Authority or by the Secretary of State. However, clause 17 does not specify any involvement on the part of the Police Authority if the Chief Constable, for operational reasons, wishes to depart substantially from the plan for the year. The amendment would rectify that omission.

My hon. Friend the Member for North-East Cambridgeshire said that it was highly likely that an annual policing plan would contain contingency provisions to deal with circumstances such as variations in the levels of violence and the perceived threat to security. However, there are other reasons why the Chief Constable might wish dramatically to revise the plan or to depart from it that would have an impact on the performance targets and the priorities.

For example, at the moment, fortunately, the drug problem in Northern Ireland is not as great as in the rest of the United Kingdom; it is also appreciably less serious than in the Republic of Ireland. Perish the thought that it should happen, but if the drug situation in Northern Ireland suddenly deteriorated, the Chief Constable might wish to transfer substantial resources. That is one example in which the existing annual plan may not contain the necessary contingency provisions.

Another example is that of animal health. There may be a need to police much more effectively the movement of animals across the border with the Republic. At present, few police resources are devoted to that task.

I have given two instances in which the Chief Constable, for operational reasons, may wish suddenly to depart substantially from the annual plan.

The amendment would require the Chief Constable, in circumstances such as those that I have outlined, to consult the Police Authority and to seek its approval. It could be argued that that would happen in any event, and that the amendment is therefore unnecesary. On the contrary, the Bill explicitly covers many eventualities and circumstances requiring consultation. According to clause 19(2), the Chief Constable, rightly

    ``shall have regard to the annual policing plan issued under section 17.''

For the sake of completeness, he should also be required to consult, and to seek the approval of, the Police Authority if he intends to depart substantially from the plan.

It is all very well to say that in a nice cosy world disagreements and problems will not happen, but what would happen when there was disagreement? It would no longer be the case, under clause 19 (1), that the police force

    ``shall be under the direction and control of the Chief Constable''

because he would have to seek the approval of the Police Authority, which could withhold it.

Fundamental principles are at stake here. A judgment in Regina v. Metropolitan Police Commissioner was made by Lord Denning. He said of that commissioner's duty:

    ``I have no hesitation, however, in holding that, like every constable in the land, he should be, and is, independent of the executive. He is not subject to the orders of the Secretary of State, save that under the Police Act 1964 the Secretary of State can call on him to give a report, or to retire in the interests of efficiency.''

Under the judgment,

    ``No Minister of the Crown can tell him that he must, or must not, keep observation on this place or that; or that he must, or must not, prosecute this man or that one. Nor can any police authority tell him so. The responsibilty for law enforcement lies on him. He is answerable to the law and to the law alone.''
