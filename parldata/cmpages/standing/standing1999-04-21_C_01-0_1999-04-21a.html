Breeding and Sale of Dogs (Welfare)

10.30 am

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I beg to move,

    That during proceedings on the Breeding and Sale of Dogs (Welfare) Bill the Committee do meet on Wednesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and half-past Four o'clock.

It is a great pleasure to serve under you on this Committee, Mr. Cunningham. The Bill has been a while coming and many people deserve credit for its genesis. Now that it is here, we hope to make progress with fulfilling its aim, which is to improve the welfare of dogs kept, and puppies bred, in commercial breeding establishments.

It is well known that some establishments not all, by any means fall well short of the standards that should appertain for bitches and puppies. Such cases have come to light all too often when members of the public have had occasion to buy puppies. The Bill is a step towards addressing those problems.

I want to pay tribute to the role of some people in the Bill. First and foremost, I pay tribute to Baroness Wharton for her indefatigable work. I also thank the group of 11 organisations representing animal welfare, dog breeders, local authorities, the veterinary profession and the pet care industry that have worked together to prepare the Bill. They are the Blue Cross, the British Dog Breeders Council, the British Veterinary Association, the Chartered Institute of Environmental Health, Justice for Dogs, the Kennel Club, the National Canine Defence League, the National Dog Wardens Association, the Pet Care Trust, ProDogs, and the Royal Society for the Prevention of Cruelty to Animals. I mention especially the work of Barry Huckle of the Pet Care Trust.

I pay tribute to the work of the all-party group on animal welfare. I am grateful to the Minister for the personal interest that he has taken in the Bill, for the support of members of his office, and for the Government's support.

Mr. Maxton, I [Interruption.] I am sorry, Mr. Cunningham. I have had 24 hours of addressing Mr. Maxton, so I hope that you will forgive one slip. I commend the motion.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 1

Inspection and report before grant of licence

Mr. Clappison: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 1, line 6, leave out from `In' to end of line 2 on page 2 and insert `the Breeding of Dogs Act 1973 ("the 1973 Act"), after subsection (2 of section 1 insert

    "(2A) On receipt of an application by a person to a local authority for the grant of a licence under this Act in respect of any premises

    (a) if a licence under this Act has not previously been granted to the person in respect of the premises, the authority shall arrange for the inspection of the premises by a veterinary surgeon or veterinary practitioner and by an officer of the authority; and

    (b) in any other case, the authority may arrange for the inspection of the premises by a veterinary surgeon or veterinary practitioner or by an officer of the authority (or by both).

    (2B) Where an inspection is arranged under subsection (2A) of this section, the local authority shall arrange for the making of a report about the premises, the applicant and any other relevant matter; and the authority shall consider the report before determining whether to grant a licence.".'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to consider amendment No. 10, in clause 6, page 5, line 19, leave out `and (2B)'.

Mr. Clappison: These are largely technical amendments to give effect to the original intention of the working group. They provide that before a licence is granted, breeding establishment premises must be inspected by a veterinary surgeon or practitioner and a local authority officer. For subsequent inspections, the local authority is given the flexibility to send a vet, an officer or both. That will often save costs, but it will not weaken inspection. The Bill will improve existing inspection arrangements and, therefore, the welfare of dogs and pups.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. George Howarth): I support the amendment and the Bill and I congratulate the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) on his good fortune in drawing a place in the ballot. I associate myself with his praise of a number of people and organisations. Modesty forbids me from joining in with the compliments that the paid to me.

My hon. Friend the Member for Weaver Vale (Mr. Hall) introduced such a Bill some 12 months ago. Unfortunately, it failed to complete its parliamentary procedure and make the statute book. I know that he has been supportive to the hon. Member for Hertsmere in moving the issue forward.

I should pay a compliment to you, Mr. Cunningham. I know form previous experience that your Scottish sense of fair play, coupled with your midlands sense of getting the business done efficiently, will stand us in good stead. It would not be wise to detain the Committee unnecessarily, so I will not. I am reminded by two members of the Committee of a related issue and with a bit of latitude on your part, Mr. Cunningham, perhaps I could tell the story.

My hon. Friend the Member for Warrington, South (Ms Southworth) has a good young offenders institution in her constituency called Thorn Cross, which I visited before the general election. It was an interesting visit. The governor asked me why the then Member for Don Valley, Martin Redmond, who, as many members of the Committee will recall, was a prolific tabler of written questions, took such a strong interest in the prison. I said that I was not aware that he had and asked in what sense he had taken an interest. It transpired that he had tabled a series of written questions about a donkey that had managed to escape from Thorn Cross, how long it was at large, where it had got to, how it was recaptured and any other aspect that he could imagine.

The governor's concern was that he and his staff had spent so long providing information to answer the written questions that the management of the establishment had suffered. I tell that story only because there is clearly a connection between Don Valley and animal welfare that continues to this day.

The background to the Bill has already been given by the hon. Member for Hertsmere, but it would be remiss of me not to mention that the hon. Member for North Thanet (Mr. Gale) has played a prominent role over several years on the issue. I know that he will be pleased that the Bill is moving forward. It certainly reflects the concerns that the parliamentary group that he formerly chaired has voiced over several years.

The Bill has the support of numerous organisations, many of which the hon. Member for Hertsmere has acknowledged. I join him in paying tribute to the work that Baroness Wharton has carried out with the various interested parties in developing the Bill, which is certainly better for having gone through that process.

The Bill seeks to address three concerns. The first, as the hon. Member for Hertsmere said, is the welfare of bitches and puppies and the conditions in which they are kept. The second is the increased likelihood of unhealthy puppies being sold to the public. The third is the practice of some unscrupulous dog breeders who taint the legitimate business interests of others.

The Committee will have gathered from my remarks that the Government are sympathetic both to this largely technical amendment and to the Bill. I know that our deliberations will be careful. All the amendments that have been tabled are sensible and I shall not detain the Committee by opposing any of them. I wish the Bill speedy progress. Let us all hope that it will end up on the statute book this time.

Mr. Clappison: I am grateful to the Minister for his remarks. He did what I should have done at the outset by paying tribute to the hon. Member for Weaver Vale and my hon. Friend the Member for North Thanet. I endorse what he said about them.

    4A. (1) References in this Act to the keeping of a breeding establishment for dogs shall be construed in accordance with this section.

    (2) A person keeps a breeding establishment for dogs at any premises if he carries on at those premises a business of breeding dogs for sale (whether by him or any other person).

    (3) Subject to subsection (5) of this section, where

    (a) a person keeps a bitch at any premises at any time during any period of twelve months; and

    (b) the bitch gives birth to a litter of puppies at any time during that period.

    he shall be treated as carrying on a business of breeding dogs for sale at the premises throughout the period if a total of four or more other litters is born during the period to bitches falling within subsection (4) of this section.

    (4) The bitches falling within this subsection are

    (a) the bitch mentioned in subsection (3)(a) and (b) of this section and any other bitches kept by the person at the premises at any time during the period;

    (c) any bitches kept by him elsewhere at any such time; and

    (d) any bitches kept (anywhere) by any person at any such time under a breeding arrangement made with him.

    (5) Subsection (3) of this section does not apply if the person shows that none of the puppies born to bitches falling within paragraph (a), (b) or (d) of subsection (4) of this section was in fact sold during the period (whether by him or any other person).

Caroline Flint (Don Valley): I want to comment on what my hon. Friend the Minister said. A year or so ago, I discovered that my local authority had once attended a conference on stray horses in Ireland. I was pleased to support my hon. Friend the Member for Weaver Vale in his attempt last year to get such a Bill on to the statute book. I congratulate the hon. Member for Hertsmere on taking up the challenge.

There are many reputable dog breeders who have welfare as their first concern. However, some people see dog breeding as an easy way to make a quick buck and have no consideration for the dogs or for families who purchase them in good faith. As a child, I experienced the problem of getting a pet that turned out to be ill and had to be put to sleep. There is nothing more distressing than seeing children's great expectations of giving love and care to an animal being cruelly betrayed when an animal is not fit to become a pet.

The Bill addresses an important matter. We must ensure that the welfare of the animals is uppermost in the minds of those who breed them and that we have the necessary regulation and authority to enforce that. Most pet owners take pet ownership seriously. We are known as a dog-loving nation and it is terrible to think that despite that reputation, there are countless examples of abuse of pet dogs. Abuse can start with the birth of the dogs and the manner in which they are passed on to owners.

I welcome the Bill. I received countless letters from constituents when the issue last came before the House. I have continued to keep them informed of progress. I hope that the Bill will be successful and that this Parliament can start to put right the bad practice that remains a sad feature of the breeding and selling of dogs.
