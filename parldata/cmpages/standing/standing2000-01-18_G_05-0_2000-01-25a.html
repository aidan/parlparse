Clause 46

Payments, services etc. not to be

regarded as donations

Amendment moved [this day]: No. 38, in page 28, line 8, at end insert—

    `(h) any payment made by an individual or organisation in respect of, or any facilities or services provided in pursuance of, education activity by the party for the benefits of its members'.—[Mr. Walter.]

Mr. Robert Walter (North Dorset): I presume that Room 9 has television facilities so that our proceedings will be available to those who want to watch them.

Amendment No. 38 is a probing amendment. Clause 46 may not even be the appropriate clause in which to insert the amendment, which seeks to add paragraph (h) to subsection (1), but if I explain our intention, perhaps the Minister will give us some guidance.

The amendment deals with our parties' international aspects. Although the Neill report and the whole tenor of the Bill are directed against the concept of foreign donations to our parties, modern political parties have many international links with like-minded parties around the world and with a number of foundations, charitable and otherwise, which are connected with those parties. It would be a shame if, in passing the Bill for legitimate reasons, we cut off those international links.

I shall speak from the Conservative party's perspective, but I know that there are parallel links in the Labour party. Conservative Members have been on joint conferences organised by the Konrad Adenauer Stiftung, a German political foundation that was set up after the war. My hon. Friend the Member for Beaconsfield (Mr. Grieve) and I have enjoyed their hospitality. I should say that the United Kingdom had a great deal to do with the design of the German political system. The Stiftung is concerned with democratic political education. It sponsors many conferences in the United Kingdom and abroad; it has offices in this country and it has close links with the Conservative party. The so-called Democratic party in Germany has similar links with the Labour party. There are also links across the Atlantic.

The Democratic party in the United States has an organisation called the Democratic National Institute, with which I have had dealings in the past, and the Republicans have the International Republican Fund for Co-operation. Those bodies are concerned not with propagating political ideas to the wider electorate, but with promoting good international links through international bodies—whether it be the Liberal International, the Socialist International or, in our case, the International Democratic Union—and bilateral links between political parties.

We would like to ensure that those links can continue. We do not want suddenly to find that we are no longer able, as a political party, to co-operate with the Konrad Adenauer Stiftung. That would be cutting off one's nose to spite one's face.

I shall give an example in which I was involved last summer. The Conservative party was invited to send a representative to Cyprus to address the annual conference of the Democratic Rally-Liberal party, which is the governing party. The invitation was sent to my party leader, who was unable to go, and asked me if I would attend in his place. I went to Cyprus and addressed the conference. My fares and major expenses were met by the Conservative party and I received some modest hospitality from the Democratic Rally party while I was there. However, the fact that I had a meal paid for by a foreign political party would not be deemed to be foreign funding of the Conservative party. It is essential to continue that kind of linkage between parties, which is why we are seeking to draw out the Minister in the context of the amendment.

We phrased the amendment carefully so that it would be all-embracing. It refers to

    ``payment made by an individual or organisation in respect of . . . education activity by the party for the benefits of its members''.

That money would not be paid to the party so that it could spend it on propagating its ideas or fighting elections or referendum campaigns, but so that it could use it internally in its educational activities. Does the Minister think that the amendment is appropriate, or would he prefer the matter to be covered elsewhere in the Bill?

Mr. Dominic Grieve (Beaconsfield): I want to add a few points to what my hon. Friend said.

As he pointed out, there are two distinct issues: first, educational activity in general and, secondly, the foreign element. In respect of domestic funding, of course, there would be no prohibition on such activity—it would be a political donation. Although it may be hedged around with a number of caveats, it would certainly be declarable and transparent. In the case of foreign donations, one of the features of the Bill is that if such funding became a donation within the meaning of part IV, it would then be prohibited.

One approach may be to review the prohibition on foreign funding. The alternative approach that we have tried to adopt here is to change the meaning of ``donation''. The issue will have to be addressed one way or the other. Otherwise, many activities that most people would regard as beneficial—and far removed from the sort normally associated with political party funding—could disappear. My hon. Friend has given a number of examples; there are many others.

The Conservative party has a foreign affairs forum—perhaps the Labour party does too—that organises trips abroad for Members and people on the candidates' list, to keep them informed about foreign affairs. I went on a trip to Moscow in 1990 with the forum. I have not the slightest idea how the funding arrangements worked on that visit. However, it was apparent that some of the tab was picked up by the then Soviet Government, which was on its last legs. Some of it was picked up by the Conservative party, and some, ultimately, by the participants who paid to go on the trip. I cannot believe that the Labour party does not have similar complex issues to address.

It would be regrettable if the implementation of Lord Neill's report were either to make such trips or activities impossible because they constituted fiendish foreign funding of a party's activities, or to make it extremely complicated to disclose, account for and explain such donations. It is a very complicated area.

Mr. Ian Davidson (Glasgow, Pollok): I propose to speak against the amendment, as it seems to suggest that anything described as ``education activity'' would be exempt from being considered a donation, and having to be openly declared and above board.

I think back to my time as a councillor in Strathclyde. Every time the social work department made a complete mess of something, it described it as a valuable learning experience. There are few experiences in life from which one does not learn something, although some of the speeches made earlier may be an exception to that rule. For example, an election campaign is a learning experience; winning or losing an election campaign is a learning experience. The amendment has been drafted so widely that it would destroy the entire Bill, and I hope that it will be rejected.

I understand the point about foreign trips and the need to consider them educational. However, if they had to be declared and above board, one would have to ensure that they were genuinely a means of imparting information through experience, and not merely a means of transferring money to the party in question by defraying costs that the party would otherwise be required to meet. The amendment is so loosely worded as to make nonsense of the rest of the Bill. I accept that it is a probing amendment, but it probes a little too far.

Mr. Andrew Stunell (Hazel Grove): I certainly would not support the amendment in its present form. However, it raises issues that need the Minister's consideration.

I should declare a non-interest, because in a previous existence I made two visits overseas courtesy of the Westminster Foundation. There may be an issue here connected with the work of that foundation. Will the Minister confirm that the functions or duties carried out and funded by the Westminster Foundation, supporting international travel and visits by political parties, would not be classified as a donation? Perhaps we should explore that point. Assuming that it would not be a donation and that it would be dealt with in some other way, it might be possible to achieve what the hon. Member for North Dorset (Mr. Walter) wishes through a Westminster Foundation mechanism. The principle is fairly easy to establish. We are talking about expenditure incurred by a member of a political party overseas, not in this country.

My hon. Friend referred to a bottle of House of Commons whisky. It is valued in the Souvenir Shop downstairs at under £20, but it might well be sold at auction for several thousand pounds because it had acquired the signatures of various party leaders, past and present. Last year, I got the signatures of all three major party leaders on a bottle of whisky, which was then auctioned for charity. Of course, the provision would not apply in that case, but it does apply to political auctions.

Handbags are another example—[Laughter.] It is not unknown for Baroness Thatcher to donate a handbag that she no longer uses, which is then auctioned to raise money for the Conservative party. The market value of such a handbag in a second-hand shop might be about £15 or £20—if it were of good quality—but it might well sell at auction for several thousand pounds. The question is, has the Baroness or the man bidding at the auction given several thousand pounds, or have neither of them, it simply being a commercial transaction? There is no clear answer to that question in the provisions dealing with the value of donations, but we will come back to the issue at a later stage. If the gentleman bidding for the handbag paid several thousand pounds, would the auctioneer have to decide whether that was a permissible donation by asking whether the gentleman were on a United Kingdom electoral register and therefore eligible to make that sort of donation to a political party? Such questions must be answered.

Mr. Stunell: In many ways I wish I had the same problems with these provisions as Conservative Members have. Before the recess, the Minister undertook to consider some of the practical issues. Presumably, as in a commercial operation, one is entitled to defray one's reasonable expenses in acting as the auctioneer, acquiring the venue and so on, so the net donation to the party would be the difference between the value of the item as given to the party and the purchase price. That might be one solution to the problem.

Clause 48

Permissible donors

Mr. Martin Linton (Battersea): I beg to move amendment No. 28, in page 29, line 12, after `register', insert—

    `and ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom'.

The Chairman: With this we may take the following amendments:

No. 40, in page 29, line 12, at end insert—

    `or eligible to be registered in an electoral register'.

No. 29, in page 29, line 28, at end insert—

    `(2A) The Secretary of State may by order define the term ``ordinarily resident'' for the purposes of this section.'.

Mr. Linton: While we digest the fascinating insight into the prices paid at the auctions organised by the Dorset Conservative Association—I am sure that we are all jealous of the hon. Gentleman's fund-raising powers—I shall explain the amendments. Amendment No. 28 would restrict the category of permissible donors to political parties not just to those on the electoral register, as in the Bill, but to those

    ``ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom''.

Amendment No. 29 would allow the Secretary of State to define ``ordinarily resident''. I make no apology for aiming the amendment at tax exiles. I start from the simple premise that British political parties should not be financed from tax havens by people who left Britain specifically in order to avoid paying taxes. That is wrong in principle because someone who does not pay tax in this country should not have a hand in deciding how much tax we pay. By moving abroad to escape taxation in this country, such people should forfeit the right to interfere in UK elections and to fund UK parties. It is also wrong in a practical sense, because if parties can be financed by tax exiles, it gives them a potential interest in maintaining tax loopholes for the benefit of people who do not even live in this country. It is similar to the argument against overseas funding by people who are not even British citizens.

The amendment does not deal with the right of overseas voters to vote in UK elections—we shall later debate clause 130, which reduces the qualifying period from 20 to 10 years—as my concern is not whether tax exiles can vote in official elections, but whether they can fund British political parties. It is not a question whether overseas voters can give any money to political parties. As my hon. Friend the Minister just reminded us, people can always give up to £200 under clause 45(2), which will be an unrecordable donation. That certainly covers subscriptions to a political party—even the subscription to the North Dorset Conservative Association is not quite that much—but it would affect a person's ability to give more substantial funds to parties.

5.15 pm

When I first raised this issue in July when the draft Bill was published, I was accused of trying to tailor the Bill against one person—Michael Ashcroft—and I should like to explain why that is not so. Two things have happened since then that change the situation. First, my right hon. Friend the Home Secretary has added clause 130 to the Bill to reduce the qualifying period from 20 to 10 years. That will affect Michael Ashcroft who moved to Florida in 1985. Secondly, Mr. Ashcroft announced, of his own volition, that he is going to ``regularise'' his affairs, in his quaint phrase, and move back to this country. I am happy if I have played a small part in forcing him to choose between living abroad and living, voting and paying his taxes in Britain.

Arguably, Mr. Ashcroft showed more sense than Conservative Front-Bench Members on this issue, because he realised before they did that in the long run it was both indefensible and unsustainable for one of the great parties in this country to be financed by a tax exile. As far as I know he still sends a cheque for £83,300 to Conservative central office every month. I hope that the Bill stops that money being given even before he moves back to this country. That would certainly be the effect of my amendment. Even if Mr. Ashcroft came back tomorrow and started paying taxes, I still would not be happy.

The amendment was never aimed at Michael Ashcroft. It is aimed at all tax exiles. The Neill committee report was less concerned with the treasurer of the Conservative party than with donors to the Scottish National party and the nationalist parties in general. Indeed at paragraph 5.28, it said:

    ``To some extent, the Scottish and Welsh diaspora might be assisted by the provisions concerning overseas voters . . . Some who have recently left Scotland and Wales to live abroad would be able to take advantage of these provisions.''

The provision has been included almost with them in mind. We can think of members of the Scottish diaspora, perhaps recently knighted, who can take advantage of this provision. I do not know who the Patagonian wool millionaires are who fund Plaid Cymru, but some rich members of the Welsh diaspora may fund it. The Neill committee received evidence from the Scottish National party, which pleaded in favour of an open regime of overseas funding. The report concluded:

    ``we see little difference in such support coming (at a time of globalisation) from London, Lisbon or Los Angeles.''

The origin of a donation makes an enormous difference. My hon. Friend the Member for Pollok, who is temporarily out of the Room, is very knowledgeable on this subject and keen to raise the issues involved. I base my argument on that put forward in the Neill report. Having mentioned the position taken by the Scottish and Welsh parties and their belief that they should be allowed to be funded from their diaspora, Neill disagrees saying:

    ``We would expect, however, that parties such as the SNP, faced with a bar on foreign donations, would campaign even more vigorously for funds in other parts of the UK''.

It then enunciates a principle that everyone should be able to accept:

    ``We have, therefore, concluded that . . . it is right to take the opportunity to lay down the principle that those who live, work and carry on business in the United Kingdom should be the persons exclusively entitled to support financially the operation of the political process here.''

I could not agree more. If one translates that into the rather more relaxed syntax that my hon. Friend believes should be used, it basically says that only people who live, work and carry on business in Britain should be allowed to fund political parties. That was the principle enunciated in the report's conclusion. Unfortunately when it comes to the recommendations it was watered down to those ``registered to vote'' or

    ``eligible to be registered to vote''.

So a tight definition in the conclusion expands to include not only everyone who is registered to vote, but anyone eligible, including some 8 or 9 million British citizens who have moved overseas. It was greatly to the Home Secretary's credit that when he drafted the Bill he narrowed it down to those registered to vote and cut out the other 8 or 9 million who are the subject of an amendment tabled by the hon. Member for Beaconsfield. My right hon. Friend made the point that it is much easier to tell whether someone is registered to vote than it is to tell whether they are eligible to vote. He thereby demonstrated that he is not tied to the letter of the Neill report, and can disregard it where it is plainly wrong.

I suggest that he has not gone far enough on this small point to disregard those recommendations. He should go back to the original principle set out by Neill, which is that only those who live, work and carry on business here should fund political parties. Let us not forget that it is possible to be registered as an overseas voter on the electoral register in a constituency, while at the same time being a foreign resident, a foreign national, a foreign dual national, a foreign taxpayer and indeed, as we have seen recently, even a representative of a foreign Government. Mere citizenship coupled with overseas residence is not a guarantee of anything.

All those problems could be resolved if we could just add the words

    ``and ordinarily resident in the United Kingdom''

to clause 48. That is the test used by the Inland Revenue to determine whether someone should pay tax. It is also used by the national health service to determine whether a person is entitled to go to hospital and receive free care and attention. We should also apply it to political parties. Residence is defined by custom and practice by the Inland Revenue, but if the Secretary of State felt the need to define it according to the electoral roll, amendment No. 29 would give him the power to do so.

Overseas voters are already in a special category in many different ways. Their registration is voluntary, whereas it is compulsory for voters in this country. They vote by proxy, not in person. They have to register every year, and if they fail to do so they fall off the register. It would not be inconsistent to add a fourth difference that although they have the right to vote and to belong to political parties in this country, they do not have the right to fund them. Alternatively, if one preferred, they would not have the right to give recordable donations to political parties, which would rule out only large donations over £5,000. That is the argument for amendments Nos. 28 and 29. I hope that my hon. Friend the Minister will reconsider that and take on board some of the points that I have made.
