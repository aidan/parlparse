Mr. Breed: I add my congratulations to the Government Front Bench team and I welcome them to the Committee. I look forward to their contributions and, I hope, to their approval and even acceptance of some of the amendments that we have tabled to try to improve the Bill.

As we all know, a large number of small friendly societies still exists today. If the amendments are not accepted, costs will be imposed on those societies that will be out of proportion to their size, membership and activities. Often they have no formal insurance function, but make occasional small benevolent payments while their main functions lie elsewhere. Some small societies write insurance and so should be regulated, but again at appropriate levels to ensure that the costs of such regulation are not disproportionate.

There is room here for exemptions, Where a society does not write insurance, or writes no new business, but merely runs previously written insurance, it should be eligible for exemption. The real problem, as we have found many times, is trying to set a sensible level to comply with the arguments of de minimis, so that we can properly target those firms that should be regulated, while allowing others to be exempt.

One example I have come across--and I suspect there are many others--illustrates some of the difficulties that the Bill will cause these societies if it is not amended. In 1997 the Lonach Highland and Friendly Society which was instituted in 1823 had income from its members of £330. It received in addition dividend income of £50.22. Against that £186 was paid out in annuities to members who had reached 70 and to some widows of members. Prior to regulation the annual income exceeded the annual expenditure which meant that the fund was gradually increasing.

However, in 1997 this small friendly society was subject to costs of £30 to the Personal Investment Authority, £335 to the Registry of Friendly Societies, £411 to its accountants and £822 to its actuaries. Even on the first morning back after a long break I suspect that most hon. Members will have worked out that that somewhat exceeds the £380 that it had accumulated in the year. The fund therefore had to rely on donations from vice-presidents to alleviate the problem.

That is not an isolated case. There is a large number of similar small friendly societies and local benevolent societies that were set up in the l9th century. It could be argued that they are an anachronism and that they should come in to the real world, that they should get up to date and either grow and live within regulations or fail and close down. That would be a great shame. If they are to be subjected to inordinate regulatory costs it would not only remove from the life of many communities these longstanding friendly societies, it would discourage the setting up of any new societies. Combining two criteria, therefore, might protect existing friendly societies and would not discourage new benevolent societies.

The essence of our amendments is that a specified maximum asset level should be low enough to support the de minimis argument and that societies should not be authorised to write insurance business or, if their book is now closed, it should be run off.

Clause 34 provides the power to order exemptions of classes of persons, on which the Treasury may rely to exempt small friendly societies. The Bill ought to contain a more specific provision, enabling small societies to proceed without the burden of regulation.

I trust that Ministers will accept our amendments. However, should they not be prepared to do so, will they provide reassurance about how they intend to deal with those rather small societies that may face regulatory costs out of all proportion to the benefits and which may sometimes exceed their annual income?

Dr. Vincent Cable (Twickenham): I beg to move amendment No. 134, in page 195, line 6, at end insert--

    `Mortgages for house purchase

    23A. The provision of loans for the purchase of property for residential use, where the obligation of the borrower to repay the lender is secured on the value of the property in question.'.

We are returning to a subject--mortgage regulation--that was extensively debated before the summer break. I recall that there was no explicit reference to the subject in the Bill. The amendment is an an attempt to deal with that.

I recognise that there have been movements and much public debate about the matter over the summer. The Financial Services Authority and the mortgage lenders have changed their position and I expect that the Government will respond to that quickly. By the end of this week, consultation will have been completed and the Government will propose recommendations. We have an opportunity now to flag some of the issues that will be debated more intensively over the next few weeks when the Government have formulated their reply.

I want to put on record some of the arguments that have been widely held for incorporating mortgage regulation explicitly in the Bill and to address some of the concerns that have been expressed in recent debates. We have been over the ground before in different contexts, but it is worth summarising the argument for building mortgage regulation into the Bill.

There are five main points. The first is the asymmetry and imbalance between the house purchaser and the mortgage lender, which can be observed in many different ways. When buying a house, people are often under great time and financial pressure. Under those circumstances, they may not be in a position to shop around and obtain the best value. They are signing up to agreements that have enormous long-term costs--redemption penalties being the most obvious of those. The lack of information for the mortgage purchaser as opposed to the lender is not addressed by the market process.

The second problem is the odd way in which the FSA now functions. It is responsible for the endowment part of an endowment mortgage, but not the mortgage bit of it. I am not making a dogmatic point about endowment policies--many people have benefited considerably from them--but there are difficulties, and because of the need for the FSA to have an active role in policing them, it is only sensible that the two parts of the financial transaction should be dealt with together.

A third problem is that there are many gaps in the Bill. We know that there are protective elements for consumers, such as consumer credit, but there are many gaps in the system that mortgage regulation would cover. A fourth point is the idea of the FSA being a one-stop shop. After all, that is what the proposed legislation was about originally. Why leave out one of the key elements in financial services and the financial market?

Lastly--a point that has been recognised by the mortgage lenders themselves--there are deficiencies in the voluntary code. There is large-scale non-compliance but no deterrent. Mortgage lenders have a vested interest in creating a system that suits their interests. To their credit, they now publicly acknowledge the limitations of the scheme.

The background to the politics of the Bill is that the Treasury Select Committee considered the matter and the Joint Committee on Financial Services and Markets came up with a strong recommendation for incorporating mortgages within statutory regulation. We have moved on from the summer in some key respects. To summarise, the FSA, which was thought originally to have some reservations because of its work overload, has now recommended that mortgages should come within statutory regulation. It is willing to cope with the issues, the biggest of which is a question of time rather than principle.

More importantly, mortgage lenders themselves appear to have come on board and have accepted, whether by public pressure or the limitations of their own schemes, to work with mortgage regulation. The only reservations that I have picked up concern some of the Treasury's comments in the past few days, suggesting that it has doubts about whether statutory regulation is desirable.

Two arguments are being advanced, the first of which is a little difficult to understand. The Treasury seems to be saying that if the mortgage lenders are in favour of the proposal, there must be something wrong with it because, clearly, they will capture the system. There may be some truth in that up to a point, but that is even more true of self-regulation and it is a little difficult to follow the logic.

The Treasury's other concern is about the cost of regulation acting as a barrier to new entry into the industry. The answer is partly provided by the FSA, which has now done its costings on barriers to entry and the cost of compliance. It suggests that those are rather modest; they may be big in aggregate terms, but small in relation to financial transactions as a whole. The estimate is about 15p on a monthly mortgage payment--not a big cost proportionately. We are dealing with an industry that already has a profusion of lenders and products. The problems arise not from the number of entrants into the industry, but from the inability of consumers to manage the vast quantity of information that is thrown at them.

The traditional objection to regulation, which is that it blocks new entry and creates barriers, is not appropriate in this case. I shall be interested to hear whether the Minister will rehearse those concerns and tell us how the issue will be handled.
