Formulation and implementation of strategies

4.30 pm

Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I beg to move amendment No. 53, in page 7, leave out lines 22 to 24 and insert--

    ``such period as the responsible authority shall specify''.

The amendment concerns the duration of strategies for the reduction of crime and disorder. Why have the Government fixed a time scale of three years?

The Minister of State, Home Office (Mr. Alun Michael): That is a sensible question. In the past, an analysis has been made of a particular problem, a strategy has been developed, and people have been left to tackle the problem according to that strategy. However, experience shows that one's understanding of a problem needs to be renewed after a period of time.

Several years ago the problem of crime on the St. Mellons estate, which is in my constituency, was examined. We drew together all the agencies concerned, made recommendations to the police, the city council and the county council, among others, and reached agreement on a strategy. That strategy led to a reduction in the level of crime on the estate of approximately 85 per cent. However, that reduction would not now be recognised by those who live on the estate. Progress was interrupted by a murder, which damaged police and community work on the estate. Continuity therefore proved difficult to maintain. Moreover, it became clear that the problems of four or five years ago differed significantly from current ones.

In practice, baselines therefore need to be re-established over a reasonable period of time. One needs to go back, ask questions, and re-establish the ways in which improvements can be made within a given local authority area. Indeed, problems need to be analysed at a yet more local level, as was argued this morning. A relevant period of three years is based on lessons learnt from experience. A period of one year is too short. Certainly, the progress made in one year should be measured against the baseline. We would also expect objectives to be set and met within a year, and the general public to be informed of the progress made.

On the other hand, five years is probably too long a period. A great deal can change in five years. The local superintendent of police may move on, or the local chief executive may move on. Moreover, the community will hopefully develop and change, because that is the purpose of the crime and disorder strategy.

Three years is therefore our best estimate. It is based on personal experience and on discussions with those involved in local partnerships. It will not involve turning over stones to discover what is underneath them, or opening the oven door every few minutes to see whether the souffle has risen. Rather, the baseline will be re-established within a reasonable period of time in order to refocus the attention of partners in the wider community on the area's problems. The problems of 2002 may be different in quantity and priority from those of 1999.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: To deal with the amendment and the issues that it raises, it is important for me to explain the aim and the effect of the clause so that the core nature of the counselling requirement is understood. Clause 9 makes provision for courts to impose a new court order called a parenting order. It is designed to help and support parents to control the behaviour of their children by requiring them to attend counselling and guidance sessions and, if necessary, to comply with specific requirements, which the courts may place on parents. The order can be made in a number of situations: when a child safety order, an anti-social behaviour order or a sex offender order is made; when a child or young person has been convicted of a criminal offence, or when parents have been convicted of failing to send their children to school. One of the central tenets of our youth justice reform programme is the need for young offenders and their parents to face up to their offending behaviour and to take responsibility for it. That was one of the main themes in our White Paper entitled ``No More Excuses'', which was published in November.

Parents of young offenders may not directly be to blame for the crimes of their children, but they have a responsibility for providing their children with proper care and control. We believe that, until now, insufficient help or attention has been given to parents to change their children's behaviour. We aim to address that by means of the parenting order.

Research has shown that inadequate parental supervision is associated strongly with offending. For example, a Home Office study showed that 42 per cent. of juveniles who had low or medium levels of parental supervision had offended whereas, for those juveniles who had experienced high levels of parental supervision, the figure was only 20 per cent. The same research showed that the quality of relationship between the parent and the child is absolutely crucial. We know also that parents who are harsh or erratic are twice as likely to have children who offend.

The order will consist of two elements. The first will impose a requirement on parents to attend counselling and guidance sessions where they would receive help in dealing with their children. They would learn, for example, how to set and enforce consistent standards of behaviour and how to respond more effectively to challenging adolescent demands. The sessions would be no more than once a week and up to three months' duration.

However, for those parents who wilfully neglect their responsibilities there will be a second element. The court could impose a requirement on them to exercise control over their children's behaviour. That could include, for example, seeing that their child gets to school every day or ensuring that he or she is home by a certain time of night. That element could last for up to 12 months.

An important feature of the parenting order is that its requirements will be supervised by a responsible officer. That person will have responsibility for, among other things, the provision of counselling and guidance sessions and for ensuring that the parent complies with any other requirements that the court may impose.
