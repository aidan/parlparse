The Minister for Energy and Competitiveness in Europe (Mrs Helen Liddell): I beg to move amendment No. 712, in page 55, line 25, at end insert-

    `( ) No person shall be required by virtue of this section to provide any information which he could not be compelled to give in evidence in civil proceedings in the High Court or, in Scotland, the Court of Session.'.

This is a technical amendment designed to limit the sort of information that electricity suppliers can be required to supply under a renewables order. It is designed to make the procedures in the Bill conform with those in section 28 of the Electricity Act 1989: suppliers cannot be required by an order to supply information that they could not be required to supply in court proceedings. It is clearly appropriate for companies to have that protection. The amendment meets the anxieties that the industry has expressed.

Amendment agreed to.

Mrs Liddell: I congratulate the hon. Member for Lewes (Mr. Baker) on having the good sense not to move amendment No. 749, which was anyway defective. Under section 36 of the Electricity Act 1989, generating stations above 50 MW may not be constructed without the consent of the Secretary of State. Indeed, in section 14 of the Energy Act 1976, the Secretary of State has a similar role for plant fuelled by natural gas or oil of 10 MW or more. The power already exists also for large-scale conventional generation. The amendment would have extended that to small-scale generation, having the opposite effect from that which he wanted to achieve. For example, if he had wished to put a photovoltaic system on to his roof, he would have had to obtain planning permission from the Secretary of State. Therefore I commend, again, his good sense.

In relation to energy efficiency targets, the hon. Gentleman raised some interesting points, such as the distribution of low-energy light bulbs. The Energy Saving Trust Ltd. encourages the use of energy-efficient appliances. There are now a number of schemes designed to increase energy efficiency, particularly for poorer customers. Indeed, on Tuesday, during the break between sittings, I launched one called the neighbourhood energy service team-NEST-makers.

One reason why the Government do not take the line suggested by the hon. Gentleman is that it would restrict their power to assist energy efficiency. A judgment has to be made as to whether demand is likely to grow faster than the establishment of energy-efficient measures. It is impossible to predict demand with that degree of certainty because it depends on many different issues, such as the price of oil, the weather and so on. The provisions of clauses 58 and 79 are more relevant to setting specific targets for energy efficiency of the sort suggested by the hon. Gentleman.

On the hon. Gentleman's points about least-cost planning, it is for the market to decide on the nature of new generating capacity and that is subject to planning constraints. We discussed some of those issues this morning. We address environmental matters through the renewables obligation and the energy efficiency targets in clauses 78 and 79. I should like therefore to take the opportunity of commending cluase 51 to the Committee because it provides that the size of an obligation may vary over time, and the Government intend that that should increase by steps towards the 2010 target.

An order may include or exclude specific power sources or types of generating station as counting towards an obligation. For example, it is well known that the Government intend to excude large-scale hydropower above 10 MW declared net capacity. An order may specify that a minimum amount of electricity must come from a particular source or type of station. The clause further provides for an order to specify how the level of a supplier's obligation is to be determined; how dual-fired generation-stations fuelled by renewables and non-renewables-is to be treated; and the information-gathering powers that the authority will need to establish and monitor the discharge of its obligations by suppliers.

The information powers have an important qualification. A person subject to them may not be required to provide information that he could not be compelled to produce in a court. That mirrors a protection elsewhere in legislation-in section 28(3) of the Electricity Act 1989 for example.

I am glad that the hon. Member for Lewes recognised the difficulty with his amendments, and I commend the clause to the Committee.

Mrs Liddell: I am happy to expand on the provisions of the clause because it is very interesting and important. It paves the way for trading in renewables obligations by making it possible for a supplier to discharge his obligation by arranging for others to supply renewables electricity to customers. Indeed, the ability to do so is a very important way of encouraging the industry. If a supplier under a 10 per cent. obligation cannot in the first instance meet it, but could do so by supplying through another supplier, another supplier that may decide to specialise in renewables generation may be allowed to build up a critical mass of supply.

The clause further supports renewables trading by allowing certificates relating to renewables generation issued by the authority to be accepted as evidence of the discharge of the supplier's obligation. A certificate could be issued to the operator, or via a generating station, on the basis of evidence that is provided to the authority that the generator has generated renewables electricity and that it has been supplied to customers in Great Britain. The clause is a practical manifestation of how we can achieve that. It will allow a true market in renewables certificates to develop, and that can only benefit economic efficiency in the discharge of old renewables obligations-to the benefit of customers and the economy as a whole. The provision is an exciting means of ensuring critical mass in renewable energy generation, and I commend the clause, as amended, to the Committee.

The hon. Gentleman asked when that policy was likely to be lifted. The Secretary of State made it clear that that will happen once NETA is in place. Opposition Members have already asked when we expect NETA to be established, and the answer is October. There are two classic opportunities to introduce it: autumn and spring. We believe that the industry is on target for its introduction in the final three months of this year. The Electricity Association, the main player in the field, has assisted me. I chaired a seminar that gave the industry an opportunity to express its anxieties, and I am assured that the industry is proceeding apace to put the new arrangements in place. It is in the interests of the not only domestic consumers but large industrial consumers to have a plain and transparent market for electricity.

The pool has an inadequate regulatory oversight of its operations. It has had 10 years to address its shortcomings, but it has failed to do so. The new arrangements will increase competition and put downward pressure on electricity prices, remove the distortions that are presently in the pool and level the playing field for both generators and suppliers. They will involve buyers directly in setting prices, which is important to energy users, and will introduce better governance arrangements and proper regulatory oversight. The arrangments will be important to an industry that has long criticised the pool. We shall end up with a much more competitive market that works to the benefit of the consumers.

Given that Conservative Members have said that they support the new electricity trading arrangements, I am surprised by the nature of the amendments tabled by the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton. Amendments Nos. 380, 381 and 383 would narrow the scope of the powers under clause 56, but the provisions, together with the Government amendments, have been drafted carefully precisely to ensure that we have the right scope to introduce the new arrangements. The only purpose that could be served by the changes proposed by Opposition Members would be to put at risk the Government's ability to introduce licence conditions that would ensure the effective implementation of the new arrangements.

Amendment No. 382 would reduce the period in which the powers will be available to one year from two yeras, as in the Bill. The Government intend to introduce the new trading arrangements as soon as possible. I believe that we are on target for the autumn of this year. The amendment would not allow for the possibility that implementation may slip. Such things happen in the real world and it is a prudent provision, but one that we do not anticipate will be needed. It would be undesirable to maintain the power indefinitely, because it would overrule the protections for companies contained in the usual licence modification processes established under the Bill. That is why we have provided that the power may be available only for a limited period. The Government believe that a period of two years is prudent.

In any case, the amendments reflect a fear that somehow the Government will decide to use the powers for some purpose other than introducing new trading arrangements. That fear is completely unfounded. The new arrangements have been the subject of extensive consultation with the industry and with all interested parties. The consultation continues. Yesterday, Ofgem published its consultation document on the proposed scope and content of the connection and the use of system code, which will constitute a new contractual framework for connection to and use of the National Grid Company's transmission system under the new trading arrangements. Members of the Committee have been sent copies of that consultation paper and other relevant papers, including drafts of the proposed licence conditions. Through consultation, everyone knows exactly how we intend to implement the new trading arrangements. I cannot therefore ask the Committee to support amendments Nos. 380 to 383.

Government amendment No. 732 clarifies the scope of the provisions to ensure that we can do everything necessary, in particular with respect to transmission and distribution, to ensure effective implementation of the new arrangements. Government amendment No. 733 deals with the unlikely possibility that we might need to exercise the power after the separation of electricity supply from distribution and the creation of standard electricity licence conditions. Those technical changes match the development of the general provisions on standard licence conditions and will ensure that the standard licence conditions in licences granted subsequently properly reflect the changes to licences under the clause. The amendment provides for the publication of the modifications made to licences under those powers.

I shall now deal with some of the specific points that have been made. The hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton criticised the Government for a lack of consultation, but nothing could be further from the truth. I have referred to the consultations that have taken place. The development of the new electricity trading arrangements has been an extremely open process. Industry and consumer representatives have been heavily involved in shaping the proposals through their participation in the development and implementation steering group, which has met more than 25 times so far, and various other expert groups. In addition, all interested parties have had an opportunity to have an input during the extensive consultation process. All public documents on NETA are available on the Ofgem website, and NETA also has telephone and e-mail help desks.

The hon. Gentleman asks when an order will be published, but there is no order under the clause; there will be new licence conditions. The draft licence conditions have been published in a consultation document. The deadline for comment was 27 March and the Government are considering the responses. The draft balancing and settlement code is due to be published in May. The consultation has been taken into account in that code.

The hon. Gentleman and I have had private discussions, as non-experts, about the complexity of NETA. I am not an expert on electricity generation. I find it complex, and I know that he shares my view, which is why I made the Department's experts available to all members of the Committee. However, we should not frighten ourselves unnecessarily; the majority of electricity will be traded in the bilateral market, which is no more complex than any other market.

I think that the hon. Gentleman acknowledges that delivering electricity in real time is complex, hence the focus on the balancing mechanism. Participation in that part of the market will be voluntary. The balancing mechanism has been widely discussed, and he referred to some of the academic criticisms that have been made. He referred to Derek Bunn of the London business school, who criticised the arrangements, saying that the compulsory market and central dispatch for all generation should be retained because doing so would minimise system costs. However, the compulsory market makes collusion much easier, which can increase prices and reduce the pressures on electricity suppliers to decrease prices.

Derek Bunn's other criticism was that auction was better than pay as bid, as proposed under NETA. However, a single clearing price is incompatible with a non-compulsory market such as NETA, although prices in bilateral trades are likely to converge towards the marginal costs of generation. A uniform price in a compulsory market is much more susceptible to manipulation-one of the main criticisms of the pool. A uniform price mechanism may be unable to provide appropriate differential rewards for flexible and inflexible plant.

It has been suggestedthat NETA will fail to deliver price reductions. That suggestion is unfounded. A business simulation model has been created as part of the NETA programme. That model suggests that the NETA proposals should lead to a much more competitive market and lower electricity prices, as we expect.

Even beyond the modelling exercise, there is growing evidence, in nominal terms and because the market is expecting the introduction of NETA, of future prices of 14 per cent. below those at the time of the White Paper's publication. NETA represents a significant shift towards real-time pricing. The pool prices are set 24 hours ahead. Under NETA, prices will continue to evolve until three and a half hours before real time, and prices in the balancing mechanism will not be known until after the event. That is full, real-time pricing.

Some academics disagree with NETA. We know where they are coming from, but others agree with it. It is like consulting a lawyer: ``You pays your money, you takes your choice.''
