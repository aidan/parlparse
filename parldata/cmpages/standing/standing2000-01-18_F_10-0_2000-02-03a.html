Mr. Rooker: I beg to move amendment No. 44, in page 75, line 39, at end insert--

    '6. The Secretary of State may by regulations provide for the application, in connection with child support maintenance payable following a variation, of paragraph 7(2) to (7) of Schedule 1 (subject to any prescribed modifications).'.

Unlike many of the Government amendments, amendment No. 44 is not technical. It relates to applications for a variation from the normal rules by which child support maintenance will be calculated, and inserts a new paragraph into part II of schedule 4B of the Child Support Act 1991. The amendment forges an essential link between two key elements of the reformed scheme: variations and shared care.

Part I of schedule 1 of the 1991 Act, as substituted under clause 1, specifies the normal rules by which the weekly amount of child support maintenance will be calculated. Paragraph 7 of part I details the manner in which the liability may be adjusted when the care of the child is shared. New schedule 4A contains detailed provisions supplementing the rules governing applications for variations in new section 28A under clause 5. New schedule 4B specifies the cases and circumstances in which a variation may be allowed, and the regulatory controls governing the operation of the variations scheme.

As the Bill stands, the shared care rules may be applied only to cases in which the non-resident parent is liable to pay the basic reduced rate. They may not be applied when, as a result of a successful variation application, the non-resident parent is treated as having more--or less--income than would otherwise have been the case. The amendment corrects that omission by introducing a further regulation-making power in Schedule 4B which will allow the shared care rules to be applied, with prescribed modifications, to cases in which a variation of the normal rules by which the maintenance calculation is made has been agreed.

Without the amendment, two key elements of the new scheme would be prevented from working in tandem, as it would not be possible to apply the shared care provision to cases which are the subject of a variation. I am happy that I had notes to put that on the record clearly and openly.

Mr. Paul Burstow (Sutton and Cheam): I beg to move amendment No. 76, in page 10, line 43, at end insert

    ', save that the appeal tribunal shall consist of three members one of which must be a legally qualified member and the tribunal shall, if practicable, include at least one member who is of the same sex as the appellant.'.

I should like briefly to outline the purpose of the amendment. It would add to the end of clause 10(4)(b) a provision whereby, if practicable and possible, child support tribunals would include someone of the same sex as the appellant.

Until June 1999, child support tribunals consisted of three members, of whom at least one had to be the same sex as the appellant, if that was practicable--we have retained that caveat because we believe that it is still necessary. However, the Social Security Act 1998 changed the situation. The only stipulation relating to tribunals now is that they must include one legally qualified member. The amendment would provide that a child support appeal tribunal must consist of three members, one of whom must be legally qualified--as now--and at least one must, if practicable, be of the same sex as the appellant. The reason for this goes back to some of our previous debates, particularly in respect of the very sensitive issues with which the appeal tribunals will be dealing, not least that of good cause for not co-operating with the agency, which was discussed in Committee on Tuesday.

We understand that the Government are currently carrying out extensive research and evaluation into the perceptions of customers of the Child Support Agency. We want to know whether that includes their experiences of the appeal process, particularly how appellants felt if the tribunal did not include a person of the same sex. It would be useful to have objective evidence on that. Even if the Government are unwilling to accept the amendment, we hope that they will be willing to include within their evaluation process a means by which such evidence could be gathered, so that a rational and sensible assessment can be made of whether the change could be made in the future.

We appreciate that the words in the amendment may not exactly give effect to what we are seeking, but the principle is a sound and important one and would ensure that justice is not only done, but is seen to be done.

Our proposals build on changes to the appeals system for child support which were introduced from June 1999. During the passage of the Social Security Act 1998, there were lengthy and detailed debates about tribunal composition. Amendment No. 76 would provide for appeals tribunals to comprise three members, one of whom should be legally qualified and one of whom should be the same sex as the appellant. That would take us back to the position that existed before the 1998 Act was passed.

It is less than a year since the administrative changes were made to the appeals system in June 1999, so it is rather odd to say that only one of five different tribunals run by the Department of Social Security should be taken back to the previous position. That does not make sense. Hon. Members may recall that the 1998 Act allowed for the creation of two-member and one-member appeal tribunals. Clearly, a one-member appeal tribunal cannot have a gender balance--to imagine that is beyond my ingenuity. I remind Committee members of why the changes were made. When we came into government, the average length of time for an appeal to be heard was seven months, but many people waited for well over a year for their appeal to be heard. We examined carefully why backlogs were developing and the system was clogging up. We found that one reason was the rigid requirement that all tribunals should have three members. Gathering together in one place three busy people who have other jobs often takes time. The requirement for three people to sit for a tribunal to be properly constituted was one of the main reasons for delays, hence the move to allow one and two-member tribunals.

The president of the appeals tribunal, whose position is an independent judicial appointment, has followed closely the results of the introduction of the new, more flexible appeals. Appeal delays have been reduced to an average of 16 weeks. Appeals are being heard much more quickly and expeditiously, so one of the main alms of the new system is being achieved as we speak. Much of the backlog has been dealt with.

When the 1998 Act was in Committee, the Opposition expressed concern that changes to composition would affect the outcome, that the new arrangements were a way of getting appeals through quickly as that they could be dismissed. Let me tell them that the success rate of the CSA CSA three-person tribunal hearings prior to the 1998 Act was 57.5 per cent. and that the one-person tribunal hearings that have replaced them have a success rate of 57.8 per cent. Therefore outcomes have not changed. There is no obvious trend of more of these appeals being dismissed by one-member tribunals than by three-member tribunals. As we have one-member tribunals we clearly cannot ensure a gender balance, but we can--I give the Committee a pledge--try to ensure that the pool of people from which the judge can appoint tribunals to hear these cases contains equal numbers of men and women.

We are trying to recruit more women to serve in the tribunal service; they currently stand at 35 per cent. The hon. Member for Beckenham (Mrs. Lait) was right that there are a lot of women, but it is still not 50 per cent. Our clear aspiration is to have 50 per cent., but 35 per cent. is the same as the current proportion in the legal profession so we have achieved a sort of balance. I hope that I can reassure the hon. Member for Sutton and Cheam that the president of the appeals tribunal takes the issue seriously. He has agreed to ensure that adequate equal opportunities training on some of these sensitive issues is given to everyone who serves on the child support appeal tribunal.

It seeks to provide that the criminal offence of failing to supply information under clause 12 will not apply to an alleged parent who has reasonably refused to take a DNA test intended to establish parentage. We have established that, in the vast majority of cases, there is not a great deal that is reasonable about denying parentage.

Clause 12 makes it an offence for anyone to fail to provide information or give false information to the CSA, with a fine of up to £1,000 on conviction. Those new criminal sanctions will ensure that more non-resident parents meet their obligations to their children and show that the Government are committed to the radical reform of child support.

In the current scheme, disputing paternity is a way of delaying the payment of child maintenance. That is why, through clause 14, we intend to allow the CSA to presume that a man is the father of the child when he has refused to take a scientific test aimed at establishing parentage. Thus, a refusal to take such a test will not normally stop the child support process. We also intend that presumption to apply where the child is living in England and Wales and the alleged father was married to the child's mother at any time between the date of conception and the child's birth--following an existing presumption in Scottish law--or where the alleged father 301 302 is named on the child's birth certificate. In the vast majority of cases--there are always exceptions, as the hon. Member for Brentwood and Ongar amusingly illustrated--any man who is presumed to be the father of a child has the right to apply to the courts for that presumption to be rebutted if he can prove his case.

This is a permissive presumption. If the Secretary of State thinks that there is a reasonable reason, such as religious grounds, for an alleged parent to refuse to take a DNA test, he would not assume that that alleged parent was the child's father. In other words, we do not assume that a refusal to take a DNA test is in all circumstances proof or a proxy of proof of parentage. In such circumstances, we would instead go to court for a declaration of parentage and the Secretary of State would not pursue information until the court had established parentage. Thus, in those situations action under clause 12 would not be appropriate.

Clause 12 is designed to make it a criminal offence to fail to provide the CSA with the information required to calculate or collect child support or to give it misleading information. It is not intended to settle disputes about parentage. Clause 14 deals with that. As I said, clauses 61 and 62 also modernise related provisions in existing law.

If a non-resident parent refuses to take a DNA test without good reason, he will be presumed to be the parent, and the Secretary of State will take action to ensure that the necessary information is provided. If it is not, criminal action may be taken.

These provisions toughen up the existing situation following the CSA's experience in dealing with denials of parentage over the past few years. We think that they are fair and reasonable, so I ask the Committee to oppose the amendment and support the clause.
