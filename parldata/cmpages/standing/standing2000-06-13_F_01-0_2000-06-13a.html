Dr. Burnett: Did I hear the Minister correctly? Dr. Howells: As the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon knows, the liability of members of a limited liability partnership was discussed in the other place. Lord McIntosh of Haringey made clear the Government's intention to rely on the effect of general law to provide for the liability of negligent members of a limited liability partnership rather than making specific statutory provision.

It would be undesirable and potentially dangerous to amend the law of negligence by a side wind, as it is obviously of wider application than to limited liability partnerships. The explanatory notes to the Bill state that a limited liability partnership's existence as a corporate entity means that the effect of the general law on members of a limited liability partnership is different from its effect on the partners in a partnership. Should a partner be negligent in the work that he carries out for a client, there will be generally be two possible causes of action against him—contract and tort. However, because a limited liability partnership will be a separate legal entity, if the client is contracted with the limited liability partnership itself, only the tort action is potentially available against the member.

Concerns raised in the other place focused also on the fact that recent developments in case law might make it less likely that a case could be proved against a negligent member of a limited liability partnership whose conduct had resulted in an economic loss for the client. A wronged third party would still be able to pursue the limited liability partnership. That puts third parties in a similar position to clients of a company, but in a somewhat different position from clients of a partnership. We have considered the considerable complexities of the matter carefully, and we have concluded that we have achieved an appropriate balance.

Employees of a limited liability partnership will be in the same position as employees in a solicitor's partnership. If they personally do something fraudulent, they will be liable; but not otherwise.

I shall try to explain what is meant by a designated member. It is intended that a limited liability partnership will have at least two designated members who will carry out a number of administrative functions: for example, taking responsibility for communications with Companies House and—importantly—the approval and signing of the annual accounts, filing of the accounts at Companies House, and, in the event of insolvency proceedings, providing any statement setting out the affairs of the limited liability partnership, assets, debts and liabilities. Mr. Fabricant: That is a helpful clarification. Would the Minister say that a designated member is almost equivalent to a company secretary in an ordinary plc or limited company?

Dr. Howells: The designated member will carry out some of the statutory obligations that company secretaries carry out.

The limited liability partnership's incorporation document will set out who will be the designated members fulfilling the functions I have just outlined. A limited liability partnership must have at least two designated members. If no members are specified as designated in the incorporation document, all the members will be considered to be designated members.

A number of the powers assigned to a company secretary under the Companies Act 1985 will be transferred to a designated member; for example, the signing of the annual return. Other responsibilities of designated members echo those of the company secretary, as the hon. Member for Lichfield point out. A definition will not make the role of a designated member any clearer than the current provisions in the Bill and in regulations. We have followed the format adopted in the Companies Act, which imposes duties and powers on officers of the company, but gives no single definition of the role of an officer of the company.

Mr. Fabricant: Would it be as easy for the designated member to be changed if circumstances altered within the partnership as it is to register another company secretary, which is achieved simply by filling out a form and filing it with Companies House?

Dr. Howells: Yes. As I made clear, the limited liability partnership incorporation document is intended to specify the identity of the designated members. It does not prohibit changes to those members, but any change requires the agreement of other members, which means that consultation and discussion must occur within the partnership. If the document does not stipulate that particular named members are designated, all the members will become designated. The Registrar of Companies must be informed when the designated members change. The notice has to be made in a form that is approved by the registrar and must be signed or authenticated by a designated member. When a person stops being a member of the limited liability partnership, he or she will automatically stop being a designated member.

If a partnership agreement, which links different members within a limited liability partnership, is silent on an issue, or if there is no such agreement between members and the limited liability partnership, should the law that governs the relationship between members of a limited liability partnership default to the body of partnership law in the Partnership Act 1890 and case law of the past century? The majority opinion of the Law Society and others was that there should be such a provision. Indeed, the first draft of what was clause 6(1)(c) stated that the rights and duties of members of a limited liability partnership shall be governed

    subject to paragraphs (a) and (b), by the rules and principles which would apply if the law relating to partnerships applied to them.

That paragraph, which forms the basis of amendment No. 6, was removed by the Government for the reasons set out in paragraphs 6 to 10 of their February 2000 consultation document. The consultation document says:

The former member or his representative would often not want to continue entitlement, but would prefer to have his share in the firm bought out once and for all. That might also be favoured by the limited liability partnership, which otherwise would face a continuing liability.

Mr. Burnett: What is wrong with future payments if they form part of a pre-arranged agreement between members of a limited liability partnership? Such payments to an individual who is bankrupt would simply go to the trustee in bankruptcy.

Dr. Howells: I am not saying that there is anything wrong with that, but the amendment replaces the problematic phrase with one that refers to the right to receive an amount. That will ensure that a buy-out provision in the members agreement is not hindered by the clause.

Mr. Burnett: I rise to speak to amendment No. 5. There has been much discussion between the Law Society and the Department of Trade and Industry on clause 7, in particular on the arrangements that members of a limited liability partnership can make between themselves when someone ceases to be a member. Government amendment No. 1 is a distinct improvement and as such is welcome. It permits members of a limited liability partnership to determine the financial rights of an individual's successor, such as his personal representatives. That resolves the blatant unfairness of invalidating a buy-out provision in an agreement between members of a limited liability partnership. The amendment cures that defect.

Nevertheless, the clause as amended will invalidate an agreement that was freely entered into by members of a limited liability partnership, which entitles a non-member to interfere in its management or administration. At first sight, that may sound reasonable because a non-member does not owe the same duties to a limited liability partnership and because it can be argued that a non-member has no right to interfere in the business. However, it is a most undesirable restriction of the freedom to contract. If the members of a limited liability partnership wish to confer a right to interfere in the management on a non-member, it is reasonable to assume that the members regard such a move as being in their interests and therefore in the interests of the limited liability partnership.

I shall give two examples. Two members, one of whom provides a loan to the limited liability partnership for its working capital, form a limited liability partnership. The loan is secured by a floating charge. The lender dies and the limited liability partnership is unable to pay the loan when it falls due. Clause 7 appears to prevent the personal representatives of the deceased member from appointing a receiver or otherwise enforcing the charge because that would constitute interference in the management or administration of the limited liability partnership. The courts may reach a different conclusion, but it is an area of uncertainty.

My second example may be simpler and more compelling. A limited liability partnership is formed by two members and both members die simultaneously. Under clause 7, their personal representatives would be precluded from interfering in the management of the limited liability partnership. The amendment would address that problem if an agreement between the members provided expressly otherwise or the court implied an agreement to the contrary. I understand that the DTI objects to the amendment on the grounds that a limited liability partnership might be managed by a person who is not a member and who did not owe the limited liability partnership the same duties as a member. However, the draft regulations prepared by the DTI, which are to be made under the Bill when it becomes law, apply to limited liability partnerships the provisions of section 741 of the Companies Act 1985. In doing so, they substitute for the references in the section to director and shadow director references to member and shadow member. That means that a person who occupies the position of a member will be treated as a member under the law. People, in accordance with whose directions or instructions the members of a limited liability partnership are accustomed to act, will be effectively members. They will be shadow members, but will be treated as members.

 

Mr. Fabricant: When the hon. Gentleman talks about members being those with whom other members are accustomed to act in accordance, is he not echoing how the Companies Act views directors? A director need not be registered at Companies House. The law deems a director as being anybody according to whom other directors are accustomed to act.

Mr. Burnett: The hon. Gentleman makes a powerful point. That is what the Companies Act provides for in certain circumstances. The Taxes Acts make the same provisions.

The amendment is not a means by which liability can be avoided because the Bill and the regulations under the Bill will address that problem. The Bill and the regulations, are sufficient to protect the limited liability partnership from the actions of somebody who is not expressly a member, but who has the right to interfere in the management or administration of its business.

The Bill does not attempt to preclude a limited liability partnership from employing a manager who is not a member in other circumstances. For example, a professional manager can be recruited to manage a limited liability partnership even though he has no interest in it. I hope that the Minister will give thought to that point, which is important for continuity, certainty and the smooth management of the transition between partnerships and limited liability partnerships.

Dr. Howells: In drafting the clause we sought a balance between the interests of the ex-member or his representatives and the limited liability partnership. The clause would prohibit the ex-member or his representatives from taking part in the management of the limited liability partnership. The intention is to minimise the disruption to the limited liability partnership by the transfer of a member's interests.

Mr. Burnett: Will the Minister give way?

Mr. Howells: In a moment. If I may, I shall deal with some of the important points raised by the hon. Gentleman.

The clause is designed to reflect the fact that the limited liability partnership will continue as a going concern, managed by the continuing members, without interference from an ex-member or his representatives.

Mr. Burnett: I think that the Minister is suggesting that real problems might arise, but is he aware that under current partnership law a partnership agreement can make arrangements with respect to outgoing partners or their personal representatives subsequently having certain rights in the management of the firm? That has not prejudiced the existence of partnership, the partnership ethos or the success of partnerships.

Dr. Howells: The provision is by no means unique. Section 31 of the Partnership Act 1890 states that an assignment of a partner's share

    does not . . . entitle the assignee . . . to interfere in the management or administration of the partnership business or affairs.

As to companies, if a director dies, his personal representatives cannot manage the business of the company.

Mr. Burnett: Surely a partnership agreement can contract out of that.

Dr. Howells: As I have explained, we are attempting to continue with that flexibility in arrangements between partners.

During consultation on the draft Bill a variety of views were expressed. Some argued that a liquidator or the trustee of a bankrupt should not be entitled to intervene in the management or affairs of the limited liability partnership and some suggested that the clause should be subordinate to what was agreed with the ex-member before his departure. On balance, we decided that it would be prudent to leave the provision undisturbed. It does not seem to be in the interest of the limited liability partnership—which, it should be remembered, for there seems to be some confusion about it, is a legal entity separate from its members—that members should be able to reach agreement allowing an ex-member or his representatives to interfere in the management or administration of the limited liability partnership. For example, if that third party had a controlling interest it might not be in his interest for the limited liability partnership to continue trading. He would not have to have regard to the interest of the clients.

The original version of the clause, published in 1998, went further than the present one, in that it would have prevented an ex-member from requiring any accounts from the transactions of the limited liability partnership and from inspecting its books. It required him to accept the account of profits agreed by the members. There was general agreement that the restrictions were too great and clause 7 takes account of those views.

The amount that an ex-member could receive from a limited liability partnership was restricted to a share of the profits to which he would otherwise have been entitled. That restriction was removed in the light of consultation, ensuring that a member is free to contract with the limited liability partnership to receive a capital sum in the event of his ceasing to be a member, for whatever reason. In addition, if a member has loaned money to the limited liability partnership during his lifetime, the loan will constitute an asset in the hands of his personal representatives and a debt to the limited liability partnership. The personal representatives of the deceased member will be able to enforce such a debt against the limited liability partnership. That is why I ask the hon. Gentleman not to press his amendment.

Mr. Burnett: On the latter point, it may not be in the interests of the lender or the business to ask for repayment of a loan, say, on death. I am not clear what the Minister means by suggesting that clients could be prejudiced by the desire of a majority member to wind down the business of the firm. That is a matter for the members and for the agreement between them. Nevertheless, the Minister said that the Government wanted to retain the sort of flexibility that is provided by existing partnership law and, not least, the Partnership Act 1890.

My amendment would provide that flexibility. It is, more or less, a probing amendment, so I shall ask leave to withdraw it. I hope, however, that the Minister will consider it carefully and think again, in the spirit of his own words, about retaining the partnership flexibility that has been so valuable in the past.
