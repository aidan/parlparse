Question again proposed.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): At the end of our previous sitting, I was in the middle of asking the Government about those who will be running the consultation scheme. I was prompted to do so by having read about the width of the powers and the scope of the responsibilities of the scheme manager. For example, under clause 188(3), the measures that the scheme manager may take in relation to insurance companies include giving them assistance to enable them to continue insurance business. Thus, an ailing or bankrupt insurance company may be given funds so that it can continue in being, which could be an enormous continuing liability. The judgments that will have to be made will be difficult, controversial and far-reaching.

Will those starting the scheme, formerly the scheme manager in the corporate sense, be authorised persons and have to undergo the necessary procedures to obtain permission to operate in such a way? The decisions that will have to be made will be akin to market decisions and judgments. A great deal of money will be at stake and the implications could be profound for both the stability of the market and the security of investors. What procedures will those people need to go through to be registered for such business?

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): The right hon. Gentleman is right. Some subtle judgments will have to be made. The existing arrangements with the policyholders protection board work in the same way, so the judgments will need to be made currently by those who are carrying out that work.

I shall explain why such an approach has been taken. Usually, policyholders and the compensation scheme are at a significant disadvantage in the event of liquidation, because of inevitable delays, disruption for policyholders and the extra costs associated with liquidation. The administrative costs of liquidation can be extremely high and higher than the alternative arrangements that can be made under section 425 of the Companies Act 1985. I refer to the Kingscroft Insurance Company, the Walbrook Insurance Company, the El Paso Insurance Company, the Lime Street Insurance Company and the Mutual Reinsurance Company—the KWELM companies—all of which had provisional liquidators appointed to them in 1992. The additional administrative costs incurred as a result of liquidation amounted to £60 million.

The general view is that it would be in everyone's interest if it were possible to avoid liquidation, not to facilitate the companies carrying on as normal, but to ensure that there is a more orderly run-off and, if possible, that the liabilities for existing policies are honoured. I understand from the latest accounts of the policyholders protection board that about 14 companies are currently on the books. The problems faced by policy holders will be minimised as will the liability on the compensation scheme.

The people involved will require significant skills, as do those doing it now. We are aiming for the same thing, even though the FSA rather than the policyholders protection board will do the work. I do not know, but I imagine that a number of the same individuals will do the work under the new arrangements. The FSA is consulting on a proposal to set up management committees for each of the sectoral sub-schemes, of which this will be one. In so doing, it will be aware of the need for relevant expertise and continuity between existing schemes.
