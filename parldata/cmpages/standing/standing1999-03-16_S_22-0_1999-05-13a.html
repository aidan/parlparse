Mr. James Clappison (Hertsmere): I beg to move amendment No. 422, in page 60, line 4, at beginning insert—

    `(1) In this Part an ``immigration officer'' means an immigration officer who is authorised by the Secretary of State to exercise the powers conferred by this Part and the Secretary of State shall not so authorise an immigration officer unless he is satisfied that the immigration officer has received such training as the Secretary of State considers to be appropriate'.

We now come to a new part of the Bill and a new subject—the powers of immigration officers. The Bill will extend some of their existing powers—for example, to arrest for certain offences for which they currently do not have a power of arrest and take them into new areas. In many cases they are marginal extensions of existing powers, but in some cases officers will be dealing with sensitive matters. All members of the Committee will be aware that immigration matters often have a sensitive background and require careful handling.

We fully support the important work of immigration officers in enforcing immigration law, but it is also part of our job to ensure that proper safeguards are in place to enable the powers to be used properly. That brings me to the subject of the amendment, which requires that immigration officers receive proper training to exercise their powers. The amendment gives the Minister an opportunity to tell us about the Government's plans for such training in regard to present and new powers.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department (Mr. Mike O'Brien): The Government's intention is to ensure effective enforcement of our immigration laws. One way in which that has been done up to now is by the removal of people who have no continuing right to be in the United Kingdom. The police usually accompany immigration officers to wherever the person is who is to be removed and assist the immigration service in removing him. However, increasingly, the police merely stand by because there is no problem while removal is effected, and it is a substantial draw on police resources.

We want to increase the effectiveness of removal and the number removed. For many years, large numbers of people have been told that they will be removed, but the resources have not been available to effect removal. We want to increase the resources available to the immigration service to effect removals. However, that will mean increased pressure on the police to provide officers to join the immigration officers in carrying out removals.

Some police chiefs are reluctant to provide substantial numbers of officers, sometimes at short notice, to effect removals, and the officers often have no role to play other than to stand by and see whether anything transpires that might give them a role—if the person resists, obstructs or seeks to do something that he should not do.

The aim of the clause is threefold. First, it makes clearer the current powers that immigration officers have and are trained to use. Secondly, it makes marginal changes to the powers as a result of changes in the law, and those need not detain the Committee. Thirdly, and most important, in limited circumstances it will enable immigration officers to effect removal without the presence of police officers.

We currently intend to train only about 20 immigration officers to carry out these tasks and we envisage that that small team will, in circumstances to which I shall come in a moment, carry out the removals without the presence of a police officer. It is a sensitive area that we intend to approach with caution. The immigration officers do not want to go into situations where they may be required to exceed their powers; however, they want to be sure that they can carry out removals effectively.

We shall provide substantial training to the team of immigration officers that will take on these new responsibilities and exercise the new powers. They will work initially with the police, but will be able to use the new powers in joint operations with the police. In due course they will carry out removals without the attendance of police officers in cases where all the evidence suggests that no problem or difficulty is likely to arise. If a difficulty arose—no one can guarantee that one never will—the immigration officers would have powers to deal with it. However, it is envisaged that, in the long term, if a difficulty is expected to arise, the police will be present. Where immigration officers have reason to believe that there will be difficulty in effecting a removal, they will not be expected to do so without the presence of police officers. The new powers will be used where there is unlikely, in the intelligence estimate, to be a need for force. Most removals are effected with relative ease, although, as I have said, there is no guarantee that that will always be the case.

The amendment raises important issues. I do not believe that there is much disagreement about the principles involved. The immigration officers are warranted under the Immigration Act 1971 and receive training in all the legislation that they are required to administer. The directors of the immigration service authorise warrants. There is no question that immigration officeres will be authorised by the director to exercise the new powers conferred on them by this part of the Bill without comprehensive and appropriate training.

Considerable financial provision of £200,000 for each of the next five years has already been made for the estimated cost, and that is reflected in the financial effects of the Bill. A training package is currently being developed in consultation with the police, with the aim that immigration officers will be able to exercise their powers with sensitivity and restraint, relying on interpersonal skills to resolve conflict. There will be no widespread introduction of these powers until training has been fully validated. Only around 20 officers will be trained intially, and they will work alongside the police in a pilot scheme designed to test the powers before they are introduced more widely. However, the immigration service will continue to call on police support where the risk assessment is that they should, for example in particularly sensitive cases in certain localities and in larger operations. It will also keep the police informed when it is carrying out operations, so that, even if the police are not present, they are aware that the operations are being carried out.

The pilot scheme would allow working practices and training to be evaluated and fine-tuned and for the views of community groups to be considered. Immigration officers are not novices in this field. The immigration service has been working alongside the police for many years, and those working in enforcement already operate under the safeguards of the Police and Criminal Evidence Act 1984 and are familiar with the procedures. I do not, therefore, consider it necessary to place a commitment to training in the Bill. I hope that we can show the Committee that the training for these new powers will be properly addressed and authorised.

When I read the amendment from the hon. Member for Hertsmere (Mr. Clappison) yesterday, it provoked a lively discussion between myself and senior immigration officers. Although none of us felt that this version of the amendment was appropriate, we may want to draw out some of the points that it raises. The measure codifies many of the powers that immigration officers already have and adds to them for some officers. I agree with the principle that only those officers who are properly trained should be allowed to exercise the powers of search, entry and seizure but the amendment encompasses existing powers, in addition to new ones, and therefore needs further thought.

I want time to look at the amendment because one or two of its suggestions may deserve further consideration and enable us to introduce additional safeguards to ensure that the new powers in the Bill can be used as we intend and are not spread further. It must be clear that the only officers who can exercise the powers are those who are authorised to do so under the rules of the immigration service.

We shall reconsider the ideas behind the amendment; I do not want to include training in the Bill, but there may be a way to authorise groups of officers to use the powers that go beyond those already exercised by immigration officers. With that undertaking, I hope that the hon. Gentleman will ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Mike O'Brien: I am afraid that I cannot support the amendments. I am aware that they were suggested in the evidence that we received from the ILPA, but I do not think that they are necessary.

Amendment No. 958 would remove the power of arrest without warrant when someone is suspected of committing an offence under the new section 24A of the 1971 Act, which will be introduced by clause 16 of this Bill. The new section 24A will be made up of two provisions. First, it will repeat the existing law under which it is an offence to obtain, or seek to obtain, leave to enter or remain by deception.
