The Bill is important because it touches on various crucially important parts of our national life. That is why it must given a fair wind. Labour Members were assiduous in scrutinising the National Lottery etc. Act 1993, and we shall do the same with this Bill in Committee.

I welcome the two Liberal Democrat Members to the Committee, although only one of them is here now. I hope that they will listen carefully to the debates on the amendments that we shall move. We shall, of course, consider sympathetically any amendments that they may move. I hope that we can find common ground.

Our approach is essentially based on the determination to preserve the best aspects of the national lottery, which has become something of a national institution. However, I have reread the long speech that the right hon. Member for Caithness, Sutherland and Easter Ross (Mr. Maclennan) made on Second Reading, and I am concerned because he seemed to get lost in the woods. He found himself in a quandary and said in conclusion that he could neither support the Bill nor vote against it. I hope that, if possible, Liberal Democrat Members will clarify their position further.

It is right that the Committee should consider the Bill fully. On Second Reading, we made it clear that we take issue with the Government on several important matters. We hope that during these sittings we can persuade them to modify their stance. In doing so, I hope that they will ensure that the credibility of the national lottery, which has been such an enormous success and is widely supported by the public, can be enhanced rather than endangered, which is my fear if the Bill is not amended. We hope to persuade the Government of the wisdom of our views.

We seek to defend the principles that have been essential to the national lottery's success, including, absolutely set in stone, the arm's length principle between the Government and the national lottery and its distribution mechanisms, and the principle of additionality. Our central objection to the Bill is that the national lottery is being effectively turned not into the people's lottery, as was suggested so disingenuously in the White Paper, but into the Government's lottery. That is why we need time to consider such matters in Committee.

An unprecedented array of powers for the Secretary of State is proposed. The Bill flies in the face of the arm's length principle on which the lottery was set up and it threatens to undermine the independence of the lottery and reduce it to a form of voluntary taxation.

The national lottery has been a considerable success, and I pay tribute to the nobility of purpose of the original founders of the national lottery--namely, my right hon. Friend the Member for Huntingdon (Mr. Major), the former Prime Minister, who set up the lottery with true integrity of purpose, and my right hon. Friends the Members for Cities of London and Westminster (Mr. Brooke) and for South-West Surrey (Mrs. Bottomley).

In contrast to what happened when we were in government, the Opposition are concerned that, under the Bill, the lottery will increasingly become the Government's lottery and that, in particular, the new opportunities fund will increasingly become the Secretary of State's fund. Given the eloquence with which my right hon. Friend the Member for Horsham (Mr. Maude) made the Opposition's case, it was almost unnecessary to listen to the many other speeches on Second Reading.

To understand the import of the Bill, it is sufficient to recall the procession of pork barrel supplications by members of the Government, each commending the Government for finally getting to grips with the lottery and each earnestly hoping that the Secretary of State would roll a log towards his or her constituency. Our fundamental objection to the Bill is that it undermines the principle of additionality upon which the lottery was founded and which has made it such a success. In justifying the Bill, the Secretary of State said that there would be no breach of that principle and the Prime Minister is quoted in the White Paper, ``The People's Lottery'' as saying:

    ``We don't believe it would be right to use Lottery money to pay for things which are the Government's responsibilities.''

That principle is correct, but is undermined by the Bill. That is why we need to give it adequate consideration.

Despite their protestations, Ministers cannot be under the illusion that the additionality principle is not being flouted; as so many commentators, including Peter Riddell who is not automatically opposed to the Government, have pointed out. It is unhealthy for the lottery to fund services such as health and education, which are core Government responsibilities. The Bill flouts the spirit on which the lottery was founded, which was bi-partisan and achieved remarkable consensus across the political divide. We shall table amendments that seek to preserve the principle of additionality--a principle that the Government supported when they were in opposition, at the time of the original lottery Bill. We hope that on further reflection they will support the amendments.

In their manifesto, the Government said that they were not satisfied with the way in which lottery money was being distributed, and the Bill is their solution to that. It will allow the Secretary of State to take direct control of up to 80 per cent. of lottery proceeds and spend them as he or other members of the Government see fit. That is why the Bill needs to be considered with care. All Labour Members made it clear on Second Reading that they understood that. They want the Secretary of State empowered to react to special pleading. Part of the political agenda underlying the Bill is an attack on the arm's-length principle. That principle has had a hard time of it of late; it has been more honoured in the breach than the observance. Our amendments also seek to highlight that.

We believe that the Bill is the thin end of the wedge and that if it is passed in its present form, it will increase the Secretary of State's powers in a way that would undermine the foundations of the success of the national lottery. The Department of National Heritage was given charge of the lottery--rather than the Treasury--so that the principles of arm's length and additionality could be maintained. The Bill breaks that relationship.

Stategic plans, initiatives specified by order and the power to divert more money to Government purposes lie behind the Bill. It would be more honest if the Government increased the amount of money taken from the lottery in taxation and left the original good causes intact; and that would be less costly to administer. The real good causes will now have a smaller share of the money.

I understand the Government's embarrassment on education and health. In the largest town in my constituency, ambulance cover has recently been withdrawn. In the past six months, waiting lists in Suffolk have doubled. That has come about under a Government who made expansive promises about 14 days to save the NHS. Similar changes have occurred in education. Higher education has been attacked, teachers have been sacked and class sizes have increased despite the cry of ``Education, education, education''.

Every hon. Member knows perfectly well that that is happening. The Bill is an attempt to fill a gap in public perception. It is slowly dawning on people that Government policies are wholly unsatisfactory in practice in those specific regards.

The good causes are simply losing out. It is not just me who is saying that. Lord Rothschild made the same point in another place. We have had correspondence with the Sports Council and many others who made assumptions about spending plans that are now under threat and attack because the money will be diverted. That is why the Opposition are tabling amendments to limit the damage to good causes by postponing the introduction of the New Opportunities Fund.

As it has been said, the Bill is a £1 billion raid on the lottery. It entirely destroys the basis on which the lottery was set up, and on which it maintains its public credibility. The Opposition plan to press the Government hard to commit themselves and to give specific assurances about the percentages that they will guarantee for the five original good causes. We seek clarification and assurance because the matter is crucial to the sustained credibility of the lottery. The Secretary of State persists in saying that the good causes will get the money that was originally promised. They will not get their promised share, as the Government want a share--it is as simple as that.

The Opposition look forward to pressing those points, and to hearing the Government's answers. We look forward to testing the strength of their commitment to their election promises and to some of the principles that they espoused when the lottery was introduced. We make no apology for tabling amendments considered in another place. The arguments were advanced and rejected, but we believe that the Government were incoherent and confused. We will seek clarification.
