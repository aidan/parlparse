My third point, on which I do not intend to dilate even if you were so minded to allow me, Mrs. Dunwoody, is that I presume that all members of the Committee--certainly Opposition Members, and I expect most Labour Members--are interested in those who are or may be in receipt of low wages. In the pre-war period, my father was very pleased to be able to pay his workers a shilling a week over the minimum, which was something like 28 shillings a week. We do not want people to be in receipt of low wages. We want them to have jobs that pay them well. That brings me to some of the equivocations in the Bill, which will be the subject of legitimate criticism and probing. I hope, and intend, that it will be constructive criticism that is designed to draw out what is proposed. It is clear that the whole thing could be a void for uncertainty, as lawyers would say--although I am not a lawyer, thank goodness.

It is no accident that the decision-making process has been reversed, although the Minister claimed that as a virtue. It is extraordinary that the Bill and our debates on it come before the recommendations of George Bain's Low Pay Commission on a rate--putting the cart before the horse has become almost an art form. Those of us who once studied classical languages will know that the precise meaning of the word ``preposterous'' is backwards-frontwards. That is exactly the impact we fear from the Bill. It is no accident that that is how the matter is arranged, because it will shut down debate on the likely costs and impact. We cannot know precisely what they will be until we are given the figures. In the days of yore--and even not so yore--when there was a Conservative Government, the Department of Trade and Industry was able, through its experts, to forecast on certain, not unreasonable, assumptions that there would be up to 1 million job losses at £4.15 an hour. In a sense, the cart has been put before the horse in the Bill. The Government argued on Second Reading that, as their proposal to proceed with the principle of a national minimum wage had gone as far as appointing a non-statutory Low Pay Commission, the Bill should be introduced at an early stage so that Parliament could express its views on the principle and the Low Pay Commission could undertake its work. However, it does not follow that consideration of the Bill should proceed ahead of the report of the Low Pay Commission.

I shall illustrate that point by referring to the inter-relationship between the explanatory and financial memorandum of the Bill and our consideration of the Bill. I had a little experience of such matters before coming to the House, not least when representing business organisations. The purpose of the explanatory and financial memorandum, among other things, is to set out the impact of the legislation on public sector manpower and business compliance costs. Those implications should be considered in detail before proceeding to legislation.

In the explanatory and financial memorandum under ``Financial effects of the Bill'', the Government state:

    ``It will not be possible to estimate the majority of the Bill's financial effects on the public sector with any accuracy until the level of the National Minimum Wage, and consequently the number of people who may be directly affected by it, are known.''

Enforcement is a major area of the Bill where the Government seek to take powers. The explanatory and financial memorandum states:

    ``Operational details as to who enforces the National Minimum Wage have yet to be decided.''

The implications for public sector manpower are given on page vii, which states:

    ``Any increase in workload from the introduction of a National Minimum Wage will depend on which if any of the enforcement options is chosen and on the actual rate and coverage of the National Minimum Wage.''

My experience suggests that the most important purpose of the explanatory and financial memorandum is to set out the business compliance costs associated with the Bill, and page vii states:

    ``The National Minimum Wage will clearly have both direct and indirect effects on business costs, but these are difficult to predict without knowing its rate, definition or coverage.''

The Government admit that we are proceeding to consideration of the Bill not with unseemly haste, but at a time of the Government's choosing which is in advance of the point at which they are able to state in the explanatory and financial memorandum the financial effects on the public sector, the manpower effects on the public sector, the implications for enforcement and how that will be accomplished by Government Departments and so on, and the effects on the business community because we do not yet know the rate of the minimum wage, its definition or its coverage.

The Government have failed to make a decision on the level of the national minimum wage and the Bill gives Ministers curious powers to introduce exemptions. For example, it allows them to exempt trainees, but does not allow them to make other exemptions. It is a curious beast that we are dealing with. My hon. Friend made it clear that we are dealing with an enabling Bill, but the Government have designed it to enable them not to respond to the debate in Committee. The Bill is not designed--looking at clause 4, for example--to give Ministers the freedom to make exemptions where they think that those are necessary.

I listened carefully to the hon. Member for Eastleigh (Mr. Chidgey). After discussing the impact of the minimum wage on different regions and speculating on what rate the Low Pay Commission might recommend, the Committee might conclude that there are grounds for a ministerial power to exempt certain regions or to set different rates for different regions, but the Bill is designed to exclude that power from Ministers. We are dealing with a curious beast which is an enabling Bill to an extreme extent: a national minimum wage at a single rate is to be set, but we know little of the detail, such as exemptions and how practical implications might be translated into legislation. Ministers have not given themselves the freedom that they ought.

That returns us to the question of timing.
