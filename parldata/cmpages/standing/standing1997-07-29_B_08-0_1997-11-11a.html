Mr. Keith Bradley: Clause 20 is essentially a re-enactment of section 54 of the Social Security Administration Act 1992. It provides for people to be referred for a medical examination if that is necessary to make a decision about a claim. That is not a new requirement.

Decision makers acting for the Secretary of State have comprehensive, published guidance, issued by medical and disability experts, to assist them in deciding whether a medical examination is necessary. Medical examinations are a well established means of obtaining information that is needed to ensure that the correct amount of benefit is paid. That must, however, work both ways. Benefit should not be paid to someone who unreasonably fails to attend for a medical examination when requested to do so, and whose entitlement to benefit is therefore in doubt.

That is why clause 20 allows an adverse decision to be made if a person fails, without good cause, to comply with a request. An adverse decision does not always mean that benefit will cease entirely. It may mean that a requested increase in benefit in respect of an alleged deterioration in health cannot be paid. There is a right of appeal against any benefit decision that results from the medical examination.

Mr. Keith Bradley: Clause 22 enables regulations to provide for the suspension of payments of benefit and for those payments subsequently to be made.

Clause 35 makes the same provisions in respect of housing and council tax benefit. The effects of amendments Nos. 274 to 276 to that clause would mirror those of amendments to clause 22 and I will therefore address my remarks primarily to clause 22.

Clause 22 will come into effect at the same time as the other decision-making and appeals changes. However, we intend to introduce the power to suspend payments in lookalike cases following Royal Assent. New clause 6, which I have mentioned before, contains the relevant provision and we will be able to discuss that at a later stage.

The purpose of amendments Nos. 226 and 227, as hon. Members have explained, is to ensure that suspensions can be imposed only in those circumstances prescribed in clause 22(2), and that further circumstances cannot be added by regulations. I can say at this stage that it is unlikely that there will be any circumstances other than those listed in subsection (2) where we may want to suspend payment of benefit. However, we consider that there is some merit in retaining a degree of flexibility in being able to make regulations in other cases if the need arises.

It is conceivable that we shall need to act quickly to safeguard public expenditure in the future as unforeseen situations develop. In the early 1990s an urgent need emerged to be able to suspend payments in lookalike cases. There was no flexibility in the primary legislation at that time. Ideally, the primary legislation should have been amended, but that would have taken time, during which millions of pounds could have gone from the departmental budget. A regulation was introduced to deal with the situation but the High Court later held that the existing primary power would not support such a provision. We are therefore taking new primary powers that will allow us appropriate flexibility for the future.

As the Committee will appreciate, the advantage of secondary legislation in such cases is the speed with which it can be introduced, subject of course to parliamentary scrutiny as appropriate.

There are futher important reasons why the Committee should reject amendments Nos. 274 and 275, which apply to regulation-making powers in clause 35. In future we will want to make regulations to parallel those currently made under existing powers to withhold benefit; they are contained in sections 5(1) (o) and 6 (1) (o) of the Social Security Administration Act 1992. Those powers are being repealed as a consequence of the new decision-making and appeals provisions in the Bill, and their role will be combined within the power to suspend, which is in clause 35.

We need the general power in clause 35(1)(a) to make those parallel regulations because the circumstances specified in clause 35(2) are concerned with questions of entitlement, reviews, appeals and lookalike cases, and do not provide the power to suspend on issues relating to payment of benefit. For example, authorities would not be able to suspend benefit when satisfied that the claimant was not paying his rent. It is clearly important for authorities to have such powers to ensure that benefit is used for the purpose for which it is intended.

Amendments Nos. 228 and 229 would remove the regulation power in clause 22(2), which provides for the suspension of benefit in lookalike cases. That power is a key tool for protecting the integrity of public expenditure. Sometimes, an entitlement issue arises in a leading test case which, when resolved by the courts, will clarify the law for many hundreds, or thousands, of other benefit decisions which turn on the same legal issue. That provision will enable the Secretary of State to suspend payment of benefit in other cases, where the identical issue arises, until the final decision in the test case is known.

The effect of the amendments to clause 22, however, would mean that benefit would continue to be paid in all lookalike cases even though the identical legal issue arising in the lead case had not been resolved, and benefit had quite properly been suspended in the lead case. That cannot be right. It is our duty to ensure that benefit goes to those who are entitled to it and not to those who are not so entitled.

When entitlement to benefit is in doubt and is being resolved, the power to suspend payment in lookalike cases means the Department can avoid making substantial overpayments of public money which could not then be recovered. The Government believe that it would be irresponsible and imprudent to pay out in cases where it is later decided that there was no underlying entitlement.

Amendment No. 276 would remove the regulation power in clause 35(2) which provides for the suspension of housing and council tax benefits in lookalike cases. That mirrors amendments Nos. 228 and 229 to clause 22, which I have just discussed, and the same arguments apply.

As a rule of thumb we can anticipate about one major case a year. A recent example is that of Graham, which involved certain women who claimed invalidity benefit. It resulted in just under 50,000 instances of suspension in lookalike cases pending a reference to the European Court of Justice in the lead case.

The answer to the question about the time a suspension can last depends on the individual case. At one end it can be a matter of days--indeed, that is where the bulk of suspensions will fall--but at the other it will equate to the time it takes for a case to work through the various appeals procedures, as the hon. Member for Northavon said. As an extreme example, in the case of Bate, there was an appeal against the adjudication officer's decision that she was not entitled to a severe disability premium in September 1990. In May 1996, the House of Lords confirmed that she was not entitled to the premium. Clearly that was exceptional, but I should stress that in every case payment of benefit was only partially suspended throughout that period. Basic income support and other relevant premiums were not affected and therefore continued in payment. Where the lead case is about only part of the benefit--that is often so--it does not mean that all benefit is suspended throughout the period.

The hon. Member for Northavon mentioned the build-up of arrears leading to eviction in housing cases. Clear guidance will be given to local authorities on those matters. Local authorities will consider particular issues of hardship which may fall into the category that the hon. Gentleman identified. They will consider the circumstances of individual cases--whether other income or capital can be used--and the state of individuals: how frail they are, their overall circumstances and whether children are in the family. All those matters will be taken into account before a suspension is made.

If it is fair that someone whose claim becomes a test case, perhaps by chance, has his or her payments backdated to the time of the original claim, surely it is fair that the payments to those whose claims are held up by the test case should be similarly backdated. If we do not allow that to happen, we shall discriminate against claimants on the grounds of the time at which their cases are heard.
