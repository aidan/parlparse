Clause 3

Duty of the Commission to ensure provision of a universal postal service

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Richard Page (South-West Hertfordshire): The clause outlines the Postal Services Commission's duty to ensure provision of a universal postal service. Conservative Members welcome the inclusion of such a provision. Indeed, on Second Reading, the shadow Secretary of State, my hon. Friend the Member for Tiverton and Honiton (Mrs Browning), fully supported the commission's aim of ensuring a universal postal service and of setting minimum standards for the universal delivery of letters.

Communication by e-mail and the internet has led to shrinking costs. However, without a universal postal service, certain parts of the country would suffer much higher costs on the delivery of postal packets. Incidentally, we shall seek to amend clause 4's definition of such packets.

Clause 3(1) states:

The Commission shall exercise its functions in the manner which it considers best calculated to ensure the provision of a universal ... service. We all say amen to that. However, the explanatory notes advise that clause 3 should be read in conjunction with clause 5, subsection (1) of which states:

Subject to section 3, the Commission shall exercise its functions in the manner which it considers is best calculated to further the interests of users of postal services, wherever appropriate by promoting effective competition between postal operators.

Will the Minister confirm that that means that the commission is under a duty to set up a universal postal service at the lowest level consistent with providing a service? The 12th report of the Select Committee on Trade and Industry which was printed on 14 September 1999 stated:

The Secretary of State announced a reduction in the monopoly from the existing £1 limit to 50p or 150 grams. Earlier in our proceedings, I mentioned that reduction, which was due to be implemented on 1 April 2000. An order giving effect to the change was laid on 8 July 1999, but we all know what has happened to that. However, the same day, Minister for Competitiveness told the House:

greater commercial freedom must be matched with some liberalisation.--[Official Report, 8 July 1999; Vol. 334, c. 1176.] In response to questioning, the Minister agreed that it might be appropriate to link the 50p limit to the retail prices index. However, he also said:

the figure is pitched at a level that will ensure that the Post Office can still meet its universal service obligation.--[Official Report, 8 July 1999; Vol. 334, c. 1184.]

I do not wish to detain the Committee by repeating the point. I simply want the Minister to confirm that the commission has a duty to pitch the limit at the lowest level possible to achieve a universal postal service.

The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson): In case hon. Members are confused about terminology, I should explain that ``universal service obligation'' and ``universal postal service'' refer to the same thing. It is ironic that universal service obligation has become known as UPS, because a distinguished and reputable private courier has the same name, and it seems to have managed to get its name into the Bill.

It has been said that the Secretary of State talked about setting the USO at the lowest possible level, but he actually referred to setting the reserved area at the lowest possible level. The universal service obligation will apply to items up to 20 kg under our proposal, which we shall debate later. That will ensure that people receive items up to that weight at a uniform tariff wherever they live in this country. Registered post will also come under the universal service obligation. However, parcels up to 20 kg and registered items will not be part of the reserved area, which will involve items up to 350 g.

We are dealing with two different matters. The first is the reserved area, which used to be called the monopoly. The limit must safeguard the ability of the Post Office to fulfil the universal service obligation without other companies cream-skimming and, therefore, preventing it from achieving economies of scale. The second is the universal service obligation. I am happy to reassure Opposition Members that the reserved area should be set at the lowest limit consistent with protecting the universal service obligation. Items such as registered post and parcels over that limit are in the competitive market, but we shall insist that people have proper access to those services.

We do not believe that the universal service obligation should be set at the lowest possible level; we believe that it should be set at a level consistent with giving people in all parts of the country, no matter how remote, access to parcel services above and beyond the reserved area. This short clause deals with the universal service obligation, and the purpose of its three subsections is clear. Clause 4 contains further detail, but I hope that, given those assurances, Opposition Members will agree to the clause.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 3 ordered to stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Page: We suggest that the phrase ``relevant postal items'' should be added after the word ``packets''. I am sure that the Minister will be only too happy to agree to this seemingly innocuous series of amendments. However, this is where the reality of competition starts to bite. This is the reality behind all the fine words about setting the Post Office free, allowing competition and letting the real world into our postal services. I shall listen to the Minister's response with great interest, as no doubt will many other people.

I should like to outline what the amendments mean and then deal with the arguments in their favour. The clause fails to define postal packets and items in line with article 3 of the European Union postal services directive. Will the Minister confirm that that is correct? The universal service obligation under article 3 requires services to be provided for postal items up to 2 kg and for postal packages, not including letters, up to 10 kg. Our amendments would separately define postal items and packages in line with the EU directive, as we are worried that the clause does not conform to it.

Therefore, the purpose of the amendments is to amend the definition of the universal service obligation in order to distinguish postal items of up to 2 kg from postal packages up to 10 kg, thus bringing it in line with the EU postal services directive.

The clause proposes to include within the definition of the universal postal services, postal packets

    ``whose weight does not exceed 20 kilograms''.

It is easy to see the difference between the EU directive and the clause. As a result of the clause, a universal service provider will be required to provide

    ``postal services for postal packets at affordable prices determined in accordance with a public tariff which is uniform throughout the United Kingdom''.

Competition regarding postal packets whose weight does not exceed 20 kg will be limited--and the Minister is going to have to take decisions about that. How much real competition will there be in the marketplace? Competition will be limited in this significant part of the postal services market.

The Minister will know that the Association of International Courier and Express Services estimates that 95 per cent. of its members' business is in postal packets not exceeding 20 kg--and 20 kg is a sizeable weight. If Parcelforce has similar figures, its costs can legitimately be cross-subsidised for almost all of its traffic because it has a USO. That greatly undermines the competitive environment. Increasing the USO to 20 kg and allowing Parcelforce legitimately to receive cross-subsidies means very little incentive for efficiency gains. It will be a protective umbrella, which is inconsistent with the Government's intention to provide greater competition in the postal markets, as covered by the definition of a universal postal service.

I repeat what I said at the outset--the Minister can correct me if I am wrong--that our amendments would also bring the provisions in line with the second requirement of the directive. Under article 3, a universal service obligation requires services to be provided for postal items up to 2 kg and postal packages, not including letters, up to 10 kg. It is easy to appreciate that a letter of up to 10 kg would be a weighty missive. At present, the Bill fails to distinguish between postal packages and postal items. As I said, the amendments would separately define them in line with the directive. I look forward to hearing the Minister's reply.

Mr. Page: I beg to move amendment No. 1, in page 2, line 40, at end insert

where the prices under such agreements are geared to the costs associated of providing the services to those customers,

I hope that the Minister will not get confused about which way to take this amendment, which is quite simple. I shall set out its aims in tabular form.

The amendment would limit the threat of predatory pricing by the universal service provider in areas in which it competes with other providers. I was brushed aside when I said that the Post Office was in a position to cross-subsidise services amounting to 95 per cent. of its private competitors' business. That is a fairly high figure, so it is entirely possible that the Post Office will compete unfairly against private sector operators. The Bill therefore fails to comply with article 12 of the European postal services directive, which requires that priorities be geared to costs when the universal service provider reaches price agreements with individual customers. Consequently, the amendment would exclude from the definition of a universal postal service individual price agreements that are not related to the cost of providing postal services to the customer.

We must link prices to costs, as emphasised in the White Paper of July 1999 entitled ``Post Office Reform: A world class service for the 21st century''. The Post Office should not charge different customers--or categories of customer--different prices for the same service, when such differences do not reflect the quantity, quality or other characteristics of the service supplied. It is obvious that price flexibility should not be used anti-competitively. For example, predatory pricing should not be used to exclude competitors and discounts should not be designed to foreclose the market.

The amendment would reduce predatory pricing by the universal service provider in competitive areas. Article 12 of the European postal services directive, with which the Bill must comply, states:

Member States shall take steps to ensure that the tariffs for each of the services forming part of the provision of the universal service comply with the following principles:

    prices must be geared to costs; Member States may decide that a uniform tariff should be applied throughout their national territory.

    the application of a uniform tariff does not exclude the right of the universal service provider(s) to conclude individual agreements on prices with customers.

    Therefore, the power provided in the directive to conclude individual agreements is subject to the requirement that prices are geared to costs and the amendment would make provision for that.

    I should like to draw a parallel, as I have trodden this path before. Similar arguments were made by companies that would be in competition with BT after privatisation. BT's accounts were in such a mess that it had no idea of its profit and loss areas. Indeed, when it was privatised, it had only one qualified accountant and was making a profit in areas in which it believed that it was losing money. That made life exceedingly interesting.

    Of course, BT's difficulties were solved by the fact that the market exploded at such a rate that demand soared and everyone got a larger slice of the pie, even though the percentages may have been smaller than before. Such was the size of the pie that the division of it was perfectly acceptable to everyone.

    The position in postal services is different. Competition is building up, especially in certain parts of Europe. National postal companies are buying up the competition and becoming more powerful. When the market is liberalised--as will happen in a few years--companies protected from the effects of reality will find it much harder to change and counteract the competition. Unready companies will be bitten more severely and savagely by change. The longer change is delayed, the greater the impact.

    The amendment will rectify an inconsistency with the Government's intention to introduce greater competition into the postal market. I hope that the Minister will respond positively on cross-subsidisation. I am unaware of the accounting system inside the Post Office and of the extent to which it knows which sectors and which parts of the country bring in the most money. That makes it more difficult to compete and harder for the regulator to pinpoint sectors in which predatory pricing is taking place. I hope that the Minister will accept that prices under such agreements should be geared to the costs of providing services to customers, and accept the amendment.
