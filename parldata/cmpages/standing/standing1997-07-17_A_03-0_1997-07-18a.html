Clause 6

Fuel and power for domestic or charity use

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I beg to move amendment No. 19, in page 5, line 23, leave out `1st' and insert `17th'.

I am not so sure about your latter point, Mr. McWilliam. My hon. Friend's granny is our consultant on several matters connected with pensioner incomes, a matter that I am about to investigate.

The amendment would change the date of the value added tax reduction affecting fuel and power in domestic households. As is happening so often under this Government, there is less in the apparent benefit than meets the eye. When we start to examine why they chose the date of 1st September to put the cut into effect, various questions arise. Why did the Government not cut VAT on domestic fuel and power from Budget day? After all, they increased hydrocarbon duties, including the duty on heating oil, from that date. I think that my hon. Friend the Member for Daventry's mother, who is a pensioner, heats her home with heating oil on which she is already paying extra duty. That went up on Budget day, and she is disappointed that the reduction in VAT on domestic fuel and power does not take effect until later in the year.

The first question--why did the Government hit everyone with the tax increases on Budget day but postpone the apparent benefit until September--gives rise to a second question. Why did the Government choose the date of 1 September? I wish to ask the Financial Secretary about the effect that that date has on the consequential uprating of pensions and benefits. Members of the Committee will know that the uprating of pensions and benefits in April is based on the performance of the retail prices index to the year ending 15 September.

The September dates are important. By selecting the date of 1 September, the Government have ensured that the RPI to the year ending 15 September rises by slightly less than it otherwise would, resulting in a lower uprating of pensions and benefits on 1 April. There is a clawback: the apparent reduction in VAT is eroded by the lower uprating of pensions and benefits by the choice of date.

What does the lower uprating mean in cash terms? It has been difficult to obtain that information from the Treasury. We were told in a written answer that the reduction in VAT on fuel and power would mean an average fuel bill saving for single pensioners of 30p a week and for couples of 40p a week. What will be the effect on that cash benefit of the lower uprating that I have just described? The answer was not forthcoming, but it is important that we should know the true cash benefit to those various categories of household.

It puzzles me greatly and makes me rather suspicious to know that the information is available to the Government. We know that from other written answers received from another Department. I say that in the context of the Treasury's almost Trappist silence. I have tabled a good many written questions in recent weeks, spurred on by the professed willingness of the Government to adhere to codes of openness, but instead of the Treasury giving us more information, it is giving us less.

I said that the information is available, and I meant exactly that. My hon. Friend the Member for South Dorset (Mr. Bruce) asked a similar question about the effect on pensioner and other households of increasing VAT on fuel to 8 per cent., which we did during the previous Parliament. He deliberately asked not only about the direct cash effect of the VAT increase but also about the possible uprating of benefits and pensions as a consequence of that slight increase in the RPI. In other words, my hon. Friend had spotted that, although there was a cash disbenefit to households, it was compensated for at least in part by the increased benefits awarded at the time and the additional uprating due to the higher RPI figures.

The rather remarkable answer--all members of the Committee ought to listen at least to this--was that single retirement pensioners were no worse off as a result of the imposition of VAT at 8 per cent. because of the benefits that were awarded to them and because of the additional uprating. Moreover, couple retirement pensioners were made 5p a week better off following the increase in VAT from zero to 8 per cent. We have heard, in Committee and elsewhere, several hon. Members asserting that the imposition of VAT on fuel bills made pensioners worse off. That is not true, and we have the Government's word for that.

All that information came from the Department of Social Security in a written answer supplied on 24 June by the Under-Secretary of State for Social Security, the hon. Member for Manchester, Withington (Mr. Bradley). The methodology and the information, therefore, are available to the Government, but when I tabled a similar question about the effects on uprating of a reduction in VAT, I received no such information.

Perhaps one part of the Government does not know what another part knows, but I suspect that the Treasury has a new rule of not supplying information that is embarrassing. I regret that. It is hypocritical, as it flies in the face of all the assurances on openness, which are repeated and made a virtue of in the infamous ``The Pocket Budget''.

Incidentally, that booklet cost £50,000 to print and distribute. We pointed out in earlier debates how misleading it was on the advance corporation tax credit changes on pension funds, which were not mentioned--the proposals are all rolled up in some guff about the need to invest more. The booklet is misleading and expensive, but it takes pride in the apparent new culture of openness. That is directly contradicted--particularly starkly in this case--by the Treasury's refusal to give information about the clawback of the apparent VAT benefits, even though that information is available elsewhere in the Government.

The Financial Secretary should make amends for that and give us the information by the end of this debate. I ask her a direct and simple question; we know the effects on single and couple retirement households from 1 September, but what will the clawback be as a result of the lower uprating in benefits next April?

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Dawn Primarolo): I am delighted that the right hon. Member for Wells, in moving the amendment, now accepts that VAT on fuel should be cut to 5 per cent.; he is merely quibbling about the date. That is a bit cheeky, given that his Government increased the rate from zero to 8 per cent., having promised that they would not, and would have taken it to 17.5 per cent. if they had not been stopped by the combined parliamentary skills of the then Opposition. Clause 7

Rate of duty on spirits

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: The Government are not cutting spirits duty, as we believe they should, and the rate of duty will rise on 1 January 1998. There are two issues involved: the date and whether there should be a cut. We understand that the Government are undertaking a review of alcohol taxation, so it is not clear why an increase in line with inflation has been signalled for 1 January, which rather prejudges the outcome of that inquiry. If it is intended to be a genuine inquiry into the impact of duty increases on the industry and matters such as alcohol consumption, cross-border shopping and smuggling, surely it is unwise to signal in advance that alcohol duties will be increased in line with inflation from 1 January, when we are expecting another Budget shortly after that date.

Spirits are in a slightly different category from many alcoholic drinks in that they are taxed highly in respect of the alcohol they contain. There is a differentially high burden of duty on that form of alcoholic drink. In recognition of that, over the past two years, the previous Government cut the duty on spirits by 4 per cent. a year in real terms, which adds up to a pretty hefty cut of 13 per cent. in real terms. Not only did the previous Government think that taxation on spirits was getting out of line, they recognised that there is a particular effect of such duty on an important British exporting industry.

It is well known north of the border that the Scotch whisky industry is one of the great British success stories--it is sold throughout the world. However, the industry continually runs into the problem of foreign countries discriminating against Scotch whisky and, to a lesser extent, other spirits in favour of domestically produced drinks. That problem is frequently taken up by Ministers on visits abroad, but often without success.
