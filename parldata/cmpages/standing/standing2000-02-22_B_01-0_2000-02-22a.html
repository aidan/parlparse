Postal Services Bill

9 am

The Chairman: Before I call the Minister to move the sittings motion, it may be helpful if I advise members of the Committee of the arrangements that will apply to the tabling of amendments during the forthcoming short recess. On the assumption that the Committee agrees not to sit this Thursday, amendments received at the Public Bill Office before 4.30 pm on Friday 25 February, will appear on the blue paper on Monday 28 February, and will, accordingly, be unstarred and therefore selected for our next sitting on Tuesday 29 February. Amendments that are tabled on Monday 28 February will be starred for our sittings on Tuesday 29 February.

The Minister for Competitiveness (Mr. Alan Johnson): I beg to move,

    That, during proceedings on the Postal Services Bill, the Committee do meet, with effect from Tuesday 29 February, on Tuesdays at half-past Ten o'clock and at half-past Four o'clock and on Thursdays at Nine o'clock and at half-past One o'clock.

It is a great delight to have you chairing our first sitting of the Committee, Mr. Maxton. When I was a teenage postman, I read Willie Gallagher's autobiography and learnt about those three legends of Glasgow trade unionism: Gallagher, Mannie Shinwell and your father, John Maxton.

The Chairman: He was my uncle.

Mr. Johnson: My first mistake, and we are only two minutes into the sitting. Little did I know that John Maxton's nephew would be chairing the Committee on which I have the privilege to serve or that the Committee would discuss a Bill on the industry in which I once worked. It is ironic that you chaired the Committee stage of the Electronic Communications Bill, of which the hon. Member for Rutland and Melton (Mr. Duncan) was a member and in which I made fleeting appearances. I think that the Clerk to that Committee used a laptop for the first time in a Committee, which was also ironic because, for a short period in the long history of the Post Office between 1915 and 1981, telecommunications developed and grew as part of the old general Post Office. The city of Hull was one exception, a matter that we shall not go into at the moment, however.

Some predicted that the Post Office would sink into subsidised decline after the split and that the future lay with telecommunications alone. It is a sign of the Government's commitment to a healthy and modern communications industry that the Bills on e-commerce and postal services have come before you, Mr. Maxton, in quick succession. The Post Office, of course, is the Methuselah of the public sector. I sometimes remind those who have a single-minded obsession with telecommunications that the Post Office was the information superhighway when Dick Turpin was a superhighwayman.

I also welcome the hon. Members for Rutland and Melton and for South-West Hertfordshire (Mr. Page) to the Committee. I congratulate the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire on his promotion to the Conservative Front Bench. He is one of my predecessors at the Department of Trade and Industry and I look forward to hearing his contributions to the debates. He must be delighted to have the opportunity to discuss constructive proposals for the future of the Post Office, something that he was not allowed to do when he had ministerial responsibility for it.

You have before you, Mr. Maxton, the crème de la crème of the Government Benches. We look forward to many delightful, enjoyable hours under your astute chairmanship, discussing this important Bill. Our sitting began at 9 o'clock, the time at which post offices throughout the country opened for business to some of the 28 million people whom they will serve this week. Staff will have almost finished their first deliveries to all 26 million addresses in this country and will be gearing up to handle 77 million letters today, which is 5 million more than were handled in a year in Victorian Britain.

Although the Post Office operates on a huge scale, it manages to be appreciated as a community service and even as a personal service. It touches people's lives as no other industry does. It is rightly highly regarded and even cherished by our citizens, but faces unprecedented challenges from globalisation, liberalisation and new technology. It needs the commercial freedom that overseas postal administrations have enjoyed for many years, but it must preserve its public service ethos. The trick is for the Post Office to change while staying the same.

The Government believe that we have chosen the best way forward for the Post Office and its customers, and we seek to implement the reforms as quickly as we can. The greatest damage that we can do is to delay further. By implementing the reforms in the Bill, the Government are confident that we will provide a Post Office that effectively and efficiently meets the needs of business and private customers in the United Kingdom. The reforms will also provide that the Post Office competes head-on in international markets with major overseas postal providers, and modernises and grows to deliver an excellent service in which it is more responsive to market developments and customer demands. Most important, the Post Office will protect its social obligations on the principles of universality, accessibility and affordability so that it will provide a world-class service for communities throughout the UK.

The Bill is crucial for postal services. It will create a modern Post Office able to grow as a significant global player, establish a new regulator and a stronger users' council for postal services, provide a firm basis for competition in the market, put the Post Office on a more commercial footing, provide a better deal for consumers and enable the Government to issue social and environmental guidelines that may include setting criteria for access to counter services.

Mr. Richard Page (South-West Hertfordshire): May I also express my pleasure that you are chairing our proceedings, Mr. Maxton. I have always had tremendous admiration for Chairmen. I am engaging in this sycophancy not only because I hope to have tolerance and a little leeway extended to me during the proceedings, but because I admire the fact that Chairmen know the arcane rules of parliamentary procedure. I have been in the House for 24 years and it is still a mystery to me. I am delighted that we have an experienced Chairman who will guide us through the labyrinth that lies ahead. I may come to you for a slightly unusual ruling, Mr. Maxton, but it depends on the Minister's response.

The other thing for which I admire Chairmen is the fact that they know everyone's name and constituency. Having been in the House for 24 years, I know that names, faces and constituencies change. It is a bit like school. When one goes to school for the first time, after three weeks one knows everybody; as one gets more senior, one knows one's own form and perhaps the form below, but does not have a clue about the names of the little sprogs who have just come in. I welcome the Labour Members who recently joined the House. There used to be an hon. Member called Maxwell-Hyslop—a thin, angular, rather abrasive man. His moment of triumph came in the late 1970s, during the passage of the then Government's hybrid shipbuilding Bill; he completely ruined it and it all had to be redrafted. That was the highlight of his life and things from then on went downhill. I always admire people who understand how ``Erskine May'' operates and what impact it has on our lives.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Rutland and Melton said, we are not against the Bill. Our only worry is that it does not go far enough. I would like the Post Office to be given the chance of greater liberalisation so that it can better contend with the foreign competition. However, I pick up the Minister's comment that the greatest damage would be done by further delay. When we wanted to liberalise the Post Office in the 1990s, who objected? It was the Labour party. That is a delicious twist of fate. A union ran a clever campaign against the proposal; who was a senior figure in the union at that time, blocking liberalisation? His name will not pass my lips. I find it amazing that we have to discuss a sittings motion when the perpetrator of the delay is bemoaning that delay.

As my hon. Friend the Member for Rutland and Melton says, we will be constructive and we will try and move the Bill on in a positive, helpful—

 

Mr. Duncan: Sensible.

Mr. Page: Yes, and sensible fashion. However, we are faced with 105 clauses and eight schedules. I object to the amount through which we have to plough. Seeing immediately the black hole in front of him, he had the sense to back off and say that he would not tell us the number of amendments. That was wise because, as the debate continued and he was pressed on some aspects of the Bill, especially on the social guidance to be given by the Secretary of State to the regulator, he suddenly realised, ``Whoops, we will have to introduce another couple of amendments.'' Having dug that hole for himself, he had the sense not to jump into it.

I am expressing my doubts and worries about the sittings motion because I do not want the Minister to have to call on his Back Benchers to filibuster on the Bill while the amendments that have not been drafted are produced. I want to avoid our having to consider rafts of amendments on Report. How many times in the past two years have Ministers said in Committee, ``We will table amendments on Report''? The Report stage on the Floor of the House is expensive in money and time, and it is impossible to investigate amendments accurately there.

The Post Office is vital to so many people's everyday lives; it provides a real public service. The continuation of that service according to new market demands and challenges is extremely important to those people. Mr. Alan Johnson: I welcome the spirit in which the hon. Member for Rutland and Melton intends to approach the Committee, and I assure him that the Government intend constructively to examine the Bill to ensure that we put in place the right foundation for the future of the Post Office. I was slightly surprised by the comments of the hon. Member for South-West Hertfordshire; for a moment, I thought that I was at Sotheby's. However, leaving the auction to one side, the hon. Gentleman said that I was responsible for the damage to the Post Office. However, I ran what was then called the Union of Communication Workers between 1993 and 1997. I did not run the Government, and it was the Government who were responsible for deciding the future of the Post Office. The hon. Gentleman is wrong to suggest that the union campaign opposed giving the Post Office commercial or financial freedom or liberalising it. In fact, it suggested that alternatives were available to the dogma that the only way to give the Post Office commercial freedom was to privatise it. We decided not to privatise it, and not to go down that Railtrack, so to speak, and we argued for an alternative. I do not like to compliment people behind their backs, Mr. Maxton, but Mr. Butterfill, who will also chair our Committee, served on a Trade and Industry Select Committee that proposed that the Post Office could be given commercial freedom by being turned into a public limited company that was 100 per cent. publicly owned. The Bill is that proposal, and if only the Major Administration had picked up that proposal, the Post Office would by now have much more commercial freedom.

Hon. Members expressed a lot of excitement about amendments. The Bill is not ill prepared, and it closely follows the White Paper that we published last July. I ask the Opposition not to get excited about our amendments. Major surprises are unlikely. We are dealing with an organisation that has been a state corporation since Charles I decreed from Bagshot in 1631 that the Post Office should be state owned. An awful lot of history is involved. Uncontroversial but detailed issues relating to tax and pensions, among other issues, are involved, and most of the amendments will bore the Committee rigid. I do not expect them to cause a lot of excitement. [Interruption.]—I have disappointed my colleagues. I realise that it was not unusual for the previous Government—indeed, all Governments—to table a substantial number of amendments in Committee. We shall ensure that we give plenty of notice of Government amendments and that the Committee has time to consider them properly.

The PlU study will influence the final details of the access criteria. I want a proper job to be done there and, while I shall try to ensure that it comes before the Committee as soon as possible, it is more important to get those details right.

We have a common aim to see the Post Office prosper. It is widely accepted that it has lost ground in the past eight years while it has been under constant review. Its competitors have leapt ahead from what was for some a sedentary position back in the early 1990s. It is in all our interests to ensure that we get the Bill through smoothly, tackling the controversial issues in a proper way, so that we leave the Post Office in a much better position to face the communications market of the 21st century.
