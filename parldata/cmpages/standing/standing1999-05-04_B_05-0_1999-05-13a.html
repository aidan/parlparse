Secondly, the amendment would cause considerable confusion for haulage operators and would be quite unfair to the several thousand hauliers who have, quite properly and conscientiously, up-plated their lorries before 17 April to ensure that they were operating within the law. Dawn Primarolo: May I explain at the outset why the changes are being made and what their effect will be? The schedule relates to a business facilitation measure, in particular VAT grouping. The facility will remove some of the administrative burdens that associated companies can incur when complying with VAT rules. It enables companies to register an account for the tax as a single VAT registration. Supplies between group members are disregarded for VAT purposes. That enables business to simplify accounting arrangements and it can reduce VAT liability. Grouping is a permissive provision in European law. It has been adopted by eight member states, including Austria, Denmark, Eire, Finland, Germany, Holland and, most recently, Sweden. The permissive nature of European law has enabled each member state to develop a different approach to their grouping provisions.

The schedule implements changes that result from a review of grouping facilities which was undertaken by Customs and Excise. It tried to establish whether the balance between business facilitation and revenue costs was fair and took into account the opinion of businesses that it received during the consultation period. The changes remove the uncertainty that business has experienced as a result of recent court decisions that relate to the entitlement of overseas companies to join groups and to the scope of protection powers. Subsequent amendments deal with that.

The changes benefit business. They make the facility more flexible by allowing grouping to take place with immediate effect, which touches on some of the points made by the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton. That also helps Customs to counter abuse and ensure that revenue yield is protected. We are striking a balance between facilitating business and protecting the taxpayer.

As for the detail and the 90-day notices, the problem is that the law requires businesses to give up to 90-days' notice before an application for grouping can take effect. Business rightly regards that as an unnecessary obstacle to legitimate business, which prevents businesses from reacting quickly to changing business structures. The proposal on the 90 days, which the hon. Gentleman welcomed in his comments on his amendment, will provide more flexibility for business. The 90-day notice will be removed and, instead, grouping will be approved from the date on which Customs receives an application. Clearly, business will derive benefits from being able to use the grouping facility from that point, but the interests of the taxpayer must be balanced. If businesses are allowed immediate entry to the group on submission of application, Customs will need a power to remove a business from the group, if, on studying the application, it is clear that it should not be permitted.

There are elements in the proposal that are also anomalous. For example, let us consider someone living off a pension income--it could be a state pension, a private or an occupational pension. If it is from the private sector, the pensioner will be living off income that is derived from a pension fund that has been invested in interest-earning assets. Interest income can be charged differently, quite anomalously, arbitrarily and unfairly. Our main concern, however, is not with anomalies per se but with the effect that they have on people at the bottom end of the income scale--those who are essentially poor.
