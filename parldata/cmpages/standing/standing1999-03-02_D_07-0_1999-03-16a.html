Clause 6

Reduced rates of contributions etc: power to specify different percentages

Question again proposed.

The Chairman: It may be convenient for the Committee to know that, subject to progress, I propose to suspend the Committee for dinner for one and a half hours at 7 o'clock.

Mr. Quentin Davies (Grantham and Stamford): I hope that you had a good lunch, Mr. Malins.

Over the lunch hour, I met representatives of the National Council of Women of Great Britain, who were complaining vituperatively and vociferously that the Government had defaulted on their consultation promise. The council had been hoping to be consulted on the Bill, but was denied that opportunity. That is one more organisation with which the Minister will have to make his peace in due course.

Earlier, I said that there were at least three aspects of the clause that we should consider carefully before deciding whether it merits inclusion in the Bill. The first was the considerable complexity that appears to be involved for employers who, in some circumstances, will have to calculate four different types of class 1 contributions. I hope that the Minister will respond to that point when he seeks to catch your eye later in the debate, Mr. Malins, as I am sure he will.

The second aspect was that the Government's record on rebates is utterly deplorable. We cannot have a repetition of the experience of the past two years when national insurance rebates have been used blatantly for political and Treasury purposes. There were differential changes in the SERPS rebates for contracted-out salary-related schemes, for approved money purchase schemes and for personal pensions. Logically, all those schemes should have received the same degree of compensation for the impact of the Government's pension tax. Will the Minister assure us that that will not be repeated and that the clause will not provide the basis, as it appears to do, for an entirely arbitrary set of decisions about what the level of rebates should be?

Clause 6(1) says:

    ``An order . . . may specify different percentages in respect of earners.''

That statement must be treated warily in the light of experience, as what happened before was wrong. Those who are unlucky enough to have their pension invested in an approved money purchase scheme, as opposed to a salary-related scheme or personal pension, will pay twice. First, there is the impact of the pension tax, which is the part of their contributions that is over and above any SERPS rebate and is not protected by the change in those arrangements. They have suffered from that because of the reduction in the return on the investments in their scheme and therefore the reduction in what they can expect to receive as a pension income when they retire. Secondly, SERPS rebates have been cut to compensate the Government for the need to increase personal pension rebates to stop people contracting back into the SERPS system.

The Government have cynically treated members of approved money purchase schemes as a kind of milch cow. As I was explaining before lunch, they have compounded that cynicism by giving an entirely bogus explanation for the different rebates. If the original allowance for administration costs was too high for those schemes, that matter should have been considered independently. Presumably, it would have formed the subject of a separate report by the Government Actuary's Department. We must not have a repetition of that.

The third unsatisfactory aspect of the clause is that it refers to rebates, but does not say what they will be. A major part of the Opposition's critique is that the stakeholder pension scheme is a thoroughly bogus proposal. It purports to be something that it is not. We discovered that it is certainly not good news for most of the pensioners who are not members of a salary-related occupational pension scheme, and that the Government think that the scheme will be appropriate only for people who earn between £9,000 and £18,500 a year. One cannot tell whether the scheme will be appropriate for those at the top and bottom margins unless one knows what the level of rebates will be. That is the essence of the calculation that people will have to make.

Such concerns arise over and again. We had a long argument in a previous sitting about the need for advice. It is absurd that someone who earns £9,000 a year will need actuarial advice to decide whether he should contract out of SERPS, or the Government's new second state pension, and go into a stakeholder scheme. An important element of that calculation is the rebate that he would receive if he contracted out. There will be a dividing line beyond which it is sensible to contract out from SERPS, or the second state pension when that comes on stream. One of the most important variables in the calculation of that dividing line will be the rebate that people earning more than £9,000 a year will receive.

We do not know what the rebate will be; it is referred to in the Green Paper. The clause will provide for the Government to do what they like—to produce differential rebates and so on. That is not good enough: we cannot proceed on that basis. We need to know what the rebates will be, or at least, the basis on which they will be calculated.

Will there be a bonus for people who earn more than £9,000 and choose to contract out? Will the Government use the rebate powers that they are trying to obtain through the clause to determine that choice? That would allow them to validate, retrospectively, the otherwise completely unfounded statement that the Minister made in a previous sitting. He said that people with earnings of more than £9,000 would have an interest in contracting out of the state second pension and into a stakeholder pension.

If the Government has those powers, they could produce rebates that would make that true. We need clarification. Will the Minister say that he will use his powers to fix the rebate to ensure that, for people on salaries of at least £9,001, it will always be worth contracting out?

Two things would be gained if we heard that assurance from Ministers this afternoon. We would have greater clarity, which is important from a Government with arbitrary tendencies. Their enormous majority means that they can get away with things that a proper democratic and accountable Government should not be allowed to get away with. In addition, that assurance might reduce people's need for advice, which would also be important.

It is grotesque that someone on a salary of £9,000 a year might be told, ``To make an intelligent assessment of where your interests lie, you should go to Watson's. Get advice from a leading actuarial firm.'' That is absurd; a poor person on such a salary cannot afford that help. Instead, he will be told, ``Too bad—you'll probably make the wrong decision.'' People will no doubt say, ``At that level, the loss comes to only a few hundred pounds a year, which does not matter.'' For those on an income of £9,000 a year, a few hundred pounds is a significant sum. It might be far less than the cost of advice, so there would be nothing that they could do about it under the Government's proposed regime, but that situation is thoroughly undesirable.

The Government could change things by guaranteeing always to produce a rebate that would make it worthwhile for people who earn more than £9,000 to contract out of the state second pension and into a stakeholder pension. That was what the Minister bravely, but with no justification, said would be the case. That does not reduce the force of my argument when we discussed advice. Help may be available in only one of the eight cases in which people who need advice will not receive it under the Bill.

Those earning £9,000 will have two difficult problems. They will have to decide, first, whether to opt out of or into an occupational pension scheme and, secondly, whether it is worth making any provision at all and whether it might be better to rely on means-tested benefits. The Government have promised, although they do not always keep their promises, that income support for pensioners, which they have repackaged under the new Labour term, ``minimum income guarantee'', will be indexed to earnings and not prices.

That brings me to the other aspect of the differential rebate. The situation is grotesque. The Green Paper provides at lease a few figures, but the Bill provides none and the Minister has not added any or filled in any gaps. It is clear that the intention is that someone who makes a full contribution over a full working life to the state second pension will receive some 40 per cent of his income between the lower earnings limit of approximately £4,500 a year and £9,000 a year. That is my understanding of the Government's proposals, and the Minister will correct me if I am wrong. The pensioner will be credited with that, irrespective of his earnings between the lower income limit and £9,000.

In broad brush terms, 40 per cent. of £4,500 is around £1,800 a year. Someone who adopts the Government's strategy of a state second pension and works for the whole of his adult life and, with his employer, contributes class 1 contributions will be entitled to a state retirement pension of £66 a week, plus £1,800 a year, which is around £35 a week, making a total of just over £100 a week. That compares with a minimum income guarantee or income support, which now provides £75 a week, but will rise with earnings. Early in the next century there will be a cross-over point when the minimum income will be worth more than £100 a week. It will not then be worth anyone taking part in the state second pension. All the money that people pay in national insurance will be completely pointless because it will be taxed and they will have to pay for what they could receive for nothing by way of the minimum income guarantee if they were not working or contributing to the national insurance scheme.

After that cross-over period, the minimum income guarantee will be worth more than the combination of the state second pension and the state retirement pension. That will apply even if the Government do not do what we fear they will do—they have not promised not to do so beyond this Parliament—and means test the state retirement pension, in which case the arithmetic will be even more forceful in the direction that I am describing. After the cross-over period, someone in that position will be forced on to means-tested benefits and out of the scheme altogether. The whole structure of state second pension is bogus. It is a way of cheating working people on low incomes of up to £9,000 a year.

No doubt the Minister will be upset at the way in which I am describing his proposals, but he has at least one remedy open to him. He can fill in the blanks and explain to us what the rebates will be for those who decide to contract out. We can then see how someone who earns more than £9,000 a year will be placed in the new regime.

The unavoidable conclusion is that the Government's proposals are far from good news for people who earn less than £9,000 a year. In fact, they are extremely bad news for such people, who are being forced to contribute through the national insurance system to a level of benefit that, after a decade or two, will be less than the value of means-tested benefits. The Government are making fools of those people for their part in the system. That is not good enough.

I suppose that we do not have the voting strength to change matters in Committee.

Mr. Bayley: This clause is consequential upon the other changes that we are making, in clauses 10 and 11, with respect to pensions and bankruptcy. It will no longer be possible for a scheme, on bankruptcy, to forfeit a member's pension rights.

Under current arrangements in occupational pension schemes, if a member becomes bankrupt, the trustees of the scheme usually protect his pension rights by forfeiting them. This is provided for in scheme rules. The member no longer has any pension rights, so they cannot be taken by the trustee in bankruptcy. However, once the member retires, the trustees of the scheme will pay the pension to the member or his dependants on an ex gratia basis. In that way, the bankrupt's pension is protected. Such forfeiture arrangements are given statutory recognition in the Pensions Act 1995.

Forfeiture arrangements also exist in personal pension schemes. However, there are doubts about their effectiveness because of the contractural nature of personal pension arrangements. Measures in the Bill will provide a new basis for the protection of pensions on bankruptcy. All pension arrangements that are approved for tax purposes will have statutory protection against the pension being seized on bankruptcy. This protection is contained in clause 10. Forfeiture clauses will therefore no longer be needed.

We cannot, however, simply ignore these forfeiture arrangements. We must also formally override forfeiture clauses, and that is the purpose of the clause. There are two reasons why we must take that step: first, because of the potential knock-on effects in relation to unapproved pension schemes; and, secondly, because such clauses could prevent a trustee in bankruptcy from applying to a court for an income payments order against the bankrupt who was drawing his pension.

It may be useful if I say something more about the potential knock-on effects to unapproved schemes. As I have said, we plan to protect approved pension arrangements. On the other hand, unapproved pension arrangements will be allowed to vest in the trustee in bankruptcy, and will thus be available for the benefit of creditors. We believe that that strikes a reasonable balance between encouraging people to save for retirement and having regard to the rights of creditors. If unapproved schemes are allowed to retain their forfeiture provisions, that would undermine the proposals. Unapproved schemes would be protected, like approved schemes, from the consequences of bankruptcy, albeit by a different route.

If forfeiture arrangements were retained, on bankruptcy the unapproved pension arrangement would be forfeited by the trustees of the scheme, and the person's pension rights would be kept completely out of the reach of the trustee in bankruptcy. That would be contrary to the policy that I have outlined. It might also create a perverse incentive. There are limits on the amounts that people can put into tax-approved pension arrangements, but no limits on the amounts that can be put into unapproved pension arrangements. A person who envisaged that he might become bankrupt could put large sums of money, properly due to his creditors, into his unapproved pension arrangement, secure in the knowledge that, on bankruptcy, they would be protected from creditors. Clearly that kind of manipulation must be prevented.
