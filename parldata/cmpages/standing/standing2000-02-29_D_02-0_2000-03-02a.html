New clause 2

Custodial officers

    .--(1) After section 75M of the Army Act there is inserted--

    "Custodial officers. 75N.--(1) Custodial officers shall be appointed for the purposes of the Act by the General Officer Commanding.

    (2) Custodial officers shall be commissioned officers who have been trained in dealing with accused persons kept in military custody or subject to court-martial proceedings."

    (2) After section 75M of the Air Force Act 1955 there is inserted--

    "Custodial officers. 75N.--(1) Custodial officers shall be appointed for the purposes of this Act by the Air Officer Commanding.

    (2) Custodial officers shall be commissioned officers who have been trained in dealing with accused persons kept in air-force custody or subject to court-martial proceedings."

    (3) After section 75N of the 1957 Act there is inserted--

    "Custodial officers. 75O.--(1) Custodial officers shall be appointed for the purposes of this Act by the Admiral Commanding.

    (2) Custodial officers shall be commissioned officers who have been trained in dealing with accused persons kept in naval custody or subject to court-martial proceedings.".

Mr. Blunt: The last thing that I want to do is to repeat our debate on clause 1. The amendments to clause 2 are designed to deal with custody after charge. We dealt with the question of custody before charge when debating the amendments to clause 1 about custody officers that were moved by my hon. Friend the Member for Salisbury (Mr. Key). Although those amendments were rejected, I have had time to refine those ideas so that we can give the armed forces the flexibility that they need to deal with custody after charge and to make the system bombproof against taking their cases to the European court under the European convention on human rights.

The amendments are simple and straightforward. They would simply add the words "or custodial" between the words "judicial" and "officer", so that either a judicial officer or a custodial officer could adjudicate on the continuation of the custody of someone who has been charged.

I wonder, Mr. Malins, whether you could give me some guidance. We are presumably debating new clause 2 with this group of amendments.

The Chairman: Yes.

Mr. Blunt: I am grateful, Mr. Malins. I shall start by dealing with new clause 2 and the definition of a custodial officer.

Clause 2 includes provisions for a judicial officer, but I believe that, for custody after charge, we should allow not only judicial officers to adjudicate on whether a suspect who has been charged should be remanded in custody; we should also allow custodial officers to adjudicate in such cases. The difficulty is that it may be difficult in an operational environment to ensure that a judicial officer is available every eight days or every 28 days, when it may be necessary to remand someone in custody again.

The Bill makes clear the difficulty of getting, judicial officers in touch with suspects, because it contains a provision for video links. The difficulty of ensuring that judicial officers are in a position to make adjudications on custody is recognised. The Government are unable to produce a draft statutory instrument that sets out how electronic and media links will work. The civilian system has only limited experience of video links between prisons and to facilitate children giving evidence. We have to make a decision on bandwidths and the electronic spectrum that is available to the military. It is a difficult statutory instrument to draft, which is no doubt why the Government have not produced it. Had it been easier, the Minister would have acceded to my request on Second Reading to see a draft statutory instrument. That would have allowed us to see how they will write the regulations to enable the judicial officer to adjudicate on custody cases. An alternative arrangement of custodial officers would assist the armed forces.

If there is no judicial officer to adjudicate on whether someone should be remanded in custody, the amendment would provide an alternative in the form of a custodial officer. He would be a commissioned officer, as defined in the new clause, who has been trained in the proper tests to apply for custody and court martial procedure. To avoid the problem of custodial officers being associated with the commanding officer, who is part of the summary justice system for the individual concerned, new clause 2 recommends that custodial officers should be appointed by the general commanding officer, depending on the service. That appointment would be two steps removed in the chain of command. Custodial officers would permanently take on the responsibility and would be able, in whatever theatre of operations the armed forces were acting in, to make decisions. That would enable the armed forces to meet the enormous difficulty that the Government face in trying to ensure that judicial officers are in the right place.

Mr. Blunt: I am grateful to the hon. Lady for her intervention, which exposes some of the issues and misconceptions. I thoroughly enjoyed our time on the Defence Committee and valued her expertise on defence medical services, but it does not apply to this issue. The problem of video links will be discussed later, but we must consider the difficulties associated with bandwidth and whether the picture will be good enough to allow the judicial officer to see the defendant. It is plainly not an easy matter. Otherwise, the Government would have produced a statutory instrument or included it in a schedule to the Bill.

The proposed alternative deals simply with remand, which used to be in the hands of the commanding officer. No one can say that it is unfair for a commanding officer to make decisions on remanding prisoners in custody based on legal advice obtained from the Army Legal Corps, which is the usual means for making such judgments. That is regular procedure, and it was followed in my regiment, where the commanding officer remanded people facing serious charges every eight days. A prisoner would be marched out of the guardroom and taken before the commanding officer. He would then be formally remanded and go back into a further eight days' custody while the pre-trial procedure continued. That is the simple procedure currently in place.

We are being told, however, that following the European court judgment in the case of Findlay--if I am correct--that is not acceptable. That is why such matters will now be dealt with by judicial officers. Those officers are, however, enormously expensive and it will sometimes be difficult for them to adjudicate on the simple remand decisions in question. We propose an alternative system of custodial officers who are not commanding officers but commissioned officers trained to whatever standards in custody law are deemed appropriate. They would be appointed by a major-general, an air vice marshal or a rear admiral two steps up the command chain and removed from the commanding officer himself. Such an alternative to judicial officers would provide flexibility when it was not possible for them to make remand decisions.

Mr. Blunt: I have given thought to those matters. I am obviously relying on my Army experience, but I believe that the general commanding officer would be likely to appoint such people. It is also likely that the post would relate to one of the divisional troops. The major commanding a Royal Military Police company would be part of the divisional troops available to the general officer in command, as would the equivalent in the other two services. Anyone to do with the military is part of a chain of command, which is the difficulty that we face in the Bill. The armed services are part of a chain of command starting with our Sovereign. We have discussed the independence of the judiciary and the legal system, but whatever system one creates, a case can be made to the European court to say that it is not independent. That applies not least because we live under a rather wonderful and peculiar constitution in the United Kingdom. Our constitution is unwritten, and the rules establishing the independence of the judiciary, the purpose of the Lord Chancellor being part of the Government and so on will be explored by the European court under the convention.

The amendments seek to present a reasonable alternative using the judgment of reasonable people, which, following the Findlay judgment, can be used by the armed services as a satisfactory alternative to expensive judicial officers. We should bear it in mind that such officers are either permanently employed as lawyers with an additional overhead or have to be brought in and paid a fee for their services to remand people in custody. Allowing a major commanding an RMP detachment. a lieutenant or a commissioned officer trained in custody law to exercise judgment on whether somebody should be in custody would solve a vast part of the problem of the legal overheads imposed on the armed forces by the new custody rules. It is a substantial overhead, and the cost is being borne by the Ministry of Defence, as was made clear in the other place, but every opportunity we have to reduce the burden on the defence budget and to invest that money in the front-line capability of the armed forces, rather than on legal overhead, is a step forward. If we can establish a system of custodial officers, we will reduce that legal overhead.

My amendments are different from those tabled by my hon. Friend the Member for Salisbury (Mr. Key). Instead of deleting "judicial" and inserting "duty", as my hon. Friend's amendment would, I propose to include the words, "a judicial or custodial officer." That would give the armed forces the opportunity to use a system of custodial officers to see whether it works.

If someone were to challenge that successfully in the European Court, at the appropriate moment, in a quinquennial Bill or on the happy day that we have a tri-service discipline Act, the words "or custodial officer" could simply be deleted. The amendment would give the armed forces flexibility to try out a system that consists of a general officer in command, who appoints a custodial officer, who is outside the immediate chain of command of the individual, and who is a reasonable person in the judgment of reasonable people--which the European court judges should be but, in the eyes of the armed forces, have not always been as a result of the peculiar decisions that they have made.

The amendment does not prevent the use of judicial officers if they happen to be easier and more available, but the system that I propose will certainly be considerably cheaper and easier to administer. Therefore, I hope that the Minister and his officials, who, I hope, have had an opportunity during the past 24 hours to examine the proposals, will see that the amendment takes nothing away from the Bill. The proposals give the armed forces flexibility and, if it is decided that the system of custodial officers works, it will offer an alternative to the issue of remand in custody and present the opportunity to remove a huge administrative burden from the armed forces and the defence budget. Therefore, I commend the amendments to the Committee.
