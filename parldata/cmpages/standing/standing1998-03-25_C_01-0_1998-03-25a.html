Clause 1

Energy efficiency surveys of dwellings

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this, we may take new clause 1--Energy efficiency report--

    `.--(1) Subject to the provisions of this Act, it shall be the duty of any person in whose favour a mortgage of any legal estate in a dwelling is granted (in this Act referred to as ``the lender'') to ensure that, if any qualifying survey of the dwelling has been undertaken by him or on his behalf in connection with the mortgage--

    (a) a report on the energy efficiency fo the dwelling (in this Act referred to as ``an energy efficiency report'') has been prepared by or on behalf of the lender in accordance with regualtions under section (Regulations) on the basis of the physical inspection undertaken for the purpose of the qualifying survey, and

    (b) the person granting the mortgage (in this Act referred to as ``the borrower'') has been provided, before the grant of the mortgage, with a copy of the energy efficiency report.

    (2) No duty arises under subsection (1) unless--

    (a) the mortgage relates only--

    (i) to a single dwelling, or

    (ii) to a single dwelling together with premises occupied or intended to be occumpied for business purposes,

    (b) the loan to which the mortgage relates was made in pursuance of an applciation made to the lender by the borrower,

    (c) the borrower is an individual, and

    (d) the dwelling is occupied by, or is intended for occupation by, the borrower as his residence.

    (3) The lender may, in fixing the amount of any fee to be charged for a qualifying survey, take into account any additional costs reasonably incurred inpreparing the energy efficiency report, but the duty in subsection (1) arises whether or not the borrower has paid, or agreed to pay, any such additional costs.

    (4) In subsection (1) a ``qualifying survey'', in relation to a dwelling, means any survey or valuation which includes a physical inspection of both the exterior and the interior of the dwelling.'.

New clause 2--Exemption where dwelling provided within previous three years--

    `.The duty in section (Energy efficiency report) does not arise in relation to any dwelling which was provided (by construction or conversion) less than three years before the date on which the borrower applied for the loan to which the mortgage relates.'.

New clause 3--Exemption where report prepared within previous twelve months--

    `.The duty in section (Energy efficiency report) does not arise where, before the grant of the mortgage, the borrower has obtained, or been given a copy of, a report which--

    (a) relates to the dwelling,

    (b) complies with regulations under section (Regulations), and

    (c) was prepared less than twelve months before the date on which the borrower applied for the loan to which the mortgage relates.'.

New clause 4--Exemption for loans between family members and by family trusts--

    `.--(1) The duty in section (Energy efficiency report) does not arise where--

    (a) the lender is a member of the borrower's family and is not lending in the course of a business carried on by the lender, or

    (b) the lenders are the trustees of a trust created by an individual for the benefit of members of his family and their descendants.

    (2) For the purposes of subsection (1) a person is a member of another's family if--

    (a) he is the spouse of that person, or he and that person live together as husband and wife, or

    (b) he is that person's parent, grandparent, child, grandchild, brother, sister, uncle, aunt, nephew or niece.

    (3) For the purposes of subsection (2)(b)--

    (a) a relationship by marriage shall be treated as a relationship by blood,

    (b) a relationship of the half-blood shall be treated as a relationship of the whole blood, and

    (c) the stepchild of a person shall be treated as his child.'.

New clause 6--Compensation for failure to give a copy of report--

    `.--(1) If the borrower suffers loss by reason of the failure of the lender to comply with his duty under section (Energy efficiency report), the borrower is entitled to compensation for that loss from the lender.

    (2) In determining for the purposes of this section whether the borrower has suffered loss as mentioned in subsection (1) and, if so, the amount of that loss, a court--

    (a) shall assume that the borrower would have taken every measure that would have been recommended in a report complying with section (Regulations),

    (b) shall have regard to the cost that would be likely to have been incurred in taking those measures if they had been taken immediately after the grant of the mortgage,

    (c) shall determine the likely expected life of each measure and apportion that cost rateably over that life, and

    (d) shall calculate the savings that would have been made during the relevant period if the measures had been taken.

    (3) In subsection (2) ``the relevant period'' means the period beginning with the day on which the mortgage was granted and ending immediately before the sixth anniversary of that day or, if earlier--

    (a) in relation to any measure falling within subsection (2)(a) which has in fact been taken by the borrower, with the day on which the taking of the measure is completed,

    (b) in a case where the lender provides the borrower with, or with a copy of, an energy efficiency report complying with section (Regulations) prepared not less than 12 months before the day on which it is provided, that day,

    (c) the day on which the borrower ceases to own any interest in the dwelling,

    (d) the death of the borrower, or

    (e) the day on which any proceedings under this section are first determined by a court or by the award of an arbitrator.'.

Mr. John Burnett (Torridge and West Devon): I am grateful to you, Mr. Cook, for chairing the Committee and for the assistance that you and the Clerk have provided. I am also grateful to the Minister and her officials, with whom we have discussed the Bill over a long period of time.

The Bill requires mortgage lenders such as banks and building societies to carry out an energy efficiency survey as part of any survey they require before lending money on a house. The Bill aims to provide information to house buyers about the energy efficiency of their home and methods of improving that. We expect that showing the cost-effective measures that canbe undertaken will lead house buyers to carry out improvements, especially as people are more likely to improve their new homes at present. That will reduce the energy needed to heat homes and thus create financial and environmental benefits.

The Bill does not impose onerous duties. It applies only when a surveyor is already inside the property. The additional time required for the energy efficiency aspect of a survey is likely to be five to 10 minutes. It will add £5 to £15 to the survey's cost. That is a modest price, which could easily be repaid by savings from undertaking the work that the survey recommends. It is a small amount compared with the large cost of moving house, which is usually several thousand pounds.

The Bill received an unopposed Second Reading in February and was warmly welcomed by the three main political parties. Indeed, I believe it was welcomed by all parties, including the numerous Northern Ireland parties. The Government agreed to provide drafting assistance and all amendments apart from amendment (a) to new clause 5 were drafted by parliamentary counsel, to whom I am also grateful.

New clauses 1 to 4 would delete the Bill that was discussed on Second Reading. [Interruption.] As the Division bells are ringing, Mr. Cook, may I ask your guidance as to what happens now? Do we abandon ship and then return?

Mr. Burnett: I shall go through the new clauses as swiftly as I can. New clause 1(1) puts a duty on the mortgage lender to prepare an energy report in accordance with regulations made by the Secretary of State and to make that report available to the borrower. Subsection (2) makes it clear that the report need be produced only for purchasers of residential properties. It does not apply to multiple dwelling purchasers, but only to dwellings being purchased for owner occupation.

New clause 1(3) would allow the mortgage lender to take account of the cost of the energy efficiency report when deciding how much to charge for the survey. The fact that

    ``the duty in subsection (1) arises whether or not the borrower has paid, or agreed to pay, any such additional costs'',

means that even if a borrower should refuse to pay for the energy efficiency report, the mortgage lender would still have to prepare one. That blocks a loophole that could otherwise exist should a customer refuse to pay the extra cost. It will not be a burden on mortgage lenders, as they will refuse to carry out the survey or produce an energy efficiency report unless the borrower has agreed to pay for it.

New clause 1(4) defines a ``qualifying survey''. The energy efficiency report will need to be produced only as part of a qualifying survey, which means that it will be required only if a surveyor can inspect both the interior and exterior of a building. Surveyors would thus not need to make a special trip to produce an energy efficiency report, which would be time consuming and onerous.

New clause 2 would exempt houses less than three years old from needing an energy efficiency report. New clause 3 would exempt properties if a report had been prepared and provided to the borrower during the previous 12 months. New clause 4(1) provides that an energy efficiency report would not be required if the lender was a member of the borrower's family or the trustees of a family trust. Subsections (2) and (3) define exactly what the Bill means by a member of a family.

New clause 6 provides for compensation to be paid for a failure to give a copy of the report. The new clause is the result of advice from parliamentary counsel that a sanction should be included in the Bill. Subsection (1) would entitle the borrower to compensation if a lender failed to provide a report. Subsection (2) sets out what compensation a borrower would be entitled to. The loss is to be calculated as the savings that would have resulted had the measures that should have been in the report been carried out. Subsection (3) defines the period over which that loss should be calculated, which is six years at most from the granting of the mortgage. That time would be reduced if, first, energy efficiency measures had been taken earlier than the end of the six-year period; secondly, a report had been produced by the lender later than the granting of the mortgage but before the end of the six years; thirdly, the borrower had ceased to own the property; fourthly, the borrower had died; or, finally, the matter had been brought to court for arbitration.

 

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Angela Eagle): May I say what a pleasure it is to serve under your chairmanship, Mr. Cook. We have not met like this before; I hope that this is the first of many such occasions.

On Second Reading, I made clear the Government's support for the principles that underlie the Bill. The measures proposed by the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon (Mr. Burnett) have the potential to make a valuable contribution to improving energy efficiency in the home and thereby to help us reach the targets on emissions savings that we signed up to at the Kyoto climate change conference.

I also explained on Second Reading that we saw difficulties with the original drafting of the Bill, which is why we have tabled the new clauses. I am grateful to the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon for his co-operation as we worked out how best to achieve the principles and aims of the Bill. I am grateful for all the co-operation that there has been in the interim and I believe that we have come up with a good rendering of what the hon. Gentleman wanted to achieve in the Bill.

Clause 1 requires the lender to provide the borrower with information on the energy efficiency of the dwelling--``dwelling'' is defined in clause 3. As I said on Second Reading, the Government do not want the Bill to apply to all dwellings. The Bill will apply when there is a survey or valuation in connection with an application for a mortgage, for example, to assist with the purchase of a single dwelling for owner occupation. That would be the most obvious and convenient time for the work to be done. We propose that the Bill should apply to mortgage applications from prospective homeowners and mortgages and re-mortgage applications from existing homeowners. It should apply, too, to mortgages enabling a leaseholder to purchase the freehold of his house, or to a long leaseholder to extend his lease or to purchase a new one.

The Government do not want the Bill to apply to surveys or valuations in connection with applications for mortgages or re-mortgages for the purchase of residential property by a landlord which is, or will be, let for occupation by tenants. That includes cases when a group of leaseholders purchase the freehold of a block of flats under part I of the Leasehold Reform, Housing and Urban Development Act 1993.

It is necessary to exclude dealings with the Landlord's interest in residential property for several reasons and I shall take a little of the Committee's time to set them on the record. Leases will usually give the landlord or his agent, a right of entry for limited purposes only, in connection with the lease, for example, a right to enter and inspect in connection with a landlord's repairing obligation. Section 11(6) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 provides that a landlord has a right of entry only in connection with a landlord's repairing obligation imposed by that section. If the Bill applied to dealings with the landlord's interest, a right of access to a tenant's home would be required for a surveyor or valuer, instructed by a lender to a potential purchaser of a landlord's interest. The Government do not intend to legislate to give a lender's surveyor such a right of access. Further, in the case of leases granted for a term of 21 years or more, a landlord would not usually have a right of entry to carry out work on the property identified in an energy report.

As drafted, clause 1 would require an energy efficiency report to be prepared whenever a lender requires a survey or valuation, which is defined in clause 3 as meaning

    ``any physical inspection of the property''.

That could be expensive because the ``physical inspection'' could consist of a brief inspection of the exterior of the dwelling, leading to a special visit to obtain the information required for the energy efficiency report. The new clause makes it clear that the report should be prepared only when the survey or valuation would require a physical inspection of the interior and exterior of the property and provides for the energy efficiency report to be based on that inspection. That will ensure, as far as possible, that the provision of the information on energy efficiency will not be excessively costly. The Bill should not require costly special visits to the dwelling to obtain the information required. I know that there is widespread agreement about that.

Clause 1(2), as drafted, will exempt mortgage lenders from providing an energy efficiency report if the dwelling is less than three years old at the time of the survey or valuation. That is helpful as it will exempt properties under the Building Regulations (Amendment) Regulations 1994, which have increased energy efficiency requirements significantly. In future, the more recently built properties will be exempt because it is expected that they will be close to the then prevailing energy efficiency requirements. We want to put a duty on those who are purchasing properties only when it is necessary.

To avoid confusion, new clause 2 clarifies the detail of clause 1(2) as drafted. It makes it clear that the provision relates to dwellings created by conversion, as well as those that are newly built. The three-year period will start on the date of the application for the loan to which the mortgage relates.

The Bill would exempt mortgage lenders from providing an energy efficiency report if the borrower had equivalent information, which was prepared within the previous 12 months. We agree with that in principle. If the borrower had the up-to-date information required under the Bill, there would be no need to prepare a new energy efficiency report. For example, the borrower might have applied to another lender for a mortgage on the same property. In addition, an option being considered under the Government's housing transaction study is the possibility of the seller providing more information, perhaps including information on energy efficiency, at the start of the process.

New clause 3 clarifies the meaning of the Bill. It makes it clear, first, that the information must relate to the dwelling in question and, secondly, that the report must comply with regulation prepared under the Bill. It also makes it clear that the 12-month period starts on the date of the application for the loan to which the mortgage relates.

Clause 3 would exempt from the definition in the Bill of a mortgage lender an individual or individuals acting as such or as a family trust. The meaning of that is unclear. For example, the exclusion of ``an individual'' would exclude some professional money lenders. New clause 4 clarifies that issue by restricting the exemption, first, to loans between members of a family where the loan is not made in the course of business and, secondly, to loans made to beneficiaries by the trustees of family trusts.

New clause 4 clarifies the meaning of the term ``family trust'' as

    ``a trust created by an individual for the benefit of members of his family and their descendants''

and defines those who can be counted as family members.

It is important that any law is enforceable. Therefore, we are concerned that the Bill contains no sanction against mortgage lenders that do not provide borrowers with an energy efficiency report. New clause 6 would remedy that shortcoming by setting out the arrangements for compensating the borrower should the mortgage lender fail to provide an energy efficiency report as required under the Bill. The new clause specifies that the borrower is entitled to compensation if he suffers loss because of the failure of the mortgage lender to comply with the duties of the Bill and deals with the way in which the courts should determine whether the borrower has suffered a loss and, if so, how the amount of that loss should be calculated.

Angela Eagle: Thank you for your clarification earlier, Mr. Cook. These amendments and new clause 5 would delete the clause as drafted which deals with guidance issued by the Secretary of State and replace it with a new clause which would require the Secretary of State to specify the requirements to be met by the lender in regulations.

I shall take a little time to deal with new clause 5 because, following advice from parliamentary draftsmen, we find ourselves in a slightly different position from the one we were in on Second Reading. It is important to explain to the Committee why. I said then that the form of advice to be offered would be the subject of statutory guidance from the Secretary of State and that we see no need for primary legislation to specify in detail what that guidance should contain.

On reflection, we have concluded that the advice should be included in secondary legislation. That is partly because of a code of practice which those who draw up legislation for us issue to ensure consistency between different statutes. It appears that if the criminal or civil law is involved in enforcement, it is always seen as better to have a statutory presence rather than simply guidance.

Regulations would effectively replace guidance on the face of the Bill which is what new clause 5 does. They would be made by statutory instrument and they would have two advantages apart from ensuring that the legislation was in keeping with similar Acts and conformed to what is almost a house style. First, they would be readily available to the public at large. Secondly, they would be more appropriate to the circumstances than guidance. That is because the guidance would place specific legal obligations on mortgage lenders rather than simply explaining or supplementing the Bill's provisions. In that instance, it is the current form to ensure that they are regulations rather than issued guidance.

Where specific legal obligations are imposed, breach of which leads to civil liability, the legal obligations will be spelt out in secondary legislation. Subsections (1) and (2) set out the duty to make regulations and give an example of the items that the regulations may require, but they do not tie the Secretary of State to a list of items that must be included in the regulations.

As I said, we see no need for primary legislation to specify in detail what the guidance should include. We want flexibility over time as technologies change, so that our understanding of how energy efficiency can be better achieved can be properly reflected in subsequent regulations.

Subsections (3) to (7) contain a number of safeguards to ensure that the regulations are flexible and do not impose undue burdens on mortgage lenders, or undue costs on borrowers as both factors play an important part in the Bill's success. Flexibility is provided by subsection (3) which allows regulations to make different provisions for different cases and different areas. I do not see how we can be more flexible.

Burdens on mortgage lenders and costs for borrowers are limited by subsections (4) to (7). Subsections (4) and (5) restrict the information to be provided on a dwelling to that which is readily ascertainable without undue difficulty on a visual inspection of the building and may exempt the common parts of a building containing a flat from the requirements of the Bill.

Subsections (6) and (7) require the Secretary of State to consult before issuing regulations and to have regard to the cost of preparing an energy report in making those regulations. Those are sensible safeguards to ensure that the Bill stays focused on its primary intention, which the hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon explained well on Second Reading.

Subsection (8) provides that the regulations should be made by statutory instrument subject to negative resolution. Subsection (9) clarifies the preceding subsections by defining the terms used in them.

The system of house purchase in Scotland essentially involves blind bids around an asking price. New clause 1

Energy efficiency report

    `.--(1) Subject to the provisions of this Act, it shall be the duty of any person in whose favour a mortgage of any legal estate in a dwelling is granted (in this Act referred to as ``the lender'') to ensure that, if any qualifying survey of a dwelling has been undertaken by him or on his behalf in connection with the mortgage--
