Mr. Bruce: I remind the Committee that I have made several statements about my interests in electricity, telecommunications and so on. I wish to make it clear that this group of amendments and others are mine alone: I thought them up on my own, and did not table them on anyone's behalf. Indeed, I have no registrable interest in the strict sense that pertains to the Register of Members' Interests.

Committee Members may find it strange that my amendments attempt to widen the scope of the Bill. However, they are probing amendments, designed to get to grips with what the Government intend to do with so much regulation. I shall remind members of the Committee of the reason for regulation in such fields.

I have a long-standing interest in energy matters. Indeed, when the Conservative Government set about privatising the energy industry, I was especially interested in how that was going to happen. Colleagues who have been in Parliament a while will remember Sir Walter Marshall, a former chairman of the Central Electricity Generating Board, telling the then Government and, indeed, every Member of Parliament whom he could get hold of, that it was impossible to privatise the electricity industry and break it up at the same time. He said that the CEGB was a natural monopoly and would need to be privatised as a single entity because having lots of different bits competing against one another would be unachievable. The Conservative Government disagreed with that, as we wanted a market mechanism allowing prices to be driven down. We therefore decided to privatise the industry by breaking it into constituent parts—the original intention was to split it in two—to create competitive businesses and encourage others to come in.

Sir Walter disagreed and resigned as chairman of the CEGB. Privatisation was not easy, as people could not envisage how the industry would be broken up. Special problems arose in relation to the nuclear part of the industry, which no one wanted to buy and which was regarded by the City as a total liability with no benefits. The City wanted the Government to give away the whole electricity industry, along with a few extra millions or billions to get it off their hands. That was extraordinary, especially as we now know how valuable the electricity industry was.

When John Wakeham decided not: proceed with the privatisation of the nuclear industry on becoming Secretary of State for Energy, I wanted to find out why. I remember standing in the Lobby behind Baroness Thatcher, who had just arrived back from an overseas visit and was rather worried that, in rushing to fix everything, the new Secretary of State was taking the nuclear industry out of the privatisation. However, that was necessary to launch the other part of the strategy.

Mr. Hood, you have been patient with me. However, I was putting into context what I want to say about the energy industry. That industry needed little regulation when it was nationalised. The then Government knew best. If consumers thought electricity was too expensive, a Minister would get up in Parliament and say that electricity prices would be put up by only half the rate of inflation or whatever. It was almost in the gift of Ministers to decide. Of course, markets are not run in that way.

Members on both sides of the House now accept that allowing private enterprise to operate in the energy market creates benefits for everyone. Not least of the benefits is the fact that the Exchequer no longer has to subsidise the energy industry; the taxes paid by shareholders in privatised industries alone represent a massive amount of money for the Treasury. Hon. Members can see that our aim is to make those markets work better. The White Paper ``Modem markets: confident consumers'', published in July, states:

    ``The Government will look first for opportunities to make markets work, including through better information and self-regulation . . . Open and competitive markets are the best guarantee of a good deal for consumers.''

I tabled the amendments to tease out from the Government the reason why we have such a massive Bill, in which the Government appear to be saying, ``We have launched the electricity and gas industries on to the marketplace. We have had great difficulty, and need to regulate quite tightly to make sure that those markets work.'' Now that the markets are starting to work and people can decide who will supply electricity down the one available wire to their house, or gas through the one available pipeline, the Government are saying, ``Privatisation was a terrible thing. The safeguards for the consumer were insufficient. Let's set up an authority to regulate the electricity and gas industries''.

The Government are also saying that there should be a statutory level of consumer support in the system, despite the fact that we have experienced the awkward transition from a nationalised industry that was virtually unregulated—Ministers were left to decide what was in the public interest—to regulation that was supposed to work for the public good in what is almost a free market. One might argue that the aim should now be to get rid of regulation, yet the Government are saying that on those particular items—electricity and gas—there must be more regulation.
