    (b)in a case where paragraph 15 above applies, the time when the loan is made.".

I hope that today's proceedings are long and fruitful, and that the tone of the debate and the room temperature remain similarly moderate.

The amendment will correct a minor technical defect in provisions that bring reinvestment relief and the existing enterprise investment scheme together in the new, unified EIS.

Where chargeable gains are invested in companies and the tax on the gain is deferred under the new EIS, a number of provisions will protect the relief from being exploited. Two such rules will prevent abuse of the scheme where the investor receives value from the company or a loan is made to the investor on terms that are linked to the investment. They will ensure that relief is not available in circumstances where the investor's money is effectively returned. Equivalent provisions have always applied for reinvestment relief.

The amendment will provide for the two anti-avoidance measures to take effect where the investment is made by a trust and the loan or value is received by a beneficiary rather than the trustees. There is a corresponding fine-tuning provision for reinvestment relief, but it was overlooked when schedule 13 was drafted. Failing to close the gap in the new EIS provisions would provide trusts with tax-saving opportunities that could bring the scheme into disrepute. It is clearly desirable that the amendment be made.

Carry forward of non-trading deficit on loan relationships
 

Mr. Gibb: I beg to move amendment No. 37, in page 67, line 7, at end insert

    `Section 797(3) shall have effect as if any amount carried forward to the applicable accounting period under section 83(2)(d) of the Finance Act 1996 were an amount capable of being allocated only to any non-trading profits of the company.'

    (d) in section 797A(7) insert at the end

    'In determining whether the company has a non-trading deficit for the applicable accounting period, any deficit carried forward to that period under the provisions of section 83(3) Finance Act 1996 is to be ignored.'.".

The clause relates to a complex area of tax law, which relates to the taxation of interest income and expenses. I apologise to the Economic Secretary for having such a large comfort blanket, but I do not have the advantage, which she has, of four or five highly expert Inland Revenue officials passing her notes every two or three minutes.

The taxation regime for income and expenses was changed by the previous Government in 1996. The clause seeks to remedy a remaining anomaly. Unfortunately, the way in which the clause is drafted means that it not only fails to remedy the anomaly but changes the regime for the worst, and does so retrospectively.

The new regime means that if there is an excess of interest expense over interest income on a loan taken out for non-trade purposes for example, the acquisition of an overseas subsidiary that excess is called, in the jargon, a non-trading deficit. It is possible to offset that deficit against other profits, make it subject to group relief or carry it back to previous years. If a company does not do one of those three things, under this clause that deficit can be carried forward as a non-trading deficit arising in the next accounting period.

Previously an unrelieved non-trading deficit was treated as a non-trading debit arising in the next accounting period. The distinction between a deficit and a debit, to which I hope Labour Members will pay attention, is important. On a strict reading of the law, debits that were carried forward would have to be included in a calculation of profit or loss in the following accounting period. That meant that the debit would be automatically offset before any other reliefs, such as double tax relief, were available. As a result of that, double tax credit relief would often be lost, because the loss brought forward would be automatically offset.

As every member of the Committee will know, double tax relief cannot be carried forward from one year to the next in other words, the overseas tax paid is wasted. To avoid that, the Revenue introduced a concession whereby deficits could be treated as deficits in the following year. The clause purports to make that concessionary basis a statutory basis. Unfortunately, the clause does not amend section 797A of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. The unintended result of that omission is that where a non-trading deficit is brought forward under section 83(3) of the Finance Act 1986, an equivalent amount of current period non-trading debits would compulsorily have to be relieved against the company's current period non-trading loan credits. That would give rise to a wasted double tax relief, which was not the Government's intention in the clause.

The amendment would remedy that oversight. If the Government do not accept it, there will be a retrospective restriction on a company's ability to claim double tax credit relief. I therefore hope that the Government accept it.
