Clause 166

Rights of entry

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

 

Mr. Gerald Howarth: I apologise for detaining the Committee, but I want to probe the Minister a little further on the clause, which provides for a right of entry into premises by officials of the licensing authority. It is incumbent on us as parliamentarians to provide a power of entry to officials only sparingly, and conscious of the power that we are conferring on them. Officials already have an enormous range of powers to enter our premises and business premises, and the public would expect us to provide such an additional power only in exceptional circumstances where it was justified.

Is it necessary for officials to have a power to enter premises without the owner's authority to establish whether they are providing workplace parking spaces without a licence, or without a licence covering all the workplace parking spaces being provided?

I accept that subsection (2) states that

    A person duly authorised in writing by the licensing authority . . . may at any reasonable time enter any premises for the purpose of issuing notice of a licensing scheme penalty charge.

However, I put it to the Minister that that could be dealt with in another way, perhaps by registered letter. Whether or not the recipient was willing to accept the charge, the fact that a registered letter had been sent would be sufficient notice of delivery of the penalty. I question whether we are right, in the circumstances, to allow this additional right of entry into private premises to be authorised by statute.

Mr. Raynsford: Clause 166 gives licensing authorities the power to enter and inspect premises to check whether a licence's terms are being complied with, or, if the premises has no licence, whether workplace parking is being provided. There are important safeguards, which I hope will reassure the hon. Member for Aldershot; he has recognised some of them.

Anyone seeking to exercise this power must be duly authorised by the licensing authority in writing. It will not be open to an individual simply to turn up and say: ``I wish to inspect your premises.'' That individual must be duly authorised by the licensing authority. As the hon. Gentleman said, the power may be used only at a reasonable time, and specific reasons are set out clause 166(1)(a) and (b), which govern the way in which the power can be used. In other words, the person inspecting the premises must look to see whether any workplace parking is being provided without a licence, or with a licence that does not cover all the workplace parking provided. That person could also look for a contravention of the conditions of a licence. The authorised person must produce evidence of his authority before entering.

Those are reasonable safeguards for the organisation of which the premises may be liable for inspection. I hope that the hon. Gentleman will accept that, as we are creating a liability of this nature, it would clearly be wrong for organisations to be able to defy it with total impunity because the local authority had no means of ascertaining whether there had been a contravention.

In due course, with the development of GPS systems, I have no doubt that satellites will provide all the necessary information, and the number of Challenger tanks parked in any particular square yard of the hon. Gentleman's constituency will be well known. In the meantime, it may be necessary to carry out inspections to ensure the integrity of the scheme. I hope that the hon. Gentleman accepts that proper safeguards will exist to prevent misuse of this power, and that this is a proportionate response to the enforcement requirements of the scheme.

Mr. Howarth: I am grateful to the Minister for that response. I can see that if someone wilfully wishes to circumvent the scheme, there must be a means of ascertaining whether they are behaving lawfully. However, I am sorry that the Minister did not take the opportunity to acknowledge that Parliament should grant sparingly the power to officials to enter private premises.

The Minister said that a person had to be duly authorised in writing. However, anyone with similar powers of entry is duly authorised. It is a question of bureaucracy. I would like to reflect on that matter, and perhaps revisit it on Report.

Question put and agreed to.

Clause 166 ordered to stand part of the Bill.
