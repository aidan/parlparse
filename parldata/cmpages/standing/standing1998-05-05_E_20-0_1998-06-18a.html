Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): At the end of our previous sitting, I was explaining that the landfill tax resulted from discussions that took place between the Dept of the Environment, when it was headed by my right hon. Friend the Member for Suffolk, Coastal (Mr. Gummer), and the Treasury, when it was headed by right hon. and learned Friend the Member for Rushcliffe (Mr. Clarke). In those early discussions, it was not clear which agency should administer the tax. The Dept of the Environment saw it as an environmental tax. It, therefore, considered that the tax should be administered and collected by itself, by an agency reporting to it or by local authorities. However, the Treasury saw it as a matter for Customs and Excise, and that is my strongly held view. The Treasury's view eventually prevailed.

I mention those discussions because they show that this is not a straightforward tax. It cannot be compared to the taxes on petrol or tobacco. In those cases, the products subject to tax are readily identifiable and familiar. Therefore, it is appropriate to increase those taxes on Budget day by resolution and for the debate on them to be held subsequently, if only to stop forestalling.

The landfill tax is a good deal more complex. Waste is not always easy to define. One person's waste is someone else's raw material. The by-product of an industrial process may be a nuisance for the industry concerned, but it may be possible to put it to a productive use. In some instances, the so-called waste can be sold and form the raw material for another process. Is it correct to classify such material as waste?

Those and other considerations took many months to resolve. Eventually, the previous Government settled on two rates for the tax. One was for inert waste and the other was for active waste. The present Government have kept that distinction and, indeed, widened the tax applicable to the two rates by putting up the standard rate to £10.

A good many exemptions also exist. When the tax was announced and put out to consultation, many industries wrote in and came to see us with good or at least plausible arguments as to why their waste should not bear the tax. For example, those who dredge harbours and canals made the point that the material that they scoop out on to the canal bank is not really waste. It may appear, and occasionally smell, like waste, but such companies engage in an important and useful activity. It would greatly increase their costs if such dumped material were classified as waste.

That problem was easy to resolve, but others created more difficulties. British Sugar came to see us about the soil that is washed off sugar beet. Although I am not tremendously familiar with that form of agriculture because sugar is grown in the eastern counties, I believe that when sugar beet arrives for processing, the soil that adheres to its roots has to be washed off and dumped somewhere. Is that soil waste? In our view, it probably is not, because it is productive and could probably be returned to the field from which it came. However, the practice in the industry is to landfill it. As the Bill was originally drafted, it attracted the lower rate of landfill tax, which would have greatly increased the industry's costs. I do not remember how that matter was resolved.

Landfill tax is not a standard tax that fits easily into an existing category, and the Government's contention that it should be subject to the Provisional Collection of Taxes Act 1968 is not necessarily valid. One reason for taking that view is the fact that there are many contracts between waste producers and disposers of waste. To take a simple example, someone who is building a house produces much waste and rubble, which is subject to the landfill tax when it is disposed of. If the tax was increased on Budget day, the house builder's costs would increase. He will almost certainly have a contract to dispose of waste in a landfill site, and he will have built that cost into his original calculations. His estimate that he can build the house for £50,000 will be based on the current rate of landfill tax.

People would be adversely affected if the Government suddenly doubled the rate of landfill tax. Either the landfill operator would not be able to cover his costs or if he successfully passed the costs up the chain to, in this case, the house builder costs elsewhere would rise unexpectedly and, in my view, unfairly.

Raising the tax is not like putting up the tax on cigarettes. The increase will interfere with several pre-negotiated contracts, which may be binding, and with the costings on which people have legitimately relied.It is right and fair for Parliament to debate the proposals before the tax increase takes effect.

I clearly remember rejecting the advice that the tax should be subject to the Provisional Collection of Taxes Act 1968 because I thought that it was right for the powers to remain with the House of Commons. The Minister might say, "Parliament will decide whether to support the increase, and it could decide to reject it." However, we all know in practice that that is not likely to happen. The debate will take place after the increase, and it will in practice almost certainly not be possible to protect the interests of those who will be affected by the tax increase.

There is more to this innocent looking clause than first meets the eye. Other proposals in the Bill will dramatically reduce the opportunities for scrutiny, especially in relation to the use of secondary legislation,[Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory] which opens a whole new subject that we shall, I hope, discuss later today.

It is wise to keep the landfill tax procedures as they are. They have not, to my knowledge, created any difficulties. They may be a minor inconvenience to the Government, but we should set that against our duties in Committee and in Parliament to scrutinise the powers and to ensure that any tax increase does not unnecessarily inadvertently damage taxpayers and the firms for which they work.
