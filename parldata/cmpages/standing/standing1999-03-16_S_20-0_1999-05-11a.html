Mr. Clappison: Amendment No. 366 relates to subsection (4), which deals with the matters that the Secretary of State has, and has not, to have regard to in the exercise of this power to provide essential living needs. The Immigration Law Practitioners Association—ILPA—observes that this is yet another clause that is entirely devoid of specific content and that, once again, Parliament is being asked to sign a blank cheque. ILPA has suggested the amendment, which would delete subsection (4).

Amendment No. 367 was also recommended by ILPA. It deals with a subject that we have touched on already and to which the Minister has given a reply. It relates to how a supported person will meet the expenses of a claim to asylum and any appeals arising from that. I have reflected on the Minister's words and on the clause's contents, and I am not entirely happy with the way that things stand.

Subsection (6) provides that expenses met in connection with a supported person's asylum appeal will be treated as essential living needs for the purposes of the expenditure cap in subsection (5). Clause 75(1)(c) states that support may be provided

    ``to enable the supported person to meet what appear to the Secretary of State to be expenses (other than legal expenses or other expenses of a prescribed description) incurred in connection with his claim for asylum and any appeal which may be made in relation to it.''

However, clause 76 states that expenses incurred in connection with a person's claim for asylum and any appeal made in relation to it will be treated as essential living needs unless they are legal expenses or expenses of a prescribed description.

I have reflected on what the Minister said in an earlier debate, but I am hard put to understand how any of a supported person's expenses in connection with his claim for asylum and any appeal arising out of it should be treated as essential living expenses. We had a long debate on the extent of support that asylum seekers will receive for their essential living expenses. Essential living needs mean food, and it concerns me that the costs of making a claim for asylum or for making an appeal may be deducted from essential living expenses. It is pernicious—to choose a word—for asylum seekers to have to decide between whether to meet the costs of making a claim for asylum or bringing an appeal and feeding and supporting their families.

ILPA states that the proposal in the clause

    ``is pernicious, and would mean, for example, that the claimant called for interview who was housed far from his port of arrival, or whose appeal was heard at a distant location or was subject to adjournments, could find expenditure on his food or other daily needs reduced by the amount of his fares.''—[Official Report, Special Standing Committee, 17 March 1999; c. 248]

The Minister earlier seemed to row back from that proposal, and said that some of those expenses would not be treated as essential living expenses. However, how can he justify regarding any of those expenses as essential living expenses, especially when they will be deducted from the small sums being provided for essential living needs? I have outlined the meaning of the clause. It is unsatisfactory when a clause provides for one thing, yet the Minister claims that it provides for something else. He is trying to get out of its meaning. I am not happy with the basic principle of the clause, which provides that expenses incurred by a supported person in presenting a claim for asylum or an appeal are to be treated as essential living expenses. Those expenses will come out of the money that asylum seekers receive for food, and to support themselves and their families. I am not happy with that. The Minister should give a clearer justification of the clause and a more detailed explanation of what will constitute expenses under clause 75(1)(c).

The clause alarmed the Immigration Law Practitioners Association; ``pernicious'' is a good description of it.

Gentleman suggests. We need to take into account the personal circumstances of asylum seekers and the fact that they are likely to be in the country for only a few months. They will either be recognised as refugees—conferring full access to the benefits system, the right to work and other refugee provisions—or, if not, they will be removed. Asylum seekers will generally be provided with fully equipped accommodation, which is an important offer by the state to ensure that they will not have to meet many of the costs facing other people on income support.

It is worth comparing the support for asylum seekers now with their support under our system, taking into account the operation of the income support system. Comparison is important. The current system is ad hoc and varied in its application among local authorities. People in some areas do better than others. Some provision is not so good; some better elsewhere. We are seeking to put a national system in place, taking the burden off local authorities, many of which find it difficult to cope—especially if they have to make special provision for a small number of asylum seekers. The national system will allow them to be dealt with on a national basis.

The effect will be that some people will be somewhat better off than they are under the local authority system, though—arguably—not as well off as those in the income support system who are awaiting an initial decision. Nevertheless, in many cases, asylum seekers will be better off than those in the appeals system—port applicants and people who have applied in country. The system that we are creating will be somewhat better, but the hon. Member for Hallam has always been an advocate of the benefits system—an honourable position, which others may well adopt.

The Government have set out their position on the benefits system very clearly. The Conservatives introduced changes in 1996 and the fall of 14,320 applicants that year—spread across 27 of the 35 major nationalities—shows that those changes had an impact. The main fall in applications was from those in some African states, which was down by 11,255, and from Asia, down by 2,800. That is unlikely to have been caused by changes within the individual states, which are so diverse. It is more likely that developments in the United Kingdom were responsible.

The peak in applications of approximately 4,715 in November 1995 coincides roughly with the announcement in October 1995 of changes in benefits for asylum seekers. Applications fell consistently from that time until June 1996. The announcement of the withdrawal of benefits from a great proportion of people applying in-country was clearly reflected in a fall in such applications, from double the number of port applications in the first 10 months in 1995 to approximately the same from mid-1996 onwards. So it is clear that the fall in applications at that time was affected by the changes in the benefits regime that the Conservatives introduced.

People who are genuinely fleeing for their lives come not for benefits but for protection, shelter, food and help, all of which we will provide. Such people will not be deterred from seeking protection by changes in the benefits system. However, people who do not apply are usually those whose application would most likely be rejected because it breached immigration controls.

We want the need for cash and vouchers to meet medium-term expenses, to be limited. When deciding on support, we want to take account of people's likely needs. We do not wish to be constrained now to a particular approach, which would be the effect of amendment No. 366. We shall set out in detail how we see the support system developing, learning from experience and from the needs of asylum seekers as we go, and investigating what would be effective in supporting them adequately and properly. Subsection (4) gives us the discretion that we need.

Member for Hallam knows, is 90 per cent. We feel that the provision should be 70 per cent., and we are monitoring carefully the development of consultations. In our view, there is no direct parallel with the needs of people who receive income support who live permanently in the United Kingdom and those who come on a temporary basis as asylum seekers, because asylum support benefits do not cover some of the items that a person on income support would be expected to meet from his own resources. There is an indication of the basic amount of income support that a person in this country needs to get by in the medium term, on a temporary basis. I hardly need remind hon. Members that were we to provide a significantly higher amount of benefit for asylum seekers, many of whom would be shown to have made unfounded claims, we would, I suspect, be criticised by some constituents for showing favouritism. There is a huge difference between the £100 watch and the £500 piece of family jewellery, which as the hon. Member for Hackney, North and Stoke Newington (Ms Abbott) pointed out may have some cultural meaning beyond its cash value and £5,000 in gold bullion.

We could probably all agree a contribution may be required if someone has £5,000 in gold bullion, but not when we start falling below that, particularly below the £3,000 limit which we apply to income support recipients. There is a good reason for that limit: we expect people on income support at some stage to revert to normal life not supported by the state, and we do not try to make them destitute by removing any fixed assets during that period. It was done before the welfare state was introduced, but under our current modern welfare state, we do not believe that it is for the public good to make a person destitute.
