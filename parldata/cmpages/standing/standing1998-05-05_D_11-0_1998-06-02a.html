I know that the hon. Member for Ryedale (Mr. Greenway) asked about the Government's plans for bringing into force sections 112, 113 and 115 of the Police Act 1997, which provide for criminal record certificates. The Government are considering the timetable for implementing that legislation, but I apologise that I cannot give the Committee a precise date. The implementation of those provisions represents a major operation, as it involves establishing a criminal records agency to deal with records and to issue the new types of certificates. However, I confirm that the Government definitely plan to establish such an agency and that it is proposed that the three types of certificate will be introduced in sequence.

The first to be introduced will be the enhanced criminal records certificates, intended for the persons themselves and those organisations considering applicants for positions involving regular care for children and for positions involving the training, supervision and sole charge of children. Those certificates will also be needed for certain gambling and lottery licences. There is also a power to include those caring for vulnerable adults.

The second to be introduced will be the criminal records certificates, which will include spent and unspent convictions for certain organisations. Finally, the ordinary criminal conviction certificates will be introduced. They will be available directly to applicants for them to pass on to others with a relevant interest.

Clearly, the new enforced subject access offence that the Committee has debated can be brought into operation only when all three sets of certificates are available. However, it is important that priority be given, in the first instance, to the protection of children and vulnerable people.

The hon. Member for Ryedale also asked what will happen in the meantime, before the certificates become available. My hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, North-East (Mr. White) asked about the position of local authorities, for example, which rely at present on enforced subject access. The straight answer to the hon. Member for Ryedale is that, under the new clause, there can be no enforced subject access offence until the other vital element--criminal records certificates--is in place. That is a deliberate Government policy. As my hon. Friend the Under-Secretary said this morning, the fact that Parliament is creating an alternative route for the supply of conviction information eases the way to the creation of the offence.

Meanwhile, however, we must recognise the practical implications for any organisations employing or appointing people in sensitive posts. Whatever view one takes of subject access as a phenomenon, in the short term the loss of all access to criminal records information could cause those organisations significant operational difficulties.

It would be invidious to identify those cases in which we believe that the practice would be justified and those in which it would not. My hon. Friend the Member for Milton Keynes, North-East made a relevant point when he drew attention to local authorities. The interval before criminal records certificates become available will allow for a transition from current, established practice. The organisations involved can begin to prepare for a regime in which only authorised conviction information will be available.

Clause 63 provides the Bill's order-making powers, of which there are many. The Government suggest that those that are listed in subsection (4) should be subject to the affirmative resolution procedure. Subsection (5) lists those order-making powers that the Government suggest should be subject only to the negative resolution procedure. We believe that some of the powers listed in subsection (5) should be subject to an affirmative order. They should not merely provide an opportunity to pray against the orders that have been laid by the Secretary of State. The order-making powers are all important to the structure of the Bill, and many of them introduce new concepts that are not currently covered by the 1984 legislation.

Clause 21(7) is first on subsection (5)'s list of the powers that are subject to negative resolution. It refers to the preliminary assessments by the Data Protection Commissioner that provide 28 days for a response plus one extension. Those assessments involve cases in which there may be substantial damage or distress, or in which the rights and freedoms of individuals may be significantly prejudiced. Any order would seek to alter the 28-day period and the arrangements for an extension. I am sure that the Minister will agree that data subjects and data controllers need to know whether preliminary assessments are going to be determined in their favour. Alteration of the 28-day period would therefore be a serious matter, and we suggest that an affirmative order would be more appropriate.

Clause 22 is next on the list in subsection (5). This clause provides for the possible appointment of a data protection supervisor and the arrangements by which data protection supervisors might assume an independent role in monitoring data processing that is carried out by, or on behalf of, a data controller. That is an important role, and Parliament should have the opportunity to debate the matter before an order could take effect. Therefore an affirmative resolution would be more appropriate.

Clause 50(3) provides for the Secretary of State to decide which areas of data processing must follow codes of practice. That is also a potentially far-reaching provision. Commerce needs a chance to consider and make representations before any order-making power comes into effect. That could be realised only by an affirmative resolution procedure.

You will recall this morning's debate, Mr. Welsh, about the functions of the Data Protection Commissioner as the designated authority for the United Kingdom in respect of the European Union, and as the means of effecting the United Kingdom's international obligations. The Committee's view was confirmed by the Under-Secretary of State for the Home Department, the hon. Member for Knowsley, North and Sefton, East (Mr. Howarth)--he recognised that they were important powers and agreed that they should be covered by the Data Protection Commissioner's annual report. He also agreed that clause 51(1) already provided a mechanism for that. Given the importance of those functions, the orders giving them effect should be subject to the affirmative rather than the negative resolution.

I stress that the affirmative resolution procedure would give those affected by a prospective order the opportunity to consider it and to lobby Members of Parliament as appropriate, so that they could agree on what should be done, rather than trying to pray against the order. The other paragraphs in the schedules are important for the interpretation of various parts of the Bill. Because many provisions break new ground, Parliament ought to have the opportunity to consider them in advance of any order coming into effect which hon. Members might want to annul, under the negative resolution procedure.

I hope that the Minister agrees that an affirmative resolution procedure is appropriate for at least some, if not all, of the provisions in the negative resolution list. In particular, I would single out clause 50(3) and subsections (2), (3) and (4) of clause 53, which we feel are extremely important. We welcome the Government's decision to subject the order-making powers under the provisions listed in subsection (4) to the affirmative resolution, but we cannot see the case for treating clause 50(3) and clause 53(2), (3) and (4) any differently.

Clause 63(4) already sets out eight instruments that are subject to affirmative resolution. We have taken care to include in the list all those provisions that directly affect the scope of individuals' rights. I invite the hon. Member for Ryedale to give some thought to what the intellectual underpinning of the distinction should be. Having regard to individuals' rights and to questions of particular sensitivity is the appropriate way to draw a distinction between matters that should be subject to affirmative as opposed to negative resolution.

The affirmative group comprises the power to prescribe the processing that is to be subject to preliminary assessment under clause 21(1) and the power to prescribe additional circumstances in which sensitive data may be processed under paragraph (9) of schedule 3. The Government have also tabled an amendment that would add to the list of orders that are subject to affirmative resolution to give the Secretary of State the power to extend the scope of the new offence relating to enforced subject access, which is set out in new clause 5. Because of the nature of those issues, it is right that Parliament should be invited positively to approve the Government's proposals.

The Select Committee on Delegated Powers and Deregulation in another place commented on the Government's original proposals for parliamentary scrutiny of the subordinate legislation that is provided for in the Bill. We willingly tabled an amendment in another place to give effect to its recommendation that orders under clause 13(5)(b) should be subject to affirmative rather than negative resolution, as we originally proposed. The Government also accepted an amendment in another place to make the designation by the Secretary of State of media codes of practice under clause 31(3) subject to affirmative resolution. We are, therefore, alert to the need to ensure that parliamentary procedure is tailored to the nature of the powers in question.

However, the statutory instruments mentioned in clause 63(5) are not of the same degree of significance or sensitivity as those in clause 63(4). That is why we propose the negative resolution procedure. Those statutory instruments relate essentially to regulatory or procedural issues or to matters of detail. They do not directly affect the scope of individuals' rights or matters of inherent sensitivity in the same way as those instruments that are subject to affirmative resolution.

The clause achieves the right balance between affirmative and negative resolution. We cannot agree to making all the subordinate legislation except fees regulations subject to affirmative resolution. In those circumstances, I invite the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Hoon: These amendmements were tabled in response to expressions of concern that paragraph 7 of Schedule 3 may not be fully consistent with the data protection directive.

Paragraph 7 permits the processing of sensitive data where necessary for a range of public sector functions. Article 8.4 of the directive allows member states to prescribe circumstances in which sensitive data may be processed in addition to those that are specifically set out in article 8. Article 8.4 specifies that such processing must be accompanied by appropriate safeguards, and must be carried out for reasons of substantial public interest.

At present, paragraph 7 makes no express reference either to substantial public interest, or to safeguards. That is because we think that both these requirements will normally be met in the circumstances already covered by the paragraph. However, given the breadth of the provision, we think it prudent to make provision to deal with any cases that may arise where they may not be. That will allow us to ensure that the processing in question is carried out in full compliance with the directive.

Amendment No. 248 allows the Secretary of State by order to specify cases in which the public sector processing may not involve sensitive data. It would, for example, allow processing that was conceivably not in the substantial public interest to be excluded from the scope of the paragraph. The order may also specify cases in which sensitive data may only be processed if specified conditions are met. The conditions could take the form of additional safeguards to ensure full conformity with the directive in any doubtful case.

Amendment No. 247 provides that the power, which is essentially directed at providing further protections for data subjects, is subject to the negative resolution procedure.

It is desirable to include provision of this kind so that we can be confident that we are in a position to ensure that the requirements of the directive are fully met in every case.

Mr. Hoon: I shall try to break down this rather large group of amendments into a more manageable form.

The purpose of amendments Nos. 133, 134, 136 and 142 was explained when we debated amendment No. 123, which removed the definition of inaccurate data from clause 12. Together with amendment No. 123, the amendments represent a series of small, but helpful, changes to the Bill. They remove two identical definitions of inaccurate data from clause 12 and part II of schedule 1; they remove from schedule 11 a reference to the clause 12 definition; and they insert a single definition of the same terms in clause 64, which contains supplementary definitions with a cross-reference to the index of expressions defined in clause 65.

For the benefit of the odd Committee member who did not follow that explanation, I emphasise that the amendments make no change of substance. They merely avoid unnecessary duplication.

In the second group--Government amendments Nos. 161, 163 and 165--clause 13A, which is temporarily added to the Bill by schedule 11, makes special arrangements to allow data subjects to rectify, block, erase or destroy inaccurate existing manual records during the second transitional period. Government amendments apply this provision to Gaskin records and to eligible manual data about financial status that is held by credit reference agencies during the first transitional period as well. During the transitional periods the third data protection principle, which requires personal data to be adequate, and which therefore deals with the question of completeness, does not apply to manual records. However, the directive expressly requires, at least insofar as the second transitional period is concerned, that the data subject should be able to rectify manual records that are incomplete as well as those that are inaccurate. For Gaskin records and credit reference agency files, the Government also wish to preserve the existing right of individuals to have their data rectified. That is why we are applying this provision to those records during the first transitional period also.

Amendments Nos. 161, 163 and 165 make the necessary additions to clause 13A to ensure that the right to rectify applies to data that are incomplete as well as inaccurate. When the second transitional period ends, the data protection principles, including the third principle, will apply in full to the manual records that are covered by the Bill. The transitional arrangements, including the express reference to completeness, will therefore no longer be necessary.
