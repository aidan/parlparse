Government new clause 4 Application of section 7 where data controller is credit reference agency.

Mr. Howarth: The amendments relate entirely to subject access in regard to consumer credit.

As members of the Committee are well aware, one of the Bill's purposes is to consolidate into the general data protection regime the special arrangements that apply to personal data held by credit reference agencies under the Consumer Credit Act 1974.

The matter is dealt with in clauses 7(7) and 59 of the Bill, but those provisions do not completely achieve the desired effect. The purpose of the amendments is to bring the arrangements in the Bill more closely into line with the current arrangements under the 1974 Act.

It might be helpful if I explained to the Committee how the provisions work. The key provision is new clause 4. The 1974 Act gives individuals a subject access right to credit reference agency information. The permitted turn-round time is quicker and the fee is cheaper than those under data protection legislation.

Clause 7(7) of the Bill makes provision for individuals to request limited information from credit reference agencies. It also permits shorter turn-round times and lower fees to be prescribed for such cases.

It implies a turn-round time of seven days, subject to subordinate legislation. Under clause 7(7) as drafted, however, individuals would have to make it clear in their request that they were interested only in the limited information. Given the large number of subject access requests to credit reference agencies there are about 1 million a year that is the wrong way round.

The right approach should be for the special arrangements to be presumed to apply in every case, unless the individuals make it clear that they want access to all their personal data. That is the effect of subsection (2) new clause 4.

Subsection 3 of new clause 4 preserves a right that individuals currently have under the 1974 Act that is a key point. Section 159 of the 1974 Act allows individuals to attach what that Act calls a "notice of correction" to their credit reference agency file. That right will continue to exist.

The Act requires credit reference agencies that respond to subject access requests to inform the data subjects of their rights under section 159. Subsection (3) of the new clause 4 ensures that that right is retained. I hope that people will agree that it is a worthy objective.

Amendments Nos. 233 to 238 make the consequential amendments that are required by the addition of new clause 4.

Amendments Nos. 239 and 240 deal with transitional relief for manual data to which the special arrangements apply. They ensure that individuals continue to have a right of access to and correction of their data during the first transitional period. As we discussed on Tuesday, that period runs from 24 October 1998 to 23 October 2001.

Amendments No. 241 ensures that subject access requests to credit reference agencies that are made before the Bill comes into force and which have not been dealt with will continue to be dealt with under the 1974 Act unless they expressly state that they are made under the Bill.

The provisions are rather complex, but most people would agree that the intention behind them is worthy. I hope that my explanation was helpful, and that the Committee will support the amendments.
