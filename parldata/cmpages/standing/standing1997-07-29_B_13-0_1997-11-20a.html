Social Security Bill

Clause 70

Restrictions on backdating of benefit

Amendment proposed [18 November]: No. 297, in page 44, to leave out lines 38 to 43 and insert--

    `(a) in respect of contributory benefits and non-contributory benefits--

    (i) in respect of any period (or, in the case of a widow's payment, any death occurring) more than three months before the date on which the claim is made or treated as made; or

    (ii) in such cases or circumstances as may be prescribed, in respect of any period before that date; and

    (b) in respect of, income-related benefits, industrial injuries benefit and child benefit--

    (i) in respect of any period more than one month before the date on which the claim is made or treated as made; or

    (ii) in such cases or circumstances as may be prescribed, in respect of any period before that date.

    ( ) In this section ``contributory benefits'', ``non-contributory benefits'', ``income related benefits'', ``industrial injuries benefit'' and ``child benefit'' shall have the meanings given in the Contributions and Benefits Act.'.--[Mr. Burns.]

Question again proposed, That the amendment be made.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. Keith Bradley): It is important when considering the amendment to put into context the issue of backdating, so it may be helpful to the Committee if I outline in some detail--and, I am afraid, at some length--our proposed approach to backdating and how that is reflected in the Bill, before turning specifically to the amendment.

Clause 70 provides for a general limit of one month for the backdating of claims for all benefits. It also provides for regulations to prescribe exceptions to that time limit. It is proposed to introduce that provision from June 1998 for all benefits, except housing benefit and council tax benefit, which the provision will cover from October 1998.

Clause 70(1) replaces section 1(2) of the Social Security Administration Act 1992 so as to provide that a person shall not be entitled to benefit in respect of any period of more than one month before a claim is made, or treated as made. We propose to use the provision for the automatic backdating of claims for any non-income related benefits, such as retirement pension, widow's benefit and child benefit, for a period of up to one month.

Clause 70(1) will also be used to provide, as a general rule, that claims to income-related benefits, such as income support, will not be backdated.

Clause 70(2) amends section 3 of the 1992 Act which provides for late claims to widow's benefits in rare cases such as those involving a missing person, where death is difficult to establish. The new provision aims to ensure that although the time for making a claim will continue to run from when it is determined that the husband has died, or from when the widow first knew of the death, the time limit for claiming benefit following notification of the death will be one month. That will be consistent with clause 70(1).

I bring to the Committee's attention, however, an anomaly that has arisen in the current drafting of clause 70(2). An unintended effect has been identified which restricts also to one month the time allowed between identification of the husband's body and the widow being made aware of his death. Clearly that is unreasonable. I can, therefore, give the Committee an undertaking that an amendment will be tabled on Report to ensure that in those circumstances, although the widow, once notified of the death, will have one month to claim benefit, 12 months will continue to be allowed between a body being discovered and the widow being advised of the discovery. That will cater for circumstances in which, for example, the widow has changed address or must be traced.

Mr. Simon Burns (West Chelmsford): May I just seek clarification on what the Minister has said? If a widow's address and whereabouts are known, but the Department of Social Security is having difficulty in tracking her down, will the time limit for claiming benefit still be one month from the discovery of the body?

Mr. Keith Bradley: The limit will be one month from the widow being notified of the discovery and identification of her husband's body. A change of address is just one example of a reason why a widow might not quickly be traced.

I draw the Committee's attention, however, to the fact that clause 70(1) also provides powers to prescribe in regulations different backdating periods as well as cases or circumstances in which a person could be entitled to no backdating. I have considered carefully whether exceptions to the general time limits should be allowed. In that, I have obviously taken note of the points put to me in correspondence from individuals and various welfare rights groups. I am grateful for those representations. Taking into account those views, we propose to introduce regulations to provide for a range of exceptions to the general time limits.

The exceptions will fall into three broad categories. The first category will allow backdating beyond the general one-month time limit. They will apply to non-income related and income-related benefits. They will include cases where entitlement to a benefit is dependent on the claimant's, or another person's, entitlement to another benefit. It is obviously unreasonable for administrative delays in awarding one benefit to deprive a person of entitlement to another.

In addition, refugees will be allowed, where appropriate, backdated awards of income support, housing benefit and council tax benefit to the date of a successful asylum application. Incapacity benefit, severe disablement allowance and maternity allowance claims will also be able to be backdated to protect customers from delays by employers in notifying them that they had no entitlement to statutory sick pay or statutory maternity pay.

The second group of exceptions will be for income related benefits where the basic rule will be no backdating. Backdating up to the one month time limit will be permitted where special reasons apply. Those will include cases where, first, the customer has difficulty communicating because he has learning, language or literacy difficulties, or he is deaf or blind and there is no one else to make the claim on his behalf; secondly, except in the case of a claim for jobseeker's allowance, the customer is ill or disabled, and there is no one else to make the claim; thirdly the customer is caring for a person who is ill or disabled, and there is no one else to make his claim; fourthly, the customer was given misleading information by an officer of the Department of Social Security or of the Department for Education and Employment, or the customer was given misleading advice, in writing, by a solicitor or other professional adviser, or by a medical practitioner, a local authority or by a person working in a citizens advice bureau or a similar advice agency.

When the death is registered, the widow is provided with a notification of death certificate that is designed specifically for social security purposes. The certificate asks whether the person wishes to claim widow's benefit. If the answer is yes, the form should be sent to the Benefits Agency, which will return a detailed claim form to the widow for completion. The date of claim will be the date on which the death was notified and not the date on which the claim form is completed.

Although the system appears to work well and does not hinder the claiming of benefit, in the interests of streamlining the process even further, I again have asked my officials to see whether further improvements can be made, perhaps by collecting more information at the time of registering the death, rather than relying later on additional information.

I am examining especially the availability and content of the widow's payment forms that are available when death is registered. In the context of streamlining the process, the Government are not expecting people to visit, for example, two agencies more than once at an especially difficult time. They should not have to in circumstances in which, by simplifying the system, we can resolve the matter at the initial stage.

I have announced a general review of benefit leaflets, which will more closely examine client-based leaflets, so that available information covers a range of benefits, and so that people do not have to find an appropriate leaflet from a whole series.

Anxiety has been expressed about our proposals' effect on housing benefit and council tax benefit. I recognise that that issue is difficult. I have, therefore, considered very carefully whether those benefits should be included in the backdating proposals. I have concluded that they should for a number of reasons. Under the proposals, thos benefits will, dependent on the range of exceptions that I have outlined, still be able to be backdated for up to one month. The fact that current rules allow up to 52 weeks backdating, subject to the local authority deciding that good cause exists, does not mean those benefits will generally be backdated for that period.

The current system, which depends on the local authority accepting that continuous good cause existed for backdating, can be confusing for staff and customers. A number of local authorities have suggested that more prescriptive rules should be introduced to achieve greater efficiency in determining such claims.

That said, as with other benefits I have mentioned, it is important to consider whether the claims process should be simplified. As hon. Members may be aware, there are two types of housing benefit and council tax benefit claims. First, there are claims in which the claimant receives income support and is automatically entitled to maximum benefit, subject to eligible rent. Secondly, there are those claims in which there is no income support in payment, and benefit entitlement is subject to the claimant's income and capital. It is important to remember that housing benefit and council tax benefit may be paid to people in full-time work who have an additional source of income.

If specific conditions have been satisfied, existing legislation allows local authorities to treat a claim as having been made on an earlier date. For example, income support claim forms contain a short tick-box for claimants to show whether they want to claim housing benefit and/or council tax benefit. The form can be given to the Benefits Agency with the income support claim, and it is then passed on to the local authority. If the Benefits Agency delays passing the form to the local authority, housing benefit and council tax benefits claims should still be treated as having been made on the date from which income support entitlement starts, even though the period involved may be more than one month.

Clause 49

Payments treated as remuneration and earnings

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Chairman: With this, we may take Government new clause 7--Payments treated as remuneration and earnings.

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Social Security (Mr. John Denham): We propose that clause 49 should not stand part of the Bill, and that it should be replaced by new clause 7. New clause 7 repeats the measures already in clause 49, which tackle avoidance of national insurance contributions using non-monetary payments under restrictive undertakings. It also introduces a new measure, treating as earnings for national insurance purposes the taxable gain on exercise of a share option in an unapproved share scheme. This enables the national insurance treatment of share options to be brought closer into line with income tax.

Although they are quite different in character, the measures for restrictive undertakings and share options are grouped together in a new clause because both measures require amendments to subsection (4) of section 4 of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992. That subsection treats as earnings for national insurance purposes payments that would not ordinarily fall within the definition of earnings for the purposes of the Contributions and Benefits Act. I shall deal first with non-monetary payments made under restrictive covenants.

Proposed subsection (4)(b) of the new clause is intended to block national insurance contributions avoidance schemes, which exploit a loophole in the current legislation. Under those avoidance schemes, instead of the usual cash bonus, employers and employees collude to enter into a restrictive covenant. Such convenants are legally binding agreements, usually not to leave and join a competitor, in return for which the employee is paid non-cash compensation in gold bullion or gilts. Payments in cash for entering into a restrictive covenant are already liable to national insurance under current legislation. Non-cash payments are not. Gold bullion and gilt payments made under restrictive covenants have no commercial purpose other than the avoidance of contributions.

12.15 pm

Mr. Burns: Does the Minister have any calculation of the amount that blocking up this loophole will save the Exchequer?

Mr. Denham: In just one case of this form of avoidance, the director and main shareholder of a company was given a £10 million payment in gilt-edged stock under a restrictive covenant. The company thus avoided £1 million in national insurance contributions, and the director sold the gilt immediately for £10 million in cash. Our estimate is that the likely effect of introducing the amendment would be to raise some £20 million to £30 million.

The clause treats non-monetary payments made under restrictive covenants as payments of earnings for the purposes of the Social Security Contributions and Benefits Act 1992, thereby making them subject to national insurance contributions. The clause is retrospective to the date of introduction, which was 10 July 1997. Without that, some companies would simply move their bonuses forward to take advantge of the loophole that we have highlighted by introducing amending legislation.

We are determined to act firmly against national insurance avoidance abuses such as the one tackled by the clause. Contributions paid for benefits and pensions go to some of the most vulnerable members of society. The national insurance scheme is founded on rights and responsibilities. We will take necessary measures to counter the actions of employers who blatantly seek to avoid their responsibilities.

The new clause would introduce changes to the national insurance treatment of options on non-approved shares. The changes will put right problems with existing regulations. Under the regulations that were introduced by the previous Administration in November 1996, payments to employees, by way of certain non-approved shares and options, were brought for the first time into earnings for national insurance and pay-as-you-earn. The shares brought into pay-as-you-earn and contributions were those that are tradeable, and options over those shares. Shares and options of that type were quite readily exchangeable for cash, and prior to the regulations they were used widely to avoid national insurance.

The proposals for share options in this new clause would raise some additional revenue from some employers, but that is not their primary aim. The Government have listened to criticism of the current regulations and propose to overhaul the charge to ensure greater fairness and to make the charge easier for business to administer. No new types of share scheme will be brought into national insurance.

The starting point for our overhaul of the share options provision is sub-paragraph (1) of new clause 7. This introduces a new measure to treat as earnings any gain taxable under section 135 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. Currently, contributions on share options are based on the undervalue--the amount by which the sum paid by the employee is less than the market value of the shares--at the time that the option is granted. We will replace that charge with national insurance on the taxable gain when the share option is exercised. That will enable the national insurance treatment of share options to be brought closer to the income tax position.

In bringing forward the new clause and in drafting the regulations that will follow, we are keen to work with business to ensure successful implementation.

Clause 50

Class 1A contributions

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Denham: The clause ensures that a class 1A national insurance contribution will be payable in respect of cars provided to employees and directors taxable under section 157 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988, even if the benefit of the car is the only payment of earnings made to that employee.

Class 1A national insurance contributions are paid only by employers, and only, in respect of employees who receive over £8,500 a year, and most directors.

At the moment it is possible for a company to organise payment of its employees or directors in such a way that, if they receive no earnings other than the car, there may be no liable secondary contributor--that is, no employer--who can be held responsible for the payment of the class 1A national insurance contribution. The class 1A charge therefore may not bite.

We do not believe that it is fair that employers should be able to escape payment of contributions in this situation, simply because the employee or director has no other payment of earnings. For example, an employer may avoid a class 1A charge of up to £700 in respect of a director provided with a car valued at £20,000. The clause will put an end to that gap in the law.

I should make it clear that it does not in any way widen the scope of class 1A national insurance contributions beyond employees taxed under section 157 of the Income and Corporation Taxes Act 1988. The clause both simplifies and removes an anomaly in the class 1A arrangements, making the law fairer in distinguishing between those who have earnings and those who do not. It will remove the scope for national insurance avoidance in this area. I commend it to the Committee.

Clause 52

Contributions paid in error

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

 

Mr. Burns: We would be grateful if the Minister explained exactly what the clause is designed to achieve.

Mr. Denham: The clause addresses the situation where class 1 contributions have been paid in error because workers who were classified as employees were actually self-employed.

Under current rules, refunds of national insurance contributions can be made for up to six years. Under this clause, contributors will be able to claim refunds of class 1 contributions up to a maximum of only 24 months. Once that period has expired there will be no further opportunity to question the class of contribution paid and it will, for benefit purposes, then be treated as of the proper class.

Mr. Letwin: Does not the Minister discern any incoherence or oddity in setting the period at 24 months, in the context of our recent debate?

Mr. Denham: I will deal with that point, but it would help the Committee and the hon. Member for West Dorset if I first set out in full the reasoning behind the clause.

The purpose of the clause is to protect the national insurance fund and to limit the administrative burden currently placed on the Contributions Agency when it has to go back and assess and pay refunds for six years.

Decisions on employment status are not always straightforward--there can sometimes be a thin line between self-employment and employment, and we know that mistakes can occur. However, it must be right that at some point we draw a line under those decisions and limit the extent to which we go back to pay refunds and correct records.

The Government have to strike a balance, and we have concluded that it is fair both to employers and to individuals to pay refunds for up to two years, rather than six. That will protect the national insurance fund and thus the wider interests of contributors generally.

An example will perhaps illustrate how the clause will work. In May 2000 a worker is engaged as an employee liable for class 1 contributions. In March 2006 it is confirmed that the worker was in fact self-employed and that the class 1 contributions had been paid in error. All the class 1 contributions paid from May 2000 to March 2004 would be treated as being of the proper class and no refund could be made. A refund may be made from March 2006, when the error is discovered, back to the start of that tax year, April 2005. In addition to those 11 months, a refund could be made in respect of wrongly paid contributions in the previous tax year, 2004-05. In the example, a total of 23 months of wrongly paid class 1 contributions could be refunded.

I hope that that explanation is helpful. I can, if hon. Members wish, give further information. We have tried to strike the right balance between the period for which refunds can be made, the protection of the national insurance fund and the protection of employees' interests which is appropriate to the work of the Contributions Agency.
