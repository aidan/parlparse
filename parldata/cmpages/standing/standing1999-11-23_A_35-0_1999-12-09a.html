Clause 357

Manager

Mr. Howard Flight (Arundel and South Downs): I beg to move amendment No. 934, in page 184, line 15, after `corporate' insert—

    `(other than an open-ended investment company)'.

The amendment raises an important drafting point, and relates to subsection (3), which defines ``manager'' as someone who manages part of the business. That definition relates to the body corporate. However, if the body corporate is an open-ended investment company, its manager is the external firm managing its assets. When the Bill refers to the manager of an open-ended investment company or OEIC—as the investment services directive does—it should be clear that it does not mean a manager in the sense given in subsection (3).

The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Miss Melanie Johnson): I thank the hon. Gentleman for tabling the amendment. I agree that the clause is designed to specify who a firm's managers should be. However, the amendment is unnecessary because ``manager'' is not used in relation to an open-ended investment company. Part XVI refers to the operators of an OEIC—not their manager—and the term ``manager'' is not used in relation to OEICS.

However, the term ``manager'' is used in relation to unit trusts, hence the exclusion of unit trusts from the definition in clause 357. I hope that that persuades the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Flight: I thank the Minister. On the basis of her explanation, I shall ask leave to withdraw the amendment. If there is any confusion, the record will show what is meant.

I therefore beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

Clause 361

Consequential and supplementary provision

Mr. Flight: I beg to move amendment No. 935, in page 185, line 13, after `they', insert `reasonably'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss amendment No. 936, in page 185, line 14, leave out `or expedient'.

Mr. Flight: Amendment No. 935 is another instance of our old friend, ``reasonable''. It would make a simple insertion in relation to the discretionary power to make supplementary provisions.

Amendment No. 936 would delete ``expedient'', as the word means helpful, and gives the Treasury wide discretion to include extra provisions that are not restricted by the Bill.

Miss Melanie Johnson: The hon. Gentleman has got my point.

I accept that the amendment would insert in the Bill the Opposition's long-running desire to debate the notion of reasonableness. We have already discussed the use of that term. However, we have said that we shall consider in more detail the requirement for the FSA to be reasonable in its undertakings. The amendment concerns the Treasury and how it may make consequential and supplementary provisions. At present it is for the Treasury to decide whether an order is necessary or expedient. The provision will enable the Treasury to act promptly when the need arises and to make sure that it is doing what is right and appropriate in the circumstances. To stipulate that the Treasury should consider whether something is reasonable and necessary is to miss two points; first, that in reaching a decision the Treasury would be likely to consider whether it was reasonable and weigh up such factors as an adverse affect on millions of people; secondly, the Treasury may sometimes need to take decisions that are, in some people's eyes, unreasonable. Taking into account, and acting upon, the possible concerns of such groups would leave the Treasury unable to act quickly or rationally.

The word ``expedient'', with which amendment No. 936 is concerned, is part of a standard legislative phrase which confers on the Treasury powers to make regulations amending other legislation. That will be necessary to bring the Bill into force effectively. I do not believe that we should remove the phrase ``or expedient'' from the clause, as that would undermine the Treasury's carrying out of its functions under the Bill.

Mr. Flight: Some big issues arise, which we should perhaps deal with in a brief clause stand part debate. The Government are clearly not inclined to accept either of our amendments. The clause gives the Treasury substantial power to rewrite the whole Bill—a provision that we felt needed tightening a little. We shall not press the amendments to a vote, but we should like to put the larger issues on the record. I beg to ask leave to withdraw the amendment.

Amendment, by leave, withdrawn.

The clause gives the Treasury powers virtually to rewrite the Bill from top to bottom. It goes far too wide and our amendments only scratch the surface of what is required. I believe that the Committee has accepted 440 amendments, six of which were tabled by the Opposition. That was out of a total of 970 amendments that were tabled, as well as 18 new clauses. We have had 35 sittings and 100 hours work on the Bill. On no fewer than 63 occasions—I counted—Ministers have agreed to give further attention to other parts of the Bill, in addition to the amendments that I have mentioned. Why on earth have we been doing all this work if, whenever the Treasury feels like it, it can just alter the Bill at will?

The first thing that I felt I needed to discover in response to the clause was whether such a provision existed in the Financial Services Act 1986. After all, if the Government and previous Administrations have carried on in this way in the past, perhaps I should not be so concerned about it. Incidentally, lest any hon. Member should feel that I am over-egging things, it is important to read the clause. It states that the Treasury may ``by order''—that is by negative resolution—

    ``make such incidental, consequential, transitional or supplemental provision as they consider necessary or expedient for the general purposes, or any particular purpose, of this Act''.

Not content with that, in subsection (4) we find:—

    ``No other provision of this Act restricts the powers conferred by this section.''

In other words, the licence is to do exactly what the Treasury wishes. That is why it is worth examining the 1986 Act.

The 1986 Act also enables the Treasury to vary the provisions, but it is far more tightly circumscribed than what is before us in the Bill. For example, section 2 gives the Secretary of State the power to alter, by order, ``the meaning of investment'' in schedule 1. However, anyone reading the Act can see that the power is much narrower than in clause 361 as it applies to one specific aspect and deals only with legitimate transitional concerns. Other sections of the 1986 Act have a similar effect. For example, delegation orders can be made under section 114, which allows transitional and supplementary provision to be made in connection with a delegation order. The sub-heading to the section describes the provisions as transitional and supplementary measures and they are designed to deal with the transition and the creation of new legislation during which some bedding down and transitional effects may be relevant. By contrast, clause 361 makes provisions that will be on the statute book—end are intended for use—indefinitely, and that is unacceptable.

My second major concern is how the courts might interpret clause 361(1), and I would be grateful to hear the views of my right hon. and learned Friend the Minister for North-East Bedfordshire (Sir N. Lyell), Words such as incidental, consequential, transitional and supplemental could be interpreted narrowly by the courts, and that would restrict Treasury discretion under the clause to some extent. But what about the term ``general purposes''? I was unable to find it in previous legislation, so why does it appear here? It could be deemed broad enough to include anything in the long title of the Bill. If so, might that not lead to further amendments having to be made?

Will the Minister clarify her interpretation of ``general purposes'', as that would greatly assist the courts when the provisions came up for judicial review? Will she explain the wide scope of the clause, which seems to cover every topic that we have debated—for 100 hours, as I said? Will she also explain why we need on the statute book a provision that is clearly not a transitional measure, unlike similar clauses in previous legislation?

Mr. Flight: The amendment would leave out ``order'' and insert ``statutory instrument''. The term statutory instrument includes both regulations and orders and it is appropriate that all statutory instruments should fall within the clause. As this effectively adds up to parliamentary control of the territory, we feel that it is better to use the wider term.

Amendments Nos. 938 to 940 are intended to improve parliamentary control of statutory instruments. In practically all cases, there should be a positive rather than a negative resolution procedure and we have sought to make all such instruments—except for orders under section 19(5), which introduces exemptions, and under section 365(2), which allows the Treasury to bring different provisions into force on different days—subject to the positive approval regime. Amendment No. 940 is consequential and the deleted lines provide that only orders—

    ``made under section 19 to which subsection (4) applies''

are to be subject to positive resolution. As I said, apart from the exceptions, we believe that all orders made under section 19 should be subject to a positive resolution.

Amendments Nos. 941 and 942 are consequential. There is no need to provide that orders made under subsection (4) are excluded from the orders that are subject only to negative resolution, because they would automatically be subject to a positive resolution. Similarly, the two negative resolutions would become positive as a result of the amendments.

 

Miss Melanie Johnson: The amendments would have the effect that all secondary legislation made under the Bill would be by statutory instrument subject to affirmative resolution. I hope that Opposition Members will agree that we have applied the affirmative resolution where necessary throughout the Bill. That might help the Opposition Whip accommodate the hon. Member for Chichester's difficulties.
