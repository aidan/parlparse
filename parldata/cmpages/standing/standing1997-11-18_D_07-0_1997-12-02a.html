I had recourse to the repeals part of the Bill and noted that that subsection is not being repealed. We are concerned--as, I am sure, were the inspirers of the 1946 Act--that the Bank, in its legitimate and necessary request for information, should not go on unnecessary fishing expeditions into the affairs of individuals.

The Economic Secretary will know that one of her colleagues has attracted some attention in the media, and I shall say only that while issues of principle should properly be raised, it would be an abuse of power if the Bank, with the connivance or under the instructions of the Chancellor, or on its own initiative, were to pry into the private relationship between any client and his banker--or, as it is now more widely worded, ``undertaking''--if it were not absolutely necessary for the compilation of information for the Bank. In terms of the compilation of proper statistics that would not normally be a problem, unless a strange bank or financial institution that had only one client were involved.

That is the first amendment that we propose. The second, which reinforces the first and reproduces the substance of the wording of the 1946 Act, sets out the need for the Bank to be prepared to consider representations, from an institution or undertaking with which it has dealings and from which it has requested information, about why it might be inappropriate to furnish that information. Those are reasonable safeguards--what the lawyers present would, I am sure, call natural justice--on how matters should be handled. They are designed to prevent the Bank from being--I am not saying that it wishes to be--oppressive or inquisitive about individuals' affairs or from upsetting the relations between bankers and their clients, and also to give the institutions and undertakings a reasonable chance of making representations to the Bank. Nobody is seeking, overtly or covertly, to suppress the flow of necessary information; we are merely anxious that it should be given on a fair basis and should not intrude unnecessarily ino individuals' affairs.

The other example, which we did not discuss but which I might give as a further illustration of that point, is the American experience.
