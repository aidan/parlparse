Mrs. Jacqui Lait (Beckenham): I had just risen to my feet when you adjourned the Committee, Mr. Malins. We had an exhaustive discussion this morning, but that will not stop me from adding my might to the general fund of human happiness on the subject before us. I wish to concentrate on a matter which my hon. Friend the Member for Grantham and Stamford (Mr. Davies) mentioned, but which was not explored in as much detail as it merits. The clause attacks the national insurance principle and the purpose of our amendments is to ameliorate that. We shall vote against the clause in an attempt to reinstate the positive principle of national insurance.

I am sure that all Committee members know from conversations with pensioners and disabled people that their instinct—they do not articulate it—is that the national insurance deductions from their earnings have been tucked away in the Benefits Agency or the Department of Social Security. They believe that a capital sum, with interest added, accumulates during their working lives and that it is theirs to call on in time of need.

The original idea of national insurance was first class, but since it was introduced the Government have paid out money as soon as it comes in and the sum of money tucked away in the Treasury is a mirage. People are attached to the national insurance principle and the Government's proposal for incapacity benefit agitates them because it breaks that principle. They believe that if they have worked and contributed through national insurance for their time of need, they have a right to receive benefit if they become incapacitated.

Sadly, the Government are breaking that principle for the group that we have highlighted. We have discussed women who make contributions while they work, but stop working to bring up their children and later return to work. They may return to low-paid jobs because that fits in with the family pattern. They may not pay national insurance contributions because their incomes are so low, but if they were hit by crippling incapacity they would be unable to claim incapacity benefit under the Government's proposal.

I understand from the Equal Opportunities Commission that 2 million women may not meet the incapacity criteria, thank goodness, but all of the other criteria that I mentioned. If things went disastrously wrong, a large demand would result.

We have discussed low earners, who may have had a good contributions record for many years. Many people—especially men over the age of 50—are, unfortunately, vulnerable to early retirement, largely because of changes in technology. That fact is often dressed up in other ways, but sometimes such people are simply made unemployed by their company. It is hard for them to get a job with an income similar to their previous income, and they may become a low earner for two years.

As we grow older, the chances of our becoming incapacitated are higher, although they are still low. People who believe that they are insured through their national insurance contributions will have no access to incapacity benefit. The hon. Member for Newbury (Mr. Rendel) discussed the problems facing people with illnesses such as multiple sclerosis. Such people may not be able to build up the contributions record that they need.

We need to bear in mind the interests of carers who do not receive invalid care allowance on the ground that it might affect the income of the person for whom they care. The Government may have already decided to look kindly on such people. If so, I hope that the Minister will confirm that.

We are all concerned about the circumstances that have led to increasing numbers of people claiming incapacity benefit. Many Committee members will remember the huge rise in the number of people who claimed invalidity benefit. The Conservative Government rightly said that that benefit needed to be re-examined. Committee members will remember constituents who came to our surgeries and said, ``I have been made redundant by my company and the human resources people have told me to claim invalidity benefit.'' Other constituents said, ``The Benefits Agency staff told me to claim invalidity benefit.'' For those reasons, incapacity benefit and a much more stringent medical test, the all-work test, were introduced.

The Government said that we should be more positive about the all-work test, which they renamed the personal capability assessment. They believe that the personal capability assessment will help people who are able to get back into work to do so. However, I do not understand why they need to curb IB, unless that proposal is Treasury driven. If the Government believe that the assessments will help people who can get back into work to do so, they should have the courage of their convictions and say, ``Anyone who needs IB should be able to claim it.'' They should return to the insurance principle in which the British people have a fundamental and deep belief. The clause challenges that principle, and our amendments seek to alleviate the clause's effects in a small way.

If the Government cannot say, with their hand on their heart, that the personal capability assessment can deal with the possibility of everyone's returning to work, the only conclusion that one can come to is that the Treasury is insisting that the Government should save money. Savings are being made in the most heartless and thoughtless way. Vulnerable groups who believe that they have insured for the worst may find that they do not have that insurance.

The hon. Member for Winchester acknowledged that the Government intend to protect the rights of carers in receipt of invalid care allowance to claim incapacity benefit. He asked for more detail on that. It is a technical point. The intention is to maintain the status quo for carers on ICA. ICA recipients can qualify for incapacity benefit by using credits that have been earned in the previous two tax years, in addition to contributions paid at any time before the ICA claim. No minimum of ICA credits is required.

In combination with other credits, such as unemployment and sickness credits, one ICA credit in the most recent tax year will suffice. The power to make regulations to modify the first contribution condition for particular groups will be used so that former ICA recipients will simply have to pay contributions in any one tax year. They will still have to have credits worth 50 times the lower earnings limit in both the previous two tax years, and at least one credit in the most recent tax year would need to be a credit for ICA. In practice, no one receives ICA for just one week. The minimum requirement of one ICA credit has the effect of protecting people who either ceased or started to care in the most recent tax year, as well as those who have been caring throughout it.

The hon. Member for Winchester also pressed the Government to consider extending the protection that we intend to provide through regulations to people who are carers but who do not qualify for ICA. That would affect people with less onerous caring responsibilities who care for fewer than 35 hours a week. Many of those people are able to work part time and do so. Others qualify for home responsibility protection, which is open to everyone who receives child benefit. To passport a large number of people who have had the opportunity to work but who, for the good reason of bringing up their children, have rejected that opportunity, would be inappropriate.

The hon. Member for Grantham and Stamford depicted the Bill as a Treasury-driven, cost-cutting exercise. It is not; it is a welfare-driven reform programme. We are making changes to improve welfare for long-term sick and disabled people and, in other parts of the Bill, for other categories of people. I remind Committee members that welfare goes wider than social security. The problem with the previous Government was that they saw welfare as something that the social security budget provided for people whose welfare was compromised, perhaps because of economic decisions or the failure of the labour market to provide employment opportunities.

Our approach to welfare is wider. We believe that joined-up government will enable health, education, social security, local authorities and the NHS to work together to provide opportunities for disabled people who, in the past, were simply consigned to a life on benefits.

We have in our reform programme measures that will save money. There is no doubt about that, and we say it clearly in the financial memorandum to the Bill. The clause is one of those measures. We also have measures in the Bill that will cost money. We have both kinds of measure in the Bill because they will improve the welfare of people whom we want to support.

In the first year in which the changes will operate—2001-02—it is estimated that the incapacity benefit changes will provide savings to the Exchequer of about £70 million or, as I am talking savings to the Exchequer, £65 million net of income tax. The severe disablement allowance changes will save in the order of £10 million in year one, but nothing in the second year. Those figures are in the financial memorandum.

One must set those figures against others. In the first year, the extension of the higher rate mobility component of DLA to young children will cost an additional £15 million. The introduction of a disability income guarantee will cost an additional £60 million. The disabled person's tax credit is not part of the Bill and there is no costing in the financial memorandum, but by 2001-02 it will cost something in excess of £100 million. Therefore, the difference between the savings and the cost in year one of the welfare reform programme will amount to a net additional cost, and rightly so. Providing the support that is needed will require a net additional cost.

Gentleman ought to know—judging by some of the things that he said earlier today, I am not sure that he does know about his own benefit system. Incapacity benefit can last indefinitely. Thus, the amount of money that people will lose as a result of the means-testing of jobseeker's allowance will be defined, whereas the financial loss resulting from the means-testing of incapacity benefit is open ended.

I find it extraordinary that the Under-Secretary needed me to point out those essential matters to him. The essential difference, of course, is between those of us who are able bodied and those who are not. Special help by the state is justified by virtue of the fact of disability, but the Under-Secretary clearly does not believe that. That is the fundamental divide between the two sides of the Committee. That difference has enabled the Labour party to introduce measures which the Conservative party would never have dreamt of considering when in office.

I am returning to the amendment as rapidly as I can, Mr. Malins, but you will understand that I had to respond to such an extraordinary statement. I know that you will not allow the Government so to conduct the proceedings on the amendments as to forestall a clause stand part debate. There are many good reasons why the Government would want to prevent Parliament from discussing clauses in the Bill, but Parliament should not be bamboozled or manipulated by the Government in that fashion, otherwise the whole balance between the Executive branch and the legislature will break down rapidly.

The first aspect of the compulsion to take an early pension which the Government wish to impose on disabled people is that it will be very expensive. I can only endorse the point made so clearly by my hon. Friend the Member for Arundel and South Downs, who, if he was not an actuary, certainly has a professional background in pensions.

Secondly, in the present circumstances, the proposal comes particularly ill. All those who are nearing retirement and about to take their pension face the problem of falling annuity rates. To force people to take an early pension, in a climate of falling annuity rates, is to double the financial damage imposed on them. The Government are proposing a double blow for disabled people. They will find that their incapacity benefit, to which they are fully entitled by the contribution conditions, and which is already restricted by the previous clause and the all-work test, is reduced. The other aspect of the double whammy is that their pension income will be reduced as well by virtue of their being forced to take it early. That seems an extraordinarily invidious position in which to place people who, through no fault of their own, have had to leave the labour market. They are being penalised in a heartless way by the new Labour Government for doing so.

Mr. Rendel: Is not it a treble whammy? Some people may have become incapacitated in terms of the job that they have been doing, and have had to leave it.
