Mr. Hill: I confess that I do not wholly recognise in the comments made by the hon. Member for North Shropshire (Mr. Paterson) the substance of the amendments. However, I shall endeavour to deal with the purposes of the amendments.

Perhaps I could preface my remarks by saying a brief word about the purposes of paragraph 17, which deals with the possibility that the personal interests of members of the SRA may be in conflict with the interests of the authority itself. This issue arises in any public body and there is a standard approach for dealing with it. Paragraph 17 follows the standard approach. Any interest, direct or indirect, leads to disqualification unless covered by a dispensation. That blanket approach recognises the fact that it is not possible to anticipate all the ways in which an interest may arise. When an interest arises that has not previously been contemplated, it is automatically a disqualifying interest unless the Secretary of State grants a dispensation for it. That may be a general dispensation, given to cover all cases of that kind, or a specific dispensation to cover the particular case. At the same time as the SRA is established, the Secretary of State will issue a general dispensation under sub-paragraphs (5) and (6).

Amendments Nos. 868 and 869 would specify on the face of the statute certain types of interests that do not disqualify a member from participating in the board's discussions or decisionns. Amendment No. 870 would then remove the Secretary of State's power to issue general or specific dispensations. The problem with amendment No. 870 is that it would set the disqualification rules in concrete, with absolutely no scope for flexibility. Any kind of interest that we do not anticipate now would automatically be a disqualifying interest, however innocent it might be. That plainly will not work. Even if we specify some automatic exemptions now on the face of the Bill, we will need flexibility later.

Amendments Nos. 868 and 869 attempt to specify some automatic exemptions: there should be no disqualification merely because a member is a user of public transport or because of the relevant experience for which they were appointed in the first place. I agree with the purposes of the amendments: those should not be matters for disqualification. However, the amendments are not needed. Paragraph 17 should not be read in isolation.     The Secretary of State shall have regard to the desirability of appointing a person who has experience of some matter relevant to the functions of the Authority.

If that were a matter for disqualification, the board would never reach any useful decisions because all its members would be permanently disqualified. When paragraph 17 is read together with clause 177, it is clear that that cannot be the correct interpretation of the paragraph, so no amendment is needed to achieve the intended result.

The hon. Member for North Shropshire might ask why we do not make the common-sense interpretation explicit in the Bill.
