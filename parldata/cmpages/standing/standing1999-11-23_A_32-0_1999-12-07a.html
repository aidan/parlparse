The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): I welcome you back to our deliberations, Mr. O'Brien.

This is an interesting group of amendments, and I sympathise with the intention behind some of them. However, I hope that I shall be able to persuade hon. Members that the amendments are inappropriate in the context of the procedures that the clause sets out and the decisions that we have taken.

Recognised bodies are different animals from ordinary authorised firms. They have their own regulatory functions and make rules. As we confirmed last week, they enjoy statutory immunity for their regulatory responsibilities. Their regulatory provisions and practices are subject to competition scrutiny. They are part of the system of regulation that the Bill establishes.

Therefore, it is appropriate in cases where the FSA proposes to give a direction under clause 267 or to make a revocation order under clause 268(2) that the procedures in clause 269 should be different from those that apply when the FSA imposes requirements or withdraws permissions for an authorised firm. It would be inappropriate, for example, for such a proposal to be referred to the tribunal. Recognition establishes a different relationship with the regulator from that which exists between the regulator and the authorised community. The arrangements in the Bill take account of that. The proper forum for a recognised body and its members to challenge such a decision after they have had an opportunity to make representations is in the courts, by way of judicial review. That is broadly consistent with the approach under the Financial Services act 1986, which continues to be appropriate under the new arrangements . I hope, therefore, that Opposition Members will not press amendments Nos. 872 and 874, which refer to the tribunal.

I have sympathy with the intention behind amendment No. 871, which would require the FSA to give access to the information and documents on which it had relied when it decided on its proposed action. The recognised body is clearly entitled to know on what basis the FSA proposes to act. In reality, however, the information and documents on which the FSA relies will almost always already be in the hands of the recognised body; indeed, it will frequently be the source of that information. In any case, a notice under subsection (1) must already state why the FSA intends to give the direction or to make its proposed order. If the recognised body needs further clarification or documents, the FSA will normally treat a request favourably. We do not need to make special provision in the Bill.

Amendment No. 873 proposes two requirements. First, the FSA must consider the suitability of alternative action in the light of representations to it. However, the clause already contains that qualification, and subsection (5) clearly states:

    "In deciding whether to"

    (a) give a direction, or

    (b) make a revocation order,

    the Authority must have regard to any representations made in accordance with sub section (3)."

That requirement applies as much to an aternative direction or order"the formal sanctions that are available in respect of a recognised body"as it does to the original direction or order.

Secondly, the amendment proposes that the FSA should be required to give its reasons for taking alternative action. I agree that that would be good practice, particularly if the FSA decided to take substantially different action from that which was originally proposed. In most cases, however, its reasons for taking alternative action will be clear from the original proposals and reasons and from the representations to which it will have had to have regard. the Bill should not formally require it to give further reasons at the decision stage.

I hope that that clarification is helpful in the light of the concerns that the hon. Member for Arundel and South Downs (Mr. Flight) raised. I hope that he will feel able to withdraw the amendment.

Mr. Timms: As the Hon. Gentleman said, the clause and the following clauses create a competition scrutiny regime for recognised bodies. They are similar in form to the competition scrutiny mechanism created for the FSA by part IX of the Bill. There is already a competition scrutiny mechanism for recognised bodies under section 119 of the Fiancial Services Act 1986. There are some similarities with that regime but there are also differences given the different functions of the FSA and the recognised bodies.

As with the FSA competition mechanism, the scheme will create special arrangements for the examination of the competition effects of rules, guidance and codes"collectively referred to as "regulatory provisions""made by recognised bodies. The arrangements will also apply to the practices of those bodies"how they carry out their regulatory functions"and to the trading practices of their members or, in other words, the business practices that members will be obliged to adopt as a result, for example, of a rule made by a recognised body.

As in part IX, the mechanism will involve the examination by the Director General of Fair Trading of the regulatory provisions and practices to establish whether they have a significant anti-competitive effect. If they do, there will be a further stage of examination to decide whether the rule is nevertheless justified as having an anti-competitive effect no greater than necessary for the achievement of one of several permitted purposes. If that effect is considered unjustifiable, action may be ordered to remedy the problem. At present, the Bill provides that the second stage of the process should be carried out by the Treasury. However, following Don Cruickshank's recommendations, we propose to put the procedures for recognised bodies on a comparable basis to that for the FSA. The Competition Commission will carry out that task, subject to a ministerial power to override its decision, which would be used only in exceptional circumstances. We shall table amendments to achieve that in due course.

There are several differences, as I said, between this regime and the FSA competition scrutiny arrangements, of which two are particularly significant. First the recognised body regime provides for competition scrutiny at two stages. When a new body applies to the FSA for recognition, its proposed rule book will be scrutinised for any anti-competitive effects. Having achieved recognition, the body will be subject to continuing scrutiny to ensure that it does not, for example, make new anti-competitive rules.

The second difference between these clauses and part IX is that they provide that regulatory provisions and practices should be scrutinised not only for any significant anti-competitive effect, but to see whether they might result in an abuse of a dominant market position by a recognised body or its members.

I am grateful to the hon. Gentleman for explaining that amendment No. 875 is a probing amendment. While I can understand that that may be aimed at ensuring that the protection that competition scrutiny provides should be available to UK consumers and businesses, it would not have that effect in practice. It would cut back unhelpfully on the available protection.

Let us suppose that a UK-recognised exchange made a rule that was anti-competitive but which had an impact only on foreign stockbrokers. If they stayed, it is likely that they would pass on the cost of the disadvantage to their customers, who might well include UK consumers. As we would all agree, none of those outcome would be desirable.

Authority's general duty

Mr. Timms: I beg to move amendment No. 887, in page 144, line 27, leave out from 'must' to end of line 31 and end insert

    'keep informed about"

    (a) the way in which the Council supervises and regulates the market at Lloyd's; and

    (b) the way in which regulated activities are being carried on in that market.

    (a) any of its powers under this Part;

Part XVIII is complicated unlike the rest of the Bill because it plugs gaps to bring the Society of Lloyd's and those operating in the market within the FSA's regulatory powers under the Bill. This special approach is needed to reflect the unique nature of the market.

The Bill gives the FSA some discretion over how it discharges its obligations for the regulation of Lloyd's.

Clause 285 provides that the society"a body corporate"is to be authorised to carry on certain regulated activities. The permission will be defined by the FSA, as if it had been granted under part IV. Primarily, the permission will cover making arrangements which enable members to carry out contracts of insurance. The permission does not include carrying out contracts of insurance, which is done by the society's members, not the society itself.

The activities of managing and members' agents, or underwriting agents, will be specified in an order made by the Treasury under clause 20(5) and so will be regulated activities for the purposes of the Bill. Underwriting agents will need to be authorised and have relevant permission under parts III and IV. Therefore, no special arrangements for such agents are needed here.

As authorised persons, the Society and underwriting agents will be subject to the full range of the FSA's powers under the Bill. On the other hand, members of Lloyd's"both the traditional names and newer corporate members"will benefit from an exemption from the need to be authorised in relation to their insurance business at Lloyd's. None the less they will be subject to regulatory arrangements as directed by the FSA under powers contained in this part and to the powers of the Council of Lloyds. The FSA will be able to issue directions in one of two ways: either under clause 286, which will apply directly to members, or under clause 288, which is aimed at the council and which allows it to direct the council to impose reuirements on members.

There is an extra dimension to regulation of Lloyd's which the FSA does not have in relation to authorised persons generally. Not only must it consider the need to exercise its powers in pursuit of its regulatory objectives and in accordance with the regulatory principles, but the FSA must also take into account the role of the Council of Lloyd's under the Lloyd's Acts and form a view about the extent to which it needs to intervene by regulating activities itself, or alternatively use its powers to make directions under this part of the Bill.

This is a fundamental element of the regime that is being created for Lloyd's. On the one hand, it recognises that Lloyd's is unique; on the other, it gives the FSA powers to supervise and regulate the society and the regulated activities carried on by its members and their agents. To an extent, the approach will be governed by provisions in the single market directives that relate to insurance, but a good deal of scope is left for the FSA to exercise its judgment.

This is a balanced approach. It will help to deliver continued protection to policyholders, but it will also help to deliver higher standards of regulation for the protection of capital providers to the market, in whatever form, and the integrity of the market as a whole. At the same time, it will enable the FSA to be sensitive to the special needs of Lloyd's and avoid the possibility of the Society and its members having to face unfair and unnecessary regulatory burdens.
