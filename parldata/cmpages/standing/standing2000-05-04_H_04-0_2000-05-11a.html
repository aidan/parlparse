Clause 13

Basis of calculation of ad valorem element of duty on cigarettes

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. David Heathcoat-Amory (Wells): I do not want a long debate on this clause. I hope that the main debate on tobacco and smuggling will take place under clause 14, so I have only one question. Clause 13 appears sensible. As I understand it, the excise duty is calculated by reference partly to the number of cigarettes and partly to the price, and a percentage is charged. Reading the notes on clauses, I understand that there is an avoidance problem, in that manufacturers can have a recommended price, but in fact charge more than that by putting a higher price on the packs. Therefore, the duty should be calculated on the higher price. If I have understood that correctly, I shall not quarrel with that.

However, I am slightly intrigued to know what happens when there is neither a recommended price nor a marked price. Let us suppose that a trader imports a consignment of cigarettes from a new source. How much tax is payable? Let us suppose that an individual at the frontier brings in a huge number of cigarettes, on which duty has already been paid, but at a lower rate in another country, perhaps outside the European Union. That person wants to pay duty on the cigarettes, because he does not want to be accused of doing anything illegal. He does not intend to smoke them all himself, but wishes to sell them on to workmates or friends and, therefore, wants to pay the full duty. Presumably, he has no recommended retail price for those cigarettes and nothing, certainly not in English currency, is stamped on the pack. How does the system work in such eventualities?

The Financial Secretary to the Treasury (Mr. Stephen Timms): Mr. Cook, I welcome you back to the Chair. As the right hon. Gentleman said, the clause changes the basis for calculating the ad valorem or price-related element of the duty on cigarettes. It will prevent tax avoidance by cigarette manufacturers and importers by ensuring that the duty charged is based on the highest retail price at the time duty is payable. The excise duty on cigarettes has two parts-a specific duty calculated by the number of cigarettes and an ad valorem duty calculated as a percentage of the retail price.

Of late, there has been some evidence that, in a small number of cases, some advantage has been taken of the current law, which requires the calculation and payment of the ad valorem component based on published recommended retail prices, even though individual packs are marked for sale at a higher price. That system has the potential to become more widespread. It has been used to avoid paying the higher ad valorem duty due following a price increase. The measure provides that the basis for the calculation of ad valorem duty is the higher of either the price marked on the cigarette pack when duty is due or the published recommended retail selling price. That complements our action to curb trade forestalling of duty increases, which will be considered in the longer debate that the right hon. Gentleman and I expect will take place on clause 14.

In an example of the kind about which the right hon. Gentleman asked, the requirement is technically to charge duty on the highest price at which cigarettes are sold in the United Kingdom. If there is any doubt, it is for Customs and Excise officials to use their best judgment about the correct price to use. In practice, Customs and Excise tends to consult its records and use the most popular price category.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I am grateful to the Financial Secretary, but I am none the wiser. I gave an example of a new importation of which there would not be a record of any sort. By definition, there would therefore be no established retail price. My second example related to an individual attempting to pay duty at the frontier. Clearly, Customs and Excise would have nothing to go on, as everything would lie in the future. The person might modestly mark up his purchase of cigarettes from a country such as Turkey or another further afield in order to resell them. There would be no published tariff and nothing on which Customs and Excise could work.

It is all very well to say that Customs and Excise could make a judgment, but I would like to know on what it would be based. Tax cannot be levied purely at its discretion if that tax is based on a figure that is simply plucked out of the air. Will the Financial Secretary give a fuller explanation now, or let me have one in due course?

Mr. Timms: I have made the point that the strict interpretation of what is required is for duty to be charged at the highest price for which cigarettes are on sale in the UK. In the example that the right hon. Gentleman gave, I imagine that the person importing the cigarettes would be required to state the price at which it is intended that they be sold in the UK. Duty will then be payable on that price, so the question is about the highest price at which cigarettes will be sold and on which duty is payable.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I still find the situation in which someone does not know at what price he eventually wishes to sell the cigarettes unsatisfactory. If it were a new consignment, possibly of a new brand of cigarettes, from a country that had not previously exported to the United Kingdom, we could be in entirely novel territory. I am surprised that there is no definitive answer on the revenue payable. If the individual brought in a large consignment, the sums involved could be considerable. So it is unsatisfactory for someone to turn up at the border, say that he will resell the cigarettes, hand over a cheque and walk away free. That is a very confused and confusing way of levying an important duty on an importer.

I am hardly the wiser after the Financial Secretary's reply. I understand it in terms of an established system of importation of a known brand from a previous supplier through a route that is known to customs, but I am talking about a new situation or about someone who has in his possession many cigarettes that he has bought very cheaply, which he does not intend to smoke himself, who has a crisis of conscience at the border and wishes to pay the duty. Will he simply be asked the price at which he intends to resell them and be asked to pay the duty? If so, I find that unsatisfactory and subjective.

Mr. Timms: The answer depends on the circumstances. He described the case of someone who decided, mid-journey, that he wanted to sell the cigarettes that he was importing, but the normal situation would be a commercial one and commercial records will be available for that. Such packs will need UK health warnings to be sold legally in the UK so one could not decide to sell such cigarettes on the spur of the moment. In the off-the-cuff situation to which the right hon. Gentleman referred, officials will be able to refer to the price established for that category of cigarette-king-size for example-in the UK. Commercial documentation will normally be available, a commercial arrangement will have been entered into and UK health warnings will be printed on the pack. On that basis, those cigarettes can legally be sold in the UK and information will be available for the application of the correct rate of duty.

Clause 14

Fiscal marks on tobacco products

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: I beg to move amendment No. 6, in page 8, leave out lines 40 to 46.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take amendment No. 7, in page 10, leave out lines 4 to 26.

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: This clause is important, because it breaks new ground in giving Customs and Excise additional powers to counter smuggling, by setting up a fiscal marking system that will place a ``duty paid'' mark on packs of cigarettes and tobacco. People possessing cigarettes without such marks may be assumed to have imported them illegally or obtained them from illegal sources. That is not a new idea, but it is a new means of countering smuggling.

Last week, we debated on the Floor of the House the issue of smuggling of tobacco products and alcohol. We agreed about the seriousness of that, but we did not get to the bottom of the connection between the level of duty on those products and the smuggling incentive or incidence. Correspondingly, it is difficult to assess the likely effectiveness of other measures against smuggling. That is particularly regrettable, because people have studied that issue and commented on it. Mr. Martin Taylor was asked to investigate it and made a report to the Treasury. As I understand it, he also wrote to the Chancellor about his findings.

The Select Committee on the Treasury is Labour controlled and has a Labour Chairman, so it is worth my quoting its comments on the Government's failure to publish Mr. Taylor's report. It said:

    We consider it unacceptable that the Treasury should commission a review of an important area of Government policy, and announce policy measures apparently based on the outcome of that review, without allowing Parliament access to the review's conclusions. We recommend that the Chancellor make available for Parliamentary scrutiny the results of Mr. Martin Taylor's recent evaluation of Government policy to tackle tobacco smuggling.

We are considering the measures to combat smuggling mentioned in that quote, so perhaps the Minister can tell us more about the report. We have all made it clear that sensitive intelligence or national security matters could be deleted from such a report.

We need a better explanation of why the Government are keeping the report secret, because that makes it more difficult to discuss such amendments. The Financial Secretary hinted previously that sensitive matters were involved, but Opposition Members feel that they are sensitive for the Government only because they are an embarrassment. The report points out that unless the root cause of the problem-the duty level-is tackled, all the other measures will be in danger of failing. Perhaps the Financial Secretary will tell us a little more about his reasons for keeping the report secret, especially as the Government have apparently made a commitment to openness and the public's right to know. If public money has been used to commission and write a report, we are entitled to more of an explanation of why it has not been published.

Although the report that the Government produced on the back of the Taylor report was sanitised and incomplete, it nevertheless highlighted an extremely serious problem. More than one in five cigarettes smoked in this country are imported illegally, as is about 80 per cent. of hand-rolling tobacco. It is especially alarming that, even after the introduction of the measures that the Government propose, including the fiscal marking scheme that we are now considering, the problem is due to continue getting worse for the next three years. An increase in the number of smokers is anticipated, especially among young people. It especially appals me that the illegal trade is growing, putting increasingly more money into the hands of criminals and smugglers, and less into the pockets of the retail and distributive trade, including small shopkeepers, who are also threatened by developments in the high street.

Bitterness is developing among people in the retail and distributive trade, making them rather less inclined to look favourably on the fiscal marking scheme. They realise that many of the costs involved are likely to fall on them, and they may fall foul of the date marking scheme, if it is introduced. It will certainly involve extra expense in setting stock levels and managing businesses. They realise that they have an obligation-indeed, a civic duty-to counter illegality and under-age smoking, but they are increasingly annoyed that the Government do not seem to be playing their part and are placing the full obligation on the trade, while running a fiscal policy that the criminal fraternity regards as a complete joke. It has been put to me that, ``You up in Parliament have no common sense if you really think that making cigarettes more expensive is not going to increase smuggling. Where do you get your information?'' I find it difficult to answer that.

Turning to the details of the fiscal marking scheme, it is to be introduced through rather general regulations. My eyebrows always shoot up when I read phrases such as

    The Commissioners may make provision by regulations as to such matters relating to fiscal marks as appear to the Commissioners to be necessary or expedient.

The phrase ``necessary or expedient'' always gives the Government great latitude.

I am also slightly worried that other details of the fiscal marking scheme can be introduced or amended simply by notice-in other words, without reference to Parliament, simply by pinning up, publishing or issuing a notice. People may incur penalties under a law that can be altered by a simple notice, which should concern the Committee, but is a matter for a separate amendment. Does the Minister really believe that the fiscal notice scheme will make a material difference? Will he quantify his hopes for the scale of that difference?

In our debates last week, some Labour Members were looking for a panacea for the smuggling problem. The Government said that they would purchase X-ray machines that could examine lorries that came in from the continent. The hon. Member for Workington (Mr. Campbell-Savours), who is sadly not a member of the Committee, displayed a touching faith in technology's ability to counter the problem. He, and members of the Committee, have perhaps not had experience of the sheer ingenuity of the criminals engaged in that trade. If one blocks one avenue, they find another means to import the product.

Dr. Nick Palmer (Broxtowe): Is the right hon. Gentleman saying that he would not be opposed to the increase in duty on health or other grounds, were he not sceptical about the ability of Customs and Excise to cut back the smuggling, or that, even if those anti-smuggling measures were effective, he would still regret the increase?

Mr. Heathcoat-Amory: Let me put it this way. I would be sympathetic to the proposition that higher tobacco prices deterred smoking if I could be sure that the increased duty rate raised the price of illegal tobacco products. However, raising the price of legal tobacco does not, by definition, increase the price of illegally imported tobacco. Indeed, it gives an incentive for smokers to switch from legal to illegal supplies. The Government's own investigations and report show that that undermines the concept that a higher price has a benign health effect. The evidence shows that, regrettably, the incidence of smoking is increasing, so the health policy cannot be working.

I would be more sympathetic to the policy if I could be sure that measures to crack down on smuggling were likely to be effective-but they are not. The Government report shows that they believe that the problem will continue to worsen for the next three years, despite the recruitment of an extra 950 civil servants, the deployment of the X-ray machines and the fiscal marking scheme.

We are faced with a policy that has broken down, as an intolerable limit of illegality has been reached. The Government must address the issue of the duty level, which is why we ask for evidence in the matter. I do not want to make any bold assertions about the link between duty levels and smuggling. However, common sense tells us that smuggling must be affected by the price, in some way; we need the exact correlation from the Government.

Other countries, notably the Nordic countries and Canada, have investigated the matter and come to the conclusion that they have to work on the duty level as well. It will not be sufficient to erect more effective barriers against smuggling unless the price issue is addressed. That might also be an important matter for the Committee, but I do not have the relevant information. The Government do have that information, which is why I persist in asking for such evidence before we proceed with anti-smuggling measures.

I congratulate the Government on their provisions to add fiscal marking to the panoply of measures that Customs and Excise has to combat smuggling. That is an important and welcome step. The amendments would have two effects. First, they would weaken the Government's ability to tackle forestalling tax avoidance devices. Mr. Timms: Amendments Nos. 8 and 9 would have two effects. First, they would prevent the commissioners of Customs and Excise from specifying some technical aspects of the fiscal mark by notice. That means that any changes to the slightest detail of the pack mark specification would have to be subject to revised regulations. Secondly, they would require the commissioners to post a notice to all persons to whom they thought that the offence of suffering the use of premises for the sale of unmarked tobacco might apply.

Clause 14 distinguishes between three levels of provision, which we believe warrant different levels of parliamentary scrutiny. A high-level change, such as extending the scope of the fiscal marking scheme to cigars, for example, could be achieved only by an order approved in draft by both Houses of Parliament. Other provisions deal with less important issues in which Parliament might none the less still have a strong interest, such as when products should and should not carry the mark. Such provisions need to be contained in regulations that may be annulled by a resolution of either House.

There is then a third level, which consists of detailed, technical provisions, which it would be sensible for the commissioners to be able to make by notice. We have been careful to provide for such a course by direct delegation from the Finance Bill, rather than by permitting sub-delegation through regulations. I hope that the Committee will welcome that caution. There is a directly comparable example in schedule 1 to the Finance Act 1997-the last Finance Act before the general election. The schedule makes provision for the commissioners to give directions on all aspects of making a gaming duty return. The provision that we are implementing goes one stage further and introduces an additional protection by requiring the publication of a notice, which will add to transparency. At my request, the Committee was sent copies of the draft regulations and the notice on 3 May.

The matters that can be dealt with by notice will include the exact specification of the fiscal mark, the exact words used, the size and font of the lettering, whether the mark needs to be on the outer wrapping as well as the retail packet, the exact position of the mark on the pack and so on. Those matters will require action on the part of the half a dozen manufacturers involved and of a small number of importers-they are the people who will need to act on the information once it is published. The ability to prescribe such matters by notice will allow the commissioners to act relatively quickly-for example, in response to manufacturers' requests to accommodate marks on unusual packaging and to any counterfeiting that emerges. In such cases it will be necessary to respond quickly.

Of course it is right that changes to the specification of the mark should be the subject of proper consultation with the trade. As with many of the matters that the commissioners of Customs and Excise already look after, they will continue to discuss changes to the detail of the pack mark scheme with the trade before making them and to allow sufficient time for them to be made. It is right that that should continue to be the case, and it is in everyone's interests that it should. However, I see no reason to reserve such matters to regulations. Members of the Committee will note that we have made provision for such matters to be prescribed in regulations. That power could be used to make changes that are perceived to be sensitive or to alter the more long-lasting elements of the scheme once it has become well established. However, the power is not needed for minor matters, and the notice route will be fine.
