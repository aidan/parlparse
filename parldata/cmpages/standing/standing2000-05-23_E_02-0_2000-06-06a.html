Mr. Malcolm Moss (North-East Cambridgeshire): I beg to move amendment No. 4, in page 2, line 22, leave out `other disposal' and insert

    `disposal by other such means as the appropriate authority may specify in regulations'.

The amendment provides that the disposal of animals that are the subject of a forfeiture order must be carried out in an appropriate way. In the overwhelming majority of cases, disposal, particularly of mink, will be by means of destruction. Other means of disposal, such as farming for another purpose or the use of the animals for pets or for export, are not appropriate for mink.

As we have said on many occasions, the Bill is intended to cover all animals farmed primarily or solely for fur, not just mink. If other animals came within the provisions of the Bill, alternative means of disposal might be feasible. In such an event, the amendment would ensure that the only means of disposal appropriate for the species concerned were used.

The Parliamentary Secretary to the Ministry of Agriculture, Fisheries and Food (Mr. Elliot Morley): I understand the points that the hon. Gentleman makes. I argue that the amendment is unnecessary because the courts judge what is most appropriate in the circumstances. The hon. Gentleman and I do not disagree about disposal. There might be a number of methods of disposal that do not require destruction. Destruction might be necessary for mink, given the damage that they can cause if they escape, but chinchilla, for example, could be used as pets. It is for the courts to decide the most appropriate means of disposal, which might not require destruction.

Mr. Moss: I am grateful to the Minister for his comments. Our amendment mentions ``the appropriate authority'', which in the context of the Bill is the Minister at the Ministry for Agriculture, Fisheries and Food. That is why we included the phrase

    the appropriate authority may specify in future regulations.

I can nevertheless assure the hon. Gentleman that welfare and other regulations will continue to apply.

Mr. Moss: The amendments and the new clause deal with the qualifications of the people employed to deal with matters under subsection (1)(a) and (b). Amendment No. 5 specifically requires the court to appoint a specially qualified person to enforce a forfeiture order. The Bill does not prescribe by whom the forfeiture order should be carried out. It is possible that a convicted fur farmer could be charged with the responsibility for the destruction or disposal of the animals under his care. It may be more likely, however, for responsibility to be given to a third party who is so charged because the court is unlikely to trust the farmer to carry out the order properly or fully.

In those circumstances, it is important that whoever is appointed by the court is suitably qualified in the welfare and handling of the particular species. Much play has been made—both on Second Reading and in our earlier proceedings—of the dangers posed to local wildlife by mink, if released or if they escape. Most people would agree that mink require experienced handling. It would not be desirable for the safety or welfare of the animals if an inexperienced person were given the job.

Amendment No. 10 would ensure that the persons authorised to enter premises where it is suspected that fur farming is being carried out are suitably qualified. It would prevent the authorisation of inappropriate persons—people unqualified in the handling of animals or representatives of animal pressure groups, for example—to enter premises for enforcement purposes.

The entry and enforcement powers in the Bill permit inspection for the gathering of evidence and the carrying out of a forfeiture order. They are wide powers, especially as the authorisation is general, so there is no requirement to make an application for authorisation in any particular case. There could be a significant invasion of privacy and it is proper that such powers should be available only to an appropriate person.

In the case of entry to carry out a forfeiture order, it is important that the authorised person is experienced in the handling of animals, both to protect their welfare and to prevent the risk of escape.

An appropriate person might be one of the Ministry's own vets from the state veterinary service, or someone licensed to operate on behalf of that service.

New clause 1 would ensure that only suitably qualified persons were given responsibility for entering premises for inspection purposes and for carrying out forfeiture orders. The same arguments as I have advanced for the other amendments apply to the new clause. The powers of entry and enforcement are wide-ranging and general and we want the courts to be mindful of the considerable powers that have been given to people. To avoid unnecessary escapes and problems for the animals involved, we seek an assurance that the courts will nominate qualified persons to do the jobs necessary under the Bill.

The amendments are an attempt to address their concerns.

Amendment No. 11 is simple. It would change the word ``may'' in clause 5(2) to ``shall''. That would give a stronger sign that the Government are serious and committed. ``May'' is conditional—they may do this or they may do that, depending on how they feel. ``Shall'' would make it clear that they are committed to introducing compensation schemes. Amendment No. 12 specifies the elements of fur farming for which compensation would be payable. That would reassure fur farmers that the basis for the calculation is transparent and takes into account all the critical aspects of their business.

Fur farmers are likely to incur major losses. First, it might take time to clear and recover land before it can be used for other purposes. Secondly, buildings and equipment for fur farming are specialised. It is generally accepted—as I am sure the Minister's Department will concur—that they cannot be converted to other uses. Thirdly, the livestock provide the income stream for mink farming businesses. The mink will have to be destroyed. There is no other use for them because they are reared only for the value of their fur. Fourthly, it would be difficult to transfer the goodwill that arises from trading with the industry and customers, both here and abroad, to a new business undertaking because fur farming is so specialised.

We are pleased that the Government have dropped from their Bill the statutory restriction that the private Member's Bill would have placed on the payment of compensation for loss of income. However, they have not given an assurance that loss of income will be reflected in the compensation scheme. It is our view and that of the National Farmers Union that it is imperative to build the key elements of the compensation scheme into the Bill to reassure the industry and make the Government's intentions clearcut.

Amendment No. 15 relates to the payment of legal fees for the cost of the advice that fur farmers need to receive. That cost should be reimbursed in the compensation scheme. A similar amendment was tabled when the private Member's Bill was debated in Committee. It was rejected—probably on the Government's advice—because it referred to ``advisers''. The promoter's side argued that that placed no limit on the number of people that fur farmers could consult, so creating a blank cheque. Therefore, amendment No. 15 refers to

    the payment of reasonable fees for professional advice.

That would rule out paying a list of advisers. To my knowledge, no other Government have introduced a Bill to ban a whole sector of farming, so the valuation of the businesses and the negotiation of compensation will be a novel process for all concerned.

It has been standard practice for many years for claimants under the land compensation code to be entitled to have their professional advisers' reasonable fees paid for by the acquiring authority. That rule would apply, for example, if the Department of the Environment, Transport and the Regions wanted to force a motorway through a mink farm, which was for that reason subject to a compulsory purchase order. Those circumstances are analogous with the Bill, which would impose a compulsory ban on fur farming. The effect would be the same in each case: the use of state compulsion to confiscate an individual's property, on grounds of wider public benefit, public morality or whatever the Government wanted to give as the main reason. The ban on fur farming is a unique occurrence. Fur farming is a specialist form of farming. It is essential for the farmers to have access to good professional advice in negotiating their compensation claims. The amendment would help to ensure that such professional advice was available to them.

Amendment No. 16 would provide for the cost of land clearance to be included in the compensation payment in appropriate cases. A similar amendment was tabled in Committee when the private Members' Bill was being considered. The Government then gave an assurance that as a general principle they would consider the cost of demolition in relation to a claim in particular cases. However, they entered a caveat, saying that it would not be unreasonable for claims to be considered on their individual merits rather than under blanket provision. We agree and our amendment conforms to that requirement. Nevertheless, no professional opinion is available to the effect that the specialised buildings and equipment associated with mink farms are capable of conversion to another type of farming or business use. On the contrary, a clear view is held that after a ban on fur farming the land would need to be cleared before being put to another use.

The amendment would provide that the costs of clearing the land could be paid under the compensation scheme. The estimated costs of clearing land in that way are, as the Minister knows, not insignificant. Clearing land to return it to a green-field site would cost in the region of £17,000 per hundred breeding females. For a 6,000-female farm the figure would be £100,000. Those are substantial sums of money. It would be wholly unreasonable not to include those costs in any compensation calculation as land clearance is a prerequisite of allowing the land to be used for any other type of farming or business.

Amendment No. 17 would provide for compensation to cover costs associated with winding up a fur-farming business. There is a general rule in compensation schemes—and the Minister will no doubt make play of it—that claimants should mitigate their losses where possible. Nevertheless, the cost of the losses associated with the winding up of a business can be wide-ranging and significant. It is right therefore that that should be reflected in the compensation arrangements.

Amendment No. 17 would provide for compensation to be payable for forced sales of capital such as property, land or equipment. If a farm had to terminate a contract prematurely—one covering its lease of land, perhaps—the costs of that withdrawal would be covered. The leasing of buildings and equipment is subject to time scales. There is always a cost when people wish to terminate such leases in advance of the appropriate date. There might be contracts for the cleaning of buildings or equipment. The fur farmer might have to pay compensation to cover contracts that he or she had entered into. There might be redundancy payments. Employees will no longer be needed because the farmer will, if he can, make his livelihood in some other way.
