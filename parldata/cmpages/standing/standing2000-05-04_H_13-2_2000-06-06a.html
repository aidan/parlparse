Miss Melanie Johnson: I believe that I can help the hon. Gentleman. I concluded that it was probably a probing amendment, although I was not sure.

Section 632A of the Taxes Act 1988 specifies the conditions under which people are eligible to contribute to a stakeholder or personal pension. For EMI, the changes would undermine the policy of the scheme, which is to help the smaller, more risky companies to recruit and retain the key employees that they need to make them grow, especially if the same benefits could be obtained by staying put. An unlisted company for which there would be no test of size under the proposal could be large. Some private companies, if quoted, could be large enough to be considered for the FTSE 100.

Market capitalisation would be unreliable as a test for quoted companies. A newly floated company, for example, may have a high share price but no profits and few assets. The small companies at which EMI is aimed often have volatile share prices, and some of the small dot.coms that would otherwise qualify would be excluded because of their large market capitalisations. The proposal would run the risk of excluding the very companies that EMI is designed to help.

The enterprise investment and corporate venturing schemes, which encourage equity investment in small companies, are targeted in that way because smaller companies experience the greatest difficulty in raising funds for growth and development, and benefit most from corporate venturing relationships.

By no stretch of the imagination do I believe that a company with gross assets of £30 million can be regarded as small. Raising the requirement in that way would open the scheme to much larger companies, which do not share the same problems in raising finance. Worse still, because such companies are more secure, bringing them into the scope of the scheme would divert funds away from the small companies that incur greater risks. The purpose of the scheme would be undermined and costs would escalate sharply.

The gross asset requirement of £15 million to £16 million was introduced for EIS as recently as 1998, and was adopted for CVS because the focus of both schemes was the same. Our policy is to keep the rules of the venture capital schemes in line so far as possible, for obvious reasons of simplicity for all concerned. We keep the requirement under review, but it is too soon to consider an increase. An increase of this magnitude would be inappropriate, as it would completely destroy the small business focus of the scheme. The amendments would vastly increase the costs and undermine the purpose of the scheme, so I hope that the Committee will reject the proposals.

The hon. Member for Torridge and West Devon made a point about the gross asset test and how companies apply it. The test has been used for EIS and VCT for the past six years and has not produced any problems. The Inland Revenue's statement of practice was last updated in 1998, and will be extended to cover EMI and CVS. There are no problems in practice from the EIS and VCT rules. Normally, the figures from ordinary balance sheets are used.
