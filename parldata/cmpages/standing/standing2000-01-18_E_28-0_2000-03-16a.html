Schedule 11

Road user charging and workplace parking levy: financial provisions

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for the Environment, Transport and the Regions (Mr. Keith Hill): I beg to move amendment No. 657, in page 178, line 20, leave out 'by'.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to take Government amendments Nos. 658 and 660.

Mr. Hill: A hearty good morning to you, Mr. Gale. I am sure that we are all raring to go for another sitting of this fascinating Committee.

I shall provide a tour of the horizon on schedule 11, as it contains several important provisions including the financial arrangements and requirements that will apply to the revenue stream generated by any road user, charging or workplace parking levy scheme. It includes arrangements for the hypothecation of charging revenues and proposals for separate and published income and expenditure accounts for those moneys.

Paragraph 1 defines a relevant scheme for the purpose of schedule 11. A relevant scheme will be any road user or workplace parking charging scheme introduced by a local authority, either singly or jointly, or a trunk road user charging scheme introduced by the appropriate national authority.

Paragraph 2 defines an expense of a charging or licensing scheme for the purpose of deriving the net proceeds of a scheme from its gross proceeds. We will table some technical amendments to schedule 11 and Report to clarify the definition of net proceeds in paragraph 2. [Interruption.] Does the hon. Member for Poole (Mr. Syms) want to intervene?

Mr. Robert Syms (Poole): No.

Mr. Hill: Well, that is a relief. I have a note on the subject that might content the hon. Gentleman.

Paragraphs 3 and 4 provide that local authorities that introduce joint charging or licensing schemes can share the net proceeds between themselves. That also applies to trunk road charging schemes that may be introduced by the appropriate national authority at the request of a local authority.

Paragraph 5 requires local authorities to set up and maintain separate accounts for the income raised from new charges and for the expenditure of that income on local transport improvements. Those accounts will be published annually and to ensure transparency and local accountability, and will help to demonstrate the additionality of charging revenues, to which we are committed.

Paragraph 6 provides that charging revenues can be used for 10 years to make good any deficits in general funds that might be incurred in setting up charging and licensing schemes.

Paragraph 7 provides that the net revenue raised from every local authority charging scheme introduced within 10 years of the commencement of the schedule will be retained locally for exclusive spending on local transport improvements. That will be the case for at least 10 years for each scheme. We recognise that the hypothecation is crucial to the public acceptability and success of any charging scheme and will lead to greater transport choice for all. The paragraph also enables the appropriate national authority to prescribe circumstances in which schemes that are varied during the first 10 years of the commencement of the schedule are to be considered as new or amended schemes. That is to determine when the period of guaranteed hypothecation for transport spending should apply for those schemes.

Paragraph 8 empowers the appropriate national authority to make regulations to set out the arrangements that will apply to the retention and use of charging revenues for local charging and licensing schemes, once the period of guaranteed hypothecation has expired.

The regulations will also cover schemes introduced within the first 10 years of the commencement of the schedule, once the scheme's guaranteed period of hypothecation has ended. They will also cover schemes brought forward after the first 10 years following the commencement of the schedule. The regulations were made following a thorough review of our policy of hypothecation.

Paragraphs 9 and 10 require local authorities to prepare plans to set out how the revenue stream from new charges will be spent. A 10-year plan will cover the initial opening period of the scheme and a more detailed plan will cover the remainder of the period of the current local transport plan. The more detailed plan will be rolled forward in successive local transport plans. The spending proposals will be approved by the appropriate national authority. The approval process will provide a safeguard to ensure that charging revenues will be spent on value-for-money transport measures that support local transport objectives.

Paragraph 11 provides that the Secretary of State shall make regulations to provide for the use of charging revenues in a joint scheme involving a charging authority outside London and a London charging authority. The Secretary of State must consult the mayor before making the regulations.

Paragraph 12 applies only to trunk road schemes. It provides that the net proceeds from trunk road charging schemes that are made at the request of a local authority and brought forward within 10 years of the commencement of the Schedule will be retained for spending on transport for 10 years for each scheme. As far as charging on trunk road bridges and tunnels of at least 600 m is concerned, the period of hypothecation will be for 10 years from whenever the scheme is introduced. That acknowledges the fact that new structures can take a significant amount of time to build. As with local authority schemes, paragraph 12 enables the appropriate national authority to prescribe circumstances in which schemes that are varied are to be regarded as new or amended schemes. It also empowers the appropriate national authority to make regulations to set out the arrangements that will apply for the retention and use of trunk road charging revenues once the period of guaranteed hypothecation has expired. The regulations will also provide for the hypothecation arrangements for trunk road charging schemes produced after the 10th anniversary of the commencement of the schedule.

I am afraid that I remain wholly unconvinced by the underlying purpose of the amendments tabled by the official Opposition. Amendment No. 793 would result in the indefinite hypothecation of the revenues raised from local authority road user and workplace parking charges for transport spending. Similarly, amendment No. 792 would result in the indefinite hypothecation of trunk road charging revenues, with amendment No. 791 requiring the revenues raised from trunk road charging schemes to be spent exclusively on the trunk road where the charge was levied.

It is a trifle rich on the part of the Opposition to press for the indefinite hypothecation of the revenues from charges that they claim to oppose in principle. I do not want to get too aggressive about this as it is early in the morning and I am sure we feel a little fragile, but honesty itself, to which the Government are dedicated, requires that I at least draw attention to that inconsistency in the Opposition's position. The inconsistency in the Opposition's position is highlighted by the fact that their 1996 Green Paper, to which we have referred, contained a presumption in favour of giving local authorities new charging powers, but gave no guarantee that the revenues would be hypothecated for local transport improvements. That is the ground-breaking aspect of the Bill.

The arrangements that we included in the Greater London Authority Act 1999, and which we have extended to the Bill by guaranteeing hypothecation, represent a major breakthrough. Unlike the previous Conservative Government, we recognise that that is crucial to the success and acceptability of any such scheme. The Bill therefore provides that every penny of the net revenues raised from local authority charging or licensing schemes introduced within 10 years of the schedule's commencement will be retained locally and ring-fenced for transport spending for each scheme's initial period. The expectation is that the initial period will be 10 years, beginning from the implementation of a scheme. However, paragraph 7(6) of schedule 11 enables the appropriate national authority to guarantee the hypothecation of charging revenues for more than 10 years for individual schemes. We have debated this point to a limited extent, but it might interest the hon. Member for Bath to know that that flexibility could be particularly valuable if a local authority wanted to undertake a private finance initiative deal, and the private sector required a guarantee that a revenue stream would be available for more than 10 years.

We propose that the revenue from trunk road charging schemes that are introduced at the request of the local authority, and which are introduced within 10 years of the schedule's commencement, will be ring-fenced for transport spending for 10 years. For charging schemes on trunk road tunnels and bridges of at least 600m in length, the period of hypothecation will be 10 years. For all schemes, the Bill provides that the arrangements for the retention and use of charging revenues will be reviewed after 10 years. It would be irresponsible to guarantee hypothecation for transport spending indefinitely, because spending charging revenues on transport in perpetuity might not deliver value-for-money improvements in the medium to longer term. That is why it is appropriate to have a review after 10 years.

The review may recommend that 100 per cent. hypothecation of charging revenues for transport spending should continue for all schemes for a further period. I am sure that many hon. Members think that hypothecation will be needed for more than 10 years to make up for the inadequate amounts spent on our transport infrastructure under the previous Administration. I can assure them that the Bill explicitly allows for hypothecation for transport spending to continue after the review, although, clearly, I cannot pre-empt the outcome of such a review.

In addition, amendment No. 791 would limit the use to which trunk road charging revenues can be put. It says that any amount raised from a trunk road charging scheme can be spent only on the charged trunk road in question. That suggests that the hon. Gentlemen would prefer the proceeds from such charging schemes to be spent on gold plating trunk roads, bridges and tunnels rather than--

Mr. Woolas: Golf courses.

Mr. Hill: Gold plating, not golf courses. I make that clear, in case any confusion arises from my hon. Friend's comment. Such gold plating of specific parts of our motorway and trunk road network would be frivolous and shortsighted.

That may include maintaining or improving the trunk road network rather than simply the charged road in question. The amendment tabled by the hon. Members for Bath and for Tweeddale, Ettrick and Lauderdale would allow for borrowing on the strength of future charging revenues as a means of funding up front improvements to local transport. I agree that we can expect a charging scheme to need to be accompanied by some improvements to local transport before the scheme starts operating. That is known as early evidence. For example, it will be necessary to accomodate commuters who switch from cars to public transport. The Government do not envisage approving any charging scheme unless some improvements to local transport have been made before it becomes operational.

As the Committee will know, we are providing £755 million in 2000-01 to help local authorities implement their local transport plans. That is set to rise to more than £1 billion in 2001-02. That increase will make a difference to improving transport choice for all and help to provide motorists with a genuine alternative to the private car. We have also pledged that any real increases in fuel duty will be ring-fenced for transport spending. We await my Right hon. An extra £18 million was made available at the end of 1999, and partnership authorities will obviously be well placed to receive additional support.

I recognise that allowing borrowing on the strength of future charging revenues may seem an attractive means of funding even more up front improvements to local transport, but local authorities already have powers to borrow for capital spending purposes such as transport improvements. Under present rules, the amount of such borrowing depends on credit approval issued by the Government. Those rules are an essential part of the mechanism for regulating public expenditure in the interests of the national economy. That local borrowing has to be counted against the public sector borrowing requirement.

We are, however, currently reviewing the local government capital finance system. In particular, we are considering the possibility of allowing a local authority's leval of borrowing to be determined mainly by its affordability to the authority, taking account of the authority's overall financial position. We intend to issue a Green Paper on our proposals in the summer. To accept amendment No. 720 now would pre-empt the outcome of this consultation. That would not be wise, as I am sure that the hon. Member for Bath would agree.

Where a charging scheme could yield significant revenues, it would be worth exploring the scope of a deal or some other form of partnership with the private sector to fund up front private finance initative transport improvements. Paragraph 7(6) of schedule 11, which allow the appropriate national authority to guarantee more than 10 years of hypothecation for transport spending for individual schemes, makes that an exciting possibility. Such options can already be pursued under existing rules, and I assure the hon. Gentleman that the provisions in the Bill are sufficient to allow local authorities to make private finance initiative deals to fund transport improvements. No further provisions will be needed.

Clauses 168 to 175 deal with a number of issues, including supplementary powers for charging and licensing authorities, the disclosure of information in the operation of a scheme, arrangements for dealing with disputes and appeals, Crown application, the definitions used in part III and the issuing of guidance by the appropriate national authority.

Clause 169 contains general powers for the Secretary of State, for England, and the National Assembly for Wales, to issue guidance to local authorities on how to exercise their powers to introduce road user charging or workplace parking levy schemes. As the Committee is aware, we are working closely with the first authorities interested in developing schemes through our charging development partnership. Clause 170 allows the disclosure, of information by public bodies to a charging or licensing authority. That is essential to allow, for example, information about who is the registered keeper of a particular vehicle to be divulged by the Driver and Vehicle Licensing Agency for the purpose of liability for road user charges and the imposing of any penalty charges.

We also expect that an authority introducing a workplace parking levy scheme will want to use information that it already has on non-domestic ratepayers, for example, as a starting point for liability for the levy. Clause 171 gives the Government the power to make regulations about appeals, disputes, and evidence in relation to charging schemes and the workplace parking levy. New clause 28

Public parking places at business premises

    '.--(1) The Secretary of State may, by order, extend any or all of the provisions in Chapter II of this Part to include the provision of public parking places at premises connected with business activities.

    (2) For the purposes of subsection (1), public parking places are provided at any premises at any time if a parking place provided at the premises is at that time occupied by a motor vehicle used by anyone who is not included for the purposes of Clause 158.

    (3) For the purposes of subsection (1), "premises connected with business activities" are the same as those included under "business" in subsection (4) of Clause 158.

Mr. Don Foster: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The Minister told us in an earlier debate that he would not seek to persuade his hon. Friends to support this new clause. Nevertheless, I hope that in a relatively brief contribution I can persuade him to think again.

Hon. Members will be well aware of the contents of the White Paper that was produced in 1998. The Government argued in Paragraph 4.108 that

    There are strong arguments for workplace parking charges to be levied in all types of location, whether in the town centre or at out of town sites, in order to be consistent with our planning policy, particularly on the revitalisation of towns and cities, by influencing individuals' travel choice and businesses' location choice.

Sadly, during our deliberations on Tuesday, the Under-Secretary and the Minister made it clear that they are no longer persuaded of the need to consider workplace charging at out-of-town venues. We were told that it was extremely unlikely that the Secretary of State would be prepared to consider proposals from local authorities unless it was in an area where the prime problem was one of congestion. There has been a significant shift in the Government's thinking about the location of workplace charging levy schemes since the 1998 White Paper was published.

Another shift in the Government's thinking is even more recent than that that occurred between the publication of the White Paper and now. Indeed, it has occurred since the publication of "Breaking the Logjam", the Government's response to the consultation. Paragraph 6.4 of that document states:

    The Government proposes that only parking associated with the workplace should initially be subject to the levy.

In the White Paper, however, the Government clearly suggested that they were prepared to consider charges on other types of parking space. "Breaking the Logjam" does not deny that possibility, given the sentence that I have just quoted, which suggests that the Government are minded to consider other possibilities in future. Indeed, paragraph 6.7 shows that they have not ruled out other options, such as introducing a parking levy at out-of-town retail and leisure centres.

10.45 am

However, the Government acknowledge that they are not yet persuaded of the need for such schemes. Paragraph 6.7 states:

    Over-generous provision of parking for customers and visitors can have other undesirable effects, such as encouraging car-dependent development on edge-of-town and out-of-town sites. However, the Government is not yet persuaded that a levy on non-workplace parking would be the most effective way of addressing the issue of changing customer travel patterns.

Since the White Paper, the Government have had second thoughts, but have not ruled out the possibility of such a levy. They suggest going for an initial levy on workplace parking but are not yet persuaded of the merit of including the alternatives that I mentioned.

The Government have asked the Commission for Integrated Transport to examine the matter to discover whether extending the scope of the Bill's provisions would be a good idea. I do not wish to detain the Committee on why extending parking levies is a good thing, so I shall merely say that the sole purpose of new clause 28 is to provide the Government with an opportunity to introduce an extension by an order approved by both Houses of Parliament, should they be persuaded of the value of such an extension in light of the commission's report.

The Under-Secretary has said on several occasions in Committee that transport legislation does not often come before the House. If the commission recommends that the proposals be extended, the Government do not have a vehicle for introducing legislation unless they accept new clause 28.

There are strong arguments for and against the introduction of extensions. However, I put on record the fact that I have had detailed discussions with several organisations that could be affected by such extensions. I pay tribute to them, especially the supermarkets, for the work that they already do on green transport plans. I acknowledge the efforts of several supermarket chains to provide non-car transport to out-of-town shopping centres. However, much more needs to be done. In some parts of the country, certain roads may be more congested on Sundays than they are in the rush hour.

Such matters are being examined on the Government's request. They have not yet ruled extensions out, although they are not necessarily persuaded that they are a good idea. New clause 28 merely provides a vehicle that would enable the Government to introduce the necessary legislation by an order approved by both Houses if they were convinced of the merits of extension.

Mr. Hill: I thank the hon. Member for Bath for raising the important matter of the scope of the workplace parking levy provisions in the Bill in his usual reasonable well-informed fashion. However, I must disappoint the hon. Gentleman as I am not prepared to accept the new clause, although I appreciate the spirit in which it was tabled. Not for the first time, Liberal Democrat Members are kindly offering the Government an enabling provision, but it would be premature to accept it. Indeed, such an action could be misinterpreted as a signal of future Government policy, which would be unhelpful for our purposes and for bodies potentially affected by any such policy development.

As the hon. Gentleman said, our transport White Paper and the follow-up consultation paper "Breaking the Logjam" made it clear that the levy would initially apply only to workplace parking. We are not yet persuaded that a levy on customer parking is the most effective way of changing customer travel behaviour.

As we have said, we expect major retail and leisure operators to work with local authorities to build on the initiatives that some major retailers have already taken to tackle overdependency on the car. Those could include providing bus shelters, funding bus priority measures on the surrounding road network and providing secure cycle parking. Retail outlets could also extend or introduce easy and affordable home delivery services.

I assure the hon. Gentleman that we expect to make real progress on such matters. As we said in our White Paper, we shall ask the Commission for Integrated Transport to assess the effectiveness of such an approach and to consider the case for extending the scope of the levy to include customer parking. I shall ensure that the Committee's deliberations this morning are conveyed to the Commission. It would be better to wait for the commission's recommendations before taking a final decision on extending the scope of the parking levy. Such an approach would also provide greater flexibility when deciding how to draft any legislation for a levy on customer parking. I therefore request the hon. Gentleman to withdraw the motion.
