The Minister of State, Northern Ireland Office (Mr. Adam Ingram): I welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Benton. I am sure that you will have as interesting a time as your two co-Chairmen. I know that you are familiar with many of the issues that we shall be discussing.

Perhaps for ease of reference I should recap what I was saying before the break. I was about to conclude my remarks.

If Government amendment No. 137 is accepted, it would set the quorum for ordinary meetings of the board at seven. The quorum for the selection of the second or subsequent chairman and vice-chairman of the board would be 12 for a 19-member board. I said that I would take into account amendment No. 153A, which was moved by the hon. Member for Fermanagh and South Tyrone (Mr. Maginnis), and amendment No. 181 in the name of the hon. The Secretary of State has already accepted that the number of members who should support an inquiry should be lower than the figure of 12 in the Bill. I said earlier that I was minded to move to a majority of board members voting for an inquiry, whatever the size of the board—10 for a 19-member board and eight for, say, a 15-member board. In the devolved structure we would expect to have 19-member boards. Any variation of that would apply only if there were direct rule.

I fail to understand why my hon. Friend the Member for Newry and Armagh (Mr. Mallon) does not accept the reasonable proposition that, for an inquiry to be triggered, a majority of board members should want it. It would be, in the words of Patten, an extreme measure, so it is not unreasonable to expect the majority to be convinced of the merits of an inquiry before it was triggered. That is the working premise on which this approach has been developed.

My hon. Friend put strong arguments on the other considerations for being forward looking, imaginative and trusting. If I correctly picked up the tenor of his approach and the way in which he questioned how the new Policing Board would operate, he seemed to lack an element of trust and to base his view on the expectation that the type of society, the new police service and inter-party and inter-community relationships would be the same in the future. Clearly that is not our aim in all that we are hoping to do, and are doing, as we implement the Good Friday agreement. Therefore, I ask my hon. Friend to consider a different type of environment from the one that he was predicating, which was backward looking.

My hon. Friend referred to previous cases and said that if they had taken place at some point in the future, an inquiry would not be held, but that seems to presume that all the worst features of the past 30 years—as he would describe them—would remain in place. He is basing his judgment on that, and I ask him to consider the merit of our proposal. Given that Patten—we are trying to be true to Patten, as my hon. Friend undoubtedly is—referred to the power of inquiry as being an extreme measure, there can be nothing unsound in persuading a majority of the board to hold such an inquiry. Such inquiries would probably be time consuming and demanding and will clearly be expensive. The board would then have to make a firm commitment that it wanted to move into that arena, instead of finding a different approach to the matters that concerned it.

My hon. Friend also said that he would expect a large attendance of members at meetings held to discuss such inquiries. Clearly there would be. If the issue was to be considered by a board, it is likely that a large number of the members—probably all of them—would turn up for the meeting. That strengthens the view that the power of argument should prevail in the board rather than the power of numbers: it should not be a neat percentage calculation based on suspicion and mistrust. If an inquiry is justified, the members of the board who want to pursue it must persuade others of the merit of doing so. That is no different from the process that the newly established Executive is going through of trying to convince people of arguments across a boundary that would hitherto have been difficult to cross. The Policing Board will be introduced into that new environment, and it is not unreasonable to believe that a majority of board members will want to adopt the extreme approach of holding an inquiry.

A strict reading of amendment No. 153A could suggest that, if the board has a quorum of seven, four people could initiate an inquiry. Four out of 19 does not seem a sound approach. As I have explained, it would be likely to be a major inquiry, with a great deal of commitment being given to it by many people. Heavyweight powers would be allocated to it to call people to give evidence and so on.

On that basis, I ask the Committee to accept Government amendments Nos. 137 and 138. I accept in principle amendments Nos. 153A and 181 and I ask the Committee to resist amendments Nos. 100, 182 and 64, although it has been suggested that there is no determination to press them to a vote.
