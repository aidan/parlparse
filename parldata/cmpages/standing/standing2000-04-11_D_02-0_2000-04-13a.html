Mr. Hughes: I welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Sayeed.

This important group of amendments relates to the definition of public authorities. As the long title states, the Bill extends the application of the Race Relations Act 1976 to the police and other public authorities. The first part of the Bill amends that Act by inserting a new section 19B. Each amendment in the group amends part of the new section.

Amendment No. 8 would omit lines 9 to 12, which refer to schedule A1, replacing the list of public authorities with a general proposition. Amendment No. 9 would do the same for lines 15 to 17, which would go because they also refer to schedule A1, as would amendment No. 10 for lines 18 to 20. Amendment No. 11 would delete the lines that provide for the way in which the Secretary of State would amend schedule A. Amendment No. 12 would omit the part that allows the Secretary of State to amend schedule A1 when offices, people and functions change. Therefore, it gives the Secretary of State all sorts of opportunities to vary the list when there are changes in public authorities' activities. Amendment No. 13 would delete the proposed new subsection (7), a sweep-up subsection that allows ancillary changes to be made. The last of the group, amendment No. 14, relates to the schedule itself.

I intended to be brief, so I shall simply put the proposition and then allow him to give the Government's response.

First, as I said, the public authorities are listed. The Government started from that position in part because it gives clarity and allows the public authorities in question to identify themselves. Moreover, a list of public authorities was tied to the statutory duty to promote equality. During the Bill's passage through the other place, and particularly on Third Reading, my colleagues, including Lord Lester, suggested an alternative approach. They proposed that a list of public authorities with a specific duty to promote good race relations be retained, and that the authorities be defined generically, as in amendment No.14. That amendment derives from the Human Rights Act 1998, which this Government brought before Parliament . It would ensure that the Bill's definition of a public authority is the same as that in the 1998 Act.

If we are to create coterminous and comfortably coincident legislation, it is important, particularly for those who have to interpret and work with legislation, that the same definitions are used where possible. When the 1998 Act comes into force in October, there will be significant interrelationship between it and the Race Relations Act 1976. By definition, both draw greatly on principles enshrined in human rights legislation. Given the direction in which the Government moved in section 6(3) of the 1998 Act, it is right to argue that they should take the same approach in the Bill.

Secondly, by definition, one may omit items from a list. One might also want to add items to a list, and the Bill, as drafted, provides for adding to the list of authorities. The list might need to be amended, but that would require that an amending order be sought from Parliament on each occasion. We are all aware that that is a cumbersome procedure, but there is a further difficulty. If a public authority is defined not by a generic definition but as a list definition, those seeking to exercise their rights under the Bill, once it is enacted, would be unable to do so if the relevant public authority had been inadvertently omitted from the list. Retrospective legislation would be regarded as inappropriate, and unreasonable discrimination might occur purely by accident.

The amendments relate to the definition of public authority for the purposes of the Bill. The choice is between a list approach-as used, for example, in the Freedom of Information Bill-and a generic definition approach, as used in the Human Rights Act. As has been mentioned, the issue was debated at some length in another place, at which time the Government believed that their original listing approach was preferable. However, we agreed to take the matter away, consider the arguments raised in another place and decide whether, on reflection, that was correct.

The Government's main reason for favouring the list approach was that it provided clarity as to which bodies had obligations under the Bill and which were to be regarded as public authorities. That was especially important to the duty to promote race equality. However, the arguments were always finely balanced and we listened to the views expressed in another place, which favoured a generic approach. As this House was also likely to favour a generic approach, we took the view that we could move in that direction. It is possible, to some extent, to have the best of both worlds. A generic approach could be used for the purposes of defining public authorities in relation to unlawful discrimination under new section 19B-with some exceptions, to which I shall return-while a listing approach could be used in relation to the duty to promote race equality.

We must be careful when dealing with the duty to promote race equality, because it will place obligations on public authorities. We must be sure that it will apply to particular authorities and that there will be no doubt about whether an organisation should implement mechanisms to enable it to promote race equality. Mr. O'Brien: I beg to move amendment No. 5, page 1, leave out lines 13 and 14.

The Committee will be aware of the Government's intention to table an amendment to make indirect discrimination unlawful under new section 19B of the 1976 Act. That was announced in the House on 27 January. The decision followed discussions in another place, and was a result of the Government listening carefully to the arguments and following further consideration by Departments. The Government have always been in favour, in principle, of extending the indirect discrimination provisions of the Act. However, we must ensure that such an extension would be effective without leaving public bodies open to routine and unnecessary legal challenge in circumstances in which their policies were entirely proper.

We have listened carefully to the arguments and considered further the theoretical risks of challenge. We established that they were, largely, theoretical. We concluded that, although the Government implement economic and social policies based on requirements or conditions that will inevitably have a differential impact on different racial groups-whether because of different age profiles or regional distribution-these were justifiable. If challenged, therefore, we believe that a court would be likely to reach the same conclusion and not find them unlawful. Case law in employment and the provision of goods, facilities and services under the Act has shown the courts to have given public authorities a fair wind when deciding cases of indirect discrimination. That provided some comfort, both on the risks to our policies overall, and to those that benefit ethnic minorities the most.

None of this would avoid the problems of spurious challenges being brought, and we are concerned about that. However, as the Government pointed out during debates on the Bill in another place, the number of claims for indirect discrimination brought under the Act historically has been low. In 1998, for example, less than 1 per cent. of the employment cases were on the grounds of indirect discrimination-that is, setting conditions. Less than 1 per cent. of non-employment claims, the closest comparator to the functions to which we want the Bill to apply, were on the grounds of indirect discrimination. A similar position applied in 1997.

We concluded, therefore, that we could confidently justify our economic and social policies, and, taking into account the approach that has been adopted by the courts as well as the historically low level of claims, it seems right to include indirect discrimination in the Bill. In our view, inclusion outweighed the need to avoid the risk of having to deal with spurious challenges and we are pleased to have been able to reach that decision. The debates on the matter were serious and detailed, and the theoretical risks were examined with considerable care. We listened to the arguments put forward in another place, following which we were pleased to move to this decision.

I shall deal with some of the issues that might concern hon. Members. It might be useful if I set out briefly the difference between direct and indirect discrimination. Direct discrimination occurs when one person treats another less favourably than he treats, or would treat, other people. Indirect discrimination occurs when there is a requirement or a condition that, though applied equally to people of different racial groups, has the effect that the proportion of one racial group that can comply is considerably smaller than that of another racial group, or when such a requirement or condition is only honourable if it cannot be justified irrespective of racial group and if it is detrimental to the claimant who cannot comply.

I shall give some examples of direct and indirect discrimination. The question of whether the police would be affected by these provisions has raised some concern. We have examined that issue with a great deal of care and concluded that, for example, in relation to stop and search, indirect discrimination would not be an issue. Stop and search will not be affected by the inclusion of indirect discrimination in the Bill because it does not impose any condition or requirement that cannot be complied with. Any discrimination arising from the use of stop and search would be dealt with in relation either to direct, rather than indirect, discrimination, or by way of victimisation, which would be unlawful under the Bill in any case.

If a police officer stopped a black man in circumstances under which he would not have stopped a white man, he would be treating the black man less favourably than another person of a different racial group. That is direct discrimination. If a Department awarded a grant to a black person in different circumstances to those under which it awarded a grant to a white person, the Government would have treated the black person less favourably than the white person. Again, the issue would be direct, not indirect, discrimination.

We have concluded that the Bill will operate in such a way that indirect discrimination will not cause the Government problems. We canvassed various Departments on whether they considered that any of their policies would be inhibited so as to prevent beneficial effects from reaching ethnic minorities. Having carefully examined their policies, they concluded that ethnic minorities would not be disadvantaged by the inclusion of indirect discrimination, which had been one of our worries. We also believe that no proper function of the Government will be disadvantaged by the inclusion of indirect discrimination.

We were therefore satisfied that we could include indirect discrimination in the Bill, and are pleased to have been able to do so. I hope that members of the Committee will support the amendment.

    To outlaw indirect discrimination-

in all the functions to be newly covered by the Bill-

    would have uncertain and potentially far-reaching effects on the Government's ability to make policy. Any policy or practice that has a different impact on different racial groups because of a requirement or condition would be open to challenge in the courts.-[Official Report, House of Lords, 14 December 1999; Vol. 608, c. 178-82.]

That could include any age-based policy, because of the different demographic profiles of different racial groups, and any regional policy, because of the different regional spread of different racial groups.

The most succinct definition of indirect discrimination that I could find when I trawled through the literature was probably the one given by Mr. Justice Waite in the case of Raval v. the Department of Health and Social Security, in which he said that indirect discrimination was

    a form of words which conveniently abbreviates (though it does not wholly describe) a complex statutory formula, widely enough drawn to apply to almost any situation involving a distinction of any kind between races but rescued from application in artificial or absurd instances by affording on the one side an opportunity of justification and by insisting on the other side upon proof of a consequential detriment suffered by the complainant.

The aspects of indirect discrimination that I want to explore and on which I wish to press the Minister to make further comments are as follows. How demanding is the test required of the complainant to prove both the fact of discrimination, if it is indirect, and the harm that it causes to him or her? Secondly, how robust is the defence available to a public body that, if discrimination exists and can be demonstrated on the facts, it is justifiable under the 1976 Act and the defence provided there in section 1(1)(b)(iii)? We are not talking about something as self-evidently repugnant as an employer sticking up a notice saying, ``No blacks need bother to seek a job here''. We are talking about actions found by the courts to be discriminatory, even though the organisation may not have intended to discriminate and even though the arrangements later found to be indirectly discriminatory were established for what seemed to most people at the time to be sensible and practical reasons.

Thirdly, how confident are the Government that there will not be a surge of vexatious litigation once the amendment becomes law? The Minister will understand as well as anyone else in the Committee that on many occasions a public sector body that is not flush with money, such as a school governing body, may find it safer and simpler to settle out of court and perhaps to make a compensatory payment to a complainant, even though it believes that it is unjust to have to do so, simply because it wishes to avoid the risk, the expense and the debilitating drain on the time of a limited number of professional people to pursue a defence through the courts and perhaps through the whole appellate process.

The definition of indirect discrimination in the 1976 Act requires that four elements must be present to establish the fact of indirect discrimination. First, the requirement or condition applied to the complainant must have been applied equally to all persons, regardless of race. Secondly, the proportion of persons in the complainant's racial group who can comply with the condition or requirement must be ``considerably smaller''-I stress the word ``considerably''-

    than the proportion of persons not of that racial group who can comply with it.

Thirdly, the condition or requirement must be unjustifiable. Fourthly, the condition or requirement must have operated to the detriment of an individual.

It is important to note that direct discrimination, as defined by the courts, can happen unintentionally. The courts have also concluded that a complainant's inability to comply with the condition or requirement need not be due to his or her race. Discrimination can be established in law if it can be shown that fewer members of a racial or ethnic group would be able to comply with a requirement, at a particular time, even if the reasons for their inability to comply are not necessarily connected with the fact that they are members of that racial group.

The best example of the application of that principle that I can give relates to the Sex Discrimination Act 1975. That Act has a read-across to race relations legislation, which is framed in almost exactly the same terms. In the case of Greater Manchester Police Authority v. Lea in 1990, Mr. Lea claimed that he had been indirectly discriminated against because of his sex. He had applied for a job as an operations room assistant with the police, but was not appointed, because he was in receipt of an occupational pension, having accepted medical retirement after an accident. The policy of the police authority, designed to take into account the needs of unemployed people, was that those who received occupational pensions or had accepted voluntary redundancy or early retirement should not, as a general rule, be considered for appointment.

At the time of the case, 4.1 per cent. more men than women in the economincally active population were in receipt of occupational pensions. Therefore, the evidence presented to the court was that, whereas 99.4 per cent. of women could comply with the condition set by the authority, only 95.3 per cent. of men could do so. The court found that discrimination had taken place.

I draw two points from that case. First, the fact of discrimination was established, even though the rules that were relevant to the case were based not on the sex of the employee but on whether or not the applicant had taken early retirement or was receiving an occupational pension. Secondly, Mr. Lea's inability to comply with the condition laid down by the police authority was not due to the fact that he was a man, but to the fact that he was in receipt of an occupational pension and had retired. Nevertheless, the court concluded that indirect discrimination on the grounds of sex had, in fact, taken place.

The final key point about that case is that it showed that the court was prepared to interpret a difference of 4.1 per cent. as considerable, within the terms of the Sex Discrimination Act. Reading the lessons of that case across to race relations shows that there are causes for concern as to how the concept of indirect indiscrimination on grounds of race may be interpreted by the courts. That and other cases have established that the court will define the group with which to compare the proportion of the complainant's group who are able to comply with a condition, before it decides whether the latter is considerably smaller. It is for the court to decide what is a considerably smaller proportion. The court in the Lea case decided that a difference of 4.1 per cent. was large enough to meet that test.

Once a condition has been found to be discriminatory on the facts of the case, it is for the respondent-the public body, under the Bill-to prove to the tribunal court that a policy condition or requirement is justifiable in law. The court must then objectively weigh up the balance between the discriminatory impact of the condition and the reasonable needs of the public body that is applying it.

There are many examples of courts wrestling with such matters. On several occasions, judges have warned of the risk that the burden of providing detailed statistical evidence, or that of spurious or vexatious litigation to seek to prove discrimination, may become intolerable for those who are being sued. In the case of Perera v. Civil Service Commission in 1982, Mr. Justice Browne-Wilkinson said:

    On the one hand, the burden is on the complainant to prove his case and, viewed in isolation, the statitistics produced do not prove it. On the other hand, it is most undesirable that, in all cases of indirect discrimination, elaborate statistical evidence should be required before the case can be found proved. The time and expense involved in preparing and proving statistical evidence can be enormous, as experience in the United States has demonstrated. It is not good policy to require such evidence.

In the case of Jones v. Chief Adjudication Officer, Lord Justice Mustill said:

    The suggestion that it is enough to prove differential impact to throw the burden of proving justification on to the legislator, the employer or whatever by expensive and time-consuming litigation, seems to me to be wholly inimical to the interests of society and the cause of equal treatment in particular.

I therefore question whether the defence provided in section 1(1)(b)(iii) of the 1976 Act will be adequate to allow public bodies confidently to defend practices that not only they but most reasonable people who support anti-discrimination legislation, consider to be common-sense, practical rules for the services that they provide.

I want to cite a few examples to illustrate my fear that the amendment could encourage spurious litigation, or even lead to sensible public policies being overturned in the courts. I hope that the Minister, when he responds to the debate, or having reflected on the matter, will be able to demonstrate to me that my fears are groundless. I would find it encouraging and helpful to be persuaded that the points that have been made to me by various people outside the House and aired in some quarters of the media in recent months are based on worries rather than a proper understanding of what the Bill will bring into force.
