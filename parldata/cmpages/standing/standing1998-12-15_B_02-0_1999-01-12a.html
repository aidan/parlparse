Dr. Harris: I am delighted to reciprocate your best wishes for the new year, Mr. Gale, and to say how pleased I am to be serving on this Committee under your chairmanship for the first time.

Amendment No. 9 draws attention to a potentially distorting anomaly in the phrasing of the Bill. I invite the Minister to explain how he believes safeguards can be included in the Bill to ensure that the anomaly can be avoided because it would have significant effects, especially on the practice of discharge from acute hospitals.

With the current phrasing, the charging of insurance companies for third parties injured in traumatic accidents applies only to national health service care delivered in an NHS hospital setting at a certain amount per day, which is fixed by regulation. I have two concerns about that, one of which is directly addressed and the other indirectly addressed by the amendment.

First, if the fixed charge per day is not very close to the amount that it actually costs the hospital to look after such people, there could be an incentive to retain people in hospital or discharge them early for financial reasons that do not apply when charging arrangements are not made. In other words, if the cost per day, suggested to be about £600, is higher than the actual cost to the hospital, there will be a financial incentive not to push people out of a hospital bed because to retain people will become a net income generator.

Obviously, there will be other pressures on beds, and one hopes that those will be paramount. Indeed, at the moment, we can see that they are. However, there is a danger that there will be less incentive to remove people, where appropriate, from hospital and into NHS care at home. If the amount is too little, hospitals may be seen to be making a financial loss, and there will be a perverse financial incentive to try to move people out of hospital to free up beds. However, I do not wish to address that problem now.

Where NHS care is provided in a home package, which may cost the health service as much, more or less than care in hospital, there is a danger that people will not be discharged to such a package if the income generating situation that I described applies. If the cost that is claimable through hospital care is seen as a net income generator, discharge to home under NHS care will be regarded as an expense that may not be provided for in the contracts. I will return to my concerns about the perversion and distortion of the contracting arrangements when we debate a later amendment.

It would be logical and, given the overall dangers of the bureaucracy inherent in the Bill, not unreasonable for the charges to apply to NHS care at home. I wish to stress, as we have previously, that we do not like the principle of this sort of charging but if it has to be done, there is a strong argument that it should be done in a way that does not distort decisions about discharge. Will the Minister reassure me and my hon. Friends that that has been considered by his Department and that mechanisms will be in place to ensure that those factors do not come into play? If, sadly, they do, will there be a way of monitoring what is taking place and will his Department be able to act?

The Parliamentary Under-Secretary of State for Health (Mr. John Hutton): I also welcome you to the Committee, Mr. Gale. I, like you, hope that we can deal with the rest of the Bill in today's sitting.

I am sure that the hon. Member for Oxford, West and Abingdon and the hon. Member for Rutland and Melton (Mr. Duncan) are trying to be helpful in raising these issues, but let me reassure them that we do not believe that the Bill will have the perverse incentive effect that they imagine. It is unfair and improper to think that clinicians will allow the Bill to interfere with their clinical judgment on how to treat a patient.

There are three specific reasons why the amendment is unnecessary and why the Committee should reject it if it is pressed to a vote. First, hospitals would no longer be required to ask whether a patient intends to claim compensation as a result of a road traffic accident: hospitals would not routinely know which patients might be affected by the Bill's provisions. The hon. Member for Oxford, West and Abingdon overlooked that important point. Secondly, even if hospitals had that information, they would have to rely completely on the patient to provide it in possibly traumatic circumstances of admittance to an accident and emergency department. It may not be appropriate to rely on such information.

The third reason which—given what the hon. Member for Rutland and Melton said about the role of money—is possibly the most important, is that the money recouped under the Bill's provisions is unlikely to be received by an NHS trust for at least two years after the accident. That is the normal length of time for an accident claim to be settled. Therefore, it would be unusual for a hospital trust, on the possibility of financial recovery two years in the future, to interfere with the clinical judgments that nurses and doctors must make immediately after a road traffic accident.

For those three reasons, I do not believe that anything in the Bill would give rise to any perverse incentive to treat a patient inappropriately, which was the main concern of the hon. Member for Oxford, West and Abingdon. We do not believe that clinicians will treat road traffic accident victims inappropriately after their trauma. The Bill is simple and the least bureaucratic-option available in collecting important resources for the NHS. The amendment would add to the complexity of the system, provide no tangible benefit to the NHS and risk reconnecting front-line NHS staff to income collection. We do not think that that is right and do not want it to happen. For those reasons, I hope that he is reassured about what is in the Bill and not press his amendment to a vote.

New Clause 5

Review of operation of Act

    `The Secretary of State shall carry out a review of the operation of this Act before the expiry of five years from its commencement and shall report the findings of that review to both Houses of Parliament.'.—[Dr. Harris.]

Brought up, and read the First time.

Dr. Harris: I beg to move, That the clause be read a Second time.

The Chairman: With this it will be convenient to discuss the following: New clause 6—Expiry of Act—

    `This Act shall cease to have effect on 31st December 2004, unless before that date both Houses of Parliament have resolved that it should continue in force.'.

Dr. Harris: I rise to discuss the penultimate amendment to the Bill—new clause 5— which I hope is welcome to the Minister. New clause 6 would have a similar effect to new clause 5, although it has a slightly different emphasis.

We raise this matter again because the Bill's confirmation of existing rights under old legislation marks a significant departure for the NHS, which is traditionally financed through taxation, is free at the point of delivery, and involves neither general charges, especially for in-patient treatment, nor indirect charges, such as insurance premiums for motor vehicle accidents.

Mr. Hutton: Does not the hon. Gentleman recognise that under the Bill's provisions NHS treatment will continue to be free at the point of use?

Dr. Harris: Yes, I accept that under the Bill's provisions NHS treatment will be free at the point of use but, for the treatment under discussion, it will not be both free at the point of use and funded by general taxation. Most people identify both those approaches with the NHS, especially for in-patient treatment. However, I accept that such charges can already be introduced under current legislation.

We debated this matter fully on Second Reading, so I shall not labour my point, but the Government had an opportunity to remove all such charges. If they had done so, we should now be debating a Bill to remove all those charges and to restore perhaps all treatments—certainly in-patient and out-patient treatment—to the ambit of those treatments that are funded through general taxation. The Government decided to follow another path, which is their right, just as it is ours to oppose them as strongly as we can.

The new clause asks the Government—not this Government but a future one—to pause after five years, which is a reasonable length of time, and to ask, ``In the light of experience, is this the direction in which we want the services to be supplied under the NHS?'' That will involve current procedures and others that may occur to the Minister and to other Ministers who want to develop alternatives to taxation as ways of funding the NHS. On many occasions we have said that such discussions about the future funding of the NHS should go beyond the narrow political arena; they should be heard in a standing conference attended by users, political parties, professionals and taxpayers. We could then have a proper discussion about the right way to fund the NHS.

The new clause would allow the Government to accept our broader proposal, although we shall have to discuss that elsewhere. They should accept the need for a broad review of the way to proceed as there are rising demands on the NHS and the Government, and recent previous Governments, appear to have a limited desire to raise NHS funding in the traditional manner through general taxation. The new clause will allow the Government to say, ``Let's have a review and assess the Act's operation.'' I hope that we shall hear the population's views in the standing conference that I described, and that the Government will not go down their proposed path. Once the Bill is enacted, new clause 6 would allow the Government to let the legislation lapse by omission.

I hope that the Minister will respond broadly, although I predict that he will not look favourably on either of the new clauses.

Mr. Hutton: I am afraid that I am not going to disappoint the hon. Gentleman on that score.

The hon. Gentleman widened the scope of his concerns when he moved the new clause, although we also heard the familiar recitation of his fundamental objection to the Bill's principles. That is fine, but we have had that argument, and he lost it. He repeated his allegation that the Government are not properly funding the NHS. I flatly refute that. The NHS is being properly funded—indeed, it is receiving record funding, thanks to the Government's commitments. It is disingenuous of him to suggest otherwise. I must point out one fact to him: in the NHS, 30 new hospitals are under construction or have been approved. That is testimony to the Government's commitment to modernise and renew the NHS. I do not wish to undermine the spirit of harmony that has prevailed, but we will not listen to lectures from Liberal Democrats on NHS funding.

We have already made it clear in the regulatory appraisal that accompanied the Bill, which I am sure the hon. Member for Oxford, West and Abingdon has read thoroughly, that the scheme will be reviewed within six months, not five years. The result of that review will be published when they are available.

By incorporating an obligation to review the Act after five years, new clause 5 implies that the Government are doing something unprincipled, outrageous or unprecedented in the history of United Kingdom legislation. In fact, we are marrying an old principle—the entitlement of hospitals to recover treatment costs in such cases—with the established system of benefit recovery. That is hardly a leap in the dark.
