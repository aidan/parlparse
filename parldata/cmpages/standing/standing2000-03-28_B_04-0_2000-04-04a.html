My experience is of the Scottish highlands, which are not relevant to the Bill, but I believe that it is also the case that, in parts of England and Wales, there are many remote cemeteries and graveyards. It is a matter of common decency and respect for the dead that they should not be tramped over unnecessarily. Therefore, we believe that admission to those areas should be a matter for their keepers, whether that is a current church body or heritage organisation.

Amendment No. 38 refers to land used for a sports pitch or athletics track. The Bill excepts golf courses and race courses, but I am puzzled about why it does not also include other sports pitches or athletics tracks. I suspect that there are few athletics tracks on open country, but that is not true of sports pitches. In the constituency of the hon. Member for Meirionmydd Nant Conwy, village football pitches are often situated at the skirt of a mountain.

 

Mr. Llwyd: The rugby club in Dolgellau, of which I am vice-president, has had a devil of a job preventing people from taking dogs on to the pitch and fouling it. We have no legal way of preventing that, so the hon. Gentleman has hit on a vital point and I should be grateful if the Minister would respond to it.

Mr. Paice: In addition to that important point there is the issue of commons. Cricket pitches are often situated on common land, and they are also used for other games. I hope that the Minister will explain why the Government did not think it necessary to include those areas.

Amendments Nos. 40 and 41 both refer to shooting; the first to land used as a shooting range and the second to land used for clay pigeon shooting. It is probably self-evident why we wish to exclude such areas, primarily for safety purposes. There are other issues about disruption and other problems that could result from people walking on to such areas, but the issue is primarily one of public safety, and public access could compromise that safety, particularly in the case of a shooting range. Ranges can cover considerable distances and it may not be immediately obvious to casual walkers that they are crossing between the shooters and their target. Some shooting ranges can be several hundred metres long. Also, there is a problem if there is no proper barrier behind the target such as a sandbank. The gunfire disturbing dogs is another problem, although a much lesser one. The Under-Secretary may suggest that the issues raised by these two amendments are covered by the 28-day rule, but I suggest to him that many of the activities will take place on more than 28 days a year. An example is clay pigeon shooting, which can be held regularly at purpose-built sites where people have invested in constructing towers and other launch sites for the clay pigeons. These events can be held almost every weekend, so the 28-day rule does not cover them.

Amendment No. 43 refers to land use for motor car and motorcycle racing, including speed trials, and other motor sports and practising for those activities. Golf courses and race courses are excepted land, perfectly rightly in our view, although they are an odd choice, partly because of safety. I know that the hon. Member for Pendle is proposing that golf courses should not be included, but that is a matter for him. We agree with the Government that golf courses and race courses should be excepted, but it seems odd that we do not exclude land used for the various motor sports for the same reason of safety. Security is another reason to except land because obstructions may be left behind, such as safety barriers. The question of liability if someone is hurt is another issue, which will be debated later in our proceedings. The Under-Secretary may have been informed that the definition that we have used for motor sports is based on that in the Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) Order 1995.

Amendment Nos. 44 and 69 may have raised a few eyebrows and I can understand that, but this is again an issue of clarity and ensuring that we know what is and is not included. There is an increasing interest—though I personally do not share it—in war games. Given the desperate state of agriculture, many landowners facilitate the activity, if they have the appropriate terrain, in an attempt to generate extra income. A number of management development schools and companies use it as a development technique. This is not an issue that should be dismissed in a derisory way and I am sure the Under-Secretary did not intend to do that. The amendment seeks to include land used for the purpose under the definition of ``excepted''. War games have become a popular activity and we believe that land used for that purpose should be excepted. The definition comes from the same Town and Country Planning (General Permitted Development) Order 1995.

Amendment No. 46 refers to land used as a film or television studio. We are seeing the increased use of land for this purpose. I am not referring to the one-off situation in which a film company uses an area for one scene. We are talking about large open areas that are used regularly. I understand that a new film studio is being opened at Leavesden airfield in Hertfordshire. That is an example of a film studio being built on land that might otherwise be considered open country. Obviously, all manner of disturbances and consequences can be envisaged.
