Amendment No. 228 seeks to explore the reasons for our proposed use of an asset over the past 10 years of ownership to determine how much of the gain on the asset is to qualify for the business asset taper relief and how much for the non-business asset taper relief. Under the provisions as drafted, the relevant period of ownership is defined as the period after 5 April 1998 for which the asset has been held at the time of its disposal, or a period of 10 years ending with the disposal, whichever is the shorter. It is the use during the relevant period of ownership which determines whether or how much of the gain is a gain on a business asset and therefore qualifies for the more generous business asset taper relief.

Our sole motive in limiting the relevant period of ownership to the last 10 years is that it will prove a helpful compliance saving to those having to prepare their capital gains tax computation. On disposal of an asset which has been used partly as a business asset and partly as a non-business asset, the seller will have to look back over only the last 10 years and determine the use during that period. The extent to which the seller will have to delve into the past is limited and that will prove helpful in terms of compliance benefit.

Amendment No. 228 would require the seller to look back over the whole period of ownership and determine the proportion of business use throughout the whole period. The approach proposed in the Bill can, depending on the circumstances, produce results which are favourable or less favourable to the taxpayer compared with taking the whole period of ownership.

The usual situation will be that the asset is used wholly as a business asset or wholly as a non-business[Dawn Primarolo] asset and our judgment is that apportioning the gain into business and non-business parts should be simple and straightforward, even though in some circumstances it might produce some rough edges. Dawn Primarolo: The amendments explore the interaction of losses brought forward and the operation of the taper. As the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton acknowledged, the subject is complex.

Under existing legislation, losses brought forward to a year must be used only to the extent necessary to reduce the net gain to the annual exempt amount. The taper adds a new dimension. For gains and losses that arise in a particular year, the losses are set against the gains before the taper operates on the net gain.

Neither amendment would disturb that treatment of losses of the current year. However, where losses arising in an earlier year cannot be used in that year, and are brought forward to the current year, the treatment proposed is that the losses brought forward should be set against the net gains of the year again, before the operation of the taper relief. The hon. Members for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton and for Cotswold (Mr. Clifton-Brown) both raised queries about that. The arrangement is consistent with respect to the treatment of losses that have arisen in a current year and can be offset against gains of that year, and losses that arose in an earlier year and are brought forward to the current year. That seems right to the Government.

It would be a strange result if losses brought forward to a year were treated differently from those arising in that year, with respect to their interaction with the taper relief.
