Sites of special scientific interest

The Minister for the Environment (Mr. Michael Meacher): I beg to move amendment No. 672, in page 34, line 20, at end insert-

    `(2) In section 15(2) of the Countryside Act 1968 (which provides for agreements between the Nature Conservancy Council and those with interests in land which is included in an area of special scientific interest, or is adjacent to such land), for ``adjacent'' there is substituted ``other''.'.

The hon. Member for Ashford (Mr. Green) is looking startled, perplexed and dismayed. I am unsure whether that is because we are starting part III of the Bill, but that is where we are. Part III covers two main issues: the notification and protection of sites of special scientific interest and provisions for strengthening species enforcement. Both provisions build on the firm foundations established by the Wildlife and Countryside Act 1981. We shall first consider provisions relating to sites of special scientific interest-SSSIs to those in the trade-which are areas of special interest by reason of their flora, fauna, geological or physiographical features. To put it more simply, they are the best examples of our natural heritage.

SSSIs represent our finest wildlife sites of national conservation importance and we are committed to securing improvements in their identification, protection and management. That does not mean that we wish to undermine the constructive approach taken by many land managers. We recognise that the most serious threats to SSSIs come not from deliberate damage but from neglect and management that fails to protect them. More than a quarter of sites show features that are in poor condition. That is far too many.

We are significantly improving the protection and management of SSSIs through the first new wildlife legislation for nearly 20 years. Measures in part III of the Bill will give conservation agencies new powers to refuse consent for damaging activities and to serve management notices to combat neglect. The Bill will increase penalties for deliberate damage, introduce a new court power to order restoration and improve powers to act against cases of third party damage. Public bodies own some 40 per cent. of SSSIs, and their activities can significantly affect those sites. We are introducing a statutory duty on public bodies to further the conservation and enhancement of SSSIs. Those substantive measures are all included in schedule 9, which we shall discuss shortly.

I shall speak briefly to amendment No. 672. Section 15 of the Countryside Act 1968 contains important powers for the conservation agencies to enter into management agreements with owners and occupiers of land that is notified as being of special scientific interest. Such agreements are an essential component in the partnership approach, which involves land managers working together with the conservation agencies to ensure that the special features on their land are in good condition. They enable the agencies to support and encourage positive management with appropriate financial consideration.

The agencies are able to enter into agreements with owners and occupiers of notified land or land that is adjacent to a notified site. Sometimes, a site will be affected by activities that are occurring some distance away. In such circumstances, it might be useful to reach agreements with the land managers to ensure that special features on the SSSI are appropriately conserved. One example is the protection of an aquifer that is situated some distance away from-but not adjacent to-the SSSI. The amendment allows the agencies to act in such circumstances and improves the provision as drafted.

Mr. David Heath (Somerton and Frome): I warmly welcome the Government amendment, which covers much of the ground contained in my proposed new clause 7, which we will debate later. The inability of the countryside bodies to enter into management agreements to secure the continuing prosperity of an SSSI was a clear deficiency in the Bill. The amendment goes a long way towards dealing with that deficiency and I am grateful to the Government for having tabled it.

Mrs. Brinton: The overall purpose of all four amendments is to strengthen the responsibilities of statutory undertakers and public bodies in relation to conservation of sites of special scientific interest.

Amendment No. 423 would increase the scope of the duty of statutory undertakers to further the conservation and enhancement of SSSIs. It would ensure that the duty extends to include the earlier activity of formulating plans. For example, Rainham Marsh SSSI is the largest area of grazing marsh in the inner Thames and is home to many wintering waterfowl, water vole and rare insects, such as the scarce emerald damselfly. The unitary development plan for the London borough of Havering has identified Rainham marshes as an area for major development, which would destroy much of the SSSI. The amendment would ensure that proper regard was had to the SSSI in the plan.

Amendment No. 422 would ensure that the duty on statutory undertakers to further the conservation of SSSIs would apply not only to their activities on sites but to their activities outside SSSIs, where a site would be affected. The existing activities of statutory undertakers away from SSSIs-those that would not be captured by section 28F-can have a major impact on SSSIs. For example, the Lower Derwent Valley SSSI is one of the most important species-rich wildflower flood meadows left in Europe, but water abstraction elsewhere in the catchment area now leads to very low water levels in the SSSI, especially in dry periods. A wider duty to further the conservation of SSSIs would ensure that water abstraction was managed to the benefit of the SSSI.

Amendment No. 424 would require statutory undertakers to give weight to the conservation of SSSIs when there is a conflict between their new duty to further the conservation of SSSIs and other primary functions, in a similar way to that required of national park authorities. For example, the Crown Estate is under a statutory duty to achieve best financial return from the estate. It currently disposes of unlet shooting rights on the foreshore. Wildfowling is not necessarily incompatible with the conservation of SSSIs, as long as sufficient wildfowl refuges are available within the estuary. However, many estuaries have insufficient wildfowl refuges. For example, only 5 per cent. of the Wash is a wildfowl refuge. The amendment would ensure that the Crown Estate has due regard to SSSI interest when managing the estate.

Amendment No. 425 would allow statutory undertakers to exercise their existing powers, such as their power to make byelaws, for the purposes of furthering the conservation of SSSIs. In most cases, existing powers can be exercised only for very specific purposes. For example, the Sea Fisheries (Wildlife Conservation) Act 1992 gave sea fisheries committees a duty to have regard to marine conservation, but their powers could be exercised only for the benefit of fisheries and not for that of marine conservation. It was not until the Environmental Protection Act 1995 took effect-it was enacted under the previous Government-that a new purpose was added to their powers, which enabled them to make byelaws to conserve the marine environment.

The amendments would increase the responsibilities of statutory undertakers and public bodies. That is nothing less than private landowners and the public would expect, so I hope that Ministers will favour the amendments.
