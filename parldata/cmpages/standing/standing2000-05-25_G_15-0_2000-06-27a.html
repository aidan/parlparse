The amendment inserts two new subsections into proposed new section 79E, which deals with applications for registration of child care providers. The first subsection deals with people who, were they able to withdraw an application unilaterally, might try to disguise the fact that they were considered unsuitable to be registered providers. The provision is linked to paragraph 4 of new schedule 9A to the Children Act 1989, which is being inserted by schedule 2 of the Bill. Paragraph 4 of the new schedule provides the power to make regulations automatically to disqualify certain people from being registered as child care providers.

Included in the list of people whom the regulations may cover are those who have never been refused registration under part X or, in future, part XA of the Children Act. It will be important for the registration authority's records to show the true reason why an application was not granted should the person ever reapply for registration. When the refusal is for a reason that will have no bearing on a future application, the registration authority may agree to the applicant withdrawing the application.

I should make it clear that refusal of an application will not necessarily mean that the person concerned is automatically barred for all time from being a registered provider. The precise reason for the refusal will be the key factor in determining any future application. That will need to be shown clearly on the registration authority's records. For example, an applicant who is refused registration because her premises are unsuitable for child care, could become registered if a further application was made in respect of other or improved premises.

The effect of the second subsection of the amendment is clear, as are our reasons for proposing it. Child care is an important and sensitive issue and deliberate attempts to undermine the protection that a regulatory system provides for children should not go unpunished. Therefore, in line with other provisions in this part of the Bill, the offence would attract a fine of up to £5,000.
