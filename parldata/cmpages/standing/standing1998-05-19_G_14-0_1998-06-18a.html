Mr. Lansley: I am grateful for the opportunity to speak to this long list of amendments. I shall take the Committee through each of them and explain what they would achieve. As we discuss them, it will become obvious that the amendments would be mutually exclusive in that if amendment No. 183 were accepted, it would not follow that I could move on logically to the other amendments. Clause 54 introduces concurrent powers of sector regulators. Amendment No. 183 is the radical amendment; it is designed to remove those powers and to introduce a debate on the appropriateness or otherwise of providing concurrent powers to regulators. Amendments Nos. 210 and 211 should be considered together. They require the Director of Fair Trading and the sector regulators to be obliged to work together in pursuing the application of the Bill's powers to the sector for which any given regulator is responsible.

Amendment No. 211 has two parts, the first of which sets out that a regulator, in exercising concurrent powers provided under the clause, would, under proposed subsection (4), have an obligation to work not only with the Director of Fair Trading, but with

    "any other regulator who may have jurisdiction in relation to that matter" .

The director general, together with the regulator with primary jurisdiction and any other regulator with jurisdiction, will have an obligation under the Bill to determine the manner in which they are to exercise their functions.

The second limb of amendment No. 211 concerns circumstances in which a regulator leaving aside the concurrent jurisdiction of the director general, but considering the sector regulators themselves does not have exclusive jurisdiction in relation to a certain matter, or when that jurisdiction exercised by the regulator would affect competition outside the scope of the jurisdiction of that regulator. The regulator would then have to bring together the director general and any other regulator who was affected to any extent to make joint decisions decisions for convenience being defined in this context by clause 47, which, helpfully, we discussed earlier. Of course, that would include the two main prohibitions, enforcement penalties and so on. The purpose of that amendment is to require joint working between the two concurrent jurisdictions.

There is a direct relationship between amendment No. 211 and amendments Nos. 212 to 216, which, it will be observed, have essentially the same form, but apply to schedule 10, which includes the detailed application of the concurrent jurisdiction for sector regulators. For each of the sector regulators governed by separate sectoral statutes, the amendments provide for the regulator to exercise functions in consultation with the director general.

Before I move on to a further set of amendments, it may be helpful to explain why amendments Nos. 212 to 216 are desirable. At present, the sector statutes governing the several regulators for whom concurrent jurisdiction is proposed impose an artificial constraint on joint working by the regulators with the director general. He and the regulators may be required to consult informally as was made clear in the other place. Lord Simon of Highbury, the Government spokesman, talked about that and I shall refer to it in a moment.

What is unclear in the Bill is the extent to which the two parties are able to determine the manner in which they are to exercise their functions without constraint. Under the current sector statutes, there appears to be a number of obstacles to that. The obstacle to their working as a team, rather than one or other of them exercising a primary jurisdiction, is what is known as the first-past-the-post system. In effect, the first regulator to act takes over the jurisdiction and cannot then share it with, or pass it to, another regulator, or to the director general. They are specifically unable to make decisions together.

Even given the fact that I am new to Committee proceedings, it is still a novelty to be able to refer specifically to proceedings in another place. As we are now able to do so, it may be helpful to note that on three occasions Lord Simon mentioned the desirability of introducing an additional amendment to allow regulators exercising concurrent jurisdiction to work together with the director general.

I hope that the Committee will forgive me for the length of my explanation, but it is a large group of amendments, all of which have different perspectives. It begs the question of the two principal issues raised by the clause, and how the amendments would bite upon them. Is it appropriate for such powers to be given to sector regulators? If regulators are given such powers, how are they to be applied consistently and fairly?

The qualifications and concerns that the Opposition express do not arise from hostility to the work of the utility regulators. That would be perverse, as the statutes under which the utility regulators work derive from legislation made under a Conservative Administration, and many of those regulators have succeeded in doing precisely what the Conservative Administration wanted. I was at the Department of Trade and Industry in an official capacity rather than a political one when the Telecommunications Act 1984 came into force. The telecommunications industry, as much as any other, shows how the original intentions of securing competition and consumer benefits in the marketplace were derived from the 1984 legislation.

The telecommunications industry is also an example of why it may not be appropriate for regulators to have such powers. The scope of the responsibilities of regulators such as I described goes much wider than simply the promotion of competition. In the early years, competition certainly was at the heart of delivering consumer benefits in telecommunications. However, a point will come it is fast approaching when the regulator does not act, through regulation, as the proxy for competition in diminishing the market power and market share of the dominant player in the market, but that is supplied through the achievement of competition.

To an extent that we have not yet seen from the Government, several regulators are beginning to look beyond the promotion of competition to a wider range of public interests that they are trying to secure through regulatory activity. For example, the water regulator considers the environment, supply, viability of industries and investment programmes and compliance with legislation. Similarly, the gas regulator has interesting, but sometimes conflicting, regulatory objectives.

We might reasonably deduce that those objectives are in a sense complementary, as they all provide benefit to the public and are construed as part of the public interest. In some circumstances for example, when setting prices for dominant suppliers in the marketplace, such as regulated utilities we may even deduce that the consumer benefit is a direct result of the regulator's activity. Nevertheless, there is an inherent tension among regulators, which often compete not so much within their own sector, but against other regulators to try to deliver the maximum consumer benefit as fast as they can. They may not necessarily look to the development of competition in the long term. They may sometimes look to the delivery of specific benefits in the short term to justify their position as regulators under the statutes that originally established them, and may compete one against another. We therefore need to be careful and consider how appropriate it is for regulators to use those two sets of powers. The heart of amendment No. 184 is that the more that regulators move towards achieving success and establishing a competitive marketplace for their industry, the more important it is that we move the industry towards a conventional Competition Act regime.

Even before the Government's present Bill was presented, it was clear that one of the objectives for the telecommunications industry was to move its pricing and market from a separately regulated structure into the same structure as any other industry. It should be a policy objective not to have concurrent jurisdictions and separate regulation for the utilities, but to have a competitive marketplace for utilities in which they are subject to the Competition Act regime administered by the Director General of Fair Trading, in the same way as motor vehicle, engineering and other industries are. The first question is, is it, in that sense, appropriate and desirable? In so far as it is provided, is it desirable in achieving the Bill's objectives?

It would be reasonable to assume that if the director general were not required to have concurrent jurisdiction with the sector regulators, he would be perfectly able to work alongside the sector regulators if we were to provide for that in the Bill and maintain responsibility for the consistent implementation of the Bill's provisions. Mr. Nigel Griffiths: I hope now to set out why I believe that the principle of concurrency is right. I want to ensure that the concerns that have rightly been raised by Opposition Members including double jeopardy, potential inconsistency of application and the need for proper co-ordination between the director general and the regulators have been thought about and catered for. The principle of concurrent functions is right because it will allow sector regulators to co-ordinate their use of sector-specific regulation with the exercise of general competition law functions. it will allow their sector expertise to be brought to the application of the prohibitions.

The importance of concurrent functions for the future of sector regulation was set out in the Government Green Paper, "A Fair Deal for Consumers: Modernising the Framework for Utility Regulation" and the principle of concurrent functions was established by the previous Administration in utility statutes. I will speak about that shortly. Their intention to extend concurrent functions to their proposed Competition Bill was spelt out in the explanatory document to the draft Bill that was published in August 1996. Amendment No. 183, which seeks to remove concurrent powers, is therefore unacceptable.

Amendment No. 184 seeks to remove a regulator's concurrent powers when the last of any price controls in his or her sector expires. It, too, is unacceptable. It seems to assume that the central function of sectoral regulation is the imposition of price caps, so that when the need for price caps falls away, so too will the case for sector regulators to be involved in wider economic regulation or competition issues. That is not the Government's view.

The purpose of amendment No. 217 is to ensure that, where a regulator can use concurrent powers under the Bill in relation to any matter, he or she must do so, and not use sector-specific powers such as enforcement of licence conditions that may cover the same matter. Let me set out the Government's position. Sector-specific regimes quite properly cover competition matters and can rightly prescribe a more focused regime than would be the case with the Bill's prohibition regime alone. The Office of Fair Trading and the regulators have taken five key steps to ensure consistency of approach. They have set up a joint working party to develop working arrangements and to produce draft guidelines for consultation. They have agreed on a single notification point at the Office of Fair Trading. They have also agreed to set up effective mechanisms to share information, for example on complaints. That will prevent two directors inadvertently addressing the same matter. They have agreed to set out these arrangements in a draft concurrency guideline that is being developed by the working party.
