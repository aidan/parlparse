Mr. Simon Hughes (Southwark, North and Bermondsey): We partly debated schedule 2 on Thursday, during our debate on clause 3. However, following the Minister's answers to Opposition Members, I would like to pursue a couple of points.

First, my reading of what the Minister said was that the Government did not have it in mind actively to consider adding United Kingdom organisations to the list, but that they were minded to add international organisations. If so, by what process do they intend to enable that consideration? Would they consider consultation, at any level, with members of the Committee from other political parties? Would they consider consultation between the Secretary of State and his opposite numbers? Would they consider consultation at Privy Council level?

It is obviously desirable to be able to add to the list. However, additions would be more controversial than deletions; they would have to be the subject of political agreement. Self-evidently, that applies also to Northern Ireland and Irish organisations, although—based on our experience of the Northern Ireland (Emergency Provisions) Act 1996 and the Prevention of Terrorism (Temporary Provisions) Act 1989 over the past 25 years—the Irish list is relatively stable.

Secondly, the Minister has said that such matters are kept under permanent review. It will help us to know whether the Government expect to make additions to the list as soon as the Bill is enacted or whether it will not happen until a minimum period has passed. I understand, of course, that one must respond to immediate events. However, by definition, a process is being gone through. As we said the other day, the Bill will introduce, for the first time, new ways of defining terrorism, and new categories of organisations can therefore be included. I hope that the Government will be as forthcoming and as open as possible. Do they expect to start considering the matter with colleagues as soon as the Bill becomes law, or do they envisage that, unless something untoward happens, no organisation that could be a candidate for addition to the list will be added for three or six months—or whatever minimum time has been decided? I am trying to discover how the Government see the process of starting to review the list given in schedule 2, and how it will be done in the political context.

Lastly, the hon. Member for Aylesbury (Mr. Lidington), I and others said last week that the information on which the Government based their decisions should be objectively checked and tested. We explored the idea that that might involve the diplomatic service, the foreign service, the secret services, international police networks and so on. Will the Government undertake to consult human rights organisations and international political watch organisations—for instance, Amnesty International was consulted by the Home Secretary in the Pinochet case—as they have an interest and a network of information? Any Government who proscribe an organisation internationally risk endless controversy, not least in the House of Commons, unless they tread carefully and act on the best balanced advice. By definition, significant controversy in and beyond Parliament would be likely in the case of, for example, Kurdish organisations active in Turkey or elsewhere in the middle east. Would it be appropriate to proscribe an organisation that was campaigning for the freedom of its people from a power that was a NATO ally and—let me put it neutrally—a developing democracy?

The matter is sensitive. It is important that the Government are seen not to act only on the advice of those within Government and their agencies.
