Clause 3

Transfer to Authority and Council of functions, property etc

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

The Minister for Competition and Consumer Affairs (Dr. Kim Howells): Clause 3 provides for the transfer of the functions of the Director General of Gas Supply and the Director General of Electricity Supply either to the new Gas and Electricity Markets Authority—GEMA—or the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council. Broadly speaking, functions associated with the regulation of the gas and electricity markets will transfer to GEMA, while functions associated with information about consumer matters and about complaints handling will pass to the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council.

The functions of GEMA will include: licensing of gas and electricity companies; modification of licence conditions; where companies are price regulated, setting price caps in accordance with the licence conditions; setting overall and individual standards of performance; ensuring compliance with licence conditions and standards of practice; dispute resolution and enforcement action; and concurrent powers under the Competition Act 1998 and the Fair Trading Act 1973. The functions of the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council will include publication of information in the interests of consumers and complaints handling. It will also have a new role as a consumer advocate.

I shall deal in due course with the question that the hon. Member for South Dorset (Mr. Bruce) asked. It was a proper subject to raise.

After Royal Assent, transfers of property, rights or liabilities of the directors will need to take place, as appropriate, to reflect the transfer of functions. For example, databases containing information about consumer complaints should pass from the regulators to the new consumer council. Therefore, under clause 3 the Secretary of State has the power to make transfer schemes to achieve that. The clause allows for the transfer of the property, rights and liabilities of the gas consumers council to the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council.

The clause brings into effect schedule 3 with regard to the Gas and Electricity Markets Authority and the Gas and Electricity Consumer Council. That schedule contains more detail relating to the transfer of functions, property, rights and liabilities, including continuity of employment of transferred employees. As schedule 3 also deals with water, it will be dealt with following consideration of the clauses on the regulation of water.

Mr. Gibb: The Government and Opposition amendments are similar, except in one detailed and important respect. Both sets of amendments alter clause 4 and clause 87 to ensure that the estimate of the cost of forward work programmes includes the cost not only of special projects to be undertaken during the year, but of routine work. Those clauses as drafted require only the special projects to be costed.

It is clear that the Government share our anxiety about the escalating costs incurred by the authorities and councils, because they have tabled similar amendments. Those costs come not only from the plethora of new projets but are part of a gradual incremental increase in the routine investigation work carried out by the two bodies, evidence of which was discussed during earlier debate on the building of staff numbers.

The Government have pointed out in a number of documents, including the regulatory impact assessment, that those costs will ultimately be passed on to the consumer. It is therefore vital that every possible step is taken to minimise the cost and any increase in it. A requirement for the regulatory authorities and consumer council to publish an estimate of expendisture on their forward work programmes will provide a small but potentially effective brake on large increases. Unfortunately, however, that requirement will not completely provent expansion, so our overall concern about escalating costs remains, although at least the amendments would go some way towards dealing with the problem.

The Opposition amendments are different from those of the Government because they would require a further breakdown of the estimates. They would require estimates to be made of the cost of each specific project to be carried out rather than just of the overall cost of the authority and/or council. Such provision would enable scrutiny of whether individual projects are achieving value for money for the authority or council and would allow clear assessment of whether costs are proportionate to the specific objectives of particular projects. it would therefore prevent non-cost-effective projects from being lost in the aggregate of overall figures.

Clauses 4 and 87 require an estimate to be made of the costs of special projects taken as a whole, but they do not split that estimate to cover each project. The Government amendments rectify that problem merely by requiring an estimate of total cost, but they remove any requirement for a separate estimate of the projects as a whole and so take the Bill one stage away from where it was before. The Opposition amendments are far more comprehensive and are likely to concentrate the minds of authority and council members on the efficacy of each of their numerous activities. Such a requirement is far better than that inserted into the Bill by the Government amendments. I hope that the Minister will take on board our amendments and include them within his own proposals, which we support.

 

Dr. Howells: I shall resist the amendments, which would increase both the scope and the required level of detail of the forward work programmes. First, they would require the programmes to contain a description of the routine activities of regulatory authories and consumer councils. That information is intended to supplement that which is already required by clause 4 in relation to non-routine projects. Secondly the amendments would add a requirement to provide an estimate of the costs of each project that the bodies plan to undertake.

In respect of the requirement for the forward work programmes to contain a description of routine activities, I have a degree of sympathy with what the hon. Member for Bognor Regis and Littlehampton is trying to achieve. After all, Ofgem's recently published plan and budget for 2000–01 refers to a particular amount of routine activity. I confirm that we consider the Ofgem plan and budget to be a good example of the sort of forward work programme that we expect to be produced under the clause 4 obligation.

Let us consider that routine activity is included in that document. Ofgem has chosen to set up its work programme in terms of themes and priorities, which is a good method. For example, the document deals with themes of social and environmental action, competition in wholesale markets and so on. Ofgem has included in the document only those aspects of its routine activities that fit in with and contribute to those themes. For exmaple, in relation to the theme of social and environmental action, it refers to the dedication of significant resources to the investigation of customer complaints that will flow from increased competition in the gas and electricity markets.

It is easy to see why Ofgem has decided to include information about routine activities at that point. It will add colour and context, and will help explain how Ofgem plans to pursue its broader objectives through its day-to-day activities. We welcome the inclusion of such information and we intend nothing that would prohibit its inclusion in the forward work programmes produced to comply with clause 4.

That is a different matter from requiring that forward work programmes should contain a description of all routine activities planned for the year ahead, which is what the amendments would require. Such an obligation would result in forward work programmes being choked with extraneous material relating to routine activities, and it would add nothing of real value.

Ofgem has expressed concern that a requirement to estimate the future costs of its work as a series of discrete projects would be unduly onerous and unworkable. It says that it would constitute a significant impediment to effective regulation, and that it would tend to reduce its flexibility in managing its finances.

We listened to those arguments, and I tabled Government amendments Nos. 3 and 7 as a result. We want to encourage financial transparency on the part of the regulatory authorities and consumers councils. However, the Opposition amendments would require that the breakdown of estimated should be done in such detail as to impose an unacceptable administrative burden on those bodies. If a project is of such a size that it becomes feasible to provide an estimate of costs, the information is likely to be provided. For example, Ofgem's plan and budget contains financial information about the costs of new electricity trading arrangements.
