In all parliamentary democracies, political parties take their manifesto commitments very seriously. That is so throughout the world. Indeed, when political parties come to office, they usually try to carry out their manifesto commitments. Only in rare circumstances do they decide not to go ahead with those commitments--perhaps because of unexpected external events or, as has often happened in other parliamentary democracies, because an economic catastrophe blows the Treasury's calculations off course. No political party has made more of its manifesto commitments than the Labour party, but the so-called five early pledges of the Labour manifesto are already disappearing further into the distance.

The purpose of the amendments is to invite the Government to honour their general election manifesto, which included a pledge that there would be a new millennium fund--to be called the New Opportunities Fund--to replace the existing fund once the millennium has passed. The manifesto, ``new Labour because Britain deserves better'', stated that

    ``Labour has already proposed a new millennium commission to commence after the closure of the Millennium Exhibition, to provide direct support for a range of education, environment and public health projects, including those directed at children's play, currently excluded from lottery benefit.''

That is clear evidence that the change was intended to take place after the millennium fund had used up its resources.

The Bill is a £1 billion raid on the lottery. It will deprive the good causes of substantial sums of money. As my noble Friend Lord Skidelsky said in another place, even by January more than £60 million may have been segregated into the shadow account for the New Opportunities Fund. The fund was created simply to get that money quickly. It was orginally to be a replacement for the millennium fund, not a sixth good cause, as it has now been described.

I fully accept that the Government have said that they want to remain within their public expenditure constraints, but in effect the Bill is a way of breaching those constraints by funding core Government responsibilities by raiding the lottery. During the general election campaign, the Labour party changed its mind about its manifesto commitment. It was said that the creation of the New Opportunities Fund had something to do with the Wednesday draw. The fact is that it was always Camelot's intention to introduce a mid-week draw; the idea that the New Opportunities Fund arose from that is unsustainable.

There is no guarantee that the original good causes will be protected--as we have seen in parts of the United States, there is such a thing as lottery fatigue. In breaching its manifesto commitment, the Labour party seeks to obtain money for the New Opportunities Fund as soon as possible.

As I have said, substantial sums have been hived off to that shadow account since October. It is difficult to estimate how much money is involved, but it might be well in excess of £100 million. By the time that the Bill receives Royal Assent--Heaven forbid--the sum might be in excess of £200 million or more, which would have gone to the original good causes.

We question giving parliamentary approval to such retrospection; there is no precedent for it. The National Lottery etc. Act 1993 contained no power of retrospection. However, given their desire to fill the void in their election commitments on education, health and other matters, the Government simply could not wait, frankly, to grab the loot for the New Opportunities Fund. Of course, under the original arrangements, the five good causes received 20 per cent. each.

On the point made by the Minister for Sport, section 28 of the 1993 Act gave the Secretary of State power to vary by order the percentages allocated to each cause, not to substitute a new good cause or to vary the percentages so that they do not add up to 100 per cent. However, using the shadow accounting mechanism, the Government have sought unilaterally to alter the percentage so that the five original causes do not add up to 100 per cent. The New Opportunities Fund will receive the money hived off from the worthy causes.

It would be absurd to suggest that the distributing bodies agreed to the new mechanism. On Second Reading, I invited the Secretary of State to publish the agreements that he made with the distribution bodies to show how anxious they were to co-operate with the Government. We have heard nothing from the Secretary of State, so I hope that Ministers will take the opportunity to give us further information.

The Bill represents an essentially political raid. The power of retrospection is being introduced to get the New Opportunities Fund up and running as soon as possible to fill the public expenditure gaps that are now screamingly apparent in health and education.

Lord Redesdale, a Liberal Democrat, made the issue perfectly clear when he sought to limit the Secretary of State's scope to vary the percentages of proceeds of the lottery given to each good cause. Lord Redesdale said:

    ``The Minister was kind enough to send me a letter emphasising his point that the new opportunities fund would not grow so large. He strenuously denied our point about the funding of the cause being illegal and said that if the Government were not allowed to fund the new opportunities fund before legislation had been passed, then to bring the new opportunities fund up to the level of funding they wished, they would initially have to spend 18 per cent. of lottery funds on it and then they would have to fund it at 24 2/3 per cent. from Autumn 1999. I would find it worrying that if the Government had failed through their rather interesting accounting practices to secure the money for the new opportunities fund they would be quite prepared to take almost 25 per cent. of the lottery for one of the six good causes''--[Official Report, House of Lords, 9 March 1998; Vol. 587, c. 15.]

The precedents do not exist and those that the Government have advanced are not substantive; the amendments to the Criminal Justice and Public Order Act 1994 and to the Recreational Charities Act 1958 merely return the law to the state in which it was before a judicial decision interpreted it differently. It is a legal, technical matter. The powers under section 88 of the Local Government and Housing Act 1989, which has been mentioned, made legal a consultation process that had taken place before the Act was passed and that is wholly different from the appropriation of funds that has taken place and that is specifically proscribed by the original Act.

I shall conclude on a critically important point about the powers of retrospection. Retrospective provisions of the Stamp Duty (Temporary Provisions) Act 1992 allowed the Government to forego taxes to which they would have been entitled. The proposed retrospection has no basis. The Secretary of State has been challenged, but has not been able to respond. The power of retrospection is damaging and sets a wholly unacceptable precedent. I hope that the Committee will reflect seriously on the matter.

The amendments raise some important wider points, principally that they seek to protect the principle or additionality, which everyone agrees has been an important component of the lottery. If the lottery is viewed as a pot into which a Minister can dip his or her hand at the appropriate time, it will fall into disrepute and cease to have the widespread public support that has enabled it to do so much good in the past four years. We all agree that defining additionality has proved to be rather difficult, but I am willing to rely on the Prime Minister's own definition in the White Paper when he said:

As was pointed out in another place, two principles underlie the definition of additionality. First, continuing public services should not rely on voluntary contributions, with the obvious dangers that that can cause and, secondly, national lottery money should be used for what is desirable rather than essential.
