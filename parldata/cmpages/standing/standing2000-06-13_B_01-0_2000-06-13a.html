I will say that it is premature in that the Patten report was written against a predication, an expectation, an anticipation, a premise that the Belfast agreement would be discharged in all its aspects, and it was a prescription or a recipe for policing in Northern Ireland in peace on the far side of the fulfilment of all of the Good Friday agreement.

Mr. Seamus Mallon (Newry and Armagh): I, too, look forward to working in the Committee under your chairmanship, Mr. O'Hara'. First, I thank the Government and the Whips for facilitating us in our request about numbers, due to extraneous circumstances over which I have no control.

As you said, Mr. O'Hara, this is an important Bill. The Patten report required

    a new beginning to policing in Northern Ireland with a police service capable of attracting and sustaining support from the community as a whole.

That mandate came directly from the Good Friday agreement which stated—and it is worth remembering—that we were seeking a police service that was

    Fair and impartial, free from partisan control, accountable, representative, conforming with human rights norms ...and delivered in constructive and inclusive partnerships with the community.

That was what the agreement required and Patten recommended.

I am afraid that the Bill does not measure up to that. My hon. Friend the Member for Hull, North (Mr. McNamara) described it on Second Reading as a miserable Bill. It is miserable and minimalist, first, because of its failure to implement the recommendations of the Patten report on name, badge and flag. Secondly, it is a failure in respect of delegation to the Chief Constable of critical Patten reforms on the code of ethics, human rights appraisal, plastic bullets, and training and development. Let us remind ourselves that police have never reformed themselves. It is naive to believe that they will do so now. Thirdly, the Bill virtually nullifies the Policing Board's powers to commission reports and inquiries and to ensure that the police are efficient and effective. Those restrictions are so grave that the even the Government-appointed Police Authority, of which I am no fan—nor is it a fan of mine—has described the recommendations as ``ludicrous and totally unacceptable''. For probably the first time, I agree with the Police Authority. Fourthly, the key Patten recommendations on the power of the police ombudsman have not been implemented in the Bill. Fifthly, the office of the new oversight commissioner has not been placed on an adequate statutory basis. Those are the major defects in the Bill.

Dr. Lynch from the United States, a member of the Patten commission who has remarkable experience of the issue, described the Patten report as having been ``gutted'' by the Police (Northern Ireland) Bill. That was not the only observation that was made. I refer to the statement made on behalf of the hierarchy of the Catholic Church, not because it is a Church, not because it is the Catholic Church, but because its position is relevant to the debate, as we shall see as we progress. The statement said:

    Any appeal to the Catholic Church to make it a priority to encourage Catholics to join the police is unlikely to be taken up by Church leaders in the context of this Bill.

I shall put our party position clearly and without equivocation. We want a system of policing that we can support, in which we can involve ourselves and ask others to be involved, and which we can defend. We want that to be put in place for the first time since the Northern Ireland state was formed and we want it badly. It may have been suggested that Patten was premature. Patten was 86 years late, but even now we have to get it right; otherwise, our party will not appoint people to serve on the Policing Board and we will not appeal to Catholics—young Catholics especially—to join or support the police. I say that without equivocation because I do not want any doubt in anybody's mind as to where we stand.

A few miserable crumbs thrown at us by way of amendments will not keep us quiet. We shall not be happy unless the Bill is fundamentally changed, not in my direction or in that of others who comment on it, but in that of the Good Friday agreement, to which we all subscribed, and in that to which the Patten commission has pointed. I say that not as a threat, because I do not believe in threats, or as rhetoric—I do indulge in that sometimes, but it does not get me very far. I say that simply as a statement of political reality.

I look forward to working with everyone on the Committee and to getting it right so that the resulting legislation will allow all of us to go into a new dispensation of policing for the first time.

Mr. Ken Maginnis (Fermanagh and South Tyrone): I welcome the opportunity to look at the Bill in detail. As well as welcoming you to the chair, Mr. O'Hara, I am happy to be a member of the Committee. You will recall that, on Second Reading, most of us were confined to a 10-minute contribution, analysing the background and how the Bill has to be moved forward. That was totally inadequate and did not give us the chance fully to set out the past, present and future basis of policing in Northern Ireland.

Like many experienced and responsible policemen, I do not look forward to what we are almost destined—given the Government's majority—to produce as a result of the Bill. There is an element of deja vu in this because I remember the previous police Bill about two years ago. We cautioned the Government at that time that it was totally inadequate and so it turned out to be—a Bill full of flaws, that addressed political issues with more vigour than policing. Today, we are embarked on a similar exercise and that is something to which the Committee will have to attend.

No doubt a number of people will stress that a new beginning is required in Northern Ireland, without recognising what that entails. Policing is a matter not for the police, but for society—it must decide how it wishes to be policed. The function of the force is to fulfil the needs of society within a modern democracy. When I speak about a new beginning, I am not considering how we can dismantle a police force that has served society well during many difficult periods and has been politicised, not so much by those who support it as by those who oppose it for political reasons.

In our society, we have a hugely dangerous mafia sub-culture. I do not know whether that is a sign of the times, an inevitable outcome of liberal democracy in the 50-odd years since the end of the war, or a result of the 30 years of terrorism, but it exists. Society has to be prepared to embark on a new beginning that indicates respect for life and property, for young and old, and for all the things that most of us believe are ethical and proper.

To draw a brief comparison between policing and the health service, particularly in Northern Ireland, a few years ago it was fashionable to reform, to change and to make more professional—to move training in the health service away from the wards and into universities. Yesterday, there were two debates. One was about the abuse of the health service and of people by professionals who fell short of the ethos of their profession. We heard about a health service in Northern Ireland in which the waiting lists are longer than they have ever been and which, as I know from personal experience, is falling into chaos.

I am a village schoolteacher by profession. I would be the last to denigrate raising education standards, but life is not only about book learning, hence the problems in our health service. Similarly, we must beware the problems in policing. In Northern Ireland and throughout the United Kingdom, many things are going wrong in policing at present. That is not simply connected to our new, sophisticated culture or to huge, complicated financial fraud. It is an undercurrent that pervades society. Some people call it the Bramshill syndrome, and I have a great affinity with that term. The idea that policing is primarily about education, books and university degrees is nonsense, if one looks at what the highly educated RUC command has achieved over the past six years since the violence started to reduce in terms of the increase in the drug culture in Northern Ireland. There is an inbuilt prejudice against the drug culture throughout society, yet it is frightening to see what it has achieved.

I apologise if hon. Members have heard me say this before, but if one compares the £1 billion tax fraud relating to smuggled motor and heating fuel with the £8 billion budget for Northern Ireland that was introduced yesterday, one begins to understand the extent to which evil people are beginning to grasp control of our society in Northern Ireland. Those issues must be addressed.

There are other, more emotional tribal issues—if I may call them that—and more emotional political issues, but the crux of the matter is this: how do we introduce a Bill that will create a new beginning in which the people in the society of which I am a part can function with confidence? In other words, how are we going to create a happy society? The police cannot do it for us. A new beginning does not apply to the police. It applies to society, and when other hon. Members use that phrase, I hope that they will bear that in mind.

I must also caution against the idea of foreshortening and restricting the terms of our debate by addressing the Bill, as the Minister has suggested, in terms of the one word ``Patten''. Over the past few days, I have heard quite a few contributions being made about Patten. People talk about the spirit of Patten. The reality is that the Patten report was a sloppy report, in so far as Patten set himself above, beyond and outside the terms of reference on which his commission was supposed to function. He did not recognise that politicians from all sections of society in Northern Ireland had spent many years bringing forth the Belfast agreement in May 1998.

It is not, therefore, the spirit of Patten—the spirit of sloppiness—that must guide our thoughts when we discuss the Bill, but the spirit of the Belfast agreement, which came to the only conclusion possible if we are to have a politically stable community. That was that Northern Ireland will remain part of the United Kingdom for as long as the people of Northern Ireland so desire. That was stated in such a way in the agreement that it was not aggressive and did not impose on people with different aspirations a sense of guilt that they had not achieved those aspirations or that the majority wanted to dominate the minority and its views. The agreement recognised the de facto situation and the Irish Republic withdrew its territorial claim. Its realistic approach was to bring two diverse traditions together to work, live and function with stability and mutual trust, although that will take some time to achieve.

When Patten was given his mandate or direction to examine policing in Northern Ireland, he was told that the agreement provides the opportunity for a new beginning for policing in Northern Ireland with a police service capable of attracting and sustaining support from the community as a whole. It seems to many of us that Patten chose the lowest common denominator. He tried to satisfy those who are currently dissatisfied without considering those from both traditions--I emphasise both traditions--who, for years and years, supported the police quietly and faithfully, recognising the extent to which they had to operate in difficult circumstances. Patten was told that his report needed widespread support, but it was so radical and challenging that, instead of satisfying anyone, it has brought two political friends--the hon. Member for Newry and Armagh (Mr. Mallon) and me--here today with totally different opinions of the Bill. The Patten report was divisive and has not served Northern Ireland well.

When the report was published, Patten attended a news presentation and then disappeared with some fellow members of his commission--not all of them--to America to explain it to the Americans. He did not explain it to those of us who live and work in Northern Ireland and make decisions on how to move the process forward.

Perhaps the ad hoc committee for foreign affairs, Mr. Gilman and Congressman King, were more important in terms of selling the Patten report than the people of Northern Ireland. Congressman King approved of the mortaring of Newry police station, which resulted in the death of Protestant and Catholic policemen and women, and has been an overt supporter of the IRA. We have been left to struggle through with many of the changes. I say again that it is not the spirit of Patten but the spirit and letter of the Belfast agreement that most people look forward to, and that has yet to be recognised. Not a single bullet or ounce of Semtex has been delivered up by those who for 30 years have held society to ransom.

The hon. Member for Solihull (Mr. Taylor) referred to the codes of ethics and codes of practice that are mentioned in the Bill. If Committee members are not given some idea of what those codes will be, our work will not have been worth the crack of your finger, because such codes can be manipulated to undermine our intentions for Northern Ireland. They can be manipulated to pander to the most forceful elements in our society, which are still the loyalist and republican paramilitary organisations that have a finger in every single pie in Northern Ireland.

We are approaching what ought to be a celebratory season of the year, yet many of us hold our breath, given what the next few months may herald. We hear that one person who has a say over every tablet in the drugs trade is peripherally involved—perhaps not peripherally—in likely events in Northern Ireland in the next few months. We hear such things about some of the most evil people in our society. If Committee members think that I am straying from the point, I remind them that I am talking about policing and how to protect society in Northern Ireland.

We have been told that the last drugs murder in Northern Ireland was carried out by the Provisional IRA on the orders of the senior command. That makes matters exceedingly difficult for those such as me who have struggled for years to create an equitable society in Northern Ireland. We do not want confrontation. We want a society that respects the police force, and a police force that provides reassurance for ourselves, our children and our children's children.

Human rights will be an essential element of the Bill. It will place a huge emphasis on human rights. If one teaches a policeman anything now, one teaches him about human rights: everything revolves around those two simple words, as though somehow they were new words. We used to refer to implementing law and order. If we were worthy of our name of legislators, that law and order meant protecting and sustaining the human rights of the society that we represented. So what is new? What is new is that the term ``human rights'' is being used again and again to constrain the police as they try to do their work in our society.

I am worried about the future of policing and the treatment of those who are asked to leave the RUC. In that context, we also need to consider the 8,000 men and women who retired from of the RUC and those who have been widowed. There would be major discontent among RUC members and the public if they were not treated generously.

On Second Reading, I discussed a young widow of a police sergeant, who had young children, and I make no apology for discussing her case again today. I add, for no particular reason, that he happened to be a Roman Catholic. Many people feel that no opportunity was afforded to members of the Roman Catholic tradition to join the RUC. The spirit of the Stormont or Belfast agreement is not about doing down the RUC, although some people—and the Bill—appear to suggest that it is. The spirit of the Belfast agreement is about creating an equitable, peaceful and stable society. It seeks to ensure that Northern Ireland remains part of the United Kingdom until the people decide otherwise and that those things that go with Britishness are respected, in a quiet, unobtrusive and non-aggressive way. Those aims are not reflected in the Bill's treatment of the name and emblems of the RUC.

Patten must be made better. The Bill should not be about implementing Patten; it should be about implementing the Belfast agreement and creating something of which our society of which we can be proud for the next 25 or 40 years—even though some of us cannot think even that far ahead, for obvious reasons. People must believe that we have done an honourable job for society and not merely paid lip service or pandered to political pressures, some of which are of dubious.

Clause 1

The Northern Ireland Policing Board

Question proposed, That the clause stand part of the Bill.

Mr. Ingram: I shall give a brief outline of clause 1 because the important debate will flow from schedule 1. The clause establishes the Northern Ireland Policing Board to replace the Police Authority for Northern Ireland. It dissolves the Policy Authority on the creation of the board and transfers its property, liabilities and staff to the board.

I want to take the opportunity to pay tribute to the work of the Police Authority for Northern Ireland and its staff. The threats to its members and, indeed, the murder of two of its members must never be forgotten. They have carried out their duties and their difficult role to the best of their abilities. I have had points of conflict with them and they with me, but we have tried to deal with those maturely. When they have raised issues of concern we have met them--as we have when we have been concerned--to find a way through for better policing and better management of the police service in Northern Ireland.

The board's membership is determined by schedule 1 and depends on whether there is devolved government or direct rule. In either case, the board will have 19 members. During devolved government, 10 members will be nominated from the Assembly using the d'Hondt method of selection; the other nine will be appointed by the Secretary of State. Assembly Members will give the board a democratic mandate to hold the police to account. That was recommended in the Patten report which stated in paragraph 6.11 that a majority elected membership is essential if the board is to command ``respect and credibility''. That will bring the board into line with practice in Great Britain with a democratic presence in relation to that part of the tripartite structure relating to policing anywhere in the United Kingdom.
