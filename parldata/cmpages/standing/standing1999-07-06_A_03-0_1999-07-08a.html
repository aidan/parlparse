The Economic Secretary to the Treasury (Ms Patricia Hewitt): Thank you, Mr. O'Brien. May I say, as I was not here for our first sitting, what a pleasure it is to have you in the Chair for our deliberations?

Let me return briefly to what I said earlier about amendments Nos. 37 and 39, which would remove references to the FSA's general functions as defined under clause 2(4) with the purported effect that the authority's objectives and principles would bite directly on all the FSA's functions, not just those set out in clause 2(4).

It must be clear by now how committed the Government are to ensuring that the authority is properly accountable and that it acts fairly and reasonably at all times. It is equally important to allow the FSA to do its business without undue or unnecessary hindrance. In the light of that balance, the amendments are inappropriate and possibly a recipe for complete disaster.

The general functions cover the FSA's rule making powers and, we propose, its code making functions. It also provides guidance and determines policy principles by reference to which other functions are performed. For example, in determining enforcement policy or rule waivers, the FSA will need to try to meet its statutory objectives and to have regard to the principles. However, individual enforcement decisions or rule waivers will not be subject to challenge on the grounds that the objectives have not been met.

That is entirely proper. Otherwise, each and every member of staff will have to make a judgment whether and, if so, the extent to which all the FSA's objectives and principles have been met in every single decision. They would also have to decide on a proper balance between the objectives and the principles. Individual members of staff would have to decide, before doing anything at all, how to balance the consumer protection objective and the principle of facilitating innovation or not necessarily impeding competition. Requiring such judgments to be made every day by every employee every time he or she wants to do something would be a formula for delay, inconsistency and trouble.

That is not to say that FSA staff will not be obliged to have regard to objectives and principles in other ways. As with every organisation, FSA staff will be subject to policies and guidelines that would be set by people at an appropriately high level in the organisation so as to deliver consistently and across the board on the principles and objectives. Indeed, it is quite likely that both would go into much greater detail than the objectives to ensure that staff had appropriate guidance. That is akin to what would happen in the usual corporate structure and is not at all dissimilar to the application of both the FSA's rules and a regulated firm's own detailed rules to its employees.

The issues of competition and competiveness were raised this morning in amendments tabled by the Opposition. The amendments address two matters that also were debated by the Joint Committee and attempt to change the requirements imposed on the FSA to have regard to those matters under the principles set out in clause 2(3) into fully fledged objectives.

The Government have no doubt at all about the importance of maintaining the strong competitive position of United Kingdom financial businesses in world markets. There is no disagreement between us 63 64 [Ms Patricia Hewitt]

about that being vital to our national well-being. The Government keep competitiveness high on their list of priorities. Similarly, we are clear about the benefits to consumers of strong competition in our home markets.

The Bill does not stop competition law applying to firms in the financial services sector. It simply disapplies that law where it might otherwise conflict with firms' compliance with FSA rules. The rules will be subject to rigorous competition scrutiny by the Director General of Fair Trading under the Bill. I want to stress an important point, which is that all FSA rules and practices, including capital adequacy rules for banks and insurance companies, will be subject to scrutiny by the Office of Fair Trading. Contrasting that with existing regulations, under which only investment business rules are subject to that scrutiny, makes it clear that the Bill takes a very important step forward by subjecting the regulatory regime itself to competition scrutiny. In other words, the regulatory activity previously carried out by the Bank of England will be covered for the first time by competition scrutiny. That is an important innovation that does not apply to other areas of regulation such as that for the environment and health and safety.

The fact that competition and competitiveness figure in the FSA's principles, but not in its objectives does not represent a downgrading of the importance of these issues. Instead, the Government believe that such an approach best equips the FSA to take them into account along with other matters such as protecting innovation and ensuring a proper cost-benefit analysis when applying its objectives. The Joint Committee agreed that it was not appropriate to give the FSA extra new objectives.

It is clear that the amendments are intended to act as a constraint on executive regulation. We share the belief that regulation must be proportionate. As my right hon. Friend the Chief Secretary said in Committee on Tuesday, we want regulation to have a light touch where possible, but to give effective protection where necessary.

The principles set out in clause 2 (3) deliver that aim. The subsection addresses directly several factors that the FSA must have regard to when carrying out its functions. It covers competition, competitiveness and innovation as well as equally important issues relating to regulatory costs and burdens and the role of senior management. The need to have regard to such factors is part and parcel of the FSA's statutory duty, and will have a pervasive influence on the way in which it carries out its functions. I think that that answers the question that the right hon. Member for Wells (Mr. Heathcoat-Amory) asked this morning.

Paragraph (f) is intended to reflect fully our commitment to ensuring that competition is protected. In this interesting debate we have not heard any reason why the principles in subsection (2) and in paragraph (f) in particular will not have the effect that we intend.

One amendment relates to how the Treasury considers competition and competitiveness. I fully agree with Conservative Members that the Treasury should be aware of the impact of its rules on competition in United Kingdom markets and the competitiveness of UK firms. Those issues will certainly be in our minds when we or our successors exercise Treasury powers under the Bill.

The FSA and the Treasury are fundamentally different. The FSA is an independent statutory regulator, whereas the Treasury is a Government Department and Treasury Ministers are directly accountable to Parliament for their actions. Indeed, there will be Parliamentary procedures for many of the powers exercisable by Ministers under the Bill.

The non-statutory objectives that are set for the Treasury by the Government include increasing the productivity of the economy through competition, among other means, as well as securing an efficient market in financial services. In addition, the Treasury, like other Government Departments, must follow the better regulation guidance issued by the Cabinet Office in preparing public regulatory impact statements outlining the costs and benefits of proposed regulations.

We intend to consult widely on the most significant items of secondary legislation made under the Bill. Indeed, we have already set that process in train. That approach has been widely welcomed and it will allow firms and others to bring to our attention any concerns that they may have.

However, I do not see why the Treasury should only take account of competition and competitiveness when it exercises its powers. We might also wish to consider to take just two examples the protection of consumers or the international character of financial markets. I do not want the specific issues singled out in the amendments written into the Bill in the way that Conservative Members propose.

We have had an extremely interesting debate. I have no doubt that we shall continue to reflect on the issue of competition. However, in the light of what I have said, I hope the amendment will be withdrawn. If it is not, I shall ask my hon. Friends to vote against it.

To which activities of the FSA should regulatory objectives and principles relate? Should those activities be limited or prescribed, subjective or objective? In specifying that the objectives and principles relate only to general functions, the Bill makes that territory pretty narrow. Those functions, which we have discussed, are rule-making, giving advice and guidance, and determining general policy and principles.

Leaving such matters undefined would mean that the objectives would be too wide. By contrast, the definitions in the Bill are far too narrow.

Should the FSA's conduct be subjective or objective? Under the Bill, it is subjective what the FSA considers to be most appropriate. In other words, the FSA will be off the hook if it says that its conduct is appropriate, even if the result of that conduct is extremely undesirable.

The objectives should be objective. The FSA should act so as to achieve the automatic defence proposed in amendment No. 19. Leaving the FSA's conduct as subjective places its legislative powers above the law.

Amendment No. 36 would require the FSA to conduct itself in a

We do not accept the Economic Secretary's arguments against that. No one is suggesting that those words or its context mean that the FSA should make confidential documents available to the public. It is about the manner in which it should act, just as the FSA requires the financial services industry itself to act in like manner on the other side of the fence. We cannot see why there cannot be a pari passu balance between regulator and regulated in terms of transparency.

Pulling those two areas together, it must be right for objectives to relate to the activities of the FSA generally and not only to rule-making, advice, policy and principles.
