Sir Nicholas Lyell (North-East Bedfordshire): Your guidance, Mr. Maxton, is always valuable. I was concluding my remarks regarding amendment No. 2. We do not recognise when we are in danger of becoming oppressive. The Minister would not see himself as oppressive, nor would the Parliamentary Secretary. I warn them that they are making a huge jump in that direction. The amendment highlights the problem.

Lord Devlin, who referred to the jury as a safeguard against repugnant laws made his point succinctly. He said:

    The second and by far the greater purpose that is served by trial by jury is that it gives protection against laws which the ordinary man may regard as harsh and oppressive. I do not mean by that no more than it is a protection against tyranny. It is that: but it is also an insurance that the criminal law will conform to the ordinary man's idea of what is fair and just. If it does not, the jury will not be a party to its enforcement.

The Parliamentary Secretary jumped up when that point was made earlier. He asked me whether that meant that I thought that juries did not obey their oath. I think nothing of the sort. When one studies the list of either-way offences referred to by the Minister, one realises what a huge swathe of offences it covers and how important it is for the community at large to have its say on where the boundaries of justice should be drawn, as the amendment emphasises.

Studying the list highlights another good point raised by Government Members in their brief interventions. They have been astute in saying that magistrates can be trusted. I agree that, by and large, magistrates provide an excellent service. I have said that almost to the point of tedious repetition. Serious charges such as those concerning honesty are the most sensitive and are those about which people who have never had a criminal record feel most strongly. Such cases are very much for the jury. When one flips through the pages of either-way offences, one passes violence against the person, sexual offences, theft, handling, fraud, forgery, criminal damage and drug offences. One then reaches a series of offences—pages of them—including gaming, immigration, trade descriptions, health and safety at work, public health and environmental protection. I know from personal experience that clients are entirely happy to be tried by magistrates, who do a huge amount of this work extremely effectively. The ability of the defendant to choose the Crown court or the magistrates court gives enormous confidence in the system.

It is to do with how cases are tried in the magistrates court. Anyone with any experience of the magistrates court, including the Parliamentary Secretary, knows that a two-day case there is a bit of a nightmare. The process takes time. Furthermore, a good deal of practical court experience is needed to handle it, but it is not the type of experience that most court solicitors gain. The youngish counsel who attend to such cases in the Crown court do them day in, day out. The Crown court is used to them and the system works well. For example, if a jury is out deciding a case, the Crown court does not wait for it to return before starting the next case. The court moves on straight away and interrupts the next case when the jury is ready to give its verdict. The Crown court system is very efficient.

I shall deal with costings in the clause stand part debate. At this stage, I want the Government to recognise that the quality of defence available in magistrates courts, though not negligible, is not equal to that in the Crown court.

Mr. Simon Hughes (Southwark, North and Bermondsey): I want to make one specific point that relates to the Government's justification for the Bill. The argument, which I hope to demonstrate is flawed and outdated, is that many people who plead not guilty in the lower court and elect to go to the Crown court, change their plea. It is commonly agreed that there are two main reasons for that. First, the prosecution has time to reveal its hand. It nearly always produces more evidence subsequent to the committal. I cannot recall more than the odd time when there was no additional evidence, other than in the simplest cases. Secondly, the process of testing the case and examining the evidence is more substantial.

When the Crown Prosecution Service was established, it was envisaged that it would do its work, review cases and hand them over to a prosecutor, who would be one of its people or independently engaged. There would be a debate about whether the two tests had been met and a decision would be taken to proceed. However, there is often pressure to get that stage of the proceedings out of the way so that a case is seen to be making progress. Magistrates are rightly concerned that cases should not drift. If there is an adjournment for the prosecution to get the evidence together, they are keen for the case to be put off for only one month, rather than six. The CPS and the prosecution scramble to get the evidence together, which is often just over the 50 per cent. needed to get them past the threshold. There is usually a lot of work still to do.

My most recent example of that was a murder case at the Old Bailey last year. Someone was killed in my constituency and there was a committal on the basis of certain evidence, although more was served later. One reason why the evidence was served as late as possible was to protect the witnesses. It is not at all uncommon to add the details of evidence later in the day. The police and prosecuting authorities had an interest in revealing enough to get the case up to the Old Bailey, but, for witness protection reasons, not everything.

There are many reasons why evidence comes late in the day. In spite of the hearings for procedures and timetables, the prosecution often waits to within about a week of the hearing of the trial before revealing the full hand. Often only at that stage do the defendant's advisers form a view on how the defendant should plead and they often know all along that more evidence will probably or definitely be served.

Mr. Lock: The debate has been short but interesting and illuminating. The effect of the amendments would be that any either-way offence, no matter whether it would previously have been considered in the magistrates court or whether it would have gone on to the Crown court on indictment, would henceforth be treated for the purposes of representation, legal aid and advance disclosure as if it was a trial in the Crown court. For practical purposes, that would attract the benefit of solicitor and counsel.

As I mentioned in my intervention on the hon. Member for Southwark, North and Bermondsey, the effects of the case of R v. Kingston-upon-Hull, ex parte McCann, with the statement of principle affirmed by the Director of Public Prosecutions as a result of the case, leave the position for advance disclosure more or less identical in summary trials as it is in trials on indictment. The 1996 Act makes no difference with regard to unused material, so the rules are the same.

Of course there will be differences in practice. If a trial happens reasonably quickly in a magistrates court, evidence that the prosecution might have wanted to adduce had it had six, eight or 10 weeks to try the matter in the Crown court, will not come to light. The thrust of the Opposition's argument is to protect the rights of the defence, but that would restrict the prosecution to the evidence that it had at the beginning of the trial.

The main issue is defence representation by counsel or solicitors. There are several principles involved. The first is that a proper defence is important. The Government believe that. We have brought the European convention on human rights into domestic law. It covers the right to a fair trial, which includes an element of having a proper defence. That right will be in British law for the first time from October, when it will be exercisable in our courts before our judges. That demonstrates our commitment to human rights and to providing a proper defence.

Sir Nicholas Lyell: I am sure that the Minister would not wish to overstate the benefits of incorporating the European convention into our law. It happens in Scotland in slightly more limited circumstances and in those jurisdictions in the United States that follow our system.

Mr. Lock: I am grateful for that intervention. The right hon. and learned Gentleman is correct. The convention does not preserve the right to a jury trial in any particular case. It guarantees the right to a fair trial, which must be determined by each state, according to its local traditions.

The second principle is that the level of expertise of defence that is appropriate to a case should be proportionate to its gravity. That principle has operated in this country for a long time. We do not have leading counsel for every case in the Crown court or very experienced juniors for every case in the magistrates court. There is a level of expertise for the defence that is appropriate to the seriousness of the case.
