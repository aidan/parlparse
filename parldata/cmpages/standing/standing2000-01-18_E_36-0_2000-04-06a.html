Mr. Hill: The provisions are sought principally to allow transposition of a European Union directive on the interoperability of the trans-European high-speed rail network. The directive, which was agreed by the previous Administration, came into force in 1996 and should have been implemented last year. The new clause will allow for the transposition of a similar directive relating to the conventional rail network, which Transport Ministers are currently discussing in Brussels.

The new clause is necessary because the European Communities Act 1972, under which European Community legislation is usually incorporated into United Kingdom law, does not allow us to make regulations to ensure that the technical specifications issued under the directive can come into force as soon as they are published. That is a requirement of the directive, and the Commission has already started infraction proceedings against the UK for non-transposition. Subsection (2) of the new clause will allow reference to be made in implementing regulations to standards that are set by persons specified in the regulations. That will allow the regulations to refer to the technical specifications issued under the directive. The new clause will also give the Secretary of State a power to make further regulations to cover areas of the railway industry for which there are no current standards—for example, the insides of trains.

Amendments Nos. 1030 and 1031 are consequential, and apply the new clause to Northern Ireland. That is necessary as Westminster is to retain powers to legislate in this respect for Northern Ireland, because implementation of EC directives is a UK duty.

Mr. Hill: The operator licensing system plays a crucial role in maintaining high standards in the road haulage industry. The licensing by traffic commissioners of operators of goods vehicles ensures the safe and responsible use of such vehicles and fair competition between operators. In deciding whether to issue an operator's licence, a traffic commissioner must be satisfied that the applicant is fit to hold a licence, that he has proper maintenance facilities and sufficient finance to keep the vehicles roadworthy, that he has an operating centre suitable physically and environmentally for his vehicles, and that he has proper arrangements to ensure that the rules on drivers' hours are followed and that the vehicles are not overloaded.

Those who choose to operate heavy goods vehicles without an operator's licence escape those checks on professionalism, safety and environmental impact. Traffic commissioners are also enabled to use against illegal operators the sanctions that they employ against licensed operators who commit serious or persistent offences. Those sanctions include the revocation, suspension or curtailment of an operator's licence.

Illegal operators are a small minority in a largely responsible industry. As well as avoiding the other requirements of the licensing system, they often run vehicles in a dangerous condition. The Government are determined to take action against operators who choose to operate outside the licensing system. An impounding system would provide Vehicle Inspectorate examiners with powers to detain and impound illegally operated heavy goods vehicles at roadside checks.

In the previous session, Earl Attlee introduced a Bill in another place that would have introduced an impounding system. The Government supported that Bill but, unfortunately, it failed in the Commons. The Transport Bill should therefore be amended to allow for impounding provisions. The schedule provides for detailed regulations to be laid before Parliament setting out the practical procedures for an impounding scheme. We carried out a consultation exercise in 1998 that confirmed widespread support for such an impounding scheme, especially from the road haulage industry and the traffic commissioners. It is a commitment in our integrated transport White Paper and has the support of the Commission for Integrated Transport. There will be further consultations on the draft regulations.

At present, operators found to be operating without an operator's licence are secure in the knowledge that they can be fined only relatively small sums which, for many, do not cancel out the profits that they earn by cutting corners, so they cannot be prevented from continuing to run their businesses. The amendment would give magistrates and sheriffs the power to impose heavier fines for carrying goods without an operator's licence by raising the maximum penalty from level 4—currently a maximum of £2,500{**mrl;**}to level 5, currently up to £5,000. The possibility of a higher fine alone might not be a sufficiently serious deterrent to illegal operators. However, coupled with the impounding scheme, increasing the maximum fine level would send a fresh signal to offenders of the seriousness with which the Government take the offence.

The present legislation allows newly acquired vehicles within the maximum number authorised under an operator's licence to be used for up to a month without the traffic commissioner being notified and without a disc being displayed. That makes it difficult for the Vehicle Inspectorate to check at a roadside whether a vehicle is being operated without a valid operator's licence. We therefore propose that the existing provision should be abolished. The new computer system being developed for the traffic area network will allow for operators to provide immediate notification of vehicles included within the margin allowed on their licence. An effective means of dealing with illegal operators has been long awaited. The measures will prevent illegal operation and act as a powerful deterrent making all operators work within the goods vehicle operator system.

Those who wish to run bus or coach services must first obtain a public service vehicle operators licence from the relevant traffic commissioner. Under current legislation, however, the traffic commissioner has no powers to refuse to grant a licence on the basis of the adequacy or suitability of an operating centre. There is no requirement that the commissioner should approve such centres. New clause 9 would give him such powers.

I have noted the hon. Gentleman's arguments, but I am not convinced that there is a need to give traffic commissioners powers over public service vehicle operating centres. Under the current arrangements, the matter is largely one for local planning authorities, which have powers to refuse planning permission should they be convinced that the site of a proposed operating centre or access to it is unsuitable.

Local authorities are also responsible for traffic management matters on local roads and have wide powers under the Road Traffic Regulation Act 1984 to make traffic regulation orders to prohibit or restrict particular types of vehicle from using local roads on environmental grounds. They also have powers to impose vehicle restrictions to prevent vehicles over a certain length from using unsuitable roads, as well as powers to introduce controlled parking zones. Local authorities are directly accountable to their local communities and have the proper experience to deal with planning decisions and local transport matters. New clause 19 relates to the goods vehicle operator licensing system. At present, traffic commissioners have wide powers to decide whether an operating centre is suitable for use by the goods vehicles that an operator plans to use from a site. For example, he can decide whether the site is big enough, whether is has safe access and whether it is in an acceptable location in terms of the environment and the residents in the immediate vicinity. In considering those issues, traffic commissioners can take into account the effect of the proposed operating centre only to the point at which the centre or any private road from it joins the public highway. We doubt that there is a case for extending the scope of traffic commissioners' powers to assess the effect of operating centres, because they might not be sufficiently informed to form a judgment about the effect of additional vehicles on the local road network.

Schedule 11 contains provisions on the use of the proceeds of road-user charging and workplace parking levies. Paragraphs 7(2) to (4) specify that authorities can use the proceeds of such schemes for the purpose of directly or indirectly facilitating the achievement of policies in the authority's local transport plan.

Therefore, provided that enforcement activities inside an LEZ were consistent with the objectives of an authority's LTP, there would be nothing to prevent the authority from using the revenue raised from congestion charging and workplace parking levies to fund enforcement activities inside an LEZ.

Subsection (I)(c) would give special constables and traffic wardens new powers to stop vehicles for the purposes of emissions testing. This is perhaps the most difficult part of the new clause. Special constables already have such powers: section 163 of the Road Traffic Act 1988 allows a constable in uniform, and, therefore, a uniformed special constable, to stop a person driving any vehicle or cycling on a road. Traffic wardens, however, have no such powers. In principle, I support the idea that it might be helpful for them to have such powers in limited circumstances—for example, where that would facilitate roadside emissions testing.

The Government have carefully considered the issue. We consulted the police representative organisations a little while ago on the possibility of further extending police powers to civilians. However, this is a sensitive policing issue, and we do not want to make a decision on it in the present context without resolving the wider policy aspects. We need to do more work, including conducting further consultation with police representative organisations and other interested parties. I hope that it will help the hon. Gentleman if I say that we undertake to reflect further on the issue and we hope to be able to resolve it shortly. In deciding how to take matters forward, we will take account of the views of all interested parties. Those include, of course, the police, as well as the Motorists Forum, which is due to make its own recommendations following its meeting on Tuesday of this week.

Subsection (1)(d) relates to local authorities' powers to carry out roadside emissions tests on smoky vehicles. The Committee might be aware that several local authorities have been piloting roadside emissions tests with some success since December 1997. They have been doing so under the Road Traffic (Vehicle Emissions) (Fixed Penalty) Regulations 1997 (S.I. 1997/3058). The new clause would extend a version of that pilot scheme to all authorities. I agree that badly tuned vehicles give rise to local air quality problems, and that more should be done to tackle the issue. After all, all of us, whether as pedestrians, cyclists or motorists, suffer as a result of smoky vehicles belching out fumes. That is why we asked the Motorists Forum to consider how the current pilot scheme might be extended, and we await its recommendations with interest. We will, of course, consult widely on any extension of the scheme.

Finally, subsection (1)(e) would allow authorities to prohibit vehicles from entering LEZs and to penalise those that did. The police already have powers to enforce TROs and could therefore prevent non-„compliant vehicles from entering any LEZ. Local authorities already have powers to issue fixed penalty notices to parked vehicles, and those powers might help to enforce LEZs. However, as in the case of traffic wardens, the issue of extending stop powers to agencies other than the police is sensitive.

I therefore hope that the Committee will agree that, although the principles behind the new clause are to be warmly welcomed, it would be inappropriate to accept it, for the reasons that I have outlined. I therefore urge the hon. Member for Bath to withdraw it.
