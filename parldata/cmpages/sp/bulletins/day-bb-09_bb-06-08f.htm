Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (♦).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at: http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

*S3M-4319 Stewart Maxwell: RIP Cigarettes Cut Fire Deaths—That the Parliament acknowledges that 48.1% of fire deaths in Strathclyde and 44.8% of fire deaths across Scotland in 2007-08 were attributable to smoking materials; recognises the impact of legislation introduced in 2005 in Vermont, United States of America, that introduced reduced ignition propensity (RIP) cigarettes, also called fire-safe cigarettes, that resulted in no deaths being attributed to cigarettes in 2007 and 2008, and therefore believes that Scotland should be the first country in Europe to introduce this life-saving technology to its cigarettes.

*S3M-4317♦ Christine Grahame: The Child Support Agency, A Case for Urgent Reform or Abolition?—That the Parliament condemns the ongoing incompetence of the UK Government body, the Child Support Agency (CSA), noting that, despite several assurances of reform or abolition by UK Government ministers in July 2006, it was only in 2008 that the Child Maintenance and Enforcement Commission was set up to consider options; regrets that it was not until February 2009 that Sir David Henshaw, whose salary for a three-day week is around £234,000 a year, was appointed to head the commission while the CSA continued to cause misery and undue stress to parents in the Scottish Borders and elsewhere through inaccurate calculations of maintenance, over-complicated bureaucracy and an inadequate appeals process; considers that this combination is leading to unnecessary stress and financial hardship for both resident and non-resident parents and families, with a substantial impact on their health and wellbeing and consequently health service provision across Scotland, and believes that any further delay of reform or abolition can only bring more misery and stress.

*S3M-4316 Robin Harper: UN World Ocean Day—That the Parliament celebrates the first official UN World Ocean Day on 8 June 2009; congratulates the film, The End of the Line, based on the novel by Charles Clover, as the first major feature documentary film revealing the impact of overfishing on the world’s oceans; notes with concern the United Nations Food and Agriculture Organization’s estimation that 11 of the world’s 15 major fishing areas are in decline and in need of urgent management and that around half the world’s fish stocks are fully exploited, while another quarter are either over-exploited, depleted or recovering from depletion; expresses specific condemnation that two-thirds of Europe’s fish stocks are estimated to be over-exploited; acknowledges that there has been a five-fold increase in the number of fish taken out of the oceans over the last 50 years; accepts that such levels of fishing are unsustainable and that, without drastic changes in fishing practices and trends, by 2048 all of the world’s fishing stocks could be fully exploited, resulting in mass starvation and ecological devastation, and accepts that there is no room for further expansion of the sea fishing industry.
