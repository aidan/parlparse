Business Bulletin No. 78 / 2004

Monday 10th May 2004

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Current Motions. The full text of all motions lodged the previous week will appear in the Business Bulletin the following Monday.

 

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members' business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S2M-1292# Cathie Craigie: Arrhythmia Awareness Week-That the Parliament congratulates the Arrhythmia Awareness Week partners, STARS, SADS UK, CMA and BCPA, for their work in raising awareness of cardiac arrhythmias and their potentially fatal consequences; notes that the condition occurs when the victim's heart goes into an abnormal rhythm from which it may not recover naturally, can strike at any age and that many people die each year due to suffering a fatal cardiac arrhythmia; further notes that many people fail to be accurately diagnosed and that there is a general lack of awareness amongst the general public and healthcare professionals, and believes that the Scottish Executive should initiate a national education programme so that people who are at high risk of serious adverse events can be identified and treated.

*S2M-1291 Carolyn Leckie: Thiomersal in Vaccines-That the Parliament recognises that thiomersal in vaccines has been banned in Japan, South Korea, Denmark, Sweden, Norway, Finland, Canada and the United States of America, that in 1999 the institute of medicine in the USA declared that it was "biologically plausible" that thiomersal was the cause of neurological problems in children, that the European agency for the Evaluation of Medicinal Products recommended "it would be prudent to remove thiomersal from childhood vaccines", that the World Health Organisation recommended that this be done as soon as practicable and that an alternative thiomersal-free DTP vaccine is available and licensed but not actively promoted; is extremely concerned that thiomersal containing DTP is the predominant vaccine still promoted and administered in Scotland, and believes that it should be withdrawn immediately and that the Scottish Executive should, at least, as is within its remit, issue guidance to NHS boards and all those delivering primary care to ensure that parents are informed of, and offered, thiomersal-free vaccines and make the necessary funding available.

*S2M-1290 Mr Brian Monteith: English-Speaking Union, Juniors Debating Final-That the Parliament congratulates Craigmount High School, Dunblane High School, Kirkcudbright Academy and Webster's High School for reaching the English-Speaking Union Juniors Debating Final from the 88 entering teams; believes that there are many skills to be gained from encouraging debating in schools, including written and oral communication, deduction, reasoning, self-discipline and humility; considers the high level of participation as very encouraging for the future of school debating in Scotland, and wishes all teams to have a lively, enjoyable and informative final debate on the motion, "This House would withdraw British troops from Iraq immediately" at Glasgow University Union on 22 May 2004.

*S2M-1289 Patrick Harvie: Glasgow Homeopathic Hospital - Centre for Integrative Care-That the Parliament acknowledges the unique service offered at the in-patient unit of the Centre for Integrative Care at Glasgow Homeopathic Hospital; recognises the value to patients and to the NHS that the "whole person" integrative approach offers; expresses concern at the suggestion that this service will be closed as a result of funding cuts; believes that such a closure would result in the loss of an effective model of care and a consequent long-term increase in other health care costs, and urges the Scottish Executive to promote the availability of homeopathic care within the National Health Service.

Supported by: Ms Rosemary Byrne*, Elaine Smith*, Rosie Kane*, Tommy Sheridan*

*S2M-1288 Alex Fergusson: ME Awareness Day-That the Parliament notes that Wednesday, 12 May 2004 is ME Awareness Day; applauds the appointment of an ME specialist nurse by NHS Fife; recognises that ME is now accepted as a neurological disease under World Health Organisation ICD-10, rather than as a mental condition; therefore questions why benefits continue to be denied on mental health grounds and why appellants continue to be referred for psychiatric assessment, and regrets the lack of urgency shown by the Scottish Executive in respect of NHS boards' responses to its request for information on ME issues and that, two years after the publication of the Chief Medical Officer's report into ME in England and Wales, the research into, and treatment of, ME in Scotland has failed to make any progress.

*S2M-1287 Susan Deacon: National Condom Week 10 to 16 May 2004-That the Parliament recognises that National Condom Week, 10 to 16 May 2004, makes a significant contribution in raising awareness of the importance of using barrier protection in the prevention of both sexually transmitted infection and unplanned pregnancy; further recognises that the Durex Report 2004 shows that 34% of Scottish people had unprotected sex with a new partner in the last 12 months; expresses concern about the 20% increase in HIV cases in the United Kingdom between 2002 and 2003, and believes that the Scottish Executive's forthcoming sexual health and relationships strategy should address this issue and promote the importance of education in improving sexual health in Scotland.

Supported by: Patrick Harvie*

S2M-1286# Ms Sandra White: Eviction of Asylum Seekers-That the Parliament condemns the recent evictions of failed asylum seekers in Glasgow and notes with concern the comments of David Gormley of the city's asylum seekers' support team that "the only way the council can assist people evicted at the Home Office's request is if they have children or are in need of community care. We have no funding and have made no provision for the housing of people evicted by the Home Office"; further notes that Glasgow Campaign to Welcome Refugees estimates that there are around 170 destitute refugees in Scotland and that this number looks set to rise considerably in response to the current number of evictions, and believes that the Scottish Executive has a duty to contact the Home Office, urging it to rethink its policy of leaving failed asylum seekers destitute.

Supported by: Elaine Smith*, Rosie Kane*, Frances Curran*, Tommy Sheridan*, Carolyn Leckie*

S2M-1285# Cathy Peattie: Minor Title Newspaper Access to Royal Mail Night Flights-That the Parliament notes the emergency resolution passed at the Scottish Trades Union Congress (STUC) calling for steps to be taken to restore access for minor title newspapers to the Royal Mail night flight to Scotland; notes the resolution passed at the conference of the National Federation of Retail Newsagents to fight for the continued distribution of specialist titles; notes the detrimental consequences of the Royal Mail's unilateral decision to cancel the service for the range and diversity of the press available in Scotland, and particularly the loss of two daily newspapers, the Morning Star and the Daily Jang; notes that the cooperatively-owned Morning Star is supported by the STUC and most major trade unions and is relied upon by the trade union movement, while the Daily Jang is Scotland's only daily Urdu language newspaper and caters for Scotland's large Urdu-speaking community; notes that the Royal Mail enjoys a monopoly of night flight landings that are essential for same day distribution, that this monopoly has been associated with its status as a public service and that the withdrawal of access to minor titles represents a breach of this status, and considers that the Scottish Executive should raise these issues, both directly with the Royal Mail and with the Department of Trade and Industry, and press for the urgent restoration of access by the minor titles to Royal Mail night flights to Scotland.

S2M-1284# Janis Hughes: Carers' Week-That the Parliament expresses its admiration for the estimated 600,000 people in Scotland who provide unpaid help and support to a relative or friend who could not otherwise manage because of frailty, illness or disability; is concerned that large numbers of these carers are unknown to the statutory agencies; therefore welcomes Carers' Week, being held this year from 14 to 20 June 2004, including parliamentary events on 15 June, with the theme of carers and health; promotes the principle that carers be treated as key partners in community care and welcomes legislative progress such as the introduction of a right to an independent assessment for all carers including, for the first time, carers under 16; is concerned, however, that two-thirds of carers who provide care for more than 50 hours per week say that their health has been affected by caring; notes that, while carers are frequent users of general practitioners and primary care services, there is much work needed in primary care to identify patients who are carers; further notes that the unpaid work carried out by carers saves the taxpayer a substantial amount of money each year, and considers that there should be provision of further support and services for carers.

Supported by: Mr David Davidson, Carolyn Leckie, John Swinburne, Eleanor Scott, Robert Brown, Linda Fabiani

S2M-1283# Mr Kenneth Macintosh: Recognition of Skin Disease as a Chronic Condition-That the Parliament notes that skin diseases are disfiguring and highly distressing with one-in-four of the Scottish population affected by skin diseases that would benefit from medical treatment; further notes with concern that skin diseases are one of the main reasons for an individual being certified as unfit to work and impact directly on a child's ability to attend school, while accounting for 15% of primary care consultations and 7% of all referrals to hospital out-patient clinics; welcomes the Scottish Innovation and Redesign Collaborative for Dermatology under the auspices of the Centre for Change and Innovation as the first step in the process of giving dermatological services the priority they deserve, and considers that the Scottish Executive should, as the next step, recognise skin diseases as chronic conditions, thereby giving skin conditions and those individuals affected by them the priority and treatment they deserve by assisting them with prescription charges and making access to the most efficacious drugs easier.

S2M-1282# Irene Oldfather: Regulation of Care-That the Parliament expresses its shock and disappointment at the alleged abuse suffered by residents of nursing homes as detailed in recent newspaper reports; expresses concern at the apparent disregard of the appropriate vetting procedures on prospective care workers before commencing employment; notes that there are many private care companies providing care for elderly and disabled people, both in their own homes and in residential care homes; considers that such agencies should ensure that staff in their employment are appropriately trained for the tasks that they undertake; recognises, however, that the vast majority of care workers are suitably qualified and experienced and treat elderly and disabled people with the care, dignity and respect that they are rightly due; acknowledges that the Care Commission is responsible for regulating standards of care, and considers that the Scottish Executive should take measures to increase awareness amongst the general public of the National Care Standards and the Care Commission's role in monitoring adherence to these standards.

S2M-1281# Elaine Smith: Breakthrough Breast Cancer's Decision to Reject Nestlé Deal-That the Parliament congratulates Breakthrough Breast Cancer on the charity's decision to reject a £1 million promotional deal with Nestlé due to ethical concerns surrounding the company's promotion of formula milk in the developing world; recognises that studies indicate that a bottle-fed child living in poverty is up to 14 times more likely to die of diarrhoea and four times more likely to die of pneumonia than breastfed infants; notes that companies such as Nestlé make massive profits from artificial milk which they strive to make as like mothers' milk as possible whilst the manufacturers of the real thing, women, remain amongst the world's poorest and most oppressed; believes that breastfeeding is the best way to feed babies; supports the protection and promotion of breastfeeding, and considers that Nestlé should adhere to the International Code of Marketing of Breastmilk Substitutes as adopted by the World Health *Assembly* in 1981.
