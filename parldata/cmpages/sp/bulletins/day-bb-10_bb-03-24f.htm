Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (♦).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at: http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

*S3M-6051 Bruce Crawford: Public Services Reform (Scotland) Bill—That the Parliament agrees that, during Stage 3 of the Public Services Reform (Scotland) Bill, debate on groups of amendments shall, subject to Rule 9.8.4A, be brought to a conclusion by the time limits indicated, each time limit being calculated from when the Stage begins and excluding any periods when other business is under consideration or when a meeting of the Parliament is suspended (other than a suspension following the first division in the Stage in the morning and afternoon being called) or otherwise not in progress:

Supported by: John Swinney*

*S3M-6050 Bill Wilson: Ethiopia’s Gibe 3 Dam, Europe Must Not Fund Environmental Devastation—That the Parliament is alarmed by reports by Campagna per la Riforma della Banca Mondiale, Counterbalance, Friends of Lake Turkana and International Rivers that the European Investment Bank is considering financing the construction of the Gibe 3 Dam in Ethiopia; notes that these NGOs believe that this dam threatens the land and livelihoods of 500,000 tribal people in Southern Ethiopia and Northern Kenya and that, by ending the river’s natural flood cycle, it would destroy harvests and grazing lands along the river banks and fisheries in Lake Turkana and that it would devastate the unique culture and ecosystems of the Lower Omo Valley and Lake Turkana, both recognised as UNESCO World Heritage Sites; further notes their contentions that the dam violates Ethiopia’s constitution, international conventions, the environmental safeguard policies of international financial institutions and the strategic priorities of the independent World Commission on Dams, that most of the affected people know very little about the project, that the Ethiopian Government is clamping down on dissent and that affected people are not able to express their views or get independent information about the project; calls on the European Union to investigate the truth of these claims and to oppose the dam’s completion, rather than fund it, should even some of these fears and allegations be true, and calls on all interested parties to consider signing the petition at http://www.stopgibe3.org.

*S3M-6049 Hugh Henry: Scottish Huntington’s Association—That the Parliament notes that Huntington’s disease is a devastating, high-risk, inherited, neurological condition that causes a profound form of dementia and mental ill health; commends the hard work done by the Scottish Huntington’s Association based in Paisley in raising awareness of Huntington’s disease; welcomes the joining together of the Scottish Huntington’s Association and the Huntington’s associations of Ireland, England and Wales and Northern Ireland to work collaboratively to raise awareness of Huntington’s disease, and calls on all MSPs to support the efforts of the Scottish Huntington’s Association to redress the awareness deficit.

*S3M-6048 Jackie Baillie: Help for West Dunbartonshire Council—That the Parliament notes with concern the Accounts Commission best value follow-up report on West Dunbartonshire Council; further notes that this is the third such report and that SNP-controlled West Dunbartonshire Council is the only local authority out of 32 across Scotland that has failed to make sufficient progress; understands that the Accounts Commission has raised concerns about the lack of strategic leadership, the continuing poor level of staff morale and difficulties with officer/member relations; further understands that the report highlights the financial position of the council and the lack of reserves, which stand at only £258,000; further notes the report’s conclusion that the council faces significant pressure in achieving operational sustainability and financial stability; believes that the council’s response to these findings has been shockingly complacent, and therefore calls on the Scottish Government to intervene and provide much-needed support for the council in order to protect the interests of the people of West Dunbartonshire and the hard-working council staff.

*S3M-6047 Jamie Stone: Congratulations to Staff at Caithness General’s Baby Unit—That the Parliament congratulates staff at Caithness General Hospital’s baby unit for becoming accredited by the Baby Friendly Initiative, a programme run by UNICEF and the World Health Organisation that promotes breastfeeding and improved care of mothers and babies; recognises the positive impact that the consultant-led maternity unit has on expectant mothers in Caithness who would otherwise endure potentially high-risk journeys to Inverness, and welcomes the news that an offer has been made to strengthen the service of the Caithness unit and to fill the consultant vacancy.

*S3M-6041 Hugh Henry: Congratulations to CWU and Royal Mail—That the Parliament congratulates the Communications Workers Union (CWU) and the Royal Mail on reaching an agreement that it considers can provide stability to Royal Mail and fairness to its employees; hopes that CWU members vote to endorse the deal, which it believes can, alongside the development of a Post Bank, ensure the future of the Post Office; recognises that the issue of the Royal Mail pension fund deficit is still to be resolved, and believes that this should be done without the need to sell off any part of the Royal Mail to the private sector.

*S3M-6040 Willie Coffey: Private Training Providers, Protection of Students’ Fees—That the Parliament notes with concern the impact on students of private training providers when their training provider ceases trading; understands that, in addition to the disruption to their training, many students also lose the course fee, which is usually paid in full at the beginning of the course; expresses particular concern at the case of Advent Consulting Ltd and associated companies, in which thousands of students have been left with no training provider after paying fees in some cases above £5,000; further expresses concern at the activities of funders that provide interest-free loans to pay course fees, which can see students face demands for repayment of over twice the sum borrowed for a course that no longer exists, and considers that there could be merit in a scheme for bonded training providers, similar to ABTA in the travel industry, to ensure that students are not hit by the double whammy of no training and a huge debt.

*S3M-6039 Jackie Baillie: Congratulations to the Good Life: Positive Attitudes Group—That the Parliament congratulates the Good Life: Positive Attitudes Group in West Dunbartonshire for being awarded the Chairman’s Award at the 2010 COSLA awards; understands that the group, which was set up in 2003, is made up of adults with learning disabilities; further understands that the group aims to improve the lives of individuals with learning disabilities who face significant barriers in accessing public services; is aware that the group currently provides unique training, based on group members’ own personal experience, for organisations that deliver public services; considers this to be an innovative and worthy training forum that has been used by a number of organisations across Scotland; recognises the positive and lasting contribution that the Good Life: Positive Attitudes Group has had on the lives of people living with a learning disability; congratulates all those involved, and supports the continued development of this valuable service.

*S3M-6038 Sarah Boyack: Number Shop Cut Doesn’t Add Up—That the Parliament expresses its deep concern at the City of Edinburgh Council’s proposals to close the Number Shop, a project that has helped thousands of adults across Edinburgh improve their numeracy and literacy skills over its 25-year history, as part of a programme of cuts to save £120,000; notes the concerns of the Friends of the Number Shop campaign group that the centre is irreplaceable because of its specialist teaching equipment, ease of disabled access and supportive atmosphere; recognises that the Number Shop’s central location makes it a facility that can be accessed to the benefit of people across the city; further notes the campaign’s assertion that plans to provide the service through other facilities will not result in any savings on staff costs, resulting in only minor savings and thus making the closure pointless; further notes that the Number Shop is one of six community centres and countless other community facilities faced with closure following the passing of the City of Edinburgh Council’s budget, and considers that these closures could have been avoided through Labour amendments that would have seen cuts made to external consultants, unnecessary schemes, non-essential back-office functions and pay freezes for chief officers and those earning more than £40,000 a year.

Supported by: Bill Butler*, Karen Whitefield*, Des McNulty*, Cathy Jamieson*, Trish Godman*, Patricia Ferguson*, John Park*

*S3M-6037 Sarah Boyack: Passport Project Recognised with Kids Count Award—That the Parliament congratulates Access to Industry’s Passport Project on being awarded a special Director’s Award at the Kids Count Inspiration Awards; notes that the awards recognise individuals and organisations that have been responsible for inspiring young people in their communities through practical initiatives that make a positive difference to young people’s lives and afford them greater freedom to determine their own futures; further notes that the award recognises and reflects the motivation and hard work of the young people with whom the project works who sometimes find themselves in difficult circumstances that are beyond their control; further notes that the Passport employability programme supports young people moving on from care and prison backgrounds to overcome barriers back into employment and education by focussing the project on innovation, creativity, commitment and passion to ensure a structured, quality one-to-one service for young people; welcomes Access to Industry’s approach to working with Edinburgh-based colleges and other organisations such as John Lewis and Royal Mail to provide learning opportunities and work placements for young people; further notes that, between January 2007 and February 2010, Passport has worked with a total of 473 young people, with 240 gaining SQA qualifications, 47 securing employment, 78 moving on to college and a further 97 moving on to training programmes, and wishes the project and the young people who use it continued success into the future.

Supported by: Trish Godman*, Ken Macintosh*

*S3M-6036 Ian McKee: In Memory of Sir James Black—That the Parliament mourns the passing of one of Scotland’s greatest sons, Sir James Black, who was born in Uddingston, Lanarkshire, grew up in Fife and studied medicine at the University of St Andrews; recalls that he went on to become one of the greatest scientists of the 20th century, was awarded the Nobel Prize for Medicine in 1988 and the Order of Merit in 2000; pays tribute to a life lived in service to humanity; recognises his scientific endeavour and outstanding contribution to medicine through the invention of propranolol, the first ever beta-blocker, at the time a major medical breakthrough in the treatment of angina and heart disease, which has gone on to save millions of lives worldwide; further recognises that the invention of cimetidine, the first ever H2 antagonist revolutionised the treatment of duodenal ulcers by removing the need for painful and costly surgery; contends that this brilliant man, who remained humble throughout his life despite his many considerable achievements, was indeed one of the finest products of the Scottish educational system; extends its sympathy and offers heartfelt condolences to the family, friends and former colleagues of Sir James Black, and believes that Scotland and the entire world owes him a massive debt of gratitude.

Supported by: Bob Doris*, Hugh O’Donnell*, John Farquhar Munro*, Alasdair Allan*, Sandra White*, Shirley-Anne Somerville*, Rob Gibson*, Kenneth Gibson*, Ken Macintosh*, Ms Wendy Alexander*, Michael McMahon*, Des McNulty*, Stuart McMillan*, Bill Kidd*, Brian Adam*, Robin Harper*, John Park*, Stewart Maxwell*, Mike Pringle*
