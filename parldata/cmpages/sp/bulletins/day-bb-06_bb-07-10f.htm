Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (<>).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at:

http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

New and altered motions and amendments

*S2M-4652 Cathy Jamieson: The Draft Fire (Scotland) Act 2005 (Consequential Modifications and Savings) Order 2006—That the Justice 2 Committee recommends that the draft Fire (Scotland) Act 2005 (Consequential Modifications and Savings) Order 2006 be approved.

Supported by: Hugh Henry*

*S2M-4651 Roseanna Cunningham: Undergrounding the Beauly to Denny Power Line—That the Parliament notes the suggestion by Perth and Kinross Council, the Highland Council, the Cairngorms National Park Authority, the John Muir Trust and Scottish Natural Heritage that certain sections of the proposed Beauly to Denny electricity transmission line be buried underground; notes the work of the Perthshire-based Braco Pylons Campaign which promotes awareness of the positive aspects of undergrounding power lines, including the benefit of decreasing electric and magnetic fields (EMF) exposure limits, and the work of other campaign groups throughout Scotland who want further consideration of the undergrounding option; notes that the Olympic Delivery Authority is spending up to £200 million burying transmission lines in order to clear the site for London’s new Olympic Park for the 2012 Games, and calls on the Scottish Executive not to make any decision on the plans for the Beauly to Denny power line until the option of burying cables underground has been fully explored.

*S2M-4650 Mr Brian Monteith: ESU Scotland Juniors Debating Final—That the Parliament congratulates Dollar Academy, The High School of Dundee, Newcastle Royal Grammar School and Dumfries Academy for reaching the English-Speaking Union (ESU) Scotland Juniors Debating Final from the 88 entering teams; believes that there are many skills to be gained from encouraging debating in schools, including written and oral communication, deduction, reasoning, logic, quick thinking, humour, self-discipline and humility; considers that the high level of participation is very encouraging for the future of school debating in Scotland, and congratulates Dollar Academy on a hard fought victory and, indeed, all teams for a lively, enjoyable and informative final debate on the motion, "This House believes that America has turned its dream into a nightmare," at the Parliament on Saturday 3 June 2006.

*S2M-4649 Brian Adam: Dental Crisis—That the Parliament regrets that a further 3,539 patients are to lose access to NHS dental treatment after yet another dental practice has chosen to go private; notes that in May 2006 there were 13,892 people on the NHS dental waiting list in Grampian and that the new Aberdeen dental centre will have capacity for only an additional 24,000 appointments a year, which is inadequate to deal with the current crisis, and calls on the Scottish Executive to get to grips with this situation and to take measures to resolve the problem that exists in Grampian and throughout Scotland.

Supported by: Ms Maureen Watt*, Ms Rosemary Byrne*

*S2M-4648<> Roseanna Cunningham: Public Inquiries into Wind Farm Proposals in the Ochil Hills—That the Parliament notes the large number of planning applications to construct wind farms in the Ochil Hills, with six consecutive public inquiries scheduled between October 2006 and March 2007; considers that all appropriate expert evidence must be made available to such public inquiries; notes the Deputy Minister for Environment and Rural Development’s parliamentary answer on 8 June 2006 confirming that both Scottish Natural Heritage (SNH) and the Scottish Environmental Protection Agency (SEPA) have adequate resources to make representations to public local inquiries, and considers that SNH, SEPA and Historic Scotland should provide witnesses to all pertinent public inquiries so that the burden of providing evidence to such inquiries does not fall disproportionately on communities.

Supported by: Phil Gallie*, Ms Maureen Watt*, Alex Neil*, Tricia Marwick*, Jim Mather*, Ms Sandra White*

S2M-4643 Margaret Smith: Cash Machines—That the Parliament notes with concern the increasing use of charging cash machines in Scotland; notes research from the Nationwide Building Society which found that UK consumers spent £140 million on cash machine charges in 2004; *further notes the projection by the Nationwide Building Society that by the end of 2007 half of the cash machines in the United Kingdom are expected to charge for transactions*; is concerned that these machines can prevent people having free access to their own money; is particularly concerned that there is a particularly negative impact in deprived areas such as Muirhouse, in Edinburgh, where there are no local bank branches to provide an alternative place to withdraw cash free and where local people are less likely to have credit or debit cards; is concerned that, for individuals on incapacity benefit withdrawing funds three times in one week, charges can amount to 8% of weekly income; supports the conclusions of the Treasury Select Committee’s 2005 inquiry into cash machine charges, and calls for all charging machines to be clearly labelled, for the charges levied by cash machines to be capped at a rate that reflects the cost of transactions, for banks in Scotland to ensure that consumers have access to at least one free cash machine in every community and for the Scottish Executive to consider holding urgent talks with banks in Scotland to tackle the financial inclusion issues that this situation raises.

Supported by: Christine Grahame*
