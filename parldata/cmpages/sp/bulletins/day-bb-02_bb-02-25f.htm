Business Bulletin No. 31/2002

Monday 25 February 2002

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of outstanding motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Outstanding Motions. The full text of all motions lodged the previous week will appear in the Business Bulletin the following Monday.

 

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S1M-2773 Michael Russell: Scottish Place Names Survey—That the Parliament commends the excellent work undertaken by the Scottish Place Names Survey and by Ian Fraser who retired in autumn 2001; notes that the discipline of toponymy is of great benefit to a nation, particularly one which has three indigenous languages and whose place names are influenced by no less than six languages, more than in any other European country; accepts that state funding for toponymy is the norm in most European countries and that toponymists contribute valuable assistance to national and local government and to individuals in matters such as sourcing derivations of place names, suggesting appropriate names for new developments and preserving our cultural and linguistic heritage; agrees with the Principal of Edinburgh University that state funding could be expected for the survey while regretting that continued funding by Edinburgh University is no longer possible, and calls upon the Scottish Executive to provide appropriate financial assistance to the Scottish Place Names Survey to allow it to proceed with the next phase of the Scottish Place Names Database and offer a service to inquirers.

*S1M-2772 Mr Brian Monteith: GB Curling Team Gold Medal at Winter Olympics—That the Parliament congratulates the five Scottish members of the Great Britain curling team, Rhona Martin, Janice Rankin, Fiona MacDonald, Debbie Knox and Margaret Morton, who have won the Gold Medal in the 2002 Salt Lake City Winter Olympics; admires their great determination, perseverance and skill in overcoming the odds and predictions of the bookies and pundits and winning the first Gold medal for Britain in curling since the sport became an Olympic event and the first Gold medal for Britain for 18 years; welcomes the fresh interest shown in the sport, and looks forward to a warm welcome for the team when it returns home and increased participation in curling throughout Britain.

Supported by: Mr David Davidson*

*S1M-2771 Ms Margo MacDonald: Congratulations to Scottish Olympians—That the Parliament, on behalf of people in Scotland, congratulates Rhona Martin, Debbie Knox, Fiona MacDonald, Janice Rankin and Margaret Morton on winning the Women’s Curling Gold Medal at the Winter Olympic Games; thanks them for the quality and style of their ambassadorial role for curling and Scottish sport and sportsmanship and for their positive promotion of Scotland, and that of their team coaches, to a world-wide audience, and urges electricians’ wives everywhere to aspire to the same level of expertise and success in their chosen sport.

*S1M-2770 Michael Russell: Women's Curling Olympic Gold Medal—That the Parliament congratulates the UK Woman’s curling team, their coaches and support staff on their tremendous achievement in winning an Olympic Gold medal and on becoming the first Scots to win Winter Olympic Gold since 1936; admires their professionalism and their generous sportsmanship, and expresses the hope that their example will stimulate renewed interest, official backing and increased investment not just in curling but in other traditional Scottish sports.

Supported by: Mr John Swinney*, Fergus Ewing*

*S1M-2768 Shona Robison: Local Income Tax—That the Parliament notes that the Dundee Conservative Councillor, Neil Powrie, an avowed Thatcherite, has slated his own party’s attitude to the Council Tax; further notes that he has congratulated the self-styled Marxist, Tommy Sheridan of the Scottish Socialist Party, for bringing forward an alternative to the Council Tax; wonders whether this is a sign of the new politics that Scotland was promised following the establishment of the Scottish Parliament, and pledges support for the idea of a Local Income Tax based on the ability to pay.

*S1M-2767 Andrew Wilson: Voting Rights for 16-Year-Olds—That the Parliament recognises that young people are entitled to be treated in a fair, just and consistent manner and are entitled to have the same rights as other citizens; further recognises that adulthood begins at 16 and that young people work, pay tax and have much to contribute to society and politics; notes that the Electoral Reform Society believes that there is a strong body of opinion in favour of votes for 16-year-olds; notes with concern the reduced number of young people voting in elections and also notes recommendation 167 of the Report of the McIntosh Commission on Local Government and the Scottish Parliament, which asks for particular consideration to be given to the possibility of extending the vote in community council elections to 16-year-olds as a first step to extending the franchise in all elections to 16-year-olds, and calls upon the Scottish Executive to make representations to Her Majesty's Government to promote and support any appropriate amending legislation so that in all future elections people will be able to vote from the age of 16.

Supported by: Richard Lochhead*, Brian Adam*, Colin Campbell*, Mr Lloyd Quinan*, Tommy Sheridan*, Ms Sandra White*, Tricia Marwick*, Ms Margo MacDonald*, Irene McGugan*, Mr Kenneth Gibson*, Robin Harper*

*S1M-2766 Bruce Crawford: National Park Funding—That the Parliament recognises the very real concerns of the Association of Community Councils of Loch Lomond and the Trossachs regarding under-funding of Loch Lomond and the Trossachs National Park and calls on the Scottish Executive to reconsider the amount of finance being made available to ensure that the National Park is provided with a sustainable level of resources, bearing in mind the amount recommended by Scottish Natural Heritage of £5.6 million.

*S1M-2764 Christine Grahame: Young People at Risk—That the Parliament notes with concern that the Young People’s Unit at the Royal Edinburgh Hospital had, at 7 February 2002, 88 young people on its waiting list for only 12 beds, nine of which were at that time occupied, with NHS Lothian having access to six beds, NHS Forth Valley having access to two beds each, with two beds for Scotland-wide emergencies; notes that allocation is on the basis of clinical priority which can mean that it takes one or more suicide attempt to be allocated a bed; further notes that it is estimated that one in five young people suffer from some form of mental health problem, and therefore calls upon the Scottish Executive, which purports to put young people at the top of its agenda, to provide more resources to the NHS for mental health so that these many vulnerable young people have the care they require before the situation becomes a crisis.

Supported by: Fiona McLeod*, Mr Adam Ingram*, Mr Lloyd Quinan*, Stewart Stevenson*, Irene McGugan*, Colin Campbell*, Ms Sandra White*, Dorothy-Grace Elder*

*S1M-2763# Margaret Smith: Asylum Centre, RAF Turnhouse—That the Parliament considers that the Scottish Executive should give all possible assistance to Edinburgh City Council in relation to the possible development of an asylum seekers accommodation centre at the former RAF Turnhouse site in West Edinburgh, should do all it can to encourage the Home Office to engage in meaningful and timeous consultation with the council, local residents and elected representatives to allow a full and proper evaluation of the site and of the impact on the local community and on key council services such as housing, law enforcement, education and transport and should acknowledge also the present difficulties in relation to available public housing stock in the City of Edinburgh, and further considers that Scottish ministers should produce a national resettlement framework so that resettlement of asylum seekers from any Scottish centre would involve all areas of Scotland and would ensure distribution across all local authority areas.

*S1M-2762 Fiona McLeod: GM Crop Trials—That the Parliament believes that the GM crop trials underway in Scotland should be discontinued immediately and ploughed up in light of (a) the report by the Royal Society on 4 February 2002, (b) the study by English Nature revealing that genes from separate GM varieties can accumulate in plants that grow from seed spilled at harvest through a process known as "gene-stacking", (c) the recent admission by the Scottish Executive that horizontal gene transfer from GM foods has taken place under laboratory conditions and (d) Her Majesty's Government’s own scientific evidence, as detailed in the Sunday Times of 17 February 2002.

*S1M-2756# Cathie Craigie: Dangerous Driving and the Law—That the Parliament notes the publication of the report by the Transport Research Laboratory, Road Safety Research Report No.26, Dangerous Driving and the Law, which examines the use and application of the law on careless and dangerous driving throughout the UK; further notes the conclusions of the report and, in particular, that (a) the situation exists where those "guilty of serious negligence in their driving face the same charge of Careless Driving as those guilty of very minor offences", (b) maximum penalties are rarely used, (c) limited resources allocated to investigating fatal road traffic accidents affect the quality of the police investigations of them, and (d) the level of care for victims of the bereaved is variable across the UK; draws attention to the report’s recommendations and, in particular, the recommendations that the (i) introduction of a new offence of causing death/serious injury by negligent driving should be introduced and (ii) feasibility of special prosecutors should be investigated; supports the report where it states that in Scotland a "clearer message regarding the seriousness of these offences would be sent to both defendant and society if all causing death by dangerous driving cases were tried in the High Court", and considers that the Scottish Executive should commission specific Scottish research in this area to take on board the report’s conclusions and recommendations and should work with Her Majesty’s Government to consider the need for a change in the law or in its administration in this area if required.

Supported by: Bristow Muldoon*, Mr Kenneth Macintosh*, Mr Frank McAveety*, Mr Michael McMahon*, Helen Eadie*, Johann Lamont*, Mr John McAllion*

Supported by: Stewart Stevenson*
