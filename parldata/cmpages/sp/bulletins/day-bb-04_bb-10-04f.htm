Monday 4 October 2004

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Current Motions. Each Monday the full text of all motions lodged the previous week will appear in the Business Bulletin together with motions previously lodged that are due to be debated that week.

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S2M-1821 Linda Fabiani: Plight of the Saharawi People—That the Parliament notes the plight of the Saharawi people who have been forced out of their land; condemns the actions of Morocco over the last quarter of a century which have resulted in the partitioning of Western Sahara and the expulsion of the Saharawi; acknowledges the humanitarian actions of Algeria and compliments Algeria on the help it has given the Saharawi, and calls on Her Majesty’s Government to use the opportunities afforded to it as a permanent member of the Security Council of the United Nations, and in particular its term as President, to ensure that the referendum on self-determination promised for 1992 takes place as soon as possible and in a manner that ensures freedom of expression and fairness for the Saharawi people and includes the choice of independence.

*S2M-1820 Rosie Kane: Waste Recycling at Holyrood—That the Parliament notes with concern the lack of recycling facilities available within the new Holyrood complex; is further concerned that a new building which accommodates an Executive dedicated to addressing Scotland’s waste problems had no recycling facilities installed as a matter of course, and calls on the Scottish Parliamentary Corporate Body to install proper recycling facilities for all paper, plastic, tin and glass waste as a matter of urgency.

S2M-1814 Christine Grahame: Crisis in Urological Services in the Borders—That the Parliament notes with alarm the terms of the letter dated 23 September 2004 from John S O’Neill, Head of General and Specialist Surgery at the Borders General Hospital (BGH), to Dr Grahame Howard, Chairman of the SCAN Urology Group at the Western General Hospital, Edinburgh which states "In 2001 we (BGH) appointed Ian Mitchell to the Urology Network with the plan that he lead a local service in the Borders, but would also have sessions in Edinburgh to develop his special interest in lower urinary tract cancers … After the first year his in-patient operating would gradually be accommodated in Lothian. Since his appointment there has been no increase in capacity in Lothian … [and that] In the last 18 months we have been forced to send 50 urology patients to the private sector for surgery"; notes that these conditions alone have cost the BGH £200,000; notes that Mr O’Neill goes on to state that "in a recent review of SCAN, GPs’ views of cancer services urology received the lowest rating of all cancer services with the Borders service receiving particular criticism, particularly in relation to waiting times"; further notes that Mr Mitchell has now resigned because of the lack of progress being made and that NHS Borders has been told that it will have no access to any lists or clinics in Lothian and that "We (BGH) are therefore facing a crisis in provision of urological services for the Borders region"; notes that there are currently 121 in-patients awaiting admission for surgery, 30 of whom have waited between six and nine months, that 28 patients are awaiting prostatic biopsies for suspected cancer and that some of those have been waiting more than seven months and that 92 out-patients are awaiting appointments, and asserts that this is wholly unacceptable and that the Scottish Executive should intervene on all these matters so that an acceptable service can be accessed by Borders patients.

S2M-1807# Marilyn Livingstone: National Construction Week, 6 to 13 October 2004—That the Parliament supports National Construction Week from 6 to 13 October 2004 and recognises the many economic advantages for Scotland that an expanding construction industry will bring, notably in employment preservation and creation, training, skills and provision of the built environment; acknowledges the continuing work of the Cross Party Group on Construction which considers and debates all appropriate issues concerning the structure and operation of the construction industry in Scotland as they may affect all relevant stakeholders; supports the industry’s schemes to register construction firms through the Construction Licensing Executive and workers through approved registration schemes, thereby protecting the public by eradicating rogue traders, enhancing the industry’s image and improving the skills base and health and safety practices, and acknowledges that the Scottish Executive, local authorities and other government bodies can assist by supporting registration schemes by insisting that all public works contractors are committed to them, supporting the education and training infrastructure so that it meets Scotland’s construction requirements, acting as an enlightened client by ensuring, where possible, that there are advance programmes of work on which the industry can plan its skills requirements and provide its workforce with security of employment and ensuring that public works contracts are fair, open, effective and straightforward to administer and deliver.

S2M-1806 Linda Fabiani: Military Assistance to Colombia and Human Rights Abuses—That the Parliament notes the Early Day Motion in the House of Commons concerning military assistance and human rights abuses in Colombia and that it has been signed by more than 200 MPs; shares the expressed concern that Colombia is the most dangerous country in the world in which to be a trade unionist with 184 people killed in 2002 for their trade union activities, more than the rest of the world combined; notes that the overwhelming majority of abuses against trade unionists are carried out by paramilitary organisations which have documented links to the Colombian state security forces, and calls on the Scottish Executive to make representations to Her Majesty’s Government seeking a freeze on all military and security assistance to Colombia until the bonds between the state and paramilitary forces are severed, in accordance with the policies of Colombian, UK and international human rights organisations and trade unions, such as the TUC-backed Justice for Colombia coalition and development charity, War on Want.

Supported by: Michael Matheson, Ms Sandra White, Alex Neil

S2M-1785 Rosie Kane: Declaration on the Occasion of the Royal Opening of the Scottish Parliament—That the Parliament calls for an independent Scottish republic built on the principles of liberty, equality, diversity and solidarity; believes that these principles can never be put into practice while Scotland remains subordinate to the hierarchical and anti-democratic institutions of the British state; believes that these principles can be brought about by a freely-elected Scottish government with full control of Scotland’s revenues; believes that the right to self-determination is an inherent right, and not a favour to be granted, whether by the Crown or the British state; believes that sovereignty rests with the people and vows to fight for the right of the people to govern themselves for the benefit of all those living in Scotland today, tomorrow and in future times; further believes that the government of a country is the servant to the people, not master of the people; believes that a written constitution would guarantee, under law, everyone’s right to freely vote, speak and assemble and would guarantee the people’s right to privacy and protection and access to information on all of the government’s actions; vows to fight for the power to refuse to send our sons and daughters to kill and die in unjust wars in foreign lands and to fight for the power to banish nuclear weapons of mass destruction from our land; vows to fight for the power to acquire and restrict the use of property or lands controlled by individuals, corporations or governments beyond Scotland’s borders; vows to fight for the power to build a more equal society, free of poverty, through the redistribution of our vast wealth; vows to fight for the power to protect our soil, seas and rivers for our children and for the generations to come; swears to oppose all forms of national chauvinism, imperialism and racism, to oppose all forms of discrimination on the grounds of gender, ethnic origin, religion, place of birth, age, disability, sexuality or language; aims to fight for an independent Scottish republic in which people may live with dignity and with self-respect, free from exploitation and assuming the responsibilities of free women and men, and which will negotiate freely and as an equal with governments of other lands; affirms that its aim is not to erect walls of separation, but to build an outward-looking Scotland that will extend the hand of friendship to all the peoples of the world, and vows to continue the struggle for a free, democratic Scottish republic for as long as it may take as our fight is for freedom.

Supported by: Carolyn Leckie, Frances Curran, Campbell Martin, Tommy Sheridan, Colin Fox*, Chris Ballance*

S2M-1759 Christine Grahame: Meals on Wheels: A Vital Service—That the Parliament expresses its concern at the withdrawal of the meals on wheels service in the Scottish Borders, replacing it with a fortnightly delivery of frozen food; notes that carers, elderly people, consultants and other nursing staff at the Borders General Hospital Medical Services Directorate’s Department of Medicine for the Elderly have stated that stopping the service will result in a deterioration in elderly care in the Borders; deplores the fact that this practice has been adopted elsewhere in Scotland; notes that, although microwaves and freezers are provided where necessary, there are many frail and elderly who cannot cope with these gadgets, which may result in food being undercooked, running the risk of food poisoning; further notes that vital, often daily, human contact with the volunteers delivering the meals, sometimes the only human contact elderly people have in the day and a contact that at times has been life-saving, will be lost, and therefore calls on the Scottish Executive to require local authorities who have adopted this procedure to reinstate the meals on wheels service to those frail and elderly people who request it, giving them the dignity of choice which all Scottish citizens deserve.

Supported by: Mr Adam Ingram, Richard Lochhead, Brian Adam, Rob Gibson, Linda Fabiani, John Swinburne, Alex Neil, Mr Stewart Maxwell, Michael Matheson, Mr Bruce McFee, Mr Andrew Welsh, Ms Sandra White
