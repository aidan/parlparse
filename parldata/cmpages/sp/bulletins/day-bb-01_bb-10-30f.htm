Business Bulletin No. 154/2001

Tuesday 30 October 2001

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of outstanding motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Outstanding Motions.

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members' business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S1M-2377 Jackie Baillie: Asylum Seekers and Refugee Integration-That the Parliament welcomes the Scottish Executive's commitment to work in partnership with the public, private and voluntary sector to welcome and integrate asylum seekers and refugees to Scotland and further welcomes the Executive's commitment to build improved dialogue with asylum seekers and refugees and host communities and, through partnership working, enable the provision of more accessible, co-ordinated and good quality services, protection from the threat of racist attacks and harassment, and the sharing of good practice to assist the integration of refugees.

Supported by: Ms Margaret Curran*

*S1M-2376 Ms Margo MacDonald: Use of Cannabis-That the Parliament calls on the Scottish Executive to establish a commission of experts and lay persons to examine and report on the social and medical factors affecting the use of cannabis, following the recent announcement by the Home Secretary regarding reclassification of the drug.

*S1M-2375 Michael Russell: Literature on Scottish History, Heritage and Culture-That the Parliament congratulates Professor Michael Lynch, his editorial team, contributors and the Oxford University Press on the publication of The Oxford Companion to Scottish History; notes that 10% of the half million or so words in the companion are devoted to Scots overseas; asserts that this book will provide an indispensable and invaluable addition to the growing academic but accessible literature on Scottish history, heritage and culture, and expresses its gratitude to all those who are working to ensure that the study and practice of these subjects overcomes generations of mainstream neglect, indifference and hostility and, by so doing, contributes greatly to the re-emergence of a confident, outward looking nation which is more than capable of taking its independent place in the world.

*S1M-2374 Dorothy-Grace Elder: Close the Cattle Incinerator at Carntyne, Glasgow-That the Parliament totally opposes dead cattle being incinerated in the built-up area of Carntyne in Glasgow, under the national BSE Surveillance Scheme; believes that such an operation, involving fallen stock at official "high risk" of BSE and which involves the heads of hundreds of animals being removed and the carcasses incinerated before brain stem tests for BSE are returned from laboratories should never take place within a city; is shocked that the incinerator at Carntyne, which is in the built-up area of the East End of Glasgow, next to housing and playing fields and near schools, is undergoing tests prior to re-opening; notes that residents have already been subjected to smoke and smells which caused the Scottish Environment Protection Agency to close the burner temporarily; supports the aims of the BSE Surveillance Scheme in discovering how much BSE remains in British herds; believes that it is intolerable that part of this operation is being inflicted on Glasgow when it could be moved to an area where there is little pollution risk to people, and supports the current united efforts of East End Community Councillors, Glasgow City Councillors and MSPs from all parties who demand that the cow burner at Carntyne is closed permanently and the interests of the local people put first.

Supported by: Tommy Sheridan*
