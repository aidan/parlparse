Business Bulletin No. 82/2002

Monday 20 May 2002

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of outstanding motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Outstanding Motions. Each Monday the full text of all motions lodged the previous week will appear in the Business Bulletin together with motions previously lodged that are due to be debated that week.

 

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members' business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S1M-3133 George Lyon: Water Connection Charges-That the Parliament recognises that tens of thousands of households around rural Scotland are not currently connected to either water or sewerage mains; notes that such households are reliant on private, untreated water supplies and are continuing to discharge raw sewage directly into our rivers and lochs; further notes with concern that there are as yet no clear guidelines relating to the cost of, or criteria for, connecting these households to the new water and sewerage mains currently being installed by the water companies; acknowledges that first time connection charges to the water mains represent a considerable cost for domestic residents, and calls on the Scottish Executive to produce a clear, transparent policy on first time connection criteria and charges for Scottish Water.

*S1M-3132 Margaret Smith: Energywatch "Stop Now!" Campaign-That the Parliament welcomes the energywatch "Stop Now!" campaign which is designed to put a stop to cowboy tactics used by some gas and electricity companies, regrets the increase in unscrupulous sales tactics by such companies which, customers report, can include lying, bullying, misleading requests and intimidation; supports the demands made by energywatch to ensure that the gas and electricity companies' house is put in order by (a) the Office of Gas and Electricity Markets (Ofgem) keeping and strengthening the operating licensing conditions with which the energy companies must comply, (b) Ofgem using its power to fine unscrupulous energy companies, (c) the energy companies establishing a "rogues' register" of sales agencies and individuals who fail to meet acceptable standards, (d) the energy companies paying proper compensation to consumers for any distress and inconvenience caused by their sales malpractices and (e) the establishment of a standard format for energy supply contracts so that consumers are able to understand contracts and compare them with other suppliers; welcomes the commitment of energywatch to work with the police to combat bogus caller crime; urges the Scottish Executive to work in partnership with Scottish energy companies to improve consumer relations, and commends energywatch for raising the profile of such important consumer issues.

*S1M-3131 Roseanna Cunningham: Donation to New Labour From Mr Richard Desmond-That the Parliament deplores the acceptance by New Labour of a £100,000 donation from porn publisher Richard Desmond; believes that it is no coincidence that this followed approval by Her Majesty's Government for Mr Desmond's controversial purchase of Express Newspapers; notes with surprise and disappointment the remarks made by John Reid that New Labour does not exercise "moral judgment" over prospective donors; believes that Mr Desmond's titles are demeaning and degrading to women; notes the academic research proving a link between pornography and violence against women, and calls on New Labour to end its relationship with Richard Desmond and return this donation.

*S1M-3130# Brian Fitzpatrick: Victims of Asbestos at Work-That the Parliament notes the decision of the House of Lords over-ruling the High Court and Court of Appeal in the test cases of Fairchild and others; welcomes the opportunity to acknowledge this historic victory for victims of asbestos-related diseases, their families and trades unions and recognise the crucial role of trade union legal aid schemes in securing justice; notes that it was known as long ago as 1910 that workers who were exposed to asbestos risked injury and death; further notes that the first asbestos regulations were introduced in 1931, that by the 1940s it was known to major employers that asbestos was linked to lung cancers, that by the 1960s it was known to them that exposure to asbestos caused mesothelioma but that workers on the Clyde and elsewhere continued to be exposed to asbestos negligently and in breach of statutory duties throughout the 1970s and into the 1980s, at a terrible cost to them, their families and society; recognises that it was trade unions, including the GMB, UCATT and Amicus, that spoke up for their members and took on powerful and wealthy forces, including insurance companies who, to their eternal shame, adopted tactics that spun out legal claims to which there was no legal answer wilfully and in expectation of the victims dying; notes the work of our trades unions and their allies across the labour movement who campaigned to change the law on damages and who are working now to speed up claims and secure fair compensation; believes that the Scottish Executive and MSPs should ensure that the needs of asbestos victims and their families and carers are addressed across all Executive departments and across the responsibilities of the committees of the Parliament, and urges renewed effort in order to secure speedy resolution and fair compensation for asbestos victims. S1M-3126# Christine Grahame: Re-opening of Reston Railway Station-That the Parliament supports the campaign by RAGES for the re-opening of a railway station at Reston in Berwickshire, the former station having closed in 1964; notes that many Borderers currently commute by car to either Berwick or Dunbar to then travel citywards by train; further notes that there is agricultural land available for a new-build station and parking and that for a small sum, in the order of £10,000, a feasibility study into the re-opening of a station at Reston could be undertaken, and considers that the Scottish Executive should commission such a study now.

Supported by: Brian Adam, Mr Kenneth Gibson, Fergus Ewing, Colin Campbell, Michael Matheson, Bruce Crawford, Michael Russell, Roseanna Cunningham, Fiona McLeod*

S1M-3125# Johann Lamont: National Carers' Week, 10-16 June 2002-That the Parliament notes that 10-16 June 2002 has been designated as National Carers' Week and welcomes the opportunity to acknowledge and celebrate the crucial role of carers within the Glasgow Pollok constituency, in Glasgow and across Scotland; recognises the central role of carers in providing support for people who are frail or who have a disability which otherwise would cost social and health care services in Scotland in excess of £5 billion and, as shown in recent research by Carers Scotland, in contributing to the broader life of local communities; accepts that the demands of caring can often lead to social exclusion; invites the Scottish Executive and MSPs to ensure that the role of carers is addressed across all Scottish Executive departments and across the responsibilities of the committees of the Parliament, and asks all those with a statutory responsibility to consult and engage with carers so as to do everything in their power to ensure that the expertise, knowledge and commitment of carers are harnessed in the important work of securing social justice for carers and those for whom they care.

Supported by: Karen Whitefield, Bill Butler*

S1M-3124# Bruce Crawford: Closure of Longannet Deep Coal Mine-That the Parliament regrets the closure of Scotland's last deep coal mine at Longannet in Fife; is deeply disappointed that over 500 mining jobs were lost and that Scotland has also lost its most valuable low sulphur coal reserve; is concerned that the pit flooded at a time when Scottish Coal (Deep Mine) Ltd was experiencing financial and production difficulties and that the reasons for the flooding have not yet been adequately explained; is further concerned that, despite the substantial public investment, in excess of £45 million, inadequate steps were taken by Her Majesty's Government to protect the public's investment in the mine, save the jobs or retain this valuable energy resource for the nation, and therefore believes that the Scottish Executive should carry out a full public inquiry to determine how the flooding was allowed to take place that resulted in the mine's closure.

Supported by: Fiona McLeod

S1M-3123 Brian Adam: Family History Societies Support-That the Parliament recognises the excellent work being done by the many volunteer genealogists within the network of family history societies throughout Scotland, despite facing problems of unsuitable accommodation and lack of funding and support, and asks the Scottish Executive to consider how it might best support this network as part of the geneaology tourism niche market strategy.

Supported by: Stewart Stevenson*

S1M-3122 Mr Brian Monteith: UNISON Campaign to Avoid Cuts in Public Spending-That the Parliament welcomes the new campaign launched by UNISON, the UK's largest trade union, against the United Kingdom joining the European Single Currency and notes its concern that joining the euro would be a disaster for public services if public spending had to be cut to comply with European Central Bank spending limits.

Supported by: Alex Johnstone, Lord James Douglas-Hamilton

S1M-3121 Mr Brian Monteith: Economic Independence of Portugal-That the Parliament notes the abandonment by the Portugese Government of its economic agenda due to the threat of potential fines and a reduction in European Union development subsidies if it were to breach the tight restrictions of the Stability and Growth Pact which are required as a member of the euro currency and further notes that VAT will rise from 17% to 19%, civil service recruitment has been halted, 30 publicly funded institutions are to close and the independent Government of Portugal has withdrawn its plans to reduce corporation tax which was planned to help kick start its low growth economy.

Supported by: Alex Johnstone

S1M-3114# Ms Sandra White: Glasgow Trongate Regeneration-That the Parliament pledges its full support for the campaign launched by traders in Glasgow's Trongate for the regeneration of the area; notes with concern that the area has suffered from neglect and decline in recent years whilst other parts of the city have benefited from regeneration; supports the demands of the traders for steps to be taken for the area's regeneration, including a new railway station at Glasgow Cross as part of the Glasgow CrossRail scheme, and considers that the Scottish Executive should ensure that measures are put in place to achieve the area's regeneration.

Supported by: Michael Matheson, Dorothy-Grace Elder

S1M-3113# Karen Whitefield: Diabetes Week 9-15 June 2002-That the Parliament welcomes Diabetes Week from 9-15 June 2002 and commends Diabetes UK on its hard-hitting campaign, "Diabetes - possible to deal with, deadly to ignore", which underlines the seriousness of diabetes in that, according to research funded by Diabetes UK, people with diabetes are nearly twice as likely to die prematurely as people without the condition; recognises the implications for public health and health education of the recent diagnosis of Type 2 diabetes in children, and believes that the necessary steps should be taken to ensure that people in Scotland, including children, have access to information on diabetes, are made aware of the seriousness of the condition and are supported to adopt healthy lifestyles.

Supported by: Stewart Stevenson, Dorothy-Grace Elder, Robert Brown, Mr Mike Rumbles*

S1M-3104# Ms Sandra White: Community Police Officers-That the Parliament recognises the extremely effective role played by community police officers in combating crime, particularly youth crime; notes that such officers are often the most visible and recognisable police presence in their communities and recognises the crucial role that they play in providing high quality policing and fostering links with the communities they serve; regrets that these officers are often removed from their vital community role to perform other police duties, and therefore believes that the Scottish Executive should encourage community policing and provide the necessary resources to ensure the provision of adequate numbers of community police officers.

Supported by: Michael Matheson, Dorothy-Grace Elder, Robert Brown

S1M-3101# Bill Butler: Local Authority Funding-That the Parliament commends the initiative of Glasgow City, Argyll and Bute, Dundee City, Inverclyde, Midlothian and West Dunbartonshire councils in commissioning a special report from Professor Arthur Midwinter, Grant Distribution and the Mismatch Effect: An Assessment of its Impact on Council since Reorganisation, which concludes that there is "clear evidence of the erosion of grant income" between 1996-97 and 2001-02 in these local authority areas; acknowledges the report's view that the flawed reorganisation of local government ordered by the last Conservative government is the root cause of a situation where, in these six local authority areas in the period, government grants fell by 4.8% in real terms compared to an average drop of 1.2% across Scotland's councils and council tax rose by 20% in real terms compared to an average of 16%, and believes that the Scottish Executive should seriously consider accepting the central recommendation of the report, namely that it should provide a supplementary grant of £50 million to redress this historical mismatch of funding: £32 million to Glasgow, £5 million to Dundee, £3.4 million each to Midlothian and Argyll and Bute and £2.5 million each to West Dunbartonshire and Inverclyde respectively, such funding arising from a more equitable distribution of grant increases in the future.

Supported by: Kate Maclean, Jackie Baillie, Trish Godman, Robert Brown

S1M-3100 Shona Robison: Scottish Local Government Funding Crisis-That the Parliament notes the publication of the report Grant Distribution and the Mismatch Effect: An Assessment of its Impact on Councils since Reorganisation by Professor Arthur Midwinter; recognises the report's conclusions that Argyll and Bute, Dundee City, Glasgow City, Inverclyde, Midlothian and West Dunbartonshire councils have suffered due to the reorganisation of local government to the tune of £50 million per annum; further recognises that this additional grant would allow an average reduction in council tax of £112, and calls on the Minister for Finance and Public Services to take account of this financial injustice in advance of the local government settlement for 2003-04.

Supported by: Michael Matheson, Tommy Sheridan

S1M-3099# Pauline McNeill: Funding Mismatch for Local Authorities-That the Parliament notes the erosion of grant income resulting in a severe funding mismatch for six local authorities, including Glasgow City Council, as identified in a specially commissioned report by Professor Midwinter of Strathclyde University; further notes that the mismatch occurred as a direct result of local government reorganisation in 1996 following which grant income fell 4.6% for these local authorities against a Scottish average drop of 1.2%; expresses concern at the direct affect that these budgetary

restraints have had on council tax payers with a 20% increase in the affected authorities against a Scottish average of 16%; recognises that Glasgow City Council and the other five councils have since operated at a financial disadvantage; supports the call of the six local authorities and Professor Midwinter for restoration of £50 million, £32 million going to Glasgow, and considers that the Scottish Executive should rectify this mismatch immediately and ensure equitable distribution of future grant increases.

Supported by: Johann Lamont, Mr Jamie Stone, Tommy Sheridan, Mr Duncan McNeil, Jackie Baillie, Robert Brown
