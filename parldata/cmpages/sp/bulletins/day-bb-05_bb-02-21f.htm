Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at:

http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

New and altered motions and amendments

*S2M-2444# Frances Curran: Protect Public Sector Pensions—That the Parliament notes that the Westminster Government proposes to compulsorily raise the retirement age from 60 to 65 for all public sector workers; notes that this will result in a reduced pension for workers retiring before the age of 65; further notes that this has occurred at the same time as MPs and MSPs have enhanced their own pension rights out of the public purse; considers strongly that the Westminster Government should not take this action which will reduce the living standards of working people in their retirement, and expresses its full support for the public sector trade unions in their campaign to defend jobs, conditions and services.

Supported by: Carolyn Leckie*, Rosie Kane*, Tommy Sheridan*, Colin Fox*, Ms Rosemary Byrne*

*S2M-2443# Tommy Sheridan: Tribute to Helen MacFarlane—That the Parliament notes the forthcoming launch of the book, Helen MacFarlane: A Feminist, Revolutionary Journalist and Philosopher in mid-nineteenth century England; welcomes the fact that this radical Scotswoman will at last be rescued from obscurity and given her place of importance in 19th century politics and political movements, including the Chartists and the Vienna uprising of 1848; further notes that it was Helen MacFarlane, under the alias Howard Morton, who first translated the seminal pamphlet, The Communist Manifesto, into English for the magazine, The Red Republican, and that she played an active role in promoting the politics of revolution and equality throughout her life, and believes that the Scottish Parliament Information Centre should order several copies of her book and that libraries across Scotland should be encouraged to do likewise.

Supported by: Mark Ballard*, Rosie Kane*

*S2M-2442 Pauline McNeill: Trafficking in People—That the Parliament applauds the action by Amnesty International and Anti-Slavery International against trafficking in people; notes that every year thousands of women, children and men fall victim to trafficking for the purposes of sexual or other exploitation, both within and over European borders; further notes the Council of Europe draft Convention on action against trafficking in human beings; acknowledges the view of the Council of the European Parliament that "this phenomenon has reached such unprecedented levels that it can truly be called a new form of slavery"; believes that the human rights of trafficked persons must be fully protected and that steps taken by European member states to criminalise trafficking and prosecute traffickers must be accompanied by the protection and support of the rights of trafficking victims, and supports the Scottish Executive funding for Glasgow City Council to begin to explore the emerging issue of trafficking in women for the purpose of commercial sexual exploitation, to ascertain the extent of trafficking in Glasgow and to develop appropriate services to assist women to flee their traffickers and recover from their experiences.

*S2M-2441 Mr Brian Monteith: National Pubs Week—That the Parliament acknowledges that National Pubs Week, organised by CAMRA, the Campaign for Real Ale, will be held between 19 and 26 February 2005; recognises that it is designed to encourage more people to visit pubs throughout the week and celebrate responsibly British and Scottish pubs; notes that, according to figures from the Scottish Beer and Pub Association, the beer and pub industry in Scotland employs 150,000 people in over 5,000 public houses; acknowledges that, UK-wide, eight out of 10 adults count themselves as pub-goers, that 12% of Scottish adults visit a pub at least once a week and that over 80% of pubs are small businesses run by tenants, lessees and owners, and wishes National Pubs Week success in achieving its aims.

Supported by: Mr David Davidson*, Alex Johnstone*, Mark Ballard*, Mr Ted Brocklebank*, Scott Barrie* R

*S2M-2440 Stewart Stevenson: Microcredit—That the Parliament applauds the growth in the microcredit movement which was started in 1976 when Professor Muhammad Yunus lent 26 dollars to a group of 42 Bangladeshi workers to finance a day’s work; notes that defaults are infrequent; supports the movement as a key way of empowering the excluded around the world, especially women, and calls on world leaders to include discussions at the forthcoming G8 summit in Scotland on how they may help the movement and directly assist individuals to participate in and contribute to the growth of their local economy.

*S2M-2439 Rhona Brankin: The Food Protection (Emergency Prohibitions) (Amnesic Shellfish Poisoning) (West Coast) (No. 2) (Scotland) Order 2005—That the Health Committee recommends that the Food Protection (Emergency Prohibitions) (Amnesic Shellfish Poisoning) (West Coast) (No. 2) (Scotland) Order 2005 (SSI 2005/69) be approved.

Supported by: Mr Andy Kerr*

*S2M-2438 Brian Adam: National Science Week - 11 to 20 March 2005—That the Parliament notes that National Science Week takes place between 11 and 20 March 2005; notes that this year is designated "Einstein Year" in celebration of the fact that, 100 years ago at the age of 26, Albert Einstein published important scientific papers on Brownian Motion, the Photoelectric Effect and Special Relativity; welcomes the opportunity National Science Week gives people throughout Scotland to celebrate the work of the young Einstein and other scientists, participate in science activities and experiments and engage in science discussions; congratulates the British Association for the Advancement of Science (the BA) for co-ordinating National Science Week which last year attracted hundreds of thousands of people across the UK, and encourages MSPs to visit the BA website (www.the-ba.net/nsw/) and join in activities in their constituency or region.

*S2M-2437 Ms Rosemary Byrne: Clocks in the Chamber—That the Parliament considers that the Scottish Parliamentary Corporate Body should review the positioning of clocks in the chamber by seeking the views of members in respect of whether they are able to see the clocks from all positions in the chamber and that where the members indicate "blind spots" the matter should be resolved.

*S2M-2436 Christine Grahame: Funding for Free Personal Care—That the Parliament notes with concern the terms of a recent report produced by the Fraser of Allander Institute, Free Care of the Elderly, by Margaret and Jim Cuthbert which identifies, inter alia, that the Scottish Executive failed to identify the number of disabled persons aged over 65 in private households and that the Care Development Group’s (CDG) estimate of 142,000 fell short of the Cuthberts’ estimated figure of 350,000 based on Department for Work and Pensions’ research and figures from the Registrar General for Scotland, that the effect of this error increases the required allowance by at least £50 million per annum, that the CDG work underpinning the costing wrongly assumed that 45% of provision made for elderly in their homes by local authorities related to personal care whereas the Cuthberts believe that the figure should have been around 60% implying an additional £3 million in costs per annum and that, therefore, the overall cost has been underestimated by some £60 million; further notes that the Executive failed to secure a clawback of some £20 million in saved benefits from Westminster which would have provided additional funding for the programme, and seeks reassurances in all circumstances that free personal care for the elderly will be properly funded so that the frail elderly can feel secure and supported in their own homes.

*S2M-2435 Robin Harper: Kyoto Climate Change Protocol Into Force—That the Parliament welcomes the entry into force of the Kyoto Protocol to the United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change on 16 February 2005; recognises that this is an important step towards reducing the global pollution that causes climate change; condemns the US Government for its refusal to sign up to the Kyoto Protocol; requests that the First Minister does all in his power at the G8 summit in Scotland this year to challenge the United States’ refusal to act on climate change, including raising the matter with the Prime Minister; notes, however, that Scotland lags behind the rest of the United Kingdom in reducing carbon dioxide emissions and believes that the Scottish Executive must take urgent steps towards ensuring that these emissions are reduced, particularly ahead of the G8 summit, and supports the Scottish Environment LINK’s "Everyone" campaign to reduce emissions causing climate change.

Supported by: Shiona Baird*, Stewart Stevenson*, Tommy Sheridan*, Ms Rosemary Byrne*, Mr John Swinney*, Bruce Crawford*, Mark Ballard*, Chris Ballance*, Mr Mark Ruskell*, Elaine Smith*, Rosie Kane*

*S2M-2434 Christine Grahame: Family Mediation Borders—That the Parliament notes the concerns of Family Mediation Borders regarding the proposed funding arrangements underpinning the Family Law (Scotland) Bill which the service considers will cause it serious financial difficulties resulting in, at least, the cutting back of its core service provision and, at worst, closure; notes that Family Mediation Borders assisted 550 parents and children in the last year; further notes that, apart from the human benefit of mediated settlement with regard to contact and residency for children, there must inevitably be savings to the public purse in legal aid and other costs, such as shrieval time, because of successful mediated settlement; notes also the provision by Family Mediation Borders of contact facilities, particularly important in a sprawling rural area with poor public transport links, and urges the Scottish Executive to retain the current funding arrangements and expand such local family mediation services which will be even more invaluable with the proposed changes in family law. R

Supported by: Phil Gallie*

*S2M-2430 Rosie Kane: Victory for McLibel Two—That the Parliament congratulates Helen Steel and David Morris, known as the McLibel Two, after their epic three-year battle with McDonald’s, who have won their appeal at the European Court of Human Rights for equality of resources and legal representation, as well as being awarded £24,000 in compensation; celebrates the bravery and determination of the two in taking one of the world’s most powerful corporations to court, even though they had no legal representation and no money, for the right to be able to publicise what they felt to be the truth about McDonald’s; notes that McDonald’s spent £10 million on the original trial which they won against Steel and Morris’ awareness-raising leaflets, and hopes that the European Court’s decision will encourage more anti-globalisation campaigners to take on the world’s corporations, knowing that they have a right to a level playing field in terms of legal aid and representation.

Supported by: Frances Curran*, Carolyn Leckie*, Chris Ballance*, Mark Ballard*, Tommy Sheridan*, Rob Gibson*, Elaine Smith*, Mr Jamie Stone*, Shiona Baird*, Mr Mark Ruskell*

That is due to the fact that you have a bloated public sector and Scotland generally has suffered economically as a result. That is not going to change and even if we were to allow unlimited immigration, immigrants will still be going to settle in the south-east of England until Scotland gets its economic act together"; further welcomes his comments in The Scotsman on Thursday 10 February 2005 that "The fact that Scotland can offer a high quality of life makes it all the more frustrating that it should suffer population loss"; understands that his comments on "Good Morning Scotland" were specifically referring to the state of the Scottish economy and condemns Labour and SNP politicians for deliberately quoting these comments out of context; notes that estimates of migration to UK regions in 2001 show that a mere 4% of immigrants chose to settle in Scotland, compared with 41.5% in London; further notes that, according to the Registrar General, Scotland experiences a net loss of people aged 16 to 34 to the rest of the United Kingdom, and concludes that, in light of the fact that Scotland was ranked 10 places behind the United Kingdom in the IMD World Competitiveness Yearbook for the "extent to which government policies are conducive to competitiveness", the economic policies pursued by the Scottish Executive are responsible for Scotland’s low levels of immigration."
