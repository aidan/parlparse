Business Bulletin No. 160/2004

Monday 8 November 2004

Section F : Motions and Amendments
  

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site as Current Motions. Each Monday the full text of all motions lodged the previous week will appear in the Business Bulletin together with motions previously lodged that are due to be debated that week.

Items marked with an asterisk (*) are new or have been altered. Asterisks in the text show the extent of alterations made.

Motions which members wish to be considered for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#)

*S2M-1968 Elaine Smith: Breastfeeding etc. (Scotland) Bill—That the Parliament agrees that the Breastfeeding etc. (Scotland) Bill be passed.

*S2M-1967 Chris Ballance: Shifting Expenditure from Military Spending to Working for Peace—That the Parliament notes that "it is highly likely that the armed forces will, in future, be more frequently employed on peace support and counter-terrorist operations where the focus will be on conflict prevention and stabilisation, rather than the defeat of opposing forces" (Delivering Security in a Changing World - Defence White Paper - December 2003) and that "No conventional military threats to the UK are likely to emerge over the period to 2030" (The Future Strategic Context for Defence, Ministry of Defence Paper); believes that, to help create peace, Scotland must invest in, and prepare for, peace by shifting economic and human resources away from investment and preparation for war and towards preparation for peace; supports and commends work on projects such as that by the Scottish Centre for Nonviolence to develop a pool of well-trained civilian peacemakers who would have the requisite skills and training to serve in conflict areas, and urges significantly-increased spending of taxpayers’ money on conflict prevention, including retraining military personnel, from 2.3% of the Ministry of Defence’s budget in order to achieve the joint Ministry of Defence, Department for International Development and Foreign and Commonwealth Office Public Service Agreement goal of "improved effectiveness of the UK contribution to conflict prevention".

*S2M-1966 Mr Andrew Welsh: Angus Partnership on Domestic Abuse—That the Parliament commends Angus Council for its role in the creation of the Angus Partnership on Domestic Abuse, which instigated the Tayside Domestic Abuse Initiative, a working partnership that benefits the communities of Scotland, as highlighted in the Scottish Executive’s debate on domestic abuse services on 4 November 2004.

S2M-1965 Ms Rosemary Byrne: Low Pay in the South of Scotland—That the Parliament notes the Office for National Statistics’ annual survey of hours and earnings which shows that the gross average weekly wage for a British worker is now £421 a week, whilst the average wage in the Scottish Borders Council area is the lowest in Scotland at £299 a week; condemns the economic policies that have fostered the creation of a low-wage economy in the south of Scotland and the misery that poverty pay brings to families and communities, and calls for investment in the infrastructure in the region, equal opportunity to access education and training and the creation of skilled, higher-paid employment opportunities.

Supported by: Carolyn Leckie*, Frances Curran*

S2M-1964 Frances Curran: NHS Argyll and Clyde Meetings—That the Parliament is appalled that NHS Argyll and Clyde has run roughshod over local democracy during its recent consultation on its clinical review of services; believes that this approach is reinforced by the Chief Executive of the board moving to implement the proposals before either the board or the minister have taken any decisions; further believes that the board has demonstrated this approach by restricting public access to its meetings by allowing space for only 100 members of the public in an area which covers over 400,000 people; considers that, as NHS boards decide on the allocation of millions of pounds of public money, they should ensure that all members of the public who wish to should be able to scrutinise board meetings, especially where crucial decisions are being made, and believes that it is now clear that the only outcome of this review of services that will be acceptable to local communities is the retention of Vale of Leven Hospital and Inverclyde Royal Hospital.

Supported by: Carolyn Leckie*

S2M-1963 Colin Fox: Congratulations on a Progressive Victory in the US Elections—That the Parliament offers its congratulations on a progressive victory in the US elections; welcomes the re-election of Bernie Sanders to the House of Representatives for the state of Vermont; notes his re-election to Congress on a progressive democratic socialist programme for the fifth consecutive time; recognises that he is the only member of that body not aligned to the Democrat or Republican parties, and sends Congressman Sanders its warmest congratulations and hopes that his victory gives encouragement to all socially-progressive forces and peoples in the United States of America at this time.

Supported by: Carolyn Leckie*, Frances Curran*

S2M-1962# Colin Fox: Privatisation of our Prisons—That the Parliament notes plans to build two new prisons in Scotland; believes that they should both be publicly-owned and run; further believes that making money out of the incarceration of offenders is both immoral and a blight on our justice system, and considers that the Scottish Prison Service should ensure that both contracts to run these new prisons remain in the public sector.

S2M-1953 Dr Sylvia Jackson: Motion on Enforcement of EU Directive 95/29/EC Amending Directive 91/628 on the Welfare of Live Animals During Transport—That the Parliament recognises that the full enforcement of EU directive 95/29/EC amending directive 91/628/EEC on the welfare of live animals during transport is essential for the protection of animal welfare; recognises that the UK Welfare of Animals (Transport) Order 1997 is generally observed and enforced; notes that, following the breakdown of talks on the issue of live animal transport in April, the Dutch Presidency of the European Commission intends to introduce a compromise proposal on transport, with an emphasis on enforcement, driver training and more flexibility for individual member states to set stricter regulations for domestic and sea transport; notes that certain member states fail to enforce the requirements of the current directive consistently, and calls on the Scottish Executive to express support for the Dutch Presidency’s proposal at the meeting of the European Commission Agriculture Council on 22 and 23 November 2004.

S2M-1951 Pauline McNeill: Teenage Kicks - A Tribute to John Peel for Children in Need—That the Parliament mourns the passing of John Peel, widely regarded as singularly the most important DJ in radio history; notes that, since the 1960s, his show has been listened to by generations of music fans and musicians and that it was the one place independent music could be heard irrespective of marketing power or hype; believes that Peel was a taste-maker and trend-setter, championing punk, techno and introducing his audience to music that challenged artistic and commercial values; applauds the open door Peel provided for a flood of new talent, offering sessions to many Scottish artists as diverse as Belle and Sebastian, Degrassi, Primal Scream, Ivor Cutler, the Delgados, AC Acoustics, Bis, Mogwai, Travis, Boards of Canada, Aereogramme, Arab Strap, the Aphrodisiacs, the Jesus and Mary Chain and was instrumental in their gaining artistic or commercial success; notes that, more significantly, John Peel made bands feel important, loved new music and had the independent strength of character to resist being told what to put on his play-list; believes that his warmth and compassion will be truly missed and were such that, on occasion, he would even invite young, struggling bands to his home and cook for them; further believes that John Peel was the true spirit of free, independent radio, that he played what he loved, irrespective of commercial considerations, and that it would be tragic if this legacy of freedom was not handed down by the BBC to another presenter, and considers that MSPs should support the efforts of New Music In Scotland (NEMIS) and bands and supporters from all over Scotland to put together John Peel tribute gigs in the days running up to 19 November 2004 for Children in Need.

Supported by: Donald Gorrie, Scott Barrie*, Christine May*, Margaret Smith*, Kate Maclean*, Mr Mark Ruskell*, Mike Pringle*, Bruce Crawford*, Patrick Harvie*, Robin Harper*, Margaret Jamieson*

S2M-1536.3# Bristow Muldoon: Better Access to Small Claims and Summary Cause Procedures—As an amendment to motion (S2M-1536) in the name of Mr John Home Robertson, insert at end "recognises, however, that there is no case for increasing the limits in relation to personal injury cases and that they, therefore, should be excluded from any increase; considers that, in line with victims’ present rights, all personal injury cases with a value of £1,500 or more should be capable of enjoying the benefits of the dedicated personal injury procedure at the Court of Session without any penalty in relation to expenses; recognises that, unless personal injury cases are excluded from any increase, this would represent a substantial restriction in rights and access to justice for victims of accident and disease and would threaten the viability of trade unions’ legal services, and acknowledges that excluding personal injury cases would be in line with the position in England where personal injury cases with a value in excess of £1,000 are excluded from the small claims limit of £5,000, thereby ensuring that Scottish victims of accident and disease are not at a disadvantage as compared to English victims and are, therefore, not prejudiced by reason of geography as would be the case if personal injury actions are not excluded."

Supported by: Margaret Jamieson*
