Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (<>).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an “R”.

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at: http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

*S2M-3544 Frances Curran: First Bus Puts Profits Before People—That the Parliament is concerned at First Bus withdrawing bus services in outlying areas of Scotland against the wishes of the local communities; notes that the withdrawal of bus services in the Twechar area, which was not communicated to the local community until the last minute, has left some people with no means to get to their work; is appalled at the arrogant attitude of First Bus management, based in Glasgow, that they do not have to provide a service on any route, that it is well within their rights to cut a service if it is not profitable and that the view of the local community is irrelevant; believes that First Bus’s assertion in its code of business ethics that it will conduct its business “with honesty, integrity and openness” is an empty statement; further believes that, in future, operators’ licences should only be issued when there is a guarantee that certain bus routes within the operating area will be maintained, and notes that we are now seeing the real effect of privatisation of public transport, where bus operators are more concerned with their shareholders’ profits than providing good bus services, can cherry pick the profitable routes and, in this case, force Strathclyde Passenger Transport (SPT) to use public money to pay for an inadequate and reduced service on the routes which First Bus has cancelled which, to add insult to injury, means that SPT is giving the money to First Bus to run these reduced services.

*S2M-3543 Richard Lochhead: Energy Policy—That the Parliament considers it unacceptable that in energy-rich Scotland fuel poverty blights our society; notes with concern that, despite Scotland possessing massive energy resources, urgent action is required to avoid an energy gap in the next decade; calls for the development of an energy policy for Scotland that will deliver security of supply, affordability, self-sufficiency and energy efficiency; recognises that the transmission charges for power generators, as permitted by the UK Government, will undermine these objectives; rejects nuclear power in favour of an energy mix that includes making Scotland the world leader in renewable energy and carbon capture and storage technology; believes that such a policy will boost our economy and meet our environmental obligations, and recognises that the Scottish Parliament requires the necessary energy powers and control over our oil and gas resources to deliver the nation’s energy needs in the 21st century.

*S2M-3542 Campbell Martin: Teaching of Scottish History—That the Parliament notes with concern media reports that the Minister for Education and Young People, Peter Peacock, favours teaching history as part of other subjects, such as modern studies; considers that this would be a retrograde step and that, instead, the Scottish Executive should ensure that the teaching of the history of Scotland should be a specific core element of the general education of every pupil in Scottish schools; further considers that Scottish history is the retelling of the stories that shaped our nation; agrees with the view of Nigerian writer, Ben Okri, that “to poison a nation, poison its stories”, and considers that diluting the teaching of Scottish history or incorporating it into another subject would be to poison Scotland’s stories.

*S2M-3539 Fiona Hyslop: Scottish History—That the Parliament notes with serious concern the proposals by the Scottish Executive to make history history in S1 and S2; asserts the importance of children in Scotland having a firm understanding and appreciation of Scottish heritage, culture and history; recognises and supports creative ways to weave this into every subject in the curriculum providing a Scottish perspective; rejects, however, any attempt to remove the stand-alone subject of history from S1 and S2 in schools in Scotland, and regrets this suggestion by any Scottish education minister, reflecting that such a move would not be even countenanced in other countries who have a pride in promoting their nation’s history.

*S2M-3538 Ms Rosemary Byrne: History Teaching in Secondary Schools—That the Parliament believes that far too little Scottish history is currently taught in Scottish schools and that this leaves young Scots with little or no appreciation of their heritage; further believes that this will pose grave difficulties for a future multi-cultural Scotland because a society which is not in touch with its own culture is unlikely to be able to accommodate the cultures of others, and therefore views with grave concern the Minister for Education and Young People’s stated view that history need no longer be taught as a separate subject in the first two years of secondary schooling.
