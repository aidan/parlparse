Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (♦).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at: http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

*S2M-5440 Fiona Hyslop: Harthill Medical Practice—That the Parliament notes the deep concern of patients of Harthill Medical Practice about NHS Lanarkshire’s proposals to tender for a new contract to replace the current practice which is being dissolved, as expressed unanimously at a packed public meeting on the evening of Wednesday 17 January 2007, at which those present were required to select patients to sit on the panel to assess the tenders, in the following motion: "That this hall is unhappy with the whole end-to-end tendering, selection and representation process and all patients here present are acting under duress as there were no alternative options", and calls for an urgent review of NHS Lanarkshire’s interpretation of the law in this tendering process to determine whether or not there is a need for legal change to prevent similar action being taken by any other NHS board in Scotland in future.

S2M-5439 Shona Robison: Protect the Use of Xolair for Asthma Sufferers—That the Parliament regrets the decision by the Scottish Medicines Consortium (SMC) not to recommend the use of Xolair for people with severe allergic asthma; notes that, for 90% of people with asthma, their asthma symptoms should be controllable if they are on the right levels of existing asthma drugs but, for those who have difficult-to-control asthma and continue to experience poor quality of life and continuous symptoms, Xolair is the only treatment that may be able to reduce the impact of asthma on their lives by reducing symptoms and hospitalisation; notes that Xolair has been shown to reduce emergency admissions to hospital, reduce asthma exacerbations, improve lung function and improve quality of life, and urges the SMC to reconsider its decision not to recommend Xolair in Scotland, a drug which has been described by Donna Covey, Chief Executive of Asthma UK, as "the only hope that some people in Scotland currently have."

S2M-5421 Carolyn Leckie: Rename Queensberry House as Fletcher House—That the Parliament notes that there are few people celebrating the 300th anniversary of the Act of Union; recognises that the dissolution of the Scottish Parliament in 1707 was a defeat for the Scottish nation; notes that the central figure who colluded with the monarchy and the English state in the removal of power from that Parliament against the will of the people of Scotland was James Douglas, the Duke of Queensberry and original owner of the Queensberry House building in the new Holyrood complex; further notes that, in contrast, the person noted for the most honourable and progressive role in that period was Andrew Fletcher of Saltoun, who fought against the union and to maintain a Scottish Parliament, and who also championed democratic reforms such as annual elections to that Parliament, only the Parliament having the power to declare war and the removal of the royal veto which, 300 years later, have still never been achieved; believes that the re-establishment of the Scottish Parliament in the late 20th century testifies to the foresight of those such as Fletcher; therefore believes that it would go some way to acknowledge the wrong and injustice that was done if Queensberry House were renamed Fletcher House, and looks forward to the day when that building will reside within a Parliament with full powers in an independent Scotland.

Supported by: Frances Curran, Rosie Kane, Campbell Martin
