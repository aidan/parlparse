Section F – Motions and Amendments

Motions and amendments are normally only printed the day after the day on which they are lodged and then with the complete list of motions and amendments lodged the previous week which is published in this section of the Bulletin every Monday. That list also contains any motions lodged for debate in the forthcoming week.

Where the text of a motion or amendment is altered, the motion or amendment is re-printed with the changes marked by asterisks in the text.

Where a motion or amendment attracts additional support after it is first published, that additional support is shown separately at the end of this section.

Motions eligible for consideration for debate as members’ business in the Parliament are marked with a hash symbol (#).

Motions submitted for members’ business in the Parliament but which have not yet received the requisite cross-party support are marked with a diamond symbol (<>).

Motions in which a member has indicated a declarable interest are marked with an "R".

An indication is also provided where motions and amendments have been withdrawn.

A full list of current motions is available to view each Monday in paper copy at the Chamber Desk or alternatively on the Scottish Parliament web site at Current Motions

(http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/motions.htm). A search facility is also available on the Scottish Parliament web site at:

http://www.scottish.parliament.uk/business/motionsAndAmendments/index.htm

New and altered motions and amendments

*S2M-4725 Richard Lochhead: The Singleton of Dufftown Malt Whisky—That the Parliament congratulates Dufftown Distillery in Moray as it launches the latest Speyside malt, The Singleton of Dufftown; welcomes its traditional 19th century bottle and labelling which pay homage to Scotland’s whisky heritage; notes that Speyside malts are held in high regard throughout the world, and recognises the vital role which whisky plays in the Moray and Scottish economies, providing 41,000 jobs and contributing £2 billion a year to the balance of trade.

*S2M-4724# Dr Elaine Murray: Threat to Dumfries and Galloway Aviation Museum—That the Parliament commends the efforts of the volunteers who established, maintained and expanded Dumfries and Galloway Aviation Museum in Heathhall, Dumfries; notes that the museum’s collection includes 21 aircraft, a restored World War II watch tower and a collection of airborne forces’ memorabilia; notes that the museum has achieved a Scottish Tourist Board three-star award and a VisitScotland green tourism silver award; believes that the museum is a valuable asset to the region, providing an educational facility and a tourist attraction; notes that the museum does not receive public subsidy and is therefore extremely concerned that the present owner of the site, Knavesmuir Trust, is proposing to increase the rent by over 600% in this financial year, increasing to over 1,000% in five years’ time; believes that the owner should be encouraged to dispose of the site to Dumfries and Galloway Aviation Museum, and believes that the Scottish Executive should consider how it can assist with ensuring the survival of the museum.

*S2M-4723 Donald Gorrie: Downgrading of Monklands Hospital Accident and Emergency Services—That the Parliament deeply regrets the decision of the Scottish Executive to endorse the decision of NHS Lanarkshire to downgrade the status of accident and emergency services at Monklands Hospital; believes that this will seriously damage the health and well-being of the population and communities of Lanarkshire’s northern corridor, including Airdrie, Coatbridge and Cumbernauld, and further believes that major decisions over the provision of health services should no longer be the sole responsibility of ministers or non-elected quangos but should be brought before the Parliament for debate and vote.

*S2M-4722 Ms Sandra White: End the Genocide Now—That the Parliament extends its greatest sympathies to the people of Darfur in Sudan, where over 400,000 people have died and another three and a half million face starvation due to the ongoing conflict in the region and strongly criticises the international community for not taking any real action whilst the misery continues, despite some of its members calling the situation in Darfur genocide and accusing the Sudanese government of using famine and mass rape as weapons of war; considers that the international community is once again driven by economic concerns, namely in the form of the vast oil reserves in the region, rather than humanitarian ones, and believes that, if we are to end this cycle of global imperialism which continues unabated in Iraq and Afghanistan and has the effect of making large sections of the Muslim population feel alienated and highly suspicious of the motives behind international intervention, be it through "peacekeeping", "humanitarian intervention", "nation building" or "regime change", we must unite in our condemnation of these atrocities and the motives behind the international community’s shameful response.

Supported by: John Swinburne*, Rob Gibson*, Mr Adam Ingram*, Jim Mather*, Frances Curran*

*S2M-4721 Donald Gorrie: The Need for Common Sense Over Regulation—That the Parliament is anxious that any regulation relating to the publicising and enforcement of the law on smoking in enclosed public spaces should be composed and interpreted with common sense, especially since the law is being observed with very few exceptions; believes that it is important to retain the impressive goodwill among the public towards the policy on smoking in such spaces and not to lose this goodwill by introducing measures such as compelling retailers who have no problems with smokers and have operated a no smoking policy for many years to put up large no smoking notices, and urges those responsible for enforcing the law to deal effectively with occasions when people do smoke in contravention of the law and to let other people get on with life as normal and not harass them.

*S2M-4720 Eleanor Scott: Congratulations to the Burra Rowers—That the Parliament congratulates the rowing team from Burra, Shetland, for their tremendous effort in rowing an epic 84-mile voyage from Sumburgh in Shetland to Kirkwall in Orkney to raise money for the provision of a CAT scanner for Shetland; recognises this as an impressive feat of endurance and stamina, and notes that a CAT scanner for Shetland would reduce waiting times and minimise the number of transfers to Aberdeen for scans, reduce trauma for patients, cause less disruption to families and aid faster diagnoses.

Supported by: Rob Gibson*, Fiona Hyslop*, Bruce Crawford*, Mrs Nanette Milne*, Mark Ballard*, Chris Ballance*, John Swinburne*, Ms Maureen Watt*, Dennis Canavan*, Robin Harper*

*S2M-4718.1 Ms Sandra White: 150th Anniversary of the Birth of James Keir Hardie—As an amendment to motion (S2M-4718) in the name of Bill Butler, insert at end "but notes, however, that the present policies of New Labour must have Keir Hardie birlin’ in his grave, with one third of all Scotland’s children still living in poverty, the development of a new generation of nuclear power stations, the abolition of Clause Four and the retention of Thatcher’s anti-trade union laws, women still earning a lower average wage than men, the use of PFI/PPP in school and hospital building, the invasion of Iraq, which many consider to be based on a lie, the failure to oppose aggression against Palestine and Lebanon and the introduction of a new generation of Trident on the Clyde, all a sad legacy to Keir Hardie’s vision of egalitarianism and internationalism from today’s generation of Labour politicians."

Supported by: Jim Mather*, Campbell Martin*, Mr Adam Ingram*, Ms Maureen Watt*, Alex Neil*, Christine Grahame*

S2M-4454*#* Mr Jamie Stone: Bank Charges—That the Parliament notes the opinion issued by the Office of Fair Trading on 5 April 2006 that default charges from credit card issuers, mortgage providers and banks above the level of £12 are unfair; believes that high charges exacerbate debt and cause distress to Scottish consumers, particularly in rural areas such as the Highlands where it can exacerbate existing problems of income deprivation; commends the campaigns from Scottish student unions to recover charges, and considers that financial services should work towards protecting consumers by providing 14 days’ advance written notice of the issuing of any charge, ensuring that no default charges are set above £12, ending the practice of imposing charges on charges, doing more to promote financial literacy, making the terms of contracts clearer to consumers, investing in Citizen’s Advice Bureaux and other financial advice services and ensuring that poverty prevention is at the core of their business practice.

Supported by: Donald Gorrie, Christine Grahame, Shona Robison, Roseanna Cunningham, Dennis Canavan, Jeremy Purvis, Karen Whitefield, Margaret Smith, Jackie Baillie, Chris Ballance*, Mark Ballard*, Robin Harper*
