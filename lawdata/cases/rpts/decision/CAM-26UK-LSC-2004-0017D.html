The Application

 

1.         On the 13th April 2004, the Applicant applied to this Tribunal under section 27A of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 as amended by the Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002 for a determination as to whether costs incurred by way of service charge during the period 1st April 2004 to 31st March 2005 were reasonable.

 

2.         At the Hearing on the 20th September 2004 the parties agreed that the tribunal should also determine the reasonableness of the service charges for the periods 1st April 2002 to 31st March 2003 and 1st April 2003 to 31st March 2004

 

3.         In addition the Applicant made an application pursuant to section 20(c) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985, for an order that the Landlord’s costs in connection with these proceedings should not be regarded as relevant costs to be taken into account in determining the amount of any service charge payable by the tenants of the property.

 

4.         In addition at the Hearing the Applicant applied for an order requiring the Respondents to pay the Applicant’s Application and Hearing fees of £200.

 

The Law

 

5.         Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 as amended by the Housing Act 1996 and Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002

           

Section 18

 

(1)        In the following provisions of this Act “service charge” means an amount payable by a tenant of a dwelling as part of or in addition to the rent-

(a)        which is payable directly or indirectly for services, repairs, maintenance, improvement or insurance or the landlord’s costs of management, and

(b)        the whole or part of which varies or may vary according to the relevant costs

 

(2)        The relevant costs are the costs or estimated costs incurred or to be incurred by or on behalf of the land lord or a superior landlord in connection with the matters of which the service charge is payable.

 

(3)        for this purpose

(a)        costs includes overheads and

(b)        costs are relevant costs in relation to a service charge whether they are incurred or to be incurred  in the period for which the service charge is payable or in an earlier period

 

Section 19

 

(1)        Relevant costs shall be taken into account in determining the amount of a service charge payable for a period-

(a)        only to the extent that they are reasonably incurred; and

(b)        where they are incurred on the provision of services or the carrying out of works, only if the services or works are of a reasonable standard; and the amount payable shall be limited accordingly.

 

(2)        Where a service charge is payable before the relevant costs are incurred, no greater amount than is reasonable is so payable, and after the relevant costs have been incurred any necessary adjustment shall be made by repayment, reduction or subsequent charges or otherwise.

 

Section 27A

 

(1)                          An application may be made to a leasehold valuation tribunal for a determination whether a service charge is payable and, if it is, as to-

 

(a) the person by whom it is payable,

(b) the person to whom it is payable,

(c) the amount which is payable,

(d) the date at or by which it is payable, and

(e) the manner in which it is payable.

 

(2)        Subsection (1) applies whether or not any payment has been made.

 

(3)        An application may also be made to a leasehold valuation tribunal for a determination whether costs were incurred for services, repairs maintenance improvements insurance or management of any specified description, a service charge would be payable for the costs and if it would, as to-        

(a) the person by whom it would be payable,

(b) the person to whom it would be payable,

(c) the amount which would be payable,

(d) the date at or by which it would be payable, and

(e) the manner in which it would be payable.

 

Description of the Building and Property

 

5.         The property is situated within a development of sheltered housing, which consists of

-         a period building containing 20 flats, a common lounge, 2 guest flats and Scheme Manager’s office and flat,

-         13 bungalows held on long leases (“Leasehold Properties”) and 

-         12 bungalows held on monthly-assured non-shorthold leases (“Rented Properties”).

The development is set in grounds laid to lawns and rhododendron bushes and shrubs.

 

6.         The property is a one-bedroom end of terrace bungalow constructed of brick under a pitched roof with double glazed units in wooden window frames and comprises a hall, living room, kitchen, bedroom and bathroom. Space heating is by electrical night storage heaters and water heating is by electrical immersion heater.

 

Inspection

 

7.         The property was in fair to good condition. The grounds appeared to be well kept and there was evidence that the hedges had been recently maintained.

The Tenancy Service Charge

 

12.       The Tenancy Service Charge Account for 2002/03 and 2003/04 drawn up by the Respondents before the introduction of the Support Charge included all itemised amounts to be paid by the Applicant. In a letter to the Applicant dated 24th July 2003 Mr Brindley explained that in the past for those who were eligible, financial assistance in the form of Housing Benefit could be claimed for rent and where appropriate certain items of a service charge where a landlord gave support to a tenant for example the service of a warden or an emergency call system. In 1998 it was found by the courts that Housing Benefit could only be claimed for rent and not for any service charge. In response those who were eligible could claim financial assistance to pay for the support service in the form of a Support Grant. As a result landlords such as the Respondents who provided support services were obliged to identify them in the service charge.

 

13.       The Respondents therefore split the Tenancy Service Charge into two accounts for 2004/05. One was in the same format as had been produced for 2002/03 and 2003/04, which set out the service charge element (“Service Charge Element”) alone and omitted the support service element which was set out in a separate account called “ Supporting People General Counselling and Support Breakdown” and is known as the “Support Charge”. This account divides the Support Charge into three headings: Staff Costs, Service Costs and Depreciation. Most although not all the costs itemised were charged for in 2002/03 and 2003/04.                                                                                                                                                                                                     

 

The Applicant’s Case

 

14.       The Applicant divided his case into 10 points as follows:

 

15.       Point 1 referred to his taking the tenancy at Salter’s Gardens. The Applicant stated that he had not been given a copy of the handbook referred to in Clause 1.2 of the Tenancy Agreement. He also said that he had expressed concern in December 2002 about a potential problem with the overgrown hedge and trees by his bungalow.

 

16.       Point 2 referred to the introduction of the Support Charge. The Applicant stated that the Respondent had made number of mistakes in the accounts. In particular a letter dated 26th February 2003 from the respondent gave notice of a new rent of £364.42 per month. On the 28th April 2003 a further letter from the Respondent was received purporting to propose a new rent of £408.92 per month. The Applicant stated that the Respondents were in error in that the second charge was wrongly calculated and that the Tenancy Agreement only allowed the Respondents to increase the rent once a year.

 

17.       In addition the Applicant stated that as a result of the Tenancy Service Charge being divided into the Service Charge Element and the Support Charge the Tenancy Service Charge was increased by 88% although the National Housing Federation Booklet stated that the dividing of the Tenancy Service Charge into the Service Charge Element and the Support Charge would not cost a tenant any more and the Internet site of the Office of the Deputy Prime Minister gave guidelines which stated that for people not on Housing Benefit an objective was for equitable charging to prevail.

 

18.       The Applicant referred to a letter from the Supporting People and Housing Manager for Hertfordshire County Council Adult Care Service dated 8 December 2003 in which it was stated that the Respondent has not been passing on the full costs of the support services to tenants but that now there is the need to divide the Tenancy Service Charge into the Service Charge Element and the Support Charge, the Respondents had been prompted to include the full cost. This was confirmed in a letter 3 January 2004 dated from the Respondent’s Chief Executive to the Applicant’s Member of Parliament who had been asked to investigate the matter by the Applicant. In addition the letter stated that the increased charges would not be retrospective. The Applicant considered the charges to be retrospective as they were calculated for the year 2002/03 and payable in 2003. He also considered them to be unreasonable.

 

19.       Point 3 referred to the most recent Rent and Tenancy Service Charge demand. The Applicant stated that he noted from a letter from the Respondent dated 12th March 2004 that the Support Charge of £60.87 per month was fixed for 3 years. He was concerned that the charge was fixed for this period and also concerned as to the level of the charge at the end of this period. He stated he had requested an explanation but had not received one.

 

20.       Part 4 referred to the Scheme Manager’s Salary. The Applicant stated that the Tenancy Service Charge Accounts relating to the Rented Properties did not give the whole amount of the Scheme Manager’s Salary. The entry on the spreadsheet stated that only 80% of the item was charged to the Rented Properties as 20% of the Manager’s work in this area was included in the rent. The Applicant said that when he attempted to calculate the Manager’s full salary by taking the 80% figure adding the 20% and the proportionate contribution from the Leasehold Properties the figures did not reconcile. However the Applicant was at pains to point out that he considered the Scheme Manager did an excellent job and said was worth a far higher salary than her superiors were undoubtedly receiving.

 

21.       Point 5 referred to the cost of gardening. The Applicant said that he was aware there were two contracts for gardening held by the same contractor, one for the Leasehold Properties and one for the rented Properties. However the gardener appeared to only attend once a week for two hours on a Monday in respect of both contracts but according to the 2003/04 and 2004/05 Tenancy Service Charge Accounts received £4,255 per annum for the Rented Properties contract alone. The gardener himself had told the Applicant that he only received £1,000 per annum. The Applicant said that the work carried out by the gardener was not value for money and was not of a reasonable standard. There was moss on the path near the Applicant’s bungalow which had not been treated until recently and which may have contributed to his neighbour falling in January 2003. The gardener does not do any planting and only cuts the lawns, trims and prunes the bushes and very occasionally applies weed killer.

 

22.       Point 6 referred to the Respondents’ on costs itemised in the Support Charge Account. He could not see what these were for and were not included in the Tenancy Service Charge Accounts for 2002/03 and 2003/04.

 

23.       Point 7 referred to the item of tree surgery and communal costs in the Tenancy Service Charge Accounts. The charge for tree surgery was considered to be excessive by the Applicant. As stated in paragraph 15 the Applicant had expressed concerns about the bushes and trees within months of moving in but nothing had been done. The communal charge for the laundry was also not considered to be reasonable since the Leasehold Property tenants use the laundry but do not contribute to its cost other than through the purchase of tokens which all tenants have to purchase to operate the machines.

 

24.       Point 8 referred to the call equipment depreciation itemised in the Support Charge Account. The Applicant did not consider this charge justified and it was not included in the Tenancy Service Charge Accounts for 2002/03 and 2003/04.

 

25.       Point 9 referred to audited accounts. The Applicant noted that the tenants of the Leasehold Properties were provided with audited accounts but the tenants of the Rented Properties were not.

 

26.       Point 10 The Applicant concluded by requesting that the respondent be required to pay his Application and Hearing Fees, which amounted to £200.

 

Respondents’ Case

 

27.       With regard to point 1 the Respondents stated that the Applicant should have received a Handbook prior to entering the Tenancy Agreement. The Handbook gives details of the service charge.

 

28.       In relation to point 2 the Respondents stated that the Tenancy Service Charge Account had not been divided into the Service Charge Element and Support Charge until 2004/05. The Respondents acknowledged that an error had been made in 2003/04 in submitting a second notice, which included an increased rent with the addition of the Support Charge. The Respondent confirmed that the second notice had been withdrawn.

 

29.       The Respondents acknowledged that the requirement to account for the support service separately as a Support Charge following the introduction of the Support Grant had prompted the Respondent to standardise its policy over all its properties and charge in full for the support services it provided. The Tenancy Service Charge Accounts for 2002/03 and 2003/04 included the Service Charge Element and charge for support services, which later became the Support Charge. The Tenancy Service Charge Account for 2004/05 was in two parts. The Service Charge Element was set out on the spreadsheet, which is in the same format as that for 2002/03 and 2003/04. The support services attributable to the Support Charge are itemised on a separate account. The Respondents stated that the costs of the support services had been calculated in accordance with the appropriate Government guidelines. A proportion (48%) of the Scheme Manager’s Salary and flat rent loss costs had been transferred to the Support Charge Account as had the call centre monitoring charge, servicing maintenance of call equipment and phone link line cost. A charge for the following items had also been added: Housing Officer’s management, training costs, the Respondent’s on-costs, business telephone calls, service charge management fee and call equipment depreciation

 

30.       In relation to point 3 the Respondent stated that the Support Charge is fixed for three years under the legislation relating to the Support Grant.

 

31.       In relation to point 4 the Respondent submitted a calculation of the Scheme Manager’s salary based on the figures given in the accounts. However the figures for 2004/05 were incomplete, as they had not been finalised for the Leasehold Properties.

 

32.       In relation to point 5 the Respondent’s submitted a dated gardening agreement and 4 invoices for 2003. The Respondent’s stated that the sums entered on the accounts were paid and the contractor was not employed at an hourly rate but an annual amount for gardening services for the Rented Properties for the year.

 

33.       In relation to point 6 the Respondent submitted a statement as to how the on- costs had been calculated. It was confirmed that these were permissible costs that could be included in the Support Charge and payment claimed by tenants eligible for the Support Grant.

 

34.       In relation to point 7 the Respondent submitted invoices for the amounts stated on the accounts. It was acknowledged that the Leasehold Property tenants should pay double what the Rented Property tenants pay for their tokens to operate the machines to balance the contribution made by the Rented Property tenants to the machines in the service charge. However this had never been put into effect and therefore it was acknowledged that the Rented Property tenants should not be liable for the laundry charge.

 

35.       In relation to point 8 call equipment depreciation, no provision was made for this in the handbook or the check tenancy agreement.

 

36.       In relation to point 9 the Respondents stated that they were seeking to move away from having fixed charge accounts and to provide the Rented Property tenants with the same form of Tenancy Service Charge Accounts as the Leasehold Property tenants, which are audited accounts. However, currently the respondents were not aware of any obligation that required them to produce audited Tenancy Service Charge Accounts for the Rented Property tenants.

 

37.       In relation to point 10 the respondents said that they would leave the matter to the Tribunals discretion.

 

Determination of the Application under Section 27A of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985

 

38.       The Tribunal considered the Tenancy Service Charge Accounts for each year in the light of the submissions of the parties.

 

39.       The Tribunal determined that the list of items given in Clause 1.2 of the Agreement should form the basis of the Tenancy Service Charge and that the Handbook should could only be effective in giving a further explanation. On the basis that the Handbook was only an explanatory document the claim by the Applicant that he had not received a copy of the book prior to entering the Agreement did not affect the legality of the of items on the service charge account as only those stated on the Agreement could be charged. The Tribunal noted that the Handbook provided was a 2003 edition. However, it was felt that the entries in the Handbook on pages 22 and 31 to 35 relating to the service charge added little to the list of items that could be charged for in the Agreement.

 

40.       The Respondents did not adduce worthwhile evidence of the gardening contract. The document that was produced was very dated. Nevertheless the Tribunal was of the opinion that the grounds were well maintained and that the charge for gardening in 2002/03 was reasonable being an average of £65.77 per week. However the Tribunal determined that the costs of gardening had risen by approximately 10% per annum between 2003 and 2004 and that therefore the annual charge for 2002/03 and 2004/05 was unreasonable and reduced it accordingly.

 

41.       The Tribunal excluded the charge in the accounts for all years for Laundry servicing and repairs as the Respondents acknowledged that the Rented Property tenants should not pay more than the Leasehold Property tenants.

 

42.       The Tribunal found the evidence provided by the Respondents of the Scheme Manager’s salary unhelpful and none of the calculations submitted added up.

47.       No provision was made in the Lease for charging for depreciation of the call equipment and the Handbook made no reference to it. In addition no evidence was submitted to justify the item such as a description of the equipment itself, capital cost and anticipated period for renewal of the equipment. The Tribunal determined that the cost of providing an alarm system within the meaning of the Lease was adequately covered by the charge for maintenance and servicing of the equipment and that a further charge for depreciation was not reasonable and therefore not payable to the Respondent by the Applicant.

 

Determination of Application under Section 20(c) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985

 

48.       The Respondent applied for an order under Section 20(c) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 that the Landlord’s costs in connection with these proceedings should not be regarded as relevant costs to be taken into account in determining the amount of any service charge payable by the tenant of the property.

 

49.       The Applicant stated that the Tenancy Service Charge costs had been reasonably incurred and the statement of account properly served.

 

50.       The Tribunal determined that the proceedings might have been avoided had a timely and clear explanation been given to the Applicant regarding the co-relation between the Service Charge Element and the Support Charge of the Tenancy Service Charge. A number of the items in the Support Charge Account were not reasonable. Some of these items are already part of the Management Fee of 15%. In general the Tribunal found that the Tenancy Service Charge Account was not clear. The Tribunal therefore orders that the Respondent’s costs in connection with these proceedings should not be regarded as relevant costs to be taken into account in determining the amount of any service charge payable by the Applicant.

 

51.       Sheltered housing provides accommodation for a vulnerable section of the community. The Tribunal considered that managers of such housing had a responsibility, amongst others, to ensure that changes in service charges were reasonable and clearly explained to residents. The Tribunal were of the opinion that the Respondent did not fulfil its obligations to the Applicant in this respect, which led to this matter coming before the Tribunal. Therefore the Tribunal by virtue of paragraph 9 Leasehold Valuation Tribunals (Fees) (England) Regulation 2003 requires the Respondent to reimburse the Applicant’s fees of £200.00.
