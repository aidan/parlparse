THE PREMISES

 

1.         The subject property is an end of terrace house constructed of stone and brick with rendered gable end wall under a tiled roof.

 

Accommodation

 

The property comprises entrance hall from which rises the stairs to the first floor, two living rooms and kitchen on the ground floor. On the first floor are three bedrooms and a bathroom with w.c. Outside there is a w.c. and store and gardens to front and rear. There are also sheds erected by the Tenant. There is off road parking but no garage. Access to the other properties in the terrace is obtained at the rear of the subject property.

 

Services

 

The property has mains electricity, water and drainage but no gas. Space heating is by hearth fires in the living rooms as let but the Tenant has installed night storage heaters, Water heating is by a back boiler to one of the open fires and an immersion heater.

 

Furnishing

 

The property is let unfurnished.

 

 

Location

 

The property is situated about a mile 5 miles from Market Harborough where there is a range of facilities.

 

THE TENANCY

 

2.         According to the Rent Register the Tenancy is a weekly periodic statutory tenancy under the Rent Act 1977 that commenced on the 1st April 1988. Also according to the Register the Landlord is responsible for repairs and external decorations and the Tenant is responsible for internal decorations. Section 11 of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 applies in respect of the Landlord’s liability for repairs, the tenancy not being for a fixed period of 7 years or more.

 

THE INSPECTION

 

3.         The Committee inspected the property on 7th April 2003 in the presence of Mrs Swain, one of the Tenants. The Committee considered that externally the property was in generally fair condition. The metal windows showed signs of corrosion and there was evidence of damp on the rendered gable end wall. Internally the property had no central heating. As stated above space heating was by open-hearth fires as let but the Tenant had fitted night storage heaters and water heating was by back boiler and immersion heater. The kitchen as let was basic and dated with only a deep white sink. The Tenant had fitted units. There was an old Aga style stove that apparently has not worked since the Tenant occupied the property. The bathroom was dated and the Tenant had installed a shower. No carpets, curtains or white goods were provided. There was evidence of damp in the cupboards on the gable end of the property and around the reveals of the windows. Outside there was no garage but there was hard standing. Access to other properties the terrace was past the rear of the subject property. The subject property is situated on a busy main road although is served by buses. The nearest shopping centre and facilities are in Market Harborough some 5 miles away.

 

THE APPLICATION

 

4.         On the 2nd January 2003, following an application by the Landlord's Agent on the 12th November 2002 the Rent Officer registered a rent for the subject property of £60.00 per week effective from 29th January 2003 which was below the capped rent of £61.50 under Rent Acts Maximum Fair Rent) Order.  The Tenants objected to the new registered rent on the 21st January 2003 and an application was made to the Rent Assessment Committee requesting a Hearing. The Committee inspected the property on the 7th April 2003 in the presence of the Tenant and the Hearing took place later the same day at which Mrs Swain, one of the Tenants, and Mr Andrew Brough, for the Landlord's Agents, attended

 

THE TENANT'S CASE

 

5.         The Tenants made written representations, which were confirmed at the Hearing, stated that the gable end wall of the property was damp and extremely wet even in the airing cupboard. At the Hearing the Tenant said that Clothes kept in the cupboards on that wall become mouldy. She said that she did not accept that it was due to condensation as she kept the window open all day in the room that suffered most in order to dry the wall out. She believed that it was penetrating damp and that the Environmental Health Officer, Mr Asherton, had confirmed this. The Tenant's also stated that they had replaced the kitchen sink unit as it had become rotten due to a hole in the guttering and that they also had replaced carpet and kitchen vinyl flooring. These were the subject of a claim under the Tenant's insurance as apparently the Landlord's excess meant the claim would not be covered. The Tenants also drew attention to the items that they had fitted including:

the shower,

the central heating (except for one of the storage heaters for which the Tenant installed the wiring but the heater was fitted by the Landlords),

the kitchen units and the plumbing for the washing machine and dishwasher.

In addition they had laid the garden.

 

7.         The Tenant also said that they had suffered from vermin.

 

8.         The Tenant further stated that although £56.0 per week is registered the Tenant in fact pay £50.00 per week.

 

9.         With regard to the comparable put forward by the Landlord's Agents the Tenant stated that the rents were not comparable unless deduction s were made for double glazing and oil-fired central heating and were unlikely to have the same problems of damp. The added that they had lived in the village for 15 years whereas the comparables are on short 6 monthly lets.

 

THE LANDLORD'S CASE

 

10.              The Landlord's Agent made written representations that were confirmed at the Hearing. He said he was not able to comment on the Tenant's statement as to the condition of the property when let in that the Agents did not act for the Landlord at that time. As to the damp on the gable end wall he stated that it was not clear what caused it but may be condensation. The Landlord's Agents put forward three comparable properties, two of which were neighbouring properties in the same terraces and were so considered the best comparables at the Hearing. The floor areas were about the same for the subject property and the comparables. The two were as follows:

Comparable 1 had three bedrooms upstairs and two living rooms, a kitchen and bathroom downstairs. Externally there were front and rear gardens and outbuildings. The property had recently been refurbished with double-glazing, storage heaters, and new bathroom and fitted kitchen. It was let on an assured shorthold tenancy form December 2001 at £475.00 per calendar month.

Comparable 2 had three bedrooms upstairs and one living room, a kitchen and bathroom downstairs. Externally there were front and rear gardens and outbuildings. The property had recently been refurbished with double-glazing, storage heaters, and new bathroom and fitted kitchen. It was let on an assured shorthold tenancy form May 2002 at £475.00 per calendar month.

 

11.       With regard to scarcity it was stated that the market in Kelmarsh and the surrounding area was buoyant and that the Agents had had an increase in letting instructions of 30% in the previous 18months.

THE DETERMINATION

 

13.       The Committee assessed the rent for the subject property completely afresh as at the day of the inspection pursuant to section 70(1) Rent Act 1977 (having regard in particular to the age, character, locality, state of repair of the property and all the circumstances other than personal circumstances). The Committee took account of the relevant cases and legislation including Spath Holme Ltd v Greater Manchester Rent Assessment Committee (1996) 28 HLR 107, Curtis v The London Rent Assessment Committee [1997] 4 All ER 842 and BTE Ltd v Merseyside and Cheshire Rent Assessment Committee 24th May 1991.

 

14.              The assessment of a Fair Rent starts with an assessment of the open market rent. The comparable properties put forward by the Landlord's Agent were accepted. The Committee noted that the most recent was let in May 2002 and considered that rents had risen little since that date and the Hearing. The Committee therefore took the view that the open market rent for the subject property let on an Assured Shorthold Tenancy in good condition with modern kitchen and bathroom, double glazing, central heating would be £475.00 per calendar month.

 

15.       However the Committee there are facilities enjoyed by the properties put forward as comparable which were not present in the subject property. it did not have double glazing or central heating. In addition there was evidence of some damp. The Committee has therefore made a global deduction of £166.25 per calendar month for the following matters. The subject property does not have central heating or double-glazing, the bathroom is dated and the kitchen, as let, is very basic with only sink. There was also evidence of the damp It should be noted that this figure cannot be a simple arithmetical calculation but is the Committee’s estimate of the amount by which the rent would have to be reduced to attract a tenant.

 

16.       As to scarcity, the Committee decided that there is substantial scarcity of  “... similar dwelling houses in the locality...” available for letting and a deduction would be made to reflect this. The matters taken into account by the Committee were:-

 

a.                   The Committee interpreted the “locality” for scarcity purposes as being Northamptonshire i.e. a sufficiently large area to eliminate the effect of any localised amenity, which would, in itself, tend to increase or decrease rent.

 

b.                  The members of the Committee have many years of experience of the residential letting market between them and that experience leads them to the view that demand consistently exceeds supply for similar properties to the subject property in the locality defined above.

 

c.                   The Committee noted that there were many people on the waiting lists of Local Authorities and Housing Associations in the locality and that such lists were increasing.  It decided that whilst not everyone on those lists would be seeking a similar property to the subject property in the private sector, a significant number would.

 

17.       Assessing a scarcity percentage cannot be a precise arithmetical calculation because there is no way of knowing either the exact number of people looking for properties similar to the subject property in the private sector or the exact number of such properties available. It can only be a judgement based on the years of experience of members of the Committee.  That combined experience leads the Committee to the view that demand is particularly high for properties similar to the subject property in this locality and the scarcity deduction is assessed at 10%.

 

 

SUMMARY

 

18.       Open Market Rent:                                                       £475.00 per calendar month

Less global deduction                                                   £166.25 per calendar month

                                                                                                £308.75 per calendar month

Less Scarcity 20%                                                        £ 30.88 per calendar month

                                                                                                £277.87 per calendar month

£277.87 per calendar month equates to £64.12 per week

‘Uncapped’ fair rent say £64.00 per week

 

The provisions of the Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999 require that the registered rent is either the capped Fair Rent or the Fair Rent decided by the Committee which ever is the lower. The capped Fair Rent is calculated in accordance with a statutory formula using the existing rent as a base. The capped rent in this case is £61.50, which is lower than the Fair Rent assessed by the Committee and therefore the rent assessed by the Committee is to be registered.

           

 

FAIR RENT = £61.50 per week
