Princethorpe  House - V - Lord  Mayor  and  Citizens  of  Westminster

 

 

LON/00BK/NSP/2003/0028

LON/00BK/NLC/2003/0027

 

 

 

THE APPLICATION

 

This was an application by Mr K Smalley, the tenant of 117 Princethorpe House, Woodchester Square, London W2 5SX (“the flat”) pursuant to Section 19(2B) of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1985 (“the Act). He sought a determination as to the reasonableness of the estimated communal electricity charges to be incurred during the half year from 1st April 2003 to 30th September 2003. He also applied, under section 20C of the Act, for an order limiting the Respondent’s ability to recover costs incurred in these proceedings through the service charge. The Respondent landlord was Westminster Council (properly known as The Lord Mayor and Citizens of the City of Westminster).

 

BACKGROUND

 

Princethorpe House (“the Block”) is a 21-storey block of flats built in the 1960’s. It comprises 125 flats and maisonettes of which 23 have been sold under the right to buy legislation. The remaining flats continue to be occupied by secure tenants. The Applicant purchased the flat on 14th April 1999. The Block is one of 3 tower blocks which, together with a number of low-rise blocks, form part of the Respondent’s Warwick Estate (“the Estate”). For some years the Estate has been managed on behalf of the Respondent by Paddington Churches Housing Association (“PCHA”).

On the basis of the Applicant’s evidence (which in this respect was not disputed) the communal lighting in the Block was, until 1999, centrally controlled. It was only turned on when it was dark. The Applicant said that, taking the year as a whole, the communal lighting was switched on for approximately 12 hours per day.

In 1999 substantial electrical works were carried out to the Block and the mechanism for controlling the communal lighting was removed, although whether this was deliberate or accidental was not clear. However it was common ground that, from the date of those works, the communal lighting was left on permanently.

The Applicant objected to this policy on the basis that it resulted in an increase in the cost of providing the communal lighting which was inherently unreasonable. On 27th  December 2000 the Respondent rejected the objection saying that the Residents Association had agreed that the lighting should be kept on at all times “because it uses less electricity and is therefore cheaper to run”. The Applicant persisted with his objection.  The Respondent then treated the objection as a complaint and dealt with it under its three-stage complaints procedure. At each stage the Respondent rejected the complaint.

On 5th March 2001, at the first stage, the Respondent said that the cost differential was “less than 3 pence per lamp per anum” and that a majority of the residents were content “with the lighting arrangements and the added security that it provides”.

On 5th April 2001, at the second stage, the Respondent produced a copy of a report prepared by a Mr Brennan of Southern Consultants Ltd dated 31st January 2001 (“the Report”). On the basis of the Report it said that a lamp left on permanently would cost “in the region £13.50 …per year” more than one switched on for 15 hours continuously per day.  However as the lights “operate on a photo cell, which monitors the ambient light conditions” it was impossible to make a valid comparison. It said that the lights were left on continuously at the request of the Residents Association and it repeated the concerns over security.

On 21st May 2001, at the third stage, the Respondent repeated the points made previously saying “To obtain any revised costings based on the use of a photo cell is going to be extremely difficult and it is dubious as to whether this can be done with any degree of accuracy”.

The Applicant believing that his grievance had not been adequately addressed referred the matter to the Local Authority Ombudsman who declined to deal with it on the basis that his remedy was to make an application to the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal. The Applicant made his application but a differently constituted Tribunal declined jurisdiction on the basis that, in respect of the year in dispute, he had paid the service charge in full. On the 26th July 2003 he made the application now before the Tribunal in respect of the interim or estimated service charge accounts for the period from 1st April 2003 to 30th September 2003. In respect of the demand based upon those accounts the Applicant had not paid the charge for Communal Electricity which amounted to £2,140 in respect of the Block, of which his share was £10.87.

At a pre-trial review on 29th August 2003 a differently constituted Tribunal issued directions. The directions had in the main been complied with. The Tribunal issuing the directions considered that an inspection of the Block would probably not be necessary.  The directions authorised the Tribunal to consider requiring the Respondents to reimburse the Applicants with the whole or part of their fees under article 8 of the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal (Fees) order 1997.

 

THE LEASE

 

The Applicant’s lease dated 14th April 1999 (“the Lease”) is in a standard form. The Respondent’s relevant obligations or functions are set out in Clauses 4 and 6 and the Ninth Schedule.

Clause 3 of the Ninth Schedule requires the Respondent “To keep adequately lighted all such parts of the Reserved Property as are normally lighted or should be lighted…..”. The Reserved Property is defined in the Third Schedule and includes “the halls staircases lifts (if any) landings steps passages or other parts of  (the Block) which are used in common by the owners or occupiers of any of the Flats forming part of (the Block )”.

Clause 6(A)(ii) of the Lease entitles the Respondent “to employ architects surveyors solicitors accountants contractors builders gardeners and any other person or company properly required to be employed in connection with or for the purpose of or in relation to the Estate or the Property or any part thereof and pay them all proper fees charges salaries wages costs expenses and outgoings”.

The Respondent’s financial year runs from 1 st April in any year. In essence the Lease provides that the Applicant must pay a fair and reasonable proportion of the estimated costs to be incurred by the Respondent in carrying out the obligations or functions referred to in Clauses 4 and 6 and in performing the covenants set out in the Ninth Schedule. This interim charge is to be paid by two instalments on 1st April and 1st October in each financial year.

Although not specifically stated it is clear intended that, at the end of each financial year, the Respondent should prepare an account detailing the actual expenditure incurred during that year. If the interim charge paid by the Applicant is less than his share of the actual expenditure then the shortfall is payable upon demand. If the interim charge exceeds the Applicant’s share of the actual expenditure then the surplus is carried forward and credited to the Applicant’s account.

“A fair and reasonable proportion” is not defined in the Lease but in practice it is calculated by reference to the total number of bed spaces in either the Block or the Estate depending upon the nature of the expenditure. Communal Electricity is a Block cost and the Applicant pays 0.508% of all such costs.

There are provisions in the Lease which were presumably intended to protect Right to Buy tenants during an initial five-year period. During that period the estimated costs to be taken into account in calculating the interim charge is fixed by reference to the sums set out in Part I of the Tenth Schedule. That Schedule provides that the Block Electricity cost shall be the equivalent of £3,832.50 per half year rather than the sum of £2,140 actually charged to the estimated service charge account for the half year from 1st April 2003 to 30th September 2003. That would result, ironically, in the Applicant having to pay almost twice the sum in dispute. The Respondent did not however seek to rely upon these provisions and it would have been inappropriate for it to do so. In any event as the law stood at the date of the application it would not have been an issue for the Tribunal relating as it did to the recoverability of the sum in dispute.



 

THE HEARING

 

The hearing took place on the morning of 31st October 2003. The Applicant appeared in person and gave evidence on his own behalf. Mr G Pryce, a barrister, represented the Respondent. Evidence was given on behalf of the Respondent by Ms A White an Estate manager employed by PCHA and by Mr D McCallionan an employee of the Respondent’s Arms Length Management Organisation.

 

EVIDENCE AND REPRESENTATIONS

 

The Applicant’s case was simply put. Since 1999 he had never seen the communal lights turned off and he doubted that they were operated by means of a photo cell. A light left on for 24 hours will cost twice as much as one left on for 12 hours. There was no good reason to leave the lights on permanently: the additional cost was inherently unreasonable and half the Communal Electricity Charges should be disallowed: that is £1,070 in respect of the Block of which his share was £5.44.

He accepted that he was the only tenant in the Block to object to the Communal Electricity charges. He did not know if the Residents Association had been consulted although if it had he rather doubted that it was prior to the change of policy in 1999. He did not accept that the sum in dispute was trivial: it might only be some £5.44 per half year from his point of view but for the Block as a whole the sum in dispute amounted to £2,140.

He considered that the safety concerns had been overplayed. During daylight hours there was sufficient natural light in the common parts of the Block.

Ms White justified the policy of keeping the lights on permanently and hence the expenditure on two grounds: firstly that it addressed a genuine security issue on the Estate and secondly that PCHA had consulted the Residents Association which was in agreement with the policy.

She said that an incident of some description, in the tower blocks, was reported about every two weeks. More often than not it was nuisance behaviour caused by children but there had been rapes and burglaries. The natural lighting in the common parts was poor and leaving the communal lighting on permanently created a more secure environment. Consequently the policy and hence the cost was reasonable.

She said that she could not say when the Residents Association was first consulted: only that she had consulted them when the issue arose. She produced a letter from the Residents Association dated 1st October 2003. It is worth reciting the relevant two paragraphs of that letter: -

“You have assured us it is a minimal cost to leave the lights on 24 hours a day.

The Residents Association agrees with this at such low cost to further the health and safety aspect of the residents on the estate” (as it is written).

She could not however say when the Residents Association was first consulted.

She accepted that the lights had previously been operated by time clocks but she could not say when they had been removed. As far as she was aware photo cells did not operate the communal lights: they were left on permanently.

She accepted that there would be a saving if the communal lights were operated by time clocks and she had never suggested to the Residents Association that a saving could be achieved by leaving the lights on permanently.

She pointed out however that there would be a cost in both installing time clocks which was difficult to quantify. The time clocks would have to be changed and inspected twice a year and under the existing maintenance contract there would be a cost of £16 per year in respect of each time clock: she was not competent to say how many time clocks would be required. One also had to factor in the reduction, referred to in the Report, of about 5% in the life of a light which is switched on and off twice a day. Although not an expert she thought that if the total electricity charges incurred in operating the communal lights amounted to £4,280 per year a saving of about £1,800 could be achieved by installing time clocks.

From the time that the Applicant first lodged his objection to this point in Ms White’s evidence it had been assumed by all concerned that the Communal Electricity charges related solely to the cost of the communal lighting. However in response to enquires from the Tribunal it became apparent that this was not the case. The Communal Electricity charges included in the Service Charge accounts included also the cost of running the Lifts and the security cameras and the cleaning appliances used to clean the common parts. One meter supplied all the electricity to the common parts and it was impossible to say what proportion, for example, related to the lifts or to the communal lighting.

Mr D McCallionan explained that the substantial reduction achieved in recent years resulted form the Respondent having adopted an aggressive policy of price competitive tendering with its suppliers.

Mr Pryce submitted that the cost is dispute was so trivial that it was not appropriate to bring the matter before the Tribunal. In reality the Applicant objected to the Respondent’s policy rather than the actual cost which was in itself reasonable. Turning to the application for an order under section 20C of the Act he contended that if an order were made the costs would fall on the secure tenants because, under the provisions of The Local Government and Housing Act 1989, the Respondent had to account for housing management costs under its Housing Revenue account. That, he said, would be unfair and unreasonable.

 

INSPECTION

 

Having heard the evidence the Tribunal considered it necessary to inspect the Block, notwithstanding the comment in the directions. It did so for the purpose of establishing the level of natural light in the common parts to enable it to form a view as to the legitimacy of the security concerns raised by the Respondent. In the event the inspection was somewhat inconclusive.

The Tribunal inspected the Block during the afternoon of 31st October 2003. It was a bright day. At the request of the Tribunal the communal lights were turned off for a short time during the inspection.

As the majority of the units are two storey maisonettes the lift sto ps on every second floor. On each such floor a long narrow corridor runs the length of the Block with a large window at each end. There is no other source of natural light. The lift shaft is to one end of the Block. On every second floor the two lifts opens to a lobby and from the other side of that lobby a door leads to an emergency stairwell. Anyone could gain access to the lobbies and stairwell and they clearly present a security risk. However given the proximity of the end windows the natural lighting in both the lobbies and the stairwell is adequate and additional lighting during daylight hours would do little to enhance security

Beyond the lobby, and opposite the end window, a glass security door gives access to the corridor from which entry to the individual flats is gained. One needs either a pass or a security code to open the door so that each corridor provides a secure environment. Consequently it seems difficult to argue that additional lighting is required during daylight hours to address a genuine security issue.

However the corridors themselves are gloomy and the natural lighting is generally poor. The environment was reminiscent of the most depressing sort of institutional establishment. Although the communal lights appeared to add little to the available natural light the Tribunal could well imagine that many residents would welcome anything that alleviated the prevailing sense of gloom.

 

DECISION

 

The Tribunal had no doubt that some saving would be obtained if the communal lighting were regulated by a time clock. However it was impossible to estimate the extent of that saving because all the communal electricity charges were monitored by one meter. On the basis of the evidence it would not be great. Allowing for the cost of running the lifts, security cameras and cleaning appliances it was unlikely to exceed £1,000 per year and in all probability it would be less than that figure. The saving to each tenant would be no more than about £2.50 per year.

Between 1995/96 and 2001/02 the Respondent had halved the cost of communal electricity by adopting an aggressive policy of competitive tendering. If a Tribunal had been requested to determine the issue in 1995/96, when the communal lighting was regulated by a time clock, it would no doubt have concluded that the cost was reasonable; the electricity being metered and supplied by a reputable contractor. For the Tribunal to conclude that the estimated cost of the communal electricity in 2003/04 was unreasonable, when the actual cost had been halved, would be perverse.

Section 19 of the Act does not require a landlord to ensure that the cost, for any particular service, is as low as possible but rather that the cost is reasonable. For any particular service there will always be a band of reasonable cost and provided that the actual or estimated cost falls within that band it will be reasonable. Viewed in that light the Tribunal concluded that the cost estimated by the Respondent to be incurred, in the provision of communal electricity during the period 1st April 2003 to 30th September 2003, was reasonable.

The Tribunal next had to consider the application under Section 20C of the Act to limit the recoverability of the Respondent’s costs incurred, in these proceedings, through the service charge. The Tribunal had requested the Respondent to provide a schedule of its costs but despite a reminder this had not been forthcoming.

The Tribunal was not persuaded by Mr Pryce’s argument relating to The Local Government and Housing Act 1989. Taken to its logical conclusion the argument would deny long leaseholders, in a mixed local authority block such as this, a remedy granted by Parliament under the Act. That was an untenable proposition. Furthermore the Tribunal was not wholly convinced that the inevitable consequence of such an order was that the cost should fall on the secure tenants. An order under section 20C was in the nature of a sanction, the cost of which should surely and more appropriately be born by the authority’s general funds.

Nevertheless the Tribunal accepted that, having found in favour of the Respondent on the substantive issue, it should only make an order under section 20C in exceptional circumstances and where it was just and equitable to do so.

The Respondent’s conduct in dealing with the Applicant’s initial complaint left a great deal to be desired. It had persistently stated that a photo cell regulated the communal lighting: a statement which, on the evidence of Ms White, was plainly wrong. Its initial argument, that it was cheaper to leave the lights on permanently appeared insupportable. On the 3rd March 2001 it had stated that the cost differential was “less than 3 pence per lamp per year”: a statement which seemed irreconcilable with the Report which must, at that date, have been in its possession although not disclosed until a later date.

On the basis of the evidence before it, the Tribunal found, as a fact, that the Respondent had not consulted the Residents Association until after the removal of the time clocks in 1999: its assertion to the contrary in its letter to Applicant of 5th April 2000 was unhelpful. Even when it did consult the Residents Association it is apparent, from the Association’s letter of 1st October 2003, that there had not been a full disclosure of all the available information (although this should not be regarded as a criticism of Ms White who gave her evidence in an exemplary manner).

The Respondent could have addressed the Applicant’s concerns at an earlier stage but had failed to do so. It had failed to consider that one meter controlled the total communal electricity supply and that the electricity charges in the service charge accounts did not relate only to the communal lighting. It had not considered drawing the Applicant’s attention to the savings that had been achieved in recent years by its policy of price competitive tendering.

The Applicant had only made his application after exploring every other available option and he had done so, in effect, at the suggestion of the Local Authority Ombudsman who, had s/he been in possession of all the facts before the Tribunal, might well have been inclined to deal with the referral.

Taking all these factors into account the Tribunal concluded that it would it would be just and equitable to make an order under section 20C of the Act even though it had found in the Respondent’s favour on the substantive issue. The Tribunal therefore ordered that the Respondent would not be entitled to recover any of its costs incurred in these proceedings under the service charge provisions of the leases.

Finally the Tribunal had to consider whether to order the Respondent to reimburse the Applicant the whole or part of his fees of £500. It was one thing to deny the Respondent’s the recovery of their costs it was another to require them to reimburse the whole of the Applicant’s fees. The Applicant had after all pursued an application which most people would consider trivial. Weighing all the arguments made in this and the preceding paragraphs the Tribunal ordered that the Respondent should reimburse the Applicant with half of his fees incurred in making his application.
