HAMILTON COURT, 149 MAIDA VALE, LONDON W9 1QR

 

BACKGROUND

 

1.                  Hamilton Court was built in about 1930. It consists of 44 flats with retail units at ground floor level on one side of the building. The Respondent is Lydford Estates Limited, a subsidiary of J.Leon & Company Limited whose principal shareholders are members of the Slowe family.  The Respondent built Hamilton Court and has always owned it. The flats were originally let on short term tenancies. However the Respondent subsequently changed its policy and began selling flats as they became vacant.  38 flats have been sold on long leases and 6 remain under the control of the Respondent.

2.                  Mr Morse, the Applicant, is the lessee of flat 47. He and his former wife lived there for approximately 2 years, as tenants, before buying the flat in 1992. They continued to live in the flat until 1999 when it was transferred into his sole name. He then moved to his current address at 44 Woodstock Road, Golders Green, London NW11 8ER, since when the flat has periodically been let. Of the 38 flats that have been sold it would seem that just over half are owner occupied whilst the remainder are either second homes or let on short term tenancies.

3.                  Some18 years ago Mr Wayne, at the request of the Respondent, formed Hamilton Court Leaseholders Association (the Association). The Association was formally recognised by the Respondent by a letter dated 18th April 1985. Mr Wayne is the owner-occupier of flat 14 and has, at all times, been the Chairman of the Association.

4.                  The Association originally had a constitution which provided for an opt-in membership and payment of a small subscription to cover administration charges. However many lessees chose not to participate in the affairs of the Association and over time the constitution fell into disuse: all lessees were deemed to be members and the subscription was dropped. Ultimately the Association came under the control of a self-perpetuating committee: there was an Annual Meeting to which all lessees were invited but few attended and the committee members did not offer themselves for re-election.

5.                  The Applicant was critical of these arrangements and on any objective assessment he was entitled to be so. However, on the basis of Mr Waynes evidence, the current situation is a product of the general apathy of the majority of the lessees who have, until recently, taken no interest in the affairs of the Association. The Applicant himself could have played an active role in the affairs of the Association but chose not to do so. It seemed to the Tribunal a little unreasonable that he should now criticise those who, for many years, without remuneration or thanks have done their best to represent the interests of the lessees.

6.                  The Respondent appointed Gross Fine of 14/16 Stephenson Way, London NW1 2HD as managing agents. However they played no part in these proceedings and their role seems to be an administrative one. Certainly the Respondent adopts a very hands on approach to the management of Hamilton Court. It seemed to the Tribunal that the Respondent has perhaps failed to fully appreciate that with the sale of most of the flats the equity in Hamilton Court has effectively passed beyond its control. That is perhaps not surprising given its long association with Hamilton Court.

7.                  At the beginning of each financial year the Respondent prepares an estimate of the total service charge expenditure for the forthcoming year. That estimate forms the basis for the interim service charge payable by each of the lessees. At the end of each financial year the Respondent prepares an account of the actual expenditure incurred during that year. If the actual expenditure exceeds the estimated expenditure the shortfall is recovered from the lessees through a balancing charge: if there is surplus it is carried forward and applied against the following years expenditure.

9.                  On publication of the 2001/2002 estimate the Association raised the increasing expenditure with the Respondent. Mr Michael Slowe, on behalf of the Respondent, indicated that the company would respond sympathetically to requests from individual lessees to defer part of the service charge for twelve months. This information was circulated to the lessees.

10.              The Applicant found himself in difficult financial circumstances and wrote to the Respondent in December 2001 requesting that the second instalment of the 2001/2002 interim service charge be deferred on the grounds of financial hardship.

11.              Ms Tania Slowe, on behalf of the Respondent, rejected this request. This rejection appears to have caused the Applicant to question the reasonableness of both the estimated and the actual expenditure. His concerns were exacerbated by the publication of the service charge account for 2001/2002 and the estimate for 2002/2003. He was particularly concerned by a proposal to refurbish the ground floor common parts of Hamilton Court during 2002/2003.

12.              The Applicant then sent a number of letters to both the Association and the Respondent questioning the expenditure. This correspondence culminated in a seven-page letter to Ms Tania Slowe on 13th August 2002.  Although the Applicant raised many salient points in that letter he somewhat obfuscated his case by emphasising the upward trend of the expenditure rather than concentrating on the reasonableness of the expenditure. At the same time the Applicant wrote to all the lessees, sharing his concerns, and inviting them to contact him.

13.              Ms Slowe took exception to the Applicants approach. On the 20th August 2002 she replied to his letter of 13th August 2002. Whilst her letter dealt with some of the Applicants questions it was generally dismissive and betrayed a not wholly unreasonable sense of irritation with his persistent questioning. Whether it was reasonable to conclude by writing that she was not prepared to continue the correspondence is however open to doubt.

14.              The Applicant continued his correspondence. Although Mr Wayne did his best to deal with the Applicants questions the Respondent was only prepared to respond through its solicitors. Believing that his questions had not been satisfactorily answered the Applicant, on 5th December 2002, applied to the Tribunal for a determination as to the reasonableness of the estimated costs proposed to be incurred during the year ending 31st March 2003. The application was made under the provisions of section 19(2B) of the Landlord and tenant act 1985 (the Act). He also applied, under section 20C of the Act, for an order limiting the Respondents ability to recover costs incurred in these proceedings through the service charge.

15.              At a pre-trial review on 19th February 2003 a differently constituted Tribunal issued directions. With the agreement of the parties it limited the issues in dispute to the following: -

a.       Service charge account: year ended 31st March 2002: -

                                                               i.      Porterage and cleaning; wages and employers national insurance contribution (£25,017)

                                                             ii.      Cleaning contract (£7,492)

                                                            iii.      Internal decorations (£15,750)

                                                           iv.      Consultants fees in connection with ground floor lobby refurbishment (£1,377)

b.      Estimated cost of services: year ending 31st March 2003

                                                               i.      Internal redecorations to main lobby (£46,000)

 

16.              Other directions issued at the pre-trial review, relating to an exchange of statements, the disclosure of documents and the preparation of a trial bundle had in the main been complied with. The Tribunal was authorised by the directions to consider requiring the Respondents to reimburse the Applicant with the whole or part of his fees under article 8 of the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal (Fees) order 1997.

 

17.              By the date of the hearing draft accounts for the year ending 31st March 2003 had been published and the parties agreed that the hearing should proceed on the basis that the Tribunal would determine the actual cost of the internal redecorations to the main lobby (£34,040 plus additional consultants fees of £4,255) rather than the estimated cost as agreed at the pre-trial review.

 

THE LEASE

 

18.              The Tribunal had before it a copy of the Applicants lease dated 20th February 1992. It understood that all the leases were in a similar form.

19.              Clause 5 of the Lease sets out the landlords obligations. In particular the following are relevant: -

a.       Sub-clause (5) requires the landlord to maintain in good and substantial repair and condition the main structure and common parts of Hamilton Court (referred to in the Lease as the Building).

b.      Sub-clause (7)(i) relates to the provision of porterage and is worth reciting in its entirety: -

Toemploy during normal working hours such porter or porters or other servant or servants for the Building as the Lessor shall in its absolute discretion deem necessary to carry out such duties as are usual in a residential building of the character of the Building.

c.       Sub-clause (8) requires the landlord to keep the internal common parts cleansed decorated carpeted and reasonably furnished and lighted.

20.              The fifth schedule to the Lease sets out those expenses which can be brought into account in calculating the service charge. They include: -

a.       All the costs incurred by the landlord in carrying out its obligations referred to in paragraph 19 above.

b.      The cost of insuring the Building.

c.       The proper fees and charges of any Managing Agents employed by the Lessor for the general management and running of the Building and for the collection of rents and service charges payable in respect of the flats in the Building. Alternatively the landlord may itself manage the Building and if it does so it may charge a reasonable fee.

d.      All fees and costs incurred in the preparation of the service charge certificates and in the preparation and audit of the service charge accounts.

e.       The provision of a reserve fund to meet either current or future expenditure.

21.              As explained in paragraph 7 above the landlord prepares an estimate of its total expenditure at the beginning of each service charge year and an account of its actual expenditure at the end of each such year. The provisions relating to the payment of the service charge are to be found in sub-clause 4(4) of the Lease. The tenant pays 2.66% of the expenditure incurred by the landlord and can be required to make interim payments on account of his ultimate liability on 24th June and 25th December in each year. The interim payments are of course based upon the annual estimates prepared by the landlord at the beginning of each financial year.

22.              Sub-clause 4(4)(d) specifically permits the landlord to include in the service charge account not only expenditure which has actually been incurred during the year but a reasonable provision for anticipated expenditure of a periodically recurring nature, whether recurring at regular or irregular intervals.

23.              Taking into account the provisions of the lease the following points can be made: -

a.       There is no provision in the Lease which would enable the landlord to recover the cost of improvements carried out to the Building save to the extent that they could legitimately be considered to be repairs.

b.      There is no express provision in the Lease which would enable the landlord to recover its legal costs save to the extent, if at all, that they could be considered to be incidental to the proper fees and charges of the Managing Agents incurred in the management of the Building.

c.       Before the Tribunal Ms Slowe on behalf of the Respondent said that as a matter of policy the Respondent neither operated a reserve fund nor invoked sub-clause 4(4)(d) of the lease as a means of defraying the cost of major works. The Respondent is perfectly entitled to adopt such a policy. However the Tribunal has no doubt that the implementation of that policy contributed substantially to these proceedings and will, in future, cause substantial difficulties for many of the tenants as the Respondents implement a programme of major works which will become necessary to maintain what is now an ageing building.

 

INSPECTION

 

24.              The Tribunal inspected Hamilton Court on the morning of 19th May 2003. Mr Wayne, Ms Slowe, Mr Kingsley (from Gross Fine), Ms Wilkinson (the Respondents solicitor) and Mr Crane (the porter) were in attendance but did not accompany the Tribunal on its inspection.

25.              Hamilton Court is a seven-storey building including a semi-basement. It is situated at the junction of Maida Vale and Elgin Avenue. It is in the shape of a letter H, with the main entrance door in the bar of the letter H, facing Maida Vale. To the front of the main entrance door a circular drive, within the wings of the building, encloses a garden area. To the rear is a trade entrance, at semi-basement level, with a forecourt presumably for use by service and delivery vehicles. As has been observed, Hamilton Court consists of 44 flats with retail units at ground floor level on the side facing Elgin Avenue. These are presumably let on commercial leases. On the other side a vehicular drive runs the length of the building.

26.              Hamilton Court is of brick construction with metal frame windows. It reflects the art deco architecture of the period in which it was built. Externally it is in reasonable condition. The old metal-framed windows have been reasonably well maintained but are coming to the end of their natural life. The down pipes and ironwork are in need of some redecoration but are basically sound.

27.              The main entrance door leads to a large reception lobby. Facing the entrance door is a mirrored alcove, enclosing a small period statue, and on either side a  mirror of reasonable proportions. There are two new leather sofas in the reception lobby. On each side, the main lobby opens onto a subsidiary lobby which provides access to a flat to the rear: beyond each subsidiary lobby is a hall which provides access to a communal lift, a service cupboard, a communal staircase and the individual flats situated in the wings of Hamilton Court.

28.              The whole of the ground floor reception area has been redecorated and refurbished to a high standard using good quality materials. The doors leading to the individual flats, lifts and service cupboards have been re-varnished as part of the refurbishment. A new broadloom carpet has been laid and the original period light fittings have been overhauled. The original radiators have not been boxed in. The refurbishment is in keeping with the style of the Hamilton Court and presents an attractive entrance to the building. Although not refurbished the stairwells and landings on the upper floors have also been redecorated to an acceptable standard.

 

THE HEARING

 

29.              The hearing took place on the afternoon of 19th May 2003 and on 20th May 2003. Miss Harrison, a barrister, represented the Respondent. Miss Wilkinson, a solicitor, of Hamlins, instructed her. Ms Slowe gave evidence on behalf of the Respondent. Mr Morse, the Applicant, presented his own case and gave evidence. Mr Wayne attended on behalf of the Association and assisted the Tribunal by clarifying a number if issues as they arose.

 

PRELIMINARY ISSUE

 

30.              One of the Applicants principal arguments was that the refurbishment of the ground floor reception area amounted to an improvement: under the terms of the Lease the Respondent could not recover any costs incurred in carrying out improvements. His argument required the Tribunal to interpret the terms of the Lease and that was not currently within its jurisdiction: the Tribunal could only determine the reasonableness of the costs incurred. The Commonhold and Leasehold Reform Act 2002 (CLRA) extended the Tribunals jurisdiction in such a way that it would in future be able to interpret the terms of any lease under consideration. The relevant provisions of CLRA have not however been brought into effect. It was anticipated that the confirming order, bringing them into effect, would be introduced in July 2003. He therefore requested that the hearing be adjourned until July 2003.

31.              Miss Harrison opposed the request. There was no certainty that the confirming order would be retrospective: if it were not the adjournment would be otiose. In the meantime the Respondent was being denied the opportunity to recover substantial service charges arrears.

32.              The Tribunal declined the request. The Tribunal had to address the law as it was and not as it might be at some future date. There was no certainty that the confirming order would be introduced in July 2003: it had already been delayed. There was merit in Ms Harrisons argument. Furthermore the Applicant was not denied a remedy. The Respondent would no doubt sue for the arrears and the Applicant could defend the action to the extent that that the sum claimed was not recoverable. As the arrears were less than £5,000 the Applicant would not be at risk as to costs.

 

ISSUES EVIDENCE AND CONCLUSIONS

 

33.              Set out below is a description of each issue in dispute, a brief summary of the evidence given and the Tribunals conclusions.

 

Porterage and cleaning

 

34.              In issue were two elements of the total porterage and cleaning costs for the year ending 31st March 2002 (2001/2): -

a.       Mr Crane, the porter, had had a heart attack in about July 2001 and had been unable to work for approximately four months. Furthermore he had had a four-week holiday during the year. During both those periods the Respondent had used Golden Office Cleaning Services to provide cover. As far as the Tribunal could ascertain the additional cost over and above Mr Cranes salary amounted to £8,721 of which £6,663 related to his incapacity and £2,058 to his holiday. The Applicant disputed these costs.

b.      Golden Office Cleaning Services provided cleaning services and the total costs incurred during 2002/3 amounted to £7,492. The applicant disputed these costs.

35.              The Applicant argued that the Mr Cranes contract of employment made no provision for sick pay. To the extent that he was paid during his incapacity the cost should have been borne by the Respondent, a profitable company owned by a wealthy family. Alternatively the Respondent should have placed insurance to cover a potential liability of this nature.  He also argued that the costs incurred in using Golden Office Cleaning Services were not recoverable under the terms of the lease. Although he did not develop this argument he presumably had in mind the provisions of clause 5(7)(i) of the Lease which provide for the employment of a porter rather than the use of an independent sub-contractor to provide porterage facilities.

36.              As to the cleaning contract he pointed out that no provision for the cost had been included in the estimate for the year which, under the heading Porterage and cleaning had simply included an estimated cost of £25,000 for Wages and employers national insurance contributions. He assumed, not unreasonably, that the additional cost of £7,492 must have resulted from Mr Cranes absence. In the alternative he disputed the reasonableness of the cost on the basis that the contract had not been awarded after a process of competitive tender.

37.              Ms Slowe said that Mr Crane had been employed at Hamilton Court for 18 years. During his illness his wife had undertaken some of his duties and contractors had only been used to provide those duties which she could not undertake. She argued that it was perfectly reasonable for the Respondent to continue to pay Mr Crane during his illness; it was the act of a responsible employer. She also asserted that it was reasonable to use independent contractors to provide cover for those duties not undertaken by Mr Cranes wife.

38.              As to the cost of the cleaning contract Ms Slowe explained that the Respondent had until 1999 employed an under porter at Hamilton Court. In 1999 the Respondent came to the conclusion that the services provide by the under porter could be carried out more effectively by contract cleaners. There had not been a process of competitive tender but Gross Fine had researched the market and were of the opinion that the quotation submitted by Golden Office Cleaning Services was competitive. She said that the contract cost had been included in the service charge accounts since 1999. She explained that the global figure of £25,000 included in the 2001/2 estimate covered both Mr Cranes wages and the cleaning contract cost: she drew the Tribunals attention to the previous years service charge account which recorded a total cost of £24,045 for porterage and cleaning.

39.              The Tribunal considered that the Respondent had acted reasonably in continuing to pay Mr Cranes wages during his illness. It was entitled to recover those wages, through the service charge, under the provisions of the Lease. Its profitability and the apparent wealth of its shareholders was wholly irrelevant. In reaching this conclusion the Tribunal was fortified by the Applicants acceptance, under cross-examination, that he alone of the lessees objected to this cost.

40.              With regard to the cost of using Golden Office Cleaning Services to provide cover for Mr Crane, the Applicants argument relied on an interpretation of sub-clause 5(7)(i) of the Lease which was currently beyond the jurisdiction of the Tribunal. Nevertheless to the extent that the Respondent was obliged to use its best endeavours to provide porterage facilities it was entirely reasonable that the Respondent should hire independent contractors to provide short-term emergency cover. Whilst the Applicant may have a point it is not a practical one: in reality any other option would almost certainly have left the residents without porterage facilities for a number of months. Insurance cover did not strike the Tribunal as a realistic option and in any event it was a case of swings and roundabouts: over the years the premiums, which would have been recovered from the lessees, would no doubt have equalled the costs in dispute.

41.              As to the cost of the cleaning contract the Tribunal had considerable sympathy for the Applicant to the extent that the 2001/2 estimate had clearly caused a degree of confusion: the Tribunal had been confused until Ms Slowe had provided an explanation. The Applicant, however, had produced no evidence to show that the cost was inherently unreasonable. The contract had been in place since 1999 and had not previously been challenged: the cost for 2001/2 was consistent with the cost for the previous year. Certainly the cost did not strike the Tribunal as being unreasonable and it accepted Ms Slowes evidence which had not been challenged. The Tribunal therefore concluded that the costs had been reasonably incurred.

 

Internal decorations

 

42.              In issue was the cost of £15,750 incurred in the redecoration of the stairwells and landings on the upper floors. When the Respondent first decided to redecorate the landings and stairwells the estimated cost was put at £22,000. As has been observed the Respondent does not operate a reserve fund to cover expenditure of this type. However following representations made by the Association it agreed to collect the estimated costs over two years. Accordingly £11,000 was included in the estimated service charge expenditure for 2000/1 with the intention that the balance would be collected in the following year. However the work was not carried out during that year. The £11,000 should have been set aside; instead it was used to defray other costs actually incurred in that year. The lessees therefore had the benefit of the money.

43.              Further confusion was caused by letters issued by the Respondent, Gross Fine and the Association indicating that only a small balance of the actual cost would be included in the 2001/2 accounts; these letters having been sent in the genuine but mistaken belief that the sum of £11,000 collected in 2000/1 had been set aside to pay for the work. The work was actually carried out in 2001/2 and, no money having been retained from the previous year to defray part of the cost, the actual cost of £15,750 was included in the service charge account for that year.

44.              The Applicant had not been inclined to accept the clear explanation that had been provided to him in correspondence. Before the Tribunal the Applicant said that he did not dispute the reasonableness of the cost but rather the process by which it was charged.

45.              Ms Slowe accepted that a mistake had been made but it was a genuine one and the lessees had not suffered a loss. She did not accept that the application of funds to expenditure for which it had not been collected amounted to a breach of the RICS Service Charge Residential Management Code. However when pressed she said that she was unaware of the existence of the code.

46.              The Tribunal concluded that the costs had been reasonably incurred. Indeed they had not been disputed. The contract for the work had been placed following a tender process and after consultation with the lessees, as required by the Act.

 

Insurance

 

47.              Before the Tribunal the Applicant asked that the insurance cost for 2001/2 be put in issue notwithstanding that it was not an issue identified at the pre-trial review. Ms Harrison did not oppose the application and the Tribunal agreed to it.

48.              The Applicant based his case on the substantial rise in the insurance costs. They had risen from £5,312 in 2000/1 to £12,807 in 2002/3. Ms Slowe said that the insurance was placed by brokers who tested the market on an annual basis. The increase resulted from both the poor claims record at Hamilton Court and a substantial tightening in the insurance market resulting from a lack of liquidity.

49.              The Applicant had produced no evidence to demonstrate that the cost was of itself unreasonable. The Tribunal accepted Ms Slowes evidence which was consistent with its own experience. The cost was reasonable.

 

 

The refurbishment of the ground floor lobby

 

50.              In issue was the consultants fee of £1,377 included in the 2001/2 accounts and the sum of £38,033 actually incurred in 2002/3 (the draft accounts referred to a figure of £38,295 but before the Tribunal Miss Harrison said that the lower figure of £38,033 was the correct one). Of the £38,033 further consultants fees accounted for £4,255.

51.              The Applicant objected to both the timing and scale of these works. They could have been deferred for a year to ease the cash flow difficulties experienced by many of the lessees following previous increases in the service charge. The scale of the refurbishment had been lavish and amounted to an improvement. The use of architects to design and supervise the work had been unnecessary. He criticised in particular the use of expensive broadloom carpeting, the replacement of the mirrors and sofas and the renovation of the old light fittings. The work should have been carried out to the standard of the previous years redecoration of the stairwells and landings: if it had been he believed that the cost would have been in the region of £16,000. He concluded by saying that many of the lessees had expressed their concern to him about the scale and the cost of these works.

52.              Ms Slowe pointed out that the lobby had last been refurbished in 1983 and last redecorated in 1991/2. There was a design element to these works which justified the use of architects: their fees of 12.5% of the contract price were reasonable. If architects had not been used the work would have been undertaken by Gross Fine who would have charged a similar fee. There had been full consultation with the Association. Many although not all the suggestions made by the Association had been accepted resulting in a reduction in the total cost of some £8,000. In particular the extent of the new mirrors had been scaled back: the light fittings had been renovated rather than replaced in their entirety: the proposed radiator covers had been omitted from the scheme. The broadloom carpet had been fitted at the request of the Association. Although more expensive than originally planned it was of superior quality and would last longer. The Association had balloted all the lessees and a clear majority supported the scheme. The contractors had been appointed after a proper tender process and the lessees had been consulted in accordance with the provisions of the Act. All payments had been made to the contractors against certificates issued by the architects.

53.              The Tribunal did not believe that the works amounted to an improvement of the lobby. An obligation to repair did not mean that a landlord must only replace like with like. A landlord was entitled to take into account changing fashions, the increased expectations of residents and new building techniques and materials, to the extent that it was reasonable to do so. The Respondent had consulted with the Association which, for all its imperfections, had balloted the lessees a majority of whom were in favour.

54.              Having inspected Hamilton Court it considered that the scale of the refurbishment works was reasonable and in keeping with the architecture of the building. On the basis of Ms Slowes evidence the work was long overdue. There was a design element in the work which justified the use of supervising architects rather than managing agents. The Tribunal therefore concluded that the costs had been reasonably incurred.

 

DECISION

 

55.              The Tribunal having concluded that all the costs in issue were reasonably incurred it determined that:-

a.       The total costs amounting to £119,780 in the service charge year ending 31st March 2002 were reasonably incurred; and

b.      Subject to final audit costs of  £38,033 were reasonably incurred in the service charge year ending 31st March 2003 in respect of the refurbishment of the lobby, including consultants fees.

56.              The Tribunal next had to consider the application under Section 20C of the Act to limit the recoverability of the Respondents costs incurred, in these proceedings, through the service charge. Through Miss Harrison, Miss Wilkinson produced a breakdown of the Respondents costs which totalled £15,407.19 inclusive of VAT.  Having regard to the decision in St Marys Mansions Ltd V Limegate Investment Co Ltd ((2003) 05 EG 146) the Tribunal doubted that the provisions of the Lease recited at paragraph 20 would entitle the Respondent to recover its legal costs incurred in these proceedings. Nevertheless as the law now stands the interpretation of the Lease is not a matter for the Tribunal. It therefore considered it appropriate to consider the application on its merits leaving it to others to determine the recoverability of the Respondents costs.

57.              The Tribunal had considerable sympathy for the Applicant. His application had not been frivolous. He had restricted his arguments to a small number of issues in an attempt to reduce costs. The Respondents decision to close down communications rather than provide a full explanation to the Applicant had been unhelpful. The Respondents failure to separate out the porterage costs from the cleaning contract costs in the 2001/2 estimate had caused considerable confusion. The Respondents mistake in failing to set aside the money, collected in 2000/1, to defray the cost of internal decorations, had left the lessees with a false sense of security and had caused a degree of justifiable resentment. The Tribunal was surprised and disappointed that the Respondent had been unaware of the RICS Residential Management Code.

58.              Taking these factors into account and having regard to the decision of P R Francis FRICS in Maryland Estates Limited v Patsy Anne Lynch and David Wilson (2002) LRX/57/1999 the Tribunal concluded that it would it would be just and equitable to make a limited order under section 20C of the Act even though it had found in the Respondents favour under every head of expenditure in issue.

59.              The Tribunal considered that the correct approach was to consider the reasonableness of the Respondents costs. His Honour Judge Rich had said in Tenants of Langford Court v Doren Limited (2001) (LT) LRX/37/2000 that Section 20C may provide a short route by which a tribunal which has heard the litigation giving rise to the costs can avoid arguments under s19. To do otherwise would result in a second set of proceedings and in a differently constituted tribunal considering costs which the Tribunal was uniquely qualified to judge, having heard the case.

60.              The Tribunal considered that the costs incurred by the Respondent were wholly disproportionate both to the sums in issue and the nature of the case, which was not complex. The Respondent was perfectly entitled to be represented by both barrister and solicitor. It did not automatically follow that it was reasonable that the consequential costs should be visited on the lessees. Specialist tribunals such as the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal had been created, in part, to offer members of the public a cost effective remedy without the attendant high cost culture that is so much a feature of civil litigation. That did not mean that the cost of both barrister and solicitor would never be reasonable incurred. In complex cases involving issues of law their costs would no doubt be reasonably incurred. That was not the case here. Cases of this nature were routinely conducted by surveyors at costs in the region of £3,000 to £4,000. That seemed more appropriate for a case presented to a specialist tribunal able to rely, to an extent, upon its own knowledge and experience. The Tribunal would therefore limit the Respondents costs that may be recovered under the service charge provisions of the Lease, to £4,000 plus VAT.

61.              Finally the Tribunal had to consider whether to order the Respondent to reimburse the Applicant the whole or part of his fees of £150. The Applicant having failed to obtain any reduction in the service charge costs the Tribunal did not consider that it would be appropriate to make such an order.
