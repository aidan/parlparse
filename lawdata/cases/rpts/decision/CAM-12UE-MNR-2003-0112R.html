5.         THE LAW

 

5.1               In general terms, the task of the Committee under the provisions of section 14 of the Housing Act 1988 is to determine the rent at which the dwelling-house concerned might reasonably be expected to be let in the open market by a willing landlord. It follows that the personal circumstances of the parties are irrelevant and the Committee cannot take them into account.

 

5.2               The Committee must disregard any effect on the rent attributable to the granting of a tenancy to a sitting tenant, any increase in value attributable to “relevant” tenants’ improvements and any reduction in value attributable to a failure by the tenant to comply with any terms of the tenancy. Full Reasons (if requested) will set out the relevant legal principles in detail.

 

5.3               The Committee has no jurisdiction unless the landlord’s notice of increase is valid and duly served. The landlord’s right to an increase and the tenant’s right to apply to the Committee both depend upon due service of a valid notice. A notice of increase is invalid if it purports to increase the rent with effect from a date less than 12 months after the date of operation of the last valid notice of increase. A monthly tenant is entitled to at least one month’s notice of increase. A notice served less than one month before the date when the increase is to take effect is invalid.

 

5.4               No statutory or other special provisions relating to service of documents assist a landlord serving a section 13 notice unless the tenancy agreement makes provision for a particular mode of service. The landlord must show, on the balance of probabilities, that the relevant notices came to the attention of the tenant and when that happened.

 

 

6.         THE DECISION

 

6.1        The Committee does not need to decide when the tenant was served with the notice dated 14.9.02. If it was not served in time, it was not valid. But in that event the last valid notice took effect on 21.8.01. Either way, the Committee has jurisdiction.  Disputes over breaches of covenant are not a matter for the Committee.

 

The Committee must value the property in its present condition. The parties’ evidence is of only limited assistance on the issue of rental values. Based on the Committee's own knowledge and experience the open market rental value of the subject property, if let fully modernised and in good order throughout, with central heating, carpets and curtains, would be £525 p.c.m.

 

The Committee considers that the tenant has high standards and has laid new carpets and installed new kitchen fittings to enhance his enjoyment of the property. The Committee concludes that, with normal use, the landlord’s carpets and kitchen fittings, though not smart, would still be serviceable.  Stripping out the tenant’s improvements and allowing for the lack of a functional boiler and other matters referred to, a deduction of £60 p.c.m. is called for. This gives a figure of £465 p.c.m. excluding Council Tax.

 

The tenancy agreement appears to call for the landlord to pay the Council Tax. The Committee therefore proposes to include the Council Tax in the registered rent. It is common ground that, in practice, the tenant pays the Council Tax direct. There is insufficient evidence to determine whether the parties have agreed to vary the terms of the 1991 agreement. However, it is clear that, if the tenant pays the Council Tax direct, he is not obliged to include it in his payments to the landlord.

 

6.2        Tenant's improvements (to be disregarded) include new fronts to kitchen units and possibly the carcasses also. The Committee gives the tenant the benefit of the doubt on this issue. The tenant’s improvements have been stripped out and thus already taken into account.

 

Reasonable open market rent                   £533.54 p.c.m. including Council Tax

Undue hardship to tenant:                        Not argued
