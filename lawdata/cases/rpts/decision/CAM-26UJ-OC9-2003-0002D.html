Introduction

1.         This is an application by Glen Chess Guernsey Ltd. (the Applicant landlord) to determine the amount of costs, if any, which the Glen Chess Residents’ Association (the Respondent tenants) should pay pursuant to Section 33 of the Act.   This follows the purported service of an initial notice under Section 13 claiming the right to exercise the right to collective enfranchisement of the property.

 

2.         In their response to the application, the Respondent tenants had raised an issue as to whether the Tribunal had jurisdiction to deal with the application as both parties had accepted that the initial notice was invalid.     At the direction of the Tribunal counsel for each party had provided a very helpful skeleton argument addressing this issue which the Tribunal had directed should be dealt with as a preliminary issue.

 

The Preliminary Issue

3.         Mr. Healey, on behalf of the Respondent tenants argued that as the initial notice was invalid, Section 33 did not apply.    He said that any entitlement to recover costs is contingent upon the service of a valid notice or an apparently valid notice.      He referred to Section 38 and Schedule 3 of the Act.   He also referred to the generally accepted and authoritative textbook Hague and cases mentioned therein.

 

4.         His argument for these propositions was in the alternative.     If the initial notice was invalid, Section 33 was not triggered.     If that did not succeed as an argument then Section 33 was not triggered if the initial notice was not valid on the face of the document.      The latter argument has many similarities to the law of contract where a contract can be void or voidable.      If the initial notice was invalid on the face of it, then it was void, that is to say it never existed as opposed to a voidable notice which existed up to the point when it was avoided.

 

5.         As yet a further alternative, Mr. Healey argued that if Section 33 was triggered then it ceased to have effect immediately and thus the liability for costs would be little, if anything.

 

6.         Mr. Healey then went on to consider matters of estoppel and the policy argument ventured by Mr. Gallagher on behalf of the Applicant landlord.    He referred to the three previous LVT decisions raised by Mr. Gallagher in his skeleton argument.

 

7.         Mr. Gallagher argued that the service of the notice triggered Section 33.        He also dealt with the issues of estoppel and policy.    He then argued that although both parties eventually agreed that the initial notice was invalid, that matter was never adjudicated upon and it was perfectly possible to argue that the notice could have been valid.     His argument was based on recent court rulings which, he argued, showed a move by the courts to be more lenient than before about defects in statutory notices.    The particular case he referred to was Tudor and others v M25 Group Ltd [2003] EWCA Civ 1760.        This is a Court of Appeal case which decided that although a statutory notice served pursuant to Section 11A of the Landlord and Tenant Act 1987 did not contain the addresses of the flats of the qualifying tenants – as was required by the Act – the notice was nevertheless still valid.

 

8.         In the present case the three areas of objection to the initial notice can be summarised as being the fact that Glen Chess Residents’ Association cannot be a nominee purchaser, a problem over service of the counter-notice being too short and a problem over the fact that this is a multi building estate and would require more than one notice.     Both counsel accepted that for the purpose of this hearing the main objection was the first one.

 

The Tribunal’s Decision on the Preliminary Issue

9.         A Leasehold Valuation Tribunal (LVT) is an expert Tribunal.    In most, if not all, Statutes which enable an LVT to assume jurisdiction there is a Section which allows parties to go to a court to obtain a declaration.    The reason for this is clear.    An LVT has a valuer and, usually, two other members.     It is simply not equipped to decide upon complex jurisdictional issues.    The Tribunal came very close in this case to adjourning matters to enable the parties or either of them to seek a declaration from a county court judge on the question of whether the LVT had jurisdiction.

 

10.       However, as this particular LVT consisted of a valuer and 2 experienced lawyer members, it decided to proceed.

 

11.       The relevant part of Section 33 of the Act says:-

 

                        “Where a notice is given under section 13, then …

                        …the nominee purchaser shall be liable for the

                        (the landlord’s) reasonable costs of and incidental

                        to any of the following matters…..”

 

12.       It would be a strange piece of legislation indeed which said, in effect, that if a competent tenant serves a proper notice, then he has to pay the landlord’s costs, but if an incompetent tenant serves a notice which is invalid, he does not have to pay any of the landlord’s costs.     Mr. Healey argues that the initial notice was invalid on the face of the document.     The tribunal did not accept that.    Glen Chess Residents’ Association could have been the ‘trading’ name of a valid nominee purchaser or a court may have decided that any defect did not affect the validity of the notice.

 

13.       The Respondent tenants’ solicitors maintained vigorously that the notice was valid.   Until the 24th July 2003 when the solicitors conceded the point, the Applicant landlord was at least required, in the opinion of the Tribunal,  to take some action to protect it’s position.

 

14.       The only way in which this Tribunal can make sense of the Statute is to use the words of the Section literally.      Where a notice is given under Section 13, then Section 33 is triggered automatically.    Whatever may be said about the notice, on the 12th March 2003 when the initial notice was sent, it was “a notice given under section 13” even if it was not, in the eyes of the Applicant landlord, received as such.    Further, in the eyes of the Respondent tenants, it purportedly remained “a notice given under section 13” until 24th July.

 

15.       Whilst the members of this Tribunal were very grateful to counsel for their detailed and complex submissions on jurisdiction, it does seem to the Tribunal that the first rule of interpretation i.e. to look at the wording of the relevant Section of the Statute, perhaps became lost in other more complex arguments.       In these circumstances, the Tribunal decided that Section 33 was triggered by the service of the initial notice and that the Act does not say that one should not look at the validity of the notice in hindsight.    The Respondent tenants’ liability to the landlord did not stop until 24th July when the notice was, in effect, withdrawn.

 

Leave to Appeal on Preliminary Issue

16.       Mr. Healey asked the Tribunal for leave to appeal the preliminary issue.     Very careful consideration was given to this request because this did appear to be an issue which had not previously been argued in a court.   Having said that, it had clearly been argued before LVT’s and this Tribunal took the view that there were no reasonable prospect of an appeal succeeding.

 

17.       In these circumstances, the Tribunal refused leave to appeal..

 

The Reasonableness of the Costs Claimed

18.       The parties helpfully complied with the Tribunal’s directions in producing a document in the form of points of dispute and replies.      This enabled the Tribunal to consider each area of dispute.     Having said that, the Respondent tenants’ main point throughout was that there was no need for the Applicant landlord to have incurred most of this expenditure when it was clear to such landlord that the initial notice was invalid.   In particular it was argued that no valuer’s fees should have been incurred.

 

19.       The Applicant landlord also argued a general point namely that a proper interpretation of Section 33 (2) was that if this particular landlord was willing to pay these particular costs then that was the end of the matter.    With respect to Mr. Gallagher, that cannot be right because it would almost always exclude the jurisdiction of an LVT.     The words ‘reasonable’ and ‘reasonably’ both appear in this subsection and that must, in the view of this Tribunal, mean that the objective test of reasonableness is to be applied by an LVT when considering costs incurred by a landlord.    

 

20.       The first detailed issue to be raised was the question of the solicitors’ hourly rate.      This was closely related to the issue of counsel’s fees.      In basic terms, a Grade A solicitor, experienced in this field, should have dealt with the issues in this case speedily and with little, if any, reliance on counsel.     In this case, however, the solicitors seem to have relied almost totally on counsel for each step taken.      They really cannot expect a client to pay high hourly rates for a solicitor who appears to want to accept as little responsibility or exercise as little expertise as possible.   

 

21.       Mr. Gallagher had no real idea of the experience of the conducting solicitors.   He caused enquiries to be made which revealed that Mr. Parker qualified as a barrister in 1973 and became a solicitor in 1983.    He is an experienced litigation solicitor but not in enfranchisement in particular.    Mr. Lane qualified in 1972 and his main experience is in conveyancing with particular experience with leases over the last 10 years.    Again, he appears to have to particular experience with enfranchisement cases.

 

22.       What we know is that the initial notice was served on or about 12th March 2003.   Counsel was immediately instructed and spent 2hours 30 minutes considering the case on the 21st March.     The Applicant landlord’s acknowledgement to the initial notice was sent on the 25th March.       Counsel was then instructed to advise or settle documents on no fewer than five subsequent occasions.     There can hardly have been heavier reliance on counsel.       For the solicitors to then claim £225 per hour for their services amounting to a claim for profit costs of £3,671.25 is, in this Tribunal’s opinion, totally unreasonable.      Viewed objectively, the reasonable hypothetical client, given all the facts, would not pay such an amount.

 

23.       Trying to assess whether particular items of time spent by the solicitors was reasonable or not was very difficult because no attendance notes were produced to justify the claim.    For example, bearing in mind that the surveyor’s fee note makes no mention of a meeting with the solicitors, it is difficult to see how the solicitors can spend two hours in dealing with the surveyor in addition to claiming for letters and telephone calls.      103 minutes with counsel cannot be justified for a solicitor claiming such hourly rates as are being claimed.

 

24.       The solicitors’ general claim for all incoming letters is not reasonable when judges in the courts have been disallowing incoming letters for years in detailed assessments of solicitor’s costs.      The reason is that the time spent in perusing routine incoming letters is reflected in the charge for the outgoing letter in reply.       Specific time spend on considering complex incoming letters should be the subject of a proper attendance note and can be charged for as an item of time.

 

25.       It is the decision of this Tribunal that an item by item consideration of the solicitors’ profit costs is not possible given the paucity of information provided.      Based on the experience of the two senior solicitor members of the Tribunal who both have great experience in this field of work, this Tribunal decides that a reasonable figure to have been incurred by solicitors relying so heavily on counsel between the 12th March and the 24th July 2003 is £500 plus VAT.     

 

26.       Turning now to the surveyors’ fees, the Tribunal accepted the submission of Mr. Gallagher that it was reasonable for the Applicant landlord in this case to consult a surveyor with expertise in enfranchisement.    The point he made was that if such surveyor advised that the purchase figure in the initial notice was reasonable, then it would make commercial sense to just overlook any defect in the notice and proceed with the sale.

 

27.       The problem is that this Applicant landlord decided not to use a local surveyor with local knowledge who would charge local hourly rates.    He decided to use a well know expert from Swiss Cottage charging central London rates to value a property in Rickmansworth, Hertfordshire.      Once again, this Tribunal considered that a hypothetical reasonable landlord, with full knowledge of the facts, would not consider this to be reasonable.     There are bound to be surveyors in and around Rickmansworth who would have been able to undertake this work to a reasonable standard.

 

28.       Furthermore, it was not necessary for the surveyor to prepare a detailed valuation in order to carry out the exercise envisaged by Mr. Gallagher.    That did not require a detailed valuation.     It required a perusal of the leases and a look at the property plus a use of local knowledge.   In the view of this Tribunal, such an exercise could have been undertaken in 2 hours by a local surveyor with experience in this work who would have charged no more than £150 per hour.

 

29.       As to counsel’s fees, the Tribunal considered that Mr. Gallagher is an expert in this work and that his charging rates are reasonable.   Accordingly the only question remaining is whether it was reasonable to instruct counsel to undertake over six hours’ work?       It is the decision of this Tribunal that it was not reasonable for counsel to have been instructed to advise “on valuers letter”.      The Tribunal would also not allow the fee for settling the counter notice because there is nothing in Section 33 (1) of the Act which would suggest that this is a charge that can be recovered from a nominee purchaser.       The commentary in Hague, 4th Edition, at page 473 does not include the cost of preparing the counter notice in its list of costs which can be recovered.

 

Decision

30.       For the reasons stated above it is the decision of this Tribunal that the reasonable costs recoverable from the nominee purchaser are:-

                                                                                                   £

                        Solicitors’ profit costs                                   500.00

                        VAT                                                                 87.50

                        Surveyor’s fees                                             300.00

                        VAT                                                                  52.50

                        Counsel’s fees                                              735.00

                        VAT                                                               128.63

                                                                                            1,803.63

 

31.       Whilst the Respondent tenants did object to paying VAT, the Applicant landlord stated that it is not VAT registered and, thus, would not be able to recover such VAT as an input.    In the absence of further evidence, this was accepted by the Tribunal and VAT is therefore payable as stated above.
