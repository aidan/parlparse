Introduction

 

1                       This is a decision on an application under the Leasehold Reform Act 1967 ("the 1967 Act") made to the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal by Mr D Hickman, Mrs S. M. Hickman, Mr A Hickman and Miss F Hickman,  leaseholders of the house and premises at  18 Radbourne Road Shirley Solihull West Midlands B90 3RT ("the subject property"). The application is under section 21(1)(a) of the 1967 Act for the determination of the price payable under section 9 of the 1967 Act for the freehold interest in the subject property.

 

2                       The subject property is held under an underlease, dated 28 December 1933  for a term of 99 years less three days from 29 September 1932  at a ground rent of £6.00 per year. The underlease was vested in the applicants by virtue of an Assent dated 24 December 1998. The unexpired term at the date of the Notice of Tenant's Claim to Acquire the Freehold ("the relevant date") was twenty-eight and a half years.

 

3                       The applicants served on the respondents a tenant's notice dated  10 February 2003 claiming to acquire the freehold interest in the subject property under the terms of the 1967 Act,  and they subsequently made the present application.

 

4                       The Tribunal accepts that the qualifying conditions for enfranchisement under the 1967 Act are satisfied.

 

 

Subject property

 

5                       The property comprises the right hand of a pair of semi-detached houses built in about 1932 at the corner of Radbourne Road and Lawnwood Avenue, Shirley, Solihull. The surrounding area is mainly residential with a large number of similar semi-detached houses in the immediate area. The house is of two storey brick construction with rendered elevations and a pitched tile roof. It has a two storey front bay and a two storey projecting gable to the front over the porch and bedroom above.

 

                         The accommodation comprises an entrance porch, hall, two reception rooms and kitchen on the ground floor with a landing, three bedrooms, bathroom and WC on the first floor. Outside there is an average size front garden with a drive and lean to garage to the side. The back garden is larger than nomally found with semi-detached houses of this type. The respective parties surveyors measured the distance from the rear elevation of the house to the back boundary at 24.1m and the maximum plot width at 18.1m. The overall plot dimensions were 41.45m being the maximum depth from the Radbourne Road frontage and 18.1m being the maximum width. The plot is fairly rectangular apart from the corner by the road junction which is splayed back 11.15m by 10..4m. The property appears to have been rewired and has partial central heating.

 

Inspection and hearing

 

6                       The Tribunal inspected the subject property on 22 October 2003  in the presence of  the representatives of both parties.

 

7                       The subsequent hearing was attended by Mr A.W. Brunt (representing the applicants
and Mr K F Davis (representing the respondents).

 

8.                      The representatives of both parties at the hearing agreed that the intermediate leasehold interest had merged with the freehold and that the notice to enfranchise under the 1967 Act had been validly served and accepted.

 

Representations of the parties

 

9                       Mr Brunt, on behalf of the applicants adopted as the basis of valuation under the 1967 Act the generally recognised three-stage approach normally attributed to Farr v Millerson Investments Ltd (1971) 22 P & CR 1055. That approach involves (i) the capitalisation of the ground rent payable under the existing lease for the remainder of the unexpired term; (ii) the identification of a modem ground rent (by decapitalising the site value); and (iii) the capitalisation of the modem ground rent as if in perpetuity deferred for the remainder of the unexpired term. The price payable on this basis is the sum of the capitalisations at stages (i) and (iii).

 

10.                    Mr Brunt stated that he had adopted the standing house approach due to the lack of evidence of sales of building land in the vicinity at the relevant date. In order to support his standing house valuation of the subject property, Mr Brunt submitted evidence based on copy advertisements printed from the internet on 17 April 2003. The advertisements included photographs of houses and street names but not full addresses and, under cross examination, Mr Brunt was unable to confirm the exact house numbers. Based on this evidence and his professional experience Mr Brunt submitted that the standing house value of the subject property at the relevant date was £185,000.00.

 

11                     Mr Brunt further submitted that the Tribunal should apply a 35  per cent figure in calculating the site value on the standing house basis, and that the appropriate percentage yield rate to be applied in capitalising the ground rent at stage (i) and decapitalising and recapitalising the site value at stages (ii) and (iii) should be 7 per cent which was the rate normally used by the Tribunal in other cases and was, in his experience, the normal rate.

 

12                     On the basis of those figures, Mr Brunt submitted the following valuation:

 

                         (i) Capitalisation of existing ground rent to termination of lease

 

                         Ground rent payable: £6.00 per year

                         Years Purchase: 28.5 years @ 7%: 12.2074

                         Capitalised ground rent: £6.00 x 12.2074 = £73.24

 

                         (ii) Modern ground rent

 

                         Standing house value of subject property: £185,000

                         Percentage attributable to site @ 35%: £64,750.

                         Annual equivalent @ 7%: £4,532.50

 

                         (iii) Capitalisation of modern ground rent

 

                         Modern ground rent (above): £4,532.50

                         Years Purchase @ 7% in perpetuity deferred 28.5 years: 2.0783

                          Capitalised modem ground rent: £4532.50 x 2.0783  = £9,419.89

 

                   The addition of the capitalised existing ground rent of £73.24 and the capitalised modem ground rent of £9,419.89 produced a figure of £9,493.00 rounded down to the nearest pound.

 

13                     Mr Davis on behalf of the respondents gave evidence of personal knowledge of values in the area and, based on his professional experience, considered the freehold vacant possession  value of the existing house to be nearer £200,000. referring in particular to a leasehold property at 37 Ufton Crescent Shirley Solihull put on the market in August 2003 at £215,000. He also submitted that the subject property was capable of being developed by the construction of a substantial detached four bedroom house and, in consequence, had a development value which he had reflected in his valuation of the subject property at £300,000. However, under cross examination, it was found that there was no planning permission to redevelop the site, no enquiries had been made of the Local Planning Authority to explore development potential and no evidence was provided to show that development would comply with Local Plan policies. Furthermore, no enquiries had been made to establish whether road access could be obtained from the side road, Lawnswood Avenue, there were trees near the Radbourne Road frontage which both representatives  agreed were subject to Tree Preservation Orders and the building line along Lawnswood Avenue would mean that any new housing would be unlikely to be built nearer than 7.1m to the road frontage.

 

14.                    Mr Davis also adopted the standing house basis and agreed a 35 per cent figure in calculating the site value on the standing house basis but submitted that the appropriate percentage yield rate to be applied at both stages of the calculation should be 6 per cent. Mr  Davis cited auction sales evidence where sales had been achieved at lower than 6%. He also drew the Tribunals attention to the current low rates of interest and said that the 7% rate had not been varied from the early 1980s when interest rates in the money markets had been higher.  Under cross examination Mr Davis agreed that he had presented a case to the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal on  8 August 2002 in respect of  1097 Alcester Road South Hollywood Birmingham (Ref: M/EH/2458) advocating a rate of 7% when money market rates had also been low.

 

15.                    On the basis of those figures Mr Davis submitted the following valuation:

 

                         (i)               Capitalisation of existing ground rent to termination of lease

 

                                            Ground rent payable:  £6.00 per year

                                            Years Purchase  : 28.5 years @ 6% : 13.495

                                            Capitalised ground rent: £6.00 x 13.495 = £80.97

 

                         (ii)              Modern Ground Rent

 

                                            Standing house value of subject property : £300,000.

                                            Percentage attributable to site@ 35 per cent: £105,000

                                            Annual equivalent @ 6%:  £6,300.00

 

                         (iii)             Capitalisation of modern ground rent

                                            Modern ground rent (above) : £6,300.00

                                            Years purchase @ 6% in perpetuity deferred 28.5 years : 3.167

                                            Capitalised modern ground rent = £6,300 x 3.167 = £19,952.10

 

                         The addition of the capitalised existing ground rent of £80.97 and the capitalised modern  ground rent of £19,952.10 produced a figure of £20,033 rounded down to the nearest point.

 

 

Decision

 

16                     The Tribunal inspected several of the comparable roads referred to by Mr Brunt and considered the evidence submitted by him in this respect. The Tribunal found that some of the comparables were in poorer locations  and were smaller properties.

 

17.                    With regard to the submission by Mr Davis in respect of the development value of the subject property the Committee accept that the standing house value has to recognize the value of fully developing the potential of the property provided that the potential identified is realistic and not fanciful. In effect there must be no planning or other statutory constraints which negate the prospect of development. The Tribunal find that there is potential for a limited extension to the subject property, but not for the erection of a detached house to replace the existing semi-detached house. The Tribunal found that  the property does have an above average size garden with the potential to develop the subject property further by an extension to the existing house.

 

18.              Using its general knowledge and experience (but no special knowledge) of property prices in the locality of the subject property, and taking into account the positive and negative features of the subject property and all other relevant factors and considerations, the Tribunal determines that the standing house value of the subject property at the relevant date was £195,000.00

 

19.                    The Tribunal considered the points made by Mr Davis that appropriate percentage yield rate should be 6% rather than 7% but found that money market rates, which could fluctuate substantially in the short term, were not necessarily related to property yields. With regard to auction sales results the prices achieved depend on other factors, including the speculative element of the vacant possession value and the possibility that purchasers may be buying without full knowledge of the legislation provisions contained in the 1967 Act. . Accordingly, the Tribunal attach limited weight to this evidence. Consistently with previous practice, and in the absence of any circumstances suggesting a departure from that practice, the Tribunal holds that the  appropriate percentage yield rate to be applied at all stages of the valuation calculation should be 7 per cent.

 

20.                    Adopting those figures, and applying figures of Years Purchase from Parry's Valuation Tables, the Tribunal calculates the price payable as follows:

 

                         (i) Capitalisation of existing ground rent to termination of lease

 

                         Ground rent payable: £6.00 per year

                         Years Purchase: 28.5 @ 7%: 12.2074

                         Capitalised ground rent: £6.00 x 12.2074 = £73.24

 

                         (ii) Modern ground rent

 

                         Standing house value of subject property: £195,000

                         Percentage attributable to site @ 35%: £68,250.00 

                         Annual equivalent @ 7%: £4,777.50

 

                         (iii) Capitalisation of modern ground rent

 

                         Modern ground rent (above): £4,777.50

                         Years Purchase at 7% in perpetuity deferred 28.5 years: 2.0783

                         Capitalised modem ground rent: £4,777.50 x 2.0783  = £9,929.07

 

                         The addition of the capitalised existing ground rent of £73.24 and the capitalised modern ground rent of £9,929.07  produces a figure of £10,002.00 rounded down to the nearest pound.

 

21                     Accordingly, the Tribunal determines the price payable under section 9 of the 1967 Act for the freehold interest in the subject property at £10,002.00

 

 

Summary

 

22                     The Tribunal determines the price payable by the tenants for the freehold interest in the subject property at £10,002.00 plus the freeholder's reasonable costs calculated in accordance with section 9(4) of the 1967 Act and paragraph 5 of Schedule 22 to the Housing Act 1980.
