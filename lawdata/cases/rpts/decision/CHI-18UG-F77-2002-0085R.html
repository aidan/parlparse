1.       Background

 

On 22nd August 2002 the landlord applied to the rent officer for registration of a fair rent of £345.00 per calendar month for the above property.  The rent payable at the time of the application was £299.00 per calendar month.

 

The rent was previously registered on 17th January 2002 with effect from 24th February 2002 at £299.00 per calendar month following a determination by the rent officer.

 

On  21st October 2002 the rent officer registered a fair rent of £319.00 per calendar month with effect from that date.

 

By a letter dated 5th November 2002 the landlord asked for the matter to be referred to a rent assessment committee.

 

2.                 Inspection

 

The Committee inspected the property on 14th January 2003 and found it to be in fair condition:  the landlord has recently installed pvc-u framed and double glazed replacement windows, but the property still lacks a comprehensive heating system and there are some problems of dampness.  The accommodation is as described in the rent officers survey sheet which had been copied to the parties.

 

The following tenants improvements had been made to the property:  the installation of a stone fireplace in the living room; the installation of cupboards and worktops in the kitchen, together with other associated works; the fitting of a false ceiling above the stairs (using materials supplied by the landlord); and improvement works in the bathroom, including the installation of an instantaneous shower. 

 

3.       Evidence

 

The Committee had the benefit of written representations received from the landlord and copied to the other party:  this representation was a simple statement that the Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999 should not apply in this case because of the installation of replacement windows, but no valuation or other evidence was submitted to support that contention.  No written representation was received from the tenant.

 

Neither party requested a hearing at which oral representations could be made.

 

4.       The Law

 

When determining a fair rent the Committee, in accordance with the Rent Act 1977, s.70, had regard to all the circumstances including the age, location and state of repair of the property and disregarded the effect of any relevant tenants improvements on the rental value of the property.

 

In Spath Holme Ltd v Chairman of the Greater Manchester etc. Committee (1995) 28 HLR 107 and Curtis v London Rent Assessment Committee [1999] QB 92 the Court of Appeal emphasised

(a)   that ordinarily a fair rent is the market rent for the property discounted for scarcity.  (i.e. that element of the rent, if any, that is attributable to there being a shortage of similar properties in the locality available for letting on similar terms) and

(b)  that assured tenancy (market) rents are usually appropriate market rent comparables.

 

5.                 Valuation

 

Thus in the first instance the Committee determined what rent the landlord could reasonably be expected to obtain for the property in the open market if it were let today in the condition that is considered usual for such an open market letting.  In the absence of any rental evidence from either party, it did this by having regard to the members own general knowledge of market rent levels in the area around Plymouth.  Having done so it concluded that such a likely market rent would be £90.00 per week.

          

However, the actual property is not in the condition considered usual for a modern letting at a market rent.  Therefore it was necessary to adjust that hypothetical rent of £90.00 per week to allow for the differences between the condition considered usual for such a letting and the condition of the actual property as observed by the Committee, disregarding the effect of any disrepair or other defect attributable to the tenant or any predecessor in title.  The committee considered that this required a deduction of £6.25 per week.

 

Furthermore, to allow for the tenants improvements, as listed above, it was necessary to make a further deduction of £2.40 per week.

 

A further adjustment was made to allow for the differences in repairing obligations between the comparable properties and the subject property the Committee arrived at a fair rent of £73.50 per week for the subject property.

 

The Committee did not consider that there was any substantial scarcity element and accordingly no further deduction was made for scarcity.

 

5.       Decision

 

The fair rent confirmed by the Committee is accordingly £73.50 per week, or £318.50 per calendar month, which the committee rounded to £319.00.  The fair rent determined by the Committee is below the capped limit provided for by the Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999 and accordingly the cap has no effect.  Details are provided on the back of the decision form.

 

That rent of £319.00 per calendar month will be confirmed with effect from 14th January 2003 being the date of the Committees decision.
