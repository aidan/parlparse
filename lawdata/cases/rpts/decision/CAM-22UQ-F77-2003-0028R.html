1.                   THE PREMISES

 

1.1               This property is a small three-bedroom mid-terraced house with ground floor bathroom situated in a rural location on a main road with no off-street parking and limited local facilities. It appears to date from the late 19th Century. It is very close to the village hall, which offers car-parking facilities in return for a fee. The tenant rents an allotment at the rear of the village hall, conveniently adjacent to his sizeable vegetable garden. A gate provides access from the garden to the village hall car park. Foul water drainage is to a cesspit, shared with No 1 Gunners Vlillas, which is emptied periodically by the landlord.

 

1.2               Upon inspection the Committee found the property to be much as described in the Rent Officer's survey dated 11 February 2003. The tenant has made significant improvements. The small front living room has fitted cupboards and a fireplace installed by the tenant to a professional standard. The tenant has also fitted a dimmer switch to the lighting. In about 1989 the kitchen/diner was fitted out by the tenant with attractive modern kitchen units and a sink unit, replacing the landlord’s butler sink. At the rear of the kitchen/diner is a lobby leading to the bathroom.

 

 

1.3               Since the last registration, the landlord has carried out substantial works to the bathroom. Sheds at the end of the single-storey rear portion of the building have been demolished and the house extended. In place of the former tiny bathroom with no washbasin is a very attractive and good-sized bathroom with all modern conveniences, including UPVC double-glazing, shower fitting and heated towel rail (the last item paid for by the tenant). The lobby has been fitted with a new UPVC back door.

 

1.4               Upstairs are an adequate double bedroom, a modest single and a tiny third bedroom or boxroom. The double bedroom has wardrobes fitted by the tenant. The property benefits from oil-fired central heating fitted by the landlord, with two additional radiators by the tenant.

 

1.5               The exterior of the property is generally in reasonable repair. The windows are relatively new, mostly wood-framed and single-glazed. Outside is a modest front garden, a small rear yard and vegetable garden. The landlord’s application dated 19 March 1999 referred to a new front door costing £350 and guttering £120. These remain in good order. The property was rewired by the landlord in December 1999, at a cost of £1,165.

 

1.6               The Committee also inspected externally No 1 Gunners Villas. This end-terrace, with windows on the gable end and a driveway from the road with hardstanding for car parking (unlike No 2). The exterior was in god order. Apparently No 3 is owner-occupied.

 

 

2.                   THE TENANCY

 

2.1        The property was let unfurnished to the present tenant in 1955 at a rent calculated and payable by the week.  There is no written tenancy agreement.  The provisions of section 11 of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 apply, so that the landlord is responsible for the exterior of the building and for the provision and maintenance of the installations for the supply of services ie water, drainage (including foul water drainage), electricity and gas, space heating and water heating.  The tenant is responsible for internal decorations.

 

 

3.         THE APPEAL

 

3.1               The Rent Assessment Committee determined a fair rent of £58.50 per week on 20 March 2001. On 24 January 2003 the landlord applied for an increase to £100.00 per week. The Rent Officer inspected the property and received written representations. In particular, the landlord argued that, by reason his recent works, the rent should be exempt from capping. On 19 February 2003 the Rent Officer determined a fair rent of £63.50 per week. This figure was affected by rent capping, the uncapped figure being £86.00 per week. The Rent Officer rejected the argument that the rent was exempt from capping.

 

3.2               By letter dated 5 March 2003 the landlord objected to the Rent Officer’s determination. He pointed out that he had spent £7,500 on the property, as regards which he had supplied supporting documentation. In addition to the bathroom improvements and the new back door, he had insulated and boarded the loft, installing a dividing wall to make loft private. There were also external works.

 

3.3               A hearing was held which was attended only by Mr Derek Caton, on behalf of the landlord. He confirmed that he had carried out the works described in his letter of 5 March 2003 (most of which the Committee had seen). He had, at the same time, carried out substantial works to No 1, which the landlord also owns, completely modernising that property, including new kitchen and bathroom fittings, oil-fired central heating and UPVC double-glazing all round. No 1 was let in June or July 2002, with floorings but otherwise unfurnished, on assured shorthold terms, at a rent of £630 p.c.m.

 

4.         THE LANDLORD'S CASE

 

4.1               The landlord relies upon the works carried out since the last registration in support of his argument that the rent should be exempt from capping. He drew comparison with the rent he obtained for No1 and directed the attention of the Committee to two other very similar cottages he lets in the village at rents of £600 p.c.m. and £625 p.c.m. (the latter property having the benefit of a garage). These cottages, he told the Committee, have first floor bathrooms and off-street parking.

 

4.2               The Committee enquired how easy it was to find suitable tenants. Mr Caton said it is easy to get tenants. Stanstead Airport is just up the road (providing much employment). He advertises privately in local newspapers and finds a tenant the first week he advertises. He told the Committee there is a shortage of housing in the locality. There are people looking who cannot find housing. The Parish Council is pressing for more affordable housing in the village.

 

 

5.         THE TENANTS’ CASE

 

5.1        The tenant makes no significant representations, leaving the outcome to the expertise and good sense of the Committee.

 

 

6.         THE LAW

 

6.1               The primary task of the Committee is to determine a 'fair rent' within the meaning of section 70(1) of the Rent Act 1977.  In determining the fair rent, regard must be had to all the circumstances (other than personal circumstances) and, in particular, the age, character, locality and state of repair of the dwelling and the quantity, quality and condition of the furniture (if any).  Section 70(2) requires the Committee to assume that the number of persons seeking to become tenants of similar dwelling-houses in the locality on similar terms is not substantially greater than the number of such dwelling-houses in the locality which are available for letting.  A discount will be appropriate where there is a significant scarcity of such dwellings.  Section 70(3) sets out five matters which are to be disregarded, of which only '(a)' (improvements carried out by the tenant, other than pursuant to the terms of the tenancy) is relevant.  The Committee should make it clear what deductions (if any) are made for disregards under section 70(3). The Committee is mindful of the guidance given by the courts as to the correct approach to the determination of fair rents, in particular the review of the authorities by the Court of Appeal in Curtis -v- London RAC (No 2), reported at [1997] 4 All ER 842.

 

6.2        The Committee cannot concern itself with the rate of percentage increase in the rent. Rents do not always track general inflation, though there is obviously an indirect connection. A fair rent is a market rent adjusted for scarcity and disregards (see however paragraph 7.4 below).  There may be more than one route to determine a fair rent; but every route must have that starting point.  That is so, whether reliance is placed on market or fair rent comparables or on return on capital.  Where comparables are relied upon, there is a need to assess their validity and applicability at the time of their use as comparables.  In considering comparable evidence, the Committee must consider the market in the general locality of the subject property. Council house rents are not helpful in this regard, as they are not fixed by reference to the market. Council houses do, of course, form part of the housing pool and thus affect the market indirectly.

 

6.3        Committees should rely upon the best available evidence.  Where close market rent comparables are available, they must constitute the best evidence of the market.  In such cases, it will not normally be appropriate to consider registered fair rent comparables. If the Committee relies upon the knowledge and experience of its members, the effect of that knowledge and experience and the way in which it affects the Committee's conclusions should be spelt out.

 

6.4        Moreover, Committees must adopt a much more quantitative approach than has perhaps been usual hitherto and must also make much more transparent their reasoning.  The Court of Appeal makes this very clear in Curtis (No 2).  The assessment of a fair rent is frequently described as more of an art than a science.  The members of a Committee are expected to be experienced in such valuation and to know and have a "feel" for the rental property market in the area.  But the end product is a figure for the rent of particular premises.  Where the comparables are not exact and/or where there is a need to make disputed adjustments for scarcity or for disregards, it necessarily involves some working through, which should appear in the Reasons.

 

6.5               The first step in the assessment of scarcity is to identify the relevant locality.  The locality must not be too narrowly defined because prospective tenants generally consider broad localities and not precise locations (though there may be exceptions to this principle).  Scarcity must be distinguished from the effect of local amenities, which may enhance rental values.  Judges have spoken of considering a "really large area"(Metropolitan Property Holdings Ltd –v- Finegold [1975] 1 WLR 349), or “the area where potential tenants of the property could reasonably be expected to live” (Queensway Housing Association –v- Chilterns Thames & Eastern RAC Times 11 Dec 1998), but have declined to lay down any hard and fast rules.  It is left to Committees to decide what area to consider on the facts of each case.  In the assessment of scarcity, general knowledge and experience of the local market may, in the view of the Committee, be particularly helpful.

  


6.6               Committees should seek to apply discounts for scarcity consistently.  Where there is no significant scarcity of similar dwellings in the locality, there can be no discount.  Council housing lists may be relevant (particularly to show trends in housing demand), but must be used with caution.  Scarcity of houses to let is only one of the reasons why people seek accommodation through local authorities.  If evidence from the parties is to be disregarded, a clear explanation must be given.  In the final analysis, however, scarcity is an artificial concept which cannot generally be objectively tested and, accordingly, the actual discount is very much a matter of opinion.

 

6.7        We point out that the exercise, which the Committee is required to undertake, is often bedevilled by a lack of satisfactory evidence.  Even where evidence is available, it is often difficult for Committees to evaluate. Many cases are decided on written representations without a hearing.  Thus Committees are often required to evaluate evidence without the opportunity to test it adequately or at all.  The Committee considers that this is an exercise that must be undertaken with caution. The Committee is mindful of the fact that it must not reject evidence, particularly expert evidence, without good reason but considers that it need not be credulous in cases where the evidence appears to be flawed or amounts to mere unsupported assertion.  The Courts have made it clear that Committees are entitled to use their own local knowledge and expertise for this purpose.

 

6.9               Committees must be wary of basing their conclusions on evidence that is not open to comment by the parties. The Committee considers it is entitled to make use of published material like the Rent Service Market Rent Summaries. But if the Committee wishes to take into account specific comparables which are not provided by the parties or the Rent Officer, the parties should generally be given an opportunity, by means of an adjournment if necessary, to consider and comment upon them. The Committee is mindful of the necessity to set out in every case the evidence upon which its decision is based.

 

6.10            The Committee is also mindful of the need to have regard to the European Convention for the protection of Human Rights and Fundamental Freedoms as embodied into English law by the Human Rights Act 1998. This affects the construction of legislation and underlies the approach of the Committee to its task. In those exceptional cases where the Act has a direct effect on the Committee’s determination, the nature and consequences of that effect will be set out in paragraph 7 of these Reasons.

7.         THE DECISION

 

7.1        The Committee's view of the evidence and findings of fact are as follows:-

 

7.1.1          The Rodings form an attractive and popular group of villages in which good rents are obtainable for properties in good condition. The Committee has available much better evidence than the differently constituted Committee had in 2001. The Committee accepts the evidence of Mr Caton as regards the works done, the cost of those works, the comparable lettings in the village and the state of the local letting market. Lettings of three broadly similar cottages in the village represent good evidence of rental value. In particular, the letting of No 1 Gunners Villas provides a very direct indication of the open market rental value of the subject property.

 

7.1.2          The Committee bears in mind, however, that No1 is end-terrace and has a driveway with off-street parking. No 1 is also fully double-glazed and, as the Committee understands, let with fitted carpets throughout, which would be the norm for this type of property in the locality. Taking into account these differences, and allowing for the passage of time, the Committee concludes that the open market rental value of the subject property, if let on assured shorthold terms, with floorings but otherwise unfurnished, is £600 p.c.m.

 

7.1.3          The Chairman has the advantage of having seen the previous bathroom, the deficiencies of which exercised a considerable influence upon the decision of the Committee in March 2001. The former bathroom is described in the March 2001 Reasons, which the all the members of the Committee have read. In the judgment of the Committee that bathroom was a major factor which reduced the rental value of the property. Its replacement by an attractive modern bathroom has a substantial effect, especially in the light of the other works carried out by the landlord over the years. In the judgment of the Committee, the new bathroom, together with the other recent works, has added £60 p.c.m. to the open market rental value. Without it, the figure would be £540 p.c.m.

 

7.1.4          The Committee deducts 5% because the tenants have supplied their own carpets and other floorings, reaching an adjusted open market rental value of £570 p.c.m. Without the recent works, the figure would be £513 p.c.m.

 

7.1.5          In evaluating evidence, the Committee relies upon the knowledge and experience of its members, derived from many sources, including the property pages of local newspapers, evidence in many previous cases and from informal discussions with local agents, landlords and tenants.  The Committee does not rely upon any specific comparables of its own.

 

7.2               The Committee considers that the tenant's improvements (which are to be disregarded under the provisions of section 70(3) of the Act) justify a discount of 12.5%. In particular, the lack of a modern kitchen would have a substantial effect on the rental value of the property. Thus the adjusted open market rental value of the subject property is £495 p.c.m. Without the recent works, the figure would be £445 p.c.m.

 

7.3               The locality considered by the Committee for the purposes of section 70(2) is the Rodings Valley and the surrounding area, bounded to the east by Chelmsford, to the north by Great Dunmow, to the west by Bishop’s Stortford and to the south by Brentwood. That is the area where, in the judgment of the Committee, prospective tenants of the subject property might reasonably be expected to live. In the experience of the members of the Committee there is substantial scarcity of similar dwellings in the locality. Mr Caton’s evidence supports that view. In the view of the Committee a discount of 20% is appropriate in order to satisfy the assumption set out in section 70(2) of the Rent Act 1977. This leads to a figure of £396 p.c.m. Without the recent work, the figure would be £356 p.c.m. The difference is £40 p.c.m.

 

7.4               The Committee must then consider the effect of the Rent Acts (Maximum Fair Rent) Order 1999. This Order imposes a limit on rent increases in cases where it applies.

 

7.5               Capping does not apply if, as a result of repairs or improvements carried out by the landlord, the uncapped fair rent determined for the property is at least 15% more than the existing registered rent (at the date of the landlord’s original application). The landlord cannot claim credit for this purpose for services such as maintenance of the boiler or emptying the septic tank. These matters are his responsibility by law or under the terms of the letting. 15% of the existing registered rent amounts to £8.78 per week or £38.03 p.c.m. The Committee has concluded that the rental value of the landlord’s recent improvements is £40 p.c.m. Thus in the judgment of the Committee the rent is, on this occasion, exempt from capping.

 

7.6               Rounding slightly, the Committee reaches a figure of £91.50 per week as a fair rent for the subject property in accordance with the provisions of section 70(1) of the Rent Act 1977. The rent is, for the reasons set out above, exempt from capping. The Committee appreciates that this may be a shock to the tenant, who is a retired factory worker. However, the Committee cannot lawfully have regard to the personal circumstances of the tenant (or, indeed, of the landlord). The fair rent assessed by the Committee should be well within the range considered reasonable in this locality for a couple for housing benefit purposes.
