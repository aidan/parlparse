DECISION

 

A         BACKGROUND:

 

1.         Mr Hibbert brought this application under S19(2A) of the Act in respect of service charges for the year ending December 2002.  Although his application included the year ending December 2003 not unsurprisingly the accounts for that period had not yet been prepared.  Accordingly the issues were confined to the year ending December 2002

 

2.         The basis of these complaints were set out in a handwritten letter dated the 24 July 2003 which we will deal with in more detail under the Evidence heading of this Decision.

 

3.         In support of Mr Hibbert, Mr Henderson, Mr Thakerar, Miss Croney and Mr Davis made comments to us, Mr Davis having also submitted copies of letters he had written to the Respondents dated 4 July 2003 and 29 August 2003.

 

 4.        Mr Davis and Mr Thakerar applied to be joined as parties to the application which is accepted.

 

5.         Miss H Couldwell, the Lessee of Flat 15, had written apologising for her non-attendance due to ill health.

 

6.         The Respondents had ignored the Tribunal’s Directions issued on 23 October 2003 and did not attend the hearing.  There were no representations from them.  We were told their agents had resigned their position as Managing Agents in November 2003 but they had not written to the Tribunal or Mr Hibbert to confirm this.

 

7.         A dispute concerning the service charges for the year 2000 and 2001 had been referred to the Tribunal and a Decision (the Decision) issued on 4 February 2003 in proceedings numbered CAM/96/LVT/SC/087.  We shall refer to the Decision as necessary.

 

B.  THE LEASE:

 

8.         We were provided with a copy of the Lease for Flat 1 and we understand this was the standard form of Lease for properties in the development. It runs for a term of 99 years from the 24 June 1970 and contains the usual obligations on the part of the Lessee and the Management Company who fulfilled what are frequently the Landlords obligation of maintenance and repair.

 

9.         The Lease provides for the Lessees to contribute towards the costs of the “charges, expenses and Management fees from time to time incurred by the Company in carrying out its obligations under the Fifth Schedule”.  In addition also, at paragraph 3(d) of the Lease, provision is made for a payment of £50.00 as a reserve towards making good any default by the Lessee in paying the aforementioned contributions to the maintenance charges.  There is no other reference in the Lease to the payment of a reserve fund and we will come back to that point later in the Decision document.  The Company’s obligations in the Fifth Schedule are fairly standard and include an obligation to “maintain, repair, redecorate and renew, amongst other things, the external walls and structures, the services, common parts and the garden area and boundary walls and fences”.  There is an obligation also to keep the common entranceways and other common parts clean and reasonably lighted and to externally decorate once in every five years.  The Management Company also has the obligation to insure on a comprehensive basis with a “well established insurance company of repute”.  The accounts are to be prepared to the 31 December each year and should be audited by a qualified accountant and notices making demands under the accounts should be sent out within two months of the date to which the account is taken.  There was no real dispute between the parties as to the obligation of the Company save for the position with regard to the reserve fund.

 

10.       The block contains 26 flats. 

 

C.  ITEMS IN DISPUTE:

 

11.       As was done in the previous Decision, we believe it is easiest if we set out on a schedule attached to this Decision the service charge expenditure in dispute and our decision relating thereto.

 

D.        INSPECTION

 

12.       We viewed the premises on the 13 January 2004.  It had changed little since the Tribunal inspection in October 2002.  The garages appeared to be in a tidier condition and the fire damage to the underpass had been repaired.  A boundary wall had been improved.  The development consists of 26 flats in four 3/4 storey tiered blocks with separate entrances serving each block.  There is an underpass in the middle through which the service road runs leading to a rear garden with quite extensive car parking.  The site slopes quite considerably. The external decorations were in reasonable order and the gardens appeared to be tended, if basic.  There was evidence of litter and leaf mould but generally the external upkeep of the blocks and the environs was reasonable.    The common parts are spartan.  We noted the fire fighting equipment.  The lighting was of a push type and designed to turn off automatically.  Each block had entry phone arrangements and door with closing mechanism, which appeared to work with encouragement. The common parts were reasonably clean. The windows did not fit terribly well the metal frames having distorted.  The access road and car parking area was in need of attention. 

 

E.        EVIDENCE:

 

13.       The Evidence heading in the Decision in 2003 started with criticism of the Respondent’s failure to provide documentation.  On this occasion they have surpassed themselves.  Not only have they flagrantly failed to comply with the Directions given in October 2003 they have failed to extend any courtesy to the Applicant or the Tribunal by not attending the hearing on the 13 January 2004.

 

14.       Accordingly our Decision has been made in the absence of the Managing Agents for the Respondents or the Respondent Company.

 

15.       The items in dispute were dealt with on an item-by-item basis and for ease of understanding we will proceed on the same footing.  The headings are directly referable to the schedule at the foot of this Decision and should therefore correlate fairly easily. 

 

     The evidence we received was from Mr Hibbert, Mr Henderson, Mr Thakerar, Miss Croney and Mr Davis.

 

  • Electricity:    The amount claimed by the Respondent for this sum was £538.00.  In 2001 the figure had been £211.00.  No apparent reason was given for this increase and it was challenged by the Applicant.

 

  • Electrical and Lighting:    A figure of £744.00 was claimed in the accounts.  This had been challenged by Mr Davis in his letter of the 4 July 2003.  He indicated that the only major works he believed related to the damage to the underpass lighting caused by the fire which should have been dealt with in an insurance claim.  Some documentation was provided to Mr Davis by Parkgate Aspen and in Mr Davis’s letter of the 29 August he commented upon this.  We had the benefit of some invoices showing how the costs relating to the lighting had been reached.

 

  • Rubbish Removal:   The complaint was that the sum of £957.00 was excessive.  Miss Croney indicated that some of that would have included the costs of removing dumped cars. However again as a result of Mr Davis making enquiries of the Managing Agents, an invoice was produced showing that a sum of £620.00 plus VAT had been charged for clearing four storerooms and disposing of the dumped materials by ATP Maintenance Company, a business which appeared to do a considerable amount of work for the Managing Agents.

 

  • Windows/Locks & Doors:   The sum claimed by the Respondent was £3,065.00.  Again Mr Davis had queried this amount and invoices were disclosed indicating that the bulk of this was made up of one invoice totalling £2,416.00 from A A Locksmith and dated 15 August 2002 for the apparent supply and fitting of locks to the four doors serving the common parts.  We were told by the Applicant and Mr Davis that no Notice under s20 of the Act had been served.

 

  • Management:  A complaint was made by Mr Hibbert that in the Decision made in 2003 the Tribunal had then limited the Managing Agents charges to a figure of £150.00 per flat plus VAT which gave rise to a total of £4,583.00.  This is to be set against the amount that the Respondents have claimed for Management fees in the 2002 accounts which is a figure of £6,427.00. 

 

  • Gardening:   The Respondents sought the sum of £5,203.00 in respect of this matter.  This had been the source of concern previously and Mr Hibbert again expressed surprise that, this amount of amount of money could have been spent on the garden.  The monthly charge had risen from £203.66 inclusive of VAT to £213.85 inclusive and the works were still being done by the English Garden Company from South Mimms.  Extensive additional costs were levied on a regular basis for various items including litter picking, provision of bark, snail and slug pellets, various fertilisers, weed killers and manure.

 

16.       Concern was raised again by the Applicant and those other parties in attendance concerning the question of the Reserve Fund and reserve payments that may have been made in the past. 

 

 

 

 

F.         DECISION:

 

17.       For ease of reference we have set out in Schedule form attached those items that are disputed and our findings in relation thereto.  We will however explain in this section of the Decision document the reason we have reached our findings. 

 

18.       We start firstly with the Management element.  In our previous Decision when we had the benefit of the attendance by the Managing Agents, Parkgate Aspen we were able to hear from them as to the work they did and establish a reasonable level of Management fee.  That we had fixed at £150.00 plus VAT per flat, which rounded up, came to £4,583.00 for the year.  There is no evidence that the Managing Agents have undertaken any additional works during the year ending December 2002 and indeed a number of problems that were highlighted in the previous Decision have not been dealt with.  There has been no attempt by the Managing Agents to deal with any form of reimbursement with regard to the funds which we found had been overcharged and indeed they appear to have taken no notice of the Management fees that we found to be appropriate in fixing the level of Management fees for the year 2002.  We have no hesitation in finding that the Decision made in February 2003 (which has not been appealed) that a reasonable level of charge is £150.00 per flat plus VAT still holds good and accordingly, we reduce the amount claimed from £6,427.00 to £4,583.00.

 

19.       We then turn to the question of gardening.  This had been the source of concern particularly from Mr Hibbert at the previous hearing.  Again it appears there has been little or no control with regard to the extras incurred.    The evidence from Mr Davis was that litter picking did not happen and extensive quantities of fertiliser and other items appear to have been applied on what is a relatively small garden area.  Taking the matter in the round therefore, it is our finding that the figure that was previously allowed for the year 2001 of £3,000.00, still holds good although we accept that there should be an uplift to reflect the increased monthly charge and accordingly we allow the sum of £3,150.00 in respect of the gardening.

 

20.       Windows/locks and doors.  It is not possible for us to tell from the evidence provided whether these charges, which totalled £3,065.00 are reasonable.  They related to the repairs of locks to the common parts by ATP Maintenance in August subsequently followed by a complete replacement of those repaired items later that month.  What we can say however is that there is no evidence that any Notice under s20 of the Act was served on the Lessees which would enable the Respondents to recover the sum of £2,416.00 in respect of the A A Locksmith invoice dated 15 August 2002.  It does seem to us that £2416.00 for the supply and fitting of four locks and associated door equipment is high but no evidence of alternative costings were available.  In the absence of a s20 Notice or any relief from the court with regard thereto, the Respondent is of course not entitled to recover more than £1000.00 in respect of that particular matter.  We can make no further findings in regard to those items through lack of evidence.

 

21.       Electricity.  In the year 2001 the sum of £211.00 was charged and was not queried.  An estimate for the year 2002 was £250.00 and there is no explanation from the Respondents as to why a figure of £538.00 should be sought.  It seems to us to be out of line with previous electricity charges and we find therefore the estimated figure of £250.00 is reasonable and should be allowed and to that extent we reduce the electricity costs claimed of  £538.00.

 

22        Lighting and Electricals.  As a result of Mr Davis’s letter certain invoices from ATP Maintenance were produced with regard to electrical works.  One of these related to the installation of a new light as a result of one being burnt out in the underpass which totalled £210.32.  Mr Davis’s contention was that this is matter that should have been dealt with under the insurance provisions and we would agree.  Invoice 93430, again from ATP Maintenance Company dated 28 August 2003 was for the sum of £153.50 for the replacement of five halogen tubes and the adjustment of fittings in respect of security lights.  Mr Davis, in his letter of 29 August had indicated that a year previously works of the same nature had been carried out and he did not understand therefore why another contractor was being employed to do that which had already been done.  He questioned what quality control the Managing Agents were applying.  As we have had no response from the Managing Agents and as there is a challenge to this invoice, we have concluded that it is reasonable to disallow same and that has therefore been set off from the claim for electrical and lighting of £744.00 together also with the earlier invoice we referred to of £210.32.

 

23        Rubbish Removal.  We received evidence from Miss Croney that some costs would inevitably have been incurred in connection with the removal of abandoned vehicles.  The main invoice disputed by the Applicant was that again from ATP Maintenance numbered 93133 of 11 July 2002.  This was for £620.00 plus VAT to attend on site and clear storerooms and to take the rubbish to the local tip.  This seemed to us to be an expensive way of dealing with the matter. ATP Maintenance Company come from Southgate.  We would have thought that either a local contractor could have been used or perhaps more appropriately a skip hired and labour utilised to empty the various storerooms.  We have concluded from our own knowledge and experience that this could have been dealt with at a cost of around £250.00 plus VAT.  Accordingly we reduce the ATP Maintenance invoice from £728.50 to £293.75, the remaining costs we allow as being reasonable, leaving £522.25 payable for this head of expense.

 

24.       The question of the Reserve Fund and the major works provisions was again raised.  As was indicated in the previous Decision there is no provision in the Lease for a claim in respect of Reserve Fund payments.  It is not clear from the Statement of Account whether the major works provisions of £10,000.00 is merely an accounting exercise.  If in effect it is an attempt to claim a Reserve Fund payment then, as we have stated previously, there is in our finding no provision within the Lease for this to be done.

 

25        The Applicant had also requested that there should be no entitlement to the Respondent to seek to recover the costs of these proceedings by way of service charge payments.  We have no difficulty in making such an Order under s20C of the Act.  Firstly we have, by and large, found in favour of the Applicant.  Secondly the Respondents and their Managing Agents have failed to play any part in these proceedings at all and accordingly could not have incurred any costs in any event.
