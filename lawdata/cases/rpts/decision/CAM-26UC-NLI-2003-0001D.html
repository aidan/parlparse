0.                  BACKGROUND

 

The Property

0.1              The flat or maisonette the subject of this application is part of a development comprising an L-shaped block of shop units with dwellings above. This includes even numbers 8-42 Bridge Street and odd numbers 51-73 Waterhouse Street. Even numbers 8-24 Bridge Street and odd numbers 51-61 Waterhouse Street are shops; even numbers 26-42 Bridge Streeet and odd numbers 63-73 Waterhouse Street are maisonettes. It looks as though numbers 2-4-6 Bridge Street were built at the same time. Certainly, the two flats (4 and 6) share a common stairwell with the maisonettes even numbers 26-42 Brook Street. The footprints of the shops in Brook Street are a good deal larger than that of the maisonettes, as a result of which there is above the shops a communal drying area, a footway and a yard for each maisonette. In Waterhouse Street, the footprints of the shops are only a little larger than those of the maisonettes above. It is not clear who owns the common parts.

 

0.2              In May 1991 the freehold of the block was transferred from the Commission for New Towns to Safeland Plc. Since then the ownership of the freehold has become fragmented, which is unlikely to foster effective management of the block as a whole. In June 1994 Redglade Limited acquired the freehold of the shops at 12 and 24 Bridge Street and at 51-53-55-59-61 Waterhouse Street, with dwellings above, namely 30 and 42 Bridge Street and 63-65-67-71-73 Waterhouse Street. This now comprises title number HD291498. The shop units 51-53-55 Waterhouse Street are let to a single tenant, as are those at 59-61 Waterhouse Street. In 1991 the maisonettes at 28 and 30 Bridge Street and 63 and 69 Waterhouse Street were leased back to the Commission for New Towns. The leasehold title was transferred to Home Housing Association Limited in November 1996 and is now held under title number HD287030. Those properties are managed by Warden Housing Association Ltd, a subsidiary of Home Housing. On 16 August 1994 the Commission for New Towns granted to Syraj Miah a lease for a term of just over 121 years. This is now held by Mr Miah and his wife Parvin Begum from Home Housing under title number HD333631.

 

0.3              The shops are all let on head leases for 999 years commencing 25 March 1991, presumably at a nominal rent. The maisonettes, apart from 73 Waterhouse Street, are let on head leases of 125 years at peppercorn rents. The commencement dates appear to be between 1986 and 1990. Mr Miahs underlease of 30 Bridge Street is also at a peppercorn rent. 73 Waterhouse Street is let under a revised lease for a term of 99 years from 29 September 1999, under the terms of which the freeholder is expressly permitted to retain any commission on the insurance premium.

 

0.4              The head lease of 30 Bridge Street (now held by Home Housing) provides that the landlord shall keep the premises insured in the joint names of the landlord and (inter alia) the tenant, any sub-tenant of any part and any mortgagee in an office of good repute or at Lloyds in an adequate amount to cover demolition, site clearance, rebuilding or replacing the building and professional fees. The landlord undertakes to reinstate and, in the event the insurance monies are insufficient, to make good the deficiency out of its own money. The risks to be insured include the risks usually covered by this type of policy and such other risks as the landlord shall from time to time desire to insure.

 

 

0.5              The head lease requires Home Housing to contribute a fair proportion of the landlords insurance costs. The underlease requires the tenant to pay a fair proportion of the insurance costs charged to Home Housing by Redglade under the terms of the head lease. Rather surprisingly, Home Housing gives no covenant to its subtenant to insure or to use reasonable endeavours to procure that the freeholder shall insure. The only relevant covenant by Home Housing is for quiet enjoyment. If the freeholder does not, in fact, insure, presumably it is left to the subtenant to obtain his own insurance. No doubt Mr Miah would, in all the circumstances, be happy to do this.

 

 

1.                  THE DISPUTE

 

1.1              There has been discontent about the cost of insurance since the early days of Redglades ownership. In March 1996 Mr Miah, through his solicitors, asked the Commission for New Towns why the sum insured for 30 Bridge Street was £168,139.60 when the flat was worth considerably less than £90,000. The Commission assessed the cost of reinstatement at £66,000. Their surveyor also re-measured the property, as a result of which Redglades agents, Able Property Management (APM), reassessed the area of 30 Bridge Street at 1002.5 sq ft (93.2m2) out of a total of 5831.15 sq ft (541.9m2), or 17.19% of the whole. Previously, APM had been working on figures of 1728 sq ft out of a total of 7500 sq ft or 23.04% of the whole. It is entirely unclear how this huge error was made in the first place. Be that as it may, subsequent apportionments of premium costs were made according to the area of each unit and on the basis of the new measurements (which are not in dispute).

 

1.2              This change did not entirely satisfy either the Commission or Mr Miah. On 19 August 1996 the Commission wrote to APM to set out the Commissions position, namely, that the Commission considered the insurance charges to be excessive and payment was made on a without prejudice basis. It appears the matter was not resolved by October 1996 when the head lease was assigned to Home Housing. Home Housing was concerned about the situation, if only because Mr Miah was refusing to pay anything towards the insurance costs. However, Home Housing and its agent Warden Housing felt unable to take any effective steps. Their current stance (as stated by Home Ownership Manager Mr Cameron Jack) is that the charges are reasonable.

 

1.3              Mr Miah was not the only resident who was concerned about the insurance costs. The matter first came before the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal on the application of Harpal Singh Bains (of 73 Waterhouse Street) made in June 2002. The application was made under sections 19 and 30A of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 to challenge the reasonableness of service charges and the landlords choice of insurer for year ending 30 June 2002. Mr Miah and five other underlessees became joined applicants. On that occasion APMs management fee was reduced from £120.00 to £80.00. The Tribunal recorded that  its members are deeply suspicious that the insurance premiums have been inflated by excessive reinstatement values and that consequently the premium charged is not the true market rate for these properties. However, the Tribunal considered that there was insufficient evidence to justify an order under either section.           

 

 

 

2.                  THE ISSUES

 

2.1              The Applicant now wishes to challenge the reasonableness of the service charges (specifically the insurance costs) for years ending June 1998 to June 2004 inclusive. His argument is that the building is over-insured and that the premium rates are unreasonably high and well above the open market rate.

 

2.2              Home Housing applied for Redglade to be joined as a party, which the Tribunal accordingly ordered on 5 March 2004. Warden Housing on their behalf make three principal submissions in response to Mr Miahs application:-

 

(i)                  They are unable to establish that the premiums are unreasonable.

(ii)                The matter is not within their control and they have paid the sums demanded.

(iii)               If Mr Miah succeeds in his application, Home Housing should be entitled to like relief against Redglade.

 

2.3              By letter dated 9 March 2004 a director of Redglade, Mr Vernon Murrell, directs the attention of the Tribunal to the earlier case. He argues that the applicant is seeking to reopen matters on which the earlier Tribunal has given judgment. He says that the insurance is arranged at arms length by independent brokers who place the insurance at normal market rates. He  cites Berrycroft Management Co Ltd v- Sinclair Gardens (Kensington) Ltd (1997) 29 HLR 444; (1997) 1 EGLR 47; (1998) 75 P&CR 210.

 

2.4              Redglade received relatively short notice of the hearing. For that reason, the Tribunal felt it proper to offer Redglade the opportunity to make further written representations. A further statement by Mr Murrell was accompanied by a very helpful bundle of documents. In summary, Mr Murrell says that:-

 

(a)                The insurance is obtained through brokers who are instructed annually to find the most competitive terms to ensure full cover and prompt settlement in the event of any claim  Heath Lambert confirm by their letter dated 4 July 2003 that the terms offered by Axa remain competitive compared to those available from other leading insurers at the present time.

(b)               The quantum and allocation of insurance was determined by the previous freeholder and is index-linked to avoid averaging  the lessees are welcome to undertake revaluation and/or re-measurement at their own expense.

(c)                The head tenant is in breach of the terms of the head lease in that no notice was given of the underlease to Mr Miah.

(d)               Redglade should not be joined because Home Housing has agreed and paid the charges and is thus precluded from disputing the payments under the provisions of section 155 of the Commonhold & Leasehold Reform Act 2002 and/or the decision of the Court of Appeal in R v- London LVT ex parte Daejan Properties Ltd [2001] EWCA 1095; [2002] HLR 25.

(e)                Redglade has done its best to protect the interests of all parties without receiving proper co-operation from the tenants and should not be penalised.

(f)                 The brokers have been unable to find suitable alternative cover because of the claims record and the character of the occupants ie housing association tenants.

 

(g)                There is concern that Mr Miahs home may be overcrowded or occupied other than by his immediate family so as to contravene the provisions of the head lease and/or invalidate the insurance policy.

 

2.5              It is clear that Redglade would argue that there is no basis for an order under section 20C restricting the recovery of costs through the service charge provisions. 

 

 

3.                  THE EVIDENCE

 

3.1              Redglade have supplied APMs service charge statements for the relevant years and various insurance documents. It is clear how the service charges under the head lease are made up. This is set out in the First Schedule to this Decision. There are some discrepancies in the figures mentioned in various other documents; but these are all clearly revealed as errors or misunderstandings.

 

3.2              Mr Miah says he wants to sell his property but cannot find a buyer because the insurance costs are so high. Since Mr Miah has not paid any of the insurance costs since 1995, no doubt the issue of arrears may also be a problem. Although Mr Miah has not been receiving service charge accounts, Warden Housing are now demanding around £5,000 in respect of arrears as a condition of consenting to an assignment. Mrs Vass of 63 Waterhouse Street confirmed that she also had received no accounts.

 

3.3              In May 2004 Mr Miah obtained quotations for insurance of 30 Bridge Street from two alternative insurers. The sum insured was put at £141,000 and the cover sought the same (so far as Mr Miah could describe) as that provided under Redglades block policy. Endsleigh quoted £229.99 and Bradford & Bingley Insurance £311.87. Neither insurer undertook an inspection. However, Bradford & Bingleys office (from which the quotation was obtained) is at 2 Bridge Street. Mr Miah explained to both insurers the situation and the terms of the leases as far as he was able. Bradford & Bingley obtained their quotation from Norwich Union through Heath Lambert Insurance Services.

 

3.4              Mr Fisher said that, although the quotes are clearly not directly comparable with the Axa figures, the discrepancy in premiums is striking. Leaseholders of other properties in the block, insured by different landlords with other insurers, pay far less (see tab 5).

 

3.5              Home Housing relied upon the witness statements of Mr Jack and Mr Tom Putt, Home Ownership Services Manager.

 

3.6              The evidence for Redglade is set out in Mr Murrells statement and enclosures. The other parties had an opportunity to comment on that evidence and Shoosmiths did comment on behalf of Home Housing. Shoosmiths point out that APM has been aware of Mr Miahs underlease since at least January 1997, as correspondence shows. If, which seems unlikely, the relevant formalities were not complied with in 1994, the only possible conclusion is that strict compliance has been waived. Parvin Begum is, of course, a registered proprietor as well as the wife of Mr Miah. There is no evidence of statutory overcrowding at 30 Bridge Street.

 

 

4.                  LEGAL PRINCIPLES

 

4.1              The original application in this case was made before the new section 27A of the Landlord & Tenant Act 1985 (introduced under the Commonhold & Leasehold Reform Act 2002) came into force. The application was made under section 30A and the Schedule to the 1985 Act, to challenge the landlords choice of insurer and for a determination whether the insurance costs included in the service charge accounts were reasonably incurred and, to the extent that they were reasonably incurred, whether they are payable.

 

4.2              In fact, the relevant provisions (paragraph 8) of the Schedule apply only where the tenant is required to insure with an insurer nominated or approved by the landlord. The application is really an application under section 19(2A) to determine reasonableness and under section 27A to determine payability. By directions issued on 10 November 2003 the Tribunal decided to treat the application as though it had been made under section 19(2A).

 

4.3              The jurisdiction under section 27A is a new jurisdiction only in the sense that it is new to the Leasehold Valuation Tribunal. Previously, the determination whether service charges were due and payable rested with the Courts. It would clearly be convenient for that issue to be decided (if necessary) by the Tribunal in this case, rather than by the Court, provided that could be done without injustice to any of the parties. The only additional element introduced into the case is the possible effects of section 20B. However, it is clear that service charge demands were made by Redglade to Home Housing in good time and that Mr Miah had ample written notice from Warden Housing that insurance costs were being incurred and were chargeable to him. Accordingly, there would, it appears, be no prejudice to the parties if the Tribunal were to permit the applicant, even at this late stage, to make an application under section 27A.

 

4.4              However, there appears to be no need for Mr Miah to make an application under section 27A. He has not paid the disputed charges and, in the event of a favourable finding, would not be liable to pay them. Home Housing would be unable to rely upon the disputed charges for any purpose in connection with Mr Miahs lease and, in particular, would not be entitled to serve a section 146 notice, take forfeiture proceedings or impose a condition of payment in return for consent to an assignment. Home Housing has made no application under section 19(2A) or under section 27A. However, the Tribunal is satisfied that Home Housing has not agreed the charges and thus section 27A(4) does not apply. Home Housing is entitled to apply under section 27A and the Tribunal gives permission for such an application, subject to payment of the application fee (if any). In effect, that is what Shoosmiths asked for, in their application dated 13 February 2004, in the event of a favourable finding on Mr Miahs application.

 

4.5              As regards the reasonableness of insurance costs for service charge period 2001-2, there has already been a determination on the merits by an earlier Tribunal. New evidence is not a ground for reopening an issue unless it is evidence which could not reasonably have been brought before the earlier Tribunal. The proper avenue in such a case would be by way of appeal. In the absence of exceptional circumstances, it would clearly be an abuse of process to reopen the period 2001-2 and the Tribunal declines to do so.

 

4.6              In the Berrycroft case, the lease required the management company to insure in some insurance office of repute and if directed by the landlord through a company nominated by the landlord and if required through any agency of the landlord in that company. The Court of Appeal held that the landlords right to nominate an insurer was not fettered by a requirement to act reasonably. Provided the insurer was an office of repute and the policy was negotiated in the ordinary course of business and on the insurers usual terms of business, the landlord was entitled to select any insurer he chose, even if identical cover were available elsewhere at substantially cheaper rates. The landlord had many properties to insure and might reasonably insist upon using the same insurer for them all, thereby achieving a substantial saving in management time. On the evidence, the cost of insurance was not, in all the circumstances, unreasonable. Moreover, the costs were reasonably incurred for the purposes of section 19 and 30A of the 1985 Act.

 

4.7              It should be noted that the Berrycroft decision does not preclude the Tribunal from considering whether insurance costs were reasonably incurred. However, the costs of insurance effected with an insurer of repute at a premium negotiated in the ordinary course of business and on the insurers usual terms of business will ordinarily be considered reasonable. The landlords legitimate interests, including economy of management time and the benefits to estate management of insuring all the landlords properties with a single insurer, are important considerations. However, in the judgment of the Tribunal, a landlord is not entitled to maximise his profit from insurance commissions by neglecting his obligation to act in the ordinary course of business. In the judgment of the Tribunal the landlord is not acting reasonably or in the ordinary course of business if he neglects to act in the manner of a prudent landlord obtaining insurance at his own expense.

 

 

5.                  REASONED CONCLUSIONS

 

5.1              The first issue for the Tribunal is whether the premises are over-insured. The head lease requires Redglade to insure in an adequate amount and Home Housing to contribute a fair proportion of the cost of so doing. Given the requirement to reinstate, if necessary at Redglades own expense, it would be reasonable to allow a margin for error. In the experience of the Tribunal, reinstatement costs for this type of building in this location for 2003-4 are likely to be around £1,000-1,200 per square metre. The gross floor area of 30 Bridge Street is clearly established as being 93.2 square metres (a figure the Tribunal was able to confirm by approximate measurement). However, this includes no allowance for the terrace included in the demised premises or the common parts.

 

5.2              Obviously, the terraces, walkways and drying areas are also part of the roofs of the shops below. There must, however, have been some additional costs incurred in making these areas suitable for pedestrian traffic and constructing the small brick walls and stairways which separate the various areas. These must surely be included in Redglades title. The Tribunal considers that some modest allowance should be made for the insurance of these additional areas. Redglade do not claim to own the freehold of the common stairways, nor do they say they are liable to contribute to any insurance costs incurred by third parties. Thus there is no basis for making any allowance for the cost of insuring the common stairways.

 

 

 

5.3              Both Endsleigh and Bradford & Bingley worked on a figure of £141,000, which equates to just over £1,500 per square metre (ignoring terrace and common parts). Neither insurer inspected the premises and it is not clear on what basis they assessed the sum to be insured. Mr Miah says he thought the property was worth £125,000. For the same period, Redglades insurer used a figure of nearly £1,950 per square metre.  It appears by Mr Murrells own admission that the sum insured for 1995-6 was based on figures provided by the previous landlord. Since then the level of cover has been based entirely on indexation and no attempt has been made to consider actual reinstatement costs. It does not appear that Redglade has ever made any attempt to discover whether the premises are over-insured.

 

5.4              Reinstatement costs are readily ascertainable by the sort of enquiries a landlord could readily make. The cost of making such enquiries would be proper management expenses recoverable from tenants. If the landlord neglects to make reasonable enquiries, and in consequence over-insures, it is not reasonable to expect the tenants to bear the excessive costs thus incurred. Redglade ought reasonably to have made such enquiries when the issue was first raised in 1996. The Tribunal considers that, had such enquiry been made, the level of cover would have been reduced for all the years in issue (ie from 1996-7 onwards). The Tribunal has no reason to doubt that Axa employed reasonable figures for indexation purposes; the question is whether the original figure was correct.

 

5.5              It appears that the Commission for New Towns paid the 1995-6 charges on a without prejudice basis but did not pursue the issue of excessive insurance charges because of the assignment of the head lease to Home Housing. Home Housing paid the 1996-7 charges on a without prejudice basis.

 

5.6              The Tribunal is unable to accept that Mr Miah received no service charge demands. The trial bundle shows that he first raised the issue of excessive charges in March 1996, when he was hoping to sell his property. Also included in the trial bundle is a copy of the service charge demand for 1997-8 and 1998-9. On 18 January 2000 Mr Miah wrote to APM, having taken advice from Dacorum District CAB and after obtaining three insurance quotations ranging from £122.66 to £133.26. In addition, Mr Miah was a party to the application made to the Tribunal in 2002, which related to the service charge for 2001-2. On 21 May 2003 Mr Putt wrote to Mr Miah rejecting his proposal for compromise of insurance charges. On 22 July 2003 Mr Putt wrote to Mr Miah demanding the insurance charge for 2003-4.

 

5.7              It does not appear that Home Housing has ever investigated the matter in detail, though Mr Jack says he discussed it with Mr Tunmore, who is Home Housings insurance risk manager and a member of the Institute of Risk Management. The stance taken by Warden was that, if Mr Miah disputed the figures, he should apply to the Tribunal.

 

5.8              In the judgment of the Tribunal, the figure employed by Axa for reinstatement costs for 2003-4 is excessive. Taking into account all the above matters, the Tribunal finds that a figure of £1,200 per square metre would be reasonable. On that basis, the sum insured for 30 Bridge Street would be £111,840. Using Axas building costs indexation figures (which can be ascertained by simple calculation), the Tribunal has calculated in the Second Schedule to this Decision the proper level of cover for all the disputed periods.

 

 

5.9              The second issue for the Tribunal is whether the premium rates were excessive. The premium rate is assessed to reflect the risks insured, the level of risk attributable to the location and mode of occupation of the premises and the relevant claims record. In a block policy, the insurer may decide to load the premiums of all the properties insured by the client by the same percentage, taking an overall view rather than considering each property individually. This is obviously a pragmatic approach in cases where the landlord insures a large portfolio through a single policy or a series of policies with one insurer. Under the Berrycroft principle, the resulting premium for a particular property may be considered reasonable, even though a better deal could readily be struck taking particular premises in isolation, or by insuring with a range of different insurers.

 

5.10          On the evidence, the claims history of the subject property, taken as a whole, has been good. There is no evidence in support of Mr Murrells assertion that the claims record of this property has adversely affected the premium rates. There is some evidence that insurers load premiums on properties let weekly or monthly, particularly where the occupants are in receipt of housing benefit. It seems reasonable to assume that Bradford & Bingley were reasonably well informed about the circumstances of the subject property, whereas Endsleigh may have been at something of a disadvantage in this respect. Bradford & Bingleys premium rate for 2003-4 was £2.21 per £1,000, whereas Endsleigh quoted £1.63. These figures are strikingly different from Axas figure of just over £3.86 for the same period. Moreover, there is undisputed evidence that other landlords in the block have been insuring at much cheaper rates than Redglade.

 

5.11          Redglades answer to this evidence is that the insurance was obtained in the ordinary course of business through brokers who considered alternative providers. Mr Murrell produces insurance certificates, which appear to show that this property is insured separately and not within a block policy, and two letters from Heath Lambert Group. In the judgment of the Tribunal, the evidence from Heath Lambert is entirely insufficient to support Mr Murrells contention. The two letters are taken out of context and are unsupported by any professional analysis. The Tribunal does not know the terms of Heath Lamberts instructions; nor what was the nature and extent of the search for alternative insurers; nor what negotiations (if any) were undertaken with the insurers; nor what commissions were payable and to whom.

 

5.12          In the experience of the Tribunal, insurance commissions represent an important source of income for landlords of this type of property. Even where brokers are employed, they often share their commissions with the landlord. The 1999 revision to the lease of 73 Waterhouse Street provides direct evidence of the interest of this landlord in obtaining insurance commissions on a property in this building. The landlord is able to pass on to his tenants the whole insurance costs. There is no incentive for the landlord to take the trouble to review the sums insured or the premium rates so as to minimise insurance costs. Indeed, to do so is likely to reduce insurance commissions. By the same token, there is no incentive for the broker to advise the landlord to undertake such a review. In the judgment of the Tribunal a prudent landlord insuring at his own expense in the ordinary course of business would have undertaken a review and, having done so, would not have insured this property on the terms agreed with Axa and its predecessors. Accordingly the Tribunal is not satisfied that the insurance obtained by Redglade was negotiated in the ordinary course of business.

 

 

5.13          In those circumstances, the service charges in respect of insurance imposed on Home Housing by Redglade and now sought to be passed on to Mr Miah were not reasonably incurred. Accordingly, the Tribunal must consider what charges would have been reasonable for the periods in question. In the experience of the Tribunal, premium rates vary widely between insurers. A policy covering a commercial landlord for property including both shops and dwellings is likely to be more expensive than is available for a single shop or dwelling. Redglade cannot reasonably be expected to insure each unit separately. The Tribunal is not satisfied that the rates quoted by Endsleigh or Bradford & Bingley would be available from insurers quoting for commercial insurance of the whole of Redglades property on the site. On the other hand, it is clear that other reputable landlords in the block are paying far less. There is no reason to suppose that they are underinsured.

 

5.14          On the evidence as a whole and in the experience of the Tribunal, the rates agreed with Axa are unreasonably high. In all the circumstances, the Tribunal finds that a rate of £2.75 per £1,000 would be reasonable for 2003-4, to which 5% must be added for insurance premium tax. Thus the reasonable cost of insurance (excluding terrorist cover) for 30 Bridge Street is £322.94. For that year only Axa added £15.25 for terrorism cover, which does not seem unreasonable. Thus total insurance costs are £338.19.

 

5.15          The Tribunal has then to consider how premium rates have varied over the years in issue. Premium rates will not necessarily increase from year to year. The rates applied by Axa and its predecessors actually went down after 1995-6 and did not go up again until 2000-1. However, the Tribunal is aware that insurance premium rates have tended to increase over the past 3 or 4 years.  The Tribunal can find no reason to doubt that the percentage increases applied by Axa were reasonable. Using the percentage increases in premium rates employed by Axa and working backwards, the Tribunal has then calculated reasonable rates for the other years at issue. This has enbled the Tribunal to assess reasonable insurance costs for all the years in question. The figures are set out in the Second Schedule, together with the other elements of the service charge (which have not been challenged).
