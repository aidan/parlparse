5.         THE LAW

 

5.1               In general terms, the task of the Committee under the provisions of section 22 of the Housing Act 1988 is to determine:-

 

(a)    Whether there is a sufficient number of similar dwelling-houses in the locality let on assured tenancies to enable a determination to be made – if not, the Committee may not make a determination

 

(b)    If there is a sufficient number of such tenancies, whether the rent payable under the tenancy in question is significantly higher than the rent which the landlord might reasonably be expected to obtain under the tenancy, having regard to the level of rents payable under those tenancies

 

(c)    If the answer to both the above questions is in the affirmative, what is the rent which the landlord might reasonably be expected to obtain under the tenancy.

 

5.2               It follows that the personal circumstances of the parties are irrelevant and the Committee cannot take them into account. Full Reasons (if requested) will set out the relevant legal principles in more detail.

 

6.         THE DECISION

 

6.1        The location is rural and off the beaten track but within an easy car journey from Wisbech and the main line railway station at Downham Market. The parties offer no evidence of rental value. The Committee's own knowledge and experience suggest that there are many three-bedroom house in the broad locality let on assured tenancies (including many let on assured shorthold terms). Most do not come with agricultural land. They achieve rents around £450 p.c.m. if centrally heated, modernised and in reasonable condition, even when let without white goods.

 

6.2               This house is modernised and basically in reasonable condition. The boiler installation could be completed and the heating system got into working order at modest expense. The tenant would probably be entitled to deduct the cost from the rent. The Committee considers that the property should be assessed on the basis that central heating (though temporarily inoperative) is fitted.

 

6.3               The property looks in poor order internally mainly because the tenant and Ms Al Bandak have not finished settling in. They have not finished unpacking. They have not installed a cooker (apart from a camping stove) or a dishwasher in the places provided.

 

6.4               Ms Al Bandak’s comments about the condition of the land and outbuildings accorded with the Committee’s observations. The Committee felt able to have regard to the information about the tenancy terms as an admission against interest made on behalf of the tenant.

 

6.5               The Committee accordingly finds that the property is let with 8 acres of land almost all of which could readily be cleared with the aid of suitable machinery and equipment. Contractors with such machinery and equipment can be readily found locally. The available outbuildings are in fairly poor condition but nevertheless useful. The landlady’s mobile home and reserved rights imply a degree of loss of privacy. But the location of the mobile home minimises that effect.

 

6.6               If and insofar as the tenant’s apparent inability to resolve issues with the landlady and her husband stems from lack of funds, then that is a personal circumstance which the Committee should not take into account.

 

6.7               The inclusion of the land and the available outbuildings increases the rental value of the property above the rents paid for similar properties without land etc. Properties to let with land and extensive outbuildings are uncommon and, in the judgment of the Committee, the effect of such amenities upon rental value is considerable.

 

6.8               Bearing all the above-mentioned matters in mind, £650 p.c.m. is a reasonable open market rent for the property as a whole as let and having regard to the terms of the tenancy. In those circumstances, the Committee has no jurisdiction to determine a rent and declines to make a determination.

 

6.9               The Committee cannot advise the parties; but points out that the remedies for the tenant’s apparent problems with the landlady and her husband lie not in the Rent Assessment Committee but in the County Court. The findings of the Committee may be of use to the tenant in that respect.
